Introduction: The Anarchist and the Capitalist

In 1849, two Frenchmen conducted the most consequential economic debate of the nineteenth century in the pages of La Voix du peuple. Pierre-Joseph Proudhon, the anarchist, declared interest to be theft—a mechanism by which the idle rich extracted wealth from workers. His remedy was radical: nationalize the Banque de France, flood the economy with paper money, and drive the cost of borrowing to near zero through a People’s Bank charging just 0.5% to cover administration. The results, Proudhon promised, would be spectacular: no debt, fewer bankruptcies, guaranteed employment, and increased consumption.

Frédéric Bastiat, the liberal economist, demolished each claim. Interest was not theft but a fair reward for the use of capital over time. As Benjamin Franklin had put it, “Time is money—Time is the stuff of which life is made.” If lending went unrewarded, it would effectively be penalized—and capital would vanish. Bastiat made a prediction that would prove prophetic a century and a half later: the abolition of interest would benefit the wealthy, who could borrow for free to speculate, while the poor would find themselves unable to borrow at any price. Free credit would be a “calamity for the working classes.”

“In the sphere of economics, a habit or law engenders not just one effect but a series. Only the first is immediate; it is seen. The others occur successively; they are not seen.”—Bastiat

The debate ended with Proudhon’s chilling farewell: “Monsieur Bastiat, you are a dead man.” Bastiat died one year later. But after the collapse of Lehman Brothers in 2008, central banks effectively implemented Proudhon’s scheme, pushing interest rates to the lowest level in five millennia. Europe and Japan went further, turning rates negative. The results diverged sharply from Proudhon’s utopian expectations. Productivity collapsed. Inequality soared. Zombie companies multiplied. Housing became unaffordable. Financial fragility intensified. The outcomes ran closer to every warning Bastiat had issued.

William White, the Canadian economist, catalogued the damage in a 2012 paper: ultra-easy money encouraged households to spend more and save less at the very moment they needed to save more for retirement. Firms invested less despite cheap credit. Creative destruction was thwarted. “At best, it buys time to rebalance economies,” White concluded. “In reality, this opportunity is wasted.”

Bastiat’s framework extends beyond interest to a general principle of economic reasoning. His “Broken Window” parable warns that the bad economist sees only the immediate, visible effect of a policy—lower rates boost spending—while the good economist traces the unseen cascade: malinvestment, zombie companies, inequality, financial fragility. Henry Hazlitt elaborated the principle in Economics in One Lesson, attacking the “full employment fetish” and warning that the artificial reduction of interest rates “discourages normal thrift, saving, and investment” and “slows down that increase in productivity.”

William Graham Sumner distilled the dynamic into his parable of the Forgotten Man: reformers A and B design a scheme to help unfortunate X, but the cost falls on C, who is never consulted. In the zero-rate era, C was the saver, the pensioner, the worker whose wages stagnated while asset prices soared. The forgotten man of monetary policy received no bailout, no subsidized loan, no seat at the table where rates were set. He was, as Bastiat would have recognized, the victim of the unseen effects— the cascade of consequences that follows when the price of time is manipulated by those who believe they know better than the market.

The complexity of interest defies simple definition. Böhm-Bawerk catalogued two dozen different theories. Fisher thought them all “harmonious and complementary.” Interest is the wage of abstinence, the cost of leverage, the capitalization rate of assets, and the price of risk. But the most encompassing description remains: the price of time.

The book’s central thesis crystallizes from this wreckage: interest is not merely a policy lever for central banks to twist. It is the price of time itself—an organizing principle of capitalism stretching back to ancient Babylon— and when it is suppressed, everything breaks. What follows is a tour through five millennia of monetary history, from Mesopotamian clay tablets to Jerome Powell’s printing press, tracing the same pattern in every era: artificially cheap money inflates, distorts, and eventually destroys.

Part One: Of Historical Interest

Chapter 1: Babylonian Birth

Interest predates coined money by at least two thousand years. The ancient etymologies tell the story: the Sumerian word for interest, mas, means kid goat; the Greek tokos means calf; the Latin foenus connotes fertility; English capital derives from caput, head of cattle. Loans of seeds and livestock yielded a natural increase—you lent grain at planting and received more at harvest. Interest was, in its origins, a biological fact before it was an economic concept.

Mesopotamian clay tablets from the third and second millennia BC record a sophisticated credit system. Each tablet named the creditor, debtor, amount, date, interest rate, and collateral. Standard rates held remarkably stable across centuries: 33.33% for barley, 20% for silver. These were not usurious by context—they reflected the genuine productivity of agricultural capital. Loans financed real estate, slaves, business ventures, foreign trade, and consumption. Bottomry loans on ships’ keels charged higher interest but forgave the principal if the voyage failed—an early form of risk-adjusted pricing.

Interest also functioned as rent. When a borrower pledged land as collateral, the lender could take possession and collect rent in lieu of interest— so-called anticretic interest. Babylonians used the same word, mas, for both interest and rent, revealing what modern economists would rediscover millennia later: the return on capital and the rate of interest are deeply connected. The person who lives off interest, the rentier, takes his title from this ancient equation between lending and landowning.

“The emergence of interest to incentivize lending is the most significant of all innovations in the history of finance.” —William Goetzmann

The priestesses of the sun god Samas, known as naditu, drove male merchants from the lending business and accumulated fortunes rivaling any banking dynasty. The Egibi family of Neo-Babylonia provided an array of merchant-banking services that earned them the title “Rothschilds of the Ancient Near East.” These Babylonians also invented compound interest— mash mash, livestock replicating itself. A clay cone from before 2400 BC records a debt that accrued compound interest at 33.33% until it reached an unpayable 4.5 trillion litres of barley. When debts spiraled beyond repayment, rulers proclaimed jubilees. Enmetena of Lagash issued the first known debt cancellation; Athens’s Solon followed suit in 594 BC.

Hammurabi’s Code, engraved on a basalt stele around 1750 BC, capped rates and penalized lenders who overcharged or used fraudulent weights. Yet regulatory arbitrage was born alongside regulation: lenders applied the maximum rate to terms as short as a month, producing effective APRs of 260% or more. As Eugen von Böhm-Bawerk later observed, the rate of interest followed a U-shaped curve through ancient civilizations—declining as Babylon, Greece, and Rome prospered, then rising sharply during their decline. Very low interest rates appeared to be the calm before the storm. Not a comforting thought given the rates of the early twenty-first century.

Monetary factors also shaped ancient interest rates. When Emperor Augustus brought Egyptian treasure to Rome, money became plentiful and rates fell from 6% to 4%. After his death, Emperor Tiberius hoarded money, rates rose, and a banking crisis erupted in AD 33. Tiberius eventually lent out the imperial treasure free of interest, producing an immediate decline in rates—the world’s first experience of quantitative easing. Two thousand years later, property markets remained just as sensitive to changes in the money supply and interest rates as they had been when Augustus flooded Rome with treasure and house prices soared.

Irving Fisher called interest “too omnipresent a phenomenon to be eradicated.” Schumpeter wrote that it “permeates, as it were, the whole economic system.” Marx observed that “usury lives in the pores of production.” Interest exists because resources are scarce, production takes time, lending is risky, and humans are naturally impatient. It arose from need and greed simultaneously, and no legal prohibition has ever abolished it for long.

Chapter 2: Selling Time

The condemnation of usury runs through nearly every civilization. Aristotle called the birth of money from money the most unnatural of all acquisitions. Plato depicted it as setting rich against poor. The Old Testament banned charging interest to fellow Israelites, using the Hebrew word neschek—literally, to bite. Dante placed usurers in the seventh circle of hell. Aquinas built an elaborate theological case that charging for loans was inherently unjust because money existed solely for exchange.

Yet Seneca the Younger, who preached against usury, was himself one of Rome’s most covetous moneylenders. His “insatiable usury” allegedly provoked Boudica’s rebellion in AD 60. The hypocrisy ran deep: at one moment Seneca suggested it was fair to pay more interest than agreed; at another, he compared usury to murder. The moral condemnation of lending at interest has always coexisted with the practical necessity of it. No civilization that outlawed interest ever managed to abolish it—the prohibition merely drove lending underground into more expensive and less transparent channels.

The fatal flaw in the Aristotelian argument was its refusal to acknowledge that money is also a store of value. When a lender parts with money, he retains its stored value in the form of a debt claim but surrenders its exchange value. The temporal dimension matters: lending takes place across time, and the fourteenth-century theologian Thomas of Cobham grasped this when he declared the usurer a “thief of time”—someone selling something that belongs properly to God.

“Usurye is also saide to be the price of tyme, or of the delaying or forbearing of moneye.” —Thomas Wilson, 1572

The Renaissance secularized time. Leon Battista Alberti made proper time management a virtue; Fernand Braudel called it the shift from God’s time to “merchant’s time.” Public clocks appeared in every major Italian city. Francesco Datini, the Merchant of Prato, opened his ledgers “In the name of God and of Profits” and waited 3.5 years from first wool order to final sale in Valencia, earning less than 9% on the round trip. No wonder Italian merchants priced time obsessively.

Once time had value and was an individual’s possession rather than God’s, the clerical injunctions against usury lost their force. The word interest itself derives from the Latin interesse—compensation paid by a defaulting debtor. It came to mean the opportunity cost suffered by the creditor, what the medieval scholastics called lucrum cessans (foregone profit). Bernardino of Siena, the Franciscan, conceded that “money has not simply character of money, but productive character, which we commonly call capital.” Lord Shaftesbury declared that “Interest governs the World.” Bernard Mandeville’s Fable of the Bees exposed the paradox that private vices brought public benefits. The usurer had become the capitalist, and capitalism was transmitted across credit networks connecting lenders and borrowers through bonds of mutual self-interest.

Medieval interest rates fell steadily as capital accumulated: from 20% in Italy around 1200 to 5% by 1550. The legal prohibition never stopped lending— it merely drove it underground through dry exchange (cambi secchi), where interest hid inside foreign exchange transactions. Queen Elizabeth’s law of 1571 legalized lending at up to 10%, distinguishing interest from usury. By this point, England had already transformed into what historians call an “economy of obligation” where credit suffused every corner of commerce.

The most enduring insight is Turgot’s: “A bird in the hand is worth two in the bush.” This is time preference—the propensity to value present goods more highly than future goods. Interest is the rate at which present consumption exchanges for future consumption. The famous marshmallow test at Stanford, where only a third of children could defer gratification long enough to earn the extra treat, demonstrates that positive time preference is hardwired. Irving Fisher called interest “human impatience crystallized into a market rate.” If this is a fundamental attribute of human nature, then negative interest rates are unnatural by definition.

When interest rates fall below society’s crystallized impatience, the consequences are predictable. People borrow to consume, pulling forward spending that leaves the future poorer. Businesses invest in projects whose distant pay-offs could never justify themselves at honest rates. Böhm-Bawerk’s pupils— Hayek, Mises, Schumpeter—devoted their careers to this problem. The Austrian term for the result is malinvestment: expensive white-elephant projects that absorb capital without generating adequate returns. The opposite of malinvestment is creative destruction, and interest is the mechanism that makes the distinction.

Chapter 3: The Lowering of Interest

The seventeenth-century English debate over interest rates anticipated every argument recycled after 2008. Sir Josiah Child, leader of the East India Company and one of the wealthiest men in England, campaigned relentlessly to cap rates at 4%. Lower interest, Child claimed, would increase industry, multiply traders, and double real wealth within twenty years. His critics noticed that Child, who maintained control of the East India Company through debt, stood to benefit enormously from cheaper borrowing. A cynical pamphleteer observed the scheme was designed for “ingrossing all trade, into hands of a few rich Merchants.”

Child was not the first. His father, Sir Thomas Culpeper, had campaigned for lower rates a generation earlier, claiming the abatement of interest would “revive our dying Manufacture.” Lower interest would raise land prices and ease the debt burden of landowners. The argument appealed to a broad coalition: indebted gentry, ambitious merchants, and politicians seeking easy solutions. By the late seventeenth century, the debate had become the central question of English economic policy. Rates had been falling across Europe as capital accumulated—by 1600, Genoese loan yields dipped below 3%, and Amsterdam borrowing costs reached an astonishing 1.75%, the lowest in Northern European history. Child cited Dutch prosperity as proof that low rates caused wealth. His critics replied that causation ran in the opposite direction.

John Locke wrote the definitive rebuttal. Artificially cheap money would be a “loss to Widows, Orphans, and all those who have their Estates in Money.” Every man would rush to borrow; lenders, receiving inadequate compensation for risk, would hoard their capital rather than lend it. Locke argued the Dutch were rich not because their rates were low but because their “industry and frugality” had generated abundant capital. England’s trade had thrived under Elizabeth when interest stood at 10%.

“I think I may reasonably infer… that lowering of Interest is not a sure way to improve either our Trade or Wealth.” —John Locke

Locke’s warnings, translated into modern language, read like a checklist of post-2008 consequences: financiers benefit at the expense of savers; wealth redistributes from creditors to borrowers; creditors are inadequately compensated for risk; too much borrowing ensues; asset price inflation makes the rich richer; and rate cuts fail to revive a moribund economy. Every single prediction materialized after the Federal Reserve pushed rates to zero.

Locke won the debate practically—Child failed to secure his rate reduction in the 1660s or early 1690s. In 1713, the maximum legal rate was fixed at 5%. It took until 1854 for England’s usury law to be finally removed. But the intellectual victory was more ambiguous. Centuries of politicians would continue to reach for the same lever Child had championed, convinced that cheaper credit was the master key to prosperity. After 2008, the world’s central banks enacted precisely the policy Locke had warned against—flooding the economy with cheap money, benefiting financiers at the expense of savers, and producing every pathology Locke had catalogued three centuries earlier.

The concept of the natural rate of interest—the rate that balances saving and investment without intervention—emerged from this period. Sir William Petty dismissed the “vanity and fruitlessness of making Civil Positive Laws against the Laws of Nature.” Henry Thornton, writing at the turn of the nineteenth century, observed that usury laws forcing rates below their natural level meant borrowers “obtain too cheap they demand in too great quantity,” creating conditions for unstable booms. Knut Wicksell later formalized this: when the market rate diverges from the natural rate, the result is either inflation or deflation. But even Wicksell’s insight had a fatal blind spot—the natural rate cannot be directly observed, and using stable consumer prices as proof that rates are correct ignores asset bubbles and credit excesses entirely.

The seventeenth-century debate illuminates a permanent tension in monetary thought. On one side stand those who view interest as an obstacle to prosperity—a tax on enterprise that should be minimized. On the other stand those who see it as an organic market price whose suppression generates far worse consequences than the cost it imposes. Sir William Petty dismissed the “vanity and fruitlessness of making Civil Positive Laws against the Laws of Nature.” The Austrian school would later formalize this insight: interest set by the market coordinates investment and consumption across time; artificially suppressed, it produces malinvestment, asset bubbles, and the eventual reckoning that is always more painful than the discipline it was meant to avoid.

Chapter 4: The Chimera

John Law was a Scottish gambler, convicted murderer, and self-taught monetary theorist who talked the Duke of Orléans into the most ambitious financial experiment before quantitative easing. France in 1715 was bankrupt: Louis XIV had left a national debt of two billion livres—eighteen years of tax revenue—with interest consuming 60% of the budget. Law’s solution was paper money backed by land and trade monopolies, which would flood the economy with credit and drive interest rates to nothing.

Law founded the General Bank in 1716, acquired the Mississippi Company in 1717, and merged them into a conglomerate controlling all foreign trade, tobacco, the mint, and tax collection. Shares went from 500 livres to 10,000 in the frenzied speculation along the Rue Quincampoix. The word “millionaire” entered the French language. Note circulation reached two billion livres—fifty times earlier issuance—while interest rates collapsed exactly as Law had predicted. Government debt yielded just 2%. Margin loans cost 2%. Mississippi shares traded at roughly fifty times earnings.

For a brief, intoxicating moment, Law’s system appeared to work. He later recalled that the “Prince was head of rich people, revenues increased and burdens reduced. No longer uncultivated lands or workers without work.” The French ambassador praised Law as the “first minister” who would raise France to greater heights than ever. Law explicitly justified the astronomical share price by reference to low interest rates—falling rates, he argued, “carried value of lands to 80 and 100 years purchase, raised up stately edifices.” The logic was identical to that employed by every bubble-era bull from Irving Fisher in 1929 to the tech analysts of the Dotcom era to the Federal Reserve after 2008: low rates justify high valuations.

“Law was ‘irreversibly committed’ to easy money”—had he reversed course and hiked rates, the Mississippi stock price would have crashed. —Earl J. Hamilton

The system collapsed because paper money, unlike gold, can be produced without limit. Once banknotes escaped from financial markets into the real economy, consumer prices surged. Confidence evaporated. Riots broke out. Law was sacked, slipped out of France, and died in Venice nine years later. Richard Cantillon, the Parisian banker who both profited from and analyzed the mania, provided the key insight: newly printed money initially remains trapped in the financial system, inflating asset prices rather than consumer prices. The danger arrives when it finally leaks into the wider economy. This is precisely what happened after 2008—the Federal Reserve’s newly created money inflated stocks, bonds, and real estate for a decade before consumer inflation finally erupted in 2021.

Law’s personal collapse was total. Riots broke out. The Royal Bank was stormed. Law was sacked, tendered his resignation in December, and slipped out of France the following month, leaving behind his dreams, his fortune, his wife, and his daughter. He died in Venice nine years later. The system he built proved that paper money, unlike gold, can be produced without limit—and once confidence in unlimited paper evaporates, it evaporates all at once. But Law was no simple charlatan. He was, as Earl J. Hamilton observed, “irreversibly committed” to easy money. Had he reversed course and hiked rates, the Mississippi stock price would have crashed and the government’s debt refinancing would have unraveled. He was trapped by the same logic that would trap the Federal Reserve three centuries later: the policy creates dependencies that make reversal impossible.

Modern economists judge Law surprisingly kindly. Peter Garber of Brown calls Law’s credit theory the “centerpiece of most money and macroeconomics textbooks.” William Goetzmann of Yale credits Law’s belief that insufficient money constrains economic activity as “the essential principle underlying decisions by the U.S. Federal Reserve today.” The kindness is revealing: modern central bankers are operating from the same playbook that produced the worst financial catastrophe of the eighteenth century.

The Mississippi Scheme established the template for every subsequent episode of monetary excess: an indebted government finds a monetary alchemist who promises to dissolve the debt through credit creation; asset prices soar as rates collapse; the newly created money remains trapped in financial markets, inflating wealth rather than prices, until it leaks into the real economy and consumer inflation erupts; confidence breaks; and the cleanup falls on ordinary citizens while the architects escape or claim their intentions were noble. Three hundred years later, the template remained operational, the only difference being scale.

Chapter 5: John Bull Cannot Stand Two Per Cent

English commerce from the early eighteenth century exhibited a recurring pattern. When credit was abundant and interest rates low, merchants grew daring, bankers accommodated their recklessness, and speculative manias followed. The pattern was so reliable that Lord Overstone codified it in 1837: quiescence, improvement, growing confidence, prosperity, excitement, overtrading, convulsion, pressure, stagnation, distress—and then quiescence again. Walter Bagehot, the legendary editor of The Economist, added the psychological dimension: “Credit, the disposition of one man to trust another, is singularly varying.” Interest was the barometer of that trust.

Low rates also fueled capital-intensive industries. When Consol yields declined, the output of bricks moved inversely—a great building boom erupted in the mid-1760s when mortgage rates fell to an abnormally low 3.5%, prompting Horace Walpole to observe a “rage of building every where.” The pattern repeated with mechanical regularity: each period of easy money produced a speculative mania in whatever long-duration asset captured the public imagination—canals, railways, foreign bonds, real estate. The bubble inflated during cheap money, then burst when rates inevitably rose and Bagehot’s cycle entered its convulsion phase.

The Railway Mania of the 1840s illustrated the mechanism. Britain’s railway investment reached £560 million by mid-decade, exceeding national income, after Bank of England discount rates fell below 3% and touched 2% in 1843. The Times complained that “money is so very abundant that dealers in it have literally no employment for it.” Bagehot delivered the immortal verdict: “People could not stand two per cent.”

“The good times of high price almost always engender much fraud. All people are most credulous when they are most happy.” —Walter Bagehot

The Overend Gurney collapse of 1866 demonstrated what happens when the mania unwinds. The discount house had transformed from conservative bill broker into speculative financier, locking capital in illiquid investments while funding itself with short- term commercial paper. Black Friday, May 11, 1866, produced a “reign of terror” that inspired Bagehot to write Lombard Street and articulate the lender of last resort doctrine: in a panic, the central bank must lend freely, against good collateral, at a high rate. After 2008, central banks claimed to follow Bagehot but violated every principle—lending at the lowest rates, for years on end, against poor-quality collateral, with minimal concern for moral hazard.

After Black Friday, yields declined and money became abundant again, but credit remained broken. George Goschen, a Bank of England director, penned an essay titled “Two Per Cent.” The cheapness of money, he observed, “has not borne its accustomed fruit.” Speculation “remains cold and refuses to be comforted. Two per cent. woos in vain!” The paradox foreshadowed the post-2008 experience: after a severe financial crisis, even the cheapest money cannot restore confidence. Goschen’s subsequent conversion of 3% Consols into bonds with a lower yield—the greatest conversion of public debt in British history—had the perverse effect of stinting “the hungrier capitalists of interest,” pushing them toward riskier assets and setting the stage for the next bust.

When rates fell again after 1866, investors desperate for yield poured money into foreign bonds— Egypt at 10%, South American republics promising even more. A wave of defaults followed by 1875: Costa Rica, the Dominican Republic, Paraguay, Spain, Bolivia, Guatemala. Bagehot’s reflection was timeless: “the human mind likes 15 per cent; it likes things which promise much.” The Barings Crisis of 1890 repeated the cycle exactly. George Goschen’s conversion of Consols to a lower yield had stinted “hungrier capitalists of interest,” driving them into Argentine bonds and South African mining stocks until Baring Brothers itself broke.

Bagehot died of pneumonia in March 1877 at the age of fifty-one. He is best remembered for the lender-of-last-resort doctrine, but his more profound insight was about the consequences of cheap money: how rates at 2% or less fuel speculative manias, drive savers into risky investments, encourage reckless foreign lending, and produce crises whose severity is proportional to the preceding excess. Modern monetary mandarins invoked Bagehot constantly after 2008 but never considered his warnings about the pathologies of easy money. They took the emergency doctrine and left the diagnostic framework that made the emergency doctrine necessary.

Chapter 6: Un Petit Coup de Whisky

In July 1927, four central bankers gathered at Benjamin Strong’s estate on Long Island. Montagu Norman of the Bank of England needed lower American rates to relieve pressure on sterling. Hjalmar Schacht of the Reichsbank worried too much foreign capital was flowing into Germany. Strong agreed to cut the Federal Reserve’s benchmark rate. He told Charles Rist of the Bank of France that the rate cut would provide a “petit coup de whisky” to the New York stock market. It turned out to be pure moonshine.

The Federal Reserve had been created in 1914 to furnish an “elastic currency” —one where interest rates could be managed rather than determined by the requirement to convert notes into gold. Benjamin Anderson, chief economist at Chase National Bank, observed that between 1924 and 1928, the Fed’s elastic currency was used “to finance a stock market boom.” Bank credit more than doubled. Much new lending went to stock margin loans, real estate mortgages, and foreign securities.

Low US rates also drove massive capital outflows to higher-yielding foreign bonds. German call money rates averaged 8.5% in 1925—three times US Treasury bills. Foreign securities issuance peaked at $1.5 billion in 1927, six times the 1923 level. National City Bank sold Brazilian bonds despite internal warnings that the issuer was a poor credit; nearly two-thirds of 1928 foreign bond issues would later default. As in every subsequent era of cheap American money, the capital that flowed out during the boom returned as crisis when rates rose, devastating the borrower nations that had become dependent on it.

The period from 1922 to 1929 saw consumer prices barely move—Strong’s price stabilization policy appeared to be working perfectly. But stable consumer prices masked explosive credit growth and asset inflation. The US economy grew at nearly 8% annually between 1923 and 1928, yet the NY Fed’s discount rate averaged less than half that figure. The Austrian school understood why this was dangerous: Hayek argued that in a period of rapid productivity growth, consumer prices should fall naturally. By preventing this benign deflation, the Fed was injecting credit that inflated assets and funded malinvestment.

“‘Cheap money’ characterized both ‘new eras,’ and cheap money is the most dangerous intoxicant known to economic life.” —Benjamin Anderson

After the 1927 rate cut, margin loans soared and the S&P 500 climbed 37.5% in a single year. When the Fed belatedly tightened in 1928, raising its discount rate from 3.5% to 5%, the higher rates reversed international capital flows, draining gold from Europe. Lord Rothermere warned that “Wall Street has become a colossal suction-pump, which is draining the world of capital.” Strong died of tuberculosis in October 1928. Norman sank into depression. The crash came twelve months later, followed by the collapse of Creditanstalt and the Great Depression.

Felix Somary, a Swiss banker who had studied with Böhm-Bawerk, warned Keynes in Berlin in April 1928 that a crisis was imminent. Keynes dismissed him: “We will not have any more crashes in our time.” Within eighteen months, Keynes’s personal fortune had declined by more than three-quarters. Irving Fisher, who had declared that stocks had reached a “permanently high plateau,” lost his fortune entirely. History was written by the victors: Bernanke’s interpretation that the Fed’s error was allowing deflation— not causing the bubble—became the received wisdom that guided policy after 2008.

Hjalmar Schacht reportedly told the central bankers at the Long Island meeting: “Don’t give me a low rate, give me a true rate, and then I shall know how to keep my house in order.” Adolph Miller, a Fed Board member, bitterly opposed the rate cut, later calling it “one of the most costly errors committed by it or any other banking system in the last 75 years.” History judged Miller and Schacht right and Strong wrong— but history was written by the victors, and the victors were the deflation-phobia school that would produce Bernanke’s promise at Friedman’s ninetieth birthday never to let the 1930s happen again. The “petit coup de whisky” was rebranded as insufficient medicine, and the real disease—cheap money itself—was prescribed as the cure.

Part Two: How Low Rates Begot Lower Rates

Chapter 7: Goodhart’s Law

Alan Greenspan studied clarinet at Juilliard before turning to economics and opaque monetary communication. His defining moment came in October 1987, when the worst stock market crash since 1929 prompted him to slash the fed funds rate and flood Wall Street with liquidity. The market bounced back, and a precedent was established: the Federal Reserve would intervene to halt market declines. Wall Street called it the “Greenspan put”—an unwritten contract guaranteeing that the central bank would backstop losses.

Through the 1990s, the fed funds rate was held below nominal GDP growth, and Greenspan allowed the Nasdaq to inflate into the greatest valuation bubble in American history. His December 1996 “irrational exuberance” question was rhetorical—it produced no policy action. After Long-Term Capital Management nearly failed in September 1998, the Fed cut rates 25 basis points. Between October 1997 and the Nasdaq peak two and a half years later, the index nearly tripled.

Ben Bernanke joined the Fed in 2002 and provided intellectual justification for ignoring asset bubbles. Bubbles were impossible to identify in real time, he argued; monetary policy should deal with the aftermath rather than act preemptively. He advocated aggressive preemption against only one threat: deflation. In spring 2003, the fed funds rate was cut to 1% and held there for over a year. For five consecutive years, the key policy rate remained below the economic growth rate.

Economists called the resulting calm the “Great Moderation.” Inflation was tame. Downturns were mild. The Bank of England coined the acronym NICE—Non-Inflationary Consistent Expansion. Gordon Brown, Britain’s Chancellor, hailed the end of boom and bust. In 2004, Greenspan boasted that the strategy of addressing bubble consequences rather than preventing them had been “successful.” The illusion of stability, as Hyman Minsky would have predicted, was breeding the instability that would shatter it. In the decade to 2008, Americans extracted $9 trillion in home equity, financing consumption with debt secured against inflating house prices.

“Policy accommodation is distorting asset prices. Most of this distortion is deliberate and a desirable effect of the stance of policy. We have attempted to lower interest rates below long-term equilibrium rates and to boost asset prices in order to stimulate demand.” —Donald Kohn, FOMC, March 2004

In the decade to 2008, Americans extracted $9 trillion in home equity. Bernanke told Congress that house price increases “largely reflect strong economic fundamentals.” Only William White at the BIS dissented, warning that credit growth and asset price inflation were more reliable crisis harbingers than consumer prices. White predicted that if rates stayed too low, the economy might enter a liquidity trap and, like Japan, suffer decades of sclerosis. He was right on every count.

The riskiest subprime loans were priced off short-term rates, including adjustable-rate mortgages with negative amortization. Only after the Fed launched its easy money policy did credit growth pick up, financial leverage soar, housing markets bubble, and underwriting standards collapse. William Poole of the St. Louis Fed acknowledged that low rates had fueled the housing boom. Thomas Hoenig of Kansas City found “strong supporting evidence” that negative real rates between 2002 and 2004 contributed to the housing and credit boom. The causal chain was clear, yet it remained politically impossible to acknowledge because the same institution that had caused the crisis was now in charge of cleaning it up.

After the crisis, monetary policy’s role was downplayed. Bernanke blamed poor financial regulation— ironic, since the Fed under Bernanke was America’s chief financial regulator. But as Anna Schwartz observed, the underlying cause was “too-easy monetary policy and too-low interest rates.” John Taylor of Stanford agreed: “monetary excesses were the main cause of the boom and the resulting bust.” Only after the Fed launched its easy money policy did credit growth pick up, leverage soar, housing markets bubble, and underwriting standards collapse.

Chapter 8: Secular Stagnation

After every crisis, an explanation emerges to absolve policymakers of responsibility. Irving Fisher’s debt-deflation theory, published in 1933, argued that the Great Depression was caused by falling prices increasing the real burden of debt. The solution was reflation. Bernanke adopted this interpretation wholesale, concluding that the Fed’s error in the 1930s was allowing consumer prices to fall— not that it had caused the bubble in the first place. At Milton Friedman’s ninetieth birthday in 2002, Bernanke apologized on behalf of the Fed for the Depression and vowed it would never happen again.

After 2008, the exculpatory narrative was secular stagnation. Low rates were not a policy choice but a natural consequence of aging populations, declining productivity, and a global “savings glut.” This framework, rooted in David Hume’s eighteenth-century monetary theory, treated money as a mere veil over the real economy and interest as determined by frugality and industry rather than monetary policy. Central bankers insisted they were merely following the natural rate downward.

“If the origin of the problem was too much debt, how can a policy that encourages the private and public sectors to accumulate more debt be part of the solution?” —Claudio Borio, BIS

The historical evidence undermines this defense. The Bank for International Settlements examined data from 39 countries over 150 years and found only a “tenuous” link between deflation and economic weakness. The 1930s was the exception, not the rule. During the 1921 depression, prices fell 15% annualized and real rates exceeded 20%, yet the economy recovered within a year and manufacturing productivity jumped a massive 20%. Alexander Field went further, arguing that the 1930s were the most technologically progressive decade in American history. The Austrian view—that benign deflation from productivity gains is healthy, and that fighting it with easy money only guarantees a worse crash later—had been vindicated, but it remained excluded from the mainstream narrative.

Larry Summers himself made a remarkable reversal in 2019, tweeting that central bankers’ ultra-low nominal yields were reducing aggregate demand, breeding financial instability, “zombification,” and reduced economic dynamism. The radical implication: ultra-low rates might cause secular stagnation rather than result from it. The very architect of the secular stagnation narrative was acknowledging that the cure had become the disease.

Bernanke’s reading of the 1930s ignored a critical asymmetry. Hayek distinguished between “good deflation” from productivity improvements and “bad deflation” that follows a credit bust. When policymakers ease to ward off benign price decline, they incentivize excessive borrowing. Fisher himself acknowledged that debt deflation starts from a position of over-indebtedness—a fact Bernanke conspicuously overlooked. Attempts to prevent good deflation only make bad deflation more likely when the bubble eventually bursts.

The 1921 depression is the clearest counterexample to the deflation-phobia narrative. Prices collapsed at an annualized rate of 15%. Real interest rates exceeded 20%. The Fed did nothing to ease conditions. Yet the economy recovered within a year, and manufacturing productivity surged by 20%. Between 1929 and 1941, despite the Depression, the US economy expanded at 2.8% annually—in line with its long-term average. Americans growing up after the war suffered no permanent welfare loss. The evidence suggests that sharp, unimpeded downturns are the pit stops that keep the capitalist engine running.

The real lesson of the 1930s was not that deflation must be fought at all costs but that the preceding credit boom had created an over-indebted economy vulnerable to any shock. Chester Phillips and colleagues concluded in 1937 that “the futility of price level stabilization as a goal is evidenced by the fact that the end-result of probably the greatest price-stabilization experiment in history proved to be, simply, the greatest and worst depression.” Yet Bernanke’s version of events—blaming the Fed for allowing deflation rather than for creating the bubble—became the intellectual foundation for the post-2008 experiment in zero rates.

Chapter 9: The Raven of Basel

Claudio Borio, the Oxford-educated head of the BIS Monetary and Economics Department, rejected the secular stagnation narrative entirely. He cast aside the “money veil” and argued that the financial crisis was caused not by a savings glut but by a credit glut— banks conjure deposits, spending power, and savings when they make loans. “Economics without finance,” Borio declared, “is like Hamlet without the prince.”

Borio’s team found no stable relationship between interest rates and savings, investment, or demographics. Rates were more influenced by monetary regimes: higher under the Gold Standard, lower under Bretton Woods, lowest after 1971 when the dollar was cut loose from gold entirely. US long-term rates dropped below their post-war average only after the Fed’s influence became semi-permanent. Interest rates were a policy variable masquerading as a natural phenomenon.

Borio also challenged the conventional “natural rate” concept. Central bankers used stable consumer prices as proof that their rates were correct—the Wicksellian framework. But Borio pointed out a devastating fact: both the 1929 crash and the 2008 crisis occurred during periods of low, stable consumer inflation. Price stability was not merely an inadequate guide to the appropriate interest rate—it was positively misleading. Borio developed an alternative framework based on the financial cycle—credit growth and asset prices—which proved a far better indicator of developing imbalances than the output gap that mainstream economists relied upon.

The BIS defined interest as the “price of leverage.” A vicious cycle emerged: ever-lower rates were needed to sustain a rising debt stock, and rising debt required yet lower rates. The debt-service ratio tended to remain constant as both rates and debt moved in opposite directions—until the system snapped. Post-crisis, ultra-low rates enabled banks to delay recognizing bad debts. Zombie companies became commonplace, their cumulative effect reducing potential output growth.

“The highly abnormal is becoming uncomfortably normal.” —Claudio Borio, October 2014

The Fed’s asymmetric approach—reflexive easing after every bust but slow tightening during booms—created a downward bias to rates and an upward bias to debt. As Philip Lowe and Borio warned in 2002, “lowering rates when problems materialise but not raising rates as imbalances build up, can be rather insidious in the longer run.” Cross-border capital flows amplified the problem: low US rates after 2002 spurred massive international carry trades, and when Bernanke merely hinted at tapering in 2013, the “taper tantrum” sent emerging market currencies into freefall.

Monetary policy, Borio concluded, was path dependent—once the wrong turning was taken, the financial system was driven permanently off course. Economic growth faltered, financial imbalances accumulated, capital was misallocated, bubbles formed, and too much debt piled up. After the crisis, central bankers tried quantitative easing, paying banks interest on reserves, and zero or negative rates—each measure more extreme than the last, each producing diminishing returns. “If I were you,” Borio told policymakers, “I would not start from here.” He feared that monetary policy was losing efficacy, and that central bankers were running out of ammunition precisely when the arsenal was most needed.

Borio feared an “epoch-defining seismic rupture” heralding the end of globalization. Monetary policy was path dependent—once the wrong turning was taken, the financial system was driven permanently off course. The academic establishment dismissed the BIS warnings, comparing Borio to Hyman Minsky, the maverick whose insights were recognized only belatedly after the subprime crisis. Both men observed the real world rather than academic models, and both understood that finance is not a veil but the dominant force in modern capitalism.

Chapter 10: Unnatural Selection

Arthur Hadley, the late nineteenth-century Yale president, defined interest as the mechanism of natural selection in business: it eliminates the “industrially unfit.” Joseph Schumpeter called this process creative destruction—the essential fact of capitalism. Interest turns time into a cost of production; entrepreneurs who save time emerge as winners. James Grant compared the interest rate to a metronome whose beat keeps musicians playing in time. At zero rates, the economy progresses at the solemn pace of a funeral march.

Sharp downturns, however brutal, drive efficiency. During the Great Depression, automobile sales fell by two-thirds, but the most efficient operators continued investing while weaker competitors failed. Survivors prospered after the recovery. Alexander Field documented that the 1930s propelled productivity improvements across multiple industries. After the “forgotten depression” of 1921, manufacturing productivity jumped 20% in a single year. Clément Juglar captured the paradox: “The riches of nations can be measured by the violence of the crises which they experience.”

“Capitalism without bankruptcy is like Christianity without hell.” —Frank Borman

The Euro crisis demonstrated the same principle in reverse. From the mid-1990s, rates among future Eurozone members converged toward the German benchmark, even though economic conditions varied enormously. Ireland’s rates fell and a housing boom gathered pace. The ECB conducted monetary policy suited for lethargic Germany but far too loose for booming Ireland and Greece. Gita Gopinath found that the shift toward construction depressed productivity: “By lowering interest rates and encouraging inflow of capital, adoption of the euro may have been partly responsible for misallocation of capital and low productivity observed in South of Europe.” One interest rate for seventeen economies was the ultimate refutation of the idea that rates could be centrally planned.

Japan demonstrated what happens when interest rates are used to suppress creative destruction. After the bubble economy collapsed, the Bank of Japan lowered its policy rate from 6% to 0.5% between 1991 and 1995, on its way to zero. Banks chose to “evergreen” non-performing loans rather than recognize losses. Zombie companies—firms unable to cover interest payments from profits—multiplied. Excess capacity spread across entire industries. Productivity growth collapsed. Japan embarked on what became a lost decade, then two, then three.

Europe repeated the pattern after Mario Draghi’s 2012 promise to do “whatever it takes.” By 2016, the OECD found that 10% of European firms qualified as zombies. Italy had the worst infestation— the country was home to two dozen tire producers in an era of overcapacity. European banks harbored more than €1 trillion in bad debts. Fifteen years into the euro project, Italy had enjoyed no increase in income per capita. As in Japan, easy money bought time, but the time was wasted.

The era of ultra-low rates also spawned a herd of unicorns—private companies valued above $1 billion. Elizabeth Holmes and Theranos achieved a $9 billion valuation for blood-testing technology that did not work. Uber raised $20 billion and lost $14 billion before its IPO. WeWork became, as Reuters put it, “a poster child of the longest U.S. economic recovery on record, and an era where money is virtually free.” Post-2008 US productivity growth fell to 0.5% annually—a quarter of its level two decades earlier. New business formation collapsed; by 2016, business deaths outnumbered births for the first time since 1978. Creative destruction had been replaced by unnatural selection and capital destruction.

The productivity puzzle that baffled central bankers was no puzzle at all when viewed through the lens of interest rates. Growing research showed that zombies lay behind the productivity debacle: they discouraged healthy firms from investing, slowed the adoption of new technologies, and trapped resources in low-productivity uses. Claudio Borio turned the conventional narrative upside down, describing an “interest rate– productivity nexus” with causality running from low rates to low investment and falling productivity. The BIS found a close relationship between the share of zombie companies and the decline in policy rates. Low rates begot zombies, zombies begot lower productivity, and lower productivity was used to justify keeping rates low—a self-reinforcing trap that Schumpeter would have recognized as the death of capitalism’s essential evolutionary mechanism.

Chapter 11: The Promoter’s Profit

When interest rates fall, Wall Street profits surge— not from productive investment but from financial engineering. Rudolf Hilferding identified the mechanism in 1910: share prices rise and fall in inverse relationship to the interest rate. When the return on industry exceeds the cost of borrowing, financiers reap the “promoter’s profit”—a windfall extracted by acquiring companies on leverage, “watering” the stock, and floating the amalgamated business at a higher price. The lower the interest rate, the higher the promoter’s profit.

The pattern first appeared in the 1890s, when US Treasury yields fell below 2% and Wall Street consolidated swathes of American industry into trusts. John D. Rockefeller’s Standard Oil, J. P. Morgan’s United States Steel Corporation, and Edward Harriman’s railway empire were all creatures of cheap money. US Treasury bond yields fell below 2% in the decade when General Electric was formed from a Wall Street merger. Federal surpluses had halved the national debt between 1880 and 1893; strong demand from National Banks sent yields on some Treasuries below zero—the first and only time long-term interest rates turned negative prior to the twenty-first century. Thorstein Veblen observed that “the certainty of gain seems rather more assured in large-scale manipulation of vendible capital than in business management.”

Wall Street’s focus on financial engineering over productive investment was not new. Only a small part of Union Pacific Railroad securities had been spent on fixed assets—the rest went “mainly to supply funds for engaging in illegal combinations or stock speculation.” Louis Brandeis inveighed against “financial recklessness,” the “mad pursuit of monopoly,” and the “curse of bigness” in his 1914 polemic Other People’s Money. The trust movement was supported by what Brandeis called “vested interests”—financiers whose profits came from manipulating share prices rather than from building anything. A century later, the pattern had returned with extraordinary fidelity.

“When a nation is running to decay and ruin, the merchant, and monied man, do what you can, will be sure to starve last.” —John Locke, 1691

A hundred years later, the same forces were at work. After 2008, the collapse in corporate borrowing costs enabled another wave of anti-competitive mergers. Global M&A topped $5 trillion in 2015. Kraft Heinz, controlled by 3G Capital and Warren Buffett’s Berkshire, doubled per-share earnings by adding debt and slashing costs. Valeant Pharmaceuticals acquired twenty-plus businesses and accumulated $30 billion in liabilities while borrowing at under 6%. The number of listed US companies halved in two decades. Three-quarters of American industries became significantly more concentrated. University of Michigan economists found that price-fixing cartels were more influenced by the level of interest rates than by any other factor.

The merger wave brought about a rise in corporate monopoly power not seen since the Gilded Age. The number of listed US companies halved in the two decades prior to 2016. CEO Mike Coupe of Sainsbury’s was inadvertently captured on camera singing “We’re in the money” after announcing a merger. The US Justice Department failed to file a single antitrust case in 2014. In 2015, global M&A topped $5 trillion for the first time. The collapse in corporate borrowing costs had made it cheaper for large companies to buy competitors than to compete with them— extracting the promoter’s profit that Hilferding had described more than a century earlier.

Rising concentration produced higher profits for insiders, higher pay for executives, declining workers’ bargaining power, falling investment, and reduced R&D. NBER economists concluded that “extremely low interest rates may lead to slower growth by increasing market concentration.” The promoter’s profit had returned with a vengeance, enriching Wall Street while hollowing out the productive economy.

Chapter 12: A Big Fat Ugly Bubble

The Federal Reserve’s response to the 2008 crisis was to double its balance sheet to over $2 trillion through what staff delicately called “large-scale asset purchases” and the world knew as quantitative easing. The S&P 500 hit 666 on March 6, 2009—the number of the Beast—then began a decade-long ascent that would surpass even the Dotcom peak on most valuation measures. By the tenth anniversary of Lehman, the US bull market had run 3,453 days— the longest uninterrupted streak of stock gains in history.

The Australian housing market provided a particularly vivid illustration. By 2014, Sydney house prices rivaled Hong Kong’s, valued at roughly nine times average incomes, exceeding Dotcom bubble highs. The Reserve Bank of Australia cut rates to their all-time low of 1.5% despite the housing bubble; Governor Glenn Stevens argued that high prices were “understandable” and required no monetary adjustment. The bubble spread nationwide, trapping Australia in what observers called a “perpetual bubble.” Every central banker running the same experiment produced the same result: when the price of time was set below the natural rate, the price of long-duration assets—above all, housing— inflated until they became unaffordable.

When discount rates fall, the valuation of companies whose profits lie in the distant future inflates disproportionately. Three-quarters of 2018 IPOs were loss-making—the same proportion as the Dotcom peak. Tesla’s market cap surpassed General Motors in 2017 and Toyota three years later, despite producing twenty times fewer vehicles. In the cryptocurrency sphere, Bitcoin went from pizza-transaction novelty (10,000 BTC for two pizzas in 2010, worth $200 million by late 2017) to a market cap exceeding Boeing, Toyota, and McDonald’s combined.

The era of ultra-low rates meant that a variety of income-less assets became wildly popular. Vintage cars quadrupled in value between 2005 and 2018, handily outperforming the world’s stock markets. Contemporary art prices climbed tenfold over a single decade. Damien Hirst’s Golden Calf was auctioned by Sotheby’s for £10 million on the very day Lehman failed. Gold, the original zero-coupon perpetual asset, surged. In a world where safe bonds yielded nothing or less, anything that might hold its value across time became a speculative target. Time had no cost, so assets whose value depended on the discount rate floated free of gravitational constraint.

“Low interest rates have messed with the temporal properties of the market and created a wormhole in time… Today’s market valuation assumes it has already happened—that capital has been raised and spent and that it cost nothing.” —Vitaliy Katsenelson, on Tesla

Between 2009 and 2018, US household wealth nearly doubled to more than $100 trillion— five times GDP, compared with a post-war average of 3.5 times. Never before had Americans been so rich, and never before had they done so little to earn it. Frederick Soddy, the Nobel-winning chemist turned economist, had warned that the “wealth” of millionaires “turns out to be virtual and to consist mostly of claims to wealth.” Adam Smith defined real wealth as the annual produce of land and labor; much of the post-2008 wealth was mere financial claims whose market value multiplied as the discount rate declined.

Corporate profits, too, were a mirage. Total profits as a share of GDP ran 40% above the post-war average between 2010 and 2014, with the single largest input being, as Jeremy Grantham noted, the steep decline in borrowing costs. Financial services accounted for over 20% of total profits—roughly double the post-war average. When Janet Yellen was asked in April 2016 whether the US was a bubble economy, she pointed to jobs growth and low inflation. Paul Volcker, seated nearby, pulled out his handkerchief and loudly blew his nose.

An unnamed Bank of Japan official had captured the delusion during Japan’s 1980s bubble: “It’s time for bubble to burst… real, productive economy won’t really be hurt.” He was catastrophically wrong. Donald Trump, on the 2016 campaign trail, accused the Fed of creating a “false economy” and inflating a “big, fat, ugly bubble.” In essence, he was correct—the Fed’s extreme policy had ended the Great Recession at the cost of creating an economy whose apparent prosperity rested on inflated financial claims rather than productive capital.

Chapter 13: Your Mother Needs to Die

Nassau Senior defined interest in 1836 as “the wage of abstinence”— compensation for the pain of consuming less today. Gustav Cassel published a table in 1903 showing that savings double in 12 years at 6%, but take 70 years at 1%. Below that threshold, saving becomes functionally pointless—retirees have no choice but to consume their capital, and if the practice became general, Cassel warned, the effect would be “quite disastrous.”

Frédéric Bastiat had made the complementary point decades earlier: “To save is deliberately to put an interval between the moment when the services are made for the society, and that when the equivalent is received from it.” The reward for this ajournement is interest. When the reward vanishes, so does the motivation. As William Easterly later put it, “Becoming rich is a choice between today’s consumption and tomorrow’s.” Ultra-low rates collapsed the incentive structure that had sustained private saving for centuries, leaving households dependent on asset appreciation— a form of wealth that could evaporate overnight.

The Fed embarked on its easy money policy without considering the impact on savers. At the peak of the housing bubble, the US personal savings rate had fallen to a third of its level a decade earlier. After 2008, the net savings rate turned negative for the first time since the Great Depression. American depositors lost an estimated $400 billion per year in interest income. UK household savings sank to their lowest recorded level. Japanese household savings, once above 10% of income, turned negative in 2014. Germans called negative rates Strafzinsen—punishment rates.

The global pattern was consistent. British household savings sank to their lowest recorded level in 2017. Canadian and Australian household savings fell to record lows. When the Bank of Japan implemented its zero interest rate policy in 1999, Japanese workers were stashing away more than a tenth of their incomes. Over the following years, savings fell remorselessly, turning negative in 2014. A commentator observed: “In a world of ultra-low rates, most households have no hope of wealth accumulation, no matter how much they save. Indeed, they are better off being profligate.” The perverse incentive was universal: thrift was being punished, and recklessness rewarded, in every country that adopted zero or near-zero rates.

“The zero interest-rate policy broke the social contract for generations of hardworking Americans who saved for retirement, only to find their savings are not nearly enough.” —Michael Burry

The pension crisis was equally devastating. After the financial crisis, the decline in long-term rates made pension commitments far more expensive. By 2016, combined US public-sector pension deficits reached $3 trillion; corporate pensions were in the hole for $425 billion. James Grant called it a “bull market in liabilities.” Cities cut services and sacked workers. Several municipalities declared bankruptcy—Detroit, San Bernardino. Chicago’s credit was cut to junk. The shift from defined benefit to defined contribution pensions meant workers now bore the full risk of inadequate retirement income.

The median pension savings for Americans nearing retirement was just $111,000— enough for an annuity paying well under $10,000 a year. The average male retiree had savings for not quite ten years, leaving him eight years short. Women faced a gap of eleven years. Unfunded pension commitments for OECD countries were estimated at twice their reported national debts. Low rates begot a pensions crisis, and the pensions crisis— as managers hedged by buying long-dated bonds— begot even lower rates.

The shift from defined benefit to defined contribution pensions meant that workers now bore the full risk of inadequate retirement income. These DC schemes generally received lower employer contributions and delivered inferior investment returns. A 2019 World Economic Forum report found that American male retirees had savings for not quite ten years, leaving an eight-year “retirement gap”. For American women, the gap stretched to eleven years. At least Americans could be thankful they were not retiring in Japan, where the gap was fifteen years for men and twenty for women. The zero-rate policy had broken the social contract that generations of savers had relied upon: work hard, save steadily, and retire with dignity.

Chapter 14: Let Them Eat Credit

The link between falling interest rates and rising inequality runs through every era of easy money. In the 1920s, the top 10% of Americans grabbed more than half of total income, driven by capital gains from the booming stock market. The turning point for the modern era came in the early 1980s as falling rates inaugurated a decades-long bull market in stocks and bonds, minting fortunes for the leveraged buyout industry. By 2008, the top decile’s income share had returned to its 1929 peak, and the top 0.1% claimed nearly a fifth of total household assets.

The financial sector’s expansion was itself a driver of inequality. Finance employed fewer workers relative to its share of national income, so its growth disproportionately enriched a small elite. Between 1997 and 2007, fund manager revenues rose from less than 1% to 2.5% of GDP. By the latter date, top hedge fund managers were earning more than $1 billion per year. Stanford historian Walter Scheidel found that “financialization” amplified inequality not just in finance but in adjacent sectors like law and medicine. On the eve of the crisis, finance workers enjoyed a 50% income premium adjusted for education. More than a quarter of Harvard graduates applied for Wall Street jobs. The entire elite talent pool was being redirected from productive enterprise into financial engineering.

Unconventional monetary policies after 2008 arrested Wall Street’s collapse but prevented another Great Compression. Bondholders were bailed out. Financial insiders bought distressed securities at bargain prices with subsidized Fed loans. By 2013, the wealth share of the top percentile reached 37%, up from 22% in 1979. CEO-to-worker pay ratios ballooned to nearly 1:300. The leading 25 hedge fund managers earned four times the total income of all S&P 500 CEOs combined.

“I have a message for my fellow filthy rich, for all of us who live in our gated bubble worlds: Wake up, people. It won’t last.” —Nick Hanauer, “The Pitchforks Are Coming”

The suffering at the bottom was acute. Middle-class households lost 44% of their wealth on average after Lehman. Median household wealth fell to its 1969 level. The net worth of African American households collapsed to $1,700. Low-wage jobs were created at twice the rate of high-wage jobs. Over three-quarters of Americans lived paycheck to paycheck. Student debt exceeded $1.5 trillion, with nearly a quarter in default. The average age of US homebuyers reached 46— the oldest on record—as prices climbed faster than incomes and developers focused on luxury properties.

The Fed’s inequality blindspot was structural. Central bank models assumed a single “representative agent” who earns the same income and holds the same wealth as everyone else. Bernanke denied that Fed policies made the rich richer. Yellen insisted the Fed was trying to help Main Street. But banks with busted credit got bailed out while homeowners got foreclosed. Wall Street received zero-cost loans while millions paid triple-digit APRs to payday lenders. As Bastiat had predicted 165 years earlier, free credit was a calamity for the working classes.

The demographic consequences were devastating. High student debt, weak income growth, and elevated house prices discouraged young couples from starting families. Britain’s birth rate moved inversely with housing costs. Spain’s population dipped in 2012 for the first time on record. Life expectancy in the United States actually declined in 2015. Case and Deaton pointed to “Deaths of Despair” among white working-class Americans—tens of thousands killing themselves with prescription painkillers, alcohol, and suicide. The economics profession, trained to think in terms of aggregate GDP and representative agents, had no framework for measuring the human cost of monetary policy that enriched the top while eroding the foundations of ordinary life.

Conspicuous consumption reached grotesque levels. Leonardo da Vinci’s Salvator Mundi sold for $450 million. Ken Griffin paid $238 million for a Manhattan penthouse. The number of dollar billionaires increased tenfold between 1987 and 2013. By 2015, an estimated 62 people claimed half the world’s wealth—down from 388 just five years earlier. Wealth was not trickling down; it was, as Oxfam put it, being “sucked upwards.”

Chapter 15: The Price of Anxiety

The Neapolitan abbé Ferdinando Galiani, whom Nietzsche called the most profound man of his century, justified interest as the “price of anxiety”— compensation to lenders for bearing the risk that their money might not return. “Keeping someone in anxiety is pain,” Galiani wrote, “hence it must be paid for.” When that price is set too low, moral hazard runs wild. On the eve of the financial crisis, insurance giant Marsh ran a campaign with the slogan “I risk therefore I am.”

Walter Bagehot understood that investors behave rashly when rates fall below a certain threshold. Modern research confirms it: risk-taking becomes extreme when bond yields fall below 3%. Investors have a “deeply ingrained notion that wealth should grow at a decent rate”—when safe assets cannot deliver, they reach for yield with increasing desperation.

The Fed was not a passive bystander in this process. Quantitative easing was explicitly designed to encourage risk-taking through the portfolio balance channel: by buying Treasury bonds and mortgage securities, the Fed forced investors further out on the risk spectrum. “The Fed became possibly the biggest carry trader of all,” earning nearly $100 billion in profit in 2015 on its securities portfolio, financed by printing dollars. Kevin Warsh, a former FOMC member, acknowledged the mechanism plainly: QE “works because we take volatility out of financial markets.” When the central bank systematically removes volatility, investors interpret it as a signal that risk has diminished—when in fact it has merely been deferred.

After 2008, the yield-chasing took myriad forms. Carry trades—borrowing cheaply to buy higher-yielding assets—proliferated in every corner of finance: hedge funds leveraging mortgage-backed securities, landlords stacking rental properties, corporations issuing bonds to fund share buybacks. Returns were asymmetric: steady small gains with exposure to sudden catastrophic losses. The mantra was “picking up nickels in front of steamrollers.”

“Never have investors reached so high in price for so low a return.” —Bill Gross, PIMCO

The leveraged loan market exploded to $1 trillion by 2018, with covenant-lite issues dominating—meaning no traditional creditor protections. Junk bond yields fell to historic lows. US companies issued record investment-grade bonds at unprecedented low yields, but the money went not to productive investment but to share buybacks in record quantities. BBB-rated debt proliferated while AAA bonds became a rarity—Gresham’s Law operating in credit markets. Subprime auto loans passed $1 trillion. By summer 2016, $12 trillion of bonds worldwide carried negative yields.

The VIX—Wall Street’s fear gauge—collapsed to historic lows as the Fed systematically removed volatility from markets. Traders sold VIX futures for steady income, creating what one observer called the “carry trade of carry trades.” Credit Suisse’s XIV fund climbed elevenfold between 2010 and early 2018. On February 5, 2018— “Volmageddon”—the VIX doubled in a single day (the largest move on record), and the XIV lost 97% of its value. US stocks shed roughly $1 trillion in market cap for no discernible reason on Main Street. The wealth that vanished was virtual, but the message was clear: low rates begot more risk, and financial fragility required that rates remain low. There was no way out.

Andrew Haldane of the Bank of England theorized that financial regulation did not reduce systemic risk but merely influenced where it appeared. “Risk, like energy, tends to be conserved not dissipated, to change its composition but not its quantum.” But Haldane understated the problem. Under ultra-low rates, the quantum of risk does not remain constant— it increases, because investors are incentivized to take on more risk than they otherwise would. As Hyman Minsky had observed, financial stability is destabilizing. When insurance premiums are set too low, people build houses on flood plains. Activist central banks, setting Galiani’s price of insurance at nothing, had produced precisely this effect across the entirety of global finance.

Jeremy Stein of Harvard, during a single term on the Fed’s board, delivered a landmark speech in February 2013 suggesting that changes in the monetary environment altered risk-taking behavior in ways regulation could not address. “A prolonged period of low interest rates,” Stein warned, “can create incentives for agents to take on greater duration or credit risks, or to employ additional leverage, in an effort to ‘reach for yield.’” Regulation was “all very well,” but only monetary policy “gets into all the cracks.” It was the most important dissent from within the Fed since William White’s warnings at the BIS—and, like White’s, it fell on deaf ears.

Chapter 16: Rusting Money

Silvio Gesell, the eccentric German-Argentine businessman, proposed in the early twentieth century that money should carry a tax for being held idle—a “demurrage” charge that would effectively impose negative interest rates. Money, Gesell argued, should rust like iron or rot like potatoes, losing value over time to incentivize spending. Keynes praised the idea in his General Theory, calling Gesell an “unduly neglected prophet.”

The rationale for negative rates drew on Keynes’s liquidity trap. Once short-term rates hit zero, the central bank supposedly lost its ability to stimulate—cash became a zero-yielding asset that savers could hoard indefinitely. Gesell’s rusting money solved the problem by making hoarding expensive. After 2008, prominent economists endorsed the concept. Some proposed abolishing large-denomination banknotes to prevent savers from escaping negative deposit rates into physical cash. Others suggested a variable exchange rate between digital and paper money. The common thread was that enforcing negative rates required penalizing or eliminating paper currency—an expansion of state power over private finance that echoed the worst fears of Bastiat and Hayek about the road from monetary activism to economic serfdom.

Nearly a century later, central banks made Gesell’s thought experiment reality. The European Central Bank pushed deposit rates below zero in 2014. The Bank of Japan followed in 2016. The Swiss National Bank went deepest, at −0.75%. By mid-2019, over $17 trillion in bonds worldwide carried negative yields—meaning investors were paying governments and corporations for the privilege of lending them money. A Tokyo insurance executive captured the derangement: “Yields don’t matter.”

The inversions became surreal. In 2015, Ireland and Belgium issued bonds with hundred-year maturities—investors locking in returns for a century based on the assumption that rates would stay low forever. Petrobras of Brazil sold bonds promising redemption in the twenty-second century and found strong demand. An Italian fifty-year bond yielding less than 3% was heavily oversubscribed. The normal logic of bond markets inverted entirely: investors were told to buy negative-yielding bonds for capital gains and equities for income. As Goldman Sachs noted, the aggregate duration of corporate bonds worldwide doubled between 2006 and 2016. Bloomberg estimated that a mere half-percentage-point rate increase would generate $1.6 trillion of losses in fixed-income markets. The bond market had become a bubble in every sense Bagehot would have recognized.

“Central banks are printing rules almost as fast as they’re printing money.” —James Grant

The transmission mechanism broke down almost immediately. Banks could not pass negative rates through to retail depositors without triggering bank runs—no saver would voluntarily pay a bank to hold their money when physical cash offered a zero return. This meant negative rates compressed bank net interest margins, squeezing the very profitability banks needed to rebuild their capital after the crisis. European bank stocks underperformed dramatically. Japanese banks fared no better: the Bank of Japan’s negative rate announcement in January 2016 sent bank shares plunging. Rather than encouraging lending, negative rates were weakening the institutions through which lending occurred. The policy was corroding its own transmission channel.

Negative rates punished savers directly while producing none of the growth their proponents promised. Banks struggled to pass negative rates through to depositors for fear of bank runs. Profitability suffered. Life insurers found themselves contractually obligated to pay out more than their investments could yield—the IMF warned that the failure of a single German life assurer “could engulf the financial system.” In December 2015, Martin Senn, CEO of Zurich Insurance, stepped down after profit warnings; six months later he committed suicide. Negative rates were not free money—they extracted a terrible price from the real economy, from savers, and from the institutions that served them.

Germans led the backlash. They called negative rates Strafzinsen— punishment rates. Finance Minister Wolfgang Schäuble complained in April 2016 that negative rates were causing “extraordinary problems” for pensioners and savers. ECB board member Yves Mersch warned that persistently low rates were forcing savers to put more money aside—precisely the opposite of the intended effect. The paradox was structural: if the purpose of negative rates was to discourage saving and encourage spending, the reality was that savers responded by hoarding more, not less, because the lower the return, the more they needed to accumulate for retirement. The policy was not just failing—it was actively counterproductive.

The deeper absurdity was that negative rates delivered none of the growth their proponents promised. European GDP growth remained anemic throughout the negative-rate era. Investment did not pick up. Credit growth to the real economy stayed sluggish even as financial speculation flourished. Claudio Borio at the BIS observed that monetary policy and “macroprudential” regulation were pulling in opposite directions—central banks urged risk-taking while regulators tried to restrain it. The experiment proved what the classical economists had always understood: interest is not a policy variable to be twisted to zero or below. It is, as Galiani had written in 1751, the price of anxiety—and when that price is set at nothing, anxiety does not disappear. It metastasizes.

Part Three: The Game of Marbles

Chapter 17: The Mother and Father of All Evil

Low US interest rates do not stay contained within American borders. After 2002, rock-bottom rates spurred massive international carry trades—investors borrowing in cheap dollars to invest in higher-yielding emerging market assets. Gross capital flows, the BIS argued, were a better indicator of financial fragility than current account balances, and by this measure the world was dangerously exposed. Emerging markets accumulated trillions in foreign-denominated debt, all of it sensitive to the moment the Fed began tightening.

The pattern was ancient. Every era of cheap American money had produced a corresponding emerging-market crisis when rates reversed. In the 1890s, the Barings Crisis was triggered by capital flooding into Argentina during a period of low British yields, then draining violently when confidence broke. In the late 1990s, the Asian Financial Crisis followed a Fed tightening that reversed the capital flows of the early decade. Each cycle of easy Western money sucked capital into developing economies during the good times—financing infrastructure, real estate, and consumption— then ripped it out during the bad, leaving devastation behind. The post-2008 cycle was the largest yet, because the rates were the lowest and the money creation the most aggressive in history.

That moment arrived in summer 2013, when Bernanke merely signaled an intention to slow bond purchases. The “taper tantrum” sent currencies and bonds across the developing world into freefall. Turkey, South Africa, Indonesia, India, and Brazil all suffered capital flight. The pattern was ancient—the same mechanism that had produced the Argentine crisis after the Barings collapse, and the Asian crisis after the Fed tightened in the late 1990s. Each cycle of easy American money sucked capital into emerging markets during the good times and ripped it out during the bad.

The BIS had been warning about this for years. Gross capital flows, Borio’s team argued, were a far better indicator of financial fragility than current account balances—and by this measure the world was dangerously interconnected. Dollar-denominated debt outside the United States had swelled to over $10 trillion, creating a vast network of borrowers whose solvency depended on the Fed keeping rates near zero. When Bernanke merely signaled an intention to slow bond purchases, the dollar surged, and every borrower in the carry-trade chain faced a simultaneous tightening of financial conditions they had no power to control. The Fed set monetary policy for America, but the dollar’s reserve status meant it was effectively setting monetary conditions for the entire world.

“We lend to countries whose condition we do not know, and whose want of civilisation we do not consider, and, therefore, we lose our money.” —Walter Bagehot

Raghuram Rajan, then governor of the Reserve Bank of India, argued that the Fed’s policies constituted a form of monetary imperialism—exporting instability to countries that had no vote on the Federal Open Market Committee. The embers of Lehman were still warm when the global carry trade rekindled, this time even larger. The BIS warned repeatedly that the longer US rates stayed near zero, the greater the buildup of global financial imbalances. By the time the Fed finally began hiking in late 2015, the damage was already baked in.

Turkey illustrated the pattern most vividly. Cheap dollar borrowing had financed a construction boom of extraordinary scale— President Erdoğan built airports, bridges, and housing projects funded largely with foreign-currency debt. When the dollar strengthened and the lira collapsed, the debt burden ballooned in local-currency terms, pushing the economy toward crisis. South Africa, Indonesia, India, and Brazil all suffered variations of the same script. The “fragile five” emerging markets became a standard watchlist for currency strategists tracking the spillover from Fed policy.

Borio feared the end state would be an “epoch-defining seismic rupture” heralding the reversal of globalization. The longer US rates stayed near zero, the greater the buildup of global imbalances— and the more catastrophic the eventual unwinding. Trade protectionism would follow financial protectionism, as countries tried to insulate themselves from capital flow volatility they could not control. The irony was devastating: a monetary policy designed to promote recovery in one country was systematically destabilizing the rest of the world, creating the conditions for the very stagnation and fragmentation it was supposed to prevent.

Chapter 18: Financial Repression with Chinese Characteristics

China’s entire development model rested on financial repression—the systematic suppression of interest rates below their natural level to subsidize investment at the expense of household savers. Deposit rates were held below inflation for decades, effectively taxing Chinese families to fund the construction boom. The result was the most spectacular infrastructure buildout in human history, but also the most spectacular misallocation of capital.

The mechanism was straightforward. Chinese households deposited their savings in state-controlled banks at rates capped below inflation—effectively paying a tax on their thrift. The banks lent these subsidized funds to state-owned enterprises and politically connected developers at rates below the natural cost of capital. The transfer from savers to borrowers amounted to as much as 5% of GDP annually—an invisible tax that financed high-speed rail, ghost cities, and the world’s most overbuilt real estate market. The system resembled post-war Western financial repression, when governments held rates below inflation to erode wartime debts, except that China was doing it on a vastly larger scale and for decades longer.

The distortions were enormous. Capital flowed not to the most productive uses but to the most politically favored ones. Provincial governments borrowed through local government financing vehicles to fund prestige projects with negative economic returns. Steel mills and aluminum smelters ran at massive overcapacity, depressing global commodity prices while generating no profit. A parallel shadow banking system emerged as households sought returns above the capped deposit rates, channeling trillions into opaque wealth management products and trust loans that promised higher yields but embedded credit risk that nobody could measure. The shadow system grew to rival the formal banking sector, adding yet another layer of fragility.

By the late 2010s, China’s property sector accounted for nearly a third of economic activity. Housing stock was valued at almost four times GDP. President Xi Jinping introduced “three red lines” in late 2020 to rein in developer leverage, and by the following summer Evergrande—the largest developer, with debts approaching $300 billion—was on the verge of failure. Housing sales collapsed nationwide. The bubble that was supposed to never pop had popped.

The demographic dimension made China’s predicament uniquely dangerous. Decades of the one-child policy had produced a rapidly aging population, and the working-age population was already contracting. Unlike Japan, which was rich when its bubble burst, China faced the prospect of growing old before growing rich. Household savings that had been taxed through financial repression for decades would now be needed for retirement—but those savings were trapped in a deflating real estate market and an opaque financial system riddled with bad debts. The social contract that had sustained authoritarian rule—accept political repression in exchange for rising prosperity— was under its greatest strain since Tiananmen Square.

“What’s being repressed today is the standard of living.” —James Grant

The Chinese model demonstrated at continental scale what the book documents in every era: financial repression transfers wealth from savers to borrowers, from households to the state, from the productive economy to speculative excess. When the reckoning comes, it is ordinary citizens who bear the cost.

China’s experience also vindicated Borio’s insight that interest rates are a policy variable, not a natural phenomenon. Beijing did not discover low rates; it imposed them as a development strategy. The consequences followed the same script that Chancellor traces through every era: misallocation of capital, zombie enterprises, declining productivity growth, rising inequality between connected insiders and ordinary savers, and ultimately a debt burden so large that normalization became impossible without crashing the system. China was not a special case—it was the general case writ large, demonstrating at continental scale what happens when the price of time is set by political fiat rather than by the market for savings.

Conclusion: The New Road to Serfdom

Mervyn King, the former Bank of England governor, acknowledged the “paradox of policy”: rates after the financial crisis were simultaneously too high for rapid demand recovery and too low to maintain a proper balance between spending and saving. “Our market economy today,” King admitted, “is not providing an effective link between the present and the future.” Since interest is the link between present and future, and rates were at the lowest level in five millennia, that link was effectively broken.

Dennis Robertson, Keynes’s Cambridge contemporary and a devotee of Lewis Carroll, thought Keynes’s interest theory as insubstantial as the Cheshire Cat’s grin. Unlike Keynes, Robertson believed in the necessity of interest as a market price that balanced the supply of savings against the demand for loans. The “true rate” of interest, as Hjalmar Schacht had called it, reflected the genuine supply and demand for actual savings—not the quantity of money central banks could conjure from nothing. But by 2020 the gap between the true rate and the policy rate had become an abyss, and the entire global financial system was structured around the assumption that the abyss would persist.

The title echoes Friedrich Hayek’s The Road to Serfdom. As governments ran ever-larger deficits financed by central bank money creation, and as regulation proliferated in response to each crisis that easy money had caused, the state expanded relentlessly. Dodd-Frank ran to tens of thousands of pages. Basel III grew from Basel I’s 30 pages to 616. Financial regulators in the UK went from one per 11,000 City workers in the late 1970s to a ratio of 1:300 after 2008. The old freedoms of market-determined interest, creative destruction, and capital allocation by price signal were disappearing under layers of monetary intervention and regulatory response—each layer a consequence of the distortions created by the layer before.

The expansion was not merely regulatory but fiscal. As governments ran ever-larger deficits financed by central bank money creation, the state’s share of the economy swelled. Modern Monetary Theory provided intellectual cover: if governments could never go bust in their own currency, there was no need to raise taxes or restrain spending. The US federal deficit reached $4 trillion in 2020, matched almost dollar-for-dollar by the Fed’s money creation. The boundary between fiscal and monetary policy dissolved. Private capital allocation by price signal—the mechanism that had driven Western prosperity for centuries—was being supplanted by state-directed credit, administrative pricing, and central bank balance sheet management. The road to serfdom was being paved with good monetary intentions.

“Our market economy today is not providing an effective link between the present and the future.” —Mervyn King

The solution, in principle, was straightforward: restore interest rates to levels that reflect the genuine supply and demand for savings. But by this point, the debt edifice had grown so large that any serious rate increase threatened to collapse it. Low rates begot debt, and debt begot lower rates—a trap from which no policymaker could see an exit.

Chancellor suggests one possible escape: Hayek’s vision of competing private currencies, now potentially realizable through cryptocurrency. A central bank digital currency whose issuance was limited to no more than the economy’s trend growth rate would function as digital gold— providing the discipline of the classical Gold Standard without its deflationary rigidity. Central bankers would lose the ability to pursue active monetary policy. The cost of borrowing would more accurately reflect the supply and demand for actual savings. Guided by the market’s invisible hand, the rate of interest would find its natural level. The prospect seems utopian, but then so did the abolition of interest seem utopian in 1849— and Proudhon’s scheme was actually implemented.

The book’s final verdict is bleak but honest. The post-2008 experiment with ultra-low rates was not merely a policy error but a civilizational gamble—the most extreme manipulation of the price of time in five thousand years of recorded history. Every era of artificially cheap money has ended badly, and there is no reason to believe this era will be different. The only question is whether the reckoning comes through the honest restoration of market-determined interest rates, with its painful but recoverable short-term consequences, or through the continued financial repression that Hayek warned would end in serfdom. The price of time, suppressed for so long, will eventually be paid.

Postscript: The World Turned Upside Down

In late 2019, Lord King warned that global debt relative to GDP was much higher than in 2008, and that the world was sleepwalking toward another crisis. Three months later, on March 12, 2020, the world’s financial markets suffered “the fastest and most violent crash in history.” The Dow lost 10%. Italy’s MIB fell nearly 18%. Treasury yields rose during the panic as leveraged players scrambled for cash—the opposite of what should happen in a flight to safety. Bond dealers gazed at blank screens showing no trades in the world’s deepest market.

Central banks responded with an operation that dwarfed everything before it. The Fed slashed rates to zero, committed to buying $700 billion in bonds, and for the first time extended purchases to funds containing junk bonds. The balance sheet doubled from $4 trillion to $8 trillion. Central banks collectively created more money than in all the years since Lehman’s bankruptcy. The US federal deficit hit $4 trillion in 2020, matched almost dollar-for-dollar by the Fed’s money creation. Modern Monetary Theory had gone from fringe academic idea to de facto government policy.

“The most dramatic thing that almost ever happened in the entire world history of finance.” —Charlie Munger

The Everything Bubble followed. US stocks hit unprecedented valuations. House prices rose faster than during the 2006 bubble. Nearly $18 trillion in bonds carried negative yields. Bitcoin climbed nearly tenfold in fifteen months. Dogecoin— originally a joke—rose 150-fold. A digital collage (NFT) sold for $69 million. An Italian conceptual artist auctioned an “immaterial sculpture” consisting of nothing. Cash from American Rescue cheques flowed into Robinhood accounts, and neophyte speculators on WallStreetBets drove GameStop from $20 to over $480 in weeks. Dave Portnoy picked stocks by pulling Scrabble letters from a bag, then tweeted: “The whole thing is a pyramid scheme. We’re living in the Matrix.”

Inflation returned. Supply chains snapped during lockdowns. Money supply soared. US consumer prices exceeded 5% annualized by early 2021. The Fed insisted it was transitory. Jerome Powell declared: “We’re not thinking about raising rates, we’re not even thinking about thinking about raising rates.” Andrew Haldane, stepping down as the Bank of England’s chief economist, warned that too much punch had been poured and tomorrow’s hangover would be terrible. Historical precedent supported him: previous bond bear markets at the turn of the nineteenth century and after 1945 had both been triggered by reversals of artificially suppressed rates. Not one of 69 financiers surveyed in 1899 had anticipated the turn. As always, the experts were the last to see it coming.

Meanwhile China’s property bubble, the largest in history, was finally popping. By late 2020, nearly a third of Chinese economic activity was exposed to property development. The nation’s housing stock was valued at nearly four times GDP. President Xi decided to grasp the nettle, introducing the “three red lines” to rein in developer leverage. By the following summer, Evergrande—the country’s largest developer, with debts approaching $300 billion—teetered on the brink of failure. Housing sales collapsed across the country. The “bubble that never pops” had popped, vindicating every warning Chancellor had catalogued about the inevitable endpoint of financial repression.

The pandemic laid bare an uncomfortable truth that the novelist Michel Houellebecq captured with characteristic bleakness: the “banal virus” would change nothing fundamental— “we will not wake up after lockdown in a new world; it will be the same, just a bit worse.” And worse it was. Big business thrived while small businesses shuttered permanently. Big Tech’s monopoly profits fattened on enforced screen time. The super-rich grew richer still, taking super-yachts to their pandemic retreats and booking rocket trips to space. Housing became less affordable. Trump asked “Who cares about the budget?” as governments ran their largest peacetime deficits. The state advanced by leaps and bounds, and the old continued to benefit at the expense of the young—every pathology Chancellor had traced to ultra-low rates was accelerated, not corrected, by the crisis.

Seth Klarman, the value investor who had spent a career warning about mispriced risk, captured the cumulative distortion in a single devastating observation: “The idea of persistent low rates has wormed its way into everything: investor thinking, market forecasts, inflation expectations, valuation models, leverage ratios, debt ratings, affordability metrics, housing prices, and corporate behavior.” By truncating downside volatility, forestalling business failures, and postponing the day of reckoning, central banks had persuaded investors that risk had gone into hibernation or simply vanished. Warren Buffett put it more simply: “Interest rates are to the value of assets what gravity is to matter.” Remove gravity, and Dogecoins, NFTs, meme stocks, and immaterial sculptures are free to float into the stratosphere. The question Chancellor leaves with the reader is what happens when gravity returns.

Lewis Carroll might have imagined a financial Wonderland where bonds subtract interest, unicorns graze on lush monetary pastures, companies are worth more bankrupt than as going concerns, and central bankers create wealth by printing money. Carroll did in fact imagine much of this in Sylvie and Bruno, where the Outland Emperor doubles the value of every coin and banknote to make his subjects rich. “You never saw such universal joy,” Carroll wrote. “Shops are full morning to night. Everybody’s buying everything!” When the price of time is set at nothing, finance becomes absurd—and the joke lands differently depending on whether you are a WallStreetBets autist or a retiree watching your pension evaporate.

Key Takeaways

1. Interest is the price of time, not a policy lever

Interest predates coined money by two millennia, arising from the biological productivity of seeds and livestock. It exists because resources are scarce, production takes time, lending is risky, and humans prefer the present to the future. To suppress it is to distort the most fundamental signal in capitalism.

2. Every era of artificially low rates produces a bubble

The Mississippi Scheme (1720), Railway Mania (1840s), the Gilded Age trusts (1890s), the Roaring Twenties, the Dotcom bubble, the housing bubble, the Everything Bubble—the pattern repeats over five millennia with mechanical consistency.

3. Low rates begot lower rates

The central feedback loop: cheap money encourages debt accumulation, and accumulated debt requires ever-cheaper money to remain serviceable. Each crisis prompts easier policy, which creates the conditions for the next crisis at an even lower starting point.

4. Ultra-low rates killed productivity

Zombie companies, discouraged investment, collapsed business formation, reduced job churn. US productivity growth fell to 0.5%—a quarter of its previous rate. By 2016, business deaths outnumbered births for the first time since 1978.

5. The poor paid for free money

Savers lost $400 billion annually. Pensions went into deficit by trillions. Housing became unaffordable. Student debt exploded. Inequality returned to 1929 levels. Bastiat’s 1849 prediction was exact: free credit benefits the wealthy and devastates the working classes.

6. Stable prices do not mean stable finance

Both the 1929 crash and the 2008 crisis occurred during periods of low, stable consumer inflation. The Austrian insight that productivity-driven deflation is healthy, and that fighting it with easy money creates dangerous asset bubbles, remains excluded from mainstream central banking.

7. Financial regulation cannot substitute for proper interest rates

Regulation expanded massively after 2008 (Basel III grew from 30 to 616 pages) but merely pushed risk into shadow banks, CLOs, and volatility strategies. Risk is conserved, not dissipated—and ultra-low rates actively increase the quantum of risk.

Contrarian Ideas

Deflation can be healthy

The mainstream view treats all deflation as dangerous. The BIS found that across 39 countries and 150 years, the link between deflation and economic weakness is “episodic and weak.” The 1930s are the exception. Productivity-driven deflation in the 1920s was healthy; fighting it with easy money created the conditions for the crash.

Low rates cause secular stagnation, not vice versa

Larry Summers himself eventually admitted this: ultra-low rates might cause the very stagnation they were supposed to cure, by breeding zombification, reducing economic dynamism, and discouraging saving and investment.

Piketty’s law is backwards

Piketty’s r > g (return on capital exceeds growth) is less predictive than its inverse: inequality rises when the interest rate is held below the economy’s growth rate (r < g), because this inflates asset bubbles that disproportionately benefit the wealthy.

The Fed’s bubble denial was deliberate

Donald Kohn admitted in 2004 that asset price distortion was “deliberate and desirable.” The Bernanke doctrine that bubbles cannot be identified in real time was not an honest intellectual conclusion but a convenient justification for policies that benefited financial insiders.

Proposals & Frameworks

The Vicious Cycle of Easy Money

Each crisis prompts rate cuts → cheap money inflates assets and encourages debt → financial fragility builds → next crisis requires even lower rates → repeat. The debt-service ratio stays constant as rates fall and debt rises, until the system breaks. This is not a theory—it is the observed pattern across every era covered in the book.

The Cantillon Transmission Mechanism

Newly created money enters the financial system first, inflating asset prices before it reaches the real economy and consumer prices. This explains why consumer inflation can remain subdued for years while enormous asset bubbles build—and why central bankers using consumer prices as their sole guide will always be blindsided.
