## Chapter 13: Your Mother Needs to Die

> "Wipe out rentiers with cheap money." — Martin Wolf, 2014

### Interest as "The Wage of Abstinence"
- Interest affects valuation/allocation of capital plus how much saving takes place
- Saving requires consuming less today — exercise of thrift not easy
- **Nassau Senior** (Oxford, 1836): "To abstain from the enjoyment which is in our power, or to seek distant rather than immediate results, are among the most painful exertions of the human will"

> "Interest is *the wage of abstinence*" — Nassau Senior

### Socialist Mockery
- **Ferdinand Lassalle** (German socialist leader) mocked: "Interest is the 'wage of renunciation!' Your European millionaires are ascetics. Like Indian penitents they stand, like stylites on one foot atop a pillar"
- **John Stuart Mill** pointed out: while rich had enormous resources, ordinary people also saved part of incomes
- Interest reflected the *marginal* pain of their abstinence

### Interest as Time Preference and Intertemporal Bridge
- **Frédéric Bastiat**: "To save is deliberately to put an interval between the moment when the services are made for the society, and that when the equivalent is received from it"
- The reward for this *ajournement* is interest

> "Interest is, as it were, human impatience crystallized into a market rate." — Irving Fisher

- Economists talk about **time preference** — how people value present vs. future goods differently
- **Finance is the intertemporal bridge** joining present and future
- By borrowing/lending and saving/investing, we shift expenditures across time
- Interest = toll on borrowers for bringing forward consumption, fee to savers for moving consumption into future

### Market Rate vs. Society's "Crystallized Impatience"
- Balance upset when market rate of interest falls below society's "crystallized impatience"
- When market rate below public's time preference: people borrow to consume
- Abnormally low rate boosts current spending, but benefits don't last

> "Becoming rich is a choice between today's consumption and tomorrow's. If I cut my consumption sharply and save a large proportion of my wage income, then in a few years I will be richer." — **William Easterly**

### Gustav Cassel on Compound Interest
- **Benjamin Franklin**: "Money is of a prolific generating Nature. Money can beget Money, and its Offspring can beget more"
- **Gustav Cassel** (*The Nature and Necessity of Interest*, 1903): published table showing years required for savings to double at different interest rates:

| Rate | 6% | 3% | 2% | 1.5% | 1% |
|------|----|----|----|----- |----|
| Years to double | 12 | 24 | 35 | 47 | 70 |

- At 6%, savings double every 12 years; at 1%, takes 70 years
- Cassel believed if rate fell below 1%, saving would no longer be viable
- Faced with such low returns, retirees could only maintain spending by consuming capital

> "If such a practice became general, it would have quite a disastrous effect." — Gustav Cassel

## The Wealth Illusion

### Americans vs. Germans: Homeownership
- If Americans complained less than Germans: more likely to own home and have direct stock market exposure
- S&P 500 soared, housing recovered, household wealth climbed to all-time peak
- This great inflation of paper wealth insured most Americans to loss of deposit income

### Soaring Asset Prices Don't Make Nation Richer
- Only produce illusion of wealth
- Investors enjoy capital gains when asset prices rise, but immediate gains offset by lower investment returns going forward
- When long-term rates decline, investors experience windfall gain as bond prices increase
- But bondholders as class suffer — must reinvest coupons at lower yields
- Over long run, equity returns inversely correlated with market's valuation
- Ten years after financial crisis: expected return on balanced portfolio around half its historic average

### Households Must Save More for Retirement
- American households would have to save more to enjoy same level of retirement income
- Commitments to pay cash to retirees = liability that increases when interest rates decline
- Steep decline in rates after 2008 produced what **James Grant** called **"bull market in liabilities"**

### Peter Fisher on Wealth Illusion

> "Because our financial assets are traded in markets and we receive statements, we promptly saw the change [increase] in the value of our assets. We are much slower to appreciate the change in the present value of our liabilities, particularly the value of our future consumption expenditures." — **Peter Fisher** (former Fed and Treasury official)

### Mervyn King's "Paradox of Policy"
- **Mervyn King**: acknowledged low interest rates discourage saving, household consumption would grow more slowly in future
- Lord King referred to **"paradox of policy"**: after financial crisis, interest rates "too high to permit rapid growth of demand in the short run, but too low to be consistent with a proper balance between spending and saving in the long run"

> "Our market economy today is not providing an effective link between the present and the future." — Mervyn King

### Market Economy Not to Blame
- **Interest connects present and future** — with rates at lowest level in history, that link effectively broken
- Record levels of paper wealth encouraged households to carry on spending
- But illusion of wealth inherently fragile
- Any attempt to normalize rates would threaten wealth bubble
- In short run: ultra-low rates boosted consumption by substituting bubble wealth for savings
- **In long run: disaster**
- Savings needed for accumulation of capital; societies that don't invest enough doomed to stagnate
- More than century earlier, **Cassel** anticipated rate of 1% or less would create "a revolution in… the total supply of waiting [savings]"
- Even **Keynes** thought it unwise to reduce rate below 3%: "It was 'socially desirable' that savers should receive a positive return"

## Pension Woes

### Falling Rates Create Headaches
- Falling interest rates discourage savings, reduce expected return on savings
- Annuity purchased in 2016 by sixty-five-year-old American paid quarter less income than decade earlier

### Pension Deficits Ballooned

- After financial crisis: decline in long-term rates made pension commitments far more expensive
- By 2016: combined pension deficits of US public sector reached **$3 trillion**
- American corporate pensions in deficit to tune of **$425 billion**
- **Bank of England** study found decline in interest rates mostly responsible for ballooning pension deficits

### Responses to Growing Deficits
- Pension providers had to put more cash into pot
- US cities/towns cut public services, sacked workers to shore up pensions
- Private companies reduced investment, cut dividends
- Some rolled dice — chasing higher returns with riskier investments, taking on more leverage
- Balancing pension books in era of ultra-low rates proved Sisyphean task

### Attempts to Reduce Promised Benefits
- 2015: **Teamsters Central States pension plan** petitioned US Treasury to cut distributions — turned down
- 2015: **Kansas City Central States** announced massive cuts in pension payments
- Britain's **Universities Superannuation Scheme** attempted to reduce benefits; furious college lecturers launched nationwide strike

### Municipal Bankruptcies and Pension Liabilities
- Weighed down with pension liabilities, several municipalities declared bankruptcy: Detroit, San Bernardino
- **Chicago's** credit rating cut to junk in 2015
- Commonwealth of **Puerto Rico's** solvency crisis due in large measure to unfunded pension liabilities
- **Moody's** warned pension deficits constituted "largest cloud overhanging" multi-trillion dollar municipal bond market

### British Home Stores and Carillion Bankruptcies
- 2016: **BHS** bankruptcy revealed massive pensions shortfall; owner **Sir Philip Green** took refuge on newly delivered £100 million yacht
- Early 2018: large UK construction company **Carillion** collapsed with pension deficit of hundreds of millions
- In US: **Pension Benefits Guarantee Corporation** (quasi-state body insuring corporate pensions) close to bankruptcy

### Shift from Defined Benefit to Defined Contribution
- Defined benefit pension plans everywhere closed to new members
- **"Defined contribution" schemes**: employers pay certain amount but give no guaranteed income on retirement
- These DC pensions generally received lower employer contributions, delivered inferior investment returns
- Henceforth: workers faced prospect of reduced retirement incomes

### Fudge and Unrealistic Assumptions
- US pension providers assumed unrealistically high returns on plan investments
- Discount rate held above market rate of interest
- IRS permitted private pension providers to use outdated mortality assumptions
- Widespread use of dubious assumptions produced **"intertemporal accounting error"**

### Americans Saving Too Little
- 2014 study: median pension savings for Americans on cusp of retirement just **$111,000**
- Invested in annuity, would produce annual retirement income well below $10,000
- Unfunded pension commitments for OECD member countries estimated to be **twice as large as their reported national debts**

> "Over the last few decades, we have been conducting a large-scale social experiment with ultralow savings rates, without a strong safety net." — Tyler Cowen

> "The zero interest-rate policy broke the social contract for generations of hardworking Americans who saved for retirement, only to find their savings are not nearly enough." — **Michael Burry**

### Retirement Gap Statistics
- **World Economic Forum (2019)**: net retirees on several continents in danger of outliving savings
- Average American male retiree had enough savings for not quite ten years, leaving him eight years short
- "Retirement gap" for American women close to eleven years
- At least Americans could be thankful not retiring in Japan (retirement gap fifteen years for men, twenty years for women)

### Pension Managers Hedge Interest-Rate Risk
- Egged on by regulators: pension managers hedged interest-rate exposure by purchasing long-dated bonds
- Given top 300 pension funds held ~$15 trillion, their bond purchases contributed to decline in long-term rates
- In UK: liability hedging drove yield on fifty-year inflation-linked gilts into negative territory
- Thus: **low rates begot pensions crisis and pensions crisis begot lower rates**

### Caught Between Rock and Hard Place
- In theory: higher interest rates would reduce pension liabilities
- In practice: would also hit value of pension assets

## Piketty's Error (Sidebar)

### Capital in the Twenty-First Century
- **Thomas Piketty's** book became overnight sensation (spring 2014)
- Proposed fundamental law: inequality rises whenever return on capital (profits, interest, dividends, rents) exceeds growth rate of economy
- Formula: **r > g** (r = return on capital, g = rate of economic growth)

### Scrutiny of Piketty's Theory
- IMF tested fundamental law in nineteen advanced economies: found in most countries, inequality declined when gap between returns on capital and growth expanded
- Historical data from US early 19th century doesn't support hypothesis
- During this period: rate of return on capital and interest rates declined and asset valuations rose

> "The wealthy pulled ahead of the rest of society especially in the cities of the Northeast, on the strength of their savings, access to credit, and capital gains, not from any rise in the rate of return. And the wealthy pulled ahead for another reason: the development of the domestic financial sector." — **Lindert and Williamson**

### Piketty's Flawed Assumptions
- Piketty assumed wealth compounds with time, whereas rich divide fortunes and most savings spent in retirement
- 'Buddenbrooks effect' — rags to riches and back to rags in three generations
- Piketty made fundamental error: employed terms 'capital' and 'wealth' interchangeably, making no distinction between *capital* (productive assets) and *wealth* (financial claims with no link to production)
- Most of 'comeback of private wealth' was in form of financial claims (*virtual wealth*) at time when capital investment weakening

### Alternative "Iron Law of Inequality"
- On closer inspection: Piketty's 'capital in twenty-first century' turns out to be wealth bubble
- Since wealth unevenly distributed and top incomes correlated with stock prices → wealth bubbles responsible for much increase in inequality
- Central argument of book: wealth bubbles occur when interest rate held below its natural level
- Thus: inequality liable to increase when rate of interest held *below* economy's growth rate
- **r < g** (r = rate of interest, g = economy's trend growth)
- Formula (inverse of Piketty's) can explain both changes in distribution during 1920s and rise in inequality since 1980s

## Chapter 15: The Price of Anxiety

### Galiani's Innovation
- **Ferdinando Galiani** (Neapolitan abbé): "most profound, sharp-sighted and perhaps the most salacious man of his century" (Nietzsche)
- **Schumpeter**: 'one of ablest minds ever became active in our field'
- First to explain why useful things like water have lower market value than diamonds

### On Money (1751)
- Galiani rejected traditional view that charging for loans inherently unjust
- Correctly described interest as representing difference 'between present money and money that is distant in time'
- But justified existence of interest on grounds that each loan carries some risk of loss, which induces anxiety in lender

> "Keeping someone in anxiety is pain, hence it must be paid for"

> "What is called the product of money, when it is legitimate, is simply *the price of anxiety*"

### Interest as Price of Anxiety/Insurance
- Interest 'bears the same ratio to the capital as the probability of loss bears to the probability of repayment'
- Since each loan carries different risk, interest

> "is as varied as are the almost infinite degrees of probability of loss, which is very great in some cases (as in maritime usury), sometimes falls to zero (as in the banks and companies of republics), and sometimes even below zero (as happened in France in the time of Law's System)"

- Interest can be seen as 'price of insurance'

### Risk and Interest Connection
- Galiani's description of interest as insurance premium strikingly modern
- Finance in twenty-first century underpinned by role interest plays in pricing risk
- Enormous variety of risks incorporated into rate of interest: credit risk, legal risk, liquidity risk, inflation risk, duration risk
- Bank spread between loans and deposits akin to insurance premium

### Moral Hazard in Credit Markets
- Moral hazard occurs when interest rate (Galiani's *price of insurance*) set too low
- Credit booms occur at times of easy money, when financial risks mispriced
- On eve of financial crisis: insurance giant **Marsh** ran campaign with slogan "I risk therefore I am"
- Capitalism is economic system which both rewards and punishes people for taking risks
- When price of risk set too low: too much risk assumed, new risks multiply, financial system becomes unstable

### Investor Behavior at Low Rates
- **Walter Bagehot** understood investors prone to behave rashly when interest rates fall below certain level
- Recent study finds: when government bond yields fall below average, investors take on more risk
- "Interest rates play an important role in affecting risk-taking behavior" — **Ma and Zijlstra**
- Investors "have a deeply ingrained notion that saving is the preservation of wealth and wealth should grow at a 'decent' rate"
- Propensity to assume risk moves inversely with interest rate
- Risk-taking becomes extreme when bond yields fall below 3%

## Carry Trades and Financial Instability

### Carry Trade Mechanics
- When short-term rates low: investors have incentive to take out loans to buy higher-yielding assets
- Difference between cost of borrowing and return on risky loans = 'carry'
- **Carry trades** involve numerous forms: hedge funds using leverage to buy mortgage-backed securities, landlords taking mortgages on rental properties
- Returns are **asymmetric**: steady small gains with exposure to sudden large losses
- "Pick up nickels in front of steamrollers" or "eat like a bird, shit like an elephant"
- Losses can't be accurately predicted since returns are **fat-tailed**

### 2007 Carry Trade Collapse
- When liquidity dried up and credit spreads widened early 2007, leveraged players forced to unwind
- **Shadow banks** collapsed: mortgage REITs, structured investment vehicles, bank conduits
- **AIG** discovered it had sold credit default swap insurance too cheaply

### Déjà Vu All Over Again
- After Fed cut rates to zero in 2008, investors desperate for yield
- Post-2008 yield-chasing took myriad forms: peer-to-peer lending, yieldcos, BDCs, preference share funds, high-yield municipal bonds, REITs
- Money market funds "broke the buck" after Lehman, forcing them to take more credit risk
- **Catastrophe bonds** became popular

> "The fixed-income market was 'acting like one giant insurance company'" — *WSJ* investor quote

### The Fed as Carry Trader
- QE explicitly designed to encourage Wall Street to take more risk via **portfolio balance channel**
- Fed sold Treasury bonds/mortgage securities to investors, who were supposed to buy higher-yielding securities
- "The Fed became possibly the biggest carry trader of all"
- In 2015, Fed earned nearly **$100 billion profit** on its securities portfolio, financed by printing dollars

## The Price of Volatility

### Volatility Basics
- **Liquidity**: ability to trade without moving prices
- **Volatility**: price movement between trades
- Inversely related: volatility declines when liquidity abundant; volatility spikes when liquidity dries up
- **VIX index**: calculates implied volatility of US stock market from options prices on S&P 500

### Post-Crisis Volatility Collapse
- After Lehman: VIX climbed to record high (just short of 90)
- Post-crisis: market volatility collapsed, VIX remained lower for longer than ever before
- Each Fed QE round occurred at successively lower stock market volatility levels
- **Kevin Warsh** (former FOMC member): QE "works because we take volatility out of financial markets"
- **Dan Galai** (VIX creator, September 2017): link between ultra-low rates and ultra-low volatility

### Volatility Selling as Carry Trade
- Once volatility declined, traders bought stock market dips, confident Fed had their backs
- Traders sold **VIX futures** ("shorting vol"), earning profits as long as markets stayed becalmed
- "Carry trade of carry trades proved the most profitable of all"
- **Credit Suisse XIV fund**: climbed **elevenfold** between late 2010 and early 2018

### Volatility Strategies and Leverage
- IMF estimated (2017): **$500 billion** managed in volatility-targeting strategies
- IMF warned: low volatility regime encouraging investors to take on more leverage
- Hedge funds implemented **"risk parity"** strategy allocating investments based on historic volatility, leveraging less volatile assets
- Complex strategies had much in common with **portfolio insurance** (triggered October 1987 crash)

### February 5, 2018 "Volmageddon"
- Slight uptick in VIX forced volatility sellers to cover positions
- Scramble to buy volatility protection drove VIX higher, creating doom loop
- Over course of day: **VIX doubled** (largest increase on record)
- Credit Suisse's note lost **97% of value**
- US stocks: sharpest decline in years, shaving around **$1 trillion** off market cap
- Yet nothing happened on Main Street
- **"Volmageddon"** turned out to be Matrix glitch — wealth that evaporated was virtual

- "Interest rates are to the value of assets what gravity is to matter"
- Once gravitational force removed: Dogecoins, NFTs, meme stocks, other speculative assets free to float into stratosphere

### Disconnect Between Finance and Reality
- Disconnect between financial world and real world at heart of all great bubbles
- **Defoe**: described John Law's Mississippi System as "inconceivable Species of meer Air and Shadow"
- **Felix Somary** warned Keynes (1928): "gap between appearance and reality" portended crash
- **Dave Portnoy**: posted video selecting stocks by picking Scrabble letters from bag
- Tweeted: "good news is that I know it's rigged … stock market is disconnected from reality. The whole thing is a pyramid scheme. We're living in the Matrix."

### Lewis Carroll's Financial Wonderland
- Carroll might have imagined financial Wonderland where:
  - Bonds subtract interest
  - Unicorns graze on lush monetary pastures
  - Companies worth more bankrupt than as going concerns
  - Money that can't be used in exchange soars in value
  - Central bankers create wealth by printing money
  - Government deficits create private savings
- Carroll did conceive much of this in *Sylvie and Bruno*
- Outland Emperor wishes to make subjects twice as rich
- "New Money-act" proposed: doubling value of every coin and banknote
- "You never saw such universal joy. Shops are full morning to night. Everybody's buying everything!"

### Carroll's Upside-Down Values
- On other side of Looking-Glass: conventional values upside down
- Two eggs cost less than one
- "Puff" [speculative promotion] worth a thousand pounds
- Time stands still at Mad Hatter's Tea Party
- Time valuable ("worth a thousand pounds a minute")
- Time's arrow reversed
- In *Sylvie and Bruno*: bond must be repaid before money lent
- Outland Professor owns watch with Reversal Peg (events happen in reverse order)

### When Price of Time = Nothing
- Carroll understood: when *price of time* set at nothing or turns negative, central banks print money without limit, finance becomes absurd
- Autists on WallStreetBets found situation intensely amusing
- Serious investors (Klarman) found it deeply worrying

### The COVID Transition
- Novelist **Michel Houellebecq**: "banal virus" would change nothing
- "We will not wake up after lockdown in new world. It will be same, just a bit worse"
- Covid aggravated existing ills
- Financial world: shutdowns accompanied by more money-printing, more debt, even lower rates
- Big Business thrived, small businesses shuttered
- Big Tech monopoly profits fattened by enforced screentime
- Super-rich became richer, took super-yachts and rocket trips into space
- Old continued to benefit at expense of young
- Housing became less affordable
- Governments ran bigger deficits (Trump: "Who cares about the budget?")
- State advanced by leaps and bounds

### China's Housing Crisis
- Acceleration of tendencies brought them closer to endpoint
- China: pandemic sent housing market into overdrive
- By late 2020: nearly 1/3 of economic activity exposed to property development
- China's housing stock valued at nearly 4x GDP
- **President Xi** decided to grasp the nettle
- End of 2020: Beijing introduced "three red lines" (reduce property developers' leverage)
- Following summer: **Evergrande** (largest developer, debts ~$300 billion) on verge of failure
- Housing sales collapsed
- "Bubble that never pops" had popped

### Western Inflation Returns
- Bursting property bubbles usually accompanied by deflation
- In West: pandemic brought gust of inflation
- Global supply chains snapped as countries locked down
- Once lockdowns lifted: Western countries faced labour shortages
- Wage growth picked up
- Barrel of oil (briefly turned negative April 2020) surged past $75
- Natural gas prices climbed
- Money supply soared in US and Europe
- US consumer prices climbed to annualized rate above 5% (early 2021)
- Federal Reserve insisted inflation was **transitory**

### Historical Bond Market Turns
- Turns in bond market occur infrequently (once every several decades)
- When moment arrives: most experts taken by surprise
- 1899: **Equitable Life** President solicited opinions from respected American financiers on future course of long-term interest rates
- Bond yields had been falling for decades
- Of 69 responses: not one anticipated any change
- **Treasury Secretary Lyman Gage**: rates headed "permanently downwards"
- Gage was wrong
- Bond bull market had already ended
- Over following decades: Treasury yields rose

### Bond Market Cycles and Globalization
- Bear market in bonds at end of 19th century coincided with reversal of globalization
- Bond bear market starting 1945 followed period when US rates kept artificially low
- Federal Reserve lent directly to government to finance war effort
- Inflation returned with vengeance after war
- Treasury yields rose for next 3.5 decades
- Not hard to draw parallels between these periods and Covid conjuncture

### Andrew Haldane's Warning (mid-2021)
- Stepping down as **Bank of England's** chief economist
- Warned balance of risks tilted towards inflation
- Central bank's role: "remove punch bowl when party got started"
- "If we keep pouring the punch, tomorrow's hangover will be bad"
- By this date: too much punch had been poured
- As globalization reverses and China's workforce declines: inflation likely to pick up
- Interest rates will have to rise to contain it
- **Claudio Borio's** "epoch-defining seismic rupture" (toxic mixture of high inflation, financial/trade protectionism, stagnation) no longer seems distant prospect

### A True Rate
- **Dennis Robertson** (Keynes's Cambridge contemporary): enthralled by Lewis Carroll
- Robertson thought Keynes's interest theory as insubstantial as Cheshire Cat's grin
- Unlike Keynes, believed in necessity of interest:

> "Something is needed to ensure the equality between the amount of tea which sellers are willing to put on to the market and the amount which buyers are willing to take off it; and that something is a certain price of tea. Similarly, something is needed to ensure equality between the amount of money which lenders are willing to put on the market for loans and the amount of money which borrowers are willing to take off it; and that something is a certain rate of interest."

### The Natural Rate vs. Policy Rates
- **Reichsbank President Hjalmar Schacht**: called this "true rate" of interest
- Reflects supply and demand for actual savings
- True/natural rate not necessarily revealed by presence of inflation/deflation
- Central bankers who fix rates by reference to consumer price index changes set themselves up to fail
- Problem: capital is limited, but medium by which savings transferred (money) can be created without limit
  - By central banks (fiat money)
  - By commercial banks (fountain-pen money)
- Result: interest rate on money loans becomes detached from savings

### Hayek's Vision: Competitive Private Money
- Hayek envisaged world where money issued by private banks competing with each other
- **Good money would drive out bad**
- Any bank offering too little interest would lose clients
- Advent of cryptocurrencies has potential to realize Hayek's vision
- Cryptocurrencies already lent on various platforms
- Early cryptocurrency history marked by seamy behaviour and uncertainty about true scarcity
- Still possible one day cryptocurrency may emerge:
  - Safe to store
  - Efficient for transactions
  - Limited in supply
- Trouble: governments won't willingly give up money monopoly

### Central Bank Digital Currencies (CBDCs)
- Alternative: central banks developing digital currencies to compete with cryptos
- CBDCs might be used to acquire government bonds (providing asset-backing)
- If issuance limited to no more than economy's trend growth rate: money would be as good as gold
- **Digital Gold Standard**: some advantages, fewer disadvantages than classical Gold Standard
- One advantage: central bankers no longer able to pursue active monetary policy
- Cost of borrowing would more accurately reflect supply/demand for actual savings
- Guided by market's invisible hand: rate of interest would find its natural level
