## Introduction: The Greatest Show On Earth

- **Premise**: Doing well with money has little to do with how smart you are and a lot to do with how you behave. Behavior is hard to teach, even to really smart people.
- A genius who loses control of emotions = financial disaster. Ordinary folks with no financial education can be wealthy if they have behavioral skills that have nothing to do with formal intelligence.

### Ronald Read vs. Richard Fuscone
- **Ronald Read**: gas station attendant, janitor at JCPenney for 17 years. Born in rural Vermont, first in family to graduate high school (hitchhiked to campus). Bought a $12,000 two-bedroom house at 38, lived there for life. Widowed at 50, never remarried. Main hobby: chopping firewood. Died in 2014 at 92 with **$8 million** net worth. Left $2M to stepkids, $6M to local hospital and library. Secret: saved what little he could, invested in blue chip stocks, waited for decades as compounding did its work.
- **Richard Fuscone**: Harvard-educated Merrill Lynch executive, MBA, retired in 40s to become philanthropist. Former Merrill CEO David Komansky praised his "business savvy, leadership skills, sound judgment and personal integrity." Crain's "40 under 40" list. In mid-2000s borrowed heavily to expand 18,000-sq-ft Greenwich mansion (11 bathrooms, two elevators, two pools, seven garages, $90K/month to maintain). 2008 crisis hit — went bankrupt. "I currently have no income." Palm Beach house foreclosed. Greenwich mansion sold at foreclosure auction for 75% less than insurance value — 5 months before Read left his fortune to charity.
- **Key insight**: Ronald Read was patient; Richard Fuscone was greedy. That's all it took to eclipse the massive education and experience gap.
- In what other industry does someone with no college degree, no training, no background massively outperform someone with the best education and connections? Only in investing/finance.

### Finance as a soft skill
- Financial success is not a hard science — it's a soft skill where behavior matters more than knowledge
- Finance is overwhelmingly taught as math-based (formulas, data, correlations, cost of capital). But knowing what to do tells you nothing about what happens in your head when you try to do it.
- Housel started writing about finance in 2008 during financial crisis. Learned that nobody could accurately explain what happened or why. For every good explanation there was an equally convincing rebuttal.
- Physics is guided by laws; finance is guided by people's behaviors. "How I behave might make sense to me but look crazy to you."
- Understanding finance better through lenses of psychology and history, not finance itself
- To understand why people bury themselves in debt: study history of greed, insecurity, optimism. To understand why investors sell at the bottom: think about looking at your family and wondering if your investments are imperiling their future.
- Voltaire: "History never repeats itself; man always does."
- Book originated from a 2018 report outlining 20 flaws/biases in dealing with money — over 1 million readers. This book is the deeper dive.
- 20 chapters, each a standalone lesson, revolving around a common theme. Can be read independently.

## Chapter 1: No One's Crazy

**Epigraph**: "Your personal experiences with money make up maybe 0.00000001% of what's happened in the world, but maybe 80% of how you think the world works."

- People do some crazy things with money. But no one is crazy.
- People from different generations, raised by different parents, with different incomes, values, in different parts of the world, different economies, different job markets, different incentives, different degrees of luck — learn very different lessons.
- Everyone has their own unique experience with how the world works. What you've experienced is more compelling than what you learn second-hand.
- What seems crazy to you might make sense to me.

### Experience shapes financial behavior
- Person who grew up in poverty thinks about risk/reward differently than child of wealthy banker
- Person who grew up with high inflation experienced something the person with stable prices never had
- Stock broker who lost everything in Great Depression experienced something the 1990s tech worker can't imagine
- Australian who hasn't seen a recession in 30 years has experienced something no American ever has

### Malmendier and Nagel study (2006, NBER)
- Dug through 50 years of Survey of Consumer Finances
- Found: people's lifetime investment decisions are heavily anchored to experiences in their own generation, especially early adult life
- If you grew up with high inflation → invested less in bonds later
- If you grew up with strong stock market → invested more in stocks later
- "Our findings suggest that individual investors' willingness to bear risk depends on personal history." Not intelligence, education, or sophistication — just the dumb luck of when and where you were born.

### Bill Gross example
- Financial Times interviewed Gross in 2019. "Gross admits he would probably not be where he is today if he had been born a decade earlier or later." His career coincided with generational collapse in interest rates that gave bond prices a tailwind.
- Doesn't just affect opportunities — affects what you THINK about those opportunities. To Gross, bonds were wealth-generating machines. To his father's generation (higher inflation), they were wealth incinerators.

### Stock market and inflation experience varies by birth year
- Born 1970: S&P 500 increased almost 10-fold (inflation-adjusted) during teens and 20s
- Born 1950: market went literally nowhere during teens and 20s
- Born 1960s: inflation tripled during teens/20s (gas lines, shrinking paychecks)
- Born 1990: inflation so low it probably never crossed your mind
- These different experiences → completely different worldviews about money

### JFK and the Great Depression
- Frederick Lewis Allen wrote that the Great Depression "marked millions of Americans — inwardly — for the rest of their lives"
- But range of experiences varied: JFK told a reporter he had "no first-hand knowledge of the Depression" — his family had one of the great fortunes, bigger houses, more servants, traveled more. Only thing he saw directly was his father hiring extra gardeners to give them jobs. "I really did not learn about the Depression until I read about it at Harvard."
- This was a major point in 1960 election — how could someone with no understanding of the biggest economic story be put in charge? Overcome only by JFK's WWII experience.
- No amount of studying or open-mindedness can genuinely recreate the power of fear and uncertainty.

### Unemployment disparities (Nov 2009)
- National average: ~10%
- African American males 16-19 without high school diploma: 49%
- Caucasian females over 45 with college degree: 4%
- No one should expect these groups to think the same way about inflation, stocks, unemployment, or money

### Foxconn / Chinese worker comment
- NYT story on Foxconn's atrocious working conditions. Nephew of Chinese worker commented: his aunt was a prostitute before working at the factory — factory work was an improvement. "The idea of working in a 'sweat shop' compared to that old lifestyle is an improvement."
- Different experiences lead to vastly different views on topics that seem black and white

### Core lesson
- Every decision people make with money is justified by the information they have at the moment, plugged into their unique mental model of how the world works
- People can be misinformed, have incomplete information, be bad at math, be persuaded by marketing, misjudge consequences
- But every financial decision a person makes, makes sense to them in that moment
- **No one is crazy** — they're just operating from their own unique set of experiences

### Lottery tickets example
- Americans spend more on lotto tickets than movies, video games, music, sporting events, and books combined
- Mostly poor people buy them. Lowest-income households spend $412/year on average — 4x the highest income groups
- 40% of Americans can't come up with $400 in an emergency — same people buying $400 in lottery tickets
- Seems crazy, but from their perspective: "Buying a lottery ticket is the only time in our lives we can hold a tangible dream of getting the good stuff that you already have and take for granted. We are paying for a dream."
- "What you're doing seems crazy but I kind of understand why you're doing it" — uncovers the root of many financial decisions

### How new modern finance is
- Money has been around since 600 BC (King Alyattes of Lydia) but modern saving/investing concepts are practically infants
- Retirement as a concept is at most two generations old. Before WWII, most Americans worked until they died. Labor force participation for men 65+ was above 50% until 1940s
- **Ida May Fuller** cashed the first Social Security check in 1940 — $22.54 ($416 adjusted for inflation). Average SS check didn't exceed $1,000/month (inflation-adjusted) until the 1980s
- Quarter of Americans over 65 classified as living in poverty until late 1960s
- "Everyone used to have a private pension" is wildly exaggerated — only a quarter of those 65+ had pension income in 1975, and among them only 15% of household income came from it
- 1980s was when the idea that everyone deserves dignified retirement took hold
- **401(k) didn't exist until 1978. Roth IRA was not born until 1998.** If it were a person it would barely be old enough to drink.
- College: share of Americans over 25 with bachelor's went from 1 in 20 (1940) to 1 in 4 (2015). Tuition rose 4x adjusted for inflation. No decades of accumulated experience to learn from — "we're winging it"
- Index funds less than 50 years old. Hedge funds only took off in last 25 years. Consumer debt (mortgages, credit cards, car loans) didn't take off until post-WWII GI Bill
- Dogs domesticated 10,000 years ago and still retain wild behaviors. We have 20-50 years of experience with modern finance and hope to be perfectly acclimated.

## Chapter 2: Luck & Risk

**Epigraph**: "Nothing is as good or as bad as it seems."

- Luck and risk are siblings — both the reality that every outcome in life is guided by forces other than individual effort
- **Scott Galloway** (NYU professor): "Nothing is as good or as bad as it seems."

### Bill Gates and Lakeside School
- Gates went to one of the only high schools in the world that had a computer
- **Lakeside School**, just outside Seattle. **Bill Dougall**, WWII navy pilot turned math/science teacher, believed in real-world experience. In 1968 petitioned Mothers' Club to use $3,000 from rummage sale to lease a Teletype Model 30 computer hooked to GE mainframe for time-sharing
- "The whole idea of time-sharing only got invented in 1965." Most university graduate schools didn't have a computer as advanced as what Gates had access to in eighth grade
- Gates was 13 when he met **Paul Allen** in 1968. Both obsessed with the school computer, became computing experts working late into the night and weekends
- Allen recalled Gates showing him a Fortune magazine: "What do you think it's like to run a Fortune 500 company?" Allen: "Maybe we'll have our own computer company someday." Microsoft is now worth more than a trillion dollars.

### The one-in-a-million math
- In 1968: ~303 million high-school-age people in the world → 18 million in US → 270,000 in Washington state → 100,000 in Seattle area → only about **300 attended Lakeside School**
- One in a million chance of attending the high school that had the cash and foresight to buy a computer
- Gates himself: "If there had been no Lakeside, there would have been no Microsoft"

### Kent Evans — luck's sibling, risk
- **Kent Evans**: Gates's best friend in eighth grade, by Gates's account the best student in the class. As skilled with computers as Gates and Allen. Shared Bill's business ambition — "Kent always had the big briefcase, like a lawyer's briefcase"
- They talked "on the phone ridiculous amounts." Gates still knows Kent's phone number: "525-7851"
- Evans and Gates were tasked by Lakeside to build a computer program to solve the school's class scheduling problem. It worked.
- Unlike Paul Allen, Kent shared Bill's business mind. "We would have kept working together. I'm sure we would have gone to college together." Kent could have been a founding partner of Microsoft.
- **But Kent died in a mountaineering accident before graduating high school.** ~3 dozen mountaineering deaths/year in the US. Odds of being killed on a mountain in high school: roughly one in a million.
- Gates experienced one-in-a-million luck by ending up at Lakeside. Evans experienced one-in-a-million risk by never getting to finish what he and Gates set out to achieve. Same force, same magnitude, opposite directions.

### Luck and risk are hard to measure
- **Robert Shiller** (Nobel Prize in economics): When asked "What do you want to know about investing that we can't know?" — "The exact role of luck in successful outcomes."
- Since it's hard to quantify luck and rude to suggest people's success is owed to it, default stance is to ignore luck as a factor
- Economist **Bhashkar Mazumder**: incomes among brothers are more correlated than height or weight. But find two rich brothers and they won't think the study applies to them.
- Failure is equally abused: was it a bad decision or bad luck? "Say I buy a stock, and five years later it's gone nowhere. Did I make a mistake, or did I just experience the reality of risk?"
- Someone else's failure = bad decisions. Your own failure = the dark side of risk. We prefer clean stories of cause and effect.
- Forbes doesn't celebrate poor investors who made good decisions but got unlucky. It celebrates rich ones who may have been reckless but got lucky.

### Vanderbilt and Rockefeller
- **Cornelius Vanderbilt**: his business advisor told him every transaction he agreed to broke the law. "My God, John. You don't suppose you can run a railroad in accordance with the statutes of the State of New York, do you?" His law-flouting is portrayed as sage wisdom, but it could easily have been an outlaw's downfall.
- "What do I care about the law? Ain't I got the power?"
- **John D. Rockefeller**: a judge called his company "no better than a common thief." How little would the narrative need to shift from "genius" to "criminal"? Very little.
- **Benjamin Graham**: majority of his investing success was due to owning an enormous chunk of GEICO stock which, by his own admission, broke nearly every diversification rule he laid out in his famous texts. "One lucky break, or one supremely shrewd decision — can we tell them apart? Not easily."
- **Mark Zuckerberg** turned down Yahoo's $1 billion offer — genius. Yahoo turned down Microsoft's buyout offer — fools. Same coin, different side.
- Line between "inspiringly bold" and "foolishly reckless" can be a millimeter thick and only visible with hindsight. **Risk and luck are doppelgangers.**

### Practical advice
- "Be careful who you praise and admire. Be careful who you look down upon and wish to avoid becoming."
- Letter to his son: "Not all success is due to hard work, and not all poverty is due to laziness."
- **Focus less on specific individuals/case studies, more on broad patterns.** Extreme examples (billionaires, CEOs, massive failures) are least applicable because they're most influenced by extreme luck or risk.
- Trying to emulate Warren Buffett is hard because the role of luck in his extreme performance is very high. But "people who have control over their time tend to be happier" is a broad, common pattern you can act on.
- **Frederick Lewis Allen**: spent his career depicting the life of the average, median American. More relevant lessons from broad observation than from extreme characters in the news.
- Gates: "Success is a lousy teacher. It seduces smart people into thinking they can't lose."
- Failure can be a lousy teacher too — seduces smart people into thinking their decisions were terrible when sometimes they just reflect the unforgiving realities of risk.
- **Arrange your financial life so a bad investment here and a missed goal there won't wipe you out**, so you can keep playing until the odds fall in your favor.

## Chapter 3: Never Enough

**Epigraph**: "When rich people do crazy things."

### Vonnegut-Heller-Bogle anecdote
- **John Bogle** (Vanguard founder) told this story: At a party on Shelter Island, **Kurt Vonnegut** tells **Joseph Heller** that their billionaire host made more money in a single day than Heller earned from Catch-22 over its whole history. Heller responds: "Yes, but I have something he will never have... *enough*."
- Bogle was stunned by the eloquence of that word: "For a critical element of our society, including many of the wealthiest and most powerful among us, there seems to be no limit today on what enough entails."

### Rajat Gupta
- Born in Kolkata, orphaned as a teenager. "People talk about the privileged few who begin life on third base. Gupta couldn't even see the baseball stadium."
- By his mid-40s: CEO of **McKinsey**, board member of Goldman Sachs, five public companies, the UN, World Economic Forum, partnered on philanthropy with Bill Gates. Worth reportedly **$100 million** by 2008 — $600/hour passively, 24 hours a day.
- But $100 million wasn't enough. He wanted to be a *billionaire*. "I think he wants to be in that circle. That's a billionaire circle, right? Goldman is like the hundreds of millions circle."
- In 2008, as Goldman Sachs faced crisis and **Warren Buffett** planned to invest $5 billion, Gupta (Goldman board member) learned of the deal before the public. Sixteen seconds after hanging up from the board call, he called hedge fund manager **Raj Rajaratnam**, who immediately bought 175,000 shares of Goldman. Stock surged when deal was announced. Rajaratnam made a quick $1 million.
- SEC claims Gupta's insider tips led to $17 million in profits total
- Both Gupta and Rajaratnam went to prison for insider trading. Careers and reputations irrevocably ruined.

### Bernie Madoff
- Most notorious Ponzi schemer since Charles Ponzi himself
- What's overlooked: before the Ponzi scheme, Madoff was already a wildly successful, legitimate businessman
- His market-making firm (Bernard L. Madoff Investment Securities) handled $740 million average daily volume, equaling 9% of NYSE volume. "He has built a highly profitable securities firm" (WSJ, 1992)
- Former staffer said the legitimate market-making arm made between $25 million and $50 million per year
- Already hugely and legitimately wealthy — and yet, the fraud.

### The core question and lessons
- Why would someone worth hundreds of millions risk everything for more? They had unimaginable wealth, prestige, power, freedom. They threw it all away because they wanted more. **They had no sense of enough.**
- **Long-Term Capital Management**: staffed with traders worth tens and hundreds of millions each, most of their wealth invested in their own funds. Took so much risk they lost everything — in 1998, in the middle of the greatest bull market and strongest economy in history.
- **Buffett**: "To make money they didn't have and didn't need, they risked what they did have and did need. And that's foolish. If you risk something that is important to you for something that is unimportant to you, it just does not make any sense."
- *There is no reason to risk what you have and need for what you don't have and don't need.*

### Four things to remember:
1. **The hardest financial skill is getting the goalpost to stop moving.** If expectations rise with results, there's no logic in striving for more. Modern capitalism generates wealth and envy in equal measure. Happiness = results minus expectations.
2. **Social comparison is the problem.** Rookie baseball player earning $500K is rich by any definition — until he plays alongside **Mike Trout** ($430 million contract). Trout makes $36M/year but the top-ten hedge fund managers in 2018 needed $340M minimum. The $340M manager compares to the top five ($770M+). They compare to Buffett ($3.5B increase in 2018). Buffett compares to Bezos ($24B increase in 2018 — more per hour than the "rich" baseball player makes in a year). The ceiling of social comparison is so high that virtually no one will ever hit it. The only way to win is to not fight — to accept that you might have enough, even if it's less than those around you. A Las Vegas dealer told a friend: "The only way to win in a Las Vegas casino is to exit as soon as you enter." Same with keeping up with others' wealth.
3. **"Enough" is not too little.** "Enough" is realizing that an insatiable appetite for more will push you to the point of regret. The only way to know how much food you can eat is to eat until you're sick — but in business and investing, many only stop reaching for more when they break and are forced to.
4. **There are many things never worth risking, no matter the potential gain.** After prison, Gupta told the NYT: "Don't get too attached to anything — your reputation, your accomplishments or any of it." Housel calls this the worst possible takeaway — the comforting self-justifications of a man who desperately wants his reputation back but knows it's gone. Reputation is invaluable. Freedom and independence are invaluable. Family and friends are invaluable. Happiness is invaluable. Your best shot at keeping them is knowing when it's time to stop taking risks that might harm them. Knowing when you have *enough*.

## Chapter 4: Confounding Compounding

**Epigraph**: "$81.5 billion of Warren Buffett's $84.5 billion net worth came after his 65th birthday. Our minds are not built to handle such absurdities."

### Ice ages and compounding
- Lessons from one field can teach us about unrelated fields. The billion-year history of ice ages teaches about growing money.
- It wasn't until the 19th century that scientists agreed Earth had been covered in ice, on multiple occasions — five distinct ice ages.
- The energy needed to freeze the planet, melt it, and freeze it again is staggering. Theories were equally grand (mountain ranges shifting climate, volcanic eruptions).
- **Milutin Milanković** (Serbian scientist, early 1900s) studied Earth's position relative to other planets. Discovered that gravitational pull of sun and moon gently affects Earth's motion and tilt toward the sun. During parts of this cycle, hemispheres get slightly more or less solar radiation.
- **Wladimir Köppen** (Russian meteorologist) dug deeper: it wasn't cold winters but **moderately cool summers** that were the culprit. When a summer never gets warm enough to melt the previous winter's snow, the leftover ice base makes it easier for the next winter's snow to accumulate, which sticks around the following summer, which attracts even more accumulation. Perpetual snow reflects more sun rays, exacerbating cooling, more snowfall, and on and on. Within a few hundred years, a seasonal snowpack grows into a continental ice sheet.
- Glaciologist **Gwen Schultz**: "It is not necessarily the amount of snow that causes ice sheets but the fact that snow, however little, lasts."
- **The big takeaway**: you don't need tremendous force to create tremendous results. If something compounds — if a little growth serves as fuel for future growth — a small starting base can lead to results so extraordinary they seem to defy logic.

### Buffett's real secret: time
- More than 2,000 books on Buffett but few pay attention to the simplest fact: his fortune isn't due to just being a good investor, but being a good investor **since he was literally a child**
- Net worth: $84.5 billion. Of that, $84.2 billion accumulated after his 50th birthday. $81.5 billion came after he qualified for Social Security, in his mid-60s.
- Had he started investing at 30 with $25,000 (instead of 10 with $9.3 million inflation-adjusted), earned the same 22% annual returns, but quit at 60 to play golf — his net worth would be **$11.9 million**. 99.9% less than his actual net worth.
- "His skill is investing, but his secret is time. That's how compounding works."

### Jim Simons comparison
- **Jim Simons** (Renaissance Technologies) compounded at 66% annually since 1988 — no one comes close. Buffett compounded at roughly 22%, a third as much.
- Simons' net worth: $21 billion. 75% less rich than Buffett despite 3x the annual returns. Why? Simons didn't start his investment stride until age 50 — he's had less than half as many years to compound.
- If Simons had earned his 66% returns for the 70-year span Buffett has been investing, he'd be worth sixty-three quintillion nine hundred quadrillion seven hundred eighty-one trillion seven hundred eighty billion seven hundred forty-eight million one hundred sixty thousand dollars. Ridiculous, impractical — but the point is that small changes in growth assumptions lead to ridiculous results.

### Linear vs. exponential thinking
- Linear thinking is much more intuitive than exponential. 8+8+8+8+8+8+8+8 = 72 (easy). 8×8×8×8×8×8×8×8 = 134,217,728 (head-exploding).
- IBM made a 3.5 megabyte hard drive in the 1950s. By 1990, 200-500 MB. Then *wham*: 1999 iMac = 6 GB; 2003 = 120 GB; 2006 = 250 GB; 2011 = first 4 TB; 2017 = 60 TB; 2019 = 100 TB. From 1950-1990: gained 296 MB. From 1990 to today: gained 100 million MB.
- In 2004 Bill Gates criticized Gmail for offering a gigabyte of storage. Author Steven Levy: "Despite his currency with cutting-edge technologies, his mentality was anchored in the old paradigm of storage being a commodity that must be conserved."
- None of the 2,000 Buffett books are titled *This Guy Has Been Investing Consistently for Three-Quarters of a Century*. But that's the key — it's just hard to wrap your head around because it's not intuitive.
- Most powerful book should be called *Shut Up And Wait* — just one page with a long-term chart of economic growth.
- **Good investing isn't about earning the highest returns** (those tend to be one-off hits). It's about earning pretty good returns that you can stick with and repeat for the longest period of time. That's when compounding runs wild.

## Chapter 5: Getting Wealthy vs. Staying Wealthy

**Epigraph**: "Good investing is not necessarily about making good decisions. It's about consistently not screwing up."

### Jesse Livermore vs. Abraham Germansky
- **Jesse Livermore**: greatest stock market trader of his day. Born 1877, professional trader before most people knew you could do such a thing. By age 30: worth inflation-adjusted equivalent of $100 million.
- By 1929, one of the most well-known investors in the world. During the October 1929 crash (Black Monday, Black Tuesday, Black Thursday), his wife Dorothy feared the worst — Wall Street speculators were committing suicide across New York. She and her children greeted Jesse in tears at the door.
- But Jesse had been short the market. "No darling, I have just had my best ever trading day — we are fabulously rich and can do whatever we like." In one day he made the equivalent of more than $3 billion.
- **Abraham Germansky**: multimillionaire real estate developer who made a fortune in the roaring 1920s, bet heavily on the surging stock market. On October 26, 1929, the NYT published an article about his wife asking an attorney to help find him — missing since Thursday, last seen near Wall Street "tearing a strip of ticker tape into bits and scattering it on the sidewalk as he walked toward Broadway." That was the end of Abraham Germansky.
- The October 1929 crash made Livermore one of the richest men in the world. It ruined Germansky, perhaps taking his life.
- But fast-forward four years: Livermore, overflowing with confidence, made larger and larger bets, wound up far over his head in debt, and eventually lost everything. Broke and ashamed, he disappeared for two days in 1933. He returned, but his path was set. **Livermore eventually took his own life.**
- Both were very good at getting wealthy, and equally bad at **staying** wealthy.

### Getting money vs. keeping money are two different skills
- Getting money requires taking risks, being optimistic, putting yourself out there
- Keeping money requires the opposite: humility, fear that what you've made can be taken away just as fast, frugality, acceptance that at least some of what you've made is attributable to luck
- **Michael Moritz** (Sequoia Capital) asked by Charlie Rose why Sequoia was so successful. Moritz cited longevity — some VC firms succeed for 5-10 years, but Sequoia has prospered for four decades. "I think we've always been afraid of going out of business... We assume that tomorrow won't be like yesterday. We can't afford to rest on our laurels."

### Survival as the key
- "If I had to summarize money success in a single word it would be 'survival.'"
- 40% of companies successful enough to become publicly traded lost effectively all of their value over time
- Forbes 400 has ~20% turnover per decade for causes unrelated to death or transferring money
- **The ability to stick around for a long time**, without wiping out or being forced to give up, is what makes the biggest difference
- Compounding only works if you can give an asset years and years to grow. Like planting oak trees: 10 years = meaningful difference; 50 years = extraordinary.
- What Buffett didn't do matters as much as what he did: didn't get carried away with debt, didn't panic and sell during 14 recessions, didn't sully his business reputation, didn't attach to one strategy, didn't rely on others' money (public company = investors can't withdraw), didn't burn out.
- Survival gave him longevity — investing consistently from age 10 to at least 89 is what made compounding work wonders.

### Rick Guerin — the third member
- **Warren Buffett and Charlie Munger** are a famous duo. But 40 years ago there was a third member: **Rick Guerin**. Warren, Charlie, and Rick made investments together and interviewed business managers together. Then Rick kind of disappeared.
- **Mohnish Pabrai** asked Buffett what happened to Rick. Buffett: "Charlie and I always knew that we would become incredibly wealthy. We were not in a hurry to get wealthy; we knew it would happen. Rick was just as smart as us, but he was in a hurry."
- In the 1973-1974 downturn, Rick was levered with margin loans. The stock market went down almost 70% in those two years — he got margin calls. He sold his Berkshire stock to Warren — "I bought Rick's Berkshire stock" — at under $40 a piece. Rick was forced to sell because he was levered.
- Charlie, Warren, and Rick were equally skilled at getting wealthy. But Warren and Charlie had the added skill of **staying** wealthy.
- **Nassim Taleb**: "Having an 'edge' and surviving are two different things: the first requires the second. You need to avoid ruin. At all costs."

### Three things for the survival mindset:
1. **Be financially unbreakable over chasing big returns.** Cash feels conservative during a bull market (1% vs 10%). But preventing one desperate, ill-timed stock sale can do more for lifetime returns than picking dozens of big winners. Compounding doesn't rely on earning big returns — merely good returns sustained uninterrupted for the longest period of time, especially in times of chaos and havoc.
2. **Plan, but plan on the plan not going according to plan.** Few plans survive their first encounter with reality. Think about the last 20 years: 9/11, housing boom and bust, financial crisis, nine million jobs lost, record-breaking rally, coronavirus. Margin of safety is different from being conservative — it raises the odds of success at a given level of risk. The higher your margin of safety, the smaller your edge needs to be.
3. **A barbelled personality — optimistic about the future, paranoid about what will prevent you from getting to the future — is vital.** Sensible optimism isn't believing things will go well — it's believing the odds are in your favor and over time things will balance out, even if what happens in between is filled with misery. By age 20 the average person can lose roughly half the synaptic connections they had at age two — destruction in the face of progress is not only possible, but an efficient way to get rid of excess. You need short-term paranoia to keep you alive long enough to exploit long-term optimism.

### Growth amid loss
- US economy over last 170 years: real GDP per capita went from ~$1 to ~$100 (log scale). But during that time: 1.3 million Americans died in nine wars, 99.9% of all companies went out of business, four presidents assassinated, 675,000 died in flu pandemic, 33 recessions lasting 48 cumulative years, stock market fell 10%+ from recent highs at least 102 times, stocks lost a third of value at least 12 times, annual inflation exceeded 7% in 20 separate years, "economic pessimism" appeared in newspapers at least 29,000 times.
- Standard of living increased 20-fold, but barely a day went by that lacked tangible reasons for pessimism.
- Livermore figured this out the hard way: he associated good times with the end of bad times. Getting wealthy made him feel invincible. After losing nearly everything he reflected: "I sometimes think that no price is too high for a speculator to pay to learn that which will keep him from getting the swelled head."

## Chapter 6: Tails, You Win

**Epigraph**: "You can be wrong half the time and still make a fortune."

### Heinz Berggruen — art as index fund
- **Heinz Berggruen** fled Nazi Germany in 1936, studied literature at UC Berkeley. Didn't show particular promise in youth. But by the 1990s he was one of the most successful art dealers of all time.
- In 2000 he sold part of his massive collection of Picassos, Braques, Klees, and Matisses to the German government for more than 100 million euros — such a bargain they considered it a donation. Private market value: well over $1 billion.
- **Horizon Research**: "The great investors bought vast quantities of art... A subset of the collections turned out to be great investments, and they were held for a sufficiently long period of time to allow the portfolio return to converge upon the return of the best elements in the portfolio. That's all that happens."
- The great art dealers operated like **index funds** — bought everything they could, bought in portfolios not individual pieces, sat and waited for a few winners to emerge. Perhaps 99% of Berggruen's acquisitions were of little value, but the other 1% was Picasso.

### Long tails in business and investing
- **Long tails** — the farthest ends of a distribution of outcomes — have tremendous influence in finance. A small number of events account for the majority of outcomes.
- It's not intuitive that an investor can be wrong half the time and still make a fortune. It means we underestimate how normal it is for a lot of things to fail. Which causes us to overreact when they do.

### Walt Disney
- *Steamboat Willie* put Disney on the map as an animator. Business success was another story. Disney's first studio went bankrupt. His films were monstrously expensive and financed at outrageous terms. By the mid-1930s Disney had produced more than 400 cartoons — most were short, beloved by viewers, and most of them lost a fortune.
- ***Snow White and the Seven Dwarfs* changed everything.** The $8 million it earned in the first six months of 1938 was an order of magnitude higher than anything the company earned previously. All debts paid off, key employees got retention bonuses, new state-of-the-art studio purchased in Burbank. By 1938 he had produced several hundred hours of film, but the **83 minutes of Snow White** were all that mattered.

### Venture capital numbers
- **Correlation Ventures**: out of 21,000+ venture financings from 2004-2014: 65% lost money, 2.5% made 10x-20x, 1% made more than 20x, 0.5% (~100 companies) earned 50x or more. That's where the majority of the industry's returns come from.

### Russell 3000 — tails in public markets too
- **J.P. Morgan Asset Management** published returns for the Russell 3000 Index since 1980.
- **40% of all Russell 3000 components lost at least 70% of their value and never recovered** over this period
- Effectively all of the index's overall returns came from **7% of component companies** that outperformed by at least two standard deviations
- The Russell 3000 has increased more than 73-fold since 1980 — a spectacular return — even though 40% of its components were effectively failures
- **Carolco** (first three Rambo films, Terminator 2, Basic Instinct, Total Recall): went public 1987, half a billion revenue in 1991, $400M market cap. Then the blockbusters stopped, a few big-budget projects flopped, bankrupt by 1996. Stock goes to zero. 4 in 10 public companies experience this over time.
- Within winning companies, tails also drive: Amazon drove 6% of S&P 500's 2017 returns, almost entirely due to **Prime and AWS** (out of hundreds of products including the Fire Phone flop). Apple was responsible for 7%, driven overwhelmingly by the iPhone.
- Google hiring acceptance: 0.2%. Facebook: 0.1%. Apple: 2%. The people working on tail projects that drive tail returns have **tail careers**.

### Implications for investors
- Napoleon's definition of military genius: "The man who can do the average thing when all those around him are going crazy." Same in investing.
- **Sue, Jim, and Tom example**: If you saved $1/month from 1900-2019: Sue (invests every month regardless) = $435,551. Jim (invests only outside recessions, cash during them) = $257,386. Tom (invests during non-recession, sells 6 months into recession, returns 6 months after recession ends) = $234,476. Sue wins by a mile. 1,428 months total, just over 300 were during recession. Keeping cool during 22% of the time = three-quarters more money.
- "Your success as an investor will be determined by how you respond to punctuated moments of terror, not the years spent on cruise control."
- **Peter Lynch**: "If you're terrific in this business, you're right six times out of 10."
- No one makes good decisions all the time. The most impressive people are packed full of horrendous ideas that are often acted upon.
- **Jeff Bezos** after Fire Phone disaster: "If you think that's a big failure, we're working on much bigger failures right now. I am not kidding."
- **Reed Hastings** (Netflix): "Our hit ratio is way too high right now... We have to take more risk. You have to try more crazy things, because we should have a higher cancel rate overall."
- **Chris Rock**: the Chris Rock on TV is hilarious, flawless. The Chris Rock in dozens of small clubs each year is just OK. Every big comedian tests material in small clubs first. Rock did 40-50 shows at the Stress Factory in New Brunswick getting ready for his last tour. The good jokes are the tails from hundreds of attempts.
- **Buffett at 2013 Berkshire shareholder meeting**: said he'd owned 400-500 stocks during his life and made most of his money on 10 of them. **Charlie Munger**: "If you remove just a few of Berkshire's top investments, its long-term track record is pretty average."
- When we pay special attention to successes, we overlook that the gains came from a small percent of actions. Our own failures feel like we're doing something wrong — but we may be wrong just as often as the masters. They were just *more right* when they were right.
- **George Soros**: "It's not whether you're right or wrong that's important, but how much money you make when you're right and how much you lose when you're wrong."

## Chapter 7: Freedom

**Epigraph**: "Controlling your time is the highest dividend money pays."

- The highest form of wealth is the ability to wake up every morning and say, "I can do whatever I want today."
- People want to become wealthier to make them happier. If there's a common denominator in happiness — a universal fuel of joy — it's that people want to control their lives.

### Angus Campbell — happiness research
- **Angus Campbell**, psychologist at University of Michigan, born 1910. His research focused on what made people happy (unique at a time when psychology focused on disorders).
- His 1981 book *The Sense of Wellbeing in America*: people are generally happier than psychologists assumed. Some were clearly happier, but you couldn't group them by income, geography, or education — too many in each category are chronically unhappy.
- **The most powerful common denominator of happiness**: "Having a strong sense of controlling one's life is a more dependable predictor of positive feelings of wellbeing than any of the objective conditions of life we have considered."
- More than salary, house size, job prestige. **Control over what you do, when you want to, with the people you want to** is the broadest lifestyle variable that makes people happy.
- **Money's greatest intrinsic value**: its ability to give you control over your time. Independence and autonomy from unspent assets give you greater control over what you can do and when.

### The ladder of financial freedom
- Small amount of wealth = ability to take a few days off work when sick without breaking the bank
- A bit more = waiting for a good job instead of taking the first one after being laid off
- Six months' emergency expenses = not being terrified of your boss
- More still = taking a lower-paying but flexible job, shorter commute, dealing with medical emergencies without financial worry
- Then = retiring when you want to, instead of when you need to

### Housel's investment banking internship
- Throughout college, wanted to be an investment banker — they made a lot of money. Got a summer internship at an investment bank in LA. On his first day he realized why they made so much: they work longer and more controlled hours than any human could handle. Going home before midnight was a luxury. "If you don't come to work on Saturday, don't bother coming back on Sunday."
- The job was intellectually stimulating, paid well, made him feel important. But every waking second was a slave to his boss's demands. It was a four-month internship. He lasted a month.
- "The hardest thing about this was that I loved the work. And I wanted to work hard. But doing something you love on a schedule you can't control can feel the same as doing something you hate."

### Reactance
- **Jonah Berger** (UPenn marketing professor): "People like to feel like they're in control — in the driver's seat. When we try to get them to do something, they feel disempowered... So they say no or do something else, even when they might have originally been happy to go along."
- Aligning money toward a life that lets you do what you want, when you want, with who you want, for as long as you want, has incredible return.

### Derek Sivers story
- **Derek Sivers** (successful entrepreneur) was asked about how he got rich. His story: day job in midtown Manhattan paying $20K/year. Never ate out, never took a taxi. Cost of living $1,000/month, earning $1,800/month. Saved $12,000 in two years by age 22. Then quit to become a full-time musician — "I was free." His friend wanted to hear about selling his company for millions, but Sivers said the real difference was at 22, when he got freedom.

### Wealth paradox — richer but not happier
- US is richest nation in history, but little evidence Americans are happier than in the 1950s when wealth/income were much lower (median adjusted for inflation).
- 2019 Gallup poll (150,000 people in 140 countries): 45% of Americans felt "a lot of worry" the previous day (global average: 39%). 55% of Americans felt "a lot of stress" (rest of world: 35%).
- We've used greater wealth to buy bigger and better stuff, but simultaneously given up more control over our time. At best, those things cancel each other out.
- Median family income (inflation-adjusted): $29,000 in 1955 → $62,000 in 2019. Median American home: 983 sq ft (1950) → 2,436 sq ft (2018). But what's happened to our time barely looks like progress.

### Knowledge work and the Rockefeller paradox
- **Rockefeller** was a recluse who spent most of his time alone, thinking. In 1870, 46% of jobs were agriculture, 35% crafts or manufacturing (economist **Robert Gordon**). You labored — work was visible, tangible, and ended when you left.
- Today: 38% of jobs are "managers, officials, and professionals." Another 41% are service jobs relying on your thoughts. Our days don't end when we clock out — we're constantly working in our heads.
- **Derek Thompson** (*The Atlantic*): "If the operating equipment of the 21st century is a portable device, this means the modern factory is not a place at all. It is the day itself."
- Control over your time has diminished compared to generations prior. Since it's a key happiness influencer, we shouldn't be surprised people don't feel much happier even though we are richer.

### Karl Pillemer — 30 Lessons for Living
- Gerontologist **Karl Pillemer** interviewed a thousand elderly Americans for their most important life lessons:
  - No one — not a single person out of a thousand — said to work as hard as you can to buy the things you want
  - No one said it's important to be at least as wealthy as the people around you
  - No one said to choose your work based on desired future earning power
- What they valued: quality friendships, being part of something bigger, spending quality unstructured time with their children. "Your kids don't want your money (or what your money buys) anywhere near as much as they want you. Specifically, they want you with them."
- **Controlling your time is the highest dividend money pays.**

## Chapter 8: Man in the Car Paradox

**Epigraph**: "No one is impressed with your possessions as much as you are."

- As a valet, Housel drove Ferraris, Lamborghinis, Rolls-Royces. He dreamed of having one because they sent a signal: you're smart, rich, important. *Look at me.*
- The irony: he rarely if ever looked at the **drivers**. When you see someone driving a nice car, you rarely think "that guy is cool." You think "if *I* had that car, people would think *I'm* cool."
- **The paradox**: people want wealth to signal that they should be liked and admired. But in reality others bypass admiring you — they use your wealth as a benchmark for their own desire to be liked and admired.
- Letter to his son: "You might think you want an expensive car, a fancy watch, and a huge house. But I'm telling you, you don't. What you want is respect and admiration from other people, and you think having expensive stuff will bring it. It almost never does — especially from the people you want to respect and admire you."
- If respect and admiration are your goal, be careful how you seek it. **Humility, kindness, and empathy** will bring you more respect than horsepower ever will.

## Chapter 9: Wealth is What You Don't See

**Epigraph**: "Spending money to show people how much money you have is the fastest way to have less money."

### Roger and the Porsche
- As a valet in mid-2000s LA, appearance took precedence over everything but oxygen. Many Ferrari/Porsche owners were mediocre successes who spent a huge percentage of their paycheck on a car.
- "Roger" drove a Porsche — enough for people to draw assumptions. Then one day Roger arrived in an old Honda. Porsche was repossessed after defaulting on his car loan. Not a morsel of shame. LA is full of Rogers.
- Someone driving a $100,000 car might be wealthy. But the only data point you have is that they have $100,000 less than they did before (or $100,000 more in debt).
- We judge wealth by what we see (cars, homes, Instagram). But wealth is what you don't see.

### Rich vs. wealthy
- **Rihanna** nearly went bankrupt after overspending. Her financial advisor: "Was it really necessary to tell her that if you spend money on things, you will end up with the things and not the money?"
- When most people say they want to be a millionaire, they mean "I'd like to spend a million dollars." That is literally the opposite of being a millionaire.
- **Bill Mann** (investor): "There is no faster way to feel rich than to spend lots of money on really nice things. But the way to be rich is to not spend money you have, and to not spend money you don't have. It's really that simple."
- **Rich** = current income. Someone driving a $100,000 car is almost certainly rich (needs income to afford the payment). It's not hard to spot rich people — they go out of their way to make themselves known.
- **Wealth** = hidden. It's income not spent. It's an option not yet taken to buy something later. Its value lies in offering options, flexibility, and growth to one day purchase more stuff than you could right now.
- Exercise analogy (**Bill Bryson**, *The Body*): people overestimate calories burned in a workout by 4x, then consume 2x as many calories as they burned. Exercise is like being rich — "I did the work and I now deserve to treat myself to a big meal." Wealth is turning down that treat meal and actually burning net calories.
- It's easy to find rich role models. It's harder to find wealthy ones because their success is hidden by definition. Ronald Read was nobody's financial role model while alive — every penny of his wealth was hidden, even to those who knew him.
- The world is filled with people who look modest but are actually wealthy, and people who look rich but live at the razor's edge of insolvency.

## Chapter 10: Save Money

**Epigraph**: "The only factor you can control generates one of the only things that matters. How wonderful."

### Building wealth = savings rate, not income or returns
- Past a certain level of income, three groups: those who save, those who don't think they can save, and those who don't think they need to save. This chapter is for the latter two.
- **1970s oil crisis analogy**: the world looked like it was running out of oil. We didn't run out — not mainly because we found more oil, but because we started building cars, factories, and homes that are more energy efficient. US uses 60% less energy per dollar of GDP than in 1950. Average miles per gallon doubled since 1975. A 1989 Ford Taurus averaged 18.0 MPG; a 2019 Chevy Suburban (absurdly large SUV) averages 18.1 MPG.
- The world grew its "energy wealth" not by increasing supply but by decreasing demand. Conservation and efficiency mattered more than production.
- Same with money: **investment returns can make you rich, but whether an investing strategy will work and for how long is shrouded in uncertainty. Personal savings and frugality are parts of the money equation that are more in your control and have a 100% chance of being as effective in the future as they are today.**
- You can build wealth without a high income, but you have no chance of building wealth without a high savings rate.

### The value of wealth is relative to what you need
- If two people have the same net worth but one needs half as much to be happy, the person who needs less is better off despite the same portfolio — getting more benefit from investments despite lower returns.
- Learning to be happy with less creates a gap between what you have and what you want — similar to the gap from growing your paycheck, but easier and more in your control.
- Think about how much effort goes into 0.1% of annual investment outperformance (millions of hours, tens of billions of dollars) vs. how easy it is to exploit 2-3 percentage points of lifestyle bloat.

### Savings = the gap between your ego and your income
- Past a certain level of materialism, spending beyond a pretty low level is mostly a reflection of ego approaching income — spending money to show people you have money.
- **One of the most powerful ways to increase your savings isn't to raise your income. It's to raise your humility.**
- People with enduring personal finance success tend to not give a damn what others think about them.

### You don't need a specific reason to save
- Saving for a downpayment, car, or retirement is great. But saving does not require a specific goal.
- **Save just for saving's sake.** Only saving for specific goals makes sense in a predictable world. Ours isn't. Saving is a hedge against life's inevitable ability to surprise the hell out of you at the worst possible moment.
- Savings not attached to a spending goal give you options, flexibility, the ability to wait for opportunities. Every bit of savings is like taking a point in the future that would have been owned by someone else and giving it back to yourself.

### Flexibility as an unseen return on wealth
- What is the return on cash in the bank that gives you the option of changing careers, retiring early, or freedom from worry? Incalculable — so large and important we can't put a price on it, and since we can't measure it like interest rates, we tend to overlook it.
- Savings at 0% interest might actually generate an extraordinary return if they give you flexibility to take a lower-salary job with more purpose, or wait for investment opportunities.

### The hidden return is becoming more important
- 100+ years ago: 75% of Americans had neither telephones nor regular mail service (**Robert Gordon**). Competition was hyper-local — average intelligence might be the best in your town.
- Now the talent pool spans the globe. **Marc Andreesen**: "software eats the world." 600 people ace the SATs each year, 7,000 come within a handful of points. Intelligence is not a reliable advantage in a hyper-connected world.
- But **flexibility is**. In a world where intelligence is hyper-competitive and technical skills get automated, competitive advantages tilt toward nuanced soft skills — communication, empathy, and most of all, flexibility.
- Having more control over your time and options is becoming one of the most valuable currencies in the world.

## Chapter 11: Reasonable > Rational

**Epigraph**: "Aiming to be mostly reasonable works better than trying to be coldly rational."

- You're not a spreadsheet. You're a person. A screwed up, emotional person.
- Do not aim to be coldly rational when making financial decisions. Aim to just be pretty reasonable. Reasonable is more realistic and you have a better chance of sticking with it for the long run.

### Julius Wagner-Jauregg — fever and malariotherapy
- **Julius Wagner-Jauregg**: 19th-century psychiatrist who specialized in severe neurosyphilis (then a fatal diagnosis with no known treatment). Noticed that syphilis patients tended to recover if they had prolonged fevers from an unrelated ailment.
- Began injecting patients with low-end strains of typhoid, malaria, and smallpox to trigger fevers strong enough to kill off syphilis. Some patients died from the treatment. He settled on a weak version of malaria (countered with quinine after a few days of fever).
- 6 in 10 syphilis patients treated with "malariotherapy" recovered, vs. 3 in 10 left alone. Won the **Nobel Prize in medicine in 1927**.
- Fevers have always been feared — ancient Romans worshiped **Febris**, the Goddess who protected from fevers. But Wagner-Jauregg was onto something: fevers DO play a role in fighting infection. A one-degree increase in body temperature slows replication of some viruses by a factor of 200.
- Yet fevers are still universally treated as bad — eliminated with Tylenol. Despite millions of years of evolution as a defense mechanism, no parent, no doctor, and no drug company views fever as anything but a misfortune.
- Why? Because fevers hurt. People don't want to hurt. That's it. A doctor's goal is not just to cure disease — it's to cure disease within the confines of what's reasonable and tolerable to the patient.
- **It may be rational to want a fever if you have an infection. But it's not reasonable.** That philosophy — aiming to be reasonable instead of rational — is what more people should consider with money.

### Harry Markowitz — minimizing future regret
- **Harry Markowitz** won the Nobel Prize for exploring the mathematical tradeoff between risk and return. When asked how he invested his own money in the 1950s: "I visualized my grief if the stock market went way up and I wasn't in it — or if it went way down and I was completely in it. My intention was to minimize future regret. So I split my contributions 50/50 between bonds and equities."
- Not rational. Not irrational. **Reasonable.** Investing has a social component — you make decisions surrounded by co-workers, spouse, brother-in-law, your own personal doubts. "Minimizing future regret" is hard to rationalize on paper but easy to justify in real life.
- **Jason Zweig** reflected: "People are neither rational nor irrational. We are human."

### Yale leveraged retirement study — rational but unreasonable
- 2008 Yale study argued young savers should use 2:1 margin (two dollars of debt for every dollar of their own) when buying stocks, tapering as they age. Math works on paper — even if wiped out by a 50% market drop, savers would be better off in the long run if they picked themselves back up and kept saving.
- "The expected retirement wealth is 90% higher compared to life-cycle funds." It is also **100% less reasonable.** No normal person could watch 100% of their retirement evaporate and carry on unfazed.

### Love your investments
- If lacking emotions about your strategy makes you walk away when things get difficult, rational thinking becomes a liability. Reasonable investors who love their technically imperfect strategies have an edge — they're more likely to stick with those strategies.
- Historical odds of making money in US markets: 50/50 over one-day periods, 68% over one-year, 88% over 10-year, and (so far) **100% over 20-year periods.** Anything that keeps you in the game has a quantifiable advantage.
- **Home bias**: people prefer investing in companies from their own country — not rational (ignores 95%+ of the planet), but reasonable if familiarity helps you take the leap of faith required to stay invested.
- **Day trading and individual stocks**: not rational for most, but reasonable in small amounts if they scratch an itch hard enough to leave the rest of your diversified investments alone. **Josh Brown**: "I'm not buying individual stocks because I think I'm going to generate alpha... I just love stocks and have ever since I was 20 years old. And it's my money, I get to do whatever."
- **Jack Bogle** — the man who said high-fee funds violate "the humble rules of arithmetic" — invested some of his own money in his son's high-fee hedge fund and mutual fund. Explanation? "We do some things for family reasons... If it's not consistent, well, life isn't always consistent."

## Chapter 12: Surprise!

**Epigraph**: "History is the study of change, ironically used as a map of the future."

- **Scott Sagan** (Stanford professor): "Things that have never happened before happen all the time."
- History is mostly the study of surprising events, yet investors and economists use it as an unassailable guide to the future. History helps calibrate our expectations and offers a rough guide of what tends to work, but it is not, in any way, a map of the future.
- **"Historians as prophets" fallacy**: overreliance on past data as a signal to future conditions in a field where innovation and change are the lifeblood of progress.
- If you view investing as a hard science, history should be a perfect guide. But investing is a massive group of people making imperfect decisions with limited information about things that will massively impact their wellbeing — making them nervous, greedy, and paranoid.
- **Richard Feynman**: "Imagine how much harder physics would be if electrons had feelings." Investors have feelings. That's why it's hard to predict what they'll do next based on what they did in the past.
- **Bill Bonner** on Mr. Market: "He's got a 'Capitalism at Work' T-shirt on and a sledgehammer in his hand." Few things stay the same for very long — can't treat historians as prophets.

### Two dangers of relying too heavily on history:

**1. You'll likely miss the outlier events that move the needle the most.**
- 15 billion people born in 19th and 20th centuries. But imagine how different the world would be if just 7 never existed: Hitler, Stalin, Mao Zedong, Gavrilo Princip, Thomas Edison, Bill Gates, Martin Luther King. 0.00000000004% of people were responsible for perhaps the majority of the world's direction over the last century.
- Same for events: The Great Depression, WWII, Manhattan Project, Vaccines, Antibiotics, ARPANET, 9/11, Fall of the Soviet Union — billions/trillions of events in the 20th century, but those eight alone impacted the world orders of magnitude more.
- 9/11 → Fed cut rates → housing bubble → financial crisis → poor job market → tens of millions sought college → $1.6 trillion in student loans with 10.8% default rate. It's not intuitive to link 19 hijackers to student loans, but that's what happens in a world driven by outlier tail events.
- The most common plot of economic history is the role of surprises. The reason surprises occur isn't that our models are wrong or our intelligence is low — it's because the odds that Adolf Hitler's parents argued on the evening nine months before he was born were the same as them conceiving a child.
- The forecaster who assumes the worst/best events of the past will match the future is not following history — they're accidentally assuming the history of unprecedented events doesn't apply to the future.
- **Nassim Taleb** (*Fooled by Randomness*): In Pharaonic Egypt, scribes tracked the Nile's high-water mark as a worst-case estimate. Fukushima was built to withstand the worst past historical earthquake — the builders didn't imagine much worse, and didn't think the worst past event had to be a surprise (since it had no precedent).
- **Daniel Kahneman**: "The correct lesson to learn from surprises is that the world is surprising."
- **The correct lesson**: not to use past surprises as a guide to future boundaries; to use them as an admission that we have no idea what might happen next.

**2. History can be a misleading guide because it doesn't account for structural changes relevant to today's world.**
- 401(k) is 42 years old. Roth IRA created in the 1990s. Venture capital barely existed 25 years ago. **Phil Knight** (Nike founder): "There was no such thing as venture capital. An aspiring young entrepreneur had very few places to turn, and those places were all guarded by risk-averse gatekeepers with zero imagination. In other words, bankers."
- S&P 500 didn't include financial stocks until 1976. Technology stocks were virtually nonexistent 50 years ago. Accounting rules, disclosures, auditing, market liquidity — all changed.
- Average time between US recessions has grown from ~2 years (late 1800s) to 5 years (early 20th century) to 8 years (last half-century). 12 years between last recession (Dec 2007) and when Housel was writing — longest gap since before the Civil War.

### Benjamin Graham's formulas stopped working
- Graham's *The Intelligent Investor* gives practical formulas. Housel read it as a teenager and found them appealing — step-by-step instructions on how to get rich. But few of the formulas actually work anymore.
- Graham advocated buying stocks trading for less than their net working assets — few stocks trade that cheaply anymore (except penny stocks accused of fraud). His criterion to avoid stocks above 1.5x book value would have left you owning almost nothing but insurance and bank stocks for the last decade.
- Graham was a wildly successful investor himself, but he was practical and contrarian — not wedded to investing ideas that became too popular. In each revised edition of *The Intelligent Investor*, Graham discarded the previous edition's formulas and replaced them with new ones.
- **Jason Zweig** (annotator): "One of the common criticisms of Graham is that all the formulas in the 1972 edition are antiquated. The only proper response to this criticism is to say: 'Of course they are! They are the ones he used to replace the formulas in the 1965 edition, which replaced the formulas in the 1954 edition...'"
- Just before he died, Graham was asked whether detailed stock analysis remained a strategy he favored: "In general, no. I am no longer an advocate of elaborate techniques of security analysis... This was a rewarding activity, say, 40 years ago, when our textbook was first published. But the situation has changed a great deal since then."
- Competition grew, technology made information accessible, economy shifted from industrial to technology sectors.
- **The further back in history you look, the more likely you're examining a world that no longer applies to today.** Recent history is often the best guide to the future because it includes conditions relevant to today.
- **John Templeton**: "The four most dangerous words in investing are, 'it's different this time.'" But Templeton admitted it IS different at least 20% of the time. Those changes are what matter most over time. **Michael Batnick**: "The twelve most dangerous words in investing are, 'The four most dangerous words in investing are, it's different this time.'"
- Don't ignore history — but the further back you look, the more general your takeaways should be. General things like people's relationship to greed and fear, how they behave under stress, how they respond to incentives — those are stable in time. Specific trends, trades, sectors, causal relationships are always evolving. **Historians are not prophets.**

## Chapter 13: Room for Error

**Epigraph**: "The most important part of every plan is planning on your plan not going according to plan."

### Blackjack card counting as a financial metaphor
- A tiny group of blackjack card counters can teach something extraordinarily important about managing money: the importance of room for error.
- Blackjack card counting: you can't know what card comes next, but by tracking dealt cards you can calculate odds. Bet more when odds favor you, less when they don't.
- **Kevin Lewis** (*Bringing Down the House*): "Although card counting is statistically proven to work, it does not guarantee you will win every hand... We must make sure that we have enough money to withstand any swings of bad luck. A rule of thumb is that you should have at least a hundred basic units." With $10K, play a $100 unit.
- There is never a moment when you're so right that you can bet every chip. You have to give yourself room for error. You have to plan on your plan not going according to plan.

### Benjamin Graham on margin of safety
- Graham: "The purpose of the margin of safety is to render the forecast unnecessary."
- Margin of safety is the only effective way to safely navigate a world governed by odds, not certainties. Pursuing the grey area — where a range of potential outcomes are acceptable — is the smart way to proceed.
- People underestimate room for error: stock analysts give price targets not ranges, pundits who speak in unshakable certainties gain larger followings than those who say "we can't know for sure."
- Harvard psychologist **Max Bazerman**: when analyzing others' home renovations, people estimate 25-50% over budget. For their own projects, they estimate on-time and on-budget.

### Two things cause people to avoid room for error:
1. The idea that somebody must know what the future holds (uncomfortable admitting the opposite)
2. The feeling that not taking actions based on an accurate view of the future is doing yourself harm

### Room for error is not conservative — it's the opposite
- Room for error lets you endure a range of outcomes and stick around long enough for low-probability outcomes to fall in your favor. The biggest gains occur infrequently. The person with cash (room for error) to endure hardship in one part of their strategy has an edge over the person who gets wiped out.
- **Bill Gates** (Microsoft, early days): "Came up with this incredibly conservative approach that I wanted to have enough money in the bank to pay a year's worth of payroll even if we didn't get any payments coming in."
- **Buffett** (2008): "I have pledged — to you, the rating agencies and myself — to always run Berkshire with more than ample cash... When forced to choose, I will not trade even a night's sleep for the chance of extra profits."

### Volatility — the gap between technical and emotional
- Can you survive your assets declining by 30%? On a spreadsheet, maybe yes. But mentally? A 30% decline can shatter your confidence at the worst moment. You — or your spouse — may decide it's time for a new plan. "Spreadsheets are not good at modeling how you'll feel when you tuck your kids in at night wondering if the investment decisions you've made were a mistake that will hurt their future."
- Gap between what you can technically endure vs. what's emotionally possible = an overlooked version of room for error.

### Retirement savings — assume ⅓ lower returns
- US stock market has returned an annual average of 6.8% after inflation since the 1870s. Reasonable to use as a baseline for retirement planning. But what if future returns are lower? Or your target retirement date falls in a brutal bear market?
- Housel's approach: assume future returns will be ⅓ lower than the historic average. So he saves more than he would if he assumed the future resembles the past. That's his margin of safety. The future may be worse than ⅓ lower — but no margin of safety offers a 100% guarantee. A one-third buffer is enough to sleep well at night. "The best way to achieve felicity is to aim low," says **Charlie Munger**.

### Russian roulette syndrome — optimism bias in risk-taking
- "You can be risk loving and yet completely averse to ruin." — **Nassim Taleb**
- You have to take risk to get ahead, but no risk that can wipe you out is ever worth taking. The odds of Russian roulette are in your favor. But the downside is not worth the potential upside. There is no margin of safety that can compensate for the risk.
- If something has 95% odds of being right, the 5% odds of being wrong will almost certainly hit you at some point in your life. If the cost of the downside is ruin, the upside the other 95% isn't worth the risk.

### Leverage is the devil
- Leverage pushes routine risks into something capable of producing ruin. Rational optimism masks the odds of ruin most of the time. Housing prices fell 30% — that's capitalism, it happens. But those with high leverage had a double wipeout: left broke AND erased every opportunity to get back in at the moment opportunity was ripe. A homeowner wiped out in 2009 had no chance of cheap mortgage rates in 2010. Lehman Brothers had no chance of investing in cheap debt in 2009.
- Housel: "I take risks with one portion and am terrified with the other. This is not inconsistent, but the psychology of money would lead you to believe that it is. I just want to ensure I can remain standing long enough for my risks to pay off."

### Stalingrad and the field mice — unknown unknowns
- Battle of Stalingrad, late 1942: a German tank unit sat in reserve on grasslands. When desperately needed at the front lines, almost none of 104 tanks were operable. Engineers found the issue: "During the weeks of inactivity behind the front lines, field mice had nested inside the vehicles and eaten away insulation covering the electrical systems." The most sophisticated equipment in the world, defeated by mice.
- In 2006 **Buffett** announced a search for his eventual replacement — someone "genetically programmed to recognize and avoid serious risks, including those never before encountered."
- Housel's firm **Collaborative Fund** portfolio companies have dealt with: water pipes flooding an office, a company broken into three times, a company kicked out of its manufacturing plant, a store shut down because a customer called the health department over a dog, a CEO's email spoofed mid-fundraise, a founder's mental breakdown.

### Avoiding single points of failure
- Everything that can break will eventually break. If many things rely on one thing working, and that thing breaks, you're counting down to catastrophe. Modern jets have four redundant electrical systems. Suspension bridges can lose many cables without falling.
- **The biggest single point of failure with money: sole reliance on a paycheck to fund short-term spending needs, with no savings gap.**
- Save for things you can't possibly predict or even comprehend — the financial equivalent of field mice.
- "In fact, the most important part of every plan is planning on your plan not going according to plan."

## Chapter 14: You'll Change

**Epigraph**: "Long-term planning is harder than it seems because people's goals and desires change over time."

### The friend who became a doctor
- A friend from no privilege or natural intellect, but the hardest-working guy Housel knew. Life's mission: become a doctor. Odds stacked against him — no reasonable person would consider it a possibility. But he pushed through, a decade older than his classmates, and became a doctor.
- How much fulfillment comes from bulldozing from nothing to the top of medical school? A few years later, Housel called him: "Awful career, man." The stress and hours had worn him into the ground. He was as disappointed in where he was today as he was driven toward where he wanted to be 15 years ago.
- **People are poor forecasters of their future selves.** Imagining a goal is easy and fun. Imagining it in the context of realistic life stresses is entirely different.

### The tractor-to-lawyer-to-parent trajectory
- Every five-year-old wants to drive a tractor. Then as a teenager they want to be a lawyer — their plan is set. Then as a lawyer they face crushing hours and rarely see their family. They take a lower-paying job with flexible hours. Then realize childcare is so expensive they become a stay-at-home parent. At 70, they realize a lifetime of staying home means they're unprepared for retirement.
- Only 27% of college grads have a job related to their major (Federal Reserve). 29% of stay-at-home parents have a college degree.
- Long-term financial planning is essential, but things change — both the world around you and your own goals and desires. It's one thing to say "we don't know what the future holds." It's another to admit that you, yourself, don't know today what you will even want in the future.

### The End of History Illusion
- **Daniel Gilbert** (Harvard psychologist): tendency for people to be keenly aware of how much they've changed in the past, but to underestimate how much their personalities, desires, and goals will change in the future.
- "All of us are walking around with an illusion — an illusion that history, our personal history, has just come to an end, that we have just recently become the people that we were always meant to be and will be for the rest of our lives." Research shows people from age 18 to 68 underestimate how much they will change.
- **Charlie Munger**: the first rule of compounding is to never interrupt it unnecessarily. But how do you not interrupt a money plan when what you want out of life changes? Part of why Ronald Read and Warren Buffett succeeded is they kept doing the same thing for decades, letting compounding run wild. But many of us evolve so much over a lifetime that we don't want to keep doing the same thing for decades.
- Rather than one 80-year financial lifespan, our money has perhaps four distinct 20-year blocks.

### Two things to keep in mind:
1. **Avoid the extreme ends of financial planning.** Assuming you'll be happy with very low income, or choosing to work endless hours in pursuit of a high one, increases the odds of future regret. The fuel of the End of History Illusion is that people adapt to most circumstances, so the benefits of extremes (simplicity of having hardly anything, thrill of having almost everything) wear off. But the downsides — not affording retirement, or looking back at a life spent chasing dollars — become enduring regrets. Compounding works best with years or decades to grow. Balance at every point in your working life — moderate savings, moderate free time, moderate commute, moderate time with family — increases the odds of being able to stick with a plan.
2. **Accept the reality of changing our minds.** Some of the most miserable workers are people loyal to a career only because it's the field they picked when deciding on a college major at age 18. Accept the End of History Illusion — the odds of picking a job at 18 that you'll still enjoy when you qualify for Social Security are low. The trick is to accept the reality of change and move on as soon as possible.

### Kahneman and sunk costs
- **Jason Zweig** worked with **Daniel Kahneman** on *Thinking, Fast and Slow*. Zweig was amazed by Kahneman's ability to "detonate what we had just done" — sending utterly transformed versions that began differently, ended differently, incorporated new research. When Zweig asked how he could start again as if they had never written an earlier draft, Kahneman said: **"I have no sunk costs."**
- Sunk costs — anchoring decisions to past efforts that can't be refunded — are a devil in a world where people change over time. They make our future selves prisoners to our past, different selves. It's the equivalent of a stranger making major life decisions for you.
- Embracing the idea that financial goals made when you were a different person should be abandoned without mercy can be a good strategy to minimize future regret. The quicker it's done, the sooner you can get back to compounding.

## Chapter 15: Nothing's Free

**Epigraph**: "Everything has a price, but not all prices appear on labels."

### GE — the price of avoiding the price
- **General Electric** was the largest company in the world in 2004, worth a third of a trillion dollars. Then everything fell to pieces. The 2008 crisis sent GE's financing division (more than half the company's profits) into chaos. Oil and energy bets were disasters. Stock fell from $40 (2007) to $7 (2018).
- **Jeff Immelt** (CEO since 2001) was harshly criticized. When he stepped down in 2017, he told his successor: **"Every job looks easy when you're not the one doing it."**
- S&P 500 increased 119-fold in the 50 years ending 2018. But "hold stocks for the long run" is easier said than done when stocks are collapsing.

### The price of market returns
- Successful investing demands a price. Its currency is not dollars and cents — **it's volatility, fear, doubt, uncertainty, and regret** — all easy to overlook until you're dealing with them in real time.
- Dow Jones returned about 11% per year from 1950 to 2019 (including dividends). But the shaded periods when the index was at least 5% below its previous all-time high cover most of the chart. **This is the price of market returns.** The fee. The cost of admission. And it hurts.
- Netflix returned more than 35,000% from 2002 to 2018, but traded below its previous all-time high on 94% of days. Monster Beverage returned 319,000% from 1995 to 2018 — among the highest returns in history — but traded below its previous high 95% of the time.

### Three options — pay, find cheaper, steal
- Like buying a car: 1) pay the price, 2) find a cheaper alternative (used car), or 3) try to steal it. Many investors choose the equivalent of option 3 — tricks and strategies to get the return without paying the price of volatility. Trading in and out, trying to sell before recessions and buy before booms.
- **Morningstar** looked at 112 tactical mutual funds (which switch between stocks/bonds to capture returns while avoiding downside). Only 9 had better risk-adjusted returns than a simple 60/40 stock-bond fund. Less than a quarter had smaller drawdowns than the leave-it-alone index.
- Average equity fund investor underperformed the funds they invested in by half a percent per year (Morningstar) — the result of buying and selling when they should have just bought and held.
- **By trying to avoid the price, investors end up paying double.**

### GE under Jack Welch — the illusion of smoothness
- Under **Jack Welch**, GE became famous for beating Wall Street's quarterly earnings estimates. Welch would deliver $0.26 when analysts expected $0.25, no matter what — by massaging numbers, pulling future gains into current quarters. *Forbes* reported GE "sold" locomotives to unnamed financial partners in transactions that left most risk with GE.
- Welch wrote in *Straight from the Gut* that during crises, business leaders "immediately offered to pitch in to cover the [earnings] gap. Some said they could find an extra $10 million, $20 million, and even $30 million from their business to offset the surprise."
- Shareholders got consistency and predictability — a stock that surged year after year without the surprises of uncertainty. Then the bill came due. GE shareholders suffered through a decade of mammoth losses previously shielded by accounting maneuvers. The penny gains of Welch's era became dime losses.
- **Freddie Mac and Fannie Mae** were caught *under-reporting* current earnings by billions — spreading gains out over future periods to give investors the illusion of smoothness and predictability.

### Why people try to avoid the price
- The price of investing success isn't immediately obvious. It's not a price tag you can see, so when the bill comes due it doesn't feel like a fee for something good — **it feels like a fine for doing something wrong**. Fees are acceptable; fines are to be avoided.
- **Thinking of market volatility as a fee rather than a fine** is an important part of developing the mindset that lets you stick around long enough for investing gains to work in your favor.
- Disneyland tickets cost $100 — but you get an awesome day with your kids. 18 million people thought that fee was worth paying last year. Few felt it was a punishment. Same with investing: **volatility is almost always a fee, not a fine.**
- Market returns are never free and never will be. You're not forced to pay this fee, just like you're not forced to go to Disneyland. But you'll usually get what you pay for.
- **The trick is convincing yourself that the market's fee is worth it.** That's the only way to properly deal with volatility — not just putting up with it, but realizing it's an admission fee worth paying.

## Chapter 16: You & Me

**Epigraph**: "Beware taking financial cues from people playing a different game than you are."

### Bubbles — not greed, but different games
- Dot-com bubble reduced household wealth by $6.2 trillion. Housing bubble cut away more than $8 trillion. Bubbles ruin lives.
- The common answer is greed. But remember chapter 1: no one is crazy. Blaming bubbles on greed misses important lessons about how and why people rationalize what in hindsight look like greedy decisions.
- Bubbles are hard to learn from because they're not like cancer (where a biopsy gives clear warning) — they're like the rise and fall of a political party (outcome known in hindsight, causes never agreed upon).

### The notion that assets have one rational price is flawed
- How much should you pay for Google stock today? Depends on who "you" are. 30-year horizon → discounted cash flows. 10-year → tech industry's potential. 1-year → product sales cycles and whether we'll have a bear market. Day trader → "who cares?" — just trying to squeeze a few bucks between now and lunchtime.
- When investors have different goals and time horizons, prices that look ridiculous to one person can make sense to another.

### Bubbles form when time horizons shrink
- Momentum of short-term returns attracts enough money that the makeup of investors shifts from mostly long-term to mostly short-term. As traders push up short-term returns, they attract even more traders. Before long, the dominant market price-setters are those with shorter time horizons.
- **Bubbles aren't so much about valuations rising. They're a symptom of time horizons shrinking** as more short-term traders enter the playing field.

### Dot-com bubble
- Common to call it a time of irrational optimism. But one of the most common headlines was record trading volume — investors buying and selling in a *single day*. Average mutual fund had 120% annual turnover in 1999 — thinking about the next ten months at most.
- **Maggie Mahar** (*Bull!*): "By the mid-nineties, the press had replaced annual scorecards with reports that appeared every three months. The change spurred investors to chase performance, rushing to buy the funds at the top of the charts, just when they were most expensive."

### Housing bubble
- Hard to justify $700,000 for a two-bedroom Florida tract home for the next 10 years. But makes perfect sense if you plan on flipping it in a few months into a rising market. **Attom** data: homes flipped (sold more than once in 12 months) rose fivefold during the bubble — from 20,000 in Q1 2000 to over 100,000 in Q1 2004. Flipping plunged to under 40,000 per quarter after the bubble burst.

### The real damage: long-term investors taking cues from short-term traders
- Cisco rose 300% in 1999 to $60/share, valued at $600 billion (implying growth rate larger than the entire US economy within 20 years, per **Burton Malkiel**). Day-traders didn't care — they planned to sell by end of day. But if you were a long-term investor, $60 was the only price available. You might have thought "these other investors know something I don't" and went along.
- What you don't realize is that the traders setting the marginal price were playing a completely different game than you.
- **Rising prices persuade all investors in ways the best marketers envy.** They are a drug that can turn value-conscious investors into dewy-eyed optimists, detached from their own reality by the actions of someone playing a different game.
- Consumer spending is also socially driven — subtly influenced by people you admire, done because you subtly want people to admire you. But you don't get to see their goals, worries, and aspirations. A young lawyer's lifestyle spending might look similar to a writer's, but they're playing completely different games.

### Identify what game you're playing
- Few things matter more with money than understanding your own time horizon and not being persuaded by the actions of people playing different games.
- Housel: "I am a passive investor optimistic in the world's ability to generate real economic growth and I'm confident that over the next 30 years that growth will accrue to my investments." Once you write that mission statement, everything unrelated — market this year, recession next year — is part of a game he's not playing.

## Chapter 17: The Seduction of Pessimism

**Epigraph**: "Optimism sounds like a sales pitch. Pessimism sounds like someone trying to help you."

- **Deirdre McCloskey** (historian): "For reasons I have never understood, people like to hear that the world is going to hell."
- Optimism is the best bet for most people because the world tends to get better for most people most of the time. But pessimism holds a special place: it's not just more common than optimism, it also sounds smarter, is more intellectually captivating, and it's paid more attention.
- **Real optimism** is not complacency (believing everything will be great). It's a belief that the odds of a good outcome are in your favor over time, even when there will be setbacks. **Hans Rosling**: "I am not an optimist. I am a very serious possibilist."

### Igor Panarin — pessimism on the front page
- December 29, 2008 — worst year for the economy in modern history. WSJ ran a front-page article on Russian professor **Igor Panarin** who predicted the US would break into six pieces by mid-2010 (Alaska reverting to Russia, California to China, Texas to Mexico, etc.).
- This was on the front page of the world's most prestigious financial newspaper — not a tinfoil-hat newsletter.
- Yet the mirror opposite — outrageous optimism — is rarely taken seriously. Imagine a Japanese academic in the late 1940s (economy gutted by war, famine limiting food to 800 calories/day) predicting: economy 15x larger, life expectancy nearly doubled, stock market producing returns like no country in history, unemployment never topping 6% for 40+ years, world leader in electronics and management. They would have been laughed out of the room. But that's *what actually happened* in Japan in the generation after the war.

### Why pessimism is more persuasive
- Tell someone everything will be great → shrug or skepticism. Tell someone they're in danger → undivided attention.
- A smart person tells you a stock will rise 10-fold → nonsense. Someone full of nonsense says your stock is about to collapse due to accounting fraud → clear your calendar and listen to every word.
- Investing newsletter industry is populated by prophets of doom despite the stock market going up 17,000-fold in the last century (including dividends).
- **Matt Ridley** (*The Rational Optimist*): "A constant drumbeat of pessimism usually drowns out any triumphalist song... If you say the world has been getting better you may get away with being called naïve and insensitive. If you say the world is going to go on getting better, you are considered embarrassingly mad. If, on the other hand, you say catastrophe is imminent, you may expect a McArthur genius award or even the Nobel Peace Prize."
- **Hans Rosling** (*Factfulness*): "Every group of people I ask thinks the world is more frightening, more violent, and more hopeless — in short, more dramatic — than it really is."
- **John Stuart Mill** (1840s): "I have observed that not the man who hopes when others despair, but the man who despairs when others hope, is admired by a large class of persons as a sage."

### Why pessimism seduces us with money
- **Kahneman**: asymmetric aversion to loss is an evolutionary shield. "Losses loom larger than gains... Organisms that treat threats as more urgent than opportunities have a better chance to survive and reproduce."

### Three specific reasons pessimism is so persuasive:

**1. Money is ubiquitous, so bad things affect everyone and capture everyone's attention.**
- A hurricane in Florida poses no direct risk to 92% of Americans. But a recession could impact every single person. More than half of US households directly own stocks. Even non-stock-owners watch the Dow like a barometer. Stocks rising 1% = briefly mentioned. Stocks falling 1% = reported in bold, all-caps, blood red.
- Only 2.5% of Americans owned stocks before the 1929 crash — but the majority of Americans watched in amazement as the market collapsed, wondering what it signaled about their own fate.
- **Eric Rauchway** (historian): "This fall in value immediately afflicted only a few Americans. But so closely had the others watched the market and regarded it as an index of their fates that they suddenly stopped much of their economic activity."
- Two topics affect your life whether you're interested or not: money and health. Health is individual; money is systemic.

**2. Pessimists often extrapolate present trends without accounting for how markets adapt.**
- **Lester Brown** (2008 environmentalist): "By 2030 China would need 98 million barrels of oil a day. The world is currently producing 85 million barrels a day and may never produce much more than that. There go the world's oil reserves."
- He was technically right — the world would run out at that rate. But that's not how markets work. Iron law of economics: extremely good and extremely bad circumstances rarely stay that way for long because supply and demand adapt in hard-to-predict ways.
- Oil surged from $20/barrel (2001) to $138 (2008). At that price, drilling oil was like pulling gold from the ground. Hard-to-tap supplies became the bonanza of a lifetime. This sparked a surge of **fracking and horizontal drilling** technologies. US oil production went from 5 million barrels/day (2008) to 13 million (2019). World production now over 100 million barrels/day — some 20% above what Brown assumed was the high mark.
- **Daniel Yergin** (oil historian): "86% of oil reserves in the United States are the result not of what is estimated at time of discovery but of the revisions that come when our technology improves."
- Assuming something ugly will stay ugly is an easy forecast. But problems correct and people adapt. Threats incentivize solutions in equal magnitude.

**3. Progress happens too slowly to notice, but setbacks happen too quickly to ignore.**
- There are lots of overnight tragedies. There are rarely overnight miracles.
- January 5, 1889: *Detroit Free Press* said airplanes "appear impossible." Six months later, Orville Wright dropped out of high school to help his brother Wilbur build a printing press. The airplane would become one of the most important inventions of the 20th century.
- After the Wrights conquered flight, nobody cared. **Frederick Lewis Allen** (1952): "Several years went by before the public grasped what the Wrights were doing; people were so convinced that flying was impossible that most of those who saw them flying about Dayton [Ohio] in 1905 decided that what they had seen must be some trick." It took nearly four and a half years after the first flight before experienced reporters were sent to observe.
- Even then, the airplane was underestimated for years: military weapon → rich person's toy → transport for a few people. *Washington Post* (1909): "There will never be such a thing as commercial aerial freighters." First cargo plane took off five months later.
- Compare that slow, years-long awakening to how quickly people pay attention to catastrophe — corporate bankruptcy, major war, plane crash. The first major mentions of the Wright brothers' plane came in 1908 when Army Lieutenant **Thomas Selfridge** was killed during a demonstration flight.
- **Growth is driven by compounding**, which always takes time. **Destruction is driven by single points of failure**, which can happen in seconds, and **loss of confidence**, which can happen in an instant.
- In stock markets: a 40% decline in six months will draw congressional investigations, but a 140% gain over six years can go virtually unnoticed. Reputations take a lifetime to build and a single email to destroy.
- Consider medicine: any single year or decade doesn't show much. But over 50 years, the age-adjusted death rate from heart disease has declined more than 70% (NIH) — enough to save the population of Atlanta every year. Yet since progress was so slow, it captures less attention than quick, sudden losses.

### Stephen Hawking — low expectations as a form of optimism
- 2004 NYT interview: Hawking, paralyzed and unable to talk at age 21, was selling books to lay people. "Are you always this cheerful?" the *Times* asked. **"My expectations were reduced to zero when I was 21. Everything since then has been a bonus."**
- Expecting things to be bad means a best-case scenario that feels flat. Pessimism reduces expectations, narrowing the gap between possible outcomes and outcomes you feel great about.
- Maybe that's why pessimism is so seductive. Expecting things to be bad is the best way to be pleasantly surprised when they're not. Which, ironically, is something to be optimistic about.

## Chapter 18: When You'll Believe Anything

**Epigraph**: "Appealing fictions, and why stories are more powerful than statistics."

### The alien economist thought experiment
- Imagine an alien dispatched to Earth to study the economy, comparing 2007 and 2009. From the air: same office buildings, same factories, same highways, same universities, same patents, same smartphones (better in 2009), same internet connections, same everything. Economy looks the same or better.
- Then he looks at the numbers: US households are $16 trillion poorer. 10 million more are unemployed. Stock market is worth half. Forecasts of economic potential have plunged.
- "I don't get it. Nothing has changed! Why are you poorer? Why are you more pessimistic?"
- **The one change the alien couldn't see: the stories we told ourselves about the economy.** In 2007, we told a story about the stability of housing prices, the prudence of bankers, and the ability of financial markets to price risk. In 2009, we stopped believing that story. That's the only thing that changed. But it made all the difference in the world.
- We had an identical — if not greater — capacity for wealth and growth in 2009 as 2007. Yet the economy suffered its worst hit in 80 years. This is different from Germany 1945 (manufacturing obliterated) or Japan 2000s (shrinking workforce). In 2009 we inflicted *narrative* damage on ourselves.
- **Stories are, by far, the most powerful force in the economy.** They are the fuel that lets the tangible parts work, or the brake that holds our capabilities back.

### Two things to keep in mind about a story-driven world:

**1. The more you want something to be true, the more likely you are to believe a story that overestimates the odds of it being true.**
- Documentary *How to Live Forever* asks a centenarian: "What was the happiest day of your life?" "Armistice Day," she said (end of WWI). "Because we knew there would be no more wars ever again." WWII began 21 years later, killing 75 million people.
- **"Appealing fictions"**: things we think are true because we desperately want them to be true. They happen when you are smart, want to find solutions, but face limited control and high stakes.
- **Ali Hajaji's** son was sick in a Yemeni village. Elders proposed shoving a burning stick through his chest to drain the sickness. Hajaji told the NYT: "When you have no money, and your son is sick, you'll believe anything."
- **Daniel Defoe** on the Great Plague of London (1722): "The people were more addicted to prophecies... the posts of houses and corners of streets were plastered over with doctors' bills and papers of ignorant fellows, quacking and inviting the people to come to them for remedies." The plague killed a quarter of Londoners. You'll believe anything when the stakes are that high.
- 85% of active mutual funds underperformed their benchmark over 10 years ending 2018 — stable for generations. You'd think the industry would struggle. But there's almost $5 trillion invested in these funds. Give someone the chance of investing alongside "the next Warren Buffett" and they'll believe with enough faith to put their life savings behind it.
- **Madoff** — in hindsight the red flags were obvious: returns that never varied, audited by a relatively unknown firm, refused to disclose methods. But he told a good story and people wanted to believe it.
- **The bigger the gap between what you want to be true and what you need to be true to have an acceptable outcome, the more you are protecting yourself from falling victim to an appealing financial fiction.**
- Fed's last 2007 meeting predicted 2008-2009 growth between 1.6% and 2.8%. In reality the economy contracted by more than 2%. It's hard for a policymaker to predict an outright recession — an appealing fiction, because expecting anything worse is too painful.

**2. Everyone has an incomplete view of the world. But we form a complete narrative to fill in the gaps.**
- Housel's daughter at about a year old has no idea why dad goes to work every morning. Concepts of bills, budgets, careers are completely foreign. But her world isn't dark — she's written her own internal narrative of how everything works. "Dad isn't playing with me, and I wanted him to play with me, so I'm sad."
- All of us, no matter our age, do the same thing. We don't know what we don't know. So we explain the world through the limited set of mental models we have.
- **B. H. Liddell Hart** (*Why Don't We Learn From History?*): "[History] cannot be interpreted without the aid of imagination and intuition... Those who read history tend to look for what proves them right and confirms their personal opinions."
- **Kahneman**: "Hindsight, the ability to explain the past, gives us the illusion that the world is understandable. It gives us the illusion that the world makes sense, even when it doesn't make sense."
- We confuse fields of precision with fields of uncertainty. NASA's New Horizons spacecraft reached Pluto after a three-billion-mile, nine-and-a-half-year trip and arrived **one minute early** (99.99998% accurate — like forecasting a trip from New York to Boston within four millionths of a second). But astrophysics is a field of precision. Business, economics, and investing are fields of uncertainty driven by decisions that can't be explained with clean formulas.
- **Philip Tetlock** (psychologist): "We need to believe we live in a predictable, controllable world, so we turn to authoritative-sounding people who promise to satisfy that need." Wanting to believe we are in control is an emotional itch, not an analytical problem.
- **Kahneman** on how stories mislead us:
  - When planning, we focus on what we can do, neglecting the plans and skills of others whose decisions might affect our outcomes
  - In explaining the past and predicting the future, we focus on skill and neglect the role of luck
  - We focus on what we know and neglect what we do not know, making us overly confident
- Kahneman asked startup founders: "To what extent will the outcome of your effort depend on what you do in your firm?" The answer has never been less than 80%. "Even when they are not sure they will succeed, these bold people think their fate is almost entirely in their own hands."

## Chapter 19: All Together Now

**Epigraph**: "What we've learned about the psychology of your own money."

### Clarence Hughes — the dentist parable
- **Clarence Hughes** went to the dentist in 1931 with radiating mouth pain. The dentist put him under crude anesthesia. When he woke, 16 teeth had been removed and his tonsils taken out. Clarence died a week later from surgical complications.
- His wife sued — not because the surgery went wrong (every surgery risked death in 1931), but because Clarence never consented to the procedures and wouldn't have if asked.
- For most of medical history, the ethos was: the doctor fixes the patient, and what the patient thinks about the treatment plan is irrelevant. In the last 50 years, medicine shifted toward laying out options and letting the patient decide.
- **Dr. Jay Katz** (*The Silent World Between Doctor and Patient*): "Medicine is a complex profession and the interactions between physicians and patients are also complex."
- Financial advice is the same. "I can't tell you what to do with your money, because I don't know you. I don't know what you want. I don't know when you want it. I don't know why you want it."
- There are universal truths in money, even if people come to different conclusions about how to apply them to their own finances.

### Sandy Gottesman — "What do you own, and why?"
- **Sandy Gottesman** (billionaire investor, founded First Manhattan) asks one question when interviewing: "What do you own, and why?" Not "What stocks are cheap?" or "What economy is about to have a recession?" — just show me what you do with your own money.
- Half of all US mutual fund portfolio managers do not invest a cent of their own money in their funds (Morningstar). Like **Ken Murray** (USC professor) who wrote "How Doctors Die" — showing that doctors choose very different end-of-life treatments for themselves than they recommend for patients. A doctor may throw the kitchen sink at a patient's cancer but choose palliative care for herself.
- There is no one right answer. There's only what works for you and your family, checking the boxes you want checked, sleeping well at night.

### Summary of actionable lessons:
1. **Go out of your way to find humility when things are going right and forgiveness/compassion when they go wrong.** It's never as good or as bad as it looks. Luck and risk are both real.
2. **Less ego, more wealth.** Saving = gap between your ego and your income. Wealth is what you don't see — created by suppressing what you could buy today for more options in the future.
3. **Manage your money in a way that helps you sleep at night.** "Does this help me sleep at night?" is the best universal guidepost.
4. **If you want to do better as an investor, the single most powerful thing you can do is increase your time horizon.** Time makes little things grow big and big mistakes fade away.
5. **Become OK with a lot of things going wrong.** You can be wrong half the time and still make a fortune, because a small minority of things account for the majority of outcomes. Measure your full portfolio, not individual investments.
6. **Use money to gain control over your time.** The ability to do what you want, when you want, with who you want, for as long as you want to, pays the highest dividend that exists in finance.
7. **Be nicer and less flashy.** No one is impressed with your possessions as much as you are. You gain respect through kindness and humility more than horsepower and chrome.
8. **Save. Just save. You don't need a specific reason to save.** Savings not earmarked for anything in particular are a hedge against life's inevitable ability to surprise the hell out of you.
9. **Define the cost of success and be ready to pay it.** Uncertainty, doubt, and regret are common costs in finance. View them as fees (worth paying) rather than fines (to be avoided).
10. **Worship room for error.** A gap between what could happen and what you need to happen gives you endurance, and endurance is what makes compounding magic over time.
11. **Avoid the extreme ends of financial decisions.** Goals and desires will change over time; the more extreme your past decisions were, the more you may regret them.
12. **You should like risk because it pays off over time.** But be paranoid of ruinous risk because it prevents you from taking future risks that will pay off over time.
13. **Define the game you're playing.** Don't be influenced by people playing a different game.
14. **Respect the mess.** Smart, informed, and reasonable people can disagree in finance. There is no single right answer; just the answer that works for you.

## Chapter 20: Confessions

**Epigraph**: "The psychology of my own money."

### How my family thinks about savings
- **Charlie Munger**: "I did not intend to get rich. I just wanted to get independent."
- Independence has always been Housel's personal financial goal. Not chasing highest returns or leveraging assets for the most luxurious life. Just waking up every day knowing his family can do whatever they want on their own terms. Every financial decision revolves around that goal.
- His parents lived in two stages: dirt poor and moderately well off. Father became a doctor at 40, already had three kids. The frugal mentality from supporting three hungry kids in medical school never left — they spent the good years living well below their means with a high savings rate. This gave them independence. Father was an ER doctor (one of the highest-stress professions) — after two decades of painful circadian rhythm toggling, he decided he'd had enough and just quit. Moved onto the next phase of his life.
- **That stuck with Housel.** Being able to wake up one morning and change what you're doing, on your own terms, whenever you're ready, seems like the grandmother of all financial goals. Independence doesn't mean you'll stop working — it means you only do the work you like with people you like at the times you want for as long as you want.
- Independence at any income level is driven by your savings rate, and past a certain level of income your savings rate is driven by your ability to keep lifestyle expectations from running away.
- Met his wife in college, settled into a moderate lifestyle with entry-level pay. As incomes rose over more than a decade (himself in finance, wife in healthcare), they've more or less stayed at that lifestyle ever since. Virtually every dollar of raise has accrued to savings — their "independence fund." They rarely feel repressively frugal because their aspirations for more stuff haven't moved much. **Nassim Taleb**: "True success is exiting some rat race to modulate one's activities for peace of mind."
- So committed to independence that they've done things that make little sense on paper: own their house without a mortgage (worst/best financial decision they've ever made — the independent feeling far exceeds the known gain from leveraging with a cheap mortgage), and keep around 20% of their assets outside the value of their house in cash. "Indefensible on paper... it's just what works for us."
- Cash is the oxygen of independence. They never want to be forced to sell stocks to cover an expense. Not being forced to sell also means increasing the odds of letting stocks compound for the longest time. **Munger**: "The first rule of compounding is to never interrupt it unnecessarily."
- "What are you saving for?" "A world where curveballs are more common than we expect."

### How my family thinks about investing
- Started as a stock picker — owned about 25 individual stocks at any given point (Berkshire, P&G, and deep value investments). Now every stock they own is a low-cost index fund.
- "I don't have anything against actively picking stocks. I think some people can outperform the market averages — it's just very hard, and harder than most people think."
- **Investment philosophy**: every investor should pick a strategy that has the highest odds of successfully meeting their goals. For most, dollar-cost averaging into a low-cost index fund will provide the highest odds of long-term success. Beating the market *should* be hard; if it weren't, everyone would do it and there'd be no opportunity.
- Over the years he concluded that if you consistently invest in a low-cost index fund for decades and leave it alone to compound, there's a high chance of meeting all financial goals — especially when paired with frugal spending. If you can meet all your goals without the added risk of trying to outperform, what's the point of even trying? "I can afford to not be the greatest investor in the world, but I can't afford to be a bad one."
- Invest money from every paycheck into index funds — a combination of US and international stocks. No set goal — just whatever is left over after spending. Max out retirement accounts, contribute to kids' 529 college savings plans.
- Entire net worth: a house, a checking account, and some Vanguard index funds. Doesn't need to be more complicated.
- **Deeply held belief**: there is little correlation between investment effort and investment results. The world is driven by tails — a few variables account for the majority of returns. Simple strategies can work great as long as they capture the few things that matter. His strategy doesn't rely on picking the right sector or timing the next recession. It relies on a high savings rate, patience, and optimism that the global economy will create value over the next several decades.
- "I've changed my investment strategy in the past. So of course there's a chance I'll change it in the future. No matter how we save or invest I'm sure we'll always have the goal of independence, and we'll always do whatever maximizes sleeping well at night."
- "We think it's the ultimate goal; the mastery of the psychology of money. But to each their own. No one is crazy."

## Postscript: A Brief History of Why the U.S. Consumer Thinks the Way They Do

- To understand the psychology of the modern consumer, you have to know how they got here — how *we all* got here.
- If you fell asleep in 1945 and woke up in 2020 you would not recognize the world. The amount of economic growth during that period is virtually unprecedented.
- The short story: things were very uncertain, then very good, then pretty bad, then really good, then really bad, and now here we are.

### 1. August 1945 — World War II ends
- Japan surrendering was "The Happiest Day in American History" — but quickly met with "What happens now?"
- 16 million Americans (11% of population) served in the war, average age 23. Within 18 months, all but 1.5 million would be home and out of uniform.
- Three forces built up: housing construction had halted (fewer than 12,000 homes/month built in 1943); wartime jobs building ships/tanks/planes vanished overnight; marriage rate spiked (soldiers wanted families, homes, jobs immediately).
- Council of Economic Advisors warned Truman in 1946 of "a full-scale depression some time in the next one to four years." Europe and Japan were in ruins, and America was buried in war debt.

### 2. Low interest rates and the intentional birth of the American consumer
- First thing: keep interest rates low after the war. Fed was not politically independent before 1951 — it announced in 1942 that short-term rates would stay at 0.38% to finance the war. Rates didn't budge for seven years. Three-month Treasury yields stayed below 2% until the mid-1950s.
- The explicit reason was to keep the cost of financing $6 trillion (equivalent) in war spending low. But low rates also made borrowing to buy homes, cars, gadgets, and toys really cheap for returning GIs.
- Consumption became an explicit economic strategy. **Sheldon Garon** (Princeton historian): "After 1945, America again diverged from patterns of savings promotion in Europe and East Asia... Politicians, businessmen and labor leaders all encouraged Americans to spend to foster economic growth."
- Two things fueled the push: (1) the **GI Bill** — 16 million veterans could buy a home with no money down, no interest in the first year, fixed rates so low that monthly mortgage could be lower than a rental; (2) an explosion of consumer credit — first credit card in 1950, store credit, installment credit, personal loans, payday loans. Interest on all debt, including credit cards, was tax deductible at the time.
- US household debt: $29.4B (1945) → $125.7B (1955) → $331.2B (1965). Household debt in the 1950s grew 1.5x faster than during the 2000s debt splurge.

### 3. Pent-up demand + hidden 1930s productivity boom = economic boom
- The 1930s were the hardest economic decade in American history. But a silver lining that took two decades to notice: the Great Depression had supercharged resourcefulness, productivity, and innovation.
- Nobody paid attention to the productivity boom in the '30s (focused on how bad the economy was) or the '40s (focused on the war). Then the 1950s arrived: "Wow, we have some amazing new inventions. And we're really good at making them." Appliances, cars, phones, air conditioning, electricity.
- GIs were married, eager to get on with life, and emboldened with new cheap consumer credit — they went on a buying spree like the country had never seen.
- **Frederick Lewis Allen** (*The Big Change*): "During these postwar years the farmer bought a new tractor, a corn picker, an electric milking machine..."
