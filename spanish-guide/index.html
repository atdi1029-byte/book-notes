1. The Portuguese Advantage

You already know more Spanish than you think. Here's the map of what transfers and what doesn't.

The Numbers

Portuguese and Spanish share about 89% lexical similarity — meaning nearly 9 out of 10 words have a recognizable cognate in the other language. No other major language pair is this close. For comparison, English and German share about 60%, and English and French about 27%. You are starting from a position of extraordinary advantage.

Both languages descend from Vulgar Latin as spoken on the Iberian Peninsula. They diverged around the 9th-10th centuries, but the core grammar, vocabulary, and sentence structure remained remarkably parallel. Portuguese kept more archaic Latin features (like the personal infinitive and nasal vowels), while Spanish simplified in certain directions (losing nasal vowels, simplifying the vowel system).

Here's what transfers directly with zero effort:

What Transfers Perfectly

Sentence structure: Subject-Verb-Object order is identical. "Eu comprei um livro" maps directly to "Yo compré un libro."

Verb system: The same tenses exist — present, preterite, imperfect, future, conditional, subjunctive (present, past, future). The endings are slightly different but the logic is identical.

Gender system: Nouns are masculine or feminine, adjectives agree, articles agree. Same rules, same logic, with only a handful of gender flips (covered in Chapter 5).

Plural formation: Add -s or -es. Nearly identical rules for both languages.

Question words: quem→quién, onde→dónde, quando→cuándo, como→cómo, quanto→cuánto — all recognizable instantly.

Conjunctions: e→y, ou→o, mas→pero (this one changes!), porque→porque, se→si, quando→cuando.

The 15% That's Different

The differences between Portuguese and Spanish fall into predictable categories. Once you learn the patterns, you can convert on the fly. Here's what this guide will teach you:

Sound shifts: Portuguese has sounds that Spanish doesn't (nasal vowels, the "lh" and "nh" digraphs, the /ʃ/ "sh" sound). Spanish has sounds Portuguese doesn't (the /θ/ "th" in Castilian, the /x/ "jota" sound). Chapter 2 maps every shift.

Vocabulary gaps: About 10-15% of common words are completely different. "Longe" becomes "lejos." "Janela" becomes "ventana." "Frango" becomes "pollo." These aren't predictable from any pattern — you simply need to learn them. Chapter 3 is a massive reference of these swaps.

False friends: Words that look identical but mean different things. "Exquisito" in Spanish means "exquisite/delicious," not "weird" like "esquisito" in Portuguese. "Embarazada" means "pregnant," not "embarrassed." Chapter 4 covers 100+ of these traps.

Grammar tweaks: The verb conjugations are 95% similar but differ in small ways. Pronouns are placed differently. Some prepositions swap. The personal infinitive doesn't exist in Spanish. Chapters 6-11 cover all of this systematically.

Your Learning Strategy

Because your goal is reading and listening — not speaking — you have an even bigger advantage. You don't need to produce perfect Spanish. You need to recognize it. When you see "ventana" in a text, you need to know it means "janela." When you hear "lejos," you need to map it to "longe."

This guide is built as a reference you can return to. Read it through once to absorb the patterns, then keep it bookmarked. When you encounter an unfamiliar Spanish word, check the swap list or false friends list. Within a few weeks of reading Spanish texts, most of these will become automatic.

The Recognition Principle

As a Portuguese speaker reading Spanish, you will understand most sentences on first contact. The words you don't recognize will almost always be from the swap list in Chapter 3 or the false friends in Chapter 4. Those two chapters are your most important reference. Everything else (verb forms, prepositions, pronouns) will feel natural because the underlying grammar is the same.

2. The Sound Map

Every systematic pronunciation shift between Portuguese and Spanish, mapped out so you can recognize words by ear.

Why Sounds Matter for Listening

Even though you're focused on reading, understanding the sound shifts is critical for listening comprehension. When a Spanish speaker says a word, you need to mentally "convert" it back to the Portuguese equivalent. The good news: the shifts follow patterns. Once you internalize these patterns, Spanish speech becomes dramatically more transparent.

Vowel Differences

This is the single biggest phonetic difference. Portuguese has one of the richest vowel systems of any Romance language — 9 oral vowels plus 5 nasal vowels. Spanish has just 5 vowels: a, e, i, o, u. That's it. No nasal vowels, no reduced vowels, no schwa sounds. Every vowel in Spanish is pronounced clearly and fully, even in unstressed syllables.

In Portuguese, unstressed vowels get reduced. The "e" at the end of "cidade" practically disappears, becoming a schwa or even silent. In Spanish, "ciudad" has every vowel clearly pronounced: thee-oo-DAHD. This makes Spanish easier to hear clearly — there's no vowel swallowing.

Listening Tip

Spanish speakers pronounce every single vowel with full value. No vowel reduction, no schwa, no swallowed endings. When listening, expect to hear every syllable clearly articulated. This actually makes Spanish easier to understand by ear than Portuguese for most learners.

The Nasal Shift: -ão → -ón / -án

Portuguese's signature nasal diphthong -ão has no equivalent in Spanish. It systematically maps to -ón or -ción. This is one of the most reliable patterns in the entire language pair.

Notice that LH doesn't always map to LL. In many cases, the Latin "fl-", "pl-", "cl-" clusters became "lh" in Portuguese but "ll" or "j" in Spanish. The F → H Shift

One of the most famous differences between the two languages. Latin words that started with F kept the F in Portuguese but changed it to H (silent!) in Spanish. This is an ancient sound change from medieval Castilian, possibly influenced by Basque (which lacks the /f/ sound).

The J/G Sound

In Portuguese, "j" and "g" (before e/i) are pronounced like the "zh" in "pleasure" — the voiced postalveolar fricative /ʒ/. In Spanish, "j" and "g" (before e/i) make a harsh, throaty /x/ sound — like clearing your throat. This is the "jota" sound, and it's one of the most immediately recognizable features of Spanish.

Same Letters, Different Sounds
PT jardim → "zhardim"
ES jardín → "khardín" (throaty h)

The S Sound

In Brazilian Portuguese, "s" between vowels sounds like "z" (casa = "caza"), and final "s" can sound like "sh" in Rio dialect. In Spanish, "s" is almost always a clean /s/ sound — no voicing, no "sh." The word "casa" in Spanish sounds exactly like it looks: "cah-sah" with a clean /s/.

The R Sound

Portuguese has a guttural/uvular R (like French) at the start of words and when doubled. Spanish has a trilled/rolled R (tongue tip against the palate). The single "r" between vowels in Spanish is a quick tap (like the Portuguese single "r"). The double "rr" or word-initial "r" in Spanish is a full trill.

The D and T Sounds

In Brazilian Portuguese, "d" before "i" sounds like "dj" (dia = "djia") and "t" before "i" sounds like "tch" (tipo = "tchipo"). In Spanish, "d" and "t" are always dental — no palatalization. "Día" is just "dee-ah" and "tipo" is just "tee-po."

Big Difference: Days of the Week

Portuguese numbers its weekdays (segunda, terça, quarta, etc.) while Spanish uses the old Roman planet names (lunes=Luna/Moon, martes=Mars, miércoles=Mercury, jueves=Jupiter, viernes=Venus). This is one of the few areas where there's zero overlap between the two languages. You simply need to memorize the Spanish weekday names.

Where Ser/Estar Rules Match

Here's where it gets tricky. In a few cases, Portuguese and Spanish make different ser/estar choices:

Hay — The Existence Verb

This is a big one. Spanish has a special impersonal form "hay" (from haber) that means "there is" or "there are." Portuguese uses "tem" (from ter) or "há" (from haver).

Expressing Existence
PT Tem um gato na mesa / Há um gato na mesa
ES Hay un gato en la mesa
EN There is a cat on the table

"Hay" is invariable — it never changes form, regardless of singular or plural. "Hay un libro" (there is one book), "hay muchos libros" (there are many books). In the past tense, it becomes "había" (imperfect) or "hubo" (preterite). In the future, "habrá."

Hay vs Está/Están

Use hay when introducing something new (existence): "Hay un banco aquí" (There is a bank here).

Use está/están when locating something already known: "El banco está aquí" (The bank is here).

This is similar to Portuguese "tem/há" (existence) vs "está" (location), but hay is used much more consistently in Spanish than "há" is in modern Brazilian Portuguese.

Compound Tenses: Haber vs Ter

This is another major structural difference. Portuguese uses "ter" as the auxiliary for compound tenses (eu tenho falado, eu tinha falado). Spanish uses "haber" (yo he hablado, yo había hablado). The verb "tener" in Spanish means "to have" (possession) and is NEVER used as an auxiliary.

Si Clauses — Conditionals

"If... then..." sentences are one of the most important structures in any language. Spanish has three levels, and they map closely to Portuguese — with one critical difference.

Type 1: Real / Possible (Present)

If it rains, I'll stay home
PT Se chover, fico em casa
ES Si llueve, me quedo en casa

Portuguese uses future subjunctive (chover). Spanish uses present indicative (llueve) — NOT subjunctive. This is the big difference. Spanish NEVER uses subjunctive after "si" for real conditions. When you read "si + present indicative," it's a real/possible condition.

Type 2: Unreal / Hypothetical (Present)

If I had money, I would travel
PT Se eu tivesse dinheiro, viajaria
ES Si tuviera dinero, viajaría

Both languages use imperfect subjunctive in the "if" clause and conditional in the "then" clause. Very similar! Portuguese -sse maps to Spanish -ra (or -se): tivesse → tuviera.

Type 3: Unreal Past (Impossible)

If I had studied, I would have passed
PT Se eu tivesse estudado, teria passado
ES Si hubiera estudiado, habría aprobado

Past impossible conditions: imperfect subjunctive of haber + past participle in the "if" clause, conditional of haber + past participle in the result. Note: "hubiera" (not "tuviera") because Spanish uses haber for compound tenses, not tener.

Si Clause Summary
Real: Si + present indicative → present/future
"Si puedes, ven" (If you can, come)

Hypothetical: Si + imperfect subjunctive → conditional
"Si pudiera, iría" (If I could, I would go)

Impossible past: Si + pluperfect subjunctive → conditional perfect
"Si hubiera podido, habría ido" (If I could have, I would have gone)

NEVER: Si + present subjunctive. This is the #1 mistake. "Si tenga" does NOT exist. Always "si tiene" (real) or "si tuviera" (hypothetical).

13. Reading Spanish Now

Practical strategies to start reading and listening to Spanish immediately.

Your Reading Algorithm

Step 1: Read Naturally
Read the Spanish text as if it were Portuguese. You'll understand 70-80% immediately. Same sentence structure, most words are cognates, and verb forms are recognizable.
Step 2: Apply Sound Shifts
For unrecognized words, reverse the sound shifts from Chapter 2. "h" → "f" (hacer→fazer). "ue" → "o" (puerta→porta). "ie" → "e" (tierra→terra). "-ción" → "-ção" (nación→nação).
Step 3: Check False Friends
If a word looks familiar but the sentence doesn't make sense, it's a falso amigo. Check Chapter 4. Top offenders: largo (long), embarazada (pregnant), oficina (office), polvo (dust).
Step 4: Look Up Swap Words
Completely unfamiliar words are usually swap words from Chapter 3 — where the languages chose different words. Bookmark Chapter 3 as a reference. After weeks of reading, the common swaps become automatic.

Spanish uses "menos" (minus) for times after the half hour: "las cuatro menos diez" (3:50 = four minus ten). Portuguese uses "para" (to/for). Both use "ser" in the third person: "es la una" (singular), "son las tres" (plural).
