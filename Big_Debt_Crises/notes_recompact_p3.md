## Part 3: 48 Debt Crisis Case Studies

### Methodology and Purpose

- Systematically screened 48 debt crises across major countries over the last century
- Criteria: Real GDP decline >3%, triangulated against IMF work and prominent academics
- Not comprehensive of all debt crises, but a representative sample highlighting key similarities (from Part 1 template) and differences
- Each case includes computer-generated "auto-text" analysis plus charts showing basic stats
- Helps reveal principle-level commonalities and differences from the "Archetypal Big Debt Crisis" template

## United States 1926-1936: Deflationary Deleveraging

### The Bubble Phase (1926-1929)

- Self-reinforcing cycle: rising debt + strong equity returns + strong growth
- **Debt peaked at 125% of GDP** (pre-crisis), denominated in domestic currency, majority domestically owned
- Growth strong at 3%; GDP gap peaked at 13%
- **Equities averaged 31% annualized returns** over bubble period
- Policy makers initiated moderate tightening (short rates rising ~250 bps)

### The Depression Phase (1929-1933)

- **1929 stock market crash** triggered self-reinforcing bust
- GDP fell 26%, stock prices fell 84%, home prices fell 24%, unemployment increased 23%
- **Debt paradox**: despite needing deleveraging, debt as % GDP rose 98% (26% annualized) driven by falling real incomes, deflation, and interest payments financed with new debt
- Financial institutions under considerable pressure; pulled 8 out of 9 classic policy levers

### The Reflation Phase (1933-1936)

- Government **broke peg to gold**, M0 increased 6% of GDP, interest rates pushed to 0%
- Real FX averaged -5% during stimulative phase
- Nominal growth averaged 8%, sovereign long rates fell to 3%
- Unemployment declined 14%, debt as % GDP fell 70% (21% annualized)
- Debt-to-income ratio reduction driven primarily by rising real incomes, secondarily by inflation
- **Recovery**: 7 years for real GDP to reach prior peak; equity prices recovered within 25 years
- Set stage for **FDR** (populist leader) to take power

## United Kingdom 1927-1936: Deflationary Deleveraging

### The Bubble Phase

- No broad-based domestic bubble, but **tied to other economies** experiencing bubble conditions
- Debt reached **210% of GDP** -- a critical vulnerability threshold
- Debt in domestic currency, majority owned domestically

### The Depression Phase (1929-1931)

- External shock: US stock crash + early Great Depression
- GDP fell 10%, stock prices fell 61%, unemployment rose 7%
- Debt/GDP rose 13% (6% annualized) despite deleveraging need -- driven by interest payments and falling real incomes

### The Reflation Phase (1931-1936)

- **Broke peg to gold**, M0 increased 2% of GDP, interest rates pushed to 0%, real FX depreciated -8%
- Used only **1 out of 9 classic policy levers** -- not aggressive on financial institutions
- Nominal growth averaged 4%, sovereign long rates fell to 3%
- Debt/GDP fell 29% (5% annualized), primarily through paying down existing debt + rising real incomes
- **5 years** before real GDP reached prior peak; equity prices in USD recovered within 8 years

## France 1926-1938: Deflationary Deleveraging

### The Depression Phase (1929-1936)

- Deflation prevented effective deleveraging despite debt reduction efforts
- Debt peaked at ~70-75% of GDP; deflation hurt deleveraging efforts
- Key insight: deflation prevented effective deleveraging even with active debt reduction

### The Reflation Phase (1936-1938)

- **Broke peg to gold**, interest rates pushed to 2%, real FX averaged -4%
- Policymakers **allowed inflation to run high** -- averaged 10%; nominal growth averaged 15%
- Pulled 3 out of 9 policy levers
- Debt/GDP fell 37% (15% annualized) -- driven mostly by inflation (inflating away debt)
- **21 years** before real GDP reached prior peak -- one of the longest recoveries
- Crisis helped **Leon Blum** (populist leader) take power in 1936

## United Kingdom 1941-1967: Wartime Deflationary Deleveraging

### The War Phase (1941-1943)

- Debt crisis rooted in **WWII financing**, not bubble dynamics
- UK borrowed heavily for war; debt in domestic currency, domestically owned
- Real growth strong at 6% (aided by wartime spending), GDP gap peaked at 10%
- Equities averaged 16% annualized during war

### The Postwar Phase (1943-1947)

- GDP fell 15% during postwar adjustment
- **Debt/GDP went up 102%** (25% annualized) as incomes declined and government shouldered war costs
- Peak fiscal deficit: **31% of GDP** during ugly period

### The Reflation Phase (1947-1967)

- Currency devalued versus gold by 30%, interest rates pushed to 1%
- Inflation averaged 4%, nominal growth averaged 7%, sovereign long rates fell to 2%
- **Debt/GDP fell 139%** (7% annualized) -- reduction came mostly from income rising, driven primarily by inflation
- Took 10 years before real GDP reached prior peak
- Military spending surged from 7% GDP (1938) to 44% peak (1944-1945), then fell to 8-12%

## Norway 1984-1996: Deflationary Deleveraging

### The Bubble Phase (1984-1987)

- Self-reinforcing cycle: strong growth (3%), housing prices averaged 19% annualized returns
- Debt peaked at **211% of GDP**, domestic currency, domestically owned
- Debt service reached **58% of GDP** -- extreme vulnerability
- Investment inflows averaged 4% of GDP; short rates rose ~700 bps

### The Depression Phase (1987-1992)

- Shock via **commodity price declines** (Norway as commodity exporter)
- GDP fell 4%, home prices fell 38%
- Debt as % GDP roughly flat -- no net deleveraging occurred
- Financial institutions under considerable pressure

### The Reflation Phase (1992-1996)

- M0 increased 2% of GDP, rates pushed to 4%, real FX averaged 3% depreciation
- **Nationalized banks** and provided liquidity; pulled 4 out of 9 policy levers
- Enacted structural reforms for **labor market flexibility**
- Nominal growth averaged 6.1%, sovereign long rates fell to 5.5%
- Unemployment declined 4%, debt/GDP fell 35% (7% annualized)
- **5 years** to return to prior GDP peak
- Key lesson: high debt service ratios (58% of GDP) create extreme vulnerability to any shock

## Germany 1918-1925: Wartime Hyperinflationary Deleveraging

### The War Phase (1914-1918)

- Debt rose to **158% of GDP**; **64% was foreign currency debt** -- severe vulnerability
- Growth -6% average (war economy); economic stats distorted by war production shifts

### The Postwar Depression Phase (1918-1923)

- Both depression and balance-of-payments crisis simultaneously
- GDP declined 5%, stock prices collapsed 97%, unemployment jumped 16%
- Debt/GDP rose 165% (31% annualized) -- incomes falling while government absorbed war costs
- Peak fiscal deficit 11% of GDP

### The Reflation Phase (1923-1925)

- Initial tightening **insufficient** -- spiral of declining exchange rates and hyperinflation
- **Inflation peaked over 10,000%**; nominal short rates spiked to ~5000%
- Biggest risk factor: fiscal deficit (11% of GDP)
- Aggressive on 5 of 9 policy levers
- Resolution required **abandoning hyperinflated papiermark** and adopting new **reichsmark** (1924)
- **15 years** before real GDP reached prior peak
- Debt reduction of 98% -- virtually complete wipeout via currency collapse
- Gold price (local FX) went exponential; foreign investors suffered massive losses
- Key lesson: war debts + FX exposure = severe vulnerability; currency reform ultimately necessary when hyperinflation entrenched

## Appendix: Macroprudential Policies

### Definition and Purpose

- **Macroprudential policies** = tools for directing credit through central bank's regulatory authorities
- Distinct from monetary policy (broadly available credit) and fiscal policy (apportions it) -- macroprudential sits between
- Need arises because bubbles can form in one area while others are credit-starved
- Classic countercyclical approach: easier standards during busts, harder during bubbles

### Historical Context

- Not new -- Federal Reserve changed margin requirements for equity holdings **23 times** since formation
- Pattern: tightened during rallies/excess, eased during bear markets
- 1990s-mid-2000s = historical exception when macroprudential tools became rare
- Post-2008: global return to macroprudential tools

### When Deployed

- Economy needs stimulation but rates already at zero
- Tightening needed in specific sectors but overall rate hikes would drag growth
- QE creates pockets of frothiness while broader economy still recovering

### Policy Tools Taxonomy

**Demand-side** (changing demand for credit):
- Loan-to-value ratios, debt service-to-income ratios, loan maturity requirements
- Margin requirements for financial assets
- Cost of loans via interest rate subsidies/tax policy/regulations

**Supply-side** (changing supply of credit):
- Capital/reserve requirements for certain lending types
- Portfolio restrictions on assets financial institutions can hold
- Supervisor pressure for certain lending behaviors
- Interest/lending rate ceilings

### Key Questions for Policy Makers

1. **Where are bubbles emerging?** How confident? (1935-36 example: premature tightening caused severe recession)
2. **Which sectors are credit-starved?** (1950s four-test framework developed)
3. **Political costs** of picking winners/losers?
4. **Which tools** -- demand vs. supply, or both?
5. **How to measure effectiveness?**
6. **Second/third-order consequences?** (Regulation Q: helped small banks in 1930s, created distortions by 1950s driving deposits to shadow banking)
7. **Policy coordination** across legislators, executive, central bank? (Most successful US cases = broad coordination)

### Historical Cases

**Margin Requirements (post-1934)**:
- Securities Exchange Act gave Fed ability to set margin requirements
- Used counter-cyclically: Regulations T (brokers) and U (banks)
- Declined post-1974 as derivatives enabled circumvention

**Underwriting Standards**:
- Great Depression: FHA (1934) insured home loans at 80% LTV, 20-year maturity
- WWII: Roosevelt Executive Order limited installment credit for consumer durables (Regulation W)
- Postwar: Regulation X (1950) tightened housing credit; multiple adjustments through 1950s
- 1982: Congress abolished LTV and maturity restrictions for national banks

**Voluntary Guidelines**:
- 1947: Banks encouraged to voluntarily restrict lending programs
- 1965: Balance of Payments Committee recommended voluntary lending restraints (5% ceiling on foreign loan growth)
- Carter's 1980 Special Credit Restraint Program: limited loan growth to 6-9%, targeted speculative lending while protecting small businesses and homebuyers

**Reserve Requirements**:
- Used as counter-cyclical tool from 1930s onward
- 1935: Congress gave Fed authority to set reserve requirements
- 1940s: adjusted multiple times for war financing and postwar adjustment
- Loopholes emerged: commercial paper, eurodollars, repos bypassed requirements
- Volcker era: reserve requirements on wholesale liabilities rose to 8-10%, plus unprecedented asset-based requirements (15% special deposit on consumer credit)
- 1990, 1992: Fed lowered requirements to promote credit availability

**Interest Rate Ceilings (Regulation Q)**:
- 1933: Imposed 3% ceiling on deposit rates
- Pattern: raised/lowered as market conditions warranted
- 1966: FDIC and FHLBB could set ceilings on deposits
- **March 1980**: Depository Institutions Deregulation Act eliminated Regulation Q (deemed unsuccessful due to workarounds)

**Supervisory Guidance ("Direct Pressure")**:
- Post-WWI: Fed attempted to direct credit away from speculation via supervisory pressure
- **February 1929**: Fed denounced speculation -- "largely ineffectual"
- 1990s-2000s: Multiple statements on deteriorating lending standards
- 2005: Guidance on home equity, commercial real estate, nontraditional mortgages
- During busts: Roosevelt (1938) asked for "more liberal bank examination policy"; similar encouragement in S&L crisis, 2007-2009

### Implementation Lessons

- **Effective policies** used for long periods or expanded; unsuccessful ones ended within months
- **Significant experimentation and flexibility** critical to success
- Financial innovations enabled circumvention -- policies must adapt
- **Biggest failures**: policies continued too long without adaptation (Regulation Q; S&L deregulation)
- **Most successful**: broad government coordination across all branches (WWII, Volcker era)
