## Chapter 2: The Name of the Game is Bailout

### How Banks Create Profit and Why They Fail

- Fed allows commercial banks to create checkbook money out of nothing
- Loan shows as asset (earning interest); equal liability exists
- When borrowers default: bookkeeping loss — must come from equity
- If bad loans exceed equity, bank is insolvent

### The Plays of the Bailout Game

**Perpetual Debt**: Banks prefer perpetual interest over repayment.
Governments ideal borrowers — loans never expected to be repaid.

> "If we had a truth-in-Government act comparable to the truth-in-advertising
> law, every note issued by the Treasury would be obliged to include a
> sentence stating: 'This note will be redeemed with the proceeds from an
> identical note which will be sold to the public when this one comes due.'"
> — Walter Wriston, Chairman of Citicorp

**Roll-Over**: Bank creates more money from nothing, lends to borrower to
cover interest. Old loan maintained as asset; bank's assets grow larger.

**Up-the-Ante**: When borrower stops paying, "compromise" = even larger
loans covering interest plus fresh spending money.

**Rescheduling**: Lower interest rate, longer period. Cosmetic — reduces
monthly pain but makes repayment even more unlikely.

**Final Maneuver (Protect-the-Public)**: Banks and borrower approach
Congress. Dire consequences threatened. Government guarantees or provides
funds. Burden transferred from bank ledger to taxpayer.

### FDIC: Not Insurance

- FDIC "moral hazard": all banks pay same rate regardless of risk
- Conservative banks penalized; reckless banks get higher returns plus
  same protection → system incentivizes reckless lending
- FDIC holds about $1.20 for every $100 of covered deposits (70 cents
  at time of writing) — **99.3% exposure gap**
- "Full faith and credit" = Congress borrows, Fed creates money from
  nothing, new money dilutes purchasing power of all existing money

**Hat-check analogy**: When we give hat to hat-check girl, we don't expect
her to rent it out while we eat dinner. Yet banks do exactly this with
deposits — issue more promises than money in vault.

Four forms: (1) **Commodity money**, (2) **Receipt money**, (3) **Fiat

"practically all we need to know to fully comprehend the Federal Reserve."

### Why Gold

- **Intrinsic value**, not perishable, divisible, scarce, precisely
  measurable. Selected by centuries of trial and error above all metals
- Supply doesn't matter — money is a measure of value, not value itself

> "If the supply of gold in relation to the supply of available goods is so
> small that a one-ounce coin would be too valuable for minor transactions,
> people simply would use half-ounce coins or tenth-ounce coins."

- 1913: average annual wage $633, gold at $20.67/oz = 30.6 oz/year
