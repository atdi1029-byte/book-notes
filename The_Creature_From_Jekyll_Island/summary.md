# The Creature from Jekyll Island — Summary

*G. Edward Griffin | 707 pages | First published 1994*

## Overview

In November 1910, seven men representing roughly one-fourth of the world's wealth boarded a private railcar in New Jersey and traveled under assumed names to Jekyll Island, Georgia. Over the next nine days, Senator Nelson Aldrich, Paul Warburg, Henry Davison, Frank Vanderlip, and associates from the Morgan, Rockefeller, and Rothschild banking dynasties drafted the blueprint for what would become the Federal Reserve System. Griffin's central thesis is that the Fed was never designed to stabilize the economy or protect the public — it was engineered as a banking cartel, using the police power of government to enforce agreements that would otherwise collapse under free-market competition.

The book traces the full history of central banking from the Bank of England (1694) through America's first three failed central banks, the financing of wars from Napoleon through Vietnam, and the modern machinery of bailouts, IMF lending, and fiat currency. Griffin argues that the Mandrake Mechanism — the Fed's ability to create money from nothing through fractional-reserve lending — is the root cause of inflation, boom-bust cycles, mounting national debt, and the hidden confiscation of wealth from ordinary citizens. He connects this monetary power to the financing of both sides of wars, the Bolshevik Revolution, and the deliberate inflation of the 1920s that led to the Crash of 1929. The book concludes with a sixteen-step plan to abolish the Federal Reserve and restore commodity-backed money.

## Key Themes

- **The Fed as cartel, not public institution** — Created by competing banking dynasties (Morgan, Rockefeller, Warburg/Rothschild) to stop the growth of rival banks, pool reserves, and shift losses from bank owners to taxpayers through bailouts and inflation.
- **Money created from nothing** — The Mandrake Mechanism allows the Fed and commercial banks to create money through debt, multiplying reserves up to ten times. Every dollar in circulation exists because someone borrowed it into existence.
- **Inflation as hidden taxation** — Since 1914, the dollar has lost over 90% of its purchasing power. Griffin frames this not as an accident but as a deliberate wealth transfer from savers to borrowers and from citizens to government.
- **The Rothschild Formula** — A pattern of financing both sides of conflicts to ensure perpetual war and perpetual debt, traced from Napoleonic wars through both World Wars to the Cold War.
- **Bailouts reward recklessness** — From Penn Central to Continental Illinois to the S&L crisis, the pattern repeats: banks make risky loans, losses are socialized through FDIC and Fed intervention, and taxpayers absorb the cost.
- **Gold as the constitutional standard** — The Founders experienced fiat money's destruction firsthand (Continental currency lost 99% of value) and deliberately prohibited bills of credit in the Constitution, establishing gold and silver coinage as the only lawful money.
- **International dimension** — The IMF and World Bank serve as extensions of the same mechanism globally, creating perpetual debt cycles in developing nations while advancing the infrastructure for a world central bank and common currency.
- **Historical pattern of central bank failure** — America has had four central banks; each followed the same arc of fraud, inflation, political corruption, and eventual collapse or abolition.

## Why It Matters

Whether one accepts Griffin's conspiratorial framework or not, the book forces a confrontation with questions most economics textbooks avoid: who benefits from the power to create money, why the dollar has lost most of its value since 1914, and why the national debt grows regardless of which party holds power. The detailed accounts of Jekyll Island, the Lusitania, the financing of the Bolshevik Revolution, and the deliberate engineering of the 1929 crash challenge the "accidental" view of economic history and make the case that monetary policy is too consequential — and too easily captured by private interests — to be left in the hands of an unelected, unaudited institution that operates largely in secret.
