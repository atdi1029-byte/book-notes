Chapter 1: The Journey to Jekyll Island

On a November night in 1910, with the first snow of winter falling, a private railway car labeled ALDRICH waited at a New Jersey station. Its mahogany paneling gleamed, velvet drapes framed the windows, and a fully stocked bar stood ready. Seven men arrived separately, avoiding reporters, using only first names. One carried a shotgun case to disguise the trip as a duck-hunting excursion. Their destination was Jekyll Island, a winter resort off the coast of Georgia owned by J.P. Morgan and his associates. The regular staff had been given vacation. Screened replacements were brought in. Whatever was about to happen, no one outside the group was meant to know.

The seven passengers represented an estimated one-fourth of the total wealth of the entire world. Senator Nelson Aldrich of Rhode Island, Republican whip and chairman of the National Monetary Commission, was father-in-law to John D. Rockefeller Jr. Abraham Piatt Andrew served as Assistant Secretary of the Treasury. Frank Vanderlip was president of National City Bank of New York, representing William Rockefeller and Kuhn, Loeb & Company. Henry P. Davison was a senior partner at J.P. Morgan. Charles D. Norton ran Morgan's First National Bank. Benjamin Strong headed Morgan's Bankers Trust Company. And the dominant intellectual force of the group, Paul M. Warburg, was a partner at Kuhn, Loeb and representative of the Rothschild banking dynasty in Europe.

“If it were to be exposed publicly that our particular group had got together and written a banking bill, that bill would have no chance whatever of passage by Congress.” — Frank Vanderlip

Their purpose was to draft a blueprint for a banking cartel — one that would maximize profits by minimizing competition, make entry difficult for new rivals, and harness the police power of government to enforce the arrangement. The outcome of their nine-day meeting would become the foundation of the Federal Reserve System. Five objectives guided the drafting: stop the growing influence of small rival banks, obtain the franchise to create money out of nothing for the purpose of lending, pool the nation's reserves so all banks followed the same loan-to-deposit ratios, shift losses from bank owners to taxpayers, and convince Congress the whole scheme was designed to protect the public.

The competitive threat was real. The number of banks in the United States had more than doubled in a decade to over 20,000. By 1913, 71% were non-national banks holding 57% of all deposits. Worse, between 1900 and 1910, roughly 70% of corporate funding was generated internally — industry was becoming independent of the bankers altogether. The federal government was accumulating gold reserves and reducing the national debt. The big banks were losing their grip.

Paul Warburg was the guiding intellect. Born into the M.M. Warburg banking family of Hamburg and Amsterdam, he had come to the United States in 1901, funded by the Rothschild group. His brother Max served as financial adviser to the Kaiser and director of the Reichsbank. Paul was the only man in the group who truly understood the European central bank model, and he campaigned tirelessly for reform — pamphlets, speeches, an eleven-part series in the New York Times. John Kenneth Galbraith would later write that Warburg had, “with some justice, been called the father of the system.” He also inspired the character Daddy Warbucks in the comic strip Little Orphan Annie.

The men agreed on a critical public-relations strategy: never call it a cartel, never call it a central bank. The word “bank” was to be avoided entirely. Instead, conjure the image of a federal government agency. Establish regional branches to suggest decentralization. Start with conservative provisions that could be quietly altered later. Use the public anger from banking panics to create demand for reform, then present the Jekyll Island plan as the answer.

Since its creation, the Federal Reserve has presided over the crashes of 1921 and 1929, the Great Depression of 1929–1939, recessions in 1953, 1957, 1969, 1975, and 1981, and the stock market crash of 1987. It has overseen a 1,000% inflation that destroyed 90% of the dollar's purchasing power. By 1990, $10,000 was required to buy what $1,000 bought in 1914.

“When there is a conflict between the public interest and the private needs of the cartel — a conflict that arises almost daily — the public will be sacrificed. That is the nature of the beast.” — G. Edward Griffin

Chapter 2: The Name of the Game Is Bailout

The Federal Reserve allows commercial banks to create checkbook money out of nothing. A loan appears on the books as an asset earning interest, balanced by an equal liability. When a borrower defaults, the loss must come from the bank's equity — and if bad loans exceed equity, the bank is insolvent. The entire bailout game exists to prevent that insolvency from ever touching the men who own the banks. The losses are transferred, step by step, from private ledgers to the public treasury.

The game has several plays, each more audacious than the last. Perpetual debt is the first: banks prefer that borrowers never repay principal, only service interest indefinitely. Governments make ideal borrowers because their loans are never expected to be repaid.

“If we had a truth-in-Government act comparable to the truth-in-advertising law, every note issued by the Treasury would be obliged to include a sentence stating: ‘This note will be redeemed with the proceeds from an identical note which will be sold to the public when this one comes due.’” — Walter Wriston, Chairman of Citicorp

When a borrower stops paying, the bank does not write off the loss. Instead it creates more money from nothing, lends that to the borrower to cover the interest, and records the new loan as an additional asset. This is the roll-over. Then comes up-the-ante: the “compromise ” involves an even larger loan covering both past-due interest and fresh spending money. Rescheduling follows — lower interest rates, longer terms — cosmetic changes that reduce monthly pain but make repayment even more unlikely.

The final play is protect-the-public. Banks and the borrower approach Congress together. Dire consequences are threatened — systemic collapse, frozen credit markets, international contagion. Government guarantees or provides funds directly. The burden is transferred from the bank's ledger to the taxpayer.

The FDIC is presented as insurance but operates nothing like it. Real insurance charges higher premiums for higher risk. The FDIC charges every bank the same rate regardless of how recklessly it lends. Conservative banks are penalized while reckless banks earn higher returns with identical protection. The system incentivizes exactly the behavior it claims to prevent. And the coverage is an illusion: the FDIC holds roughly $1.20 for every $100 of insured deposits — a 99.3% exposure gap.

A useful analogy: when you give your hat to a hat-check girl, you don't expect her to rent it out while you eat dinner. Yet banks do exactly this with deposits — issuing more promises than the money in their vaults.

The words “full faith and credit” behind FDIC coverage mean this: Congress borrows, the Fed creates the money from nothing, and the new money dilutes the purchasing power of all existing money. Every depositor is made whole on paper while being quietly robbed in real terms.

The entire structure is a game with rules designed so the banks cannot lose. When they win, the profits are private. When they lose, the losses are public. The Federal Reserve is the mechanism that makes this transfer possible, and the FDIC is the mask that makes it appear legitimate.

Chapter 3: Protectors of the Public

The bailout game is not theoretical. It has played out again and again, each episode larger than the last. Penn Central Railroad collapsed in 1970. A congressional investigation revealed that directors had received dividends on worthless stock, earned interest on loans that paid those dividends, and unloaded 1.8 million shares at inflated prices before the fall. Arthur Burns, the Fed Chairman, opened the Discount Window on a Monday, changed Regulation Q the same day, and Congress passed the Emergency Rail Services Act providing $125 million in federal loan guarantees. The railroad was eventually nationalized into AMTRAK for passengers and CONRAIL for freight.

Lockheed, the nation's largest defense contractor, carried $400 million in bank loans it could not service. Treasury Secretary John Connally engineered $250 million in government guarantees. The government then awarded lucrative defense contracts to ensure profitability — taxpayers paid either way.

New York City in 1975 employed 49 city workers per 1,000 residents versus the national average of 31 — 58% excess. The city owed $2.3 billion held primarily by Chase Manhattan and Citicorp. David Rockefeller personally persuaded the German Chancellor to warn of an international crisis. Congress authorized $2.3 billion in direct loans.

Chrysler in 1978 received up to $1.5 billion in guaranteed new loans from Congress. The company obtained credit at 10.35% while competitor Ford paid 13.5%. Previously uncollectable debt was converted overnight into government-backed assets.

The most dramatic episode was Continental Illinois in 1984, the nation's seventh-largest bank with $42 billion in assets. A shopping-center bank in Oklahoma called Penn Square had sold $1 billion in bad oil and gas loans to Continental. When Penn Square failed, the world experienced its first electronic bank run: $1 billion in Asian deposits fled on the first day. Within seven days, outflows surged to $6 billion.

“The Continental run was like some modernistic fantasy: there were no throngs of hysterical depositors, just cool nightmare flashes on computer screens.” — Ron Chernow

The FDIC took $4.5 billion in bad loans, paid $3.5 billion, injected $1 billion in capital, and assumed 80% ownership. The bank was effectively nationalized. Only 4% of Continental's deposits were technically FDIC-insured, yet regulators covered the entire 100%. That same week, the FDIC closed tiny Bledsoe County Bank in Tennessee and Planters Trust in Louisiana. During the first half of the year, 43 smaller banks failed with no such rescue. The lesson was unmistakable: size determines who gets saved.

“The operation is the most basic function of the Federal Reserve. It was why it was founded.” — Fed Chairman Paul Volcker

Chapter 4: Home, Sweet Loan

The savings and loan crisis of the 1980s followed the same pattern at vastly larger scale. During the Great Depression, Americans embraced socialism in banking. Hoover created the Federal Home Loan Bank Board. Roosevelt created the FDIC and the FSLIC. The FHA subsidized home loans, distorting the natural balance of supply and demand. The Fed required commercial banks to offer lower interest rates than S&Ls, channeling funds toward housing — a political decision dressed up as market mechanics.

The system worked until interest rates spiked in 1979. S&Ls found themselves paying 15.8% on money-market certificates while charging only 12.9% on new mortgages — and their old mortgages were locked at 7–8%. The weakest institutions paid the highest rates to attract brokered deposits, accelerating the death spiral. In 1979, for the first time since the Depression, total S&L net worth went negative.

Early Reagan-era deregulation made things worse. S&Ls were no longer restricted to home mortgages and could finance 100% of deals. The Garn-St. Germain Act allowed lending at appraised rather than market value, enabling “appraised equity” schemes that pyramided deposits. At least 22 failed S&Ls showed evidence of Mafia and CIA involvement. Between 1980 and 1986, 664 insured S&Ls failed. By March 1986, the FSLIC had only three cents per dollar insured; by year's end, two-tenths of a penny.

Congress authorized a minimum of $66 billion for the bailout through FIRREA in 1989. Alan Greenspan said the true cost would be $500 billion. Veribank estimated $532 billion including hidden costs. The total burden including interest exceeded $1 trillion — extracted from taxpayers and inflation.

One of the cruelest ironies was the destruction of the junk bond market. High-yield bonds had returned 14.1% from 1981 to 1991, well above Treasury bills at 10.4%. The small-company sector that used these bonds created 18 million jobs while Fortune-500 companies cut 3.6 million. But FIRREA forced S&Ls to liquidate their high-yield holdings, crashing a market that had been functioning well. The government destroyed the market and then blamed Michael Milken for the wreckage. Salomon Brothers, formerly Drexel's fiercest critic, became the leading trader in the market Drexel had created.

“The true cost to the American taxpayer will be at least half a trillion dollars.” — Alan Greenspan on the S&L bailout

The S&L industry was a cartel within a cartel — it could not function without the Fed as lender of last resort, and it could not fail without dragging taxpayers into the pit. The entire episode illustrated the bailout game's ultimate logic: when the losses are large enough, they always become the public's problem.

Government intervention at every stage — creation, regulation, deregulation, and rescue — produced outcomes worse than a free market would have delivered. Each fix created the conditions for the next crisis, and each crisis justified the next expansion of government power.

The pattern was not an accident. It was the system working exactly as designed — privatizing profits during the boom and socializing losses during the bust.

Chapter 5: Nearer to the Heart's Desire

In July 1944, delegates from 44 nations gathered at Bretton Woods, New Hampshire, to design the post-war monetary order. The announced purposes were to facilitate international trade and stabilize exchange rates. The actual goal was to terminate gold as the basis of international currency and lay the groundwork for world socialism. The two institutions created — the International Monetary Fund and the World Bank — would become the instruments of that ambition.

The architects were telling. John Maynard Keynes, representing England, was a Fabian Socialist. Harry Dexter White, the American delegate and Assistant Secretary of the Treasury, was later revealed as a member of a Communist espionage ring. He became the first U.S. Executive Director at the IMF. Virginius Frank Coe, technical secretary of the conference and member of the same espionage network, became the IMF's first Secretary. The foundations of the world monetary system were laid by men whose loyalty ran to ideologies hostile to the free market.

The Fabian Society itself deserves attention. Founded in 1884, it was an elite group of British intellectuals who preferred gradualism over Communist revolution. Their turtle symbol emphasized patience. Their original crest depicted a wolf in sheep's clothing. The early leaders — Sidney and Beatrice Webb, George Bernard Shaw — created a stained-glass window showing themselves striking the earth with hammers, reshaping it “nearer to the heart's desire.”

In 1970, the IMF created Special Drawing Rights — pure bookkeeping entries with no relationship to gold. On August 15, 1971, Nixon ended the dollar's convertibility to gold entirely. The IMF reinvented itself from a guardian of fixed exchange rates to the world's lender of last resort, positioned to bail out governments the same way the Fed bails out banks.

The World Bank's record reveals its true function. Structural-Adjustment Loans require no specific projects — the money flows to governments for “policy changes,” meaning the construction of state control over economies. The Bank funded despots across the developing world. Tanzania under Julius Nyerere received over $3 billion. Before the loans, Tanzania fed its own people. After the loans, food had to be imported. Nyerere's program of forced villagization displaced millions. Ethiopia under Mengistu used World Bank funds to finance a planned famine modeled on Stalin's Ukraine.

“The stained-glass window shows the Fabian leaders striking the earth with hammers, reshaping it ‘nearer to the heart's desire.’” — Description of the Fabian Society window

The numbers tell the story. Argentina saw inflation peak at 1,000,000%. Brazil's prices rose to 164,000 times their original level since 1960. Mexico's average wage fell from 31% of the American wage in 1981 to 10% in 1989, despite having the world's fourth-largest oil reserves. Every nation that received World Bank development loans ended up poorer, more indebted, and more dependent than before.

The IMF and World Bank are not instruments of development. They are instruments of control — transferring wealth from industrialized nations to corrupt governments while enriching the international banking cartel that created them. The loans are never expected to be repaid. They exist to generate interest payments and political leverage.

The final play in the international bailout game is already visible: convert the IMF into a world central bank issuing its own fiat currency. The SDR was the first step. The endgame is a single global monetary authority with the power to inflate the entire planet's money supply at will.

Chapter 6: Building the New World Order

The international bailout game follows the same logic as the domestic version, just at larger scale. Banks create money from nothing and lend to underdeveloped governments. When nations can't pay, the IMF and World Bank step in with new loans. Governments resume interest payments and the game continues. The final play: convert the IMF into a world central bank issuing global fiat currency.

The Panama Canal bailout illustrates the pattern. Carter gave the Canal to Panama so its revenue — $120 million per year — could service Panama's debt to Chase Manhattan, First National of Chicago, and Citibank. Under General Torrijos, Panama's debt had ballooned from $167 million in 1968 to $1.5 billion. A sovereign asset was transferred to bail out private banks.

The Monetary Control Act of 1980 authorized the Fed to “monetize foreign debt” — creating money to lend to any government on earth. With a single stroke, the Federal Reserve became the central bank not just of the United States but of the entire world.

A $600 million “currency swap” was arranged directly by the Fed in near-total secrecy. In 1988, a “debt swap” allowed Mexico to buy $492 million in U.S. zero-coupon bonds and issue its own bonds with U.S. securities as collateral. The industrialized nations are being bled through a global wealth transfer coordinated by leaders who meet regularly across borders. Chapter 7: The Barbaric Metal

Understanding the Federal Reserve requires understanding money itself. There are four forms: commodity money (gold, silver, or any good with intrinsic value), receipt money (paper certificates redeemable for commodity money), fiat money (paper declared legal tender by government decree with no backing), and fractional money (receipts backed by only a fraction of the commodity they claim to represent). Understanding the difference between these four forms is “practically all we need to know to fully comprehend the Federal Reserve.”

Gold emerged as the supreme money through centuries of trial and error. It has intrinsic value, is not perishable, can be divided into precise quantities, and is scarce enough to serve as a store of wealth. The objection that there isn't enough gold to serve as money misunderstands the function. Money is a measure of value, not value itself.

“If the supply of gold in relation to the supply of available goods is so small that a one-ounce coin would be too valuable for minor transactions, people simply would use half-ounce coins or tenth-ounce coins.” — G. Edward Griffin

The price stability under gold is remarkable. In 1913, the average annual wage was $633 and gold traded at $20.67 per ounce, meaning a worker earned 30.6 ounces per year. By 1990, the average wage was $20,468 and gold stood at $386.90, yielding 52.9 ounces — an increase of only 73% over 77 years, less than 1% annually, reflecting genuine productivity gains rather than monetary manipulation.

The purchasing power of gold across millennia drives the point home. In ancient Rome, one ounce of gold bought a fine toga, a belt, and a pair of sandals. Today, the same ounce buys a hand-crafted suit, a belt, and dress shoes. At the Savoy Hotel in London, one gold sovereign still buys dinner for three — just as it did generations ago.

Alan Greenspan understood this perfectly. In 1966, before his appointment as Fed Chairman, he wrote with startling clarity about the relationship between gold and freedom.

“The abandonment of the gold standard made it possible for the welfare statists to use the banking system as a means to an unlimited expansion of credit… Deficit spending is simply a scheme for the ‘hidden’ confiscation of wealth. Gold stands in the way of this insidious process. It stands as a protector of property rights.” — Alan Greenspan, 1966

When Greenspan was appointed Fed Chairman, he fell silent on the subject of gold entirely. The man who had articulated the case against fiat money more eloquently than almost anyone became the chief operator of the very system he had condemned.

Two natural laws emerge from the historical record. Law 1: Long-term price stability is possible only when the money supply is based on gold or silver without government interference. Law 2: Whenever government manipulates the money supply, the inevitable result is inflation, economic chaos, and political upheaval. These are not political opinions. They are empirical observations confirmed across thousands of years of human history.

Gold is called “the barbaric metal” by those who benefit from fiat money. The real barbarism is a monetary system that allows governments to confiscate the savings of their citizens through inflation — a hidden tax that falls most heavily on those least able to pay.

Chapter 8: Fool's Gold

Massachusetts Bay Colony issued the first fiat money in America in 1690 to finance military raids against French Quebec. The colonial government promised to redeem the notes for gold and pledged not to issue more. Both promises were broken almost immediately. The classic cycle repeated across the colonies: expand the money supply, pass legal tender laws forcing acceptance, watch gold disappear from circulation, repudiate the old money, suffer inflation.

By the late 1750s, the results were devastating. Connecticut experienced 800% inflation. The Carolinas hit 900%. Massachusetts reached 1,000%. Rhode Island inflated 2,300% — and when judges ruled its legal tender law unconstitutional, the legislature dismissed the judges.

The Revolutionary War brought the experiment to its catastrophic conclusion. In 1775, the money supply stood at $12 million. By 1779, it had expanded roughly 5,000%. Continental currency that initially traded one-to-one with gold was worth less than a penny by war's end. The phrase “not worth a Continental” entered the American vocabulary.

“A wagon load of money will scarcely purchase a wagon load of provisions.” — George Washington

From this experience emerged Natural Law 3: A nation that resorts to fiat money has doomed itself to economic hardship and political disunity. Inflation is a hidden tax — the most unfair of all because it falls most heavily on those least able to pay.

The story had a brief happy ending. The delegates to the Constitutional Convention, with the memory of worthless Continentals fresh in their minds, decided to end fiat money. They gave Congress the power to coin money — not print it — and prohibited states from emitting bills of credit. The republic recovered rapidly and became the economic envy of the world.

But the lesson was forgotten by following generations, who had not lived through the inflation and could not feel its lessons in their bones. Each new generation had to learn the same truths through its own suffering.

The colonial experience is not ancient history. It is the blueprint for every fiat money disaster that followed — including the one currently administered by the Federal Reserve. The mechanisms are more sophisticated, the vocabulary more reassuring, but the underlying dynamic is identical: government creates money from nothing, spending power is transferred from citizens to the state, and the inevitable reckoning is postponed until it can no longer be avoided.

British Parliament's 1751 order forcing colonies to cease fiat money production triggered an unforeseen miracle — a boom of genuine prosperity as colonists returned to gold and silver coins. Prices adjusted to reality, commerce found solid footing, and the economy flourished. It was a perfect example of how an economic system in distress can recover if government does not interfere with the healing process.

Chapter 9: The Secret Science

The transition from honest money to fiat money passes through an intermediate stage: fractional-reserve banking. The process began with goldsmiths who discovered that only 10–15% of deposited coins were withdrawn at any given time. They began lending 80–85% of the stored coins without the depositors' knowledge, issuing more receipts than there were coins in the vault. These receipts became fractional money — backed by only about 54% of the gold they claimed to represent.

“A ‘sound’ banker, alas! is not one who foresees danger, and avoids it, but one who, when he is ruined, is ruined in a conventional and orthodox way along with his fellows.” — John Maynard Keynes

From this history comes Natural Law 4: Fractional money will always degenerate into fiat money. It is fiat money in transition. The fraction shrinks over time — from 54% to 20% to 10% to zero — because the incentive to lend more than you hold is irresistible, and the political pressure to bail out those who do is overwhelming.

History records a handful of banks that resisted. The Banco della Piazza del Rialto in Venice, founded in 1584, was forbidden to make any loans whatsoever. Its receipts actually carried a premium over gold coin because depositors trusted them absolutely. But Venice eventually absorbed it into the fractional Banco del Giro by 1637. The Bank of Amsterdam, founded in 1609, refused to make loans for over a century. During the crisis of 1672, everyone who sought their money was paid in full. But by 1657, overdrafts had been quietly permitted. By 1790, it was declared insolvent. The Bank of Hamburg, founded in 1619, held out longest of all. When Napoleon seized it, he found 7.5 million marks in silver against 7.49 million in liabilities — a surplus. It lasted 252 years.

The pattern is clear: honest banks can exist, they function superbly, but they are eventually overwhelmed by the political and competitive pressure to begin lending deposits. Once fractional lending starts, the slide toward zero backing is only a matter of time.

The Bank of England, founded in 1694, became the world's first central bank and the model for all that followed. The formula had seven steps: government grants a charter; the bank receives monopoly over banknote issuance; it creates money from nothing through fractional backing; it lends the government all the money it needs; government IOUs back the created money; the government pays 8% interest on money created at zero cost; and those same IOUs serve as reserves for additional private lending.

“The Bank hath benefit of interest on all the moneys which it, the Bank, creates out of nothing.” — William Paterson, founder of the Bank of England

Prices in England rose 100% in just two years. A run on the bank followed. Parliament intervened — not to punish the bank, but to protect it, exempting it from its contractual obligation to return depositors' gold. The precedent was set: when fractional-reserve banks fail, government rescues them rather than holding them accountable.

After the Bank of England's founding, England was at war 63 of the next 126 years — one out of every two years spent in combat. The most permanent result of each war was not territory gained or enemies vanquished but expanded government debt. The Bank of England proved that a central bank's primary product is not stable currency but unlimited war finance.

Chapter 10: The Mandrake Mechanism

Named after Mandrake the Magician, a 1940s comic-strip character who could create things from thin air, the Mandrake Mechanism is the process by which the Federal Reserve creates money. It is not complicated, but it is deliberately obscured. Money does not exist until the instant it is borrowed. When the debt is repaid, the money vanishes. Today's dollar has zero gold backing — the fraction is literally 0%.

The process works in six steps. The government creates a bond — a promise to pay. The Fed buys the bond by writing a check on an account that contains no money. The government deposits the check and spends via checks to contractors, employees, and welfare recipients. Those recipients deposit in commercial banks, where the deposits become reserves. Through fractional-reserve lending, those reserves are multiplied roughly nine times through successive loans. The total fiat money created can reach up to ten times the original government debt.

The Fed itself has been remarkably candid about this process in its own publications.

“Currency cannot be redeemed, or exchanged, for Treasury gold or any other asset used as backing.” — Federal Reserve Bank of New York

A famous exchange during congressional testimony in 1941 between Congressman Wright Patman and Fed Governor Marriner Eccles laid the reality bare. “We created it,” Eccles said of the money. “Out of what?” Patman asked. “Out of the right to issue credit money,” Eccles replied. The nation's money supply is conjured from a legal abstraction.

Robert Hemphill, Credit Manager of the Federal Reserve Bank of Atlanta, stated the implications directly: “If all the bank loans were paid, no one could have a bank deposit, and there would not be a dollar of coin or currency in circulation.” Money would vanish without debt. The entire system depends on permanent, ever-expanding indebtedness.

The human cost is staggering. A $100,000 home purchased at 11% interest over 30 years generates $167,806 in interest payments — the lender receives 2.5 times more than the people who provided the labor and materials to build the house. And the lender “did nothing to earn the money, had not saved it, and simply created it out of thin air.” Thomas Jefferson wrote two centuries ago: “No one has a natural right to the trade of money lender, but he who has money to lend.”

Beardsley Ruml, Chairman of the New York Federal Reserve Bank, published an extraordinary article in 1946 titled “Taxes for Revenue Are Obsolete.” With a central bank, government needs no tax revenue at all. Taxes serve only two purposes: combating price rises caused by the government's own money creation, and redistributing wealth as social engineering. The income tax is not a funding mechanism — it is a control mechanism.

The Mandrake Mechanism works in reverse too. When the public reduces its debt, the money supply shrinks. This expansion and contraction cycle is the core cause of booms and busts. Every nation that has adopted fiat money has eventually been destroyed by it. The Federal Reserve did not solve this problem. It institutionalized it.

Chapter 11: The Rothschild Formula

Mayer Amschel Bauer changed his family name to Rothschild — German for “red shield,” the sign that hung over the family shop in Frankfurt. He placed his five sons in the financial capitals of Europe: London, Paris, Vienna, Naples, and Frankfurt. They became personal bankers to crowned heads across the continent and financiers to Cecil Rhodes, who built a diamond and gold monopoly in South Africa.

“Those who lampooned or vilified the Rothschilds for their ‘sinister’ influence had a considerable amount of justification… The house of Rothschild was immensely more powerful than any financial empire that had ever preceded it.” — Derek Wilson

Napoleon understood what the bankers were. He restored order in France and refused to borrow from them. “Money has no motherland,” he said. “Financiers are without patriotism.” The bankers were confident Napoleon couldn't defend himself without debt. He proved them wrong by selling Louisiana to the Americans for £3,000,000. When British financiers proposed a joint Anglo-French invasion to destroy the young United States, Napoleon discovered the plot, arrested the conspirators, and published the entire story. The Bank of England responded by creating vast quantities of fiat money to fund a coalition against him.

Nathan Rothschild smuggled gold through Napoleon-controlled France to fund Wellington's army. His teenage brother James managed the operation, tricking the imperial government into sanctioning the very process that helped ruin it. After Waterloo, Nathan received news of the outcome a full 24 hours before the official courier reached London. He went to the stock exchange, slumped dejectedly in his usual chair, and began selling. The whisper spread: “Wellington must have lost!” Prices collapsed. Nathan reversed course and purchased England's entire government debt at a tiny fraction of its worth.

From this history emerges the Rothschild Formula, a five-point strategy for converting war into profit. First, war is the ultimate discipline for any government — it ensures debt. Second, to guarantee debt, involve the government in war or the credible threat of war. Third, enemies must possess credible military might — finance them if necessary. Fourth, if a government declines debt-financed war, encourage internal revolt. Fifth, no single nation may be allowed to become militarily stronger than its adversaries — finance both sides. The unspoken objective is perpetual war.

The formula operated with ruthless consistency across centuries. The financiers did not care who won.
