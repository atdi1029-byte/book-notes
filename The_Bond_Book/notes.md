## Chapter 1: The Life of a Bond

### Key Terms

- **Indenture** — legal document binding issuer to bondholders for the bond's life; stipulates coupon payment dates, maturity, call features, revenue sources
- Example: State of New Jersey would specify tax revenues and include discussion of state economy
- **Prospectus** ("official statement" or OS) — summary of indenture terms; now archived online for munis
- Before sale: "preliminary prospectus" or "red herring" (red ink on cover)
- Three main categories: **Treasuries** (U.S. government), **Corporates**, **Municipals** (tax-exempt)
- **Par** = $1,000; minimum purchase typically $5,000 par value
- Typical issue sizes: $100-500M for corporates/munis; many billions for Treasuries

### Primary vs. Secondary Markets

- **Primary market** — buying at issue during initial offering (few days); individual investor may receive fairer shake
- **Secondary market** — trading after initial issue; very active for some (30-year Treasuries), moribund for many
- Almost always possible to sell older bonds, but commission costs may be very high if not actively traded
- Bonds seldom trade at par — typically only at issue and at maturity
- Regardless of purchase price, bonds always redeemed at par

> "Bond prices go up and down continually. Bonds seldom, if ever, trade at par."

## Chapter 2: The Bond Market: An Overview

### Market Structure

- **Not a centralized exchange** — gigantic over-the-counter market of independent dealers organized by security type
- **Primary dealers** — several dozen extremely large firms; some buy Treasuries directly from Federal Reserve
- Bond market **several times larger** than stock market
- Government bonds trade in $1M-$100M blocks routinely; smallest blocks: municipal round lots $100,000
- Individual investors = "little guys" facing higher commissions, less information, no sophisticated trading models
- Many fixed-income securities structured for pension funds/insurance companies — **unsuitable for individual investors**

### Pricing: Markups and Commissions

- **Bid** (what you sell for) / **Ask** (what you buy for) / **Spread** (markup between them) — spread is hidden, quoted net
- Spread has been hidden since "time began" and continues today
- **Typical spreads for individuals**: actively traded Treasuries ¼% or less; inactively traded bonds as much as 4%
- **Key rule**: desirable/low-risk/higher-quality bonds sell at narrower spreads; riskier bonds at wider spreads
- **Higher yield means higher risk** — always
- Unusually wide spread (4%+) = **red flag** — bond may be expensive to resell or headed for trouble
- Dealer community's information network sometimes quicker than rating agencies at spotting trouble
- During 2008 panic: spreads widened beyond all norms; many bonds could not be sold at any price

### How Bonds Are Sold

- Stocks: identify specific stock, look up ticker, see real-time price — same price for 10 or 1,000 shares
- Bonds: shop based on criteria (maturity, credit rating, yield, callable status) — unlikely to have specific bond in mind
- Bond availability varies widely, particularly in less liquid sectors (corporates, municipals)
- **Dealer** — puts own money at risk, maintains inventory; same bond may be different prices at different dealers
- **Broker** — executes trades for customers, earns commission; many don't own bonds they sell

### Electronic Platforms

- Four dominant platforms: **Bond Desk**, **MuniCenter**, **Knight Bond Point**, **Trade Web**
- **MuniCenter**: positioning as municipal market powerhouse with own inventory
- **Bond Desk**: focusing on sophisticated portfolio management software for individuals
- Same bond may appear at 100 on one site, 102-105 on another — dealers mark up independently
- Some brokers filter out bonds priced >3% above recent inter-dealer price; others exclude dealers "for idiosyncratic reasons"
- Small percentage (~5%) of orders "fail" — brokers may not fill order as submitted
- **Commission cost illusion**: discount broker $2-$5 fee per bond is NOT the markup (just tip of iceberg)
- Dealer markup and broker markup remain undisclosed; paying list price = paying full markups
- Prices often negotiable — brokers expect informed customers to bargain
- Most instances discussing purchase with broker (including discount brokers): broker willing to contact dealer for lower price

### Price Transparency Resources

- **FINRA.org/marketdata** — TRACE corporate/agency bond data within 15 minutes of trade
- **EMMA.msrb.org** — official repository for municipal disclosure and market data
- **Investinginbonds.com** — muni, corporate, agency trade data; oldest site
- All free to anyone; enable individual investors to access pricing information including markups

### Bond Conventions

- **CUSIP** — nine-digit identification number (like stock ticker); established 1967; identifies precise issue
- International issues: **CINS number** (CUSIP International Numbering System)
- Price quoted as percentage of par: 97 = $970; always add a zero to the quote
- Coupon always quoted in percentages of par: 5¼% = $52.50/year, paid semiannually ($26.25 twice)
- **Floating-rate bonds** vary — coupon rates reset at predetermined intervals
- **Accrued interest** automatically added to purchase price (buyer pays seller for days owned since last coupon)
- Formula: (annual coupon ÷ 360 days) × days accrued; year = 360 days for many bonds

### Basis Points

- **Basis point (bp)** = 1/100 of 1%; 6% to 7% = 100bp; 6% to 6.12% = 12bp
- Under normal circumstances: yields vary day-to-day by no more than a few bp
- Rise or decline of >10bp in one day = major price move and major change in direction
- Using the term immediately marks you as knowledgeable investor

### Call Risk

- Municipal and corporate bonds subject to call; Treasuries generally not (some older 30-year Treasuries callable five years before maturity; no longer issued callable)
- Call **reduces total return** two ways: (1) high interest rate disappears, (2) forced reinvestment at lower rates
- Premium bonds particularly vulnerable — unexpected early call at par = principal loss ($1,100 bond called at par = $100 loss)
- If coupon much higher than current rates, evaluate based on **yield-to-call** not yield-to-maturity

### Bond Registration

- Pre-1980: **bearer bonds** — ornate certificates with coupons to clip, no owner name (origin of term "coupon")
- Early 1980s: **registered bonds** with owner name
- Modern: all **book-entry form** — no physical certificates; payments wired automatically
- **SIPC** protects against physical loss (not market decline); brokers prefer holding certificates

## Chapter 3: Volatility — Why Bond Prices Go Up and Down

### The Two Primary Risks

- **Interest rate risk** (market risk) — ALL bonds subject to this, regardless of issuer, insurance, or guarantees
- **Credit risk** — probability of default
- Since late 1970s: **interest rate changes = greatest single determinant of bond returns**
- Managing interest rate risk = **most critical variable** in bond portfolio management

### Interest Rate Risk: The Fundamentals

- **Interest rates and prices move inversely** — rates up = prices down, rates down = prices up
- **Risk correlated to maturity** — longer maturity = larger price swings
- Bonds with very short maturities (under one year) = considered cash equivalents
- **Rough rule**: 100bp rise in yield = ~10% principal loss on 30-year bonds

### Price Impact Data (Exhibit 3.1/3.2: 7% coupon, $1,000 par)

**If rates rise 50bp**: 2-year: -0.9% | 10-year: -3.5% | 30-year: -5.9%
**If rates rise 100bp**: 2-year: -1.1% | 10-year: -6.8% | 30-year: -11.3%
**If rates rise 200bp**: 2-year: -3.6% | 10-year: -13.0% | 30-year: -20.6%
**If rates fall 50bp**: 2-year: +0.9% | 10-year: +3.6% | 30-year: +6.6%
**If rates fall 100bp**: 2-year: +1.9% | 10-year: +7.4% | 30-year: +13.8%
**If rates fall 200bp**: 2-year: +3.8% | 10-year: +15.6% | 30-year: +30.9%

- For 30-year bond: 50bp rise wipes out almost total annual interest income
- Price changes **larger when rates decline** than when they rise = **convexity** (desirable property)
- Lower coupons = somewhat larger price changes; higher coupons = somewhat smaller
- Price changes not linear — decline in rate of increase begins around 10-year mark

### Why Buy Long-Term Bonds?

- Higher coupons than shorter-term (assuming normal upward-sloping yield curve)
- **"Lock in" attractive rate** if not planning to sell before maturity
- Intermediate bonds (A to AA range) often yield close to AAA long bonds but with far less volatility
- **Holding to maturity**: recover principal in full regardless of interest rate roller coaster; premium bonds gradually pull to par, discount bonds gradually rise to par

### Bond Swaps: Won't Increase Yields

- Selling old 4% bonds and buying new 8% bonds = **wash** (you own half as many bonds at double the rate = same dollar income)
- Plus you're out transaction costs
- Valid reasons: generate capital loss for tax purposes, upgrade credit quality

### Insurance Does NOT Protect Against Interest Rate Risk

- **One of most widely misunderstood aspects** of bond investing
- Guarantee covers timely payment and principal at maturity — NOT price fluctuations
- No exceptions — applies to Treasuries and Fly-by-Night Airlines equally
- 2008 example: investors poured into 10-year/30-year Treasuries at 2%/3% yields
- TLT (long Treasury ETF) subsequently declined ~25% from $120 peak to ~$87

### Defaults

- Even when defaults occur, bondholders seldom lose 100% — defaulted bonds usually have salvage value (sometimes 10-30 cents on dollar)
- Bonds "trading flat" = coupon payments suspended; if resumed, price can soar
- Bankrupt companies successfully emerging = bonanza for anyone who purchased during default

### Credit Ratings

- **Moody's / S&P / Fitch** — three major agencies; issuers pay for ratings (conflict of interest — ratings are big business)
- **Rating scale** (descending):
  - Aaa/AAA — Gilt edged; if everything that can go wrong goes wrong, can still service debt
  - Aa/AA — Very high quality by all standards
  - A/A — Investment grade; good quality
  - Baa/BBB — Lowest investment grade; satisfactory but needs monitoring
  - Ba/BB — Somewhat speculative; low grade
  - B/B — Very speculative
  - Caa/CCC through C — Substantial risk to outright default
- S&P adds +/- signs (B+ slightly higher than B); Moody's adds "1" (A1 higher than A)
- **Investment grade minimum**: BBB (S&P) / Baa (Moody's) — fiduciary institutions (banks, pension funds) permitted by law to invest only at this level or above; denotes "fair" margin of safety, not gilt-edged
- U.S. government securities: free of default risk (not rated but considered highest quality)
- Ratings reviewed when issuer brings new bonds and pays for rating; in practice not always monitored between issues
- Some small issuers pay for only one rating (don't want to pay for two or three)
- SEC won authority to oversee rating firms in 2006: **"Nationally Recognized Statistical Rating Organizations" (NSROs)"**
- Institutions required to invest only in securities rated by NSROs above designated minimums

### How Rating Changes Affect Prices

- Downgrade = price decline; upgrade = price goes up
- Unless genuine default risk: price changes from rating shifts far less major than from interest rate changes
- Rating changes of one notch for high-quality bonds = small price impact
- **Red flags**: downgrade below investment grade, multi-notch downgrade, serial downgrades in succession
- **Any bond rated investment grade or higher continues to have good margins of safety**

> "Ratings are not recommendations to buy or sell. A low rating does not mean default will occur; a high rating does not guarantee safety."

- If ratings disagree across agencies, assume the lowest is accurate
- Diversify: $50,000 better invested as five $10,000 lots than one $50,000 lot

### Credit Quality Spreads

- Differences in yield due to credit rating differences; narrow when economy booming, wide during recession
- Pre-2008 crisis: 10-20bp between rating steps (e.g., A to AA); widen as maturities extend or quality drops
- During 2008 panic: AAA-to-A muni spreads widened from 50-70bp to **300+ bp**
- **Short-to-intermediate, high-quality bonds emerged from 2008 virtually unscathed**
- **"Sell-off" risk**: tremendous imbalance between buyers and sellers; "to whom" market — buyers disappear

### Post-2008 Rating Reform

- Rating agencies blamed for AAA ratings on subprime MBS — "too lucrative business to turn down"
- **"Global" rating scale** proposed: based on actual default experience across all sectors
- 2007 Moody's study: GO munis rated barely investment grade had default rates **lower than AAA corporates**
- 1970-2009: rated municipal bonds experienced **54 total defaults** (less than 0.07%)
- April 2010: Fitch and Moody's adopted global scales — most GO munis upgraded 1-3 notches
- October 2009: WSJ reported prominent European corporations successfully issued debt without any ratings
- User-pay model (instead of issuer-pay) proposed but not yet adopted

### A Short History of Interest Rates

- **1930-1950**: long-term yields around 2% (Great Depression/WWII); bank passbook accounts paid 2-2½%
- **1950-1960**: rates rose slowly from 2% to 4%
- **Late 1960s**: yields reached 6% by 1970
- **Post-1979**: sharp climb; long bond briefly above 15%; peak **15.2% in October 1981**
- Tax-exempt 30-year munis reached **12-14%** depending on credit quality
- Bear market lasted **over 30 years**: bonds purchased in 1960s-70s at 4-6% coupons declined 50%+ as rates approached 15%

> "During the disastrous 1970s, satirists described long-term bonds as 'fixed rate instrument(s) designed to fall in price' and 'certificates of confiscation.'"

- **1982-2009**: greatest bull market in U.S. bond history; 30-year Treasuries with 14-15% coupons sold for **$1,700/bond** (70% capital gain)
- 1990s: long bond fluctuated between 8% high and 4.78% low (1998)
- **2008**: rates fell back to 1940 levels; T-Bills at 10bp; money market yields lower than during Great Depression

> "Anyone who purchased 30-year Treasury bonds in 1982 or 1983 enjoyed returns that exceeded those of the stock market."

- **Real rate** = nominal rate minus inflation; historical average ~3% above inflation
- At constant 2% inflation over 30 years: $100 item costs $181; at 3%: $243
- **No one has consistently predicted interest rates correctly over 30 years**
- Japan example: long-term and short-term bond rates exceptionally low (under 2% for 10-year) for over 15 years

### The Federal Reserve

- Fed directly controls only **Fed Funds rate** and **discount rate** (overnight rates)
- Fed Funds rate: rate Fed charges banks for overnight borrowing to meet reserve requirements
- When Fed raises overnight rates, maturities of 2-5 years typically follow; **longer rates may or may not follow**
- **Open market operations**: buying Treasuries increases money supply; selling decreases it
- **2004-2006 "conundrum"** (Greenspan): Fed raised rates 17 times (1.00% to 4.25%), yet longer rates actually declined
- 2008 crisis: Fed bought **>$1 trillion** of MBS, became largest owner of mortgage-backed bonds
- Fed target: 0-25bp for shortest rates — appears unsustainable long-term
- At some point Fed will likely want to sell securities purchased in 2008-2009; how this affects rates = major uncertainty

> "The strategies described in this book are predicated on the assumption that interest rates cannot be predicted."

## Chapter 4: Basic Bond Math

### Reinvestment Risk

- YTM assumes constant reinvestment rate — actual future rates unknown
- For 30-year bond: at 0% reinvestment → 0% from compounding; at 5% → **49% of total interest** from compounding; at 10% → **76%**
- Longer maturity + higher coupon = YTM less reliable as predictor of actual return
- Calling this a "risk" is misleading — no risk of losing principal, only uncertainty about total return

> "Overall, there is too much emphasis on yield. YTM should not be the main criterion for selecting bonds."

### Key Yield Measures

- **Yield-to-call (YTC)**: same formula as YTM but substitutes call date/price; for premium bonds, compare YTC and YTM, select the lower
- **Yield-to-worst**: lowest possible yield from all call dates plus YTM; not necessarily most probable outcome
- **Total return**: interest income +/- changes in principal value — what you actually earned
- Bond funds: don't have maturity date, can't quote YTM; maintain constant maturity so future price unpredictable
- **Zero coupon bonds** eliminate reinvestment risk entirely

### Total Return Example

- Buy 10 bonds at par, 5% coupon, 15-year maturity. Three years later rates rise to 6%, bonds decline to ~$850 each
- Coupon income: $1,500 + interest-on-interest $176 + sale proceeds $8,500 - purchase $10,000 = **$176 total, ~0.5% annual return** (not the quoted 5% YTM)
- Even if you don't sell (unrealized loss), total return is the same

> "If you need to sell a bond before it matures, and if interest rates have moved against you, you may take a real hit to principal."

- **Marking-to-market**: professional practice of calculating daily what holdings are worth
- **Opportunity cost**: unable to take advantage of currently higher yields when stuck in low-rate bonds

> "If you are buying bonds with 30-year maturities, you are making a bet on interest rates, whether you know it or not."

### Duration

- Originated 1938; weighted average term-to-maturity of cash flows; measures sensitivity to rate changes
- **Guideline**: for every 100bp change, bond price moves up/down by the duration number
- **Example**: 5-year maturity, 6% coupon, duration 4.4 years → ~4.4% price move per 100bp; 20-year maturity, duration ~12 years → ~12% price move
- **Lower coupons = longer duration = higher volatility** (less cash flow to reinvest early)
- **Zero coupon bonds**: duration = maturity (longest possible duration, most volatile)
- Three bonds with same YTM: premium = least volatile; discount = more volatile; zero = most volatile
- Duration more accurate for smaller rate changes than larger changes
- Except for zeros, duration always shorter than term-to-maturity

### Time Value of Money

- **Future value**: $50,000 at 7%/year for 20 years = $193,484; at 10% = $336,375 (magic of compounding)
- **Present value**: need $100,000 in 20 years at 7% → invest $25,842 today
- Retirement calculators use these formulas; even ½% difference in assumptions makes significant difference over long periods

### Convexity

- Corrects some limitations of duration; adjustments generally extremely small (1-3%)
- Used by managers of extremely large portfolios; **not particularly useful for individual investors**

> "Some Web sites now list a number for convexity without explanation. Used in this manner, it is just jargon. Just ignore it."

## Chapter 5: What You Need to Know Before Buying Bonds

### The Treasury Market

- 10-year bond = most widely traded bond worldwide (current bellwether; replaced 30-year around 2000)
- **Risk premium**: every other bond yields more than same-maturity Treasury
- **Spread**: difference in yield between any security and Treasury of same maturity
- **Treasury pricing**: quoted in 32nds; one "tick" = 1/32 of a point = $0.3125; example: 99.29 = $999.06

### The Yield Curve

- Graph of Treasury YTM at key maturities (3-month through 30-year); changes continually throughout day
- **Normal/upward sloping**: long rates > short rates
- **Steep**: spread >200bp; **Flat**: spread <100bp; **Inverted**: short rates > long rates

**Historical examples**:
- November 1988: flat (3-month ~8¼%, 30-year 9%, spread ~80bp)
- December 1954: normal upward sloping (bills ~1.2%, 30-year ~2.7%, spread ~150bp)
- September 1981: inverted (3-month ~16%, 6-month 17%, 30-year 15%)
- August 2009: steepest ever (**427bp spread** — 3-month 0.16%, 30-year 4.43%)

### Yield Curve as Economic Forecaster

- **Steep upward slope** = bullish for economy and stocks; follows aggressive Fed rate cuts; also forecasts rising long-term rates
- **Inverted curve** = predicts recession; follows Fed rate increases; example: year 2000, 3-month at 6.3%, 30-year at 5.6%, followed by recession
- Does NOT precede all recessions — unreliable as sole indicator
- **"Riding the yield curve"**: institutional strategy — buy longer maturity than holding period to capture yield premium; **not viable for individuals** (transaction costs eat profits)

### Practical Yield Curve Use for Individuals

- Quick summary of current rate levels across maturity spectrum
- **Key question**: "For the extra risk of extending maturity, how much more am I earning?"
- Example: 10-year at 3.8%, 30-year at 4.8% — extra 1% yield for 20 more years of maturity with significantly higher volatility
- When curve is flat: 2-7 year maturities might yield only 30-50bp less than 30-year = 90% of yield with dramatically lower risk

> "Investors intent on boosting yield sometimes place principal at risk for very little extra gain."

- Municipal yield curves more steeply sloped than Treasuries; best risk/yield tradeoff at **7-15 year** maturities

### Yield Spreads and "Spread Products"

- Taxable bonds (corporates, international) called **"spread products"** — priced off Treasuries
- **April 2010 vs. 2009 spreads (5-year maturity)**:
  - U.S. Corporate Index: +100bp (was +400bp) — narrowed 300bp
  - U.S. High Yield (Junk): +600bp (was +2000bp) — **narrowed 1,400bp**
  - Emerging Market: +350bp (was +900bp) — narrowed 550bp
- Junk bond sector experienced enormous gains in 2009 due to massive spread narrowing
- **Contrarian indicator**: 2006-2007 record narrow spreads = red flag (not compensated for risk); 2008 wide spreads = potential opportunity (but panic can last years)
- Bond market professionals sniff out economic changes faster than stock investors

### Three Key Bond Market Websites (Detail)

**Investinginbonds.com**: oldest site; all muni trades reported same day since 2005, 15 minutes after trade; complete trade history back to 2005; daily yield curves; user-friendly

**EMMA.msrb.org**: launched July 2009; official repository for muni official statements (back to 1990s); sole repository for "continuing disclosure" (annual financials, material events); **"alert me" feature** — enter email + CUSIP for notifications; 1.5 million CUSIPs, 1 million issuers

**FINRA.org/marketdata**: TRACE engine for corporate/agency bonds; **BrokerCheck** (850,000 brokers, 17,000 firms — check registration, complaints; toll-free 800-289-9999); **Arbitration/Mediation** (filing fees $50-$1,800; decisions final and binding)

### Shopping for Bonds: Guidelines

1. **Taxable vs. tax-exempt** (retirement accounts → taxables; non-retirement → check taxable-equivalent yield)
2. Browse three websites for yield overview; parameters: maturity, credit quality, then yield (**yield comes last — always a tradeoff with safety**)
3. Check online broker availability — varies tremendously
4. Check trading history — actively traded? Wide or narrow spreads? Compare with similar bonds
5. **Prices are negotiable** — call broker, ask for better price; consulting broker usually costs nothing
6. **Treasury Direct** — cheapest, most efficient for Treasuries
7. Judge broker quality by what they try to sell you — if consistently offered risky "bucket shop" securities, red flag
8. Check your price after purchase on websites — determine if it was fair

## Chapter 6: Treasuries, Savings Bonds, and Federal Agency Paper

### What Makes Treasuries Unique

- Direct U.S. government obligation; most liquid securities; lowest markups of any debt instrument
- Since 1985: **not callable** before maturity (more generous call protection than any other debt)
- Exempt from state and local taxes (adds **50-60bp** effective yield in high-tax states)
- **Flight to quality**: during 2008 panic, stocks and bonds collapsed while Treasuries soared
- Short-maturity Treasuries (under 5 years) may occasionally compete with munis on after-tax basis

> "Before buying any other fixed-income security, check the yield of a Treasury with comparable maturity. Professionals do. Every single debt instrument is priced by professionals off Treasuries."

### Treasury Bills

- Maturity: ≤1 year (3-month, 6-month, 1-year); minimum $10,000
- Non-interest-bearing — sold at discount, redeemed at par; **bond-equivalent yield** 10-20bp higher than discount rate
- Zero credit risk; negligible interest rate risk; yield = proxy for risk-free rate
- Most liquid of all instruments; not appropriate as long-term investments

### Treasury Notes

- Maturity: 2-10 years (2, 3, 5, 7, 10-year offerings)
- Yield advantage: **50-150bp** more than T-Bills (depending on yield curve)
- **Ibbotson study** (1926-1997): average annual return **5.3%** vs. 5.2% for longer Treasuries — higher due to lower volatility

> "What can be said with certainty is that notes continue to have a significantly higher yield than T-bills; and that if you hold the notes until they mature, you will recover invested principal, in full."

### Treasury Bonds

- Maturity: 10-30 years; not callable for 25 years
- Can earn **80-85%** of 30-year yield by staying at/under 10-year maturity, significantly reducing interest rate risk

### TIPS (Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities)

- Introduced 1997; face value adjusted by **CPI-U** monthly; 5, 10, and 30-year maturities
- 10-year coupon ranged from 4.25% (early auctions) to 1.375% (January 2010)
- **How TIPS work** (hypothetical 30-year, $10,000 face, 3% coupon, 2.5% inflation):
  - Year 1 coupon: $150; after year 1 face value rises to $10,250
  - After 10 years: principal ~$12,800; coupon ~$192 twice/year
  - After 30 years: redemption ~$20,975; coupon ~$314.62 twice/year
- **Deflation protection**: redeemed at adjusted value or initial face value, whichever higher
- **More volatile than conventional bonds** — initial assumptions proved wrong
  - Lower coupon = longer duration; sometimes moves on inflation expectations rather than actual inflation
- **"Phantom income"**: inflation adjustment taxed annually though not received until maturity
- Virtually dictates purchase in **tax-sheltered accounts only** (IRAs, retirement accounts)
- **Breakeven rate**: if inflation exceeds breakeven, TIPS outperform; if below, conventional Treasuries win
- **Recommendation**: poor short-term investments (high volatility); for inflation protection, **buy and hold, don't trade**

### TreasuryDirect

- Purchase directly from Fed; **eliminates all transaction costs**; minimum $100, increments of $1,000
- Same yield as institutional investors (noncompetitive tender at average auction yield)
- No fees unless account exceeds $100,000 ($25/year)
- **Sell Direct**: sell before maturity, Fed obtains quotes from several brokers, sells at highest; $45 fee
- Auction calendars: quarterly (February, May, August, November); up to $5 million per individual per auction
- Buying individual Treasuries and holding to maturity guarantees full principal — **bond mutual funds may not**

### Zero Coupon Bonds ("Strips")

- Created 1982 by separating Treasury coupon and principal streams
- Sold at deep discount, matures at par; **no reinvestment risk** — total return known and guaranteed
- $197 in zero at 8.3% → $1,000 in 20 years
- **Extreme volatility** (Exhibit 6.2 — both yielding 7%):
  - Rates rise 50bp: 30-year coupon -5.9% vs. 30-year zero **-13.5%**
  - Rates rise 200bp: 30-year coupon -20.6% vs. 30-year zero **-43.8%**
  - Rates fall 200bp: 30-year coupon +30.9% vs. 30-year zero **+79%**
- **Phantom interest** taxed annually — suitable mainly for tax-sheltered accounts or children in low brackets
- Ideal for known future needs: college savings, retirement annuity via laddering zeros to mature sequentially
- Expensive to purchase small sums — markups high and variable dealer to dealer

### U.S. Savings Bonds

**Series EE**: sold at 50% of face; earns 90% of average 5-year Treasury rate; reset every 6 months; redeemable after 6 months (3-month penalty if <5 years); max $15,000/year purchase; exempt from state/local taxes; tax deferred until cashed

**Series I Bonds** — inflation-indexed accrual bonds:
- Fixed rate (set at purchase, constant for life) + semiannual CPI-U inflation adjustment
- **Value cannot decline** (unlike TIPS which fluctuate in secondary market); in deflation, bond stays at pre-deflation level
- 1998-2000 vintages: cumulative returns **6.03%-6.51%**; November 2009 combined rate: 3.36%
- Guaranteed minimum rate, tax-deferred compounding up to 30 years, no commissions, zero default risk, exempt from state taxes
- Why obscure: brokers can't sell them (no commission); banks don't push them (compete with CDs)
- Maximum purchase reduced to **$10,000/year per SSN** ($5,000 electronic + $5,000 paper)

> "This product is a sleeper: I have seen almost no mention of it in the financial press."

**Education Tax Exclusion**: both EE and I bonds — proceeds for college tuition/fees = **tax-free**
- Must be in parent's name (not child's); income restrictions apply (2008: full exclusion under $100,650 joint)

## Chapter 7: Municipal Bonds

### What Is Unique About Munis

- Issued by state/local governments and entities with public purpose; **federally tax-exempt** (triple tax-free if in-state)
- Only bond sector where primary buyers are individuals; **1.4 million issues outstanding**
- Credit safety second only to Treasuries; default rate extremely low despite publicized defaults
- 2008 was worst year in muni history; 2009 was one of the best

### Major 2008-2010 Developments

1. Failure of major bond insurance firms — almost total disappearance of insurance
2. **Build America Bonds (BABs)** — new taxable munis
3. New rating scales — will upgrade most GO bonds
4. EMMA.msrb.org and Investinginbonds.com bring unprecedented transparency

### Taxable-Equivalent Yield

- Formula: tax-exempt yield ÷ (1 - tax bracket)
- At 39% bracket: 5% muni = **8.2% taxable equivalent**
- At 15% bracket: 5% muni = 5.88% — may earn more buying taxable
- In high-tax states (NY, CA, MA, MN): out-of-state munis sometimes yield more after taxes than in-state
- For discount muni bonds: YTM includes capital gains component (federally taxable) — calculate impact
- **Rule**: highest brackets benefit most; lowest do not; middle depends on current yield relationship

### Credit Quality

- **General obligation (GO)**: backed by taxes and user fees ("unlimited taxing power")
- **Revenue bonds**: backed by specific enterprise revenues (tolls, electricity, patient fees)

### The Rise and Fall of Bond Insurance

- **MBIA, AMBAC, FGIC, FSA** guaranteed muni payments, extending AAA ratings to lower-rated bonds
- By early 2007: **>50% of munis** came to market insured
- Around 2000: insurers expanded into **subprime mortgage bonds** → devastating losses when housing crisis hit
- Three major firms (Ambac, MBIA, FGIC) downgraded to **junk**; essentially stopped writing insurance
- By 2009: only **6% of munis** issued with insurance; **Assured Guaranty** (acquired FSA 2009) = only meaningful insurer remaining
- **Berkshire Hathaway** announced entry in 2008 but has not insured any munis
- Record number of munis issued in 2009 despite lack of insurance — yields actually came down

### Rating Recalibrations (April 2010)

- **Fitch and Moody's** adopted "global" rating scales reflecting munis' vastly lower default rates
- Fitch re-rated 38,000 bonds; 3,750 CUSIPs moved to AAA; 7,500+ moved to AA
- Moody's: California upgraded three notches (Baa1 to A1); at least 14 states rated AA+
- These are NOT upgrades in financial strength — old scales overstated muni risk

> Analogous to a professor deciding exam grades were too severe — the answers haven't changed, but higher grades are being given.

### Safest Municipal Bonds

- **Pre-refunded bonds ("pre-res")**: backed by Treasuries in escrow — **as safe as Treasuries**; short maturities; trade at premium with higher cash flow
- **State bond banks**: quasi-insurance (Maine, Virginia, NY, NJ); **Texas PSF**: $22B fund, AAA-rated
- **"Double-barreled" bonds**: both GO backing and dedicated revenue
- Any bond rated **AA to AAA on its own merit** = extremely safe
- Shorter maturities (≤10 years) add protection — less visibility about possible economic changes

### Letters of Credit (LOCs)

- Bank credit enhancement; does NOT obligate bank to take over interest payments — it's a **line of credit**
- LOCs vary: some irrevocable, others conditional; now used primarily with variable rate demand obligations

### Municipal Bond Pricing

- Markups: under 1% ($100/$10K) for active/short/high-quality to ~4% ($400/$10K) for inactive/long
- Small lots (<$25,000): **marked up harshly** — they "punish the coupon"
- Author's 7-firm experiment: NJ Turnpike bond varied **150bp** ($612 on $40K lot); out-of-state housing bond varied **375bp** ($1,500)
- Same bond on electronic platform: 101 at two firms, 103-105 at others
- Real example (March 2010): same $50K bond sold at 112.596 and 114.975 on same day — 2% price difference, 4% markup
- Appropriate markup: **1-2%** intermediate maturity, **2-4%** long maturity

### Shopping for Munis Online

- Investinginbonds.com "Municipal Market at a Glance" for yield overview
- Browse broker sites: targeted search by state, maturity, credit quality
- **"Material events"**: never buy without finding out what it refers to (often insurer downgrade, not issuer problem)
- **Inter-dealer price = market price**; customer buys carry markup above this
- Trade data on Investinginbonds.com: trades classified as purchase from customer, sale to customer, inter-dealer
- **EMMA**: official statements (all info disclosed to rating agencies), continuing disclosure, trade activity; free alert system
- **Always talk to a broker**: verify ratings, negotiate prices, check call provisions; "firms expect you to call and bargain"
- **Buying at issue**: uniform pricing at par; particularly attractive yields; longest call provisions

### Special Municipal Bond Types

- **Municipal notes** (RANs, TANs, BANs): maturities <3 years; yield 50-100bp more than tax-exempt money markets; limit to impeccable credit quality
- **Municipal zeros**: federally tax exempt (no phantom income tax); subject to call at discounts from par; high volatility especially at long maturities
- **Put bonds**: bondholder can tender back at par — less volatile but lower coupon; if rates decline, appreciate much less
- **Supersinkers**: housing bonds with early retirement; earn long-term yield on short-term security; caution if selling at high premium

### Build America Bonds (BABs)

- Taxable munis created by 2009 stimulus; **35% Treasury subsidy** to issuers (or 35% tax credit to buyers)
- Higher credit quality than many corporates; attracting institutional and foreign buyers
- Primarily **20-30 year maturities** with **make-whole call provisions**
- Make whole: if called, redeemed at higher of par or formula (10-year Treasury + 30-50bp)
- Unintended consequence: reduced supply of long-term tax-exempt bonds

### Discounts vs. Premium Bonds

- **Common misconception**: avoid premiums because you "lose" the premium — FALSE
- Premium bonds generally yield MORE: entire dividend tax-free vs. taxable capital gain on discounts
- Premium bonds: higher cash flow, lower volatility, less likely to be called (if coupon near market)
- **De minimis tax**: discount bonds below threshold (years to maturity × 0.25) — gain taxed as ordinary income, not capital gain
- Warning: watch call provisions on premium bonds — housing bonds often have special early calls

### Author's Strategy Recommendations

**For maximum income and safety**: **8-12 year maturity** munis; capture 80-85% of long-term yield with significantly lower volatility and markups

**Safety hierarchy**: (1) Pre-refunded bonds, (2) Bond bank/double-barreled, (3) AA or higher, (4) Maturities ≤10 years

**Small portfolios (<$50K)**: pre-refunded bonds or high-quality short-intermediate bond fund; if under 25% bracket, taxable bonds may yield more

**Checklist before buying**: maturity? Issuer? Rating (underlying if insured)? Pre- or post-May 2010? Revenue source adequate? Callable? Sinking fund? YTC/YTW? Last trade date/price/type? Comparables?

> "All it takes is for one default to happen, with widespread publicity, for the entire sector to be tarred."

### NYC Default Lesson (1975)

- April 1975: NYC deferred short-term note payment; ultimately paid — no permanent loss
- Warning signs widely reported in press — downgrade should have been anticipated
- **MAC bonds** issued at 10% yields (200bp premium for bad publicity); revenue: special lien on NYC sales tax + stock transfer tax
- By 1990: debt service coverage **11x on first lien, 4-5x on second lien**; upgraded to AA
- **Lesson**: sensational headlines don't equal actual credit risk — informed investors saw opportunity

## Chapter 8: Corporate Bonds

### What Is Unique About Corporates

**Five groups**:
- **Utilities**: Electric and telephone companies; formerly highly regulated and safest corporates, but deregulation changed this (especially telecom)
- **Transportation**: Airlines and railroads
- **Industrials**: Largest, most diverse category — premier corporations (Exxon, GE, IBM) down to junk
- **Finance**: Banks and insurance companies
- **Yankee bonds**: Foreign issuers, dollar-denominated (covered in Chapter 10)

**Collateral types**: mortgage bonds (secured by mortgages), collateral trust bonds (secured by financial obligations), equipment trust certificates (secured by railway rolling stock), debentures/notes (unsecured)

- Higher yields but **fully taxable** at all levels; far more risky and complex than Treasuries
- **Credit quality declining for decades**: very few AAA credits remain; **Berkshire Hathaway** downgraded to AA; almost half now junk (**49% in 2007**)
- Each bond issue gets separate rating based on features and **seniority** (senior debt paid first in default, junior/subordinated paid after)
- 1971-2009 default rates: as low as <1%/year, as high as **12.8% in 2002**; AAA/AA bonds rarely default while ratings in force
- Total return for lower-quality corporates may be lower over time despite higher yields
- **Commercial paper ratings** (short-term): S&P A1 (strongest) to A3 (weakest); Moody's P-1 to P-3

### Event Risk

- 1980s takeover/LBO wave devastated bondholders — massive overnight downgrades, bond prices "dropped like a stone"
- Market response: **poison pill provisions**, floating-rate notes, put bonds — many became permanent features
- **Disney** issued 100-year bonds in 1997-1998; maturities shortened in 1980s due to event risk

### Call Risk and Special Provisions

- Standard: protected against calls for 5-10 years
- **Make-whole call**: par OR formula (Treasury yield + stipulated bp) — floating call price makes calling expensive when rates decline; protects investors; worst case if bought near par: called at par
- **Sinking fund provisions**: certain % retired yearly; trustee chooses by lot, or issuer buys in open market
- **Conditional calls**: triggered by tax law changes
- **Case study — R.R. Donnelley bond**: 11.25% coupon issued January 2009, priced at 128.50 by May 2010; barely investment grade (Baa3/BBB-); make-whole call at 50bp premium to 10-year Treasury; premium bonds pull to par every year

### Corporate Bonds with Special Features

- **Put bonds**: tender back at par at specified intervals; **poison pill puts** triggered by change of control; **"survivor" option** allows estate to put back at par if owner dies
- **Floating-rate notes**: coupon resets periodically (benchmark: T-bill or LIBOR); some have "floor" rate; haven't worked well during extreme volatility; issuers mainly **J.P. Morgan Chase**, **Citibank**
- **Convertible bonds**: exchangeable for common stock; price tracks stock more than rates; regarded as very specialized
- **Equity warrants**: right to purchase issuer stock; **Amazon.com** issued bonds with warrants in 1999 despite junk rating

### Junk Bonds

- **Below investment grade**; primary risk = company may not survive; correlation with stock market higher than bond market
- Typically issued with **5-10 year maturities** (interest rate risk relatively modest)
- Pre-1980s: obscure corner for small number of specialists

**Key history**:
- 1980s: **Michael Milken** / Drexel Burnham Lambert created junk for M&A; spreads initially 400-500bp, narrowed to ~200bp
- 1989-1990: Drexel exit → prices spiraled; spreads to **700bp**, then **1,100bp** (November 1990); bonds at 30-40 cents, YTM 20-40%
- 1990s: prominent issuers shifted to telecoms/start-ups (WorldCom, Global Crossing, Amazon.com)
- 1998: severe decline after Russia defaulted on debt
- Pre-2007: spreads declined to ~260bp (investors behaving as if risk ceased to exist)
- 2008: spreads **>2,500bp**; price declines **40-90%**; **catastrophic**
- 2009: total return **~40%** (high dividend yields + capital gains from spread compression)
- Corporate debt rated below investment grade: 22-30% in 1990s, **49% by 2007**

**Author's verdict**: individual junk bonds = **highly speculative, not worth the risk** for individuals
- Default rates high; predicting which bonds default requires expertise + high luck
- Extremely illiquid; markdown can reach 25-30%; catastrophic declines overnight
- Under $50K: **bond fund only**; over $50K: only if can afford loss and long-term view
- For small portfolios investing for income: **junk bonds are poor choice** despite enticing yields

### Shopping for Corporates (FINRA.org/marketdata)

- TRACE: dealers report trades within 15 minutes; trade modifiers: "B" = customer selling, "S" = buying, "D" = inter-dealer
- **Bid/offer presence** = liquidity indicator for corporates
- **Yield displayed = yield-to-worst** on FINRA; yield-to-maturity on most broker sites
- Always specify "bonds traded within 1-5 days" — omitting filter returns stale months-old prices
- **Company Information Center**: stock price, all bonds outstanding, SEC filings, recent news
- **EDGAR** (SEC): archives disclosure documents for all corporations (foreign and domestic) trading in U.S.
- **Talk to a broker**: corporates more complex and risky than munis — all muni caveats apply "in spades"

### When to Buy Corporates

- Buy investment grade when spread to Treasuries ≥**150bp for 5-10 year maturities**; higher for lower quality
- Strong economy: corporates sometimes outperform Treasuries; recession: corporates decline, "flight to quality" sends Treasury prices soaring
- 2008: Treasuries soared, all other sectors tanked; 2009: extremely high returns for both investment grade and junk
- **Aggregate returns misleading**: boosted by inclusion of lower-rated corporates; for individuals, high-grade spread may not compensate
- **Most appropriate for tax-sheltered accounts** (state tax exemption worth 50-60bp)
- Stick to maturities ≤10 years; best buys often 2-3 year range; monitor ratings continuously
- Unless portfolio >$100K: **bond fund or ETF may be best bet**

## Chapter 9: Mortgage-Backed Securities

### Historical Evolution

- **1970s**: first GNMAs (pass-throughs); **1983**: CMOs introduced
- **Late 1990s**: asset-backed securities (credit cards, auto loans)
- **2003**: CDOs and CDSs emerge — by 2007 largest segment of taxable bond market, dwarfing Treasuries/corporates
- **2008-2009**: sectors freeze; no new CMOs issued; Fed bought >$1 trillion MBS; purchases stopped March 2010

### Why GNMAs Are Unique

- **Unconditional U.S. government backing** (FHA/VA insured mortgages); monthly interest; higher yield than Treasuries
- Minimum purchase $25,000 for new GNMA; primarily institutional
- **Complex securities** — unknown cash flow timing, unknown longevity; anyone claiming GNMAs are "simple" doesn't understand
- Each GNMA unique — two with similar coupons may perform very differently
- **Taxable at federal AND state level** (erases some yield advantage vs. Treasuries)

> "If GNMAs yield more than Treasuries, then they must have features that make them less desirable."

### How Prepayments Affect Cash Flows

- GNMA pools consist of many mortgages; few held full 30 years — homeowners prepay for moves or refinancing
- Prepayments pass through directly: principal returned early, **interest income decreases** (only paid on outstanding balance)
- **Cannot predict** final payment date — yield quotes are "highly inexact estimates"
- Even 30-year pools viewed as **intermediate-term securities** due to prepayments

**When rates decline substantially**: prepayments speed up → get principal back when rates lower → must reinvest at lower rates → GNMA won't appreciate as much → total return substantially lower than anticipated

**When rates rise**: prepayments slow → pool extends longer than anticipated → coupon below market rates → price declines more steeply

- Creates **"heads I lose, tails I lose"** situation

### GNMA Yield Vocabulary

- **PSA model** (Public Securities Association): industry standard; 100% PSA = CPR begins 0.2% month 1, increases 0.2% monthly, levels at 6% after month 30
- **Cash flow yield**: comparable to YTM; meaningless without specific prepayment assumptions
- Example (5.5% coupon GNMA, Exhibit 9.3): yield ranges from 4.5% (rates +300bp, PSA 137) to **-0.46%** (rates -300bp, PSA 1192)
- **Average life**: approximate midpoint when half principal returned; market prices GNMAs on this, **ignores final maturity**
- **Factor**: percentage of mortgages not yet paid down; published by GNMA for all pools
- **Don't confuse yield and cash flow**: monthly check includes principal return (your own money back); some dealers lump all together as "monthly income" = inflated yields

### Par, Discount, and Premium GNMAs

- **Par**: no prepayment history; average life ~12 years; priced on average expectations; outstanding longest
- **Discount**: coupons below current rates; cash flow more predictable; **more interest-rate sensitive** — steeper moves both ways; if rates decline, prepaid discount principal returned at par = boost
- **Premium**: coupons above current rates; high coupon hedges against rate rises; but steep rate declines = **particular risk** — principal prepaid at par rather than premium price = **significant loss of principal**

### GNMA Varieties

- **GNMA I** ("plain vanilla"): 12+ fixed-rate 20-30 year mortgages, ≥$1M face value, all same interest rate
- **Midgets**: 15-year underlying mortgages; much shorter average lives; trade at premium to regular GNMAs with same coupons
- **GNMA II** (since 1983): larger pools, multiple issuers, wider rate range; larger size evens out prepayments somewhat; yield slightly more for complexity
- **GNMA GPMs**: graduated mortgage payments (not fixed rate); cash flows even more complex; smaller, less liquid market
- **GNMA mobile homes**: shorter history, not as exhaustively analyzed

### Stripped Mortgage-Backed Securities

- **IO (interest-only) strips** and **PO (principal-only) strips**; **floaters** (coupon moves with rates) and **inverse floaters** (opposite direction)
- All **extremely sensitive to interest rate changes** — highly volatile
- IO strip: no par value; receives interest only as long as mortgages outstanding; if rates decline and prepayments speed up, interest payments diminish and possibly disappear — declining rates can lead to **disastrous IO price declines**
- As volatile as zero coupon bonds but **much less predictable**
- Used primarily by institutional portfolio managers for hedging; **highly speculative** as standalone securities

### CMOs (Collateralized Mortgage Obligations)

- Cash flows from MBS restructured into **tranches** (3 to 50 per deal); based on much larger pools ($50M-$1B)
- **Sequential pay** ("plain vanilla"): prepayments sent to each tranche in order; early tranches = shorter maturity/lower yield; later = longer/higher yield
- **PAC tranches** (Planned Amortization Class): more predictable average lives; targeted PSA bands
- **Companion tranches**: absorb excess prepayments; more volatile, higher yield; **"busted PAC"** = companion absorbed all targetable prepayments
- **Z (accrual) tranches**: receive no payments (interest or principal) until specified tranches retired; interest "accretes" monthly; resembles zero coupon bonds; extremely volatile; highest yield
- **1993**: interest rate decline → massive prepayments → CMO average lives shortened dramatically
- **1994**: Fed raised rates 7 times → prepayments dried up → **extension risk** → tranches with 2-3 year anticipated lives treated as much longer → prices plummeted
- Companion/Z tranches designed to **limit, not eliminate** prepayment risk; sudden sharp rate changes can wreak havoc
- Purchasable for as little as $1,000

### Ginnie, Fannie, and Freddie

- **GNMA**: government agency within HUD; buys FHA/VA insured mortgages; full government guarantee
- **Fannie Mae**: created 1938; one of largest debt issuers globally; until 2006 traded at 50-100bp over Treasuries
- **Freddie Mac**: initially owned by Federal Home Loan Banks; privatized 1989
- Both GSEs: publicly traded on NYSE, can borrow directly from Treasury = **implicit AAA rating** until crisis
- **Critical distinction**: mortgages purchased by Fannie/Freddie were NOT insured; between 2003-2006 many became subprime

### Private-Label MBS and Subprime

- Starting 2003: **"limited documentation" / NINA ("no income, no assets") / "liar loans"** repackaged into private-label CMOs
- Many with teaser rates, 100% financing (no down payment)
- Banks assumed housing prices would continue rising; few kept mortgages on books — most promptly sold and repackaged
- Rated AAA based on **"overcollateralization"** and flawed diversification assumptions (models built on as little as **5 years of data**)
- Rating agencies assumed unlikely for losses to occur simultaneously across all regions
- By 2006: **~50% of MBS based on subprime** mortgages
- When housing prices declined nationally: homeowners walked away → servicers lost cash flows → AAA bonds **downgraded to junk overnight**

### CDOs and Credit Default Swaps

- **CDOs**: extremely complex pools — can contain corporate bonds, emerging market bonds, RMBS, CMBS, ABS, bank loans, or **other CDOs** ("CDOs-squared")
- Analytics behind risk profiles amounted to **guesswork** — mathematical models built on faulty assumptions
- **CDSs** ("synthetic CDOs"): insurance on CDOs, often **owned by third parties**
- Sellers of swaps **not required to hold reserves** (unlike insurance companies); called "swaps" rather than "insurance" potentially to **skirt reserve requirements**
- CDS market at peak **dwarfed any other** — several times larger than global GDP (notional amounts)
- If firm had good information about troubled assets, buying CDSs against it was a smart short

> "A synthetic CDO does not actually own the pool of assets on which it has the risk." — Frank Fabozzi

### The 2007-2008 Financial Crisis

- Began with subprime defaults; giant step when Treasury forced sale of **Bear Stearns** to **J.P. Morgan**
- Followed by **bankruptcy of Lehman Brothers**
- CDOs so complex that rating or pricing was guesswork; rumors about assets on bank books
- Firms stopped trading (feared non-repayment); banks stopped lending; buyers evaporated or bid at fire sale levels
- Government injected massive capital into largest banks and **AIG**
- **Fannie/Freddie**: conservatorship (~80% taxpayer-owned); bonds retain explicit government guarantee
- Fed bought >$1 trillion MBS to inject liquidity; stopped March 2010
- **Fed exit strategy unknown**: could sell, hold to maturity, or sell to hedge funds; massive selling could trigger rate rises
- Uncertainty will **overhang mortgage-backed market for years**

### CMO Market After 2008
- CMO market **largely ground to halt** in 2008-2009; no new CMOs issued
- At time of writing, only a couple of new deals in pipeline — **structured very carefully with high-quality mortgages**
- Brokers selling only **plainest GNMA I pools** (30-year mortgages, same rate) — no GNMA II, no CMOs, no asset-backed securities
- Online brokers **do not list mortgage-backed securities** on websites
- To investigate buying MBS, need to **ask broker to find** a specific security
- **Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac agency bonds** available — not MBS but debt issued by agencies
  - Under government conservatorship, carry **explicit government guarantee**, rated AAA
  - Prices published on FINRA and **Investinginbonds.com**

### CDOs/CDSs Warning
- Always **institutional products** — never intended for individuals
- Market operates **largely out of public view**
- Cost of CDSs becoming **accepted proxy for measuring risk**
- Proposal to create exchange with trading requirements — resisted by large financial institutions (trading **hugely profitable**)
- History: securities designed for institutions are **re-packaged for individual investors**

### Buying Guidelines for GNMAs
- Only makes sense if yields **higher than Treasuries by 100+ bps**
- Ask to see **cash flow yield** based on variety of prepayment assumptions with spelled-out terms
- Compare GNMAs to Treasuries on **estimated average life, not stated final maturity**
- For CMOs: understand underwriting standards, deal size, seniority of tranche — **if not clear, do not buy**
- Questions to ask: What kind of yield quoted? What prepayment assumptions? What type of tranche? First payment date? Can you resell?
- Small portfolios (under $50,000): **best bet is a conservatively managed bond fund**

### Tax Considerations
- Interest on MBS subject to **federal and state taxes**; return of principal is not taxed
- Essential to **maintain good records** — know how much is interest vs. principal or will overpay taxes
- Prepayments and early sales may trigger **capital gain or loss**
- Tax considerations complex — advisable to **consult tax adviser** if large portfolio

## Chapter 10: International Bonds

### Currency Risk
- **Currency risk** (also called **exchange-rate risk**) can dwarf interest income and price changes
- Over short periods (year or less), currency swings often the **most significant component of total return**
- Currency conversion occurs at **both ends** of the transaction
- Dollar-yen exchange rate has ranged from **80 to 200 yen per dollar** over two decades
- A bond can have positive return in local currency but negative total return in dollar terms (and vice-versa)
- **1998 Asian crisis**: bonds lost as much as **50% or more overnight** — just months earlier, these economies hailed as "Asian miracle"

> "Over short periods (a year or less), fluctuations in the value of currency are often the most significant component of total return, dwarfing both interest income and changes in price resulting from interest rate movements."

### Currency Hedging
- Bond funds investing in foreign-pay bonds may **hedge** to reduce currency risk
- If investing to hedge against falling dollar, want fund that does **not** hedge
- Want dollar to **decline** against foreign currency you are buying
- Hedging reduces volatility but eliminates potential gains from dollar weakness

### Emerging Markets: Brady Bonds
- Named after Treasury Secretary **Nicholas Brady**; originated from **1982 Latin American debt defaults**
- Mexico declared moratorium on debt payments; other Latin American countries followed
- Left largest U.S. banks holding ~**$100 billion** in defaulted loans
- Banks swapped defaulted loans for bonds with:
  - Steep write-off of loan amount
  - Stretched-out repayment (up to **30 years**)
  - Governments agreed to implement economic reforms (IMF/World Bank requirements)
- Bonds collateralized with **U.S. Treasury zeros** — in event of default, collateral paid on original maturity date (not at default)
- Paid **15% to 25%** yields initially
- Became "one of the most remarkable success stories in international finance"

**Success metrics:**
- By early 2007, spreads to Treasuries narrowed to **2% to 4%**
- Mexico sovereign debt upgraded to **investment grade**
- Russia went from **default to investment grade in five years**
- Latin American countries retired Brady bonds well before initial maturity dates
- Market differentiation replaced herd behavior — countries now viewed on stand-alone basis

**Geographic expansion:**
- Beyond Latin America: China, India, South Korea, Poland, Czechoslovakia, Baltic countries
- 2000-2007: many countries benefited from rising commodity prices
- Trend to issue debt denominated in **local currency** rather than U.S. dollars

### Emerging Market Risks
- **Argentina (2001)**: defaulted on **$95 billion** of debt
  - Prior to default: pegged debt to U.S. dollar, hailed as model of fiscal discipline
  - After default: paying ~**35 cents on the dollar**
  - Creditors holding $20 billion refused restructuring, suing government
- **Greece** (at time of writing): bailout structured by IMF; fear of spread to Spain, Portugal
- Markets extremely thin; one or two issuers may dominate particular markets
- Capital markets poorly developed; political risk high; bonds highly illiquid
- If market seizes up, **illiquidity itself causes major losses**

### Buying International Bonds
- U.S. dollar-denominated bonds available from most brokerage firms, listed with U.S. corporates
- Provision identical to U.S. corporate bonds: conditional calls, make-whole calls
- Yields (YTM) on barely investment-grade long-term bonds: below 6% — spread to 30-year Treasuries (~4.25%) below 200 bps

**Foreign-pay bonds — far more difficult:**
- **Merrill Lynch response** (typical): only for "high net worth" clients; official policy to discourage individuals
- Suggests doing so through bond funds
- Exceptions: foreign nationals living in U.S., U.S. citizens doing business abroad

**EverBank World Currency CDs:**
- FDIC-insured, denominated in foreign currencies
- Terms: 3 months, 6 months, 1 year; minimum $10,000 (single) or $20,000 (multi-currency)
- South African rand: **4.88%** (3-month); Mexican peso: **4.04%** (3-month)
- Japanese yen: **0%** (not a misprint); Canadian dollar: **0.10%**
- Total return governed primarily by **currency movement**, not interest earned
- FDIC protects against bank failure, **NOT** currency losses

**Foreign Currency ETFs:**
- Single currency (Australia, Brazil, China, Japan, etc.)
- Multi-currency (emerging markets, Asia regions)
- Leveraged/inverse currency ETFs — **extremely risky**

### The Case For/Against International Bonds
- **Diversification**: mainly relevant for very large institutional portfolios; for individuals with few bonds, "irrelevant"
- **Higher returns**: must be right about timing AND which currency
  - As **Yogi Berra** said: "making predictions is very difficult, especially for the future"
- **Hedging against falling dollar**: strongest argument for individuals
  - Predicting dollar value "not for amateurs"; timing has proved elusive
- If investing for **safe and predictable income**, arguments "not very convincing"
- Best method at current time: **bond funds and ETFs**, but only for small portion of portfolio
- If primarily speculating on higher returns, potentially highest return sector is **emerging market bonds**

## Part Three: Investing through Funds

### Bond Funds Overview
- Few bond funds existed prior to 1980s
- **Explosive growth during 1980s**: more money to bond funds than stock funds
- Starting 2009: about **10x as much money** went into bond funds as stock funds
- Returns of some categories almost as variable and difficult to predict as equities
- Advertising themes: high yield and safety of principal — **fine print: assets can go down as well as up**

## Chapter 11: Bond Funds — Overview and Selection

### NAV, Yield, and Total Return

**Net Asset Value (NAV):**
- Calculated daily: Total Net Assets / shares outstanding
- All bond fund NAVs fluctuate **except** money market funds ($1 constant)
- Common error: believing high-quality bond funds (Treasuries, GNMAs) cannot decline in principal value
- Bond funds are NOT certificates of deposit

**SEC Standardized Yield:**
- Snapshot of actual returns for past **30 days**
- Components: interest income, price changes as bonds move toward par, minus expenses
- Before **1988 SEC rule**, funds manipulated and inflated yield quotes
- Not comparable to YTM of individual bonds
- **Distribution yield**: based solely on dollar amount of dividends for previous month

> "Bond funds are marketed to investors on the basis of yield. That is what all the ads trumpet. But yield is only one of the factors that determine total return."

**Total Return:**
- Only accurate long-term measure = dividends + capital gains + interest-on-interest +/- NAV changes
- Example: 5% dividend yield + 10% NAV decline = **-5% total return** ($10,000 → $9,500)
- Example: 5% dividend yield + 10% NAV increase = **+15% total return** ($10,000 → $11,500)
- **Average returns** smooth out fluctuations but only apply if held for entire averaged period
- Total return tables overstate returns: **commissions/loads** not subtracted, **taxes** not accounted for
- Past returns do NOT guarantee future returns

> "If you are researching a bond fund, whatever your source of information may be, the returns listed are total returns, rather than yield. It is critical that you understand this distinction."

### Costs of Bond Funds

**Loads and Fees:**
- Standard commissions: **4%-5.75%**; "low-load" funds: **1.5%-3.5%**
- Graduated loads: 4% for <$100,000; 3.25% for $100-250K; 2.5% for >$250K
- Exit fees: 6% declining to 0% over 5-7 years
- **12b-1 fees**: capped at 25 bps (no-load) or 75 bps (load)
  - 0.75% x 10 years = **7.5% equivalent commission** (understates true cost — charged on ever-larger amounts)
- **Share classes**: A (front-end load), B (12b-1 + deferred charge), C (no load but higher 12b-1)

**Expense Ratios:**
- **Vanguard**: ~20 basis points (industry lowest)
- **T. Rowe Price Summit, Fidelity Spartan**: ~50 bps capped
- High end: ~2% annual expenses (not including 12b-1 fees)

**Why Expenses Matter (John Bogle argument):**
- $10,000 at 4% yield: Fund A (20 bps) = $380 income; Fund B (1.5%) = $250 income — **32% difference** in year one
- **Contrarian claim**: load funds offer no advantages over no-load funds
- No-load funds are low-expense leaders as a group; load funds charge higher expenses on top of commissions — **investors doubly penalized**
- High-expense funds take more risk to compete — **outstanding one year, much less stellar the next**

> "Over long holding periods, differences in expense ratios compound to lower returns by significant amounts."

- **First cut**: only consider funds with <50 bps expense ratio, no commissions, no 12b-1 fees

### Interest Rate Risk in Funds

**Duration as volatility guide:**
- Fund's duration ≈ NAV movement for 100 bps rate change
- Long-term Treasury fund, duration 8 years: rates rise 1% → NAV declines ~**8%**
- Sept 1998-Jan 1999: Treasury long bond yield 4.8% → 6.7% — long-term fund NAVs declined **15%-20%**

> "Money invested in high-quality credits (AAA municipal bonds, GNMAs, or Treasuries) is totally risk free. At the risk of being tiresome, I repeat: that guarantee extends only to credit quality."

**Duration parameters:**
- Short (1-3 years): duration 1-2 years, low volatility
- Intermediate (8-12 years maturity): duration 4-5 years
- Long-term (15-25 years maturity): duration 6-8 years
- Zero funds: duration = average maturity — NAV may move **25% for 1% yield change**

**Key difference from individual bonds:**
- Individual bond: maturity declines each year, price moves toward par
- Bond fund: maintains **constant maturity** — interest rate risk remains equally constant throughout ownership

### How Funds Destroy Principal
- Author uses term **"destruction of principal"** (not just "NAV erosion") — permanent loss, not temporary fluctuation
- **Premium bond strategy**: stuffing portfolios with premium bonds to advertise higher yield; when called at par → permanent NAV loss. Government bond funds and GNMA funds: "among the most flagrant abusers"
- **Derivatives**: broad spectrum from options/futures to obscure instruments, constantly being created
  - Two uses: (1) hedging (reduce risk, raise costs) vs (2) boosting yield (magnifies both gains and losses)
  - **1992-1993 inverse floaters**: worked as intended → municipal fund gains. **1994**: rates turned up → inverse floater prices plummeted → biggest 1994 losers
- **Red flags**: returns high vs similar funds; promises of "sophisticated" or "state of the art" quantitative strategies; if you don't understand strategy, find another fund

### Sell-Off Risk
- **1989-1990**: Drexel Burnham Lambert collapse — junk fund NAVs declined **50%** in six months
- **1998**: emerging market fund NAVs declined **50%+**
- **1994, 1999**: interest rate rises across Treasury market — all sectors experienced losses
- **2008**: massive flight to quality — every sector except Treasuries experienced epic losses; sellers found no buyers except at fire-sale prices
- Despite 2008 panic, most funds maintained operations — investors could sell shares (with steep losses)
- Credit risk less significant for fund holders (diversification) — exception: junk/emerging market funds

### Fund vs Individual Bonds

**Funds better for:**
- Portfolios under $50,000 in non-Treasuries
- Complex securities (junk, international, MBS) — funds provide efficient access
- Diversification, convenience, **monthly** dividends (vs semiannual for individual bonds)
- More frequent compounding boosts return over long periods

**Individual bonds better for:**
- **Principal preservation** — main difficulty with funds is can't guarantee full principal recovery when selling
- Larger portfolios buying at good prices; buy-and-hold strategy
- For highest safety: laddered portfolio of short-to-intermediate Treasuries (include TIPS)
- Intermediate Treasuries 5-10 years: **80-90% of long-term return** with far less volatility
- No fund expenses reducing returns

### Selecting Bond Funds
- Don't buy last year's top performer — **top performers are riskiest**
- **Michael Lipper**: fund manager wanting to be top of peer group must be willing to risk being at bottom next year
- Look for **conservatively managed funds** with 5+ year track record, consistently in **top 20%** (Lipper or Morningstar)
- Focus on: **expense ratio, duration, credit quality, actual annual returns for 10 years**

**Morningstar:**
- Star ratings (1-5): top 10% = 5 stars, main determinant is performance
- Many investors buy only 4-5 star funds — but 5-star bond funds go down in down markets just like others
- **Don't focus on stars** — focus on expense ratio, duration, credit quality, and style box
- **Lipper database** (used by Barron's, WSJ) more complete; classifies funds differently from Morningstar
- **Lipper Fund Screener**: funds.us.reuters.com — totally quantitative, can screen by expense ratio, consistent returns, capital preservation

### Volatility and Holding Periods

**Short-term (1-5 years):**
- Never buy long-term fund (or volatile NAV fund) for short holding periods
- NAV changes affect total return far more than interest income
- Simple rule: don't buy volatile funds if you need money soon

**Long-term (10+ years):**
- For conservatively managed funds, reinvested interest income (compounded) becomes main component
- Rising rates eventually lead to higher total return because you're reinvesting at higher rates
- Even over long periods, total return never guaranteed — catastrophic decline late can devastate returns (2008)
- **Match investments to risk tolerance** — investors often overestimate comfort with volatility
- Real loss makes you realize risk tolerance isn't as high as you thought

### Fund Protection
- **Investment Company Act of 1940**: fund assets held by custodian (bank), segregated from management company
- If management company fails, fund assets protected — but NOT covered by **SIPC**
- No protection against **market risk** — if manager makes bad investments, no recourse
- Many fund groups carry "blanket fidelity bond" insurance against fraud/malfeasance

### Taxes and Bond Funds
- Switching between funds (even same family) = **sale for tax purposes**
- **Cost basis** includes initial investment + reinvested dividends + capital gains distributions
- Example: $10,000 invested, $4,000 distributions over 3 years → cost basis $14,000 (not $10,000)
- Buying just before distribution = taxed immediately even if owned one day
- Tax-exempt fund capital gains distributions are **taxable** at capital gains rate
- **Average cost basis** method most common for determining tax liability
- Maintain good records — keep all records for one fund in one folder

## Chapter 12: Money Market Funds and Tax-Exempt Bond Funds

### Money Market Fund Crisis of 2008
- Sept 16, 2008: **Reserve Primary Fund** (oldest money market fund) held worthless Lehman Brothers debt
- Fund "broke the buck" — NAV declined to ~**97 cents/share**
- Not major loss vs stocks/bonds, but earthquake for money markets (price never supposed to drop below $1)
- Run on fund (primarily institutional investors); fund suspended redemptions same day
- Investors eventually recovered ~**99%** after lengthy SEC dispute
- Panic spread: run on money funds suddenly loomed as real possibility
- **Treasury Secretary Henry Paulson** issued federal guarantee (up to $250,000); expired Sept 2009
- Since 2009, **63 money market funds exited or merged**

### Money Market Fund Characteristics
- Invest in securities with maturities **60 days or less** (reduced from 90 days post-2008)
- ~**70% of taxable bond fund assets** in money market funds as of May 2010
- ~**60% of money market fund assets are institutional**, used for parking cash short-term
- Yields at time of writing: **5 to 30 basis points** annualized
- **1980s yields extremely high**: briefly reached **20%** in 1981
- **1990s-2008**: yields ranged between **6% high and 2% low**
- With ultra-short rates between zero and 15 bps, annual expenses exceed interest income — many sponsors **absorbing management fees**
- Major innovation: made **high money market yields previously available only to institutions accessible to individual investors**

**Three categories:**
- **General funds** (largest): T-bills, insured CDs, commercial paper, agency debt, repos
- **Treasury-only**: lower yields but interest **exempt from state taxes**; impeccable credit quality
- **Government**: Treasuries plus very short-term agency debt

**Expense ratios:**
- **Vanguard**: 15-30 bps (low-cost leader)
- Industry average: 50 bps; some reach 100 bps

**Historical returns (through Dec 31, 2009):**

| Fund Type | 2008 | 2009 | 5-Year | 10-Year |
|-----------|------|------|--------|---------|
| General Money Market | 2.04% | 0.17% | 2.66% | 2.47% |
| Treasury-Only | 1.12% | 0.03% | 2.26% | 2.25% |
| Tax-Exempt | 1.69% | 0.16% | 1.80% | 1.76% |

**Post-2008 regulatory changes:**
- Average weighted maturity reduced from 90 to **60 days**
- At least **10% of assets must mature in one day**; **30% in one week**
- Controversial proposal: allow money fund prices to **"float"** (opponents say this would destroy money funds as currently exist)

**Portfolio guidelines:**
- Not likely to keep up with inflation; **not appropriate as long-term investments** except ≤20% of portfolio
- Retirees: at least **one year of expenses** in extremely safe assets
- **1999**: money market funds had positive returns while most longer-term bond funds posted **-10% to -12%**
- If safety main concern: **Treasury-only money funds** or insured bank money market accounts

### Municipal Bond Funds

**Categories:**
- **General**: largest subgroup, at least 65% investment grade, geographically diversified
- **High-yield**: at least 50% below investment grade (tobacco bonds, nursing homes, private activity bonds)
- **Short** (≤3 years), **Short-intermediate** (3-5 years), **Intermediate** (5-10 years)
- **Single-state**: exempt from federal, state, and local taxes for residents; Lipper lists **27 states**

**2008-2009 Performance:**
- High-yield 2008: average **-25%**; better performers: -12% to -13%; worst: **-30% to -50%**
- **Oppenheimer Rochester Municipal fund**: yielded 13.8%, lost almost 50% total return; NAV decline over **60%**
- High-yield 2009: average gain **~31%**
- 10-year high-yield returns through Dec 2008: barely positive **0.57%** average compounded annual
- Even with outsize 2009 gains, 10-year returns of high-yield and general groups **remained below intermediate funds**

**Sweet spot — intermediate funds (5-10 year maturity):**
- 2008: average **close to flat** (some better-performing funds posted small gains)
- 2009: average total return **~11%** gain
- **Highest average total returns** of any groups for both 5-year and 10-year periods

**Short funds (1-2 year maturity) — genuine safe harbor:**
- Positive total returns in 2008, 1994, and 1999 (all bad years for long-term funds)
- 10-year (1998-2008): **2.85%** vs high-yield **0.57%**

**December 2008 anomaly:**
- Munis became **"amazingly cheap"** relative to Treasuries
- Typical "cheap" threshold: muni yields 85%-95% of Treasuries (10+ years maturity)
- **December 2008 ratios: 150%-300%** of Treasuries at every point on yield curve — unprecedented

### Short and Ultra-Short Municipal Bond Funds
- **Average-weighted maturity**: 1-3 years (short) or 1-2 years (ultra-short)
- Rationale: higher yield than money markets, negligible volatility due to short duration
- Positive total returns in 2008 (when long-term funds crashed), also in 1994 and 1999
- **Reinvestment risk** significantly higher than longer-term funds
- When interest rates unstable and future trend cloudy — attractive choice
- Current caution: rates below 1%, NAV will decline when rates rise — money markets may prove better temporarily

**Insured municipal bond funds:**
- Bond insurance firms **downgraded well below investment grade** after 2008
- Insurance no longer viewed as conferring protection; many funds merged with general funds
- If high credit quality priority: choose funds investing in **AAA or AA bonds** with intermediate maturity, duration ≤5 years

### Future Factors Affecting Municipal Bonds
- **Negative**: prolonged recession, state/local government financial difficulties, downgrades (but muni default rates extremely low even in Great Depression)
- **Positive**: federal tax increase → munis more attractive to larger investor group → price support

### 2008 and 2009 in Perspective
- Both years outliers, well outside "normal" range
- 2008 losses NOT from rising rates (unlike 1994, 1999) — caused by bond insurer demise, unprecedented credit spread widening, severe buyer/seller imbalance
- 2008 was worst year ever in muni bond market — losses "punished" risk out of proportion to real risks

## Chapter 13: Taxable Bond Funds

### Fund Naming Problems
- "Government" fund may include Treasuries, agencies, GNMAs, zeros, or any combination
- "Government-only" funds often invest mainly in GNMA bonds
- "Corporate," "income," "high income" very vague — may hold stocks AND bonds
- Before investing: investigate securities held, credit quality, maturity/duration

### Ultra-Short Funds (90 days to 1 year)

**Disasters in this category:**
- **Short world, multi-market funds** (early 1990s): invested in foreign-pay bonds, hedged currency risk — strategy failed when foreign rates declined and dollar rose
- **ARM funds** (1990s): marketed as low-risk alternative to money funds — extremely disappointing returns
- **"Yield plus" funds** (most recent disaster):
  - **Schwab "yield plus" fund**: most popular Schwab bond fund, >**$13 billion** assets
  - 2008: loss over **35%**; additional **15% loss** in 2009
  - Substantial percentage in longer-term mortgage-backed securities (some subprime)
  - Sales literature did not accurately disclose holdings; investors suing Schwab
- Top 20% all had positive returns: **1.5%-4.20%**; bottom 40% were disasters
- Successful funds invest in **higher quality credits or government-guaranteed bonds**
- If investing in ultra-short: check both maturity AND credit quality
- Alternative: buy individual Treasuries through **TreasuryDirect** — no credit risk, no need for diversification

### Short U.S. Government and Treasury Funds (1-3 Years)
- Range of returns remarkably narrow; most show positive returns for all periods
- Treasury-only 2008: average **>6%** (plummeting yields caused NAVs to soar)
- U.S. government category 2009: average **~4.5%**

### Short and Intermediate Investment Grade
- **Short investment grade**: many have "low duration" or "short term" in name
- 2009: average **9.61%** (enormous rally in investment grade debt)
- ~Half had positive 2008 returns; lower half included one **69% loss** (subprime mortgages)

### Intermediate Investment Grade (5-10 Years) — The Sweet Spot
- Holds **~40% of all taxable bond fund assets** (~580 funds in 2009)
- **Pimco Total Return Fund**: largest bond fund (and largest of all mutual funds), >**$220 billion**
  - Tracks largest U.S. index categories, includes international and emerging market debt
  - Has **3.75% load** — actual returns must be adjusted
- **Vanguard Total Return Bond Index Fund**: ~$80 billion, tracks **Barclays Capital U.S. Aggregate Index**, holds >7,000 bonds
- Duration <5 years → modest interest rate risk
- 2008: ~**-4.5%**; 2009: ~**+13%**
- 5-year and 10-year returns almost as high as longer maturities with far less volatility
- 2008 worst performers: losses >20%; one catastrophic **84% loss** (subprime)

### Long-Term Taxable Funds (10+ Years)
- Treasury-only 2008: average returns close to **20%** (flight-to-quality buying)
- Treasury-only 2009: losses averaging **~6.5%** (rates rose at long end)
- Corporate A rated: 2008 average **-6%** vs BBB average **-9%**
- Corporate BBB 2009: average gains **21%** vs A group **~15%** — riskier funds had both higher losses and gains

### TIPS Funds
- Volatility **extremely high** despite impeccable credit quality
- 2008: average loss ~4% (unlike other Treasury funds that soared); 2009: gain **10.75%**
- 2002: returns soared to ~**16%** — created excitement, funds multiplied to >100
- 2002 high returns due to **interest rate changes and high demand** (price appreciation), not inflation adjustment (~2.5%)
- **Main reason to invest**: inflation protection, not short-term outperformance
- Individual TIPS at auction through Treasury Direct = cheapest; any fund charges expenses
- Not all TIPS funds hold only TIPS — some add foreign government TIPS, commodities, junk bonds ("real return" or "real income" in name)
- Only invest in no-load TIPS fund with low expenses (Vanguard: 20 bps)

### GNMA Funds
- Two appealing themes: high dividend yield (tracks mortgage rates) + government backing
- 2008: **+5.24%** (government backing proved critical); U.S. Mortgage funds: **-1.61%**
- 10-year return: GNMA **5.70%**; Mortgage funds **5.02%**
- Interest income typically **0.5-1%** lower than current 30-year mortgage rates
- Advantages over individual GNMAs: lower initial investment ($1,000 vs $25,000), mitigate prepayment hazards

### Junk Bond Funds
- Dec 31, 1999 peak: **$100+ billion** (40% of taxable fund assets); Spring 2010: ~$70 billion (<10%)
- Only bond fund type where primary risk is **credit quality**
- Recent spreads: 250 bps (pre-2007) to **2,000+ bps** (end of 2008, yields >25%)
- 2008: **-25.86%**; 2009: **+46.41%** (highest aggregate gain in sector history)
- 10-year: **4.80%** — lower than intermediate government bonds (5.51%) and GNMAs (5.70%)

> "If we had shown 10-year total returns through December 31, 2008, the return was a barely positive 1.2%, less than you would have earned on a riskless money market fund."

- 2008: one fund lost **78%**; others declined 35-57%; some gained 8-10%
- Conservative junk funds: less volatile, more consistent, better long-term performance
- **Recommendation**: safe/predictable portfolio — view as speculative, not worth risk
- Large portfolio ($300,000+) that can sustain losses: consider when spreads wide, **max 20%**

### Multi-Sector Funds
- Managers free to select any bond, any sector, any maturity, any nationality; may use leverage
- 2008: **-14.31%**; 2009: **+28.17%**; best fund +9%, worst fund **-36%**

> "There is simply no way to predict how any fund in this sector will perform without analyzing the record and management style of its manager."

### International Bond Funds
- Total return dominated by **currency changes** — investing = bet against dollar
- **Three types** (Lipper): Global (includes U.S. debt), International (excludes U.S.), Emerging Market
- Global funds (2008: -5.09%; 10-year: 6.18%) vs International (2008: +2.00%; 10-year: 6.30%)
- Pattern: dollar declines → funds do well; dollar rises → funds do poorly; 2008 provided no shelter
- **Key selection factor**: currency hedging strategy — unhedged = purer dollar decline play
- High expenses: typically **1.5-2%**; with 5% load, giving up year's income first year

### Emerging Market Bond Funds
- 2008: **-17.46%**; 2009: **+32.49%**; 10-year: **11.09%** (highest of any taxable category)
- 10-year returns start after 1998 crash (Russia default, 50%+ losses in few months)
- **BRIC countries** (Brazil, Russia, India, China) now regarded as high-growth areas
- Paradoxically, positive attitudes may cause returns to be more in line with developed countries
- Any investment should be viewed as **speculative and suitable only for money you can afford to lose**

### Loan Participation Funds
- Invest in **bank loans** (subprime); interest resets periodically based on **LIBOR** spread
- Money redeemable only **one day each quarter**
- Marketed as higher-yielding alternatives to money funds — "stars" or "savvy investor" corner
- **Reality**: higher rates always go hand-in-hand with higher risk — no exception
- 2008 losses averaging **well above 25%**; 10-year return through Dec 2009: shade under 3%
- 10-year through Dec 2008: **actually negative, loss of almost 1%**

> "But once again, if you want to invest in safe assets, why take the risk of investing in this sector?"

### Bond Index Funds
- Passively managed; two advantages: **low expense ratio** and **low turnover**
- **Vanguard Total Bond Market Index Fund**: largest, tracks Barclays U.S. Aggregate, 8.8 years maturity, duration 5 years
- ~90 bond index funds available; Vanguard offers ~28; Fidelity: 10
- **Ultimate plain vanilla funds** — risk factors straightforward
- Choose duration close to how long you plan to own fund → lowers interest rate risk

### General Do's and Don'ts for Bond Funds

**Before buying any bond fund**, clear understanding of objectives and policies:
- If objective states "speculative securities well below investment grade" = **junk bonds**
- Risk factors should be taken seriously even if recent performance excellent

**For reliable, steady income:**
- Buy **no-load fund** with low expense ratio, no 12b-1 fees, conservative management track record
- Look for funds that haven't gone down too much in bear markets but done reasonably well in bull markets
- If fund ranks in **top quartile (25%+)** of its Lipper or Morningstar category = likely consistent performer

**Practical rules:**
- Know exactly what securities are in the fund before buying
- Check current maturity length or duration
- Don't invest more than **20%** of bond portfolio in any single long-term, international, or junk bond fund
- Only invest in specialized funds with well-diversified portfolios (minimum **$200,000**)
- Complex securities (international, GNMAs, junk, corporates) better owned via funds
- Taxable bond funds appropriate for **tax-deferred accounts**
- For non-tax-advantaged money: calculate whether taxable or tax-exempt yields higher net-after-tax return
- **Don't buy any fund whose share price fluctuates if you need money in less than a year**
- If investing primarily for "income": don't buy longest-term funds or those with highest stated yields

**Historical context:**
- Past 30 years witnessed **unprecedented bull market in bonds** — future returns will be "a lot more modest"
- 200-300 bps rate rise from current levels → **25-30% principal loss** for longest-term funds
- **The do-it-yourself bond fund**: safest route for principal preservation — buy Treasuries through **TreasuryDirect** (zero expense, zero commission) or build ladder of AAA munis (1-7 years)

## Chapter 14: Closed-End Funds, ETFs, and Unit Investment Trusts

### Closed-End Bond Funds (CEFs)
- ~600 funds, **$112 billion** assets vs ~$4.8 trillion in open-end funds
- Slightly more than half invest in municipals
- ~**85% use leverage** (95% for municipal CEFs); typical: ~30% of net assets
- Shares trade on exchanges like stocks; two values: **NAV** and **market price**
- Share price can move independently of bond values — even in opposite directions

**Premium/Discount dynamics:**
- At issue: hidden fees of **5%-8%** (underwriter commissions) — **never buy at issue**
- After underwriter "support" period, many begin trading at discount to NAV
- 10% discount = purchasing dollar's worth of bonds for 90 cents → boosts yield from 5.5% to 6.1%

**Leverage effect:**
- Borrow at short-term rates, buy longer-term bonds with higher interest
- **Optimal**: steep yield curve, low borrowing costs
- **Backfire**: flat/inverted curve → triple whammy: dividends cut, NAV declines, price plummets
- As of June 2010: ~57% of muni CEFs leverage with **auction rate securities** at penalty rates under 50 bps — situation unsustainable if short-term rates rise

**Yield cautions:**
- Quoted yield = distributions / market price (**current yield**) — not comparable to YTM or SEC yield
- **"Not exactly yield"** (**Thomas Herzfeld**): payouts may include capital gains and return of capital
- **Managed payout policies**: some return principal when income falls short — not income or "yield"
- Egregious example: fund with **0.79% income ratio** but >14% "yield" — $0.06/share income, $2.09 return of capital; trading at **44% premium** to NAV
- No standardized yield formula like SEC yield for open-end funds
- **Income ratio** (net investment income / NAV) more accurate but published only twice yearly

**2008-2009 performance:**
- Municipal CEF average 2008 (market price): **-22%**; best: +8%; worst: **-48.6%**
- Taxable CEF average 2008: **-23.4%**; best: +24.7%; worst: **-70%**
- Loan participation: 2008 average **-44%**, 2009 average **+84%**
- Several Pimco municipal CEFs suspended dividends; some 2009 gains exceeded **70%** (market price)
- 20% market price swing in a few months not uncommon; steeper in 2008

**Buying rules:**
- **Never buy at issue**
- Only buy at **wide discount to NAV** — that's when yield would be particularly attractive
- **Herzfeld strategy**: buy only when discount ≥3% wider than normal for that fund; sell when discount narrows
- Strategy "deceptively simple" — leverage magnifies losses; discounts can continue widening
- Sources: **cefconnect.com** (Nuveen), **cefa.com**, Herzfeld newsletter ($625/year)

**Questions to ask before buying a CEF:**
- Am I buying at issue? What is the underwriting spread?
- What is the NAV? Is the fund at premium or discount? What is average discount?
- Does the fund use leverage? How much and what kind?
- What is the quoted "yield" vs the most recent "income ratio"?
- Does the fund have "managed payout" policies?
- What bonds are in the portfolio? Average maturity and credit quality?

### Exchange-Traded Funds (ETFs)
- First bond ETFs: July 2002 by **Barclays Global Investors** (now BlackRock) — four funds
- As of writing: **well over 100** bond ETFs, ~$116 billion assets
- Expense ratios: **9-60 basis points** (Treasury lowest); leveraged/exotic: up to 95 bps

**How ETFs work:**
- **Authorized Participants (APs)** create/redeem large baskets ("creation units")
- Three prices: market price (continuous), NAV (end of day), **IOPV** (intra-day estimate, updated every 15 seconds)
- Arbitrage keeps market price close to NAV — but less-liquid sectors may require hours for creation/redemption
- Many use **sampling/optimization** instead of full index replication (e.g., **MUB** holds 839 bonds vs 7,550 in benchmark)

**Tracking accuracy (2008-2009):**
- **SHY** (1-3yr Treasury): traded with lowest premiums/discounts, well under 0.5%; bid/offer spread 0.035%
- **TLT** (20+ yr Treasury): within 50 bps of NAV 234 days (2008); spread 0.200%
- **LQD** (inv-grade corporate): highest discount ~**11%** (2008); within 50 bps only 53 days; spread 1.500%
- **HYG** (junk): max premium **12.71%**, max discount **-8.41%**; spread 2.000%
- **EMB** (emerging markets): max premium **13.49%**, max discount **-9.88%**
- Over longer holding periods, excessive premiums/discounts disappear

**Leveraged and inverse ETFs:**
- **Intended to be held for one day only — or less**
- Returns diverge significantly from index when held longer due to compounded leverage
- **FINRA issued alert** about extreme risks
- Principal disappears "as if in a black hole" when down

**Best uses:**
- Build low-cost laddered portfolio with clear risks
- Access sectors previously too difficult/expensive (foreign bonds, currencies)
- **85% of ETF assets** concentrated with BlackRock, State Street, Vanguard
- **Target maturity ETFs** (BlackRock): six municipal ETFs maturing 2012-2017, perform more like individual bonds

### Unit Investment Trusts (UITs)
- Sold on commission (~5%); portfolio remains **unmanaged** after creation
- Much smaller and less diversified (as few as 10 issues)
- Can be **illiquid during downturns** — difficult and expensive to sell
- Yield advantage may be illusory or based on low credit quality
- **WPPSS default** example: UITs stuffed with project bonds → substantial losses passed to shareholders
- Virtually fallen off radar screen — only few remain from Van Kampen and Nuveen

## Chapter 15: Portfolio Management

### Time Horizon Rules
- Money needed within ~2 years: **money market funds, CDs, T-bills** only
- Don't buy longer-term bonds hoping to lock in higher yields — can't guarantee principal recovery
- Individual investors **"get killed by commissions when they sell"**

### Maturity Matching ("Bullet Portfolio")
- For known future needs **2-10 years out** (vacation, car, college)
- Buy bonds maturing **exactly when principal is needed**
- Eliminates principal risk from rising rates; enables buying further out on yield curve
- Example: 5-year need → 5-year Treasury note, 5-year pre-refunded muni, 5-year CD, or 5-year Treasury zero
- **Zeros eliminate reinvestment risk** if rates particularly attractive
- For very large portfolios: select combination of maturities whose **duration approximates the target date**

### Laddering
- Divide money among bonds with different maturities (primarily short-to-intermediate)
- Example: 1yr, 3yr, 5yr in equal amounts; replace each maturity with longest (5-year)
- Maintains **~3-year average weighted maturity**; can extend to 2yr, 3yr, 5yr, 7yr, 10yr (~half longest maturity)
- Conservative approach: keep average maturity ≤**5 years**
- **Dollar cost averaging** in bond market — buying at different prices over time increases odds of good buy points
- Holding to maturity → redeems at par; if rates rise, reinvesting at higher rates boosts total return
- Makes great sense for portfolios above **$100,000**
- Online broker websites now offer **software to automatically structure ladders**

### Barbell Portfolio
- Half in short-term bonds (<2 years), half in long-term (20-30 years)
- Produces intermediate average-weighted maturity; income generally lower than pure intermediate
- Institutional use: betting on long-term rate declines → sell appreciated long bonds
- **Risk**: short rates drop + long rates rise (steep yield curve) = double whammy

### Finding Undervalued Bonds via Yield Spreads
- Different sectors not always in sync; spreads widen for various reasons (supply increase, sector falls from favor)
- When spreads widen **significantly**, two types of gains: (1) higher yields than normal, (2) price appreciation when spreads normalize
- Data now routinely published in WSJ, Financial Times, NYT
- **December 2008**: "mother of all opportunities" in every non-Treasury sector

**Municipal bond spreads:**
- Pre-2000: ratio >85% of Treasuries = reasonably attractive; 90% = buying opportunity; 100% = dramatic
- December 2008: **150%-300%** of Treasuries — unprecedented
  - Causes: flight-to-quality (Treasury yields plummeted), indiscriminate selling by hedge funds, total disappearance of institutional buyers
- If munis yield 115% and normalize to 85% → ~**30% capital gain**
- **Caution**: extremely wide spreads can signal increasing risk; narrowing spreads can signal bonds becoming expensive

**Junk bond spreads:**
- Historical: ~400 bps (1970s-80s) → narrowed to ~200 bps (signaled insufficient compensation for risk) → widened to **2,500+ bps** in 2008
- Any time yield spreads at **record lows**, anticipate eventual losses

### Yield Curve Opportunities
- Most attractive buy points: **intermediate sector** (5-10 years) captures **80%-90% of long-term yield** with far less volatility
- **1988-1989**: inverted curve — 2-5 year Treasuries yielded **above 9%**
- **If Treasury yields rise to 7-8%, no matter what pundits say, buy!**
- Treasury zeros at high yields doubly attractive — include reinvestment rates, guaranteed if held to maturity

### Swaps
- Trading one bond for another; historically used for **tax loss harvesting**
- During 1970s: annual ritual; Wall Street said "making lemonade out of lemons"
- Chief difficulty: transaction costs hidden in net prices — assume **2%-3% of principal** ($2,000-$3,000 per $100,000)
- Other reasons: upgrade credit quality, increase income, take advantage of anticipated rate changes
- **Wash sale rule**: cannot repurchase same security within 30 days
- Bond fund swaps: wash sale extends to **60 days** (monthly interest income); fund groups may restrict number of annual trades

### Total Return Management Guidelines
- **Minimize transaction costs**: buy Treasuries at auction, munis at issue when possible
- **Buy and hold rather than trade** — commission costs particularly high when selling
- Buy in lots of at least **$25,000** to minimize per-unit costs
- Check yield against Treasuries of comparable maturities — only purchase if spread warrants additional risk
- Complex securities (MBS, junk, international) bought most efficiently through funds
- Longer maturities require higher credit quality
- For long-run accounts: sweep reinvested income into **intermediate bond funds** (not money markets)
- Risk-averse core: **2-5 year Treasuries** and **5-10 year munis** = best risk/return combination

### What Bonds for Which Objective

| Objective | Securities |
|-----------|-----------|
| Cash equivalents | Money market funds, T-Bills, CDs |
| Short-term (1-5 years) | T-Notes, T-Zeros, pre-refunded munis |
| Tax-deferred accounts | Taxable zeros, BABs, intermediate investment grade funds/ETFs |
| High cash flow | Premium munis, GNMA funds, CEFs at discount |
| Interest rate bets | Zeros |

## Chapter 16: Portfolio Allocation

### Three Decades of Context
- **1980s**: bonds averaged ~**13% for decade** (high rates + declining trend)
  - At beginning of 1980s, 10-year Treasury rates still rising until peaking at almost **16% in 1982**
- **1999**: Dow first crossed **10,000**; stocks in seventeenth year of bull market (18-20% annual)
  - Consensus: 100% stocks; bonds = "dead money"
- **2000-2009**: two devastating bear markets (2000, 2008), each ~**50%** losses at peak
  - S&P 500 average annual return: **-2.72%** for entire decade
  - Barclays U.S. Aggregate Bond index: **+6.33%** (cumulative: bonds +87.59%, S&P -24.1%)
  - NASDAQ peak 5,400 (2000) → most recent high ~2,500 (2010)
- As of writing: 10x as much money into bond funds as stock funds — **pendulum swung dramatically**

> "The issue is simply this: how much are you willing to bet on the belief that all of past financial history no longer applies? More importantly, if the new era pundits are wrong, how would the volatility of stock market returns affect your standard of living?"

### The Case for Bonds
- Steady income stream (stock dividend yields historically **1.5%-2%**)
- **Non-correlation with stocks** — returns move independently
- Historical periods when bonds outperformed stocks: 1930s, 2000-2009

> "Think of a portfolio of umbrellas and straw hats. Each has a season, but they are different seasons."

### Safety Distinctions Within Bond Market
- **Least risky**: T-Bills, money markets, very short Treasuries, very short high-quality munis
- **Low risk with higher income**: short to intermediate Treasuries (<7 years), high-quality bonds/funds with duration <4 years
- **Higher risk**: longer maturities, lower quality credits subject to multiple risks
- Even "safe" Treasuries: **Barclays 20+ Treasury ETF (TLT)** lost **21.5%** in 2009

### Capital Preservation Philosophy
- **100% gain needed to recover from 50% loss** — at 10% annual, takes 10 years just to break even
- Many investors **overestimate loss tolerance** — panic sell after losses, buy back after recovery
- Flow-of-funds analysis shows this is typical investor behavior: **recipe for investment failure**
- **The less you can afford to lose, the more conservative you need to be**

> "The most important question is not, 'How much do I think I can earn?' but rather, 'What happens if I am wrong?' and, 'How would a loss affect my standard of living?'"

### Allocation Recommendations
- Author's consistent recommendation across three editions:
  - Younger investors: **40% bonds**, remainder in stocks/alternatives
  - Older/more risk-averse: **60% bonds, 40% stocks**
  - Bond portion: 2-5 year Treasuries (zero credit risk, modest interest rate risk)
  - Stock portion: broad index funds (outperform 80-85% of actively managed)
  - Affluent investors: intermediate high-quality munis can substitute for some Treasury portion
- **Warren Buffett**: never follow strategies you cannot explain on back of envelope
- **Resist temptation** of scattershot portfolio of assets you don't understand
- Huge difference in risk between broadly diversified index funds and narrow, specialized ETF benchmarks
- Author suspicious of "sophisticated" strategies — **"absolute return," "market neutral," "long short"**

### The Low-Return Environment (2010)
- Fed Funds: **0-25 bps**; discount rate under 25 bps; two-year Treasury: under 1%
- 2009 high bond fund returns primarily from **NAV gains** reversing 2008 catastrophic declines
- Fed will likely raise short-term rates within 1-2 years — benefits T-Bills, money markets, CDs
- But **no certainty long-term rates will also rise** — Fed raised rates seven times (2004-2006), yet **long-term rates actually declined**
- Future total returns "a lot more modest" than prior three decades; "far less predictable"
- 200-300 bps rate rise from current levels → **25-30% principal loss** for longest-term funds

### Challenge for Less Affluent Retirees
- Investing in safer bond sectors may not generate sufficient income in current environment
- Nevertheless: **the less you can afford to lose, the more conservative you need to be**
- High-net-worth retirees: all-bond portfolio (Treasuries + high-quality munis) can provide adequate income
- Conservative strategy: laddering 2-, 5-, 10-year Treasuries; high-quality munis no more than 10 years; short-to-intermediate high-quality funds

## Conclusion

- Author began revisions spring 2009, initially thought bond market changed forever
- Virtually all sectors that suffered 2008 losses **recovered during 2009** — record gains in riskier sectors
- Financial panic won't truly be over until short Treasury rates rise **above 1%** and stay there
- Unresolved: **Fannie Mae/Freddie Mac** conservatorship; Fed's $1 trillion+ toxic debt; state/municipal finances; **sovereign debt** (Greece, Spain, Italy)
- Common thread through three editions: significant volatility in each preceding period, crystal ball always cloudy
- Interest rates changed **more within few months** in last two decades than within several years prior to 1979
- **Best course**: stay away from both longest and shortest instruments; stick to straightforward high-quality securities

> "So once again, many happy returns."
