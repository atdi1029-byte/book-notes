Chapter 1: The Life of a Bond

A bond is a loan. You lend money to an issuer — the U.S. government, a state, a corporation — and they promise to pay you interest at regular intervals and return your principal at maturity. The legal document that governs this relationship is the indenture, which spells out coupon rates, payment dates, maturity, call features, and revenue sources. A summary version called the prospectus (or "official statement" for munis) is what investors actually read.

Bonds fall into three broad categories: Treasuries issued by the U.S. government, corporates issued by companies, and municipals issued by state and local governments. Par value is $1,000, though the minimum purchase is typically $5,000. Issue sizes range from $100-500 million for corporates and munis to many billions for Treasuries.

The primary market is where bonds are first sold — buying at issue during the initial offering often gives individual investors a fairer shake. The secondary market is where bonds trade afterward. Some issues trade actively for years; others barely trade at all. Regardless of what you pay in the secondary market, bonds always redeem at par.

"Bond prices go up and down continually. Bonds seldom, if ever, trade at par." — Annette Thau

Thau emphasizes that bond prices fluctuate constantly between issuance and maturity. Buying at par and holding to maturity guarantees your principal back, but selling early means accepting whatever the market offers — which could be significantly more or less than what you paid.

Before the 1980s, bonds came as bearer certificates with physical coupons to clip — the origin of the term "coupon rate." Today everything is book-entry, with no physical certificates and payments wired automatically. SIPC protects against the loss of securities held by a broker, but not against market declines.

The book is structured in four parts: fundamentals (how bonds work, trade, and are priced), individual securities (Treasuries, munis, corporates, mortgage-backed, international), investing through funds, and portfolio management. Parts two through four can be read in any order — each stands alone.

Thau wrote this book because she couldn't find one that explained bonds in plain English for individual investors. She worked as a credit analyst at Chase Manhattan Bank before becoming a personal bond investor herself. The third edition reflects the seismic changes brought by the 2008 financial crisis.

Every bond carries several distinct risks that interact in complex ways. Interest rate risk drives price swings as rates change. Credit risk reflects the chance of default. Call risk means the issuer can redeem your bond early, usually when rates drop and you least want to reinvest. Reinvestment risk — the danger that coupon payments can't be reinvested at the original rate — is invisible but can cut total returns by a third over long holding periods.

Chapter 2: The Bond Market

The bond market is not a centralized exchange like the stock market. It is a vast over-the-counter network of independent dealers organized by security type, several times larger than the stock market. Government bonds routinely trade in $1-100 million blocks, and the smallest municipal round lots are $100,000. Individual investors are the "little guys" facing higher commissions, less information, and no sophisticated trading models.

Bond pricing revolves around the bid-ask spread — the difference between what dealers will pay you (bid) and what they charge you (ask). This spread has been hidden since "time began." For actively traded Treasuries, spreads run a quarter percent or less. For inactively traded bonds, spreads can reach four percent or more. A wide spread is a red flag: it signals an illiquid or troubled bond. During the 2008 panic, spreads widened beyond all norms and many bonds could not be sold at any price.

A critical distinction exists between dealers, who put their own capital at risk and maintain inventory, and brokers, who execute trades for customers on commission. The same bond may appear at different prices across dealers. Four dominant electronic platforms — Bond Desk, MuniCenter, Knight Bond Point, and Trade Web — now make it easier to comparison shop, though the same bond can still show as 100 at one firm and 103-105 at another.

The discount broker fee of $2-5 per bond is the tip of the iceberg — the real cost lives in the hidden dealer and broker markups. Prices are negotiable: brokers expect informed customers to bargain, and asking for a better price usually costs nothing.

Three free websites have revolutionized bond price transparency. FINRA.org provides TRACE data for corporates and agencies within 15 minutes of each trade. EMMA .msrb.org is the official repository for municipal disclosure with 1.5 million CUSIPs. Investinginbonds.com covers munis, corporates, and agencies with trade history back to 2005. Together, they let any individual investor access pricing that was previously available only to professionals.

Every bond carries a CUSIP number — a nine-digit identifier like a stock ticker. Prices are quoted as a percentage of par (97 = $970). A basis point is one hundredth of one percent; a daily move of more than ten basis points is considered major. Using the term "basis points" immediately marks you as a knowledgeable investor in any conversation with a broker.

Call risk is a critical concept. Municipal and corporate bonds can be called — repaid early by the issuer — which kills your high interest rate and forces you to reinvest at lower rates. Premium bonds are especially vulnerable: a $1,100 bond called at par means a $100 loss. When a bond's coupon is much higher than current rates, evaluate it on yield-to-call, not yield-to-maturity.

Accrued interest is automatically added to the purchase price, compensating the seller for days held since the last coupon payment. Many bonds use a 360-day year for this calculation. It is a convention that catches new investors off guard when their confirmation shows a higher price than expected.

Chapter 3: Volatility

Two forces drive bond prices: interest rate risk (market risk) and credit risk (default risk). Since the late 1970s, interest rate changes have been the single greatest determinant of bond returns. Managing interest rate risk is the most critical variable in bond portfolio management.

The fundamental law is simple: interest rates and bond prices move in opposite directions. The longer the maturity, the larger the price swings. A 100-basis-point rise in yields costs roughly 1.1% on a two-year bond, 6.8% on a ten-year, and 11.3% on a thirty-year. A 200-basis-point rise on a thirty-year bond wipes out 20.6% of principal. Price declines are asymmetric — bonds gain more when rates fall than they lose when rates rise, a property called convexity.

One of the most widely misunderstood aspects of bond investing is that insurance and government guarantees do NOT protect against interest rate risk. The guarantee covers timely payment and principal at maturity, not price fluctuations. After the 2008 panic, investors poured into long-term Treasuries at 2-3% yields, and the TLT ETF subsequently dropped roughly 25%.

"During the disastrous 1970s, satirists described long-term bonds as 'certificates of confiscation.'" — Annette Thau

Credit ratings from Moody's, S&P, and Fitch grade bonds from AAA (gilt-edged) down through investment grade (BBB/Baa minimum) to junk. The agencies face a conflict of interest — issuers pay for their own ratings. The investment grade threshold matters enormously because pension funds and banks can only hold securities rated at or above that line.

Credit quality spreads — the extra yield demanded for lower-rated bonds — narrow when the economy booms and widen during recessions. Before 2008, the spread between rating steps was 10-20 basis points. During the panic, AAA-to-A muni spreads blew out from 50-70 to over 300 basis points. Yet short-to-intermediate, high-quality bonds emerged from 2008 virtually unscathed.

After 2008, the rating agencies moved to "global" rating scales that reflected munis' actual default experience. A 2007 Moody's study found that GO munis rated barely investment grade had default rates lower than AAA corporates. Between 1970 and 2009, rated munis experienced just 54 total defaults — less than 0.07%. Fitch and Moody's subsequently upgraded most GO bonds by one to three notches.

Interest rates have traveled an extraordinary arc. From around 2% in the 1930s-1950s, they climbed relentlessly to a peak of 15.2% in October 1981. The subsequent bull market from 1982 to 2009 was the greatest in U.S. bond history — 30-year Treasuries with 14-15% coupons sold for $1,700 per bond, a 70% capital gain. By 2008, rates had fallen back to 1940s levels with T-Bills at 10 basis points.

The Federal Reserve directly controls only overnight rates. During the 2004-2006 "conundrum" that puzzled Alan Greenspan, the Fed raised rates 17 times, yet long-term rates actually declined. In 2008, the Fed bought over $1 trillion of mortgage-backed securities, becoming the largest owner of MBS in the country. The eventual unwinding of that position remains a major uncertainty.

Chapter 4: Basic Bond Math

Yield-to-maturity (YTM) is the most commonly quoted measure of bond returns, but it carries a critical assumption: that all coupon payments will be reinvested at the same rate. This is almost never true. For a 30-year bond, 49% of total interest at a 5% reinvestment rate comes from compounding. At 10%, that figure rises to 76%. The longer the maturity and higher the coupon, the less reliable YTM becomes as a predictor of actual returns.

"Overall, there is too much emphasis on yield. YTM should not be the main criterion for selecting bonds." — Annette Thau

Yield-to-call substitutes the call date and price for maturity; for premium bonds, always compare it with YTM and use the lower figure. Yield-to-worst is the lowest possible yield from all call scenarios plus YTM. Total return — coupon income plus or minus principal changes — is the only measure that tells you what you actually earned.

Consider this example: you buy ten bonds at par with a 5% coupon. Three years later rates rise to 6%, and your bonds fall to $850 each. Your total return works out to roughly 0.5% annually, not the quoted 5% YTM. Even if you don't sell, the opportunity cost is real — you could be earning higher rates elsewhere.

"If you need to sell a bond before it matures, and if interest rates have moved against you, you may take a real hit to principal." — Annette Thau

Duration, developed in 1938, measures a bond's sensitivity to rate changes. For every 100-basis-point change, a bond's price moves roughly by its duration number — a bond with duration 4.4 years moves about 4.4%. Lower coupons mean longer duration and higher volatility. Zero coupon bonds have the longest possible duration (equal to their maturity), making them the most volatile bonds of any given maturity.

Convexity corrects some limitations of duration, but the adjustments are generally tiny (1-3%) and not particularly useful for individual investors. Thau is characteristically blunt about jargon: some websites now list convexity without explanation, and she tells readers to "just ignore it."

The time value of money runs through everything. $50,000 at 7% for 20 years grows to $193,484; at 10% it becomes $336,375. Even a half-percent difference in assumed rates compounds to a significant gap over decades. These calculations underpin every retirement planning tool.

Zero coupon bonds eliminate reinvestment risk entirely — you know your exact return at purchase. But their extreme volatility makes them inappropriate outside tax-sheltered accounts, where the "phantom interest" tax on annual accretion doesn't matter.

Thau's fundamental message: if you're buying 30-year bonds, you're making a bet on interest rates whether you know it or not. The only way to avoid that bet is to stay short or intermediate and hold to maturity.

Chapter 5: Before Buying Bonds

The Treasury yield curve is the starting point for all bond analysis. It plots yields from 3-month T-Bills through 30-year bonds and changes throughout the trading day. When long rates exceed short rates, the curve slopes upward (normal). A spread over 200 basis points is steep; under 100 is flat. When short rates exceed long rates, the curve is inverted.

Historical yield curves tell dramatic stories. In September 1981, the curve inverted with 3-month bills at 16% and 30-year bonds at 15%. In August 2009, the curve hit its steepest point ever: a 427-basis-point spread with 3-month bills at 0.16% and 30-year bonds at 4.43%. An inverted curve often predicts recession, though not all recessions are preceded by inversion.

For individuals, the yield curve answers a practical question: for the extra risk of extending maturity, how much more am I earning? When the curve is flat, 2-7 year bonds might yield only 30-50 basis points less than 30-year bonds — 90% of the yield with dramatically less risk. The municipal yield curve slopes more steeply than Treasuries, with the best risk-yield tradeoff at 7-15 year maturities.

Yield spreads over Treasuries are the market's real-time risk thermometer. Between April 2009 and April 2010, U.S. corporate spreads narrowed from 400 to 100 basis points and junk spreads compressed from 2,000 to 600 — generating enormous capital gains for anyone who bought during the panic. Conversely, the record- narrow spreads of 2006-2007 were a red flag that investors were not being compensated for risk.

"Before buying any other fixed-income security, check the yield of a Treasury with comparable maturity. Professionals do." — Annette Thau

Three websites — Investinginbonds.com, EMMA.msrb.org, and FINRA.org — give individual investors free access to real-time pricing, trade history, official statements, and even broker background checks. EMMA offers an alert system: enter your email and a CUSIP, and you'll be notified of material events. FINRA's BrokerCheck covers 850,000 brokers at 17,000 firms.

Thau's shopping guidelines: start with the taxable-versus-tax-exempt decision (retirement accounts always buy taxables). Browse the three websites for yield context. Check online broker availability. Look up trading history. And remember that prices are negotiable — calling a broker to ask for a better price usually costs nothing.

Judge a broker by what they try to sell you. If you're consistently offered risky, thinly traded securities, it's a red flag. After purchasing, check your price on the transparency websites to determine if you got a fair deal.

The taxable-equivalent yield calculation is essential for comparing munis to taxables. A 5% muni for someone in the 35% federal bracket equals a 7.69% taxable yield. Add state taxes and the advantage grows further. Thau provides a table of equivalencies that every investor should consult before deciding between municipal and corporate bonds for a given maturity and credit quality.

Chapter 6: Treasuries and Savings Bonds

Treasuries are the gold standard of fixed income: direct U.S. government obligations, the most liquid securities in the world, with the lowest markups of any debt. Since 1985, they cannot be called before maturity. They are exempt from state and local taxes, adding 50-60 effective basis points in high-tax states. During the 2008 panic, Treasuries soared while everything else collapsed — the ultimate flight-to-quality asset.

T-Bills (under one year) carry zero credit risk and negligible interest rate risk. T-Notes (2-10 years) offer 50-150 basis points more than bills. An Ibbotson study found that intermediate-term Treasuries returned 5.3% annually from 1926 to 1997 — essentially matching longer Treasuries while carrying far less volatility. You can earn 80-85% of the 30-year yield just by staying at ten years or under.

TIPS (Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities) adjust their face value monthly by the CPI-U. The 10-year coupon has ranged from 4.25% in early auctions to 1.375% in January 2010. TIPS carry deflation protection — they redeem at the higher of adjusted or original face value. But they are more volatile than conventional bonds (lower coupons mean longer duration), and the "phantom income" tax on inflation adjustments makes them unsuitable outside tax-sheltered accounts.

TreasuryDirect allows purchases directly from the Fed with zero transaction costs, starting at $100. You get the same yield as institutional investors at auction. Selling before maturity costs just $45 through the Sell Direct program. For individual Treasuries, this is the cheapest and most efficient route — bond mutual funds may not guarantee full principal recovery.

Zero coupon bonds (Strips) are created by separating Treasury coupon and principal streams. They carry no reinvestment risk — you know your exact return at purchase. But their volatility is extreme: a 200-basis-point rate rise on a 30-year zero causes a 43.8% decline, while a 200-basis-point drop produces a 79% gain. Ideal for known future needs like college savings, but the phantom interest tax limits them to tax-sheltered accounts.

Series I Bonds are one of the most attractive securities most investors have never heard of. They carry a fixed rate plus a semiannual inflation adjustment, cannot decline in value (unlike TIPS), compound tax-deferred for up to 30 years, carry zero default risk, and cost nothing in commissions. Early vintages earned 6-6.5% cumulative returns. They remain obscure because brokers can't sell them (no commission) and banks don't push them (they compete with CDs).

"This product is a sleeper: I have seen almost no mention of it in the financial press." — Annette Thau, on I Bonds

Both EE and I Bonds offer an education tax exclusion: proceeds used for college tuition are tax-free, provided the bonds are in the parent's name and income caps are met. Combined with zero commissions and state tax exemption, savings bonds deserve a place in nearly every conservative portfolio.

Federal agency securities from Fannie Mae, Freddie Mac, and other government- sponsored enterprises trade at a small premium over Treasuries. Since being placed under conservatorship in 2008, Fannie and Freddie carry an explicit government guarantee, effectively making their bonds nearly as safe as Treasuries with a modest yield pickup.

Chapter 7: Municipal Bonds

Municipal bonds are unique: their interest is federally tax-exempt, and if you buy in-state issues, they're often triple-tax-free. They are the only bond sector where individual investors are the primary buyers. With 1.4 million issues outstanding and a default rate under 0.07% from 1970 to 2009, their credit safety is second only to Treasuries.

The taxable-equivalent yield formula is essential: divide the tax-exempt yield by (1 minus your tax bracket). At a 39% bracket, a 5% muni equals an 8.2% taxable yield. At 15%, the same muni equals just 5.88% — you might earn more in taxable bonds. The highest brackets benefit most; the lowest may not benefit at all.

General obligation bonds are backed by the issuer's full taxing power. Revenue bonds depend on specific income streams like tolls or utility fees. Before 2008, bond insurers like MBIA, AMBAC, and FGIC guaranteed over 50% of munis, extending AAA ratings. Then the insurers ventured into subprime mortgage bonds and were devastated. By 2009, only 6% of munis carried insurance, and Assured Guaranty was the last meaningful insurer standing.

The safest munis are pre-refunded bonds backed by Treasuries in escrow — essentially as safe as Treasuries themselves. State bond banks, "double-barreled" bonds backed by both GO and revenue streams, and anything rated AA or higher on its own merit also belong in the high-safety tier.

Muni pricing is opaque. Thau's own experiment found the same NJ Turnpike bond varied by 150 basis points ($612 on a $40,000 lot) across seven firms. An out-of-state housing bond varied by 375 basis points — $1,500 on the same amount. Small lots under $25,000 are "punished" with steep markups. Appropriate markups run 1-2% for intermediate maturities and 2-4% for long.

Special muni types include municipal zeros (federally tax-exempt, no phantom income), put bonds (tender back at par for lower volatility), and Build America Bonds (BABs) — taxable munis created by the 2009 stimulus with a 35% Treasury subsidy to issuers, attracting institutional and foreign buyers.

A common misconception is that premium bonds are bad because you "lose" the premium. In reality, premium munis generally yield MORE than discounts because the entire dividend is tax-free, while discount bond gains may be partially taxable. The de minimis tax rule means deep discounts can be taxed as ordinary income.

Thau's strategy for maximum income and safety: 8-12 year maturity munis in the AA or higher range. You capture 80-85% of long-term yields with significantly lower volatility. For portfolios under $50,000, she recommends pre- refunded bonds or a high-quality intermediate bond fund.

Chapter 8: Corporate Bonds

Corporates offer higher yields than Treasuries but are fully taxable at all levels and carry meaningful credit risk. The universe spans five groups: utilities, transportation, industrials, finance, and Yankee bonds (foreign issuers, dollar-denominated). Very few AAA credits remain — even Berkshire Hathaway was downgraded to AA. By 2007, 49% of all corporates were rated junk.

Event risk was a hallmark of the 1980s, when leveraged buyouts devastated bondholders with overnight downgrades. The market responded with poison pill provisions and put bonds. Make-whole call provisions, which set the call price at par or a formula tied to Treasury yields, now protect investors from cheap early calls.

Junk bonds are the wild frontier. Michael Milken and Drexel Burnham Lambert created the modern junk market in the 1980s for M&A financing. Spreads that started at 400-500 basis points narrowed to 200, then blew out to 1,100 in November 1990 with bonds at 30-40 cents on the dollar. In 2008, spreads exceeded 2,500 basis points with price declines of 40-90%. The following year, junk returned roughly 40%.

Thau's verdict on individual junk bonds is unambiguous: highly speculative, not worth the risk for individuals. They are extremely illiquid, with markdowns reaching 25-30% and catastrophic declines happening overnight. For portfolios under $50,000 that want junk exposure, use a bond fund. For larger portfolios, only invest money you can afford to lose.

Shopping for corporates means using FINRA's TRACE data, where trades are reported within 15 minutes. Yield displayed on FINRA is yield-to-worst; on most broker sites it's yield-to-maturity — know which you're looking at. Always specify "bonds traded within 1-5 days" to avoid stale prices.

Buy investment-grade corporates when spreads to Treasuries are at least 150 basis points for 5-10 year maturities. Corporates are most appropriate for tax-sheltered accounts. Stick to maturities of ten years or less, and unless your portfolio exceeds $100,000, a bond fund or ETF may be the better bet for diversification.

Special features like convertible bonds (exchangeable for stock), floating-rate notes (coupon resets tied to LIBOR or T-bills), and equity warrants add complexity that most individual investors should approach cautiously. Amazon.com issued bonds with warrants in 1999 despite its junk rating.

The corporate market's declining credit quality is a structural trend, not a cycle. With nearly half of all corporates rated below investment grade, the "corporate bonds" label encompasses a vast range from premier credits to near-default situations. Due diligence matters more here than in any other sector.

Chapter 9: Mortgage-Backed Securities

GNMAs (Government National Mortgage Association bonds) carry unconditional U.S. government backing and pay monthly interest at yields above Treasuries. But they are deceptively complex securities — you don't know when you'll get your principal back because homeowners prepay unpredictably. Anyone who claims GNMAs are "simple" doesn't understand them.

Prepayment behavior creates a "heads I lose, tails I lose" scenario. When rates fall, prepayments accelerate and you get your principal back just when you can't reinvest it at the old rate — limiting your upside. When rates rise, prepayments slow, your pool extends longer than expected with a below-market coupon, and the price drops more steeply. GNMA yield quotes are "highly inexact estimates" because they depend on prepayment assumptions.

CMOs (Collateralized Mortgage Obligations) restructure MBS cash flows into tranches, from 3 to 50 per deal. PAC tranches offer more predictable lives; companion tranches absorb excess prepayments at higher risk; Z (accrual) tranches receive nothing until earlier tranches retire, resembling zero coupon bonds. In 1993, massive prepayments shortened CMO lives dramatically. In 1994, seven Fed rate hikes dried up prepayments and caused extension risk.

The 2008 crisis was born in the mortgage market. Starting in 2003, banks originated "liar loans" with no income verification and 100% financing, then packaged them into private-label CMOs. Rating agencies blessed them with AAA ratings based on diversification models built on as little as five years of data. By 2006, roughly half of all MBS were based on subprime mortgages. When housing prices fell nationally, AAA bonds were downgraded to junk overnight.

CDOs (Collateralized Debt Obligations) piled complexity onto complexity, sometimes containing other CDOs ("CDOs-squared"). Credit default swaps — insurance on CDOs often owned by parties who didn't hold the underlying assets — grew into a market several times larger than global GDP. Sellers of swaps weren't required to hold reserves, a regulatory gap that proved catastrophic.

The crisis escalated from Bear Stearns' forced sale to Lehman Brothers' bankruptcy to government bailouts of AIG and the conservatorship of Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac. The Fed purchased over $1 trillion of MBS to inject liquidity. At the time of writing, the CMO market had largely ground to a halt, with brokers selling only the plainest GNMA pools.

For individual investors, GNMAs only make sense when yields exceed Treasuries by 100 or more basis points. Always demand to see cash flow yields under multiple prepayment scenarios, and compare GNMAs to Treasuries of similar average life — not stated maturity. For portfolios under $50,000, a conservatively managed GNMA fund is the best approach.

Interest on MBS is taxable at both federal and state levels, which erases some of the yield advantage over Treasuries. Maintaining records of principal versus interest in monthly payments is essential to avoid overpaying taxes.

Chapter 10: International Bonds

Currency risk can dwarf interest income and price changes. Over short periods, currency fluctuations are often the most significant component of total return. The dollar-yen rate has swung from 80 to 200 over two decades. A bond profitable in local currency can deliver negative returns in dollar terms. During the 1998 Asian crisis, emerging market bonds lost 50% or more overnight.

Brady bonds, named after Treasury Secretary Nicholas Brady, emerged from the 1982 Latin American debt defaults that left U.S. banks holding $100 billion in bad loans. Banks swapped defaulted debt for bonds with steep haircuts, stretched repayment to 30 years, and collateral from U.S. Treasury zeros. These initially paid 15-25% yields and became "one of the most remarkable success stories in international finance." Mexico achieved investment grade, and Russia went from default to investment grade in five years.

Emerging market risks remain serious despite the Brady bond success. Argentina defaulted on $95 billion in 2001 — previously hailed as a model of fiscal discipline — and was paying roughly 35 cents on the dollar at the time of writing. Greece required an IMF bailout with fears of contagion to Spain and Portugal. Markets are thin, illiquid, and dominated by one or two issuers.

For foreign-pay bonds, most U.S. brokerages discourage individual investors outright. Merrill Lynch reserves them for "high net worth" clients. Alternatives include EverBank FDIC-insured World Currency CDs (South African rand at 4.88%; Japanese yen at 0%) and foreign currency ETFs. But FDIC protects against bank failure, not currency losses.

Thau is skeptical of the case for international bonds in individual portfolios. Diversification benefits are mainly relevant for large institutional portfolios. Higher returns require being right about both timing and currency direction. Hedging against a falling dollar is the strongest argument, but predicting the dollar's value is "not for amateurs." For income-focused investors, the arguments are "not very convincing."

The best vehicle for international exposure is a bond fund or ETF, limited to a small portfolio allocation. Expenses tend to be high (1.5-2%), and with a 5% load on top, you're giving up an entire year's income at the outset.

Emerging market bond funds delivered the highest 10-year return of any taxable category at 11.09% — but those returns begin after the 1998 Russian crisis. This is speculative money, suitable only for amounts you can afford to lose entirely.

If speculating on currency is the primary goal, currency ETFs now provide direct access to single currencies or baskets. Leveraged and inverse currency ETFs exist but are extremely risky and intended for very short holding periods.

Chapter 11: Bond Fund Selection

Bond funds are marketed on yield, but yield is only one component of total return. Net Asset Value (NAV) fluctuates daily for all bond funds except money markets. The SEC standardized yield — a snapshot of actual returns for the past 30 days — was mandated in 1988 after funds routinely manipulated and inflated yield quotes.

Total return — dividends plus capital gains plus interest-on-interest plus or minus NAV changes — is the only accurate measure. A fund paying 5% dividends that loses 10% in NAV delivers negative 5% total return. Published returns overstate reality because loads and taxes are not subtracted.

"Bond funds are marketed to investors on the basis of yield. That is what all the ads trumpet. But yield is only one of the factors that determine total return." — Annette Thau

Costs matter enormously for bond funds because yields are narrow. Vanguard charges roughly 20 basis points — the industry's lowest. Standard commissions run 4-5.75%, with 12b-1 fees capped at 75 basis points for load funds. John Bogle's calculation shows that $10,000 at 4% yield produces $380 in income at 20 basis points expense but only $250 at 1.5% — a 32% difference in the first year alone. Thau's first cut: only consider funds with expenses under 50 basis points, no commissions, no 12b-1 fees.

Duration predicts NAV volatility: a fund with duration of 8 years will lose roughly 8% of its NAV for every 100-basis-point rate rise. Unlike individual bonds, funds maintain constant maturity by buying and selling, so interest rate risk never declines during your holding period.

Some funds destroy principal permanently through premium bond stuffing (advertising high yields, then absorbing losses when bonds are called at par) or reckless use of derivatives to boost returns. In 1994, inverse floaters that had boosted municipal fund gains reversed catastrophically when rates turned up. If a fund's returns seem unusually high versus peers, that's a red flag.

Don't buy last year's top performer. As fund analyst Michael Lipper observed, a manager wanting to top the peer group must risk being at the bottom the next year. Look for conservatively managed funds with a five-year track record consistently in the top 20% of their category. Focus on expense ratio, duration, credit quality, and actual annual returns for ten years. Morningstar star ratings are marketing tools, not investment recommendations.

For short holding periods, NAV changes dominate total return. Never buy a volatile fund if you need the money within one to five years. For longer periods, compounded reinvested income becomes the primary driver, and rising rates eventually boost total return through higher reinvestment rates. But even over long periods, a catastrophic decline late in the holding period can devastate.

Fund protection under the Investment Company Act of 1940 means assets are held by a separate custodian bank. If the management company fails, fund assets are safe. But there is no protection against bad investment decisions by the manager, and bond funds are not covered by SIPC.

Chapter 12: Money Market and Municipal Bond Funds

In September 2008, the Reserve Primary Fund — the oldest money market fund — "broke the buck" when its Lehman Brothers holdings became worthless, dropping NAV to about 97 cents. Treasury Secretary Henry Paulson issued a federal guarantee up to $250,000 to prevent a run on money funds. Since then, 63 money market funds have exited or merged, and regulators tightened rules: average weighted maturity was cut from 90 to 60 days, and at least 10% of assets must mature within one day.

At the time of writing, money fund yields were 5-30 basis points — so low that expenses exceeded income and many sponsors were absorbing management fees. For context, money fund yields briefly hit 20% in 1981. They're appropriate for cash reserves (at least one year of expenses for retirees) but not as long-term investments.

Municipal bond funds cover a wide spectrum: general (65%+ investment grade), high-yield (50%+ below investment grade), and maturity-based categories. High-yield muni funds averaged -25% in 2008, with the Oppenheimer Rochester Municipal Fund yielding 13.8% but losing nearly 50% in total return. Even with the 31% recovery in 2009, 10-year high-yield returns remained below intermediate funds.

The sweet spot is intermediate muni funds (5-10 year maturity), which posted the highest average returns for both 5-year and 10-year periods while staying close to flat in 2008. Short muni funds delivered positive returns in every crisis year (2008, 1994, 1999).

In December 2008, munis became "amazingly cheap" relative to Treasuries. The typical "cheap" threshold is yields at 85-95% of Treasuries; in December 2008, ratios reached 150-300% of Treasuries at every point on the curve — an unprecedented opportunity driven by hedge fund liquidations and institutional buyer flight.

Bond insurance effectively ceased to exist as a meaningful factor after 2008. Insurance no longer confers meaningful protection. For high credit quality, choose funds investing in AAA or AA bonds with intermediate maturity and duration under five years.

The 2008 muni losses were not caused by rising rates (unlike 1994 and 1999) but by the bond insurer collapse, unprecedented credit spread widening, and severe buyer-seller imbalance. Both 2008 and 2009 were extreme outliers. Risk was punished far out of proportion to actual defaults.

For the most conservative approach, Thau recommends Treasury-only money funds for cash and intermediate, high-quality muni funds for income. If credit quality is the priority, stick to AAA/AA bonds with duration under five years.

Chapter 13: Taxable Bond Funds

Fund names are deeply misleading. A "government" fund may hold any combination of Treasuries, agencies, GNMAs, and zeros. "Corporate" and "income" funds may hold both stocks and bonds. Before buying, investigate the actual securities held, their credit quality, and the fund's maturity and duration.

Ultra-short funds (90 days to one year) have a terrible track record of disasters. The Schwab Yield Plus Fund, the most popular Schwab bond fund with over $13 billion in assets, lost more than 35% in 2008 and another 15% in 2009 despite being marketed as a safe alternative. It held substantial mortgage-backed securities, some subprime. The top 20% of ultra-short funds had positive returns of 1.5-4.2%; the bottom 40% were disasters.

The intermediate investment-grade category (5-10 years) is the sweet spot, holding roughly 40% of all taxable bond fund assets. The Pimco Total Return Fund was the largest of all mutual funds at over $220 billion. The Vanguard Total Return Bond Index Fund tracked over 7,000 bonds at about $80 billion. Duration under five years means modest interest rate risk, and 5-year and 10-year returns nearly matched longer maturities with far less volatility.

GNMA funds returned 5.24% in 2008 when the government backing proved critical, versus -1.61% for mortgage funds generally. Over ten years, GNMAs returned 5.70%. Their advantages over individual GNMAs include a lower initial investment ($1,000 vs $25,000) and mitigation of prepayment hazards through diversification.

Junk bond funds returned -25.86% in 2008 and +46.41% in 2009 — the highest aggregate gain in sector history. But the 10-year return of 4.80% was lower than intermediate government bonds at 5.51%. One fund lost 78% in 2008. For safe and predictable portfolios, junk is speculative and not worth the risk. For larger portfolios that can absorb losses, consider it when spreads are wide, with a maximum 20% allocation.

"If we had shown 10-year total returns through December 31, 2008, the return was a barely positive 1.2%, less than you would have earned on a riskless money market fund." — Annette Thau, on junk bond funds

Bond index funds are the ultimate plain-vanilla option: passively managed, low expense ratios, low turnover. The Vanguard Total Bond Market Index Fund tracks the Barclays U.S. Aggregate with about 90 bond index funds available across the industry. Choose a duration close to how long you plan to own the fund to minimize interest rate risk.

Thau's fund-buying rules: know what's in the fund, check duration and credit quality, cap any single long-term or specialized fund at 20% of your bond portfolio, prefer no-load funds with expenses under 50 basis points, and don't buy a fund whose NAV fluctuates if you need the money within a year. For the safest route: buy Treasuries through TreasuryDirect (zero cost) or build a ladder of AAA munis maturing in 1-7 years.

Emerging market bond funds are the highest-risk category, with losses exceeding -15% in 2008 and -22% in 1998 during the Russian debt crisis. Over 10-year periods, their returns barely justify the volatility compared with intermediate investment-grade funds. Thau views them as speculative holdings suitable only for portfolios large enough to absorb total loss of the allocation.

Chapter 14: CEFs, ETFs, and UITs

Closed-end funds (CEFs) trade on exchanges like stocks, with about 600 funds holding $112 billion. Unlike open-end funds, CEF shares trade at premiums or discounts to their NAV. Roughly 85% use leverage (95% for muni CEFs), borrowing at short rates to buy longer bonds. Leverage boosts returns when the yield curve is steep but creates a triple whammy when it flattens: dividends get cut, NAV declines, and the market price plummets.

Never buy a CEF at issue — hidden underwriting fees of 5-8% mean your investment starts underwater. Many CEFs eventually trade at discounts to NAV, where a 10% discount effectively lets you buy a dollar's worth of bonds for 90 cents. Thomas Herzfeld's strategy: buy only when the discount is at least 3% wider than the fund's historical average; sell when it narrows.

CEF yield quotes are notoriously misleading. Quoted "yield" is simply distributions divided by market price, and distributions may include return of capital, not just income. Thau cites an egregious example: a fund with a 0.79% income ratio but a quoted "yield" above 14%, trading at a 44% premium to NAV.

Bond ETFs launched in July 2002 with four funds from Barclays Global Investors (now BlackRock) and have grown to over 100 funds with $116 billion in assets. Expense ratios range from 9-60 basis points. Authorized Participants create and redeem shares in large baskets, keeping market prices close to NAV through arbitrage.

Tracking accuracy varies enormously by sector. SHY (1-3 year Treasury) trades within 0.5% of NAV almost always. LQD (investment-grade corporate) deviated by up to 11% in 2008 and traded within 50 basis points of NAV on only 53 of 250 trading days. HYG (junk) swung from a 12.71% premium to an 8.41% discount. Illiquid sectors can take hours for creation and redemption.

Leveraged and inverse ETFs are intended to be held for one day or less. FINRA issued an alert about their extreme risks. Returns diverge wildly from the index when held longer due to compounding effects. Principal disappears "as if in a black hole." Target maturity ETFs from BlackRock, by contrast, mature on specific dates and behave more like individual bonds — a genuinely useful innovation.

Unit investment trusts (UITs) are sold on roughly 5% commission with unmanaged, fixed portfolios as small as 10 issues. They can be illiquid in downturns and their yield advantages may be illusory. Thau notes the WPPSS default as a cautionary example where UITs stuffed with project bonds caused substantial shareholder losses. UITs have largely fallen off the radar.

Eighty-five percent of bond ETF assets are concentrated with BlackRock, State Street, and Vanguard. For most investors, ETFs and low-cost index funds are the most efficient way to access bond sectors that are complex or require diversification: corporates, mortgage-backed, international, and junk.

Chapter 15: Portfolio Management

Money needed within two years belongs in money market funds, CDs, or T-Bills — nothing else. Individual investors get "killed by commissions when they sell" bonds before maturity. The extra yield from longer-term bonds isn't worth the risk if you might need the money soon.

Maturity matching eliminates interest rate risk for known future needs. Buy bonds maturing exactly when you need the money — a 5-year Treasury for a down payment due in five years, muni zeros for college in ten. If rates are particularly attractive, zeros lock in the yield with no reinvestment risk.

Laddering is the bread-and-butter strategy: split your money across bonds with staggered maturities (say 1, 3, 5, 7, and 10 years) and replace each maturity with the longest rung. This produces dollar-cost averaging in the bond market, automatically capturing changing rates. Holding to maturity means you always redeem at par. Laddering makes great sense for portfolios above $100,000.

Barbell portfolios split holdings between very short and very long bonds. They work for institutional traders betting on rate declines but expose individuals to a double whammy if short rates drop while long rates rise.

Yield spread analysis identifies undervalued sectors. When spreads widen significantly, you earn above-normal yields AND capture capital gains when spreads normalize. December 2008 was the "mother of all opportunities" — munis reached 150-300% of Treasury yields, versus a normal 85%. If munis yield 115% and normalize to 85%, that's roughly 30% capital appreciation.

"If Treasury yields rise to 7-8%, no matter what pundits say, buy!" — Annette Thau

The intermediate sector (5-10 years) consistently captures 80-90% of long-term yields with far less volatility. In 1988-1989, an inverted yield curve put 2-5 year Treasuries above 9%. Those were extraordinary buy points.

Bond swaps — trading one bond for another — are historically used for tax-loss harvesting, but hidden transaction costs of 2-3% of principal make them expensive. The wash sale rule prevents repurchasing the same security within 30 days (60 days for funds).

The portfolio management table distills bond selection to a single reference: money market funds for cash equivalents, T-Notes and pre-refunded munis for 1-5 year needs, TIPS and I Bonds for inflation protection, muni zeros and savings bonds for college funding, and junk and emerging market bonds only for speculative capital appreciation.

Chapter 16: Portfolio Allocation

Three decades tell the story of bonds versus stocks. In the 1980s, bonds averaged 13% annually with rates declining from their 1982 peak. By 1999, the Dow had crossed 10,000 and the consensus was 100% stocks. Then came the "lost decade": the S&P 500 returned negative 2.72% annually from 2000 to 2009, while the Barclays U.S. Aggregate Bond Index returned 6.33%. Cumulatively, bonds gained 87.59% while the S&P lost 24.1%.

"Think of a portfolio of umbrellas and straw hats. Each has a season, but they are different seasons." — Annette Thau

Bonds provide steady income when stock dividends yield just 1.5-2%, and their non- correlation with stocks is their greatest asset in a portfolio. But not all bonds are safe — even Treasuries can lose heavily. The TLT ETF lost 21.5% in 2009 despite impeccable credit quality.

A 50% loss requires a 100% gain just to break even — at 10% annual returns, that takes a full decade. Most investors overestimate their tolerance for losses. Flow-of-funds data shows the typical pattern: panic sell after declines, buy back after recovery. This behavioral cycle is, in Thau's words, "a recipe for investment failure."

"The most important question is not, 'How much do I think I can earn?' but rather, 'What happens if I am wrong?' and, 'How would a loss affect my standard of living?'" — Annette Thau

Thau's allocation recommendation has been consistent across three editions: younger investors should hold 40% bonds, older or more risk-averse investors 60%. The bond portion belongs in 2-5 year Treasuries (zero credit risk, modest interest rate risk) and intermediate high-quality munis for affluent investors. The stock portion belongs in broad index funds, which outperform 80-85% of actively managed funds.

Warren Buffett's advice resonates throughout: never follow strategies you can't explain on the back of an envelope. Thau is openly suspicious of "sophisticated" approaches labeled "absolute return," "market neutral," or "long short." The less you can afford to lose, the more conservative you must be.

In the low-return environment of 2010 with the Fed Funds rate at 0-25 basis points, future bond returns will be "a lot more modest" than the previous three decades. A 200-300 basis point rate rise from then-current levels would mean 25-30% principal losses for the longest-term funds. The challenge for retirees is acute: safe bonds may not generate sufficient income, yet taking more risk is precisely what they cannot afford.

For retirees, the critical calculation is withdrawal rate sustainability. A 4% withdrawal rate requires a portfolio that generates at least 4% after inflation and taxes. With safe bonds yielding 2-3% and inflation at 2-3%, a 100% bond portfolio may not sustain withdrawals indefinitely. Thau acknowledges this tension honestly: bonds alone may not fund a 30-year retirement, but taking on stock-level risk late in life can be catastrophic.

Thau's conclusion across all 16 chapters is remarkably consistent: stay away from both the longest and shortest instruments, stick to straightforward high-quality securities in the intermediate range, and never chase yield without understanding the risks you're accepting.

Key Takeaways

Interest rate risk is the primary risk

Since the late 1970s, interest rate changes have been the single greatest determinant of bond returns. Credit risk, while important, is secondary for high-quality bonds. Insurance and guarantees protect against default, not price fluctuations.

The intermediate sweet spot

Bonds maturing in 5-10 years capture 80-90% of long-term yields with far less volatility. This applies to individual bonds, mutual funds, and ETFs across all sectors.

Costs compound devastatingly

A 1.3% expense difference reduces first-year income by 32%. Loads, 12b-1 fees, and high expense ratios permanently drag on returns. Only consider funds with expenses under 50 basis points.

Higher yield always means higher risk

There are no exceptions to this rule. Any time yields seem unusually attractive, the risk is real, even if not yet visible. Record-narrow spreads in 2006-2007 preceded the worst bond market crisis in history.

Prices are negotiable

The hidden markup system means individual investors routinely overpay. The same bond can vary by 150-375 basis points across brokers. Using free price transparency websites and asking for better prices can save hundreds to thousands of dollars.

Diversify across sectors, not just issuers

For portfolios under $50,000, funds and ETFs provide essential diversification. For larger portfolios, laddering individual bonds across maturities provides both diversification and guaranteed principal recovery at maturity.

Contrarian Ideas

Premium bonds beat discounts for munis

The common wisdom to avoid premium bonds is wrong for tax-exempt munis. Premium bonds deliver higher after-tax cash flow because the entire dividend is tax-free, while discount bonds include taxable capital gains.

Load funds offer no advantages

Despite what brokers claim, load funds do not outperform no-load funds. They charge higher expenses on top of commissions, doubly penalizing investors.

Top performers are the riskiest funds

Last year's top-performing fund is not next year's best buy — it's likely next year's biggest risk. The manager who topped the charts took outsized bets that could reverse.

Muni default fears are wildly overblown

Just 54 rated munis defaulted from 1970 to 2009, a rate under 0.07%. Even GO munis rated barely investment grade had lower default rates than AAA corporates.

Bond swaps don't increase your yield

Selling a 4% bond to buy an 8% bond is a wash — you own half as many bonds at double the rate, plus you're out transaction costs.

Frameworks & Proposals

The Yield Curve Framework

Use the Treasury yield curve as the starting point for every bond decision. Check what additional yield each maturity extension provides and whether the extra risk is compensated.

Spread-Based Buying Discipline

Buy corporates when spreads exceed 150 basis points for 5-10 year maturities. Buy munis when yields reach 85%+ of comparable Treasuries. Record- narrow spreads signal danger; record-wide spreads signal opportunity (if credit is sound).

The Ladder Strategy

Stagger maturities (1, 3, 5, 7, 10 years) and replace each rung with the longest maturity. This produces automatic dollar-cost averaging and guaranteed par recovery. Best for portfolios over $100,000.

40/60 and 60/40 Allocation

Younger investors: 40% bonds / 60% stocks. Older or conservative investors: 60% bonds / 40% stocks. Bond portion in 2-5 year Treasuries and intermediate high-quality munis. Stock portion in broad index funds.

Bond Selection by Objective Table

Cash equivalents: money market funds and T-Bills. Short-term needs: T-Notes and pre-refunded munis. Inflation protection: TIPS and I Bonds. Tax-deferred accounts: taxable zeros, BABs, intermediate investment-grade funds. College funding: I and EE savings bonds, muni zeros.

Why This Book Matters

The bond market is several times larger than the stock market, yet most individual investors understand it poorly. The Bond Book fills a void that no other single volume covers with the same combination of breadth (every major bond sector), depth (specific numbers, historical examples, pricing mechanics), and accessibility (no finance degree required). It treats individual investors as capable adults who deserve the same information professionals have.

Its timing is significant. The third edition was written in the immediate aftermath of the worst financial crisis since the Great Depression, giving Thau real-world examples of risks that were previously theoretical. The 2008-2009 crisis sections are not hypothetical — they are forensic accounts of what actually happened to specific funds, spreads, and prices. For any investor living through the next bond market disruption, these chapters provide essential context.

Most importantly, the book's core message transcends any market cycle: understand what you're buying, know the risks, don't chase yield, stay intermediate, and keep it simple. That advice was correct in 1991, 2000, and 2010 — and it remains correct today.
