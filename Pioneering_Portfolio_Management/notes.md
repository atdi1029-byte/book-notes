## Chapter 2: Endowment Purposes (Pages 35-50)

Three purposes of endowments:
1. **Maintain Independence** — reduce dependence on government, donors, student charges
2. **Provide Stability** — reliable revenue flows reduce operational disruption
3. **Create a Margin of Excellence** — fund research, attract faculty, build facilities

### Maintain Independence
- External funding (government grants, donations) comes with strings — regulatory oversight,
  donor requirements, student preferences
- Example: Yale and Connecticut — Colony of Connecticut funded Yale throughout 18th century
  (>50% of total gifts). In 1755, legislature withheld annual grant to punish Yale's president
  over religious dispute. In 1792, state got 6 seats on Yale Corporation in exchange for funding.
  State-appointed fellows served until 1871 when state support ended — only then did Yale
  gain full independence.
- Federal research grants in 1960s-70s: enormous expansion of US research universities,
  but came with costs — Congress involvement, targeted research requirements, regulations
  covering hiring, admissions, wages, construction, curricula
- Harvard President Derek Bok: compliance with federal regulations at Harvard cost over
  $8.3 million and 60,000 hours of faculty time in mid-1970s; regulatory costs absorbed
  7-8% of total institutional budgets
- **University of Bridgeport case study:** Peak 9,000 students in 1970s → fewer than 4,000
  by 1991. Rejected $50M from Professors World Peace Academy (Unification Church / Rev.
  Moon) in October 1991 to maintain independence. Ran out of options by April 1992 and
  reversed course — ceded control to Unification Church for $50M over 5 years. Three years
  later, Rev. Moon received honorary degree, called himself founder of world peace.

### Provide Stability
- Endowment smooths revenue variability from changing government policy, donor generosity, enrollment
- **Josiah Willard Gibbs story:** Yale's "greatest scholar" — creator of modern thermodynamics —
  was appointed professor of mathematical physics WITHOUT salary in 1871 due to Yale's poverty.
  Johns Hopkins offered him $3,000; Yale's James Dwight Dana persuaded him to stay with $2,000
  promise. Gibbs ultimately stayed — funded eventually by Berkeley fellowship (endowed 1731 by
  George Berkeley with a 96-acre Newport farm).
- **Stanford and the yacht:** 1991 controversy — Stanford overbilled federal government,
  seeking reimbursement for 72-foot yacht Victoria, a 19th-century Italian fruit-wood commode,
  and a Lake Tahoe trustee retreat. Federal support withdrawn; 1992 operating deficit $32.5M
  (3% of revenues). Projected 3-year deficits of $125M. Stanford raised endowment payout from
  4.75% to 6.75% for 1993-94 to release $58M. With hindsight, Stanford would have been better
  served by borrowing externally — strong returns in subsequent 5 years more than doubled
  asset values.

### Create a Margin of Excellence
- Endowment size correlates strongly with institutional quality (U.S. News rankings)
- Top quartile private research universities: average endowment $6.0 billion; bottom quartile $324M
- Top quartile endowment per student: $530,000; bottom quartile: $43,000
- Top quartile investment income = 19.1% of revenues; bottom quartile = 6.8%
- Top quartile rely on student charges for 24.5% of revenues; bottom quartile = 64.5%
- More than 100 degree-granting institutions closed in decade ending June 2007 (~3% of total)

## Chapter 3: Investment and Spending Goals (Pages 51-79)

**Core tension:** Preserving purchasing power of assets (long-term) vs. providing substantial
flows of resources to operating budget (intermediate-term)

**Tobin's formulation:** "The trustees of an endowed institution are the guardians of the future
against the claims of the present. Their task is to preserve equity among generations."
- Zero subjective rate of time preference — spend only what can be sustained indefinitely
- Consuming endowment income means existing endowment continues to support SAME activities
- Spending rises only when capital gifts enlarge the endowment

### Investment Goals
- Yale's oldest surviving teaching endowment: Timothy Dwight Professorship Fund (1822)
  — entered books at ~$27,000 historical cost. Price levels rose 27x in 185 years. Fund grew
  18x to ~$500,000, but still falls short of inflation-adjusted target by one-third.
- Harvard's 1974 spending policy flaw: targeted reinvestment rate offset general inflation
  only, not the higher rate of university expense growth (which exceeded general inflation by 2%).
  Harvard then relied on new gifts to offset the gap — but that "fails to enlarge the scope of
  activities" because a new law school chair doesn't replenish the economics department's loss.
- TIPS limitation: even inflation-protected securities may not keep pace with university-specific
  inflation, providing almost no real return.

### Spending Policy
**Yale's spending policy (designed by Tobin, Brainard, Cooper, Nordhaus):**
- Current spending = 80% × prior year spending + 20% × (long-term rate × prior year endowment
  market value), then inflation-adjusted
- Original weights: 70% prior spending / 30% current; changed to 80/20 as endowment grew
  from 10% of budget (mid-1980s) to 33% (mid-2000s) — greater smoothing needed
- Exponentially declining weights on past endowment values — elegantly squeezes out old data
  rather than dropping it abruptly like a simple moving average
- Reduces transmission of portfolio volatility to operating budget → allows taking more risk
  with expectation of higher returns

**Historical context:** Until 1965, most institutions spent only dividend/interest income ("yield").
Yale adopted "spend a prudent portion of appreciation" in 1965 — first movers.
Problem with income-based spending: a zero-coupon bond, par bond, and premium bond with
identical interest rate sensitivity generate wildly different cash flows for institutional spending.
By late 1980s, 1 in 5 institutions still used yield-based spending. Today, 70% use moving
average of endowment values.

**Target spending rates:** Range from 0.1% to 15.5%; 70%+ use 4%-6%; ~1 in 6 use 5.0%.
Analysis shows distribution rates for many institutions exceed return-producing capacity.


### Spending Sustainability and Purchasing Power (continued, pages 61-79)
- Average endowment: ~20% probability of disruptive spending decline (intermediate term);
  ~40% long-run likelihood of losing half of endowment purchasing power
- Yale's statistics: 5% probability of disruptive spending drop; 15% probability of purchasing
  power impairment — dramatically better than peers
- Higher Education Price Index (HEPI): education inflation runs ~1.4% p.a. above GNP deflator
  due to labor intensity — you can't make teaching more efficient without impairing quality
- Yale purchasing power chart 1950-2006: 1982 low point = only 42% of target (56% below by end
  of decade). By 2006: 170% SURPLUS over target. In 1970s: inflation 7.4% p.a. exceeded returns
  on domestic stocks (5.9%), bonds (7.0%), and cash (6.3%) — nowhere to hide. Yale "leaned
  against the wind" spending at 6.3% avg in 1970s vs. sustainable 4.4%; spending peaked at
  7.4% in 1971. Purchasing power declined >40% during the decade.
- Key principle: Do NOT raise spending rates after good markets; the cushion is needed for bad
  times. "Only fundamental improvements in investment and spending policies justify altering
  target spending rates."

### Foundation vs. Endowment Goals
- Foundations: must distribute minimum 5% annually per IRS; heavily reliant on investment
  income (8 of 10 largest grant-making foundations received ~100% of revenues from investments)
- Carnegie Institution example: Andrew Carnegie gave $22M endowment in 1902-1911 — nearly equal
  to Harvard's $23M. By 2006: Harvard grew to $29.2B, Yale to $18.0B, Carnegie only $720M.
  Difference: absence of gift inflows, not investment performance.
- Hansmann controversy: argues universities run investment pools with educational operations
  as a side business. Swensen rebuts: Yale clearly used endowment as operating buffer in 1970s
  (spent at unsustainable 7.4% rate to protect academic programs); endowment preservation was
  not dominant — budget stability was.

## Chapter 4: Investment Philosophy (Pages 80-132)

Three tools of portfolio management:
1. **Asset allocation** — policy portfolio, target weights to each asset class
2. **Market timing** — short-run deviation from long-term policy targets
3. **Security selection** — active management within an asset class

### The Role of Asset Allocation
- Ibbotson & Kaplan (2000): "on average, policy accounted for a little more than all of total
  return" — 90% of variability across time explained by policy returns
- BUT this is investor behavior, not finance theory. If you held one stock = security selection
  dominates. If you day trade bond futures = market timing dominates.
- Institutions hold stable, broadly diversified portfolios → asset allocation dominates by default.

### Equity Bias — Historical Evidence
Wealth multiples, Dec 1925-Dec 2005 (Ibbotson-Sinquefield):
- Inflation: 11x | T-bills: 18x | T-bonds: 71x | Corp bonds: 100x
- Large-cap stocks: 2,658x | Small-cap stocks: 13,706x
200 years (1802-2005): Large-cap stocks → $10.3 million from $1; T-bills → $4,800
Gold "bugs" disappointed: gold returned only 27x — barely above 16x inflation

Caveats:
- Survivorship bias in U.S. data — Russian, French, German, Japanese, Argentine markets were
  interrupted by war/inflation/revolution. U.S. 5% real capital appreciation may be 3% higher
  than other markets typically returned.
- Despite caveats, finance theory supports equity risk premium as enduring principle.

### Yale and Eagle Bank — Concentration Risk (1811-1825)
- 1811: Yale Treasurer James Hillhouse, Eli Whitney, William Woolsey, Simeon Baldwin
  chartered the Eagle Bank of New Haven.
- By 1825: ENTIRE Yale endowment invested in Eagle Bank (leveraged with borrowed funds!)
- After William Woolsey left to return to NY business, George Hoadley (Yale grad, lawyer,
  mayor of New Haven) became president. Founders too busy to oversee him.
- September 1825: Hoadley had loaned out nearly the entire bank value in under-collateralized
  loans → Eagle Bank declared bankruptcy. Yale lost >$21,000; total endowment reduced to $1,800.
- Unpaid debts >$19,000. Catastrophic for New Haven — local economic depression. Hoadley
  quietly moved to Cleveland.

### Clark Foundation and Nifty Fifty (1970s)
- Edna McConnell Clark Foundation tried to reduce Avon Products concentration in early 1970s
  by selling Avon shares and hiring J.P. Morgan to diversify.
- Morgan diversified into OTHER "Nifty Fifty" large-cap growth stocks.
- When Nifty Fifty bubble collapsed, Clark Foundation had traded one concentration for another
  — received no protection at all.

### Diversification as Free Lunch
- Average educational endowment (June 2005): 53% domestic equity, 23% domestic fixed income,
  5% cash = 81% in domestic marketable securities. Excessive!
- 28% in bonds/cash creates enormous opportunity cost: T-bills grew 18x vs. stocks 2,658x
  over 80 years
- Stocks AND bonds both respond to interest rates → not truly diversified
- Better diversifiers: foreign equities, venture capital, LBOs, real estate, timber, oil/gas,
  absolute return — these offer equity-like returns with low correlation to domestic stocks

### Market Timing is a Loser's Game
- Keynes: "The idea of wholesale shifts is for various reasons impracticable and indeed
  undesirable. Most of those who attempt to, sell too late and buy too late, and do both too
  often, incurring heavy expenses and developing too unsettled and speculative a state of mind."
- Tactical Asset Allocation (TAA): 1950s relative yield game (stocks yield more than bonds →
  buy stocks) worked until 1958 when it broke down permanently. TAA got popular after 1987
  crash, then faded.
- 1987 crash lesson: average endowment sold stocks AFTER crash (equity fell from 55.3% to 49.1%
  of portfolio — more than market alone explains). Bond allocations rose from 36.7% to 41.9%.
  They bought high and sold low. Bond/cash allocations stayed ABOVE pre-crash levels until 1993 —
  endowments missed the recovery.

### Yale's Real-Time Rebalancing
- Yale's tax-exempt status allows frequent rebalancing without tax drag
- FY2003: U.S. equity market returned 1.3% for full year but had massive volatility (−18% to
  the trough, +19% rebound, −19% again, +21%, −14%, +27%, etc.)
- Yale executed $3.8 billion in domestic equity rebalancing trades. Net profits: ~$26 million
  = 1.6% incremental return on $1.6B domestic equity portfolio
- Robert Shiller: "excess volatility" — prices fluctuate more than needed to reflect
  fundamentals. This creates rebalancing opportunities.

### Active Management Reality Check
Degree of opportunity (first-to-third quartile spread, 10yr ending June 2005):
- U.S. fixed income: 0.5% | Large-cap equity: 1.9% | Foreign developed equity: 4.0%
- U.S. small-cap: 4.8% | Absolute return: 7.1% | Real estate: 9.2% | LBOs: 13.7%
- Venture capital: 43.2% (!!)

Median active manager vs. benchmark (net of fees):
- Fixed income: −0.2% | Domestic equity: +0.5% | After survivorship bias adjustment:
  fixed income −0.4%, domestic equity −1.2% per year.
- Before adjustments, 80% of domestic equity managers appear to beat market. After fees +
  survivorship bias adjustment: less than 25% beat market.

Private equity: median VC underperformed S&P 500 by 11.3% annually for decade ending 2005.
LBOs: 1.9% deficit. "Only top quartile managers justify inclusion in portfolio."

### Survivorship Bias
- Russell database study: 1996 median U.S. equity return originally reported as 22.4%. By 2005,
  revised to 23.5% (130 fewer managers in sample). Survivorship bias inflates reported returns
  ~1.6% per year on average.
- In 2000: originally reported median was −3.1%. By 2005: reported as +1.2% gain. A 4.3%
  revision.

### Illiquidity Premium
- Investors overpay for liquidity → illiquid assets offer better risk-adjusted returns
- On-the-run vs. off-the-run Treasuries: otherwise identical bonds with 5-10 bps yield spread
  solely due to liquidity
- 1998 crisis: off-the-run Treasury (5 7/8s of Feb 2004) yielded 18.5 bps above on-the-run
- Overseas Private Investment Corporation (OPIC) private placement in fall 1998: full U.S.
  government backing, but 100+ bps above comparable Treasury solely due to illiquidity
- Samsung 1998: Dollar bonds yielding 16% vs. yen bonds (swapped to dollars) yielding 21% —
  500 bps extra for accepting modest illiquidity
- Sallie Mae: voting stock traded at 15-20% discount to nonvoting stock. 1.5 million shares
  refused a $91.875 offer for shares trading at $83.50 — irrational!
- Keynes: "the fetish of liquidity" is "antisocial." He even mused that making investments
  permanent (like marriage) would force investors toward long-term thinking.
- Key insight: fear failure, not illiquidity. If investments succeed, liquidity follows
  (IPOs, rising analyst coverage). If they fail, even liquid securities become illiquid.

### Value Orientation
- Tobin's "q" ratio: market value / replacement cost. When q < 1, smart buyers purchase
  corporate assets in the public market rather than building from scratch.
- Benjamin Graham's margin of safety: "favorable difference between price and indicated value.
  The buyer of bargain issues places particular emphasis on the ability of the investment to
  withstand adverse developments."
- Early 1990s real estate: Yale bought property with government-backed lease returning 14.8%
  vs. comparable Treasury Notes at 5.75%. Risk-free stream of cash flows at enormous discount,
  opportunity created by the RTC (Resolution Trust Corporation) disposing of FDIC assets.
- Real estate in late 1980s: "irreplaceable assets" with "monopoly positions" in regional malls
  purchased at <5% cash yields → became dinosaurs by early 1990s.

### Performance Chasing
- Morningstar study (Russel Kinnel): compares time-weighted (reported) vs. dollar-weighted
  (actual investor experience) returns for all domestic equity fund categories over 10 years
  ending April 2005. EVERY category showed investors underperformed the fund.
  - Technology funds: gap of −13.4% per year (reported +7.7%, actual −5.7%)
  - All categories showed negative gaps from −0.3% (conservative allocation) to −13.4% (tech)
- GMO case study: Grantham Mayo Van Otterloo. Excellent long-term record. Late 1990s:
  underperformed growth mania. Assets fell from $30B (1998) to $20B (1999) as clients fled.
  International Intrinsic Value strategy 1987-2006: +11.1% p.a. vs. EAFE +7.0%.
  Clients poured in after 8.7% excess returns (1990-93), peaked at $2.8B in 1996, then fled
  during 1997-2002 underperformance (assets fell to $578M). Missed GMO's 9.5% excess return
  p.a. in 2001-2006. Dollar-weighted: clients underperformed GMO by 2.0%/year from 1993-2003.
  Turned a +2.8% winner into a −2.0% loser through behavior alone.

## Chapter 5: Asset Allocation (Pages 133-160+)

### Asset Classes Change With Fashion
- Yale 1850: half real estate, bonds/mortgages, small stocks
- Yale 1900: mortgage bonds, railroad bonds, real estate, small stock allocation
- Yale 1950s: domestic bonds, stocks, preferred stocks, real estate
- Yale 2000s: domestic bonds, domestic equities, foreign equities, absolute return, real assets,
  private equity

- Lehigh Valley Railroad 4.5% bonds due 1989: sold by Drexel, JP Morgan, Brown Brothers in
  1891 at 102.5. Failed to pay interest in 1970; traded at 5% of face value in 1972. What
  seemed like a "safe" 100-year investment proved anything but.

### Asset Class Definition
- Key principle: define by FUNCTION, not legal structure
- High-yield bonds: legally "fixed income" but behave like equity risk — no crisis-hedge value
- TIPS: responds OPPOSITE to unexpected inflation vs. nominal bonds — different asset class
- Foreign bonds: no role in domestic crisis hedge; foreign exchange exposure adds unpredictable
  returns; should NOT be a separate asset class
- Optimal number: around 6 asset classes. Less than 5-10% allocation = too small to matter.
  More than 25-30% = dangerous concentration.

### Mean-Variance Optimization (Markowitz/Tobin)
- Finds "efficient frontier" — for given risk level, maximum return; for given return, minimum risk
- Requires inputs: expected return, expected risk (variance), expected correlations
- Problems: 
  1. Security returns NOT normally distributed — fat tails (4+ std dev events happen yearly)
  2. Correlations are NOT stable — all equity markets crashed together in Oct 1987
  3. Doesn't handle illiquid assets (can't rebalance annually)
  4. One-year time period serves neither spending stability nor 30-year purchasing power goals
  5. "Estimation-error maximizer" — overweights assets with best recent returns/most errors
- Use as starting point for human judgment, not as final answer
- "Garbage in, garbage out" — too many constraints makes optimizer just reflect prior biases


### Capital Markets Assumptions (pages 143-166)
- Mean-reversion: Grantham believes it's "the most powerful force in financial markets" — dampen
  expectations for recent winners, boost for recent losers
- Structural changes: 30-year Treasury bonds weren't issued until February 1977 — historical
  data before that date may not reflect current reality
- In mean-variance optimization, EXPECTED RETURNS are most powerful input; variances second;
  correlations least crucial (but hardest to specify)
- Yale's asset class assumptions (post-adjustment):
  - Domestic bonds: 2% real, 10% risk
  - Domestic/foreign developed equity: 6% real, 20% risk
  - Emerging market equity: 8% real, 25% risk
  - Absolute return: 6% real, 10% risk
  - Private equity: 12% real, 30% risk
  - Real assets: 6% real, 15% risk
- Absolute return: First identified as distinct asset class by Yale in 1990. Historical data
  showed 17.6% return, 11.8% risk — but badly inflated by survivorship bias. True expected
  return closer to 6-7%.
- Private equity: Infrequent marks-to-market makes risk appear artificially low. True risk
  is higher than observed; historical 12.8% return justified in assumptions.
- Real assets (real estate, oil/gas, timber): share inflation sensitivity, high current cash flow,
  opportunity to exploit inefficiencies. Within real assets, subassets have only modest
  correlations with each other. Oil and gas shows NEGATIVE correlation to real estate/timber.
  Timber: harvest can be deferred when prices are low — biological growth keeps increasing
  value. "Pay for patience."

### Problem with Naïve Mean-Variance (Real Estate Example)
- Spring 1988 Journal of Portfolio Management: Firstenberg, Ross, Zisler study recommended
  49-100% allocation to real estate. Used historical data showing RE returning 13.9% with
  only 2.6% std dev. Mean-variance optimizer naturally maxed out on real estate.
- Reality: 1988-1997, real estate returned 4.4% annually; stocks 18.0%; bonds 8.3%.
- Investors who followed the Firstenberg advice bought at peak; avoided in early 1990s when
  values were best. Classic example of historical data providing perverse timing signals.

### Portfolio Testing via Monte Carlo Simulations
- Yale defines failure as: (1) losing half of endowment purchasing power over 50 years,
  or (2) a 10% real decline in spending over 5 years ("spending trauma")
- In late 1980s when endowment = 10% of budget, "spending trauma" = 25% real decline over 5 years
- After endowment grew to 33% of budget (2001), redefined as 10% real decline over 5 years
- Simulations directly link portfolio choices to the actual goals — not abstract mean-variance
- Result: Large endowments (Yale, Harvard, Princeton, Stanford) all independently derive
  very similar asset allocations via these methods:
  - Domestic equity: only 15% for big schools vs. 42% for average endowment
  - Domestic bonds: 11% vs. 20% for average
  - Private assets (RE, VC, LBO, timber, oil/gas): 40% vs. <10% for average
  - Large endowments: expected return +1.1% above average (5.5%), with LOWER risk

## Chapter 6: Asset Allocation Management (Pages 167-189)

### Rebalancing — Core Principles
- Objective: maintain portfolio at policy targets. Without rebalancing, allocations drift toward
  highest-return asset class, increasing risk over time.
- Contrarian psychology: "The attractively priced, out-of-favor strategy provides much better
  prospective returns than the highly valued, of-the-moment alternative."
- Sally Bowles (Cabaret): "Everybody loves a winner, so nobody loved me." — The crowd loves
  what's been working; disciplined investors need to do the opposite.
- Continuous daily rebalancing: Yale executes $3.8B in trades in FY2003, adds ~$26M in profits
  (1.6% incremental return). October 27-28, 1997: market dropped 6.9% then rebounded 5.1% —
  rebalancing on those two days added 10 basis points to a 60/40 portfolio.

### Leverage Dangers — Case Studies

**Harvard and Sowood Capital (2007)**
- Harvard Management seeded Sowood Capital with $700M in 2004 (Jeff Larson, former HMC partner)
- Promised: market-neutral, event-driven, quantitative arbitrage
- By 2007: $3B+ AUM, 10% annual gains first 3 years
- Strategy: long senior corporate debt, short junior corporate debt (a hedge)
- Levered the trade up to 6x leverage to amplify small spread
- When market turned (June 2007): fund lost 5%, then doubled leverage to 12x!
- July 2007: perverse market movements — safer positions (owned) LOST value while riskier
  positions (shorted) also declined. Counterparties seized collateral.
- Result: lost 50%+ in July alone. $3B → ~$1.5B. Harvard refused bailout request.
  Harvard's total loss: ~$350 million. The lesson: leverage transferred fate from principals
  to creditors.

**Granite Capital — David Askin (1994)**
- Strategy: CMO derivatives ("super inverse interest only strips") — appeared hedged against
  interest rate movements. Investors included Rockefeller Foundation, McKinsey.
- Leveraged 2.5x on $600M equity → $2B of positions
- When Fed raised rates in early 1994: supposed hedges didn't work as advertised
- Banks seized collateral, sold positions. Entire $600M wiped out.
- Key: unlevered 30% loss = $180M pain but survivable. Levered 30% loss = total wipeout.
  Ironically, prices recovered after Askin was forced out.

**Long-Term Capital Management (1998)**
- Staffed by Wall Street wizards + 2 Nobel laureates in economics
- Jan 1998: $4.8B equity supporting $120B balance sheet = 25:1 leverage
- Off-balance sheet derivative exposure: >$1.4 TRILLION supported by <$5B equity = 290:1!
- When Russia defaulted (August 1998): correlations exploded, hedges failed
- $4.11 invested grew to... $0.33 after bailout. Partners' net: $2.85 → $0.23.
- Wall Street bailout required to prevent systemic failure. "Greatest fund ever" by IQ standards.

**The Common Fund Security Lending Scandal (1995)**
- Common Fund hired First Capital Strategists to manage security lending
- First Capital paid 25-33% of PROFITS from investing the cash collateral — but shared NO losses
- Security lending payoff structure: "make a little, make a little, make a little, lose a lot"
- Rogue trader Kent Ahrens lost $250K in 1992 equity index trade, then tried to trade out of it
  for 3+ years. Final losses: ~$138M. Common Fund AUM fell $5.2B (25%) in one year.
- Warning signs ignored: 1987 Caesar's World $2.5M loss; 1989 United Airlines LBO $2.6M loss
  — both reimbursed but clear signals of rogue risk-taking. Trustees were explicitly aware.
- Call it "arbitrage" doesn't make it riskless. Webster defines arbitrage as same-security
  trades; Common Fund was doing corporate restructuring risk arb and Mexican broker repos.

### Completeness Funds & Normal Portfolios
- Normal portfolio = manager's eligible universe, a fair benchmark
- Completeness fund = what's needed to offset gaps so aggregate of all managers matches
  market characteristics (size, sector, style)
- Without this, inadvertent bets can dominate results without anyone knowing it

## Chapter 7: Traditional Asset Classes (Pages 190-200+)

**Three criteria for a "traditional" asset class:**
1. Provides basic, valuable, DIFFERENTIABLE characteristics to portfolio
2. Relies FUNDAMENTALLY on market-generated returns (not active management)
3. Derives from broad, deep, INVESTABLE markets

### Domestic Equity
- Siegel 203 years: 8.4% p.a. | Ibbotson 80 years: 11.1% p.a.
- Historical risk premium: 5.7% p.a. over bonds (Ibbotson); 3.0% (Siegel). Must exist for
  capital markets to function — otherwise rational investors would hold only bonds.
- Survivorship bias caveat: U.S. 4.3% real capital appreciation (1921-1996); other countries
  only 0.8% median (experienced wars, revolutions, etc.)
- Dividend decomposition (Arnott, 2003): of 7.9% p.a. long-run return, 5.0% from dividends,
  1.4% inflation, 0.8% real dividend growth, 0.6% valuation expansion. With S&P dividend
  yield at 1.75% (August 2007), future returns look much lower unless dividends accelerate.
- "Dow 36,000" (Glassman/Hassett, 1999) argued for zero equity risk premium — stocks are as
  safe as bonds if held long enough. Swensen: ignores structural differences, ignores
  non-U.S. history of market closures, overestimates investors who can truly hold for 30 years.
- Stock/inflation: Stocks provide LONG-TERM hedge, but poor SHORT-TERM protection. 1973-74:
  inflation eroded 37%, stocks dropped 22% → 51% real loss. "No theory explains both the
  poor short-term and strong long-term record."

### Agency Issues in Equity Investing
- WorldCom CEO Bernard Ebbers: $400M+ in personal loans from company
- Tyco CEO Dennis Kozlowski: $600M of assets diverted, including famous $6,000 shower curtain
- Jack Welch (GE): lifetime use of $15M apartment, Boeing 737, helicopters, wine/flowers/cook,
  "tickets to top sporting events and the opera" in retirement package — WSJ called it
  "playthings of corporate opulence"
- Stock options: the no-lose game for management. When stock falls, company reprices options;
  shareholders suffer but management doesn't. Microsoft (April 2000): Ballmer awarded 34,000
  employees new options at 52-week lows after 44% stock drop — insulated employees from
  decline that shareholders suffered.
- Microsoft's fix (July 2003): eliminated options, switched to restricted stock — "whether it's
  dividend policy or how much risk to take, it's always good to have employees thinking as much
  like shareholders as possible."
- Implication for investors: seek high insider ownership, avoid companies where management
  treats itself as the primary beneficiary.

### U.S. Treasury Bonds (continued)
- Duration = effective maturity, weighing timing/present value of cash flows. Longer duration =
  greater interest rate sensitivity. NYT article got it backwards (said rates up → price up),
  illustrating widespread bond misunderstanding
- Time horizon and risk: 6-month investor finds 10-year notes risky (price varies with rate moves);
  10-year investor finds T-bills risky (19 future rollover rates unknown). Risk is horizon-
  dependent.
- Diversifying power: 1987 crash (S&P -22% in one day), T-bonds rallied. 1998 Asian/Russian crisis:
  "flight to quality" → Treasury bills dropped 2 full percentage points in days to 2.5% intraday.
  LIBOR-Treasury spread hit 3% (4x the June 2007 average). "In times of crisis, government
  securities provide the greatest degree of protection."
- 1998 Panic specific returns: Wilshire 5000 -22%, S&P 500 -19%, Russell 2000 -33%, developed
  foreign -21%, emerging -27%; long-term Treasuries +8% — "bested the results of every major
  asset class." High yield -7%, emerging debt -24%.
- Corporate bonds vs. Treasuries in crisis: Credit risk + optionality dampen corporate bond
  performance in crises. Junk bonds behave like equity in downturns. "Purity of noncallable,
  long-term, default-free Treasury bonds provides the most powerful diversification."
- Inflation risk: Traditional Treasuries offer nominal not real returns. Unanticipated inflation
  → bondholders lose purchasing power. Unexpected deflation → bonds benefit. "No theory explains
  both the poor short-term and strong long-term record" of stocks vs. inflation.
- Alignment of interests: Gov't is neutral borrower — no incentive to harm bondholders (redistribution
  would just shift from taxpayers to bondholders). 1975-1984: Treasury issued callable 30-year bonds
  (call in final 5 years). On Jan 14, 2000, exercised call on 8-1/4% bonds; on Jan 15, 2004,
  called 9-1/8% bonds with explicit note: "interest savings from call and refinancing will be
  about $544 million." Market debated whether gov't would use calls for debt management (neutral)
  or economic savings (unfavorable to holders). Answer: economic.
- GSEs: 31% of "U.S. government bonds" at end 2006 were actually GSE debt (Fannie, Freddie,
  Ginnie). Ginnie Mae = full faith/credit; Freddie/Fannie = "nether land." Many bond funds hold
  GSE paper seeking extra yield without acknowledging the risk. Investors wanting pure diversification
  must actively avoid GSE debt.
- Cash: 81-year return 3.7% p.a. → only 0.7% real. Not riskless for long-term investors (must roll
  over T-bills 19 times over 10 years, each time uncertain). Income flows, natural asset turnover,
  and manager sell decisions provide enough liquidity — "the modest transactions costs incurred in
  selling assets pale in comparison to the drag on returns created by holding cash."
- Market stats (Dec 31, 2006): U.S. gov't bonds = $3.2T ($2.2T Treasury + $1.0T GSE). Treasury
  bond yield 4.9%, avg maturity 6.9 years, duration 5.0 years.

### Foreign Developed Equity
- EAFE inception (1970) to 2007: 10.8% p.a. vs 11.2% p.a. for S&P 500. "Approximate equivalence
  between expected returns for domestic and international equities."
- Diversifying power: Japan 1980s: 28.4% p.a. vs. other non-US 16.5% vs. US 17.4%. Japan became
  world's largest market cap. Then 1990s: Japan -0.9% p.a., other non-US +13.5%, US +18.2%.
  Japan fell to less than one-fifth of US market cap. Clear evidence that country markets diverge.
- Currency exposure: Expected to be neutral over long run. If foreign positions are less than 20-25%
  of portfolio, currency adds diversification. Beyond that threshold, currency adds risk. Hedging
  is difficult (uncertain holding periods, unknown position sizes).
- Performance chasing case study: 1998-2003 foreign underperformed (-18.5% cumulative vs -6.4% US).
  Endowments held stable 12.5-13.8% allocation = rational rebalancing. Then 2004-2006 foreign
  dominated: non-US +23.9% p.a., emerging +35.8% p.a., US +13.1%. Endowments chased performance:
  raised foreign from 13.8% (2003) → 15.6% (2004) → 17.4% (2005) → 20.1% (2006). "Momentum
  strategies generally end badly."
- Inflation linkage: Foreign equity returns in USD have "so remote" a link to domestic inflation
  as to be "useless as a hedge against inflation." No paradox like domestic stocks — just no link.
- Alignment of interests: U.S. has stronger shareholder focus than most markets. Cultural norms
  overseas may prioritize workers/community. Controlling shareholder problems in many markets.
- Market stats (Dec 31, 2006): Developed foreign = $23.8T (22 countries), exceeding US market
  at $18.2T. Japan $5T, UK $3.8T, France $2.4T. Europe = 63%, Asia = 26%, Canada = 7%.
  Dividend yield 2.4%, PE 16.0, PB 2.7.

### Emerging Markets Equity
- High-risk, high-expected-return. Not guaranteed that economic growth → stock returns. Economic
  growth may benefit labor, management, or government — leaving equity holders with poor returns.
- Historical record: In 1900, 36 stock exchanges operated. "More than half suffered at least one
  major hiatus in trading — usually due to nationalizations or war." Of 36, fully 15 remained
  classified as emerging more than 100 years later. The "very term 'emerging markets' admits the
  possibility that these markets might fail." (Brown, Goetzmann, Ross — "Survival")
- Antoine van Agtmael (The Emerging Markets Century): previously viewed as crisis-prone and small;
  now "less exciting going forward" but more mainstream. Argues for core allocation.
- Malaysia 1998: restricted convertibility of ringgit, prohibiting foreign repatriation. MSCI
  removed from index; restored only in late 1999 after capital controls lifted.
- Russia governance: "Investors put money in enterprises where management attempts grand theft
  and avoid companies where management engages in petty larceny" — because managements of truly
  valuable enterprises tried to steal the whole company.
- Market stats (Dec 31, 2006): Emerging markets = $7.2T. India $824B, China $816B. Asia 53%,
  Latin America 20%, Europe 16%, Africa/Middle East 12%. Dividend yield 2.1%, PE 15.6 (vs. US
  19.5), PB 2.5.
- Historical returns (1985-2006): IFC Global Composite = 12.0% p.a. vs S&P 13.1% vs EAFE 12.4%.
  Emerging markets investors accepted higher fundamental risk without earning excess returns.

## Chapter 8: Alternative Asset Classes

### Overview
- Alternative assets push back the efficient frontier — allow higher return for same risk, or
  lower risk for same return.
- Each alternative contributes something distinctive: absolute return and real assets provide
  diversification (returns driven by different factors). Private equity provides return potential
  but little diversification (returns correlate with public equity).
- "Prices for many alternative assets lack the efficiency typical of prices for traditional
  marketable securities" — creating active management opportunities. No passive option exists.
  Even median alternative results have lagged comparable marketable security results. Only
  superior active management justifies alternatives.

### Absolute Return
- Definition: Inefficiency-exploiting marketable securities positions that exhibit little or no
  correlation to traditional stock/bond investments.
- Event-driven: Depend on completion of specific corporate finance transactions (merger, bankruptcy
  emergence). Return depends on manager's ability to assess deal probability, timing, consideration.
- Value-driven (long/short): Identify undervalued longs and overvalued shorts, hedging out
  market exposure. Returns depend entirely on stock-picking on both sides.
- Basic math: Without market risk or skill, absolute return earns only money-market rate. This is
  the starting point for performance evaluation.

**Newell/Rubbermaid Merger Arbitrage (March 1999)**
- Announced Oct 1998: Rubbermaid shares rose ~25%; volumes increased 10x (from 566K to 5.96M
  shares/day). Long-only investors exited, creating opportunity for arb.
- Terms: 0.7883 Newell shares per Rubbermaid share. Arb: buy Rubbermaid at $31.81, short 0.7883
  Newell at $43.25 → net proceeds of $2.28/share.
- Deal closed March 24, 1999: earned $2.28 spread + $0.57 short rebate = ~$2.85 total. Dividends
  roughly offset. Gross return: ~9% over 5 months = annualized low 20%s.
- MCI/WorldCom contrast: Started as BT/MCI proposal (Nov 1996 — largest cross-border deal ever).
  Then antitrust, regulatory approvals, strategy changes, operating difficulties, international
  politics, competing bidders (WorldCom ultimately won). Returns depend on event, not market.

**Event-driven in crises:**
- Oct 1987 crash: Two merger arb firms (later became Yale's absolute return base) posted -17.9%
  Q4 1987 (vs. S&P -22.5%) — disappointing. But Q1 1988: +16.3% (vs. S&P +5.7%). Viewed as
  package, essentially preserved assets and provided meaningful diversification.
- Aug 2007 credit crisis: $400B of merger financing called into question. First Data and TXU arb
  spreads jumped from low double digits to >70% annualized. Both closed — huge returns to
  intrepid investors. But some 2007 deals did fail (unlike 1987). 

**Short Selling Challenges:**
- Management resilience underestimated — companies find ways to survive seemingly inevitable failure
- Adverse price moves increase position size (opposite of longs), requiring diversification
- When shorts work and price crashes, position shrinks → need constant replacement
- Borrow risk: Short seller must borrow shares; if lender recalls → "short squeeze." Worst with
  small, illiquid companies (exactly the best short candidates)
- Constraints on AUM: Short selling requires limiting assets to remain effective

**Return Math for Long/Short Equity:**
- Top-quartile domestic equity managers (2006 10-year data): +2.6% above market → doubled for
  long+short = 5.2%. Plus short-term rates (3.7%) = 8.9% gross. Minus 1%+20% fees = 2.6% fee
  burden → 6.3% net. "Only a modest increment over money-market rates."
- Median managers: 1.1% x2 = 2.2% + 3.7% = 5.9% gross → fees → 3.9% net. "Disturbingly close
  to the 3.7% return of simply holding cash."
- Third-quartile: -0.1% x2 = -0.2% + 3.7% = 3.5% → fees → 2.0% net. Below-cash returns.
  "Even in instances where net returns to investors fail to reach the returns available on cash,
  the manager profits handsomely."
- Conclusion: Long/short managers must consistently produce better than top-quartile to justify
  fees. "Investors unable to identify the best of the best should pursue passive strategies."

**Yale's Absolute Return Portfolio:**
- Established as separate asset class 1990 — first major institution to do so
- 17 years ending June 30, 2007: 13.2% p.a. returns, 5.7% std dev
- Correlation to Wilshire 5000: 0.02; to Lehman Gov't/Corp Bond: 0.02 — essentially zero

**Survivorship & Backfill Bias:**
- LTCM: reported 32.4% p.a. from March 1994 through Oct 1997 (when it stopped reporting). Then
  lost -91.8% before Oct 1998 collapse. Tremont database contains ONLY the good years. True
  all-in result: -27% p.a. "The statistical omission of the implosion of LTCM inflates history."
- Ibbotson study (3,500 funds, 10+ years): survivorship bias = +2.9% p.a., backfill bias = +4.6%
  p.a. Total inflation: 7.5% p.a. "An enormous increment in a world where the difference between
  success and failure often depends on a few tenths of a percentage point."
- Malkiel study: of 331 funds reporting in 1996, >75% had disappeared by 2004. Live funds vs.
  dead funds: 7.4% return differential. Survivorship bias = +4.4% p.a., backfill bias = +7.3% p.a.
- Passive hedge fund = oxymoron: Goldman and Merrill Lynch created synthetic hedge funds replicating
  systematic risk components. Merrill Factor Index (S&P 500, Russell 2000, MSCI EM, USD index,
  LIBOR) had 95% correlation to HFRI 2003-2006. "The notion of a passive hedge fund investment
  turns logic on its head." If you want S&P exposure, buy the S&P.
- Egregious case: long-only managers calling themselves hedge funds and charging 20% profits
  interest on market returns they had no hand in creating. Fair fee = 20% over appropriate
  benchmark return, not over zero hurdle.

### Real Assets
- Definition: Ownership interests in vehicles that exhibit high correlation to inflation. TIPS,
  real estate, timber, oil and gas.
- TIPS tracks inflation precisely. Oil/gas change directly with energy prices (~9% of CPI). Timber
  is similar but smaller CPI weight. Real estate correlates less directly but strongly in equilibrium.
- "Unlike commodity indices, which give investors simple price exposure, well-chosen real assets
  provide price exposure plus an intrinsic rate of return." Oil/gas reserve purchases generate
  double-digit returns above and beyond price movement.

**TIPS:**
- Began January 1997. Principal adjusts for CPI. Coupon applies to inflation-adjusted principal.
  Real yields 1997-2007: ranged 1.3%-2.5%, average 1.9%.
- Not in same category as regular Treasuries: Regular bonds are harmed by unexpected inflation;
  TIPS benefit. Regular bonds benefit from unexpected deflation; TIPS are harmed (though principal
  floored at par). They are real assets, not bonds.
- Example: At end 2006, regular 4.25% Treasury note (Jan 2011) yielded 4.7%; TIPS 3.5% note
  same maturity yielded 2.4% real. Spread = 2.3% = market's inflation expectation.
- Non-US TIPS: UK, Canada, Sweden, France, Italy, etc. issue them, but foreign exchange risk
  makes them useless as USD inflation hedges. Corporate inflation-linked bonds: add credit risk +
  worst-case timing (inflation spikes when company most likely to default).

**Real Estate:**
- Returns 1987-2006: 8.4% p.a. (between S&P 11.8% and intermediate Treasury 6.5%)
- "Core" = well-located, well-leased properties. Not raw land, ground-up development, hotels.
- Tobin's q in real estate: Market value vs. replacement cost. More useful than in stocks because
  replacement cost is readily observable. Discount to replacement cost = strong buy signal.
- REITs vs. private: Public REITs swing dramatically around fair value. Green Street Advisors
  data: 1990 → 36% discount; 1993 → 28% premium; 1994 → 9% discount; 1997 → 33% premium;
  early 2000 → >20% discount; early 2004 → >22% premium; Dec 2006 → 2% premium.
  Wide swings create low correlation between public and private real estate.

**Sony Building Case Study:**
- LA real estate debacle early 1990s: Class A vacancy rate 24%. Sam Zell (the "grave dancer")
  bought Two California Plaza for $100M — ~1/4 of construction cost.
- Nearby Burbank suffered by association. Coca-Cola sold Sony Pictures building: $83M sales
  price vs. $120M+ replacement cost = 30% discount.
- 100% leased to Sony (S&P single-A credit). If only Sony's guaranteed payments received:
  all capital returned + 12.3% p.a. This included NO residual value. With reasonable assumptions:
  20-25% annual returns. In an environment where comparable Treasuries yielded 6.5%.
- When converted to REIT in October 2006 (Douglas Emmett), property appraised at >20% p.a.
  holding period return. "Contrarian instincts and real estate management expertise."
- Key lesson: "The limited downside of the Sony Building along with substantial potential upside
  created a positively skewed distribution of outcomes."

**Oil and Gas:**
- Reserve purchases vs. futures: Futures give only price exposure. Reserve purchases provide
  price exposure PLUS intrinsic rate of return.
- Merit Energy (founded 1989 by Bill Gayden, former Ross Perot colleague):
  Underwrites using 12-14% return assumptions WITHOUT energy price appreciation.
  1990-2005 actual: 19.2% net p.a. = 12.2% acquisition economics + 2.1% operations/development
  + 4.9% price appreciation. Futures investors would have received only the 5% price gain.
- Energy = ~9% of CPI → direct inflation link. Ibbotson study 1970-1998: positive correlation
  to CPI, negative correlation to domestic stocks, foreign stocks, Treasury bonds, T-bills.
- Old saw: "Sensible investors never back an energy partnership touted on Wall Street, because
  all the good deals come together in Houston." Complexity of royalty interests, overrides,
  net profits interests creates fertile ground for exploitation. Co-investment by promoter is key.

**Timber:**
- Returns: 12.7% p.a. 1960-2005 (Hancock/NCREIF), or ~8.1% real. Since 1987, cash yield ~6% p.a.
- Correlation to S&P 500: -0.17. While S&P produced calendar year losses 11 times since 1960,
  timberland lost in only 1 of those 11 years (and only 3 years overall).
- Return sources: (1) biological growth of trees, (2) timber price changes, (3) conservation
  easement sales, (4) higher and better use (HBU) land sales, (5) mineral/recreational leasing.
- Investable universe: ~$125B US (vs $3.5T real estate). 22% of US land = timberland; federal/
  state own ~30%. Only 20-30% of nonindustrial private timberland is institutionally investable.
- Ownership shift: Paper/forestry companies used to own timberlands to supply mills. As companies
  separated mill vs. timber operations, both improved. Sales funded mill modernization; timber
  became pure investment plays. ~33M acres traded last decade, avg 2.6% of investable universe p.a.
- End-product demand: Construction (50%+ of wood products), paper, furnishings. Paper/newsprint
  declining from digital substitution. Lumber has no real substitutes.
- Technology shifts: OSB (oriented strand board) displaces solid wood panels — changes relative
  species prices. Local mill proximity matters due to high log transport costs.


### Private Equity

**Private Equity Overview:**
- LBOs and venture capital generate high returns relative to equity alternatives — but at higher risk.
  Private equity addresses shareholder/management misalignment: high insider ownership + leverage
  discipline. "Over-the-top offices, excessive salaries, bloated fleets of airplanes...rarely enter
  the picture in profit-oriented private investments."
- Private equity "diversification" is largely a statistical artifact: infrequent valuations mask
  true co-movement with public markets. Two identical companies (one public, one private) will show
  different volatility solely because one is marked daily. "Illiquidity masks the relationship."

**Leveraged Buyouts:**
- Simple financial engineering = bad. Just adds risk without adding value. PE deserves no credit
  (and no carry) for leverage that any investor can replicate via margin.
- Historical performance (1985-2006): Median buyout LP return = 7.3% p.a. vs S&P 11.9%. First
  quartile = 16.1%; third quartile = -1.4%.
- Kaplan & Schoar (Aug 2005): "LBO fund returns net of fees are slightly less than those of the
  S&P 500." No leverage adjustment — making it even worse on risk-adjusted basis.
- Yale study (542 buyout deals, 1987-1998): Gross returns 48% p.a. vs S&P-comparable 17%. After
  fees: 36% p.a. — looks great. BUT: comparably-timed, comparably-sized, comparably-LEVERAGED S&P
  investments would have returned 86% p.a. Risk-adjusted, buyouts trailed by 50 percentage points.
- Yale's own 118 deals: Gross 63% vs risk-equivalent 41%. Fees eat 15 pts → net 48%. Explained by
  less leverage + more operational focus.
- Fund size kills performance: Funds >$1B returned 9.3% (10yr to Dec 2005), $500M-$1B = 10.3%,
  $250M-$500M = 11.4%. Josh Lerner & Schoar: "strong relationship between fund growth and returns—
  the more dramatic the increase between two funds, the sharper the decline in return."
- As funds grow: fee income becomes profit center → partners protect franchise → less risky deals →
  lower leverage → mega funds expand into hedge funds, real estate, etc. → less time on investments
- Fee problem: Metrick & Yasuda (144 buyout funds, 1992-2006): present value of fees consumes
  nearly $20 of every $100 under management. 62% of revenues are FIXED (management/entry fees),
  only 38% variable (carry, monitoring, exit fees).
- Clayton, Dubilier & Rice + WESCO: CDR acquired Westinghouse spin-off WESCO in Feb 1994 for
  $330M. Company had losses of $3M on $1.6B revenue, 250 branches (>50% losing money). CDR drove
  operating changes: IT, finance, internal controls from scratch. By 1997, WESCO had $90M operating
  income on $2.7B revenue. CDR's $83M equity → $511M proceeds = ~47% annual return. "Several
  measures of operating improvement." Few firms can do this.

**Venture Capital:**
- History: pre-bubble, returns inadequate for risk. Then Internet mania produced staggering gains.
  Post-bubble: firms kept bubble-era terms (higher carry, larger funds), raising the hurdle further.
- Median VC return (20 years starting 1985): 3.1% p.a. vs S&P 500 11.9%. Wide dispersion: 721%
  to -100%, std dev 51.1%. First quartile 16.9%, third quartile -6.7%.
- Even at peak bubble (2001 Benchmark Report, 20 years to Dec 2000): VC = 19.6% p.a., but
  equivalently timed/sized S&P = 20.2% p.a. Even at maximum, VC LOST to public equity.
- eBay story: Pierre Omidyar started eBay in Sept 1995 (ostensibly to help girlfriend sell Pez
  dispensers). By 1997, invited Benchmark Capital: $6.7M investment, $20M valuation. IPO Sept 1998:
  company valued $700M → first day price surged from $18 to $47. By April 1999, $175/share →
  $21B market cap. Benchmark's $6.7M → $6.7B = 1,000x ("the Valley's best-performing venture
  investment ever"). End of 2007: eBay valued $45B = 2,250x original valuation.
- Adverse selection problem: Top-tier firms (8-10 franchise firms) are closed to new investors.
  "All of the top-tier venture capital partnerships limit assets under management and none of the
  top-tier partnerships currently accept new investors." Available funds = second-tier or worse.
- Kleiner Perkins (KPCB) anecdote: Pre-bubble carry tiers: most firms 20%, a handful of top firms
  25%, Kleiner Perkins 30%. During Internet mania, rank-and-file moved from 20% to 25%, top firms
  to 30%. Kleiner held at 30% — "an extraordinary act of selflessness." Post-bubble: carry ratchet
  stuck high — nobody reduced carry back down.
- Franchise firms benefit from virtuous circle: best deal flow (entrepreneurs prefer brand names),
  better terms, better exits. Kaplan & Schoar: strong performance persistence in VC (unlike mutual
  funds where persistence is hard to detect). "Winners likely to outperform in the next fund."

## Chapter 9: Asset Class Management

### The Game of Active Management
- Markets tend to price assets accurately. Costs (fees, transactions, market impact) must be
  overcome — not just beating the market, but beating by enough to net out costs.
- Keynes analogy: Active investing is like "Snap, Old Maid, Musical Chairs — a game in which he
  is the victor who says snap neither too soon nor too late...all the players know that it is the
  Old Maid which is circulating."
- Joe Granville: Technical analyst who moved markets with predictions in late 1970s/early 1980s.
  Called April 1980 market low (bought at 759, market rallied to 1000+). January 1981 sell signal:
  "waves of selling," front-page NYT. Claimed to have "solved the 100-year enigma." Wore Moses
  costume, dropped trousers to read quotes. Missed the 1982 bull market turn, turned wildly bullish
  before 1987 crash. 1992 Hulbert's Financial Digest: Granville ranked LAST, down 93% over 12 years.
- Beardstown Ladies: Investment club claimed to beat S&P by 8.5%/year for 10 years ending 1993.
  800,000 copies sold. Five books total. Price Waterhouse audit found: returns were actually 9.1%
  p.a. — 5.8% BELOW the S&P. The treasurer misused a computer program, reporting 2-year return as
  if it were a 10-year result. Miss by 14.3% per year from what they claimed.
- Jim Cramer (CNBC Mad Money): Harvard College + Harvard Law. During Internet bubble promoted
  "expensive stocks," "stocks that move in chunks," and mocked value investors: "buy stocks of
  companies you like." Called value investors' preference for "Coke over Microsoft" as "preposterous."
  Predicted death of Albertsons (old economy) in favor of Urban Fetch, Kozmo, Webvan. Result: Fetch
  discontinued, Kozmo folded, Webvan filed for bankruptcy. Albertsons returned 0.5% p.a. vs S&P
  0.2% — beat S&P by avoiding Cramer's picks. Cramer's picks: Cisco -57%, Yahoo -84%. In 2005,
  CNBC gave him Mad Money. 3,458 stocks in database over 6 months. Barron's: "telestockmeister's
  picks aren't beating the market." Barron's estimates his picks fail to beat market even with the
  2% post-broadcast surge. In Dec 2006 interview, appeared to describe market manipulation "when
  I was short at my hedge fund." Later claimed it was hypothetical.

### Personal Characteristics of Investment Managers
- "Real estate investors invoke 'location, location, location.' Sensible investors seeking to engage
  an active manager focus on 'people, people, people.'"
- Integrity tops the list: Ethical advisors reduce the gap between their actions and investor
  interests. Structural arrangements cannot substitute for moral character.
- Loyalty flows both ways: investors owe managers reasonable time frames; managers owe reliable
  service. "Firing a poorly performing manager simply to remove an embarrassing quarterly line item
  fails to meet the test of reasonableness."
- Great investors have a "screw loose," pursuing markets for sport not profit. They are obsessive.
- Risk focus: "Risk varies inversely with knowledge" (Irving Fisher). Thoroughly researched ideas
  are inherently less risky.
- Warren Buffett's 2007 succession criteria: need someone "genetically programmed to recognize and
  avoid serious risks, including those never before encountered." "Temperament is also important.
  Independent thinking, emotional stability, and a keen understanding of both human and institutional
  behavior is vital." Even Buffett worried about losing his hire to competition.
- Money is poor motivation for fund managers seeking it for its own sake: "Profit maximizing
  business plans involve unbridled asset growth and unimaginative benchmark-hugging strategies...
  factors at odds with investment success."

### Organizational Characteristics
- Small, independent, entrepreneurial firms outperform large subsidiaries and financial conglomerates.
- Size: "In general, smaller tends to be better." High-return opportunities reside in smaller, less
  liquid markets — where excessive assets are a liability.
- Client quality matters: "Weak hands" exit during temporary underperformance, imposing transaction
  costs and morale damage. High quality clients add assets counter-cyclically, benefiting the
  manager and themselves.
- Innovation, ownership, adaptation (Schumpeter): innovators "see things which only subsequently
  prove to be true." Widely distributed ownership creates stable, long-term culture. Adaptation:
  amplify strong ideas, cut weak ones, exit mispricings when they close.
- Blakeney Management "Investment Guerrillas" letter (Miles Morland): Declined merger with large
  US firm. "We are guerrillas — our success comes from our ability to fight and forage in places
  too small and too risky for people with more to lose...Guerrillas cannot be integrated into the
  regular army without losing what makes them effective...We are rustlers by nature, not herders."
  If a majority mouse lies down with a minority elephant, it is not the elephant who ends up as
  a pancake."
- Financial supermarkets: Employee turnover is high because star managers can just open their own
  firm and keep 100% of revenues. Investment focus is diluted across many products. "Asset
  gatherers win at their clients' expense."
- Fortress Investment Group IPO (2007): $26B manager. Five principals cashed out: $888M Nomura sale
  (15% of firm), $528.5M pre-IPO distributions, $250M loan distribution = $1,666.5M total payout
  to 5 people. SEC filing rationale: "People, Permanence, Capital, Currency" — no mention of
  "Greed." Public shareholders claim income that otherwise would have compensated junior employees
  and benefited clients. "In a straightforward world, the section entitled 'Why We Are Going Public'
  would contain one header: 'Greed.'"
- United Asset Management (UAM): 45 firms, $200B AUM. Net client outflows $16B (9.4% of assets)
  in 1997. In 1998 bull market, another $20B left. Sold to Old Mutual in 2000 for 1.2% of assets
  (low end of 1-5% going rate). Had "about the same level of assets as five years ago" during US
  market's 3x increase. After acquisition, only 50-70% of revenues stayed in investment subsidiary.
  External ownership saps culture, senior manager "cashing out" destroys entrepreneurial spirit.
- Investment banks as PE sponsors: Morgan Stanley invested $46M equity in Burlington Industries
  (1987, $2.2B LBO). Over 3 years, charged Burlington $120M+ in fees (underwriting, divestitures).
  "Nearly every time Burlington needed advice, Morgan Stanley turned on the meter." Fees dwarfed
  the equity investment itself.
- Goldman Sachs Water Street Corporate Recovery Fund (1990, $783M): "Vulture fund" for distressed.
  Goldman Sachs partner Mikael Salovaara ran fund AND advised restructurings — no actual Chinese wall.
  Traders joked he had "a Chinese wall in the middle of his brain." Invested in Tonka bonds, then
  competed for Mattel advisory on Tonka acquisition, potentially trading on inside info. Fund owned
  >50% of Tonka bonds (acquired at <50% of face value). Held out for higher price in Hasbro acquisition
  at expense of shareholders. 9 of 21 Water Street investments were Goldman advisory clients.
  Shut down May 1991 — "rich series of lessons on conflicts of interest."
- Goldman GEO Fund (Aug 2007): $7.5B Global Alpha down 27% YTD. GEO Fund lost 30% in one week.
  Goldman injected $3B ($2B from Goldman itself) at NAV while existing clients suffered. New money:
  no management fee, 10% carry (vs. standard), 10% hurdle. Goldman + cherry-picked co-investors
  (Eli Broad, C.V. Starr/Greenberg, Perry Capital) got better terms than existing fund investors.
  Fund rose 12% the following week, Goldman's investment appreciated 16% in one month. Used cash
  infusion to reduce leverage from ~6:1 to ~3.5:1. "Goldman became a principal, riding roughshod
  over the interests of the firm's hedge fund clients."

### Deal Structure
- Co-investment = most powerful alignment mechanism. Managers who are principals behave differently.
  Must be large relative to manager's net worth — modest absolute amount can suffice.
- Trade-offs of co-investment: Tax-exempt institution vs. taxpaying manager (different after-tax
  returns). Mortal manager has shorter horizon than permanent institution. Manager may prefer less
  diversification. But benefits "far outweigh the likely costs."
- Passive vs. active fee structures: Size helps passive (crossing, full replication); hurts active
  (market impact, idea dilution). Barclays Global Investors (BGI) cited as example of scale benefits
  in passive management.


### Chapter 9 continued: Soft Dollars and Deal Structures

**Soft Dollars:**
- History: Pre-May 1975, fixed commissions → brokers gave soft dollar rebates to favored clients.
  After May Day (1975) SEC abolished fixed commissions, soft dollars should have died. Instead,
  investment managers realized soft dollars shift costs from their fee income to investors' trading
  expenses. Congress created safe harbor Section 28(e) protecting soft dollar usage.
- T. Rowe Price disclosure (March 2004): admits paying higher commissions for "computers and related
  hardware" and "research services." Buries this in Chapter 4 of infrequently read documents.
- "Slimy underbelly of the brokerage world." Uses include: office rent, marketing, employee
  salaries, personal expenses, theater tickets, interior design, Internet design, flights to Hong
  Kong for principal's son. "Wall Street's definition of research bears little correspondence to
  Merriam-Webster's."
- J&W Seligman ($24.3B AUM): Paid soft dollars to 7 consulting firms to get favorable placement in
  future manager searches — Callan, Frank Russell, SEI, and others. "Payoffs dishonor everyone."
- New Jersey Division of Investment: Testified at DOL working group that he uses soft dollars to
  pay administrative expenses since "he does not receive sufficient funding from the state
  legislature." Also urged expanding "research" definition to include travel and hotels.
- SEC Chairman Cox (May 2007): Called for repeal of safe harbor. Characterized system as "a witch's
  brew of hidden fees, conflicts of interest and complexity in application."

**Alternative Asset Fee Structures:**
- Hard hurdle = investor receives a target return BEFORE manager participates. Soft hurdle = manager
  can "catch up" after crossing hurdle. Soft hurdles provide "little value for investors."
- Absolute return hurdle: short-term interest rate is fair starting point (short duration strategies).
- Real assets hurdle: intermediate-term fixed income plus small premium.
- Private equity: by right, should use risk-adjusted marketable equity return as hurdle. Currently:
  most use zero hurdle (just return of capital). "The investment management industry receives
  compensation far in excess of levels justified by the degree of value created."

**KKR 1993 Fund — Structural Flaws:**
- KKR collected 20% carry on EACH DEAL independently — with no offset for losses on other deals.
  This created option-like payoff: kept 20% of winning deals, suffered NONE of the losses.
- Also: 1.5% mgmt fee (becomes profit center not cost recovery at large scale), deal fees, monitoring
  fees, investment banking fees. Contrast: Warren Buffett's original partnership charged NO mgmt
  fee, only profited if co-investors profited.
- KKR's Bruno's disaster (Aug 1995): $1.2B acquisition of Mississippi supermarket chain. KKR charged
  $15M acquisition fee (exceeded GPs' equity investment), $3.75M annual mgmt fee, $1M annual
  monitoring fee, $800K IB fee in 1997. Limited partners lost ENTIRE $250M equity in February 1998
  bankruptcy filing.
- KKR 2006 fees (from S-1 filing): $67M monitoring fees + $273M transaction fees + ~$350M mgmt fees
  (Lerner estimate) = ~$690M total to 25 senior professionals, just for "showing up." In 1996,
  under investor pressure, KKR agreed to aggregate deal results (no more deal-by-deal carry) — but
  kept the rest of the fee structure.

**Real Estate Negotiation Opportunity (Early 1990s):**
- 1980s real estate advisors: AEW, JMB, LaSalle, TCW, RREEF — all fee-driven. JMB the worst.
- JMB/Argyle Village Square: Acquired for $22M in 1986. By 1992, property worth less than its $12.4M
  mortgage. Anchor tenant vacated. Instead of handing back to lender, JMB kept the DEAD asset to
  collect fees (1.25% on gross value = 2.3% on original equity). Cash return was 1.1% (below fees),
  so JMB diverted cash from other portfolio assets to pay its own fees.
- JMB/Cadillac Fairview: "Largest real estate acquisition ever" — bought Canadian retail portfolio
  for C$6.8B in 1987. JMB charged 1% of gross value = C$68M acquisition fee on C$2B equity (3.4%
  load). Then: 0.5% annual mgmt fee on gross FMV (1.7% on equity), 1.75% participation on cash
  flow, 1% disposition fee on gross (including debt). Also reserved right to charge property
  management, leasing, insurance. By 1994, equity investors' interests traded at 20-25 cents on
  dollar. JMB voluntarily reduced its $30M annual fee to $25M — while noting the fee compensated
  "the firm for advising Cadillac Fairview, not the investors." JMB founder: "If I cut my fees by
  half, it still doesn't change their return." Cadillac Fairview filed for bankruptcy Dec 1994.
  JMB sued for C$225M in future fees. Settled for C$22.5M. JMB's Neil Bluhm later raised a new
  fund, Walton Street Capital ($3.5B by 2007) — ended up Forbes 400 (#215).
- Early 1990s turnaround: Capital drought = investor leverage. New terms: fees covering overhead
  only, hard hurdle before carry, substantial GP co-investment (sometimes from recourse loans).
  "Moving from dysfunctional fee-driven agency structure to well-aligned principal structure."

## Chapter 10: Investment Process

### Active vs. Passive Management
- Most important decision: whether to build active management capability. Without proper resources,
  active management produces disappointing results AND creates opportunities for better-prepared
  competitors.
- Passive-only portfolio for resource-constrained institutions: domestic equity, foreign developed
  equity, emerging market equity, REITs, Treasury bonds, TIPS. Efficient markets punish casual bets.
- Active required for alternatives: No passive option in absolute return, real assets, or private
  equity. Without active management, median results disappoint and fees destroy returns. Only in
  specific situations should alternatives be pursued — and only with genuine commitment.
- Keynes on group investment (The General Theory): "It is the long-term investor who will in
  practice come in for most criticism, wherever investment funds are managed by committees...For it
  is the essence of his behavior that he should be eccentric, unconventional, and rash in the eyes
  of average opinion. Worldly wisdom teaches that it is better for reputation to fail conventionally
  than to succeed unconventionally."

### Operating Environment: Investment Committee and Staff
- Investment committee = board of directors for the fund. 4 meetings per year is right — enough to
  maintain oversight without micromanaging. Policy proposals should come from staff, not committee.
- Committee members: good judgment, financial understanding, but should NOT try to manage the
  portfolio directly. Corporate instinct to "reward success and punish failure" is toxic in investing
  (pushes toward momentum, away from contrarian opportunities).
- Small decision-making groups: No more than 3-4 people. As group size increases, conventional
  consensus increases. Different small groups can handle different asset classes.
- Written recommendations: drafting exposes logical flaws. "The process of drafting memos often
  exposes logical flaws or gaps in knowledge."
- Training: invest in young professionals. James Tobin: "I never fail to learn, from the students
  themselves and from the discipline of presenting ideas clearly to them."
- Henry Paulson's (then Goldman Sachs) framework for attracting talent (Oct 1987, Yale SOM speech):
  cutting edge; global strategy; focused mentorship; early acceptance of significant responsibility.
- Yale example: every major investment initiative (absolute return, real estate, timber, oil & gas,
  buyouts, VC, international PE) initially backed a manager that failed. Yet all initiatives
  ultimately contributed enormously. "Encouraging experimentation and tolerating failure represent
  necessary preconditions for developing winning strategies."

### Organizational Structure
- Separate management companies: risk that investment operations lose connection to institutional
  mission. Compensation issues → staff identify with money, not with university → turnover inevitable.
- Stanford Management Company case: Mike McCaffrey ran Stanford's endowment, was frustrated by
  compensation constraints. Proposed using Stanford brand to manage external assets (revenue for
  retention). Board lukewarm → he left to form Makena Capital with Paul Allen (Microsoft co-founder).
  Two senior Stanford investment professionals joined him. Makena raised $7B quickly including $1.5B
  from Allen. Stanford lost its entire senior investment leadership. Root cause: hired people who
  "were in it for the money" not people who identified with Stanford's mission.
- Solution doesn't require separate entity: Yale's Investment Committee included non-trustee outside
  advisors since 1975, solving governance breadth issue without physical/psychological separation.

### Use of Intermediaries
- Fund of funds (FoF): Add a filter between fiduciary and manager. Reduces transparency. When FoF
  performs poorly, client must investigate underlying managers anyway — defeating the purpose.
  High fees on top of already high fees. Adverse selection: top managers prefer direct investors
  and exclude FoFs. Sequoia Capital (2006) publicly announced it removed all FoFs from investor
  base.
- Data: NACUBO 2006 — large endowments' hedge fund returns = 11.0% vs small endowments = 8.2%. 
  Only 2.7% of large endowments used FoFs; 47% of small endowments used FoFs.
- Lerner/Schoar/Wong study ("Smart Institutions, Foolish Choices?"): Endowment-selected PE funds
  = 20% IRR average. Fund-of-funds-selected PE funds = NEGATIVE 2% IRR average. "By far the best
  overall performance" for endowments vs. worst for FoFs.
- Two bizarre FoF manager examples: (1) Unnamed firm considered hiring convicted drug dealer/spouse
  abuser to manage energy fund. Proceeded. Devoted entire energy fund to him — not just part of
  pool. (2) Paloma Partners hired John Mulheren (Buffalo Partners) for risk arb. Mulheren had
  loaded assault rifle trunk to shoot Ivan Boesky; arrested by NJ state troopers. Paloma refused to
  disclose manager names to clients. When challenged: "Mulheren's a great investor when he's taking
  his lithium."
- Consulting firms: maximize profits by giving identical advice to many clients = cookie-cutter
  portfolios. Recommend established, large-capacity managers to clients — "bloated fee-driven
  businesses instead of nimble return-oriented firms." Consulting creates dependency (income stream
  from dependent clients). Worst case: consulting firm recommends its own FoF product. Former
  consultant: "95% of institutions rely on a consultant who comes in once a year, does a search
  conducted by someone with four years of experience, selects a manager with good 3-year numbers
  and standard fees, then fires them after two years of underperformance."

### Decision-Making Process
- Charley Ellis (Winning the Loser's Game) framework: policy (long-term), strategic (intermediate),
  tactical (short-term). Most decision-makers spend too much time on exciting tactical decisions
  and too little on important policy decisions.
- Policy asset allocation: should be reviewed ONCE per year only. Concentrated annual meeting
  → focused attention + protection from emotional market reactions.
- 1987 crash and Yale: Two extraordinary meetings convened post-crash. One committee member wrote
  memo calling Yale's allocation "super aggressive" and suggesting reducing equity targets "given
  events of past 6 months have diminished long-term prospects." Yale maintained targets AND bought
  equities in post-crash rebalancing. S&P 500 ended calendar year 1987 UP 5.2%. Outcome validated
  the approach. But: if markets hadn't recovered quickly, staff responsible for rebalancing might
  have suffered career consequences. Lesson: extra meetings create dangerous openings for emotional
  reversals.
- Report after close of fiscal year: backward-looking assessment + forward-looking strategic plan.
  Evaluates terminated managers' subsequent performance (often ignored after termination, but
  revealing). Two interim meetings: asset class deep dives or cross-cutting issues (e.g. S&L crisis
  affected PE, real estate, and absolute return all at once).

### Decision-Making Challenges
- Short-term thinking: annual performance comparisons → "horse race mentality." Cambridge Associates
  now publishes quarterly results for endowments. Even quarterly data is too short-term for long-
  duration institutions. 8% of endowments report returns GROSS of fees — gaming the competition.
- Consensus-driven behavior: deviating from the "peer group" allocation exposes the manager. If you
  fail unconventionally, staff gets fired. If you fail conventionally, staff stays employed. This
  creates rational herding.
- NYU case: Late 1970s-early 1980s: 66% bonds, 30% stocks. In 1981-82 (equity market bottom),
  dropped equity to 7%, raised bonds to 90%. Maintained >90% bonds through 1985, >86% in 1995.
  Only began reducing bonds in 1997. Board chairman Larry Tisch (after 1987 crash briefly seemed
  right) got standing ovation. Then the bull market continued. From 1982-1998, stocks beat bonds
  in 16 of 20 years, 6% per year advantage. From 1978-1998: endowment index grew 8x; NYU grew
  4.6x. Had NYU just matched medians, endowment would have been ~$1B LARGER than the actual $1.3B.
  Tisch's response to equity question: "the train has left the station."
- Boston University/Seragen: BU president John Silber invested $90M+ (operating AND endowment funds)
  in single biotech start-up. Started 1986 (lecture), 1987: $25M (14% of $175M endowment) for
  controlling interest. Massachusetts AG forced dilution through public offerings ($50M raised
  1992-93). By 1996: $200M cumulative losses. Delisted Nasdaq Sept 1997 (stock from $12 IPO to
  5/8). BU stake worth $5M by 1997. Sold to Ligand Pharmaceuticals Aug 1998. Final recovery: $8.4M.
  Loss: >90% on cost. Haunted by having missed Alexander Graham Bell's telephone, BU vowed not
  to miss Seragen. Drug eventually got FDA approval (Feb 1999). BU had almost no remaining stake.

### Performance Assessment
- Qualitative factors dominate both hiring AND firing decisions. Performance data provides input,
  not verdict. Strong performance alone doesn't justify keeping a manager. Weak performance alone
  doesn't justify firing.
- "Distinguishing between good fortune and good judgment demands thorough understanding of the
  manager's approach to investing."
- Regular face-to-face meetings = most important tool for performance evaluation. Without deep
  understanding, fiduciaries react only to numbers (buying high, selling low).

### Generational Transfer in Investment Firms
- Huge challenge: VC/PE firms built around one or two star investors face succession crisis.
  Succession successes: Sutter Hill Ventures, Greylock, Sequoia Capital, Kleiner Perkins all
  maintained excellence across generations. BUT: most firms fail this test.
- Two failure modes: (1) Sell to financial conglomerate — independence lost, entrepreneurial
  culture killed. (2) Don't sell, promote internally — often the promoted partners lack the
  founding genius. Firm drifts.
- Feshbach Brothers (Joe, Kurt, Matt): world's most notorious short sellers at peak. $1B AUM
  in 1990. Known for massive short positions and theatrical bearishness. In 1991, lost 55%
  as bull market ran over their shorts. By 1993 converted to long strategies. AUM fell to
  below $50M by mid-1990s. Classic example of a strategy dependent on specific market
  conditions; when the environment changed, track record evaporated.
- Client meeting best practice: inquisitive without intrusive. Manager must be fully transparent
  about strategy, portfolio, and changes. Investor must probe but not micromanage.

### Quantitative Performance Measurement (Ch. 10 continued)
- Backward-looking Sharpe ratios dangerous. Piper Jaffray AGF (Bruntjen) case: 5yr Sharpe
  ratio to 1993 looked exceptional. This masked fundamental interest-rate exposure. Real risk
  only emerged in 1994 bear market.
- Rosenberg Real Estate Investment Management (RIEM): time-weighted return showed +30bps
  over benchmark, suggesting competence. But dollar-weighted (IRR) showed -1.3%/yr — meaning
  due to cash flow timing (investors adding capital before bad periods, withdrawing before
  good ones), clients lost ~$500M in opportunity cost. Time-weighted hides cash flow damage.
- Rule: use time-weighted for comparing manager skill; use IRR (dollar-weighted) for measuring
  actual client experience. Private equity should ALWAYS use IRR. Public equity/absolute return
  should use time-linked time-weighted.
- Performance drives allocation: strong absolute and relative results → increase allocation.
  Weak results → reduce. Deteriorating qualitative factors → reduce faster than numbers suggest.
- Manager termination framework: establish clear investment philosophy. Deviation from stated
  approach = automatic red flag regardless of returns. If manager can't explain why they own
  what they own, that's cause for concern even if numbers look fine.

### Control Environment and Audits
- Yale uses independent auditors (PwC and Deloitte & Touche) for operational audits — separate
  from financial audits. Have conducted twice since 1990.
- First PwC operational audit flagged concerns about securities lending program. Yale
  DISCONTINUED its securities lending program BEFORE Common Fund securities lending scandal
  broke. The audit had identified the same structural risks that later caused the public scandal.
- Lesson: operational audits that flag issues deserve serious response even when the investment
  community hasn't yet experienced problems. Early exit from securities lending saved Yale
  reputational and financial damage.

## Appendix: Impure Fixed Income (Why Corporate Bonds Don't Belong in Portfolios)

### Credit Risk
- Investment-grade corporate bonds carry meaningful default and downgrade risk despite high
  ratings. IBM: went from AAA to A over 28 years — gradual but real quality erosion.
  WorldCom: went from A-rated to bankruptcy in under 3 months (2001-2002). The speed of
  WorldCom's collapse shocked credit analysts who believed investment-grade meant safety.
- Moody's historical data: 6,907 downgrades vs 4,087 upgrades over 20 years — the ratchet
  of credit quality is predominantly downward. S&P 500 debt-to-equity ratio went from 0.60
  in 1987 to 1.03 in 2007, meaning corporate America systematically increased leverage.
- Credit risk is negatively skewed: the upside of owning good credit is capped (you get
  par + coupon), but the downside is severe (WorldCom went to cents on the dollar). Risk/reward
  inherently unfavorable.

### Illiquidity
- PEFCO bonds (government-guaranteed, same credit quality as Treasuries) trade at 60bps
  above Treasuries purely due to illiquidity. This spread is real but not reliable — it
  can widen dramatically in crisis when you most need liquidity.
- In 2007-2008, spreads that looked like compensation for mild illiquidity blew out to
  hundreds of basis points. What seemed like free money turned into massive losses.

### Callability — "Heads You Win, Tails I Lose"
- Corporate issuers hold call options on their own bonds. When rates fall → companies
  refund at lower rates, removing your high-coupon bonds. When rates rise → bonds stay
  outstanding, so you're stuck with below-market paper.
- This optionality is valuable to issuers and costs bondholders. It's not properly priced
  into yields — investors consistently underestimate its cost.

### Capital Structure Conflicts of Interest
- RJR Nabisco LBO (KKR 1989): pre-buyout liabilities < $12B; post-buyout fixed obligations
  exceeded $35B. Pre-buyout bondholders lost ~$1B of value. Equity owners gained $10B
  windfall. The bondholder losses went directly to equity owners' pockets.
- Management's interests align with equity, not bonds. Management maximizes equity value:
  takes on debt (transfers risk to bondholders), negotiates loose bond indentures, includes
  call provisions. Bondholders sit across the table from adversaries with superior information
  and motivation.
- Negatively skewed outcome distribution: bonds can't go above par + accrued, but can go
  to zero. Short call options also cap the upside further.

### High Yield (Junk) Bonds — Concentrated Unattractive Traits
- All the problems of investment-grade bonds, amplified. Higher credit risk, lower liquidity,
  more valuable call options (because spreads are wider, the call provision captures more value).
- PCA (Packaging Corp of America) case study: Madison Dearborn LBO in 1999. Issued $530M
  of 9.625% Senior Sub Notes at bottom of single-B (small assurance of maintenance of terms).
  Debt/equity = 4.9x. January 2000 IPO (Goldman Sachs). Over next 3.5 years:
  → Credit improved dramatically (debt fell from $1.6B to $607M, D/E from 4.9x to 0.9x)
  → Rates fell dramatically (10yr Treasury from 6.7% to 3.3% by June 2003)
  → Both factors that should make bonds worth a lot MORE
  BUT: Call provision at 104.81 (April 2004) capped the bonds. Without call, bonds would
  have been worth 125+. Actual tender in July 2003 at 110.24.
  Junk bond holders earned 49.2% holding period return (Jan 2000-July 2003).
  A comparable Treasury earned 45.8% — noncallable, so captured the full rate decline.
  PCA equity went from $12 to $18.05 = +50.4% — BETTER than bonds despite S&P falling 24%.
- Junk bond no-win logic:
  → Fundamentals improve → stocks outperform bonds (equity captures unlimited upside)
  → Rates fall → noncallable Treasuries outperform callable junk
  → Fundamentals deteriorate → junk falls like equity, no protection
  Junk bond investors lose in all scenarios relative to better alternatives.
- High yield 10yr return to 12/31/2006: 6.6% (Lehman HY Index) vs. 6.0% Treasuries vs.
  6.5% investment-grade. Massive credit/liquidity/call risk for 60bps over Treasuries.

### Asset-Backed Securities
- Mortgage-backed securities (MBS): homeowners have refinancing option. When rates fall →
  prepay → bondholder loses high-coupon stream. When rates rise → homeowners don't prepay →
  bondholder stuck with low-duration asset that extends. Classic negative convexity.
- Worth Bruntjen / Piper Capital case: managed Piper Jaffray American Government Securities
  Fund. 5yr return to 12/31/1993: 19.3% vs 11.2% benchmark → Morningstar runner-up
  "portfolio manager of the year." State of Florida added $430M — biggest account.
  In 1994: 10yr Treasuries went from 5.3% (26yr low in fall 1993) to 7.4% by May 1994.
  Bruntjen's fund lost -28.9% in 1994 vs -1.4% for the Salomon Mortgage Index.
  Florida lost $90M (Jan-Sept 1994) on supposedly conservative operating funds.
  The "short-term, 3-5 year average life" paper was actually loaded with inverse floaters
  and principal-only strips — effectively 30-year bonds disguised as short-term assets.
  Neither Bruntjen, Piper Capital management, Florida's investment staff, nor Morningstar
  understood the actual risk in the portfolio.
- Florida LGIP (2007): $27.3B fund for local governments. Chased yield → highest yield
  of any public fund in US (5.63% in Oct 2007). When subprime crisis hit, $12B (46% of fund)
  withdrawn in November as participants panicked. Fund froze withdrawals Nov 29. BlackRock
  hired to unwind. Fund split: "Fund A" ($12B quality assets) and "Fund B" ($2B toxic waste
  including KKR Atlantic, KKR Pacific, Axon Financial, Ottimo Fund, Countrywide Bank CDs).
  Florida made same mistake twice (Piper 1994, LGIP 2007) — yield chasing without risk mgmt.
- ABS 10yr return to 12/31/2006: 6.0% — exactly matching Treasuries. Zero excess return
  for credit, option, and complexity risk.

### Foreign Bonds
- A foreign-currency bond = a USD bond + foreign exchange exposure. The currency component
  has zero expected return. You can hedge it (but then why not just own the USD bond?).
- Unhedged foreign bonds don't provide safe-haven protection in USD crises — you don't know
  what the FX will do when you need the bonds most.
- Get FX diversification through foreign equities (which have positive expected returns),
  not foreign bonds (which don't justify the added complexity and uncertainty).
- Foreign government bonds: if held primarily by citizens of that country, alignment is okay.
  If external investors dominate, political risk can override contractual rights → worse than
  corporate bonds.
- Foreign-currency bonds 10yr: government paper yielding 3.2% (far below US Treasuries at 5.97%).

### 2007 Performance Update — Crisis Confirmation
- Table of 10yr returns, Dec 2006 vs Dec 2007 (including 2007 crisis):
  US Treasuries: 5.97% → 5.91% (barely moved)
  Domestic corporate: 6.52% → 5.96% (down 56bps, only 5bps above Treasuries)
  High yield: 6.59% → 5.51% (down 108bps, 40bps BELOW Treasuries)
  Asset-backed: 5.95% → 5.43% (down 52bps, 48bps below Treasuries)
- In 2007 credit crisis: just when investors needed bond protection most, non-Treasury
  bonds failed. This confirms the structural argument: illiquidity/credit/call risk create
  false sense of safety that collapses precisely when you need it.
- Conclusion: "Sensible investors avoid the sirens' song that promises something (in the
  form of hoped-for incremental return) for nothing (in the form of ignored incremental risk)."
