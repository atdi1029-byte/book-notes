Chapter 1: An Optimistic Skeptic

The book opens with a deceptively simple contrast. Tom Friedman is the celebrity forecaster — New York Times columnist, Davos regular, the man presidents consult about the Middle East. Millions read his columns to learn what’s happening now, why, and what will come next. Then there is Bill Flack, a retired Department of Agriculture worker from Kearney, Nebraska. Bill is fifty-five, a bird-watcher, a former math PhD dropout from the University of Arizona. His parents owned a small newspaper in Madison, Nebraska. Nobody invites Bill to CNN or Davos. Nobody bases decisions on Bill’s forecasts. And yet each one of Bill’s roughly three hundred predictions about geopolitics — Will Russia annex Ukrainian territory? Will North Korea detonate a nuclear device? — has been dated, recorded, and assessed for accuracy by independent scientific observers. His track record is excellent. Tom Friedman’s accuracy has never been rigorously tested. There are only “endless opinions on opinions.”

This asymmetry is the engine of the entire book. Baseball managers would never hire a player without consulting statistics. Even fans expect scoreboards and batting averages. Yet when it comes to the forecasters who help nations decide whether to go to war, the public is content to remain ignorant. The first radical claim: forecasting is not a “you have it or you don’t” talent. It is a skill that can be cultivated, and the proof lies in the roughly two percent of volunteer forecasters — people like Bill — who consistently produce astonishing accuracy. Tetlock calls them superforecasters.

Tetlock introduces the research that made him famous — and annoyed. His twenty-year study (1984–2004) was the most comprehensive assessment of expert judgment ever conducted. The punch line went viral: the average expert was roughly as accurate as a dart-throwing chimpanzee. But the message mutated through constant retelling, like a game of telephone. “All expert forecasts are useless” became the takeaway. Tetlock tired of the joke. His research showed that some experts did have real foresight, and that the further out you tried to predict, the worse everyone got — approaching chimpanzee territory at three to five years. “Many” does not equal “all.”

To explain the “skeptic” half of his worldview, Tetlock tells the story of Mohamed Bouazizi, a twenty-six-year-old Tunisian street vendor who set himself on fire on December 17, 2010, after police confiscated his scales and humiliated him. His death triggered protests that toppled Tunisia’s dictator, then spread to Egypt, Libya, Syria, and beyond. The Arab Spring started with one poor man’s humiliation — no different from countless others. Even Tom Friedman, who knew the region as well as any journalist alive, could not have foreseen this cascade. You could have called Tunisia a “powder keg” for decades. It never blew until that specific morning.

Edward Lorenz showed why such events are inherently unpredictable. In 1972 he wrote a paper with the arresting title “Predictability: Does the Flap of a Butterfly’s Wings in Brazil Set Off a Tornado in Texas?” A decade earlier, he had discovered that rounding a data input from 0.506127 to 0.506 in a computer weather simulation produced dramatically different long-term forecasts. In nonlinear systems, small changes in initial conditions can mushroom to enormous proportions. Lorenz shattered the centuries-old dream of Pierre-Simon Laplace, who had imagined an intellect that knew every force and every position in the universe — for such an intellect, “nothing would be uncertain and the future just like the past would be present before its eyes.” The Lorenzian cloud replaced the Laplacean clock.

We may regard the present state of the universe as the effect of its past and the cause of its future. An intellect which at a certain moment would know all forces that set nature in motion… for such an intellect nothing would be uncertain. — Pierre-Simon Laplace, 1814

But dismissing all prediction as futile goes too far. A woman in a Kansas City suburb navigates her entire day on accurate forecasts: rush hour traffic, restaurant hours, other drivers stopping at red lights. Electricity producers anticipate demand surges every weekday morning. Amazon predicts what you want to buy. Kansas City Life Insurance, founded in 1895, forecasts disability and death actuarially — and has done so profitably for over a century. The question “Is the future predictable?” is itself a false dichotomy. We live in a world of clocks and clouds and a vast jumble of other metaphors. How predictable something is depends on what we are predicting, how far ahead, and under what circumstances.

The optimistic half of Tetlock’s stance rests on a simple insight: forecasting has never had the most basic prerequisite of improvement — measurement. Bill Gates observed that “you can achieve incredible progress if you set a clear goal and find a measure that will drive progress toward that goal. This may seem basic, but it is amazing how often it is not done.” In forecasting, it has almost never been done. Forecasts serve too many purposes besides accuracy: entertainment (Jim Cramer’s “booyah!” on CNBC), political advocacy, dress-to-impress (banks telling wealthy clients about the global economy in 2050), and comfort — partisans adore forecasts that confirm their worldview, “the cognitive equivalent of slipping into a warm bath.”

In the summer of 2011, Tetlock and his research partner Barbara Mellers launched the Good Judgment Project (GJP), inviting volunteers to forecast the future. Bill Flack signed up, along with thousands of others — cumulatively more than twenty thousand intellectually curious laypeople over four years. They answered nearly five hundred questions about world affairs, generating over one million individual judgments. The GJP was part of a larger tournament sponsored by IARPA (Intelligence Advanced Research Projects Activity), a defense agency modeled after DARPA, where five scientific teams competed to generate the most accurate forecasts. By year one, GJP beat the control group by sixty percent. By year two, it beat the control by seventy-eight percent, crushed university competitors from Michigan and MIT by thirty to seventy percent, and even outperformed professional intelligence analysts with access to classified data. IARPA dropped the other teams. A sixty-minute training tutorial improved forecaster accuracy by roughly ten percent for the entire tournament year — a modest improvement that, sustained over time, is the difference between making a living and going broke, as Aaron Brown of AQR Capital Management put it.

Chapter 2: Illusions of Knowledge

In 1956, a dermatologist found spots on Archie Cochrane’s hand — basal cell carcinoma, a minor cancer. As a precaution, Cochrane saw a big-name specialist who discovered a lump in his armpit, cut it out, and told him his armpit was full of cancerous tissue. Cochrane accepted the death sentence. “A curious feeling of peace came over me,” he wrote. But the specialist was wrong. The pathologist found no cancer at all. Cochrane did not die. He went on to become a revered figure in medicine — the father of evidence-based medicine. The irony is devastating: the man who would spend his career insisting that physicians must not rush to judgment had himself accepted a single specialist’s word without waiting for the laboratory report.

Medicine’s long history of confident ignorance runs through the chapter like a dark thread. When George Washington fell ill in 1799, his physicians bled him relentlessly, dosed him with mercury to cause diarrhea, induced vomiting, and raised blood-filled blisters by applying hot cups to the old man’s skin. A physician in Aristotle’s Athens would have nodded approvingly. Galen, physician to Roman emperors and the supreme medical authority for over a thousand years, never conducted anything resembling an experiment. “It is I, and I alone, who has revealed the true path of medicine,” he wrote. When patients recovered, it proved his treatment worked. When they died, it proved the case was incurable. This is confirmation bias in its purest form.

The pattern repeated endlessly: charismatic men of strong conviction, bold theories, denunciation of rivals. Historian Ira Rutkow observed that physicians debating treatments were “like blind men arguing over the colors of the rainbow.” In 1747, James Lind ran a proto- experiment on scurvy, dividing twelve sailors into pairs and giving each pair a different remedy. The citrus pair recovered. But Lind himself failed to grasp the significance of his own result, and sailors kept getting scurvy for decades afterward. Not until the twentieth century did randomized controlled trials take hold. Before that, medicine was what Richard Feynman called “cargo cult science” — it had the outward form of science but lacked what makes it truly scientific. What it lacked was doubt.

Doubt is not a fearful thing, but a thing of very great value. It is what propels science forward. — Richard Feynman

Cochrane despised the “God complex” among physicians — the conviction that they simply knew, and needed no scientific validation. When hospitals created cardiac care units, Cochrane proposed a trial comparing them with home care. Cardiologists were outraged: it was obvious the units were better. Cochrane got his trial anyway. Partway through, he showed the cardiologists preliminary results suggesting the cardiac units were slightly better. They exploded: “Archie, we always thought you were unethical. You must stop the trial at once.” Then Cochrane revealed he had reversed the results. Home care was actually doing slightly better. “There was dead silence,” he recalled, “and I felt rather sick because they were, after all, my medical colleagues.”

Tetlock introduces Daniel Kahneman’s dual-system model: System 1 (fast, automatic, unconscious) and System 2 (slow, deliberate, conscious). The bat-and-ball problem illustrates the trap: a bat and ball together cost $1.10, the bat costs $1.00 more than the ball — how much does the ball cost? System 1 shouts “ten cents!” It feels right. But it’s wrong. The correct answer is five cents. Most people, including very smart ones, never engage System 2 long enough to catch the error. This is the “tip-of-your-nose perspective” — the subjective, automatic way we see the world, always on, impossible to switch off.

WYSIATI — “What You See Is All There Is” — is Kahneman’s label for System 1’s most dangerous habit: treating available evidence as the whole story. We are “creative confabulators hardwired to invent stories that impose coherence on the world.” Michael Gazzaniga’s split-brain experiments showed this vividly: a patient whose right hemisphere saw a snowstorm pointed at a shovel, but his left hemisphere (which had seen only a chicken claw) instantly confabulated: “The chicken claw goes with the chicken, and you need a shovel to clean out the chicken shed.” This compulsion to explain operates daily when journalists attribute stock market moves to “news” that often came out after the market had already moved.

The 2011 Oslo bombing demonstrated how this works in real time. A massive car bomb killed eight people. Within minutes, everyone was certain: Islamist terrorists. Norway had soldiers in Afghanistan, a poorly integrated Muslim community, and a radical preacher recently charged with incitement. Then came word of a mass shooting at a Labour Party summer camp. Coordinated Islamist attacks, obviously. Except the perpetrator was Anders Breivik, a right-wing extremist. Suspecting Islamists was a reasonable hypothesis given the available evidence. The error was not in forming the hypothesis but in treating it as settled truth. A scientist would have called it a “plausible hypothesis” and immediately looked for alternatives.

Tetlock also introduces the concept of attribute substitution, which he calls “bait and switch.” When faced with a hard question, we unconsciously replace it with an easy one. “Do I have cancer?” becomes “Is this the sort of person who should know if I have cancer?” The specialist saw the cancerous flesh with his own eyes — of course he knows. Cochrane acquiesced. Even the champion of not rushing to judgment fell for System 1’s oldest trick. The cure, Tetlock argues, is “a tablespoon of doubt.”

Chapter 3: Keeping Score

Judging forecasts is far harder than it appears. Steve Ballmer declared in 2007, “There’s no chance that the iPhone is going to get any significant market share. No chance.” This seems spectacularly wrong. But parse the full interview: Ballmer was talking about the global mobile phone market (1.3 billion phones), predicting the iPhone would capture “2% or 3%.” The actual figure in late 2013 was about six percent of global mobile phone sales — higher, but not the laugh-out-loud error everyone remembers. His words were more nuanced than his tone. Vague terms, undefined time frames, and absent benchmarks render most forecasts unfalsifiable. An open letter to Fed chairman Ben Bernanke in 2010, signed by prominent economists warning of “currency debasement and inflation,” specified no time frame and used the word “risk” rather than “certainty” — making it impossible to declare the signatories definitively right or wrong years later.

Tetlock traces his awakening to a 1984 panel convened to prevent nuclear war, attended by Nobel laureates and intelligence specialists. He was the least impressive member — a thirty-year-old associate professor at Berkeley. The experts were confident, articulate, and certain. Both liberals and conservatives predicted the next Soviet leader would be another stern Communist Party man. Then Mikhail Gorbachev arrived, launched glasnost and perestroika, and transformed the world. Few experts saw it coming, but afterward, every one of them explained why they had seen it coming all along. Liberals said the Soviet economy was crumbling, so reform was inevitable. Conservatives said Reagan called the Soviets’ bluff. Tetlock’s inner cynic concluded that no matter what had happened, experts would have been equally adept at downplaying their failures.

Sherman Kent, the legendary CIA analyst who shaped American intelligence analysis, discovered the fundamental ambiguity problem in 1951. A National Intelligence Estimate said an attack on Yugoslavia should be considered “a serious possibility.” Kent thought this meant 65-to-35 odds. A State Department official took it to mean much lower odds. When Kent polled his own team, their interpretations ranged from 80-to-20 in favor of invasion to 20-to-80 against — exactly opposite meanings from the same phrase. Kent proposed a chart mapping words to numbers: “probable” = 75%, “chances about even” = 50%, and so on. His colleagues resisted. It felt “tacky,” like being a bookie. Kent’s response: “I’d rather be a bookie than a goddamn poet.” His proposal was not adopted until after the Iraq WMD debacle, decades after his death.

The chapter introduces Brier scores, developed by meteorologist Glenn W. Brier in 1950. They measure the distance between a forecast and reality: 0 means perfection, 0.5 means random guessing (the chimp), and 2.0 is maximally wrong. Calibration — whether your 70% forecasts come true 70% of the time — is necessary but not sufficient. Resolution matters too: the ability to assign high probabilities to things that happen and low ones to things that don’t. You can be perfectly calibrated but useless if every forecast hovers near 50%.

Tetlock then presents the results of his twenty-year Expert Political Judgment (EPJ) study: 284 professionals, ~28,000 predictions, questions spanning one to ten years. The punch line about dart-throwing chimps obscured the richer finding: there were two statistically distinguishable groups. Drawing on Isaiah Berlin’s essay about a scrap of 2,500-year-old Greek poetry — “The fox knows many things but the hedgehog knows one big thing” — Tetlock labeled them. Hedgehogs organized their thinking around Big Ideas, pushed analyses to extremes, and were reluctant to change their minds. Foxes drew on many analytical tools, shifted mental gears, talked in probabilities, and admitted mistakes. Foxes beat hedgehogs on both calibration and resolution. Hedgehogs, in their longer-range forecasts, actually lost to the chimpanzee.

Larry Kudlow is the prototypical hedgehog. The CNBC host and former Reagan-era economist had one Big Idea: supply-side economics. After the Bush tax cuts, he confidently predicted “the Bush boom” year after year, even as the economy disappointed. In December 2007, months into what would become the Great Recession, Kudlow wrote: “There is no recession. In fact, we are about to enter the seventh consecutive year of the Bush boom.” Through spring and summer of 2008, as warning signs multiplied, Kudlow insisted: “We are in a mental recession, not an actual recession.” He kept repeating this until Lehman Brothers collapsed on September 15. A hedgehog’s Big Idea is like green-tinted glasses from the Emerald City — everything looks green, and more information only increases confidence, not accuracy. The EPJ data revealed a disturbing inverse correlation: the more famous an expert, the less accurate their forecasts.

The chapter closes with the dragonfly eye metaphor. Dragonflies have enormous compound eyes, each covered with up to thirty thousand tiny lenses, giving them near-360-degree vision. Foxes are intellectual dragonflies: they aggregate many perspectives into a single acute vision. Francis Galton’s famous 1906 demonstration — where the crowd at a county fair guessed an ox’s weight to within one pound of the correct answer — shows why aggregation works: valid information from different people points in the same direction, while errors are random and cancel out. Foxes do this inside a single skull, synthesizing diverse sources into one judgment.

Annie Duke, the World Series of Poker champion and former psychology PhD student, teaches poker players to see through opponents’ eyes. Her students universally assume that an opponent who raises must have a strong hand — but when she reverses the scenario, they realize they themselves would never raise with a strong hand (they would slow-play to keep the opponent in the pot). Even passionate players paying a thousand dollars for her seminar fail to take the opponent’s perspective until Duke walks them through it step by step. Foxes are likelier to engage in this kind of perspective-taking, which is why it is a hallmark of superforecasting.

Chapter 4: Superforecasters

The Iraq WMD debacle opened the door for everything that followed. In October 2002, the intelligence community’s National Intelligence Estimate declared with virtual certainty that Saddam Hussein had weapons of mass destruction. The language admitted no possibility of surprise: “Iraq has...” and “Baghdad has...” CIA Director George Tenet reportedly called it a “slam dunk.” After the 2003 invasion, the US turned Iraq upside down and found nothing. It was arguably the worst intelligence failure in modern history. Robert Jervis, author of Why Intelligence Fails, concluded that the IC’s judgment was both reasonable and wrong — a less confident estimate (60% or 70% instead of near-certainty) might have prevented Congress from authorizing the invasion. There were no red teams, no devil’s advocates, no papers exploring alternative possibilities. Most strikingly, no one had even considered the possibility that Saddam had ended his weapons programs.

Jervis’s distinction between a reasonable judgment and a correct one cuts to the heart of forecasting. In poker, betting big on a hand with an eighty-percent probability of winning and then losing does not make the bet foolish. Winning on a reckless long shot does not make the gamble wise. Good poker players, good investors, and good executives all understand this: the quality of a decision is separate from the quality of the outcome. The intelligence community’s error was not in concluding that Saddam likely had WMDs — virtually every intelligence agency on Earth reached the same conclusion, because Saddam was behaving like a man hiding something. The error was in the certainty. Had they expressed it as a sixty or seventy percent probability, the congressional vote to authorize force might have gone differently. Stakes rarely get higher than thousands of lives and trillions of dollars.

The IC had accountability for process — did analysts consider alternative hypotheses? look for contrary evidence? — but not for accuracy. It was like ensuring doctors examine patients thoroughly but never checking whether the treatments work. Richards Heuer’s CIA analyst manual teaches the psychology of cognitive biases, fine work that has never been tested for efficacy. Senior officials claimed their analysts were right eighty or ninety percent of the time, but these were “just guesses, like nineteenth-century physicians who were sure their treatments cured patients eighty or ninety percent of the time.” The fifty-billion-dollar intelligence apparatus could not answer the most basic question about itself.

The shock of Iraq drove the creation of IARPA in 2006 and eventually the forecasting tournament. In the summer of 2010, two IARPA officials visited Tetlock in Berkeley with news that felt too good to be true: they would sponsor a massive tournament pitting five research teams against each other and against an independent control group. Questions would be in the “Goldilocks zone” of difficulty — neither so easy that an attentive newspaper reader could answer them nor so hard that no one could. Within each team, researchers could run Cochrane-style randomized experiments: give training to one randomly chosen group and not another, then compare results. Several thousand volunteers signed up in year one; roughly 3,200 passed the initial psychometric gauntlet and started forecasting.

Doug Lorch is the poster child for what the tournament discovered. A retired IBM programmer living in Santa Barbara with his artist wife, Doug has no special expertise in international affairs. His Facebook avatar is a duck. He drives a red Miata convertible. He reads the New York Times and can find Kazakhstan on a map. In year one, Doug answered 104 questions, making roughly a thousand separate forecasts, and finished with a Brier score of 0.22, ranking fifth among 2,800 competitors. In year two, placed on a superforecaster team, he achieved a Brier score of 0.14 — the best in the tournament. He beat a prediction market by forty percent and single-handedly exceeded the performance target IARPA had set for year four. His only payment was a $250 Amazon gift certificate. The man was a threat to a multibillion-dollar intelligence apparatus.

Doug was not unique. There were fifty-eight others in that first class of superforecasters. Their collective Brier score was 0.25, compared with 0.37 for regular forecasters — and the gap widened in later years. By the end of the four-year tournament, superforecasters had outperformed regulars by over sixty percent. Superforecasters looking three hundred days into the future were more accurate than regular forecasters looking one hundred days out. Regular forecasters needed to triple their foresight to match.

But could this be luck? Michael Mauboussin’s The Success Equation provides the test: regression to the mean. In a pure-luck activity like coin-flipping, people who score well in round one regress all the way back to average in round two. In a pure-skill activity, they stay put. The year-to-year correlation of individual superforecaster performance was about 0.65 — higher than the correlation between fathers’ and sons’ heights. About thirty percent of superforecasters fell from the top two percent each year, but seventy percent stayed. The probability of such consistency by chance? Less than one in a hundred million. As a group, superforecasters actually increased their lead in years two and three — the opposite of regression — because being identified, congratulated, and placed on teams of fellow superforecasters improved their performance enough to offset regression. Mostly, their results reflected genuine skill.

The GJP’s winning method combined several innovations. The key insight was to identify the top two percent of forecasters, weight their forecasts more heavily in the aggregate, and then “extremize” the result — pushing probabilities closer to 0% or 100%. The logic: if every forecaster had access to all the information held by all the others, they would each be more confident. Since we cannot share all information perfectly, extremizing simulates the effect. A few hundred ordinary people armed with this algorithm and their own careful judgment beat every other method, every university competitor, and even professional intelligence analysts reading classified intercepts. David Ignatius of the Washington Post reported that superforecasters “performed about 30 percent better than the average for intelligence community analysts who could read intercepts and other secret data.” Think about what that means: a retiree with a $250 gift certificate and a laptop was outperforming the multibillion-dollar intelligence apparatus of the United States.

Chapter 5: Supersmart?

Sanford “Sandy” Sillman was diagnosed with multiple sclerosis in 2008. By 2011, walking was difficult and typing was a struggle. He could see the writing on the wall — he would soon have to give up his career as an atmospheric research scientist at the University of Michigan. He joined the Good Judgment Project as a “transition project” to keep his mind alive. Sandy’s credentials are staggering: a double major in math and physics from Brown, a master’s from MIT in technology and policy, a second master’s in applied math from Harvard, a PhD in applied physics from Harvard, fluency in five languages. In year one, randomly assigned to a control condition where he forecasted alone, Sandy finished with a Brier score of 0.19, tying for overall champion among 2,800 competitors. It would be easy to conclude that his extraordinary mind explains his results.

The data tell a more nuanced story. GJP measured both fluid intelligence (abstract pattern recognition) and crystallized intelligence (knowledge). Regular forecasters scored around the seventieth percentile of the general population. Superforecasters scored around the eightieth. The big jump was from the general public to the pool of forecasters — self-selected people who thought forecasting sounded fun. The jump from regular forecasters to superforecasters was modest. Superforecasters are well above average but not off-the-charts geniuses. Most fall well short of the “genius” threshold. Intelligence and knowledge help, but they add little beyond a certain threshold — confirming the hunch Daniel Kahneman shared with Tetlock at a Berkeley lunch in 1988: high-powered experts might be only a tad more accurate than attentive readers of the New York Times. Robert McNamara, the man famously dubbed one of “the best and the brightest,” escalated Vietnam based on the domino theory without ever seriously analyzing the forecast. “We failed to analyze our assumptions critically, then or later.”

The chapter introduces Fermi estimation as a core superforecasting technique. Enrico Fermi loved posing questions like “How many piano tuners are there in Chicago?” to students who had no data at all. The key is to break an unknowable question into tractable sub-questions: How many pianos are in Chicago? How often are they tuned? How long does a tuning take? How many hours does a tuner work per year? Each sub-answer involves guesswork, but by separating the knowable from the unknowable and inspecting each guess individually, the errors tend to partially cancel out. Tetlock’s own worked example yields sixty-three piano tuners; the actual Chicago Yellow Pages listing was about eighty-three. Sandy Sillman told Tetlock that Fermi estimation was so critical to his scientific work that “it became a part of my natural way of thinking.”

The Yasser Arafat polonium question demonstrates Fermi-ization in action. After Swiss researchers found high levels of polonium-210 in Arafat’s belongings, IARPA asked whether tests on his exhumed remains would find elevated levels. Most people would immediately think about Middle Eastern politics — “Israel would never do that!” or “Of course Israel did it!” Both reactions answer a different question (did Israel poison Arafat?) rather than the actual question (will the test find polonium?). Bill Flack avoided this trap by starting with science, not politics: Can polonium be detected on remains dead for years? He checked the Swiss report and learned it was possible. Then he mapped multiple pathways to a “yes” result: Israel could have poisoned him, Palestinian enemies could have done it, or contamination could have been planted post-mortem. Each additional pathway increased the probability.

Superforecasters start with the outside view — the base rate — before touching the inside view. When asked whether the Renzetti family owns a pet, most people dive into the family’s specific details (Italian name, small child, widowed grandmother). Superforecasters first check: sixty-two percent of American households own pets. That is the anchor. Then they adjust based on specifics — the family lives in a single-family detached home (pet ownership rate: seventy-three percent), which provides a better anchor. Peggy Noonan once marveled that George W. Bush’s approval rating had rebounded to forty-seven percent after leaving office. The outside view: every president’s approval rises after leaving office, even Richard Nixon’s. Bush’s rebound was unremarkable.

After establishing the outside view, superforecasters conduct a targeted inside-view investigation. The key word is targeted. A good exploration is “an investigation, not an amble.” For the Arafat question, this means researching each hypothesis systematically: Does Israel have access to polonium? Did it want Arafat dead badly enough to take such a risk? Could Palestinian rivals have done it? Each question generates sub-questions that can be researched. Then the outside and inside views must be synthesized, just as the brain merges the images from two eyes into a single vision.

The chapter introduces Active Open-Mindedness (AOM), a concept coined by psychologist Jonathan Baron. Superforecasters score highly on Baron’s test, which asks whether you agree that “people should take into consideration evidence that goes against their beliefs” and that “it is more useful to pay attention to those who disagree with you.” Doug Lorch does not merely hold these views — he acts on them. He built a database of hundreds of news sources tagged by ideology, subject matter, and geography, then wrote a program that selects his reading to emphasize diversity. He is not merely open-minded. He is actively open-minded.

David Rogg, a semiretired software engineer in Virginia, demonstrated the complete method on a terrorism question posed shortly after the Charlie Hebdo massacre. IARPA asked whether an Islamist attack would occur in specified European countries within two months. Rogg started with the outside view: he counted Islamist terror attacks in those countries over the prior five years — six attacks, yielding a base rate of 1.2 per year. He then adjusted for the inside view: ISIS recruitment was surging, but security had also tightened post-Charlie Hebdo. Net adjustment: base rate up to 1.8 per year. With sixty-nine days remaining in the forecast period, the math yielded 34%. Rogg shared his analysis on the GJP forum — not as a final answer, but to invite critique. Superforecasters constantly seek other perspectives to fold into their own.

For superforecasters, beliefs are hypotheses to be tested, not treasures to be guarded. — Philip Tetlock

Chapter 6: Superquants?

Lionel Levine, an assistant professor of mathematics at Cornell with a Harvard AB and a Berkeley PhD, is one of the GJP’s superforecasters. Given superforecasters’ numeracy — many have backgrounds in math, science, or programming — it would be natural to suspect that arcane mathematical models explain their success. But Levine himself debunks the idea. “I can think of a couple of questions where a little bit of math was useful,” he said, “but otherwise I rely on subjective judgment.” In fact, Levine deliberately set out to prove he could be a good forecaster without using any math — as a point of pride. The great majority of superforecaster forecasts are simply the product of careful thought and nuanced judgment, not algorithms or Monte Carlo simulations.

The Osama bin Laden decision illustrates why fine-grained probability matters. In Zero Dark Thirty, the fictional Leon Panetta asks his analysts whether bin Laden is in the Abbottabad compound. What he wants more than anything is agreement. When he gets a range of estimates — sixty percent, eighty percent, sixty percent — he calls it “a clusterfuck.” The movie’s hero, Maya, declares “a hundred percent, he’s there” — and the audience cheers. But the fictional Panetta is wrong to want agreement: disagreement among independently thinking analysts is the wisdom of the crowd, gift-wrapped. And Maya is “right but unreasonable” — the mirror image of the intelligence community being “wrong but reasonable” about Iraq’s WMDs. The real Leon Panetta welcomed the diversity of views: “Nothing is one hundred percent,” he told Tetlock. He thinks like a superforecaster.

Amos Tversky once joked that most people have only three settings on their mental dial: “gonna happen,” “not gonna happen,” and “maybe.” This three-setting mental dial is a product of evolution. For a hunter-gatherer deciding whether the shadow in the grass is a lion, two settings (yes, run! / no, relax) give quick, clear guidance. Distinguishing a sixty-percent probability from an eighty-percent probability adds nothing useful — and the slower analysis could get you killed. Parents will pay two to three times more to reduce a disease risk from five percent to zero than from ten percent to five percent, because zero delivers certainty. Harry Truman wanted a “one-armed economist” who would not say “on the one hand… on the other hand.” We equate confidence with competence: confident financial advisers are trusted more even when their track records are identical.

Robert Rubin, the former Treasury secretary, understood this from a Harvard lecture where a philosophy professor argued there is no provable certainty. It became his axiom through twenty-six years at Goldman Sachs and his tenure in the Clinton White House. When a young aide said a bill would “absolutely” pass Congress, Rubin corrected him: “I say the probability is 60%—and we can argue over whether it’s 59 or 60%.” He was frustrated that when he told Congress there was an eighty-percent probability of something, “people would treat it as a certainty.” You had to pound the table to remind them: “this also might not happen.” People found Rubin’s probabilistic thinking so startling that they pinned his thoughts to cubicle walls alongside inspirational messages.

Granularity predicts accuracy. Superforecaster Brian Labatte from Montreal, asked in casual conversation how much fiction versus nonfiction he reads, paused and said, “I would say 70%… no, 65/35 nonfiction to fiction.” Fully one-third of superforecaster forecasts used single-percentage-point precision (three percent rather than four percent). When Barbara Mellers tested whether this granularity was meaningful by rounding forecasts to the nearest five or ten, superforecasters lost accuracy with even the smallest rounding. Regular forecasters lost little even from rounding four times as large. Charlie Munger captured the stakes: “If you don’t get this elementary, but mildly unnatural, mathematics of elementary probability into your repertoire, then you go through a long life like a one-legged man in an ass-kicking contest.”

Probabilistic thinking and belief in fate are in tension. Superforecasters scored lowest on “pro-fate” statements (“Everything happens for a reason”) and highest on “pro-probability” statements (“Nothing is inevitable”). Individual fate scores correlated with Brier scores: the more a forecaster inclined toward meant-to-be thinking, the less accurate their forecasts. Finding meaning in events is positively correlated with wellbeing but negatively correlated with foresight — a depressing trade-off.

Obama’s reaction to his intelligence officers’ estimates on the bin Laden compound is telling. After hearing probabilities ranging from thirty to ninety-five percent, the president declared: “This is fifty-fifty. Look guys, this is a flip of the coin.” Tetlock suspects Obama did not mean fifty percent literally — he meant “I’m not sure,” which is how most people use the phrase. As an executive making a decision, Obama may have concluded that whether the probability was fifty, seventy, or ninety percent did not change the decision to act, so he cut the discussion short and moved on. But if he was retreating to the “ignorance prior” — discarding the analysts’ information because their disagreement made him distrust it all — that would be a mistake. Disagreement is information. The median estimate around seventy percent was far more useful than a coin flip.

The National Intelligence Council, which produces the estimates that inform decisions like whether to invade Iraq or negotiate with Iran, asks its analysts to make judgments on a five- or seven-degree scale — “remote,” “unlikely,” “even chance,” “probably likely,” “almost certainly.” That is a big improvement over the two-setting dial, but it still falls far short of the granularity superforecasters achieve routinely. Tetlock suspects the NIC is selling itself short. With the right culture and encouragement, these top-flight analysts could push themselves to the precision that distinguishes good from great. The reward is “a clearer perception of the future — and that’s invaluable in the ass-kicking contest of life.”

Chapter 7: Supernewsjunkies?

Superforecasters do consume a lot of news, but that is not what makes them super. The chapter opens with a summary of the superforecasting method: unpack the question, adopt the outside view, then the inside view, synthesize like a dragonfly, express judgment with fine-grained probability. But all of this is “literally just the beginning.” Forecasts must be updated as information changes. Devyn Duffy, a Pittsburgh income maintenance caseworker who joined the GJP after losing his factory job at thirty-six, exemplifies the updating machine. He set Google alerts for every forecast question and, in season three alone, made 2,271 forecasts on 140 questions — more than sixteen per question on average. Superforecasters update much more frequently than regular forecasters.

The two dangers of updating are Scylla (underreaction) and Charybdis (overreaction). Bill Flack committed underreaction on the Yasukuni Shrine question: when an unofficial source said Japanese Prime Minister Shinzo Abe would visit the controversial shrine, Bill discounted it because visiting made no rational sense. He later realized he had pulled a bait-and-switch on himself — he was answering “Would a rational prime minister visit?” instead of “Will Abe visit?” Doug Lorch committed overreaction on an Arctic sea ice question, swinging from 55% to 95% on a single month-old report that later proved misleading.

The deepest source of underreaction is commitment. Beliefs build on each other like blocks in a Jenga tower. A belief near the top can be removed easily. But a belief near the base — tied to identity, career, or self-concept — threatens to bring the whole structure down. Dan Kahan’s research shows that views on gun control often correlate with views on climate change despite no logical connection, because both are identity markers. When asked to imagine conclusive evidence they are wrong, many people refuse even to consider it. The men who imprisoned 112,000 Japanese Americans during World War II illustrate the extreme: Earl Warren’s justification for continued internment was that the “very fact that no sabotage has taken place to date is a disturbing and confirming indication that such action will be taken” — the absence of evidence as evidence.

Overreaction stems from the dilution effect. In experiments, participants given a diagnostic fact (a student studies 31 hours a week) predict a high GPA. Add irrelevant information (he plays tennis, had short relationships) and their confidence drops — even though the added information is demonstrably meaningless. Stock market traders suffer from the same bias, reacting to “day-to-day fluctuations of an ephemeral and nonsignificant character” as Keynes observed. Burton Malkiel found that active traders earned annual returns of only 11.4% and would have been better off going golfing.

Tim Minto, a forty-five-year-old Vancouver software engineer, won the top spot in season three with a Brier score of 0.15. His secret: many small updates. On the Syrian refugees question, he updated his forecast thirty-four times over three months, with an average change of only 3.5 percentage points. On one question about the US-Afghanistan troop agreement, he made seventy-seven separate forecasts. A few small updates would mean underreaction. Many large updates would mean overreaction. Many small updates slip safely between Scylla and Charybdis. The tournament data confirm: superforecasters update more often AND in smaller increments. It is, Tetlock admits, “even, to be candid, a tad boring.”

The logic behind this is Bayesian, even when superforecasters do not use Bayes’ theorem explicitly. Jay Ulfelder, a political scientist and superforecaster, demonstrated the approach when a pundit predicted Chuck Hagel would withdraw his nomination for defense secretary after a bad hearing. Ulfelder started with the base rate: only one of twenty-four defense secretary nominees had ever been rejected, none had withdrawn. Prior probability: 96%. Even with generous assumptions about how much a bad hearing should matter, Bayesian updating reduced the probability to 83% — still very likely. Hagel was confirmed two weeks later. Tim Minto describes himself as “a Bayesian who does not use Bayes’ theorem” — he has an intuitive grasp of the logic but probably could not write the formula from memory.

The crucial exception: sometimes you need to make a large update. When Doug Lorch realized the Arctic sea ice report that drove his 95% forecast was clearly failing, he did not gradually adjust downward. He ditched the report entirely, throttled back to his opening forecast of 55%, then dropped to 15%. As George Orwell wrote in “Politics and the English Language,” after listing six emphatic rules for good writing: “Break any of these rules sooner than saying anything outright barbarous.”

Even if the tournament had asked for only one forecast per question with no updating, superforecasters would have won decisively — their initial forecasts were at least fifty percent more accurate than those of regular forecasters. But the updating advantage compounds. A famous political scientist declined to participate in the tournament because he had no interest in “competing with unemployed newsjunkies.” The dismissal was revealing. It assumed that paying attention and updating forecasts is trivial labor, beneath a serious scholar. In fact, good updating requires the same demanding cognitive skills as making the initial forecast — the same Fermi-ization, the same balancing of outside and inside views, the same granular probability estimation. It is not mindlessly adjusting forecasts in response to whatever is on CNN. It is the continuation of the analysis by other means.

Chapter 8: Perpetual Beta

Mary Simpson has a PhD in economics from Claremont. She managed regulatory and financial affairs for Southern California Edison before semiretiring. Like most experts in her field, she missed the 2007–08 financial crisis. Her retirement savings cratered. “I really wanted to think more about forecasting,” she recalled. “It’s one of those things where you feel like, ‘I should be better at this.’” She joined the GJP and became a superforecaster. Carol Dweck would say Simpson has a growth mindset — the belief that abilities are products of effort, not fixed traits. In Dweck’s experiments, fifth graders given harder puzzles split sharply: fixed-mindset children gave up, while growth-mindset children loved the challenge even more. “Could you write down the name of these puzzles,” one child asked, “so my mom can buy me some more when these ones run out?”

John Maynard Keynes epitomized the growth mindset in investing. From the end of World War I to 1946, he managed money for himself, family, insurance companies, and Cambridge’s King’s College — and died extremely wealthy despite investing through some of the worst years in economic history. He was nearly wiped out in 1920 by bad currency bets. He bounced back, only to be blindsided by the crash of 1929. Each time, he treated failure as an opportunity to learn. After 1929, he concluded that stock prices do not always reflect true value and independently developed what Benjamin Graham called “value investing” — the cornerstone of Warren Buffett’s fortune. “There is no harm in being sometimes wrong,” Keynes wrote, “especially if one is promptly found out.”

Effective practice requires clear, timely feedback. Police officers who try to detect lying get no immediate feedback — the truth may not emerge for months or years. Predictably, their confidence grows faster than their accuracy, making them increasingly overconfident. Meteorologists, by contrast, are well-calibrated because they get feedback every day. Brier scores gave superforecasters the ideal feedback loop: Tim Minto scored 0.07 on Syrian refugees (a nothing-but-net free throw) and 1.46 on the Yasukuni Shrine question (ball into the trash can at the back of the gymnasium). There was no ambiguity.

Superforecasters conduct rigorous postmortems. After his team got drubbed on the Arafat-polonium question, Devyn Duffy posted: “We agreed as a team that the half-life of polonium would make detection virtually impossible. We didn’t do nearly enough to question that assumption.” Jean-Pierre Beugoms, another superforecaster, started leaving longer comments on every forecast because his earlier sparse notes meant he “often could not figure out why I made a certain forecast.” The most impressive postmortem insight is distinguishing success from luck. After a correct forecast, Devyn wrote: “We lucked out, too.” In Tetlock’s EPJ research, experts welcomed “I was almost right” scenarios but rejected “I was almost wrong” alternatives. Devyn’s willingness to acknowledge luck in triumph is rare and valuable.

Elizabeth Sloane brings grit into sharp focus. Diagnosed with brain cancer, she endured chemotherapy, a failed stem-cell transplant, recurrence, and two more years of chemo. She volunteered for the GJP to “re-grow her synapses.” Anne Kilkenny, a housewife from a small town in Alaska, “four decades removed from having had a real intellectual challenge,” became a superforecaster through sheer tenacity. When a question about Central Africa Republic refugees led her to the UN’s website, she noticed the data were a week old — and e-mailed the agency to ask how often they update. The response came in French. She wrote back asking for English. A lengthy, revealing reply came back, giving her a huge analytical advantage.

The chapter closes with a summary of the superforecaster profile. In thinking: intellectually curious, reflective, numerate. In methods: pragmatic, dragonfly-eyed, probabilistic, thoughtful updaters. In work ethic: growth mindset, grit. The strongest predictor of rising to superforecaster status is perpetual beta — the commitment to continuous belief updating and self-improvement — roughly three times as powerful as intelligence. Superforecasting appears to be roughly seventy-five percent perspiration, twenty-five percent inspiration.

Michael Polanyi captured why practice matters with a detailed physics explanation of riding a bicycle: the curvature of each winding is inversely proportional to the square of the speed, and so on for paragraphs. No one has ever learned to ride a bicycle from such a description. The knowledge required is “tacit knowledge” — the kind you can only get from bruising experience. The same is true of forecasting. Reading about base rates and Bayesian updating is necessary but insufficient. You must try, fail, analyze why you failed, adjust, and try again. The cycle can take months for a single question, and luck complicates the feedback, which is why grit matters so much.

The barriers to good feedback are formidable. Vague language is elastic — Bertram Forer demonstrated this by giving every student the same personality profile assembled from an astrology book, and they rated it 4.2 out of 5 for accuracy. Hindsight bias is equally treacherous: in Tetlock’s EPJ research, experts who had estimated a 10% chance of something recalled their estimate as 40 or 50% after it happened — the “I knew it all along” effect. Forecasters operating with vague language and flawed memory are “like basketball players doing free throws in the dark.” Brier scores turned on the lights.

Chapter 9: Superteams

In January 1961, the New York Times ran a front-page story exposing the CIA’s secret training of Cuban exiles in Guatemala. The Kennedy team’s response? Concern but no reconsideration. The Bay of Pigs invasion went ahead, the fourteen hundred guerrillas were surrounded by twenty thousand Cuban soldiers, and within three days all were dead or captured. The contingency plan — escape to the Escambray Mountains — came from an earlier version where the landing site was nearby. The site had been moved eighty miles away, through swamp and jungle. Nobody noticed. Irving Janis identified the culprit in Victims of Groupthink: cozy unanimity had suppressed all doubt. Eighteen months later, the same cast of characters brilliantly managed the Cuban missile crisis — because Kennedy had reformed the process, designating Bobby Kennedy and Theodore Sorensen as devil’s advocates, setting aside hierarchy, and sometimes leaving the room so his team would speak freely.

In the IARPA tournament, teams were twenty-three percent more accurate than individuals on average in year one. When superforecasters were placed on teams in year two, their accuracy improved by a further fifty percent. The hierarchy of accuracy: wisdom of the crowd < ordinary teams (+10%) < prediction markets (+20% over teams) < superteams (+15–30% over prediction markets). Paul Theron, a South African superforecaster, demonstrated the power of teamwork on a question about the Honduran presidential election by tracking down a political scientist specializing in Honduran politics and finding an obscure analysis website. “The team is so much more effective at gathering information than one person could ever be,” he said. Superteams resembled Amy Edmondson’s best surgical teams: psychologically safe, with a shared purpose, saying “our” more than “my.”

Adam Grant categorizes people as givers, matchers, and takers. Superteams had lots of givers — people like Marty Rosenthal, who led by example, organized conference calls and backyard barbecues, and made special efforts to assure teammates that criticism was welcome. Doug Lorch distributed programming tools that got others thinking about creating their own. Scott Page’s principle — “diversity trumps ability” — suggests that deliberate diversity in team composition could yield even better results, a question Tetlock hopes to explore in future research.

The question of whether to form teams at all was genuinely uncertain. On the negative side, research literature suggested teams foster cognitive loafing — why labor when others will do the heavy lifting? Worse, groupthink could set in, with excessive politeness suppressing critical examination. On the positive side, teams allow information sharing and perspective aggregation, which is the foundation of dragonfly-eye vision. Tetlock and Mellers honestly did not know which effect would dominate. So they took Archie Cochrane’s advice and ran an experiment. The results were unequivocal: teams outperformed individuals by twenty-three percent in year one.

Teams were not immune to social friction. Elaine Rich found it difficult to question the views of strangers, especially on sensitive topics. The Arafat-polonium discussion had “a lot of tension — it was almost a taboo question.” Marty Rosenthal observed a lot of “dancing around” — people would disagree but couch it in careful words, circling the point without making it. Over time, team members explicitly invited pushback: “Everybody has said, ‘I want push-back from you if you see something I don’t.’” Offering thanks for constructive criticism gradually replaced the dancing.

The training GJP gave teams included precision questioning — a Socratic technique for dissecting vague claims. If someone says “the popularity of soccer is starting to decline,” don’t say “You’re silly” (adds heat, not light) or “I don’t think so” (mere disagreement). Zero in: “What do you mean by ‘pastime’? What evidence is there that popularity is declining? Over what time frame?” Imagine how events might have gone if the Kennedy team had asked: “How far is the Bay of Pigs from the Escambray Mountains?” Answer: eighty miles through swamp and jungle. The contingency plan would have collapsed on contact with that single fact.

The extremizing algorithm that powered GJP’s tournament-winning results also depended on team dynamics. Extremizing works by simulating what would happen if every forecaster had access to all the information: they would grow more confident, pushing probabilities toward 0% or 100%. Superforecaster teams were already good at sharing information, which reduced diversity somewhat — so extremizing helped them less. Regular forecaster teams shared less effectively, so extremizing gave them a bigger boost. Indeed, extremizing regular forecaster teams enough could push them past some superteams. The lesson: aggregation and information-sharing are not competing tools but complementary ones, and the optimal blend depends on the group.

The results speak for themselves: on average, when a forecaster did well enough in year one to become a superforecaster and was put on a superforecaster team in year two, that person became fifty percent more accurate. These remarkably inexpensive forecasts — generated by volunteers on gift certificates — should be on the desks of decision makers, including the president of the United States.

Chapter 10: The Leader’s Dilemma

Leaders need confidence, decisiveness, and vision. Superforecasting demands humility, self-criticism, and comfort with uncertainty. The contradiction looks serious but is, Tetlock argues, more apparent than real. Helmuth von Moltke, the nineteenth-century Prussian general, articulated the resolution: “No plan of operations extends with certainty beyond the first encounter with the enemy’s main strength.” The principle of Auftragstaktik (“mission command”) pushes decision-making down the hierarchy. Tell subordinates what to accomplish, not how. Give them the why so they can improvise. General Seydlitz at the Battle of Rossbach in 1757 refused Frederick the Great’s order to attack until he judged the moment right — despite the threat of execution. “Tell the King that after the battle my head is at his disposal, but meanwhile I will make use of it.” He attacked when ready and won the battle.

Dwight Eisenhower understood Moltke’s philosophy better than Germany’s own Supreme Commander. Before giving the irrevocable D-Day order, he wrote a note accepting personal responsibility in case of failure. He expected officers to debate openly. In 1954, when Army chief Matthew Ridgway advised against intervening in Vietnam, warning it would take over half a million soldiers, Eisenhower listened — because years earlier Ridgway had resisted one of Eisenhower’s own orders and been proven right. David Petraeus continued this tradition, pushing officers out of their “intellectual comfort zone” and developing training exercises that forced them to deal with genuine surprises. “The bold move is the right move except when it’s the wrong move,” Petraeus says. A leader must figure out which and execute boldly. Jeff Bezos codified this at Amazon: “Have backbone; disagree and commit.”

The key insight is the distinction between self-confidence and intellectual humility. Annie Duke captures it perfectly: “You have to have tremendous humility in the face of the game because the game is extremely complex. That being said, humility in the face of the game is extremely different than humility in the face of your opponents.” Intellectual humility is not self-doubt. It is a recognition that reality is profoundly complex, seeing things clearly is a constant struggle, and human judgment must therefore be riddled with mistakes. You can think very highly of yourself and still be intellectually humble. The combination is wonderfully fruitful.

The German military tradition valued independent thinking so deeply that officers received what we would today consider a liberal arts education with an emphasis on critical thinking. In other nations of that era, instructors laid out problems, told students the right answer, and expected them to memorize it. In Germany’s war academies, scenarios were presented and students were invited to suggest solutions and discuss them collectively. Disagreement was expected. Even the instructors’ views could be challenged. The story of General Seydlitz at Rossbach — disobeying a direct order from Frederick the Great, then winning the battle — was collective cultural knowledge, recounted and retold countless times in the officer corps. The fundamental message: think. If necessary, discuss your orders. If you absolutely must, disobey them.

The Wehrmacht demonstrated what mission command looks like in extremis. On May 10, 1940, the critical assault on the Belgian fortress of Eben Emael was entrusted to a young lieutenant, Rudolf Witzig, whose own glider crash-landed a hundred kilometers off target in Germany. Flying in radio silence, the remaining troops only discovered on landing that they had lost their commander and much of their force. A sergeant took charge of the troops on the fort’s roof and destroyed the Belgian guns. Then another glider swooped down — Witzig, who had secured a replacement. He took command and finished the job. The operation succeeded because every soldier, down to the lowliest private, understood the mission’s purpose and was empowered to improvise. The Wehrmacht ultimately failed for many reasons, but one was that Adolf Hitler took direct control of operations in violation of Moltke’s principles — nowhere with more disastrous effect than on D-Day, when his aides refused to wake him to authorize moving the tank reserves.

The tension between deliberation and action is real but manageable. The Wehrmacht manual captured it precisely: “Once a course of action has been initiated it must not be abandoned without overriding reason. In the changing situations of combat, however, inflexibly clinging to a course of action can lead to failure. The art of leadership consists of the timely recognition of circumstances and of the moment when a new decision is required.” An imperfect decision made in time was considered better than a perfect one made too late. Time devoted to a decision should be constrained by circumstances — leisurely when the situation permits, abrupt when bullets are flying.

Ralph Peters, a retired colonel, argued in a 2007 magazine article that the military needs doers, not thinkers — Henry V, not Hamlet. “Hamlet thinks too much,” he wrote. Petraeus sees the divide between doers and thinkers as another false dichotomy. Leaders must be both. “The most powerful tool any soldier carries is not his weapon but his mind,” Petraeus says. Jeff Bezos’s Amazon leadership principle — “Have backbone; disagree and commit” — captures the same idea in corporate language: leaders must challenge decisions respectfully, then commit wholly once the decision is made. Walmart’s leadership academy, designed by a former Royal Marine, is modeled on the same mission command philosophy.

Even superforecasters who are paragons of intellectual humility need substantial ego. The leaders and thinkers Tetlock admires — Moltke, Sherman Kent, Cochrane, Keynes, Soros — all had enormous self-assurance. George Soros’s most famous bet, shorting the British pound in 1992 for an estimated $1.1 billion profit, required selling sterling worth almost $10 billion. “There is nothing like danger to focus the mind,” Soros said. Eisenhower sincerely did not want the presidency, but when it became clear that an isolationist would win the 1952 Republican nomination, he concluded that he — and he alone — could prevent a catastrophe. The self-confidence to act and the intellectual humility to question your own judgment are not contradictions. They are the two wings that keep superforecasting airborne.

Chapter 11: Are They Really So Super?

Daniel Kahneman cut to the chase in the summer of 2014: “Do you see them as different kinds of people, or as people who do different kinds of things?” Tetlock’s answer: “A bit of both.” But Kahneman worries about sustainability. The continuous self-scrutiny that separates superforecasters from everyone else is exhausting. General Michael Flynn, then head of the Defense Intelligence Agency, demonstrated the problem: he declared we are in “a period of prolonged societal conflict that is pretty unprecedented.” The data show the opposite — interstate wars declining since the 1950s, civil wars since the late 1980s, violence at historic lows. An accomplished man making an easily checkable error because it felt obviously true. The Müller-Lyer illusion — two identical lines that look different — cannot be switched off even when you know it’s an illusion.

To test superforecasters’ limits, Kahneman and Mellers probed scope insensitivity, one of Kahneman’s original findings. People pay roughly the same amount to clean up a small region’s lakes or all 250,000 lakes in Ontario. Regular forecasters showed the same insensitivity: they estimated a 40% chance of Assad’s regime falling in three months and 41% in six months. Superforecasters did much better: 15% for three months, 24% for six — good enough to surprise Kahneman. Tetlock believes that through deliberate practice, some System 2 corrections have become habitual — effectively part of System 1, like a golfer whose once-awkward stance becomes automatic.

Nassim Taleb’s black swan critique poses the deepest challenge. If history makes jumps that cannot be forecast, and only those jumps matter, then Tetlock’s work is a fool’s errand. Tetlock and Taleb co-authored a joint paper finding substantial agreement: no one can forecast geopolitical events ten years out, and historical probabilities may be fat-tailed rather than normally distributed, making extreme events far more likely than we intuit. But Tetlock counters that history also crawls. Slow, incremental change can be profoundly important. Black swan investing (spread bets widely, expect most to fail, hope for one Google-sized payoff) and edge investing (forecast slightly more accurately than competitors, profit consistently but modestly) are different strategies, neither superior, both valid for different situations.

The deeper question is whether the kinds of events that superforecasters are good at predicting actually matter. Taleb would say they are trivial compared with the fat-tail catastrophes that truly shape history. Tetlock counters with the observation that history crawls as well as jumps. The consequences of the bin Laden raid, for instance, cascaded through a chain of events that each involved forecastable sub-questions. After 9/11, the Taliban could have handed over bin Laden rather than risk American invasion. Bin Laden could have fled to Pakistan or Yemen. The war in Afghanistan was not a single black swan event but a sequence of decisions, each influenced by assessments of probability. We may have no evidence that superforecasters can predict events like 9/11 itself, but we have a warehouse of evidence that they can forecast the questions that determine how such events play out.

Tetlock addresses the ten-year forecasting horizon with characteristic directness. No one can forecast geopolitical events a decade out — and yet the Department of Defense is required by Congress to produce a twenty-year forecast every four years. Richard Danzig, a former Navy secretary, noted the enormous effort that goes into this ritualistic exercise. Linton Wells, the contrarian who wrote the Rumsfeld memo, suggested a better approach: if you must plan beyond the forecasting horizon, plan for surprise. Build adaptable, resilient systems. “Plans are useless,” Eisenhower said, “but planning is indispensable.”

Taleb’s vision of the world posits that historical probabilities are distributed like wealth, not height. If a British official in 1914 had assumed war casualties were normally distributed, he would have seen the combination of World War I and World War II as a one-in-several-million event — and dismissed any forecaster who predicted it as a crackpot. With a more realistic fat-tailed distribution, the same event becomes thousands of times more probable. Understanding fat tails does not make specific catastrophes predictable, but it does mean we should take extreme scenarios far more seriously than our intuitions suggest. This is where Taleb, Kahneman, and Tetlock converge: all three see the past as one quasi-random draw from a vast population of once-possible worlds.

Kahneman and Tetlock conducted an experiment that surprised even Kahneman. They tested scope sensitivity by asking one group of superforecasters about the probability of Assad’s regime falling in three months and another group about six months. Regular forecasters showed almost no sensitivity: forty percent for three months, forty-one percent for six. But superforecasters gave fifteen percent for three months and twenty-four percent for six — a meaningful distinction that Kahneman had predicted would not appear. Tetlock believes that through sustained deliberate practice, some System 2 corrections have become automatic for superforecasters — effectively part of their System 1, like a golfer whose once-awkward stance has become effortless.

The chapter closes with Tetlock’s honest assessment. Superforecasters are always one System 2 slipup away from a blown forecast. The cognitive illusions that plague human judgment cannot be switched off, any more than you can unsee the Müller-Lyer illusion. But they can be monitored. The superforecaster’s advantage is not immunity to bias but a habit of checking for it — a mental ruler kept close at hand. The question is not whether superforecasters will occasionally stumble. They will. The question is whether the aggregate improvement they achieve, sustained over time, is worth the effort. The tournament data say unequivocally: yes.

Chapter 12: What’s Next?

After Scotland’s 2014 independence referendum (superforecasters aced this one, even beating British betting markets), political scientist Daniel Drezner asked: “What does one do with data points like this to adjust one’s worldview?” That question, Tetlock argues, is the engine of progress. The hindsight-tainted analyses that dominate commentary are a dead end. Effective learning requires unambiguous, scorable forecasts. Tournaments help researchers learn what works, help forecasters sharpen their skills, and could help society by structuring thinking about which policies are likely to succeed.

The obstacles are political. Lenin’s “kto-kogo” (“who, whom”) principle holds that the ultimate question in politics is who dominates whom. By that logic, forecasting serves power, not truth. Dick Morris defended his wildly wrong prediction of a Romney landslide by saying he felt it was his “duty” to boost Republican morale. When IARPA funded the tournament, it put the intelligence community’s mission ahead of insiders who did not want their judgment publicly tested. The precedent of Ernest Codman, the early twentieth-century surgeon who demanded hospitals track patient outcomes and was booted from Massachusetts General for his trouble, offers hope: evidence-based medicine eventually prevailed, and evidence-based forecasting may follow the same path.

Tetlock proposes adversarial collaboration as a model for depolarizing debates. When Keynesians and Austerians clashed after 2008, no meaningful learning occurred. Opponents should design precise, testable forecasts that settle their disagreements — the way Kahneman and Klein reconciled their apparently contradictory schools of thought by sitting down together. The final insight is that superforecasters and superquestioners are probably different types. Hedgehog-like incisiveness generates great questions; foxy eclecticism generates great answers. The “Tom Friedman versus Bill Flack” frame is the book’s final false dichotomy. What we need is symbiosis.

The Bloomberg reporters did Tetlock a favor in 2014 when they contacted the signatories of the 2010 open letter to Bernanke. Those who responded unanimously said they were right — they would not change a word. Some said the forecast only appeared to fail because consequences were delayed. Others pivoted to arguing they had been right about other things. Not one adjusted their thinking. When the president of the Federal Reserve Bank of Minneapolis, an Austerian, publicly acknowledged that events had vindicated the Keynesians, it made headlines precisely because it was so rare — headlines that could have read “Man Changes His Mind in Response to Evidence.”

The solution is Bayesian question clustering. Big unscorable questions (“How does the Korean peninsula turn out?”) can be decomposed into many small, scorable ones: Will North Korea test a rocket? Conduct a nuclear test? Fire on a South Korean ship? Each answer is a tiny dot on a pointillist canvas. Individually, each adds little. But as the dots accumulate, patterns emerge and the big picture comes into focus. Tetlock hopes to develop the concept further in future research.

Ernest Codman, the early twentieth-century Boston surgeon, proposed that hospitals record ailments, treatments, and outcomes. The medical establishment hated it. Codman was booted from Massachusetts General Hospital. He opened his own small hospital and continued publicizing his ideas with increasingly intemperate means. The parallel to forecasting is exact: evidence-based medicine eventually prevailed because the case for measurement was overwhelming. Evidence-based forecasting faces the same resistance from the same kind of entrenched interests, and Tetlock believes it will follow the same arc.

Tetlock acknowledges what numbers cannot capture. “Not everything that counts can be counted, and not everything that can be counted counts.” Brier scores are a work in progress, not a perfect instrument. But the alternative is judging forecasters by titles, confidence, storytelling skill, and CNN appearances. Superforecasters and superquestioners should work together: the hedgehog’s provocative questions sharpened by the fox’s calibrated answers, in an adversarial collaboration that makes everyone smarter.

Bill Flack uses pundits like “lawyers in an adversarial judicial system: they put forth the best argument they can for why X is going to happen, and I consider everybody’s arguments, dig further into background as necessary, and come up with my own forecast as a weighted sum of theirs.” The big strategic thinkers will probably never step to the plate and let us compare their batting averages with superforecasters. But they can pitch great questions for others to hit. All we have to do is get serious about keeping score.

All we have to do is get serious about keeping score. — Philip Tetlock

Trading & Investing Lessons

Base Rates as Market Anchors

Superforecasters start with the outside view before touching specifics. Investors should do the same: before analyzing a particular stock, check base rates — how often do companies in this sector grow at this rate? How often do turnarounds succeed? Starting with a meaningful anchor prevents the inside view from hijacking the analysis.

Granularity Separates Winners from Losers

The difference between forecasting 60% and 65% seems trivial but, compounded over many decisions, it is the difference between a consistent winner and the person going broke. Aaron Brown of AQR Capital: “The heavyweights know the difference between a 60/40 bet and a 40/60 bet.” This is the essence of edge in trading.

Regression to the Mean Is Your Best Friend

Hot fund managers regress. Cold strategies revert. Mauboussin’s test: if performance is dominated by luck, expect fast regression; if by skill, slow regression. Before crowning or condemning any investment strategy, check whether the results show the slow regression characteristic of skill.

Updating as Position Management

Tim Minto’s thirty-four small updates on the Syrian refugees question mirror good position management: don’t stubbornly hold a thesis unchanged (underreaction) or panic-sell on noise (overreaction). Make many small adjustments as evidence evolves.

The Dilution Effect and Information Overload

More data does not always improve decisions. Adding irrelevant information weakens the signal from relevant information. In trading, the deluge of news, indicators, and opinions can dilute the few factors that actually matter for a specific position.

Hedgehog Risk: Narrative Over Numbers

Larry Kudlow’s green-tinted glasses are a warning for every investor who falls in love with a thesis. The EPJ finding that hedgehogs get WORSE as they gain expertise in their specialty maps directly onto the concentrated-bet investor who refuses to cut a losing position because “the thesis hasn’t changed.”

Key Takeaways

Forecasting Is a Learnable Skill

Not a mysterious gift. The superforecasters prove that intelligent, determined people can cultivate the habits that produce accurate predictions. A sixty-minute training tutorial improved accuracy by ten percent for an entire year.

How You Think Matters More Than What You Think

Ideology, credentials, and access to classified data matter far less than the style of thinking. Foxes beat hedgehogs. Open-minded updaters beat confident declarers.

Perpetual Beta Is the Strongest Predictor

Commitment to continuous self-improvement is three times as powerful a predictor of superforecaster status as intelligence. The growth mindset is not optional.

Start Outside, Then Go Inside

Base rates first, specifics second. Anchoring on the outside view prevents the inside view from producing overconfident narratives.

Update Often, Update Small

Many small adjustments beat occasional large swings. The ideal is a careful Bayesian who knows when to make a large jump.

Beliefs Are Hypotheses, Not Treasures

The single best bumper sticker for superforecasting. Hold beliefs tentatively, test them relentlessly, and change them without ego.

Contrarian Ideas

Experts Are No Better Than Chimps — But Some Are

The famous punch line masks the finding that a minority of experts had real foresight. Dismissing all expertise is as wrong as trusting it blindly.

Classified Data Is Overrated

Superforecasters with no security clearance beat intelligence analysts with access to classified intercepts. The quality of thinking matters more than the quality of information.

Confidence Signals Incompetence

The more famous an expert, the less accurate their forecasts. Media selects for hedgehogs who tell simple, confident stories — the very style of thinking that produces the worst predictions.

Probability Undermines Meaning

Embracing probabilistic thinking is negatively correlated with finding meaning in life events. The psychological comfort of “everything happens for a reason” is the enemy of accurate forecasting.

Disagreement Is Better Than Consensus

The fictional Leon Panetta was wrong to want agreement. A smart executive should welcome diverse probability estimates and aggregate them. Unanimity is a warning flag for groupthink.

Proposals & Frameworks

The Superforecasting Method

(1) Triage — focus on Goldilocks-zone questions. (2) Fermi-ize — break into sub-problems. (3) Outside view first — start with base rates. (4) Inside view second — targeted investigation. (5) Dragonfly-eye synthesis — aggregate perspectives. (6) Express with granular probability. (7) Update often, update small. (8) Postmortem relentlessly.

Ten Commandments for Aspiring Superforecasters

1. Triage. 2. Fermi-ize. 3. Balance inside and outside views. 4. Balance under- and overreacting to evidence. 5. Look for clashing causal forces. 6. Distinguish as many degrees of doubt as the problem permits. 7. Balance under- and overconfidence. 8. Look for errors behind mistakes but beware hindsight bias. 9. Bring out the best in others. 10. Master the error-balancing bicycle. 11. Don’t treat commandments as commandments.

Brier Score Framework

0 = omniscience. 0.5 = random guessing. 2.0 = maximally wrong. Good forecasting = calibration (your 70% forecasts come true 70% of the time) + resolution (you assign extreme probabilities and are right). Compare against benchmarks: can you beat “always predict no change”?

Bayesian Question Clustering

Break unscorable big questions (“How does Korea turn out?”) into many scorable small questions (Will NK test a rocket? Conduct a nuclear test? Fire on a South Korean ship?). Like pointillism: each dot adds little, but patterns emerge.

What Didn’t Fit

The Gazzaniga Split-Brain Shovel

Michael Gazzaniga’s split-brain experiment deserves a deeper look. The patient’s left hemisphere saw only a chicken claw, while his right hemisphere saw a snowstorm. When asked why his hand was pointing at a shovel, the left hemisphere — which had no access to the snowstorm image — instantly confabulated: “The chicken claw goes with the chicken, and you need a shovel to clean out the chicken shed.” This is not a pathological quirk of brain-damaged patients. We all do this constantly. Journalists explain stock market moves with stories that feel inevitable in hindsight, even when the supposed cause came after the market had already moved.

The Keynes Quotation That Isn’t

“When the facts change, I change my mind. What do you do, sir?” is attributed to Keynes in countless books, including one written by Tetlock himself. But when researching this book, Tetlock tried to trace it to its source and failed. The two leading Keynes experts believe it is apocryphal. In the spirit of what Keynes apparently never said, Tetlock confessed his error. The lesson: even belief blocks at the apex of your Jenga tower can be removed painlessly when ego is not at stake.

Ellen Langer’s Illusion of Control

Psychologist Ellen Langer had Yale students watch rigged coin flips where all students got fifteen right and fifteen wrong, but some got early streaks of hits while others got early misses. Students who started with hits believed they had genuine skill at predicting coin tosses. These were students at an elite university being tested on the very symbol of randomness, yet the first pattern they encountered convinced them they could predict the inherently unpredictable. Business news does the same thing every time it introduces a pundit with “Pedro Ziff called the crash of 2008!”

Robert Shiller’s Grandfather Parable

Nobel laureate Robert Shiller tells the story of how Henry Ford’s decision to pay workers five dollars a day brought both of Shiller’s grandfathers to Detroit. If someone had offered one grandfather a better job, if the other had been kicked in the head by a horse, if Ford had been convinced his wage was crazy — Shiller would never have been born. “You tend to believe that history played out in a logical sort of sense, that people ought to have foreseen it, but it’s not like that. It’s an illusion of hindsight.”

The Rumsfeld Memo

On April 11, 2001, Defense Secretary Donald Rumsfeld sent a memo to Bush and Cheney about the difficulty of predicting the future. The attached article by Linton Wells showed that at the start of every decade from 1900 to 2000, reality was a stunning change from ten years earlier. Precisely five months later, jets hit the Twin Towers and the Pentagon. The decade that followed bore no resemblance to what anyone expected at its launch.

Superforecasting: The Art and Science of Prediction
Philip E. Tetlock & Dan Gardner · 2015 · 302 pages
Crown Publishers, New York
A rigorous, story-driven exploration of what makes some people remarkably good at predicting the future — and how the rest of us can learn their methods.

About the Authors

Philip E. Tetlock is the Annenberg University Professor at the University of Pennsylvania, with appointments in the Wharton School and the Psychology Department. He is one of the most cited political psychologists in the world, known for his two-decade study of expert prediction and the creation of the Good Judgment Project. His earlier book, Expert Political Judgment: How Good Is It? How Can We Know?, established the dart-throwing-chimpanzee benchmark that reshaped how people think about forecasting. He launched the GJP with his research and life partner, Barbara Mellers, in the wake of personal tragedy — the loss of their daughter Jenny, to whom the book is dedicated.

Dan Gardner is a Canadian journalist and author of Risk and Future Babble, both of which explore the psychology of prediction and risk perception. He helped Tetlock translate decades of academic research into the narrative-driven style that makes Superforecasting accessible to general readers.

Why This Book Matters

Superforecasting matters because it demonstrates that the quality of our collective foresight is not fixed by human nature. In a world where governments spend fifty billion dollars on intelligence analysis, corporations pay fortunes for consultant forecasts, and citizens base life-altering decisions on pundits who have never been tested, the finding that a few hundred ordinary volunteers can beat the professionals is revolutionary. It is the forecasting equivalent of the Moneyball revolution in baseball — the moment when measurement replaced intuition, and the results changed everything.

The book also matters as a manual for thinking. The superforecasting method — Fermi-ize, outside view first, dragonfly-eye synthesis, granular probability, many small updates, rigorous postmortems — is not limited to geopolitical questions. It applies to any domain where judgment under uncertainty matters: investing, medicine, business strategy, personal decisions. The core insight — that beliefs are hypotheses to be tested, not treasures to be guarded — is a philosophy of mind that, if widely adopted, could make the world measurably saner.
