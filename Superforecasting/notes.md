# Superforecasting: The Art and Science of Prediction
## Philip E. Tetlock & Dan Gardner (2015, 302 pages)

## Chapter 1: An Optimistic Skeptic

### Bill Flack vs. Tom Friedman
- Opens with contrast: Tom Friedman (celebrity forecaster, NYT columnist, Davos regular) vs. Bill Flack (retired USDA worker from Kearney, Nebraska, bird-watcher, former math PhD dropout from U of Arizona)
- Bill Flack has answered ~300 forecasting questions on geopolitics: Russia/Ukraine, Ebola, NATO expansion, Kurdistan independence, Chinese internet access
- His track record is excellent — each prediction dated, recorded, assessed by independent scientific observers
- ~2% of volunteer forecasters are as good as Bill — Tetlock calls them "superforecasters"
- No one bases decisions on Bill's forecasts; no one invites him to CNN or Davos
- Meanwhile Tom Friedman's accuracy has never been rigorously tested — "endless opinions on opinions"
- Baseball analogy: managers wouldn't hire without stats, fans expect scoreboards, yet for forecasters who help make far more important decisions, "we're content to be ignorant"
- Key claim: forecasting is not "you have it or you don't" — it's a cultivable skill

### The Dart-Throwing Chimp
- Tetlock's own 20-year study (1984-2004) was the most comprehensive assessment of expert judgment in scientific literature
- Famous punch line: average expert was roughly as accurate as a dart-throwing chimpanzee
- Results were "far richer and more constructive than the punch line suggested"
- The message mutated through media retelling (like telephone game): "all expert forecasts are useless" → "experts know no more than chimpanzees"
- Tetlock tired of the joke because it became "a backstop reference for nihilists who see the future as inherently unpredictable"
- Important nuance: "Many" does not equal "all" — it was easiest to beat chance on short-range (1-year) questions; accuracy fell off 3-5 years out, approaching chimp level
- Key finding: tells us about limits of expertise AND limits on what superforecasters can achieve

### The Skeptic: Mohamed Bouazizi and the Arab Spring
- Story of Tunisian street vendor Mohamed Bouazizi: police harassment, confiscated scales, self-immolation on Dec 17, 2010 (age 26)
- His death (Jan 4, 2011) sparked protests → Ben Ali fled Jan 14, ending 23-year kleptocracy
- Protests erupted across Egypt, Libya, Syria, Jordan, Kuwait, Bahrain → Mubarak ousted
- The Arab Spring started with one poor man's humiliation — "no different from countless others"
- Even Tom Friedman (NYT correspondent who knew the region well) could not have foreseen this specific cascade
- Could have said "powder kegs" about Tunisia/Egypt for decades — they never blew until that specific day

### Edward Lorenz and Chaos Theory
- 1972 paper: "Predictability: Does the Flap of a Butterfly's Wings in Brazil Set Off a Tornado in Texas?"
- Discovered in computer weather simulations that tiny data entry variations (0.506127 → 0.506) produced dramatically different long-term forecasts
- "Chaos theory": in nonlinear systems, small changes in initial conditions mushroom to enormous proportions
- Shifted scientific opinion toward hard limits on predictability

### Laplace's Demon vs. Lorenzian Cloud
- For centuries, scientists assumed growing knowledge → greater predictability (reality as "clock")
- Pierre-Simon Laplace (1814): imagined an intellect knowing all forces and positions — "nothing would be uncertain and the future just like the past would be present before its eyes"
- Lorenz shattered this: the "Lorenzian cloud" is the opposite of Laplacean clockwork
- Cloud formation depends on complex feedback interactions among droplets — even with perfect knowledge of how clouds form, can't predict a specific cloud's shape
- Scientists today know vastly more and have vastly more data than a century ago, but are less confident in perfect predictability

### Clocks vs. Clouds: The False Dichotomy
- Key insight: "Is the future predictable?" is a bad question — it's a false dichotomy
- We live in a world of "clocks and clouds and a vast jumble of other metaphors"
- How predictable something is depends on: what we're predicting, how far ahead, under what circumstances
- Example: stock market bull run seems predictable — until it suddenly isn't (like dinosaurs dominated for millions of years until an asteroid)
- Time-predictability relationship: generally harder to see further ahead, but prolonged exceptions exist
- Weather: 1-2 day forecasts quite accurate, 8-day not; beyond a week ≈ dart-throwing chimp
- Insurance company forecasting: Kansas City Life Insurance (founded 1895) forecasts disability and death actuarially — works well at population level

### The Optimist: Everyday Forecasting
- Woman in Kansas City suburb: her entire day is built on accurate forecasts (rush hour traffic, restaurant hours, others' behavior at red lights)
- Electricity producers anticipate demand surges; Amazon predicts what you want to buy; Google personalizes search
- Sunrise/sunset times are clocklike predictions accurate for 50+ years
- But all predictability can be punctured: restaurant might not open (fire, pandemic, black hole swallowing solar system)
- Even death and taxes aren't certain if we assign nonzero probability to brain uploading or charitable utopia

### The Need for Measurement
- Bill Gates quote: "You can achieve incredible progress if you set a clear goal and find a measure that will drive progress toward that goal"
- In forecasting, even the simple first step — setting a clear goal — hasn't been taken
- No measurement → no revision → no improvement
- Running analogy: imagine no agreed ground rules, no timekeepers, no measurement — would running times improve? Probably not
- Mostly a demand-side problem: consumers of forecasting don't demand accuracy
- Forecasts serve many purposes besides accuracy: entertainment (Jim Cramer's "booyah!"), political advocacy, dress-to-impress (banks telling wealthy clients about 2050), comfort (assuring partisans)
- John McLaughlin on The McLaughlin Group: bellows at panelists to predict likelihood "on a scale from zero to ten, with zero representing zero possibility and ten representing complete metaphysical certitude!"
- Partisans are fond of comforting forecasts — "the cognitive equivalent of slipping into a warm bath"

### The Good Judgment Project (GJP)
- Phase two of Tetlock's career: launched summer 2011 with partner Barbara Mellers (research and life partner)
- Invited volunteers to sign up and forecast the future
- Bill Flack responded, plus thousands of others — cumulatively 20,000+ intellectually curious laypeople over four years
- Questions: Will protests in Russia spread? Will gold plummet? Will the Nikkei close above 9,500? War on Korean peninsula?
- Multidisciplinary team based at UC Berkeley and U of Pennsylvania
- Part of larger IARPA (Intelligence Advanced Research Projects Activity) tournament
- IARPA reports to Director of National Intelligence; US has ~20,000 intelligence analysts
- Five scientific teams competed, each free to improvise methods
- Required daily forecasts at 9 AM EST, Sept 2011 to June 2015
- Nearly 500 questions about world affairs over 4 years
- Time frames shorter than earlier research: most 1 month to 1 year
- Gathered over 1 million individual judgments about the future

### GJP Results
- Year 1: beat official control group by 60%
- Year 2: beat control by 78%, beat university competitors (Michigan, MIT) by 30-70%
- Even outperformed professional intelligence analysts with access to classified data
- After 2 years, IARPA dropped the other teams — GJP was so dominant
- A 60-minute tutorial improved forecaster accuracy by ~10% for the entire tournament year
- Aaron Brown (AQR Capital, $100B hedge fund): "It's so hard to see because it's not dramatic, but if it is sustained, it's the difference between a consistent winner who's making a living, or the guy who's going broke all the time"
- World-class poker player: "the heavyweights know the difference between a 60/40 bet and a 40/60 bet"

### What Makes Superforecasters Good
- Two key conclusions: (1) foresight is real — some people have measurable skill at 3-month to 18-month predictions; (2) it's not who they are, it's what they do
- Foresight is "the product of particular ways of thinking, of gathering information, of updating beliefs"
- These habits "can be learned and cultivated by any intelligent, thoughtful, determined person"
- Commitment to self-improvement = strongest predictor of performance
- It's not intelligence alone (requires minimum), not numeracy alone (superforecasters rarely use much math), not being a news junkie (though they tend to be)
- What matters most is HOW the forecaster thinks: open-minded, careful, curious, self-critical
- Within a wide range of views, WHAT they thought didn't matter — it was HOW they thought

### Humans vs. Machines in Forecasting
- Paul Meehl (1954): 20 studies showed simple algorithms beat well-informed experts; now 200+ studies confirm this
- When you have a well-validated statistical algorithm, use it
- But we rarely have such algorithms for the problems at hand
- Deep Blue beat Kasparov (1997); Watson beat Jeopardy! champions (2011)
- David Ferrucci (Watson's chief engineer): the sort of forecasting superforecasters do "is a long way off" for machines
- Difference between "Which two Russian leaders traded jobs?" (lookup) vs. "Will two top Russian leaders trade jobs in the next ten years?" (judgment requiring understanding of Putin, Medvedev, Russian politics)
- Herbert Simon (1965): predicted machines would do "any work a man can do" within 20 years — naively optimistic
- Ferrucci: "There's a difference between mimicking and reflecting meaning and originating meaning"
- "Freestyle chess" model: humans + computers competing as teams, drawing on computer's strengths but human occasionally overriding — can beat both pure-human and pure-machine
- The "guru model" of punditry (Paul Krugman vs. Niall Ferguson, Tom Friedman vs. Bret Stephens) is what Ferrucci sees becoming obsolete
- Ferrucci: "So what I want is that human expert paired with a computer to overcome the human cognitive limitations and biases"
- Tetlock's forecast: we will need to blend computer-based forecasting and subjective judgment

## Chapter 2: Illusions of Knowledge

### Archie Cochrane's Misdiagnosis
- 1956: dermatologist found spots on Archie Cochrane's hand → basal cell carcinoma (minor)
- Big-name specialist found lump in right armpit (axilla), removed it → told Cochrane "your axilla is full of cancerous tissue," removed pectoralis minor muscle
- Cochrane accepted death sentence, made peace, "a curious feeling of peace came over me"
- But the specialist was WRONG — pathologist found no cancer; Cochrane didn't actually have terminal cancer
- Key: Cochrane didn't doubt the specialist, the specialist didn't doubt his own judgment, neither waited for pathologist's report
- Cochrane went on to become a revered figure in medicine (evidence-based medicine pioneer)
- Lesson: we are "too quick to make up our minds and too slow to change them"

### Blind Men Arguing: History of Medicine
- Druin Burch: "most histories of medicine are strikingly odd — they provide a clear account of what people believed they were doing, but almost none of all of whether they were right"
- Ancient Egyptian ostrich egg poultices, Mesopotamian "Keeper of the Royal Rectum," bloodletting from ancient Greeks through George Washington
- Washington's death (1799): physicians bled him relentlessly, dosed mercury, induced vomiting, raised blood-filled blisters — a physician in Aristotle's Athens would have nodded at the regimen
- Treatments seldom got better; "until quite recently it was not unusual for a sick person to be better off if there was no physician available"
- Galen (2nd century): physician to Roman emperors, the medical authority for 1000+ years — "It is I, and I alone, who has revealed the true path of medicine" — yet never conducted anything resembling a modern experiment
- Pattern in medical history: charismatic men of strong conviction, bold theories, denounce rivals, spread insights with evangelical passion (Galen → Paracelsus → Samuel Hahnemann → Benjamin Rush)
- 19th century American medicine: pitched battles between orthodox physicians and quacks (Thomsonianism, Edwin Hartley Pratt's orificial surgery — "the rectum contains the essence of life")
- Ira Rutkow: physicians debating treatments were "like blind men arguing over the colors of the rainbow"

### James Lind and the Slow Road to RCTs
- 1747: James Lind took 12 scurvy-afflicted sailors, divided into pairs, gave each pair different treatment (vinegar, cider, sulfuric acid, seawater, bark paste, citrus fruit) — citrus won
- But NOT a eureka moment: Lind "failed so completely to make sense of his own experiment that even he was left unconvinced of the exceptional benefits of lemons and limes"
- Not until 20th century did randomized controlled trials (RCTs) take hold
- Lancet asked in 1921 whether "the numerical method" was trivial or important; Austin Bradford Hill said it was the latter and laid out the template for modern medical investigation
- RCTs: randomly assign patients to groups → differences balance out → can confidently conclude treatment caused outcome
- Before RCTs, medicine was "cargo cult science" (Feynman's term): had the outward form of science but lacked what makes it truly scientific
- What medicine lacked was DOUBT — Feynman: "Doubt is not a fearful thing, but a thing of very great value"

### Archie Cochrane and the "God Complex"
- Cochrane (1950s-60s): complained NHS had "far too little interest in proving and promoting what was effective"
- Physicians had "the God complex" — they just knew, didn't need scientific validation
- Cochrane's cardiac care unit trial: proposed RCT comparing hospital cardiac units vs. home care with bed rest
- Cardiologists balked — "obvious" that cardiac units were better, denying patients access would be "unethical"
- Cochrane got his trial; partway through, showed cardiologists preliminary results suggesting cardiac units were slightly better
- They exploded: "Archie, we always thought you were unethical. You must stop the trial at once"
- Then Cochrane revealed he had REVERSED the results — home care was actually doing slightly better
- "There was dead silence and I felt rather sick because they were, after all, my medical colleagues"
- Cochrane also applied this to criminal justice: Thatcher's "short, sharp, shock" policy for young offenders — implemented everywhere, making it impossible to test whether it worked

### Cochrane's Personal Irony
- The man who championed not rushing to judgment himself rushed to judgment about his cancer diagnosis
- He accepted the specialist's word without waiting for the pathologist's report
- Two riddles: (1) why do people resist the need for evidence? (2) why did Cochrane himself fall for it?

### Thinking About Thinking: System 1 and System 2
- We identify our thinking with conscious thought, but introspection only captures a fraction of mental processes
- Dual-system model (Kahneman): System 1 (fast, automatic, unconscious) vs. System 2 (slow, deliberate, conscious)
- System 1 comes first, constantly running in background; System 2 interrogates System 1's answers
- Bat-and-ball problem: bat + ball = $1.10, bat costs $1 more than ball, what does ball cost? System 1 says "ten cents" — wrong (correct: 5 cents)
- Cognitive Reflection Test: shows most people — including very smart people — aren't very reflective
- System 1's psycho-logic: "if it feels true, it is"
- Evolutionary justification: hunter-gatherer consulting lion statistics would die; snap judgments were adaptive
- Availability heuristic: if you easily recall lion attacks, you conclude they're common → fear → run from shadow in grass

### WYSIATI and Confabulation
- WYSIATI = "What You See Is All There Is" (Kahneman) — System 1 treats available evidence as reliable and sufficient, never wonders if evidence is flawed or incomplete
- System 1 demands order/coherence — we are "creative confabulators hardwired to invent stories that impose coherence on the world"
- Michael Gazzaniga's split-brain experiments: left hemisphere shown snowstorm image, right shown chicken claw; patient points at shovel (matches snowstorm) but left hemisphere (which only saw chicken claw) confabulates: "The chicken claw goes with the chicken, and you need a shovel to clean out the chicken shed"
- Same confabulation happens daily: journalists explain stock market moves with post-hoc stories; "The Dow rose ninety-five points today on news that..." — but a quick check often reveals the "news" came after the market rose

### The Oslo Bombing (2011) — Case Study in Rushing to Judgment
- Massive car bomb killed 8, injured 200+ in Oslo, Norway
- Immediate speculation: Islamist terrorists (just like London, Madrid, Bali, 9/11)
- Supporting evidence quickly found: Norway in Afghanistan, poorly integrated Muslim community, radical preacher charged recently
- Then: mass shooting at Labour Party summer camp — dozens dead
- Everyone was certain: coordinated Islamist attacks
- Actually: lone perpetrator Anders Breivik, not Muslim, a right-wing extremist attacking the government over multiculturalist policies
- After Breivik's arrest, those who rushed to judgment were accused of Islamophobia
- But as a "plausible hypothesis," suspecting Islamists was reasonable given the evidence — the problem was treating a plausible hypothesis as The Truth
- Scientists handle this differently: they hold hypotheses tentatively, consider alternatives, ask "What would convince me I am wrong?"
- Confirmation bias: natural inclination to grab first plausible explanation and gather supportive evidence without checking reliability
- When disconfirming evidence is shoved under our noses, we become "motivated skeptics" — finding reasons to belittle or throw it out
- Galen's pure confirmation bias: "If the patient is cured, it is evidence my treatment works; if the patient dies, it means nothing"
- Kahneman: "declarations of high confidence mainly tell you that an individual has constructed a coherent story in his mind, not necessarily that the story is true"

### Bait and Switch (Attribute Substitution)
- When faced with a hard question, we unconsciously replace it with an easy one
- "Should I worry about the shadow in the long grass?" → substituted by "Can I easily recall a lion attacking from long grass?"
- Cochrane's bait and switch: "Do I have cancer?" was hard → substituted with "Is this the sort of person who should know if I have cancer?" → "Of course! He's an eminent cancer specialist"
- Even Archie Cochrane — the champion of evidence-based skepticism — fell for it
- We can't simply "flip the switch" to turn off System 1; it keeps humming beneath consciousness
- Better metaphor than "default setting": it's like vision — System 1 is the "tip-of-your-nose perspective," always on

### Blinking and Thinking: Klein vs. Kahneman
- Popular books frame it as "blink" vs. "think" — a false dichotomy
- Gary Klein studied firefighting commanders and found snap judgments can work astonishingly well
- Fire commander story: routine kitchen fire, ordered men into living room to hose it down; fire subsided but roared back; room was surprisingly hot for a kitchen fire and too quiet; vague feeling of unease → ordered everyone out; floor collapsed moments later (fire was actually in basement)
- Commander thought he had ESP — but it was pattern recognition: 50,000-100,000 chess positions encoded in expert memory
- Kahneman and Klein collaborated despite seemingly opposing views: "We agree on most of the issues that matter" (2009 joint paper)
- When intuition works: valid cues in the environment that experience teaches you to unconsciously register (fire commanders, nurses spotting infant infections)
- When intuition fails: domains without reliable cues (stock picking — if valid public info existed, the price would already reflect it)
- Key: it's hard to know when there are enough valid cues to make intuition work
- Magnus Carlsen: "I usually know what I am going to do after 10 seconds; the rest is double-checking" — respects intuition but also double-checks because "sometimes intuition can let him down"

### Tip-of-Your-Nose Perspective: The Lesson
- Peggy Noonan predicted Romney victory day before 2012 election based on "the vibrations" from big rallies — "the vibrations are right"
- The tip-of-your-nose perspective is so persuasive that for thousands of years physicians didn't doubt their convictions
- 21st-century forecasting looks too much like 19th-century medicine: theories, assertions, famous confident figures, little experimentation
- Bad forecasting steers toward bad decisions → monetary losses, missed opportunities, unnecessary suffering, even war and death
- Cure: "a tablespoon of doubt"

## Chapter 3: Keeping Score

### The Problem of Vague Forecasts
- Steve Ballmer (2007): "There's no chance that the iPhone is going to get any significant market share. No chance."
- Seems spectacularly wrong — but parse it carefully: what market? (global mobile phones, not smartphones); what's "significant"?
- Full quote from USA Today interview: Ballmer was talking about global mobile phone market (1.3 billion phones), predicting iPhone would get "2% or 3%" — actual iPhone share of global mobile phone sales in Q3 2013 was ~6%
- Not "laugh-out-loud wrong" when you read the full context; his WORDS were more nuanced than his TONE
- Open letter to Ben Bernanke (Nov 2010): prominent economists (Niall Ferguson, Amity Shlaes) warned QE would cause "currency debasement and inflation" — dollar didn't debase, inflation didn't rise
- Barry Ritholtz (2013): signatories "terribly wrong" — but they could always say "Just wait. It hasn't happened yet. But it will"
- No time frame specified, "risk" not "certainty" — the letter is technically unfalsifiable
- These two examples show why judging forecasts is much harder than it seems: no clear terms, no time frames, vague language

### "A Holocaust...Will Occur" — Origins of Tetlock's Research
- Early 1980s: nuclear war fears; Jonathan Schell's The Fate of the Earth: "a holocaust not only might occur but will occur"
- June 1982: 700,000 marched in NYC against nuclear weapons
- 1984: Carnegie and MacArthur foundations convened panel to prevent nuclear war; included Nobel laureates Charles Townes, Kenneth Arrow, Herbert Simon, and Amos Tversky
- Tetlock was "by far the least impressive member" — 30-year-old political psychologist, associate prof at UC Berkeley
- Panel invited intelligence analysts, military officers, Sovietologists — confident, articulate, and certain about what was happening
- Basic facts: Brezhnev died 1982, replaced by Chernenko (also expected to die soon)
- Liberals and conservatives both predicted next Soviet leader would be another stern Communist Party man
- Liberals: Reagan's hard line was strengthening Kremlin hard-liners → neo-Stalinist retrenchment
- Conservatives: Soviet system had perfected totalitarian self-reproduction → new boss same as old boss
- Both sides equally confident, equally WRONG
- Chernenko died March 1985 → Mikhail Gorbachev (54, energetic, charismatic) became general secretary
- Glasnost (openness) + perestroika (restructuring) → liberalized Soviet Union
- Gorbachev normalized relations with US, reversed arms race → world went from nuclear war fears to "glimmering chance of eliminating nuclear weapons altogether"
- Few experts saw this coming — but after the fact, those who didn't see it quickly explained why they "saw it coming all along"
- Liberals: Soviet economy crumbling, new cohort weary of struggle; "We can't go on living this way" (Gorbachev to wife Raisa) → it was "bound to happen"
- Conservatives: Reagan called the bluff, upped the ante → Gorbachev was folding; all predictable "in the right retrospective light"
- Tetlock's inner cynic: "no matter what had happened, experts would have been just as adept at downplaying their predictive failures"
- The national security elites looked like "the renowned physicians from the prescientific era" — overflowing with intelligence and integrity, but "tip-of-your-nose delusions can fool anyone, even the best and brightest — perhaps especially the best and the brightest"

### Judging Judgments: Tetlock's 20-Year Study
- 1988 lunch with Daniel Kahneman at Berkeley: Kahneman speculated intelligence improves forecasting but benefits taper fast — people with PhDs and decades of experience may be "only a tad more accurate than attentive readers of the New York Times"
- Kahneman was just guessing — no one had ever seriously tested expert political forecasting accuracy
- Problems with judging forecasts: (1) no time frames make them untestable; (2) vague terms ("significant," "serious possibility") rather than explicit definitions; (3) probability is the fundamental challenge
- Jonathan Schell said nuclear holocaust "will occur" — clearly wrong read literally; but what if he'd said "very likely"? Then even running history hundreds of times might not settle it
- Can't rerun history → can't judge single probabilistic forecasts

### Sherman Kent and the "Words of Estimative Probability"
- Sherman Kent: legendary CIA analyst, Yale PhD in history, joined OSS/CIA 1941, shaped how intelligence community does analysis
- "Estimating is what you do when you do not know" — forecasting is about estimating likelihoods
- Office of National Estimates: drew on all CIA intelligence to forecast for top US officials
- 1962 estimate famously said Soviets "would not" deploy offensive missiles to Cuba — they did
- March 1951 NIE 29-51: "attack on Yugoslavia in 1951 should be considered a serious possibility"
- Kent thought this meant 65-to-35 odds of attack; a State Department official thought it meant much lower odds
- Kent polled his own team on what "serious possibility" meant: answers ranged from 80-to-20 FOR invasion to 20-to-80 AGAINST — "exactly the opposite"
- 1964 essay: "Were the NIEs dotted with 'serious possibilities' and other expressions that meant very different things to both producers and readers?"
- Bay of Pigs (1961): Joint Chiefs told JFK the plan had "a fair chance" of success; the writer later said he meant 3-to-1 AGAINST; Kennedy took it as far more positive → authorized the disaster

### Kent's Probability Chart
- Kent proposed mapping words to numbers:
  - 100% = Certain
  - 93% (±6%) = Almost certain
  - 75% (±12%) = Probable
  - 50% (±10%) = Chances about even
  - 30% (±10%) = Probably not
  - 7% (±5%) = Almost certainly not
  - 0% = Impossible
- Never adopted in Kent's lifetime — people said it felt "unnatural," "tacky" ("I'd rather be a bookie than a goddamn poet" — Kent)
- Objections: numbers imply false objectivity, precision implies exactitude
- Kent's answer: words suggest the same thing as numbers, but with more confusion; forcing translation into numbers promotes metacognition
- Only after Iraq WMD debacle did intelligence community finally adopt numerical probabilities
- CIA analysts told Obama the man in the Pakistani compound was "70%" or "90%" Osama bin Laden — a posthumous triumph for Kent

### The Wrong-Side-of-Maybe Fallacy
- Can't judge a single probabilistic forecast — but CAN judge many: if 70% forecasts happen 70% of the time, forecaster is well calibrated
- People naturally judge forecasts by "which side of maybe" (50%): if you say 70% chance of rain and it rains, you're "right"; if it doesn't, you're "wrong" — even though 30% of the time it shouldn't rain
- David Leonhardt (NYT, 2012): prediction markets gave 75% chance Obamacare would be struck down; when it was upheld, he declared "the wisdom of the crowds was wrong" — but 25% events happen all the time
- This creates perverse incentives: forecasters use "rubbery words" (fair chance, serious possibility) because they can retroactively stretch them to claim success either way
- Intelligence agencies use vague language because specific probabilities risk being pilloried (65% = "wrong" 35% of the time)
- Niall Ferguson (Jan 2012): "I think the debt crisis in Europe is unresolved and may be very close to going critical... The Greek default may be a matter of days away" — "may" is a hollow word that adds no information

### Calibration
- Can't rerun history, but we CAN judge whether a forecaster is calibrated over many forecasts
- Perfect calibration: when forecaster says 70%, events happen 70% of the time; 30% → 30%; etc.
- Plotted as a chart: perfect calibration = diagonal line; above the line = underconfident; below = overconfident

### Calibration vs. Resolution
- Calibration alone is not enough — can be perfectly calibrated but useless
- "Ultra-cautious but well-calibrated": forecaster who only says 40-60% — all forecasts cluster near 50%, calibration is perfect but no real information conveyed
- Resolution: ability to assign high probabilities to things that happen and low to things that don't — being decisive AND right
- Good forecasting = calibration + resolution
- Scoring analogy: think of it as betting — saying 80% likely = offering 4-to-1 odds; saying 95% = offering 19-to-1; if wrong at 95%, you owe $1,900 on a $100 bet

### Brier Scores
- Developed by Glenn W. Brier (1950)
- Measures distance between forecast and what actually happened
- Lower = better (like golf): 0 = perfection (omniscience), 0.5 = random guessing (chimp), 2.0 = maximally wrong
- Brier score meaning depends on context — 0.2 in Phoenix weather (nearly always hot and sunny) is bad; 0.2 in Springfield, Missouri (variable weather) could be world-class
- Key benchmark: can the forecaster beat "always predict no change"?
- Nate Silver/Sam Wang predicting 2012 election: got all 50 states right, but a crude "no change from 2008" prediction would have scored 48/50 — the celebration was "a tad overwrought"
- Comparability requires level playing field: same questions, same time periods

### Expert Political Judgment (EPJ) — The 20-Year Study
- Mid-1980s: Tetlock recruited 284 serious professionals whose livelihoods involved analyzing political and economic trends
- Academics, think tankers, government officials, World Bank/IMF, media — had to guarantee anonymity
- Average age 43, average 12.2 years work experience, almost all had postgrad training, half had PhDs
- Questions covered 1-to-10-year time frames, diverse topics (domestic, international, political, economic)
- ~28,000 predictions total
- Study ran from mid-1980s (Gorbachev/Soviet Politburo era) to 2005 (USSR on historical maps, Gorbachev doing Pizza Hut commercials)
- Published as *Expert Political Judgment: How Good Is It? How Can We Know?* (EPJ)

### Foxes vs. Hedgehogs
- Two statistically distinguishable groups of experts
- Group 1 (hedgehogs): failed to beat random guessing; in longer-range forecasts, even LOST to the chimp
- Group 2 (foxes): beat the chimp, though not by a wide margin; barely beat simple algorithms like "predict no change"
- Critical factor was not WHAT they thought but HOW they thought
- Hedgehogs: organized thinking around Big Ideas, squeezed complex problems into preferred cause-effect templates, dismissed what didn't fit, allergic to wishy-washy answers, used "furthermore" and "moreover," declared things "impossible" or "certain," reluctant to change minds ("Just wait")
- Foxes: pragmatic, drew on many analytical tools, gathered info from many sources, shifted mental gears, sprinkled speech with "however," "but," "although," "on the other hand," talked probabilities not certainties, more readily admitted "I was wrong" and changed minds
- Isaiah Berlin's essay on Archilochus (2,500-year-old Greek poetry): "The fox knows many things but the hedgehog knows one big thing"
- Foxes beat hedgehogs on BOTH calibration AND resolution

### Larry Kudlow — Prototypical Hedgehog
- CNBC host, Reagan administration economist, worked with Art Laffer
- One Big Idea: supply-side economics
- Predicted "the Bush boom" after Bush tax cuts — growth was positive but "somewhat disappointing" vs. Clinton era
- Stuck to prediction year after year despite evidence; called it "the biggest story never told"
- December 2007 (months into financial crisis): "There is no recession. In fact, we are about to enter the seventh consecutive year of the Bush boom"
- Through 2008 as economy worsened: "There is no recession and there will be no recession"; "We are in a mental recession, not an actual recession" — kept repeating until Lehman Brothers collapsed (Sept 15, 2008)
- Big Idea = "green-tinted glasses" (like Wizard of Oz's Emerald City) — everything looks green; more information just increases confidence, not accuracy
- Hedgehogs' accuracy DECLINED on subjects they knew MOST about — their own specialties
- EPJ inverse correlation: the more famous an expert, the less accurate
- Media selects for hedgehogs because they tell tight, simple, confident stories; foxes say "however" and "on the other hand" — bad TV but good forecasting

### Dragonfly Eye: The Power of Aggregation
- Francis Galton (1906): country fair, hundreds guessed ox weight; collective average was 1,197 lbs vs. actual 1,198 lbs
- James Surowiecki's *The Wisdom of Crowds*: aggregating many judgments consistently beats the average individual
- Why it works: valid information from different people points in one direction; errors are random and cancel out
- Most effective when aggregating people who know different things → collective information pool is huge
- PollyVote: academic consortium aggregating polls, expert judgments, and quantitative models for presidential elections — strong track record since 1990s
- Foxes are like internal aggregators: they deploy many analytical ideas, seek info from many sources, synthesize into one conclusion — "in principle, no different from what Galton's crowd did"

### Financial Times Guess-the-Number Game
- Richard Thaler's 1997 contest: guess a number 0-100, closest to two-thirds of the average guess wins
- If everyone guesses randomly → average 50 → guess 33
- But others will think the same → average 33 → guess 22 → average 22 → guess 15... logic drives to 0 (Nash equilibrium)
- Actual winning answer was 13 (average was 18.91) — not 0, because not everyone follows logic all the way
- Lesson: you need the "Captain Kirk perspective" — synthesize the Vulcan (pure logic, guess 0), McCoy (human/lazy, guess 33), and the meta-perspective (how will real people think?)
- These fine distinctions between 10, 11, 12 matter — and they matter in superforecasting too

### Annie Duke and Poker Perspective-Taking
- Annie Duke (World Series of Poker winner, former PhD psychology student): teaches poker players perspective-taking
- Students universally assume opponent's raise means strong hand — but when Duke reverses the scenario, they realize THEY wouldn't raise with a strong hand (would slow-play to keep opponent in)
- "And it's not until I walk them through the exercise that people realize they failed to truly look at the table from the perspective of their opponent"
- These aren't dilettantes — they're paying $1,000 for her seminar, passionate about poker
- Foxes are likelier to engage in perspective-taking, by temperament, habit, or conscious effort

### Fox/Hedgehog as Spectrum, Not Dichotomy
- Not two types but a spectrum: "fox-hedgehogs" and "hedgehog-foxes" exist
- Thinking habits are not immutable — can shift gears "with effort, choose to shift gears from one mode to another"
- George Box: "All models are wrong, but some are useful" — the fox/hedgehog model is a starting point
- Key EPJ takeaway: "EPJ found modest but real foresight, and the critical ingredient was the style of thinking"

## Chapter 4: Superforecasters

### Iraq WMDs — The Intelligence Failure
- October 2002: National Intelligence Estimate (NIE) 2002-16HC stated Iraq had continued WMD programs, had chemical and biological weapons, would "probably" have nuclear weapon within the decade
- NIE = consensus view of 16 intelligence agencies (CIA, NSA, DIA, etc.) — the "intelligence community" (IC)
- IC budget >$50 billion, 100,000+ employees, 20,000 intelligence analysts
- October 2002 NIE concluded Bush administration claims about Iraqi WMDs were correct
- Even caustic critics of Bush (like Tom Friedman) were convinced Saddam was hiding something
- After 2003 invasion: US turned Iraq upside down looking for WMDs — found nothing
- "One of the worst — arguably THE worst — intelligence failure in modern history"
- Official investigations rejected the claim IC had caved to White House bullying; Robert Jervis (author of *Why Intelligence Fails*) agreed the conclusion was sincere
- Jervis's key insight: the IC's conclusion was both REASONABLE and WRONG
- "Was it a good decision?" vs. "Did it have a good outcome?" — these are different questions (bait-and-switch)
- Poker analogy: betting on a high-probability hand and losing doesn't make the bet wrong; winning on a low-probability hand doesn't make the bet right
- Every major intelligence agency on the planet suspected Saddam was hiding WMDs — because he WAS acting like someone hiding something (playing hide-and-seek with UN inspectors while risking invasion)

### The IC's "Correctable Errors" on Iraq WMDs
- Jervis didn't let the IC off the hook: "There were not only errors but correctable ones"
- "Analysis could and should have been better" — the result would have been less certain, not a different conclusion
- A less confident NIE could have been hugely consequential: if Congress required "beyond a reasonable doubt" and the IC said "60% or 70%," the congressional resolution authorizing force might not have passed
- But NIE 2002-16HC didn't say 60-70%; it said "Iraq has..." and "Baghdad has..." — language admitting no possibility of surprise
- George Tenet's "slam dunk" captured the IC's attitude: intelligence analysis always involves uncertainty, but on WMDs they fell prey to hubris
- Postmortems revealed NO "red teams" to attack prevailing views, no devil's advocates, no papers exploring competing possibilities
- "Most strikingly, no one proposed a view close to the one we now believe to be true" — "failing to conclude that Saddam had ended his banned weapons program is one thing — not even considering it as a possibility is another"

### IARPA's Creation and the Forecasting Tournament
- IC responds slowly; after Jervis's 1979 Iran postmortem, CIA head of political analysis promised a big meeting → "that is the end of the story. It never happened"
- But WMD failure was different: "They took that to heart"
- 2006: IARPA created — modeled after DARPA (which contributed to the invention of the Internet)
- 2008: Office of DNI asked National Research Council to synthesize research on good judgment; committee chaired by Baruch Fischhoff; Tetlock was there
- Report delivered 2 years later: "100% Archie Cochrane" — don't believe until you test; IC should not rely on methods that "violate well-documented behavioral principles or that have no evidence of efficacy beyond their intuitive appeal"
- Richards Heuer's CIA analyst manual: teaches psychology of cognitive biases — fine work, but has NEVER BEEN TESTED for efficacy
- IC's $50 billion budget can't answer basic question: "How accurate are the forecasts of intelligence analysts?"
- Senior officials claim 80-90% accuracy — "just guesses, like nineteenth-century physicians who were sure their treatments cured patients 80% or 90% of the time"
- IC has accountability for PROCESS (did you consider alternatives? look for contrary evidence?) but not for ACCURACY — like ensuring doctors examine patients thoroughly but never checking if treatments work

### IARPA Tournament Design
- Summer 2010: Jason Matheny and Steve Rieber visited Tetlock at Berkeley
- IARPA would sponsor massive tournament: five research teams competing to generate accurate probability estimates on Goldilocks-zone questions
- Questions in the "Goldilocks zone" of difficulty: not so easy an NYT reader could answer, not so hard no one could
- Teams competed against each other AND an independent control group ("wisdom of the crowd")
- Year 1 target: beat control by 20%; year 4 target: beat by 50%
- Within each team: Cochrane-style RCTs (give training to one random group, not another; compare)
- Recruited through blogs and professional networks: "Do you want to forecast the world's future? Here's your chance"
- Year 1: several thousand signed up, ~3,200 passed psychometric gauntlet

### GJP's Winning Method
- Key innovation: identify top ~2% of forecasters, weight their forecasts more heavily, then "extremize" (push toward 0% or 100%)
- Extremizing logic: if everyone had ALL the information, they'd be more confident → extremizing simulates this
- Result: a few hundred ordinary people + simple algorithms beat every other method, every university competitor, and even professional intelligence analysts with classified data — "by margins that remain classified"
- Washington Post (Nov 2013): David Ignatius reported superforecasters "performed about 30 percent better than the average for intelligence community analysts who could read intercepts and other secret data"

### Doug Lorch — Prototypical Superforecaster
- Retired IBM computer programmer, lives in Santa Barbara with artist wife, drives red Miata
- No special expertise in international affairs; reads NYT, can find Kazakhstan on a map
- Facebook avatar: a duck
- Year 1: answered 104 questions, made ~1,000 separate forecasts, Brier score 0.22, ranked 5th of 2,800
- Year 2: joined superforecaster team, Brier score 0.14, BEST of 2,800; beat prediction market by 40%; single-handedly exceeded IARPA's fourth-year performance target
- Only person to beat the extremizing algorithm
- Surpassed control group's "wisdom of the crowd" by >60%
- "The only way to make Doug look unimpressive would be to compare him to godlike omniscience"
- Paid $250 Amazon gift certificate for his efforts

### The First Class of Superforecasters
- 58 others at the top alongside Bill Flack and Doug Lorch in year 1
- Year 1 collective Brier score: 0.25 (vs. 0.37 for regular forecasters)
- Gap grew in later years: by end of 4-year tournament, superforecasters outperformed regulars by >60%
- Superforecasters looking 300 days out were more accurate than regular forecasters looking 100 days out — regulars needed to TRIPLE their foresight to match

### Skill vs. Luck: Regression to the Mean
- Michael Mauboussin (*The Success Equation*): regression to the mean is the key test
- Slow regression = activity dominated by skill; fast regression = dominated by chance
- Coin-toss analogy: 2,800 people predict 104 coin tosses; some get 70% right by luck; repeat and they regress to 50%
- With forecasting: year-to-year individual correlation is ~0.65 (higher than fathers/sons height correlation of 0.5)
- ~30% of superforecasters fall from top 2% each year — but 70% remain, far exceeding chance (odds of such consistency by coin flips: <1 in 100,000,000)
- Years 2 and 3: superforecasters as a group actually INCREASED their lead (opposite of regression to mean)
- Explanation: after year 1, superforecasters were identified, congratulated, put on teams of fellow superforecasters → being recognized and placed on stimulating teams improved performance enough to offset regression
- Years 3 and 4: harvested fresh crops of superforecasters, compared apples-to-apples → new cohorts continued to do as well or better
- Key conclusions: (1) don't treat any individual as infallible — luck plays a role; (2) "the superforecasters were not just lucky. Mostly, their results reflected skill"

- Extraordinary credentials: double major math/physics from Brown, MS from MIT in technology and policy, second MS in applied math from Harvard, PhD in applied physics from Harvard

- Speaks 5 languages (English, French, Russian, Italian, Spanish), voracious reader, double major + two master's degrees + PhD from Harvard
- Year 1 Brier score: 0.19 — tied for overall champion among 2,800
- Went on disability leave from MS in 2012; GJP was his "transition project"
- Wharton School gathering: superforecasters were "very sharp people who follow the news closely, particularly the elite media" — love books, highbrow reading

### Intelligence and Knowledge: Necessary but Not Sufficient
- GJP measured fluid intelligence (pattern-recognition puzzles) and crystallized intelligence (knowledge questions like "How many Supreme Court justices?")
- Results: Regular forecasters scored ~70th percentile vs. general population; superforecasters scored ~80th percentile
- Key: big jump is from public to forecasters (self-selected smart people), NOT from forecasters to superforecasters
- Superforecasters are well above average but NOT off-the-charts geniuses — most fall well short of "genius" territory (IQ 135+)
- Intelligence and knowledge help but add little beyond a certain threshold — confirms Kahneman's 1988 hunch
- "It's not the raw crunching power that counts. It's how you use it."
- Robert McNamara: "the best and the brightest" — escalated Vietnam based on domino theory, never seriously analyzed the critical forecast until years after the decisions; "We failed to analyze our assumptions critically, then or later"

### Fermi-ize: Breaking Questions Down
- Enrico Fermi's classic: "How many piano tuners in Chicago?"
- Key: break the unknowable question into sub-questions where you might have clues
- Worked example: population of Chicago (~2.5M) × piano ownership rate (2 in 100) × tunings per year (1) × hours per tuning (2) ÷ annual hours per tuner (1,600) = ~63 piano tuners. Actual answer: ~83 listings (surprisingly close)
- Fermi was renowned for back-of-envelope calculations that proved impressively accurate
- Sandy Sillman: "Fermi estimation was so critical to his job as a scientist working with atmospheric models that it became a part of my natural way of thinking"

### The Arafat Polonium Question — A Murder Mystery
- Oct 2004: Yasser Arafat fell ill, died Nov 11, 2004; cause uncertain; poisoning speculation
- July 2012: Swiss researchers found high polonium-210 levels in Arafat's belongings (Litvinenko was murdered with polonium-210 in 2006)
- IARPA question: "Will either the French or Swiss inquiries find elevated levels of polonium in the remains of Yasser Arafat's body?"
- Most people would start with tip-of-your-nose perspective: "Israel would never do that!" or "Of course Israel did it!" — classic bait and switch (answering "Did Israel poison Arafat?" instead of the actual question about test results)
- Bill Flack Fermi-ized it: first question was NOT about politics but about science — can polonium be detected on remains dead for years? (Checked Swiss report, learned it was possible)
- Then broke "pathways to yes": Israel poisoned him; Palestinian enemies poisoned him; postmortem contamination to frame Israel
- Each additional pathway INCREASED the probability of "yes" — key Fermi insight

### Outside View First, Inside View Second
- "Base rate" = how common something is in the broader class (Kahneman's "outside view" vs. "inside view")
- Renzetti family example: "Do the Renzettis have a pet?" — most people dive into family details (inside view); superforecasters first check: 62% of American households own pets (outside view), then adjust
- Peggy Noonan thought Bush's 47% post-presidency approval was remarkable — but the outside view shows ALL presidents' approval rises after leaving office (even Nixon's)
- Bill Flack on China-Vietnam border clash: first checks base rate (hostile conduct every 5 years → 20% annual probability), THEN adjusts based on current situation
- Multiple outside views: 62% of all households → but 73% of single-family detached homes → better anchor
- Anchoring effect (Kahneman & Tversky): we underadjust from initial anchors, even random ones (spin of a wheel); starting with outside view = starting with a meaningful anchor
- For Arafat polonium: Fermi-style outside view → exhumed body, reason to suspect poisoning → probability investigation finds evidence = 20-80% range → midpoint 50% as starting anchor

### The Inside View: Targeted Investigation
- After establishing outside view anchor, dive into specifics — but NOT aimlessly
- "A good exploration of the inside view does not involve wandering around, soaking up any and all information and hoping that insight somehow emerges. It is targeted and purposeful: it is an investigation, not an amble"
- For Arafat: Fermi-ize the inside view → three hypotheses (Israel poisoned, Palestinian enemies poisoned, postmortem contamination) → research each ("What would it take for this to be true?")

### Thesis, Antithesis, Synthesis — David Rogg Example
- David Rogg (semiretired software engineer, Virginia): terrorism in Europe question post-Charlie Hebdo
- Outside view: counted Islamist terror attacks in specified countries over prior 5 years → 6 attacks → base rate 1.2/year
- Inside view adjustments: ISIS surge → dropped oldest data → raised to 1.5; increased security post-Charlie Hebdo → partially offset; net raise to 1.8/year
- Math: 69 days left ÷ 365 × 1.8 = 0.34 → 34% probability
- Shared analysis on GJP forum to get more perspectives ("looking for more perspectives")
- Superforecasters constantly look for other views to synthesize

### The "Crowd Within" and Perspective-Taking
- Bill Flack writes down his reasoning, shares with teammates, asks them to critique it — also a way to distance himself and scrutinize his own thinking: "It's an auto-feedback thing"
- Research shows: assume your initial judgment is wrong, make another → combining both improves accuracy almost as much as getting a second person's estimate
- Wait several weeks, then re-estimate → same effect — called "the crowd within"
- George Soros: key to his success is stepping back from himself to judge his own thinking from a different perspective
- Flip the question's framing to check for confirmation bias: "Will South Africa grant the Dalai Lama a visa?" → also ask "Will South Africa DENY the Dalai Lama a visa?" — tiny wording change tilts investigation toward counter-evidence

### Dragonfly Forecasting
- Superforecasters do "on the one hand / on the other hand / on the third hand" analysis routinely, "long past the point where most people would succumb to migraines"
- They are "the polar opposite of the people who blurt out 'Ten cents!'" on the CRT
- "Forget the old advice to think twice. Superforecasters often think thrice — and sometimes they are just warming up"
- Need for cognition: superforecasters enjoy hard mental slogs (crosswords, Sudoku) — score high on need-for-cognition tests
- Big Five personality trait "openness to experience": unmistakable in superforecasters — Doug Lorch's reaction to "Who will win the presidential election in Ghana?" was "Well, here's an opportunity to learn something about Ghana"

### Active Open-Mindedness (AOM)
- Coined by psychologist Jonathan Baron (U Penn, office next to Tetlock's)
- Test asks agreement/disagreement with: "People should take into consideration evidence that goes against their beliefs"; "It is more useful to pay attention to those who disagree with you"; "Changing your mind is a sign of weakness" (reverse-scored)
- Superforecasters score highly AND walk the talk
- Doug Lorch: not merely open-minded but ACTIVELY open-minded — created a database of hundreds of news sources tagged by ideology, subject, geography, then wrote a program to select diverse reading
- "For superforecasters, beliefs are hypotheses to be tested, not treasures to be guarded"
- Bumper sticker for superforecasting: "Beliefs are hypotheses to be tested, not treasures to be guarded"

## Chapter 6: Superquants?

### Math and Superforecasting

- Lionel Levine (Cornell math prof, superforecaster): "I can think of a couple of questions where a little bit of math was useful, but otherwise he relies on subjective judgment"

- Bill Flack occasionally builds Monte Carlo models for currency exchange forecasts — "basic stuff for the cognoscenti, as obscure as ancient Aramaic for everyone else"
- But the great majority of superforecaster forecasts are "simply the product of careful thought and nuanced judgment" — no fancy math
- Superior numeracy helps NOT because of arcane models but because of something simpler and subtler

### Zero Dark Thirty: The Osama bin Laden Decision
- Abbottabad compound: high walls, unknown occupants who wanted to stay hidden, bits of evidence suggesting bin Laden
- Movie scene (Zero Dark Thirty): fictional Leon Panetta asks CIA officers "Is he there or is he not fucking there?" — wants agreement, gets disagreement (60%, 60%, 80%, etc.), calls it "a clusterfuck"
- Fictional Panetta is WRONG to want agreement — disagreement is actually the wisdom of the crowd, "gift wrapped"
- Maya (the hero): "A hundred percent, he's there. OK, fine, ninety-five percent" — impressive conviction, but unreasonable
- Even tiny alternative probabilities (drug trafficker, Afghan warlord, wealthy paranoid businessman) add up → can't be 100%
- Maya was "right but unreasonable" — the mirror image of the IC being "wrong but reasonable" on Iraq WMDs
- Real Leon Panetta: welcomed disagreement, "I encourage the people around me not to tell me what they thought I wanted to hear but what they believed, and to be honest"; "Nothing is one hundred percent"
- "The real Leon Panetta thinks like a superforecaster"

### Obama's "Fifty-Fifty"
- Obama listened to CIA officers ranging from 30% to 95% confident; median "wisdom of the crowd" around 70%
- Obama declared: "This is fifty-fifty... Look guys, this is a flip of the coin"
- Tetlock's analysis: "fifty-fifty" often means "I'm not sure" (not literally 50%) — Obama was probably saying "I can't base this decision on the notion that we have any greater certainty than that"
- As an executive making a decision, knowing it's >30% probability of bin Laden being there may be enough to act — the exact number (50%, 70%, 90%) doesn't change the decision
- Alternatively: Obama may have been bothered by disagreement and retreated to the "ignorance prior" (50%) — which would be a mistake, discarding valuable information
- Amos Tversky's joke (30 years earlier): "most people only have three settings: 'gonna happen,' 'not gonna happen,' and 'maybe'"

### The Three-Setting Mental Dial vs. Probabilistic Thinking
- Before 1713 (Bernoulli's Ars Conjectandi), people had no formal probability theory — relied entirely on tip-of-your-nose perspective
- Stone Age brain: two-setting dial (YES lion = run; NO lion = relax); three-setting dial at most (MAYBE = stay alert)
- Distinguishing 60% from 80% would have added little for a caveman — and the slower analysis could get you killed
- Parents will pay 2-3x more to reduce child's disease risk from 5% to 0% than from 10% to 5% — because 0% delivers certainty
- Harry Truman wanted "a one-armed economist" who wouldn't say "on the one hand... on the other hand"
- People equate confidence with competence — confident financial advisers are trusted more even with identical track records
- Forecasters who say "maybe" are perceived as "generally incompetent, ignorant of the facts, or lazy"

### Robert Rubin and Probabilistic Thinking
- As Harvard student, heard a philosophy professor argue "there is no provable certainty" — "it just clicked with everything I'd sort of thought"
- Became his axiom through 26 years at Goldman Sachs, adviser to Clinton, Treasury Secretary
- Autobiography: *In an Uncertain World*
- Young Treasury aide said "absolutely" to a question about a bill passing Congress; Rubin "didn't like that one bit. Now I say the probability is 60% — and we can argue over whether it's 59 or 60%"
- Rubin was frustrated that when he told White House/Congress "there's an 80% probability," people treated it as certainty: "You almost had to pound the table, to say 'yes there's a high probability but this also might not happen'"
- "If you say something is 60/40, people kind of get the idea" — but >60%, people collapse it to "this will happen"

### Granularity Predicts Accuracy
- Brian Labatte (superforecaster, Montreal): asked how much fiction vs. nonfiction he reads → "I would say 70%... no, 65/35 nonfiction to fiction" — remarkably precise for casual conversation
- Regular forecasters stick to tens (30%, 40%) — much less precise
- Superforecasters: fully one-third used SINGLE PERCENTAGE POINT precision (3% rather than 4%)
- Barbara Mellers tested this: rounded superforecaster forecasts to nearest 5, then nearest 10 → accuracy declined with even smallest-scale rounding (to nearest 0.05)
- Regular forecasters lost little even from rounding four times as large (to nearest 0.2)
- "Granularity isn't bafflegab. It is precision — and a key reason why he is a superforecaster"
- Charlie Munger: "If you don't get this elementary, but mildly unnatural, mathematics of elementary probability into your repertoire, then you go through a long life like a one-legged man in an ass-kicking contest"
- NIC (National Intelligence Council) uses only 5- or 7-degree scale for decisions like invading Iraq — far coarser than superforecasters

### Meaning, Fate, and Probability
- Vonnegut's Slaughterhouse-Five: "Why me?" / "Vy you? Vy anybody?"
- "Everything happens for a reason" — deeply human need for meaning; Oprah: "there are no coincidences. There are none. Only divine order here"
- Even atheists: majority believe in fate ("events happen for a reason and there is an underlying order to life")
- Probabilistic thinking and belief in fate are in tension: "Like oil and water, chance and fate do not mix"
- Laura Kray experiments: counterfactual thinking (imagining how things could have been different) actually increases belief that what happened was "meant to be"
- Robert Shiller tells the story of how Henry Ford's $5/day wage brought both his grandfathers to Detroit — an almost infinite chain of contingencies; "You tend to believe that history played out in a logical sort of sense... but it's not like that. It's an illusion of hindsight"
- Superforecasters are much less inclined to see things as fated; they take the outside view toward even profoundly identity-defining events
- Pro-fate vs. pro-probability statements tested on superforecasters vs. regular forecasters vs. undergrads vs. general public

### Fate vs. Probability: Test Results
- 9-point "fate score" (1 = total rejection of meant-to-be thinking, 9 = full embrace): adult Americans scored middle of scale; Penn undergrads a little lower; regular forecasters lower still; superforecasters lowest — firmly on the rejection-of-fate side
- Individual fate scores correlated with Brier scores: more fate-thinking = less accuracy; more probabilistic thinking = more accuracy
- "Finding meaning in events is positively correlated with wellbeing but negatively correlated with foresight" — depressing implication: "Is misery the price of accuracy?"

## Chapter 7: Supernewsjunkies?

### The Superforecasting Method Summary
- Unpack the question into components. Distinguish known from unknown, scrutinize all assumptions
- Adopt outside view (comparative perspective, treats problem as special case of wider class)
- Then adopt inside view (plays up uniqueness)
- Explore similarities/differences with others' views; pay attention to prediction markets and wisdom of crowds
- Synthesize into dragonfly vision. Express with fine-grained probability
- This is "literally just the beginning" — then comes updating

### Devyn Duffy — The Updating Machine
- Devyn Duffy: superforecaster, lost factory job at 36, became income maintenance caseworker in Pittsburgh
- "My most useful talent is the ability to do well on tests, especially multiple-choice. This has made me appear more intelligent than I actually am, often even to myself"
- Uses Google alerts for every forecast question (e.g., "Syrian refugees" + "UNHCR"); daily alerts for fast-moving topics, weekly for others
- Season 3: made 2,271 forecasts on 140 questions — more than 16 forecasts per question on average
- Superforecasters update much more frequently than regular forecasters

### Updating Is Not Just Housekeeping
- Even if tournament only allowed one forecast with no updating, superforecasters' initial forecasts were at least 50% more accurate
- Famous political scientist declined to participate because he had no interest in "competing with unemployed newsjunkies"
- But good updating requires the same skills as initial forecasting — it is not "mindlessly adjusting forecasts in response to whatever is on CNN"

### The Over-Under: Scylla and Charybdis
- Two dangers: underreaction (not enough weight to new info) and overreaction (seeing info as more meaningful than it is)
- Both can destroy a perfectly good forecast
- Bill Flack underreaction: Shinzo Abe/Yasukuni Shrine — an unofficial source said Abe would visit, but Bill discounted it because visiting made no rational sense. Bill later realized he'd pulled a bait-and-switch: "the question I was really answering" was whether a rational PM would visit, not whether Abe would
- Doug Lorch overreaction: Arctic sea ice question — swung from 55% to 95% based on one month-old report, then ice loss slowed and he was wrong

### Underreaction Sources
- Prosaic: Joshua Frankel missed updating on Syria because he was "too swamped with work"
- Psychological: Earl Warren and Japanese internment — "The very fact that no sabotage has taken place to date is a disturbing and confirming indication that such action will be taken" — the absence of evidence was used as evidence
- Commitment/identity: Dan Kahan research — views on gun control correlate with views on climate change because beliefs become part of identity (Jenga tower analogy — pulling one block risks toppling others)
- Keynes "When the facts change, I change my mind" quotation — Tetlock traced it and found it's likely apocryphal (no one has ever found the source)
- Jean-Pierre Beugoms (superforecaster): prides himself on willingness to "change my opinions a lot faster than my peers"
- Superforecasters have less ego investment in specific forecasts → easier to update

### Overreaction: The Dilution Effect
- Experiment: participants told "Robert studies 31 hours/week" → predict high GPA; then given irrelevant info (plays tennis, short relationships) → confidence drops — "dilution effect"
- Psychologists call it dilution effect: irrelevant noise causes people to sway in the wind
- Stock market: volume/volatility partly driven by overreacting to new information; Keynes said "day-to-day fluctuations in profits of existing investments tend to have an altogether excessive, and even absurd, influence on the market"
- Burton Malkiel: active traders earned annual returns of only 11.4% vs. doing nothing — "massive time and effort went into those trades and yet the people who made them would have been better off if they had gone golfing"

### Captain Minto: Many Small Updates
- Tim Minto won top spot in season 3 with Brier score of 0.15 — 45-year-old Vancouver software engineer
- Initial forecasts take only 5-15 minutes; then checks teammates' views and trawls for contrary evidence, five days a week
- On Syrian refugees question: made 34 updates over 3 months; average update only 3.5 percentage points; final Brier score 0.07
- On one question (US-Afghanistan troop agreement): made 77 forecasts
- Key: many small updates avoid both Scylla (underreaction) and Charybdis (overreaction)
- Tournament data prove it: superforecasters update more often AND in smaller increments

### Bayesian Updating in Practice
- Thomas Bayes (Presbyterian minister, born 1701): Bayes' theorem — posterior odds = likelihood ratio × prior odds
- Jay Ulfelder (political scientist, superforecaster): Chuck Hagel confirmation question
  - Tom Ricks predicted "chances of Hagel becoming defense secretary decline by about 2%"
  - Ulfelder: base rate for SecDef confirmation = 23/24 = 96% (only 1 of 24 nominees rejected, none withdrawn)
  - Even with Hagel's bad hearing, using generous estimates tilted in Ricks's favor, Bayesian update → 83% — Hagel still very likely (and was confirmed)
- Most superforecasters are "Bayesians who do not use Bayes' theorem" — Tim Minto: "I have a better intuitive grasp of Bayes' theorem than most people, even though if you asked me to write it down from memory I'd probably fail"
- Winning formula: lots of small updates — BUT know when to break the rule (Doug Lorch at 95% on failing report: "The only reason I'm at 95% is a report that is clearly failing, so I should ditch the report and make a new forecast" → dropped to 15%)
- Orwell's sixth rule in "Politics and the English Language": "Break any of these rules sooner than saying anything outright barbarous"

## Chapter 8: Perpetual Beta

### Mary Simpson — Failure as Motivation
- PhD in economics from Claremont, managed regulatory/financial affairs for Southern California Edison
- Like most experts, missed the 2007-08 financial crisis — retirement savings cratered
- "I really wanted to think more about forecasting... it's one of those things where you feel like, 'I should be better at this'"

### Growth Mindset (Carol Dweck)
- Fixed mindset: "I'm bad at math" = immutable feature, like being left-handed
- Growth mindset: abilities are products of effort, can be developed
- Dweck experiment: gave 5th graders easy puzzles (all enjoyed), then harder ones → fixed-mindset kids gave up; growth-mindset kids loved them even more ("Could you write down the name of these puzzles so my mom can buy me some more?")
- Brain scans: fixed-mindset volunteers showed interest when told right/wrong; growth-mindset volunteers "paid close attention to information that could stretch their knowledge. Only for them was learning a priority"
- Superforecasters need growth mindset — the "consistently inconsistent" Keynes exemplifies it

### Keynes as Investor: Try, Fail, Analyze, Adjust
- From end of WWI to end of WWII, managed his own money, family/friends' money, insurance companies, King's College
- When he died in 1946, was extremely wealthy — "considering that Keynes was investing during some of the worst years in history, his returns are astounding"
- Nearly wiped out in 1920 (bad currency forecasts); bounced back, made fortune in 1920s
- Missed crash of 1929, lost big again — but analyzed his failure, concluded stock prices don't always reflect true value → independently developed what Benjamin Graham called "value investing"
- "There is no harm in being sometimes wrong, especially if one is promptly found out"
- Try, fail, analyze, adjust, try again — "Keynes cycled through those steps ceaselessly"

### Try: Learning Requires Doing
- Michael Polanyi: wrote detailed physics of riding a bicycle — "the curvature of each winding is inversely proportional to the square of the speed" — yet reading this won't teach you to ride
- "Tacit knowledge" — the kind you can only get from bruising experience
- Equally obvious: learning to forecast requires trying to forecast

### Fail: The Importance of Feedback
- Not all practice improves skill — needs to be informed practice with clear, timely feedback
- GJP training booklet improved accuracy by ~10%
- Police officers' lie-detection skill: no clear immediate feedback → gap between confidence and skill GROWS with experience → increasingly overconfident
- Meteorologists and bridge players ARE well-calibrated because they get clear, prompt feedback
- Vague forecasts ("might," "may") prevent good feedback; Forer effect (vague personality profiles from astrology book) — everyone thinks it fits them perfectly
- Hindsight bias: after USSR Communist Party lost power, experts who had estimated 10% probability recalled it as 40-50% — "I knew it all along" effect
- Forecasters with vague language and flawed memory are "like basketball players doing free throws in the dark"
- Tim Minto's Brier scores gave precise feedback: 0.07 on Syrian refugees (nothing-but-net), 1.46 on Yasukuni Shrine (ball into trash can at back of gymnasium)

### Analyze and Adjust: Postmortems
- Devyn Duffy after his team got drubbed on Arafat-polonium question: "We agreed as a team that the half-life of polonium would make detection virtually impossible. We didn't do nearly enough to question that assumption"
- Superforecasters share lengthy postmortems with teammates — online discussions that go on for pages
- Jean-Pierre Beugoms: started leaving longer comments on his forecasts because sparse notes meant "I often could not figure out why I made a certain forecast"
- Key: distinguishing "I was right" from "I got lucky" — Devyn Duffy after a correct forecast: "we lucked out, too" — rare willingness to acknowledge luck in success
- Tetlock's EPJ research: experts welcomed "I was almost right" counterfactuals but rejected "I was almost wrong" ones

### Grit (Angela Duckworth)
- Elizabeth Sloane: diagnosed with brain cancer, endured chemo, failed stem-cell transplant, recurrence, two more years of chemo — volunteered for GJP to "re-grow her synapses"
- Anne Kilkenny: housewife from small town in Alaska, "four decades removed from having had a real intellectual challenge," became superforecaster
- Anne had brief fame in 2008 when she wrote an e-mail about Sarah Palin (her neighbor in Wasilla, Alaska) that went viral → kept her head amid partisan adulation, recognized that "feelings were masquerading as knowledge"
- Anne's tenacity: on Central Africa Republic refugee question, e-mailed the UN agency directly to ask how often data were updated; got response in French; wrote back asking for English; got lengthy helpful response
- Grit + growth mindset = "potent force for personal progress"

### The Superforecaster Profile Summary
- In their THINKING: Intellectually curious with "need for cognition," reflective/self-critical, numerate
- In their METHODS: Pragmatic (not wedded to ideology), analytical (step back from tip-of-nose), dragonfly-eyed (synthesize diverse views), probabilistic (many grades of maybe), thoughtful updaters, good intuitive psychologists (aware of biases)
- In their WORK ETHIC: Growth mindset, grit
- Strongest predictor of rising to superforecaster: perpetual beta (commitment to belief updating and self-improvement) — roughly 3x as powerful as intelligence
- "Superforecasting appears to be roughly 75% perspiration, 25% inspiration"

## Chapter 9: Superteams

### Bay of Pigs vs. Cuban Missile Crisis
- January 1961: NYT exposed the secret CIA-trained Cuban exile invasion force; Kennedy's team didn't reconsider
- Bay of Pigs invasion: 1,400 guerrillas landed, met 20,000 Cuban soldiers, all killed or captured within 3 days
- The plan was "harebrained" — not hindsight bias; contingency plan assumed guerrillas could escape to Escambray Mountains, but the landing site had been moved 80+ miles away through swamp and jungle
- Irving Janis (*Victims of Groupthink*, 1972): "cozy unanimity" was the key problem
- After the fiasco, Kennedy ordered changes: skepticism as watchword, Bobby Kennedy and Sorensen as "intellectual watchdogs" / devil's advocates, protocol and hierarchy set aside, Kennedy would sometimes leave the room
- Cuban missile crisis (October 1962): same cast of characters, brilliantly handled — the team that bungled Bay of Pigs performed brilliantly with different group dynamics

### Teams in the IARPA Tournament
- Year 1: randomly assigned hundreds to work alone vs. in teams; teams communicated via online forums, email, Skype
- Gave teams basic training on constructive criticism and "precision questioning" (e.g., "What do you mean by 'pastime'?" not "You're silly")
- Result: teams were 23% more accurate than individuals on average
- Year 2: created superforecaster teams (~12 per team)
- Risk of "CEO disease": tell people they're exceptional and they may get complacent
- Elaine Rich: found it difficult to question strangers; Arafat-polonium discussion had "a lot of tension... almost a taboo question"
- Marty Rosenthal: "leading from behind" — led by example, organized conference calls, hosted backyard barbecue
- Paul Theron (South African superforecaster): on Honduras election question, tracked down a political scientist specializing in Honduran politics, found obscure analysis website, had informative discussion — "The team is so much more effective at gathering information than one person could ever be"

### Superteam Results
- Superforecasters put on teams in year 2 became 50% more accurate (same result in year 3)
- Tournament hierarchy: wisdom of the crowd < ordinary teams (+10%) < prediction markets (+20% over teams) < superteams (+15-30% over prediction markets)
- Superteams resembled Amy Edmondson's best surgical teams: "psychologically safe" to correct higher-ups, shared purpose, said "our" more than "my"
- Adam Grant's categories: givers, matchers, takers — superteams had lots of givers (Marty Rosenthal, Doug Lorch who distributed programming tools)
- Scott Page: "diversity trumps ability" — superteams were fairly diverse but not designed for diversity; might have done even better with deliberate diversity

## Chapter 10: The Leader's Dilemma

### The Tension Between Forecasting and Leading
- Leaders need confidence, decisiveness, and vision — superforecasting demands humility, self-doubt, and tentativeness
- Resolution: the contradiction is "more apparent than real"

### Helmuth von Moltke and Auftragstaktik (Mission Command)
- "In war, everything is uncertain" — "No plan of operations extends with certainty beyond the first encounter with the enemy's main strength"
- German military tradition: officers received liberal arts education with emphasis on critical thinking; students invited to suggest solutions, discuss collectively, disagree — even instructors' views could be challenged
- General Seydlitz at Battle of Rossbach (1757): Frederick the Great ordered attack; Seydlitz waited for the right moment despite threat of execution; attacked when ready, won the battle — "Tell the King that after the battle my head is at his disposal, but meanwhile I will make use of it"
- Auftragstaktik: decision-making power pushed down the hierarchy; tell subordinates WHAT to accomplish, not HOW; give them the "why" so they can improvise
- Wehrmacht vs. US/UK/French armies: Wehrmacht gave short, simple orders ("I demand your divisions cross the borders... I don't care how you do it, that's completely up to you"); other armies issued detailed instructions from headquarters
- Eben Emael (May 10, 1940): critical operation given to a young lieutenant whose glider crash-landed 100km off target — a sergeant took charge, destroyed Belgian guns, and the operation succeeded
- Wehrmacht ultimately failed partly because Hitler violated Moltke's principles by taking direct control (Normandy: reserves couldn't move without his personal command; Hitler slept late on D-Day)

### Eisenhower and the American Adoption
- Eisenhower wrote a note taking personal responsibility BEFORE giving D-Day order (in case of failure)
- Expected officers to engage in open debate; respected well-founded criticism
- After Vietnam, US Army adopted "mission command" (1982); built on Israeli experience and Wehrmacht principles
- David Petraeus: pushed officers out of "intellectual comfort zone," developed flexible training with real surprises
- "The bold move is the right move except when it's the wrong move" — leader must figure out which and execute boldly
- Jeff Bezos's Amazon leadership principle: "Have backbone; disagree and commit"
- Walmart's "leadership academy" modeled on military mission command

### Intellectual Humility vs. Self-Confidence
- Annie Duke: "You have to have tremendous humility in the face of the game because the game is extremely complex... That being said, humility in the face of the game is extremely different than humility in the face of your opponents"
- "The humility required for good judgment is not self-doubt. It is intellectual humility — a recognition that reality is profoundly complex, that seeing things clearly is a constant struggle, and that human judgment must therefore be riddled with mistakes"
- Possible to think highly of yourself AND be intellectually humble — this combination is "wonderfully fruitful"

## Chapter 11: Are They Really So Super?

### Kahneman's Challenge
- Summer 2014: Kahneman asked Tetlock "Do you see them as different kinds of people, or as people who do different kinds of things?" — answer: "A bit of both"
- Concern: continuous self-scrutiny is exhausting; superforecasters are always "just a System 2 slipup away from a blown forecast"
- General Michael Flynn (DIA head): claimed we're in "a period of prolonged societal conflict that is pretty unprecedented" — empirically wrong (interstate wars declining since 1950s, civil wars since late 1980s, violence at historic lows by Steven Pinker's analysis)
- Even accomplished people fall for tip-of-your-nose delusions; Müller-Lyer optical illusion: knowing it's an illusion can't switch off the illusion

### Scope Insensitivity Test
- Kahneman predicted superforecasters would fail at scope sensitivity (his famous finding: people pay same $10 to clean up a small region's lakes vs. all 250,000 Ontario lakes; same ~$80 for 2,000 vs. 200,000 dying birds)
- Test: "How likely is it Assad's regime will fall in 3 months?" vs. "6 months?"
- Regular forecasters: 40% (3 months) vs. 41% (6 months) — virtually scope insensitive
- Superforecasters: 15% (3 months) vs. 24% (6 months) — significantly scope sensitive
- Kahneman was surprised; Tetlock believes some System 2 corrections have become habitual (part of System 1) through deliberate practice — like a golfer's stance becoming automatic

### Nassim Taleb's Black Swan Critique
- Taleb: "What matters can't be forecast and what can be forecast doesn't matter"
- Tetlock and Taleb co-authored a joint paper; areas of agreement and disagreement
- Agreement: no evidence geopolitical/economic forecasters can predict 10+ years out; butterfly dynamics of nonlinear systems
- Historical probabilities may be fat-tailed (like wealth distribution, not height) — making extreme events far more probable than normal distributions suggest
- If a British official in 1914 assumed war casualties were normally distributed, he'd see WWI + WWII casualties as one-in-millions probability; with fat tails, it's thousands of times more likely
- Tetlock's response: black swans are NOT the only things that matter; history also crawls; slow incremental change can be profoundly important
- "Black swan investing" (Taleb/Vinod Khosla approach) vs. "edge investing" (poker player approach) — neither superior, both valid for different situations
- Bayesian question clustering: break big unscorable questions into many small scorable ones, like pointillist painting — each dot adds little, but patterns emerge

## Chapter 12: What's Next?

### The Scottish Referendum Example
- September 2014: Scotland voted 55.3% to 44.7% to stay in UK; superforecasters beat British betting markets
- Daniel Drezner (Washington Post): confessed uncertainty, asked "what does one do with data points like this to adjust one's worldview?" — "a teachable moment for punditry"

### The Kto-Kogo Problem (Lenin's "Who-Whom")
- Accuracy is often only one of many goals in forecasting; sometimes it's irrelevant
- Forecasts serve political purposes: Nate Silver praised by Democrats in 2012, then attacked in 2014 when his forecasts favored Republicans
- Dick Morris predicted Romney landslide; defended himself afterward: "I felt that it was my duty" to say that
- Jonathan Schell's nuclear holocaust warning wasn't accurate but served its political purpose

### Ernest Codman and the End Result System
- Early 1900s surgeon proposed hospitals should record ailments, treatments, and outcomes — "all that counted were the clinical consequences of a doctor's effort"
- Hospital establishment hated it; Codman was booted from Massachusetts General
- Opened his own hospital, published statistics, continued publicizing — eventually evidence-based medicine prevailed

### Adversarial Collaboration
- Keynesians vs. Austerians after 2008: no meaningful learning happened; when Fed bank president changed his mind based on evidence, it made headlines ("Man Changes His Mind in Response to Evidence")
- Kahneman-Klein adversarial collaboration as model: opposing schools commit to playing by scientific ground rules, identify what they disagree about, design precise testable forecasts
- Bayesian question clustering for policy debates: break big "How does this all turn out?" into many precise, scorable sub-questions

### Tom Friedman as Superquestioner
- Superforecaster ≠ superquestioner (likely different psychological profiles)
- Hedgehog-like incisiveness generates great questions; foxy eclecticism generates great forecasts
- Ideal: "Tom-Bill symbiosis" — Friedman generates provocative questions, Flack generates calibrated answers
- "The 'Tom versus Bill' frame with which we started the book is our final false dichotomy"

## Appendix: Ten Commandments for Aspiring Superforecasters
1. **Triage** — Focus on Goldilocks-zone questions (not too easy, not impossible)
2. **Fermi-ize** — Break intractable problems into tractable sub-problems; expose assumptions; dare to be wrong
3. **Balance inside and outside views** — Search for comparison classes even for "unique" events; start with base rates
4. **Balance under- and overreacting to evidence** — Be incremental updater; ferret out telltale clues; know when to jump
5. **Look for clashing causal forces** — Consider competing hypotheses; synthesize thesis-antithesis-synthesis
6. **Distinguish as many degrees of doubt as the problem permits** — Use granular probabilities; the more fine-grained, the more accurate
7. **Balance under- and overconfidence** — Manage trade-off between decisive stands and qualifying them
8. **Look for errors behind mistakes but beware hindsight bias** — Own failures; conduct unflinching postmortems; also scrutinize successes for luck
9. **Bring out the best in others and let others bring out the best in you** — Precision questioning, perspective-taking, constructive confrontation; Tommy Lasorda: "Managing is like holding a dove in your hand"
10. **Master the error-balancing bicycle** — Learning requires doing with clear feedback; practice is not going through the motions
11. **Don't treat commandments as commandments** — Guidelines, not rules; break them when necessary (Orwell's sixth rule)
