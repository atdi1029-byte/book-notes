# Superforecasting: The Art and Science of Prediction
## Philip E. Tetlock & Dan Gardner (2015, 302 pages)

### One-Sentence Summary
Forecasting accuracy is a measurable, improvable skill — not a mysterious gift — and the people who do it best share a distinctive set of thinking habits that any intelligent, determined person can learn.

### Core Thesis
Philip Tetlock's famous 20-year study (1984-2004) showed the average expert predicted no better than a dart-throwing chimpanzee. But hidden in that data was a crucial finding: some experts DID have real foresight. What separated them was not what they thought but how they thought. The Good Judgment Project — an IARPA-sponsored forecasting tournament involving 20,000+ volunteers — identified these "superforecasters" and proved that ordinary people using the right thinking methods can outperform professional intelligence analysts with access to classified data.

### Key Themes

**1. The Superforecaster Profile**
Not geniuses (above average intelligence but not off-the-charts), not subject-matter experts, not news junkies per se. What distinguishes them: intellectual curiosity, active open-mindedness, probabilistic thinking, comfort with uncertainty, growth mindset, and relentless self-improvement ("perpetual beta"). Perpetual beta is 3x more predictive of superforecaster status than raw intelligence.

**2. The Method**
- Fermi-ize: Break big unknowable questions into smaller tractable sub-questions
- Outside view first: Start with base rates before diving into case-specific details
- Inside view second: Targeted investigation of the specifics, guided by hypotheses
- Dragonfly eye: Synthesize multiple perspectives (your own, others', contrarian views)
- Granular probability: Use precise percentages (3% vs. 4% matters), not vague words
- Many small updates: Update frequently but incrementally as new evidence arrives
- Postmortems: Rigorously analyze both successes and failures

**3. Foxes vs. Hedgehogs**
Isaiah Berlin's metaphor, applied to forecasting: hedgehogs know One Big Thing and squeeze everything through that lens (confident, media-friendly, often wrong). Foxes know many small things, shift perspectives, speak in "howevers" and "on the other hands" (uncertain, bad TV, more accurate). Superforecasters are extreme foxes.

**4. The Enemies of Good Forecasting**
- Tip-of-your-nose perspective (System 1 dominance)
- Bait and switch (answering easy questions instead of hard ones)
- Confirmation bias
- Belief in fate/determinism (inversely correlated with accuracy)
- The two- or three-setting mental dial (yes/no/maybe instead of fine-grained probability)
- Underreaction (anchored to prior beliefs) and overreaction (swayed by noise)

**5. Teams and Leadership**
Superteams beat prediction markets by 15-30%. Keys: psychological safety, constructive criticism, precision questioning, diverse perspectives, "givers" culture. The leader's dilemma — confidence vs. humility — is resolved through Moltke's Auftragstaktik (mission command): tell people what to accomplish, not how; push decision-making down; balance determination with adaptability.

**6. The Limits**
No one can forecast 10+ years out on geopolitical questions. Nassim Taleb's black swan critique has merit for extreme fat-tail events. But history also "crawls" — and on the timescales where incremental change matters (months to a few years), superforecasters demonstrably add value. The key is knowing which questions are forecastable and which aren't (triage).

### The Bottom Line
"For superforecasters, beliefs are hypotheses to be tested, not treasures to be guarded." The book's most radical claim: the intelligence community's $50 billion apparatus was outperformed by a few hundred volunteers with $250 Amazon gift certificates — because those volunteers treated forecasting as a skill to be practiced and improved, while the professionals treated it as a sideshow.
