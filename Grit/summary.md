# Grit

**Author:** Angela Duckworth
**Published:** 2016
**Pages:** 292

## One-Line Summary
Achievement depends less on innate talent than on the combination of sustained passion and perseverance — what Duckworth calls "grit" — and this quality can be cultivated from the inside out (interest, practice, purpose, hope) and the outside in (parenting, mentoring, culture).

## Key Takeaways
1. **Effort counts twice:** Talent x effort = skill, and skill x effort = achievement — effort appears in both equations, making it doubly important compared to raw talent.
2. **Grit predicts success better than talent:** At West Point, in spelling bees, sales, Green Berets selection, and Chicago public schools, grit outpredicted SAT scores, IQ, physical fitness, and conscientiousness.
3. **Passion means consistency, not intensity:** The passion component of grit is about maintaining the same top-level goal over years, not about momentary enthusiasm.
4. **The naturalness bias is real:** We say we value hard work, but experiments show we systematically prefer and reward "naturals" over "strivers" — even when performance is identical.
5. **Interest develops through discovery, not epiphany:** Passions are not found in a single flash of insight but built through repeated encounters, encouragement, and deepening engagement over years.
6. **Deliberate practice is what separates experts from amateurs:** Not just more hours, but better hours — setting stretch goals, seeking feedback, concentrating fully, and repeating with refinement.
7. **Purpose amplifies perseverance:** Other-centered motivation (contributing beyond yourself) sustains grit far longer than self-interest alone; those with both intrinsic interest and prosocial purpose are the grittiest.
8. **Hope is not wishful thinking — it's a learned skill:** Growth mindset + optimistic self-talk + mastery experiences wire the brain to persist through adversity rather than becoming helpless.
9. **Grit grows with age:** Older adults score consistently higher on grit than younger adults, likely due to both cultural forces and natural maturation.
10. **Wise parenting = supportive AND demanding:** Neither authoritarian harshness nor permissive indulgence builds grit — the research-backed approach combines warmth, respect, and high standards.
11. **Extracurriculars are the "playing fields of grit":** Multi-year commitment to structured activities with advancement predicts college graduation and career success better than grades or test scores.
12. **Culture is the most powerful grit multiplier:** Joining a gritty culture (team, school, company) leverages conformity — the basic human drive to fit in — to raise everyone's grit through social multiplier effects.
13. **The Hard Thing Rule works for families:** Everyone does something hard, you can't quit on a bad day, and you pick your own hard thing — creating structured practice in perseverance.
14. **Grit is not everything:** Character has three dimensions (intrapersonal, interpersonal, intellectual) and goodness matters more than greatness; strategic quitting of low-level goals is wise.

## Thematic Breakdowns

### The Science of Grit
- Grit Scale: 10-item questionnaire measuring passion (consistency of interests) and perseverance (resilience/effort)
- Perseverance scores typically higher than passion scores — consistency of interests is the harder component
- Twin studies: grit is 20-37% heritable, meaning 63-80% is influenced by experience
- Grit and talent are independent — SAT scores and grit are slightly inversely correlated in Ivy League students

### The Talent Myth
- Francis Galton (1869): first to study high achievement scientifically, but even he found "zeal" and "capacity for hard labor" matter alongside ability
- Darwin considered himself a "plodder" — attributed success to careful observation and industry, not brilliance
- Nietzsche: mythologizing talent lets us off the hook — "To call someone divine means here there is no need to compete"
- McKinsey's "War for Talent" led companies like Enron to prioritize perceived talent over character — with catastrophic results

### Building Grit from the Inside
- **Interest**: Fostered through play, exploration, sampling, encouragement — not forced early specialization
- **Practice**: Deliberate practice (stretch goals, feedback, concentration, repetition) — distinct from flow but complementary
- **Purpose**: Evolves from self-interest to other-centeredness; calling vs. job vs. career distinction
- **Hope**: Growth mindset + CBT-style optimistic self-talk + mastery experiences that wire prefrontal cortex

### Building Grit from the Outside
- Wise (authoritative) parenting: warmth + respect + high expectations
- Mentors who combine support with challenge (Tobi Lutke's master programmer, Cody Coleman's math teacher)
- Extracurricular activities providing deliberate practice under non-parental authority
- Organizational culture: Seahawks ("Always compete"), West Point (developmental model), UNC soccer (core values + quotes)

## Contrarian Ideas
- **"Follow your passion" is incomplete advice:** Passions aren't discovered — they're developed through extended exploration, repeated encounters, and deepening engagement. Telling people to "follow their passion" implies a pre-existing passion waiting to be found.
- **Talent and effort are not equal contributors:** Conventional wisdom treats talent and effort as comparable; Duckworth's equations show effort counts twice because it both builds skill and makes skill productive.
- **The naturalness bias undermines meritocracy:** Even people who say they value hard work unconsciously prefer "naturals" over "strivers" — creating systemic disadvantage for those who succeed through effort.
- **Deliberate practice is not flow:** The psychology establishment treats flow as the peak experience; Duckworth argues deliberate practice (effortful, planned, uncomfortable) is what builds skill, while flow is the reward during performance.
- **Grit doesn't follow an inverted-U curve:** Unlike most virtues (where too much becomes a vice), Duckworth found no evidence that excess grit is harmful — zero of 300 surveyed Americans wished they were less gritty.
- **Adversity in childhood can inoculate against helplessness:** Rat studies show controlled adversity during adolescence literally rewires the prefrontal cortex to handle future stress — but only when the young organism has some control over the outcome.

## Author's Key Proposals
1. **Adopt the two equations:** talent x effort = skill; skill x effort = achievement. Use this framework to resist the temptation to attribute success to innate talent alone.
2. **Build a coherent goal hierarchy:** Identify one ultimate concern (top-level goal), align mid-level goals beneath it, and write low-level goals in pencil while keeping the top-level goal in ink.
3. **Practice deliberately:** Set stretch goals targeting specific weaknesses, concentrate fully, seek immediate feedback, and repeat with refinement — make it a daily habit tied to a consistent time and place.
4. **Implement the Hard Thing Rule in your family:** Everyone does a hard thing requiring deliberate practice, you can't quit on a bad day, you pick your own hard thing, and in high school commit to at least one activity for two years.
5. **Parent (or mentor) wisely — be both supportive and demanding:** Combine warmth and respect with high standards and accountability. Don't choose between tough love and unconditional support.
6. **Join or create a gritty culture:** Leverage the social multiplier effect — surround yourself with gritty people and their norms become your norms.
7. **Cultivate purpose by connecting work to others:** Reflect on how your work contributes to the well-being of others, practice job crafting to align daily tasks with core values, and find purposeful role models.

## Relevance to Trading/Investing
- **Effort counts twice in markets:** Natural market intuition means nothing without the daily deliberate practice of reviewing trades, studying setups, and refining strategy — effort both builds trading skill and makes that skill productive.
- **Consistency of interest is the hardest part:** Most traders hop between strategies after drawdowns; grit research shows the passion component (sticking with one approach for years) is harder than the perseverance component.
- **Deliberate practice applies to trading:** Setting stretch goals (new setups), seeking feedback (trade journals), concentrating fully (no distracted trading), and repeating with refinement mirrors the deliberate practice framework.
- **The naturalness bias distorts trading:** We mythologize "natural" traders and dismiss consistent grinders — but research shows the grinders' effort compounds twice.
- **Purpose sustains through drawdowns:** Traders who connect their work to larger purpose (family security, financial independence, helping others learn) persist through losing streaks that eliminate pleasure-seekers.
- **Culture matters:** Joining a disciplined trading community leverages the social multiplier — surrounding yourself with consistent, process-focused traders raises your own standards through conformity.
- **Warren Buffett's three-step prioritization:** Write 25 goals, circle top 5, avoid the other 20 at all costs — directly applicable to eliminating strategy-hopping and focusing on one edge.
