## Preface

### The MacArthur Fellowship and the Genius Paradox

- Duckworth's father obsessed over genius and talent, constantly diagnosing family members as intellectually insufficient — none "measured up to Einstein"
- Two years ago, Duckworth won the **MacArthur Fellowship** ("genius grant") — ironic given her father's assessments
- She didn't win because she's smarter than fellow psychologists; "Is she a genius?" is the wrong question

> "In the long run, Dad, grit may matter more than talent."

- The award recognized that achievement depends more on **passion and perseverance** than innate talent
- Duckworth has degrees from tough schools but didn't test high enough for gifted programs in third grade
- Her parents are Chinese immigrants, but she didn't get lectured on hard work — defying stereotype
- **Grit is mutable, not fixed** — the book summarizes scientific evidence on how to grow it
- Read the finished book to her father (who has Parkinson's); he nodded and smiled

### Core Argument

- What we eventually accomplish may depend more on passion and perseverance than innate talent

## Part I: What Grit Is and Why It Matters

## Chapter 1: Showing Up

### West Point's Beast Barracks

- **West Point** admissions: at least as rigorous as most selective universities
- Top SAT/ACT scores, high school grades, Congressional nomination, fitness assessment required
- Each year ~14,000 applicants start; winnowed to ~1,200 admitted
- Nearly all incoming cadets were varsity athletes; most were team captains
- Yet **one in five drops out before graduation**
- Substantial fraction drop out during first summer in **Beast Barracks** — an intensive seven-week training program
- 5:00 a.m. wake-up through 10:00 p.m. taps, no weekends, no breaks, virtually no family contact

> "You are challenged in a variety of ways in every developmental area — mentally, physically, militarily, and socially. The system will find your weaknesses, but that's the point — West Point toughens you."

### Who Makes It Through Beast?

- **Jerry Kagan** (1955): first psychologist to study dropouts — showed cadets cards, asked them to make up stories; colorful but had nothing to do with actual attrition
- **Mike Matthews** (West Point faculty): explained that admissions successfully identified those with *potential* to thrive
- **Whole Candidate Score**: weighted average of SAT/ACT, high school rank, leadership appraisals, physical fitness
- **Key finding**: Whole Candidate Score didn't reliably predict who made it through Beast — cadets with highest scores were just as likely to drop out as those with lowest
- Mike's own air force training had similar pattern: those who dropped out rarely did so from lack of ability — what mattered was a **"never give up" attitude**

### Interviews with High Achievers

- Interviewed leaders in business, art, athletics, journalism, academia, medicine, law
- Question: *What makes the people at the very top of your field special?*
- Field-specific characteristics emerged, but across fields, most successful people were lucky and talented
- Critical difference: high achievers stuck it out through setbacks; constantly driven to improve; were their own harshest critics — **paragons of perseverance**
- They were never satisfied, the opposite of complacent

### Two Components of Grit

- High achievers demonstrated **ferocious determination** in two ways:
  1. Unusually resilient and hardworking
  2. Knew in a very deep way what they wanted — had determination *and* **direction**

> "It was this combination of passion and perseverance that made high achievers special. In a word, they had grit."

### Developing the Grit Scale

- Created questions capturing what it means to have grit
- Half about **perseverance**: "I have overcome setbacks to conquer an important challenge"
- Half about **passion**: whether interests change year to year, whether obsessions are short-lived
- Result: **Grit Scale** — measures extent to which you approach life with grit

### Grit Scale Results at West Point

- July 2004: 1,218 cadets took Grit Scale on second day of Beast
- **Finding**: Grit scores bore absolutely no relationship to Whole Candidate Scores
- How talented a cadet was said nothing about their grit, and vice versa
- Among cadets who make it through Beast, Whole Candidate Score *is* a marvelous predictor of every metric tracked — so it's surprising that talent is no guarantee of grit

### Grit as Predictor of Beast Survival

- By last day of Beast, 71 cadets had dropped out
- **Grit was an astoundingly reliable predictor** of who made it through and who didn't
- Replicated next year: 62 cadets dropped out; grit again predicted who stayed
- What matters is not SAT scores, high school rank, leadership experience, or athletic ability — **what matters is grit**

### Grit Beyond West Point

- Does grit matter beyond West Point, or just in Beast's specific rigors?

#### Sales

- Tested in sales (daily/hourly rejection is par for the course)
- Hundreds of salespeople at vacation time-share company completed personality questionnaires including Grit Scale
- Six months later, 55% of salespeople were gone
- **Grit predicted who stayed and who left**
- No other commonly measured personality trait (extroversion, emotional stability, conscientiousness) was as effective in predicting job retention

#### Chicago Public Schools

- Thousands of high school juniors completed abbreviated Grit Scale plus other questionnaires
- Over a year later, 12% failed to graduate
- **Grittier students graduated** — more powerful predictor than how much students cared about school, conscientiousness, or even how safe they felt at school

#### Educational Attainment in American Adults

- Two large American samples: grittier adults more likely to get further in formal schooling
- Adults with MBA, PhD, MD, JD, or graduate degrees grittier than four-year college graduates
- Four-year college graduates grittier than those with some college credits but no degree
- Adults who earned degrees from two-year colleges scored slightly higher than four-year graduates
- Community college dropout rates can be as high as 80% — those who defy the odds are especially gritty

### Green Berets Selection Course

- Partnership with **Army Special Operations Forces** — training comes after nine weeks boot camp, four weeks infantry, three weeks airborne, four weeks land navigation
- **Special Forces Selection Course**: starting before dawn, full-throttle until 9 p.m.; runs/marches under 65-pound load; obstacle course "Nasty Nick"
- 42% of candidates voluntarily withdrew
- **Grit distinguished those who made it through**

### National Spelling Bee Study

- Documentary *Spellbound* released during author's first year of graduate school — follows kids preparing for **National Spelling Bee** finals
- Three-day affair in Washington DC, broadcast live on ESPN
- Called **Paige Kimble** (Bee's executive director, former champion) — agreed to send questionnaires to 273 spellers
- About two-thirds returned questionnaires (for $25 gift card); oldest 15, youngest 7
- On average, spellers practiced more than an hour/day on weekdays, more than two hours/day on weekends
- Winner **Anurag Kashyap** correctly spelled A-P-P-O-G-G-I-A-T-U-R-A (musical term for grace note)

#### Analysis Results

- Measurements of grit taken months before competition predicted how well spellers would eventually perform
- **Grittier kids went further** by studying many more hours and competing in more spelling bees
- Verbal intelligence also predicted getting further, but **no relationship between verbal IQ and grit**
- Verbally talented spellers did not study any more than less able spellers, nor did they have longer competition track records

### Ivy League Study

- Separate study on Ivy League undergraduates
- SAT scores and grit inversely correlated — students with higher SATs were, on average, slightly less gritty

> "Our potential is one thing. What we do with it is quite another."

## Chapter 2: Distracted by Talent

### Career Transition from McKinsey to Teaching

- Age 27, quit job at **McKinsey** to teach full-time
- Real reason: consulting wasn't the detour — throughout college she tutored/mentored kids, started tuition-free academic enrichment program, went to Oxford for neuroscience
- Teaching felt like being back on track

### Teaching on the Lower East Side

- Dramatic salary shift: from McKinsey expense-account sushi to a sandwich eaten while grading papers
- Students were twelve years old, lived in housing projects between Avenues A and D
- Job: teach seventh-grade math (fractions, decimals, rudimentary algebra and geometry)
- First week: some students picked up mathematical concepts more easily — "quick studies" who saw underlying patterns without prompting
- Without prompting, they saw the pattern in math problems that less able students struggled to grasp

#### The Surprise at Marking Period's End

- Some very able students weren't doing as well as expected — earned lackluster grades or worse
- In contrast, students who initially struggled were faring better than expected
- These "overachievers" reliably came to class with everything needed
- Instead of playing around and looking out window, they took notes and asked questions
- When they didn't get something first time, tried again and again, sometimes coming for extra help during lunch
- Their hard work showed in grades

> "I'd been distracted by talent."

- Began reflecting on how smart even weakest students sounded when talking about things that genuinely interested them
- Basketball statistics, song lyrics, complicated social plotlines — all had mastered complicated ideas in their daily lives
- Honestly, was getting *x* by itself in an algebraic equation all that much harder?

### David Luong's Story

- Quiet freshman in regular algebra track (not accelerated) at **Lowell High School** (San Francisco's merit-based public school)
- Every graded assignment: perfect work. When author marked answer incorrect, more often author's error
- Author marched David to department chair's office; switched to accelerated track
- After switch, grades dipped — first math test came back with a D

> "I did feel bad — I did — but I didn't dwell on it. I knew it was done. I knew I had to focus on what to do next. So I went to my teacher and asked for help."

- By senior year: taking harder of two honors calculus courses; earned perfect 5/5 on AP exam
- After Lowell: Swarthmore College (dual degrees in engineering and economics), PhD in mechanical engineering from UCLA
- Today: engineer at Aerospace Corporation — the boy deemed "not ready" for harder math is now a "rocket scientist"

### Francis Galton's Study of High Achievement

- **Francis Galton** (1869): first scientific study on origins of high achievement
- Concluded outliers are remarkable in three ways: unusual **"ability"**, exceptional **"zeal"**, and **"the capacity for hard labor"**
- His half cousin **Charles Darwin** responded:

> "You have made a convert of an opponent in one sense, for I have always maintained that, excepting fools, men did not differ much in intellect, only in zeal and hard work."

### Darwin's Self-Assessment

- Biographers don't claim supernatural intelligence — he was, in a sense, a plodder
- Darwin: "I have no great quickness of apprehension... My power to follow a long and purely abstract train of thought is very limited."
- Memory was subpar: couldn't remember dates or lines of poetry for more than a few days
- But praised his own power of observation and assiduousness:

> "I think I am superior to the common run of men in noticing things which easily escape attention, and in observing them carefully. My industry has been nearly as great as it could have been in the observation and collection of facts."

- One biographer: Darwin "deliberately did not engage in semi-willful forgetting. He kept all the questions alive at the back of his mind."

### William James on Human Potential

- Harvard psychologist **William James** (1907) wrote "The Energies of Men" for *Science*:

> "Compared with what we ought to be, we are only half awake. Our fires are damped, our drafts are checked. We are making use of only a small part of our possible mental and physical resources."

> "The plain fact remains that men the world over possess amounts of resource, which only very exceptional individuals push to their extremes of use."

### The Naturalness Bias

- National surveys: Americans favor effort over talent (2:1), endorse "hardworking" over "intelligence" (5:1)
- Yet actions reveal a hidden **naturalness bias** — preferring those perceived as innately talented

**Chia-Jung Tsay's experiments**:
- Musicians heard identical piano performances; one pianist described as "natural," the other as "striver" — musicians judged the "natural" more likely to succeed and more hirable
- Business professionals evaluated identical proposals; "natural" entrepreneur rated higher
- Striver needed 4 more years leadership experience + $40,000 more startup capital to match natural's rating

> "What we say we care about may not correspond with what — deep down — we actually believe to be more valuable."

- Tsay herself: practiced 4-6 hours daily throughout childhood; performed at Carnegie Hall, Lincoln Center, Kennedy Center — attributes success to dedication, not natural talent

### McKinsey and "The War for Talent"

- McKinsey report became bestselling book: companies rise and fall based on ability to attract "A players"
- McKinsey culture prized analytic ability/"being bright" — recruited from Harvard, Stanford
- Author's interview: brainteasers testing analytic mettle ("How many tennis balls manufactured in US per year?")

**Critics of the talent mindset**:
- **Duff McDonald**: Suggested alternative title "The War on Common Sense" — highlighted companies didn't perform well afterward
- **Malcolm Gladwell**: **Enron** epitomized the approach — named America's Most Innovative Company 6 consecutive years, filed for bankruptcy in 2001 (largest corporate bankruptcy at the time)
- CEO **Jeff Skilling** (former McKinsey consultant) developed "rank-and-yank" — graded employees annually, fired bottom 15%
- Created narcissistic culture rewarding deception and discouraging integrity

### Scott Barry Kaufman's Story

- Psychologist at UPenn — degrees from Carnegie Mellon, Cambridge, Yale
- As child, considered a **slow learner**: many ear infections led to processing problems
- Repeated third grade; sent to special school for children with learning disabilities
- **Age 14 turning point**: observant teacher asked why he wasn't in more challenging classes
- For first time wondered: *"Who am I? Am I a learning disabled kid with no real future? Or maybe something else?"*
- Signed up for every challenge — Latin class, school musical, choir
- Picked up cello: practiced 8-9 hours a day that first summer; by fall, earned seat in orchestra; by senior year, second chair
- Wanted to join gifted/talented program but blocked by childhood IQ scores
- School psychologist drew bell curve on napkin: "And this is where you are"

> "At what point does achievement trump potential?"

- Applied to Carnegie Mellon's cognitive science program — rejected (likely due to SAT scores)

> "I had this grit. I said, 'I'm going to do it. I don't care. I'm going to find a way to study what I want to study.'"

- Auditioned for Carnegie Mellon's opera program; transferred major to psychology; graduated Phi Beta Kappa

### Key Conclusion

- Focus on talent distracts from something at least as important — **effort**
- **As much as talent counts, effort counts twice**

## Chapter 3: Effort Counts Twice

### The Pervasiveness of "Talent"

- Talent mentioned constantly in newspapers (sports, business, entertainment, politics)
- When anyone accomplishes something noteworthy, we rush to anoint them "extraordinarily talented"
- **If we overemphasize talent, we underemphasize everything else**
- Radio commentator example: Compared Hillary and Bill Clinton — Bill is "natural" politician, Hillary merely a "striver," implication she'll never be his equal

### Dan Chambliss Study: "The Mundanity of Excellence"

- **Dan Chambliss** (sociologist) studied competitive swimmers for six years — interviewing, watching, sometimes living and traveling with swimmers and coaches at all levels (local swim club to Olympic team)

> "Superlative performance is really a confluence of dozens of small skills or activities, each one learned or stumbled upon, which have been carefully drilled into habit and then are fitted together in a synthesized whole. There is nothing extraordinary or superhuman in any one of those actions; only the fact that they are done consistently and correctly, and all together, produce excellence."

- Talent viewed as invisible "substance behind the surface reality of performance" — great athletes seem blessed "with a special gift, almost a 'thing' inside of them, denied to the rest of us"
- **When we can't easily see how experience and training produced excellence, we default to labeling that person a "natural"**
- Most accomplished swimmers had parents interested in sport, earned enough for coaching/travel/pool access
- Crucially: thousands of hours of practice refining individual elements
- **Time-lapse film metaphor**: If you had film of hours/days/weeks/years that produced excellence, you could see what Chambliss saw — high level of performance is accretion of mundane acts

> **"Greatness is doable. Greatness is many, many individual feats, and each of them is doable."**

### Mark Spitz and Rowdy Gaines

- **Mark Spitz**: 7 gold medals in 1972 Olympics
- **Rowdy Gaines**: held world record in 100-meter free 12 years after Spitz retired
- 1984: Spitz (mid-thirties) and Gaines did sprints — Gaines won most, but entire team gathered to watch Spitz
- One swimmer, pointing to Spitz: **"My god. He's a fish."**
- Even Chambliss, student of mundanity, was lulled into talent explanations

### Nietzsche on Talent and Effort

> "With everything perfect, we do not ask how it came to be. Instead, we rejoice in the present fact as though it came out of the ground by magic."

- We *want* to believe Mark Spitz was born to swim — prefer excellence fully formed, **prefer mystery to mundanity**

> "Our vanity, our self-love, promotes the cult of the genius. For if we think of genius as something magical, we are not obliged to compare ourselves and find ourselves lacking.... To call someone 'divine' means: 'here there is no need to compete.'"

- **Mythologizing natural talent lets us all off the hook** — lets us relax into the status quo

- What about talent? Nietzsche implored us to consider craftsmen:

> "Do not talk about giftedness, inborn talents! One can name great men of all kinds who were very little gifted. They *acquired* greatness.... They all possessed that seriousness of the efficient workman which first learns to construct the parts properly before it ventures to fashion a great whole."

### Developing the Theory (Marty Seligman)

- **Marty Seligman** (author's advisor) — early research led to understanding of clinical depression; christened field of **Positive Psychology**
- Second-year meeting: **"You haven't had a good idea in two years."**
- "You don't have a theory. You don't have a theory for the psychology of achievement."
- **"Stop reading so much and go think."**
- Author left office and cried; eventually sat down and realized she hadn't worked out how talent, effort, skill, and achievement fit together

### The Two Equations

After more than a decade of thinking, published two simple equations:

```
talent x effort = skill
skill x effort = achievement
```

- **Talent** is how quickly your skills improve when you invest effort
- **Achievement** is what happens when you take acquired skills and use them
- Effort factors into calculations **twice**, not once
- **Effort builds skill. At the very same time, effort makes skill productive.**

### Warren MacKenzie Example

- **Warren MacKenzie** (celebrated potter, 92 years old): at craft without interruption for nearly entire adult life
- Early on, tried many different things with late wife — eventually gave up drawing, painting, silk-screening, textile design and concentrated on ceramics
- A good potter can make 40-50 pots in a day; only a few worth selling

> "I think back to some of the pots we made when we first started our pottery, and they were pretty awful pots."

- "The first 10,000 pots are difficult, and then it gets a little bit easier."
- With effort, skill improved (talent x effort = skill); at same time, more good pots brought into world (skill x effort = achievement)

### John Irving Example

- **John Irving**: lauded as "the great storyteller of American literature today" — dozen novels, most bestsellers, National Book Award, Academy Award for *Cider House Rules*
- Unlike fictional protagonist Garp, **Irving was not a natural**
- Earned C- in high school English; SAT verbal 475/800; needed extra year for enough credits
- Teachers thought he was "lazy" and "stupid" — he was severely **dyslexic**

> "I was an underdog.... If my classmates could read our history assignment in an hour, I allowed myself two or three."

- When own son diagnosed with dyslexia, finally understood his own struggles

> "In my case, I learned that I just had to pay twice as much attention. I came to appreciate that in doing something over and over again, something that was never natural becomes almost second nature."

> "One reason I have confidence in writing the kind of novels I write is that I have confidence in my stamina to go over something again and again no matter how difficult it is."

### Will Smith on Effort

> "I've never really viewed myself as particularly talented. Where I excel is ridiculous, sickening work ethic."

> "The only thing that I see that is distinctly different about me is: I'm not afraid to die on a treadmill. I will not be outworked, period. You might have more talent than me, you might be smarter than me, you might be sexier than me... But if we get on the treadmill together, there's two things: You're getting off first, or I'm going to die."

> "The separation of talent and skill is one of the greatest misunderstood concepts for people who are trying to excel.... Talent you have naturally. Skill is only developed by hours and hours and hours of beating on your craft."

### Harvard Treadmill Test (1940)

- Study designed to understand "characteristics of healthy young men" and "help people live happier, more successful lives"
- 130 Harvard sophomores asked to run on treadmill for up to five minutes
- Treadmill set at steep angle and cranked up to fast speed — average man held on for only four minutes
- Some lasted only a minute and a half
- By design, not just physically but mentally exhausting
- By measuring and adjusting for baseline physical fitness, researchers designed test to gauge **"stamina and strength of will"**

### George Vaillant's Follow-Up

- **George Vaillant** followed up on the 1940 test, tracking men from age 20 into their 60s
- Decades of data: income, career advancement, sick days, activities, relationships, psychiatric visits, mood-altering drug use
- **Key finding**: Run time at age 20 was a surprisingly reliable predictor of psychological adjustment throughout adulthood
- Adjusting for baseline physical fitness "had little effect on the correlation of running time with mental health"

> "When it comes to how we fare in the marathon of life, effort counts tremendously."

- Vaillant himself exemplifies grit — revived the abandoned Harvard study, traveled globally to interview subjects, now in 80s writing fourth book on what became longest continuous study of human development
- Admits he's not particularly persistent in small tasks (crossword puzzles, household repairs)
- His grit: **constant and persistent focus on the Harvard study** over decades
- "There is nothing more interesting than watching people grow"
- As prep school varsity track athlete: could do more chin-ups than anyone, not because of talent but because "I *did* a lot of chin-ups. I practiced"

### Woody Allen on Showing Up

> "My observation was that once a person actually completed a play or a novel he was well on his way to getting it produced or published, as opposed to a vast majority of people who tell me their ambition is to write, but who strike out on the very first level and indeed never write the play or book."

> "Eighty percent of success in life is showing up." — **Woody Allen**

### Staying on the Treadmill = Grit

- Author's hypothetical: let men retry the Treadmill Test voluntarily
- Some would return to try again; others content with first effort
- Some might ask for strategies to last longer, interested in a third or fourth try
- Would create a **grit score based on voluntary returns**
- **Staying on the treadmill is one thing** — committing even when uncomfortable
- **Getting back on the next day, eager to try again** — more reflective of grit
- When you permanently turn your back on a commitment, effort plummets to zero and skills stop improving

### The Treadmill as Metaphor

- ~40% of people who buy home exercise equipment use it less than expected
- Bigger impediment: **stopping working out altogether** vs. how hard we push in a single session
- Consistency of effort over the long run is everything
- Examples of quitting too early: exercise equipment gathering dust, kids quitting sports before season ends, unfinished knitting projects, abandoned vegetable gardens
- **Many quit what we start far too early and far too often**
- What matters: waking up the next day ready to get back on that treadmill and keep going

### The Math of Effort

- Someone twice as talented but half as hardworking might reach the same skill level but produce dramatically less
- Without effort, talent = unmet potential; without effort, skill = what you could have done but didn't
- **With effort, talent becomes skill and effort makes skill productive**

## Chapter 4: How Gritty Are You?

### Grit as Stamina, Not Intensity

- Young Wharton entrepreneur raised thousands, pulled all-nighters
- Author: "Grit is more about stamina than intensity"
- **Skipping from one pursuit to another — from one skill set to an entirely different one — that's not what gritty people do**
- No shortcuts to excellence — developing real expertise takes time
- **"It's doing what you love, but not just falling in love — *staying* in love"**

### The Grit Scale

- 10-item questionnaire; compare yourself to "most people"
- Odd-numbered items measure **passion** (consistency of interests)
- Even-numbered items measure **perseverance** (resilience and hard work)
- Score range: 1 (not at all gritty) to 5 (extremely gritty)
- American adult norms: 50th percentile = 3.8, 80th = 4.3, 95th = 4.7
- **Perseverance scores typically slightly higher than passion scores**
- Author's example: perseverance = 5.0, passion = 4.2

### Passion = Consistency, Not Intensity

- Grit Scale passion questions don't ask how *intensely* you're committed
- High achievers talk about commitment as **consistency over time**
- Examples: chefs fascinated with cooking from childhood to adulthood; investors curious about markets for decades; mathematicians working the same problem day and night for years
- **Enthusiasm is common. Endurance is rare.**

### Jeffrey Gettleman's Passion Journey

- **Jeffrey Gettleman**: East Africa bureau chief for *NYT*, 2012 Pulitzer Prize
- Passion emerged over years through **active construction**, not passive discovery
- At 18: majored in philosophy at Cornell; summer after freshman year visited East Africa — "This place just blew my mind"
- Took Swahili courses, backpacked around world, returned to East Africa
- Professor: "That is the dumbest idea I had heard"
- Second year at Oxford: figured out journalism was the fit
- Set out **very deliberate path**: A (Oxford paper) → B (small Wisconsin paper) → C (St. Petersburg Times) → D (LA Times) → E (NYT national) → F (overseas war stories) → G (NYT East Africa bureau chief, 2006)

> "It was a really winding road... But eventually, I got here. I got exactly where I wanted to be."

### Passion as Compass

- Passion is not fireworks (intense but brief)
- **Passion = compass** — takes time to build, tinker with, finally get right; then guides you on the long winding road

### Goal Hierarchy

- **Pete Carroll** (Seattle Seahawks coach): Fired as head coach of New England Patriots; friend urged "You've got to have a philosophy"; devoured books of **John Wooden**; developed philosophy: "Do things better than they have ever been done before"
- Top-level goal: abstract, general, important — an end in itself
- Mid-level goals serve the top-level goal
- Low-level goals: concrete, specific — merely means to ends
- At each level, ask "Why?" until the answer is "Just because!" — that's the top of the hierarchy
- Top-level goal = **"ultimate concern"**

### Tom Seaver's Top-Level Goal

- **Tom Seaver**: Hall of Fame pitcher (311 wins, 3,640 strikeouts, 98.8% Hall of Fame vote)
- Goal: **pitch "the best I possibly can day after day, year after year"**

> "Pitching... determines what I eat, when I go to bed, what I do when I'm awake.... If it means in the winter I eat cottage cheese instead of chocolate chip cookies in order to keep my weight down, then I eat cottage cheese."

### Passion = Caring About the Same Ultimate Goal

- Not capricious
- Each day, wake up thinking of questions you fell asleep thinking about
- Pointing in same direction, eager to take smallest step forward vs. step to the side toward other destination
- Focus might be called obsessive
- Most actions derive significance from allegiance to ultimate concern — your life philosophy

### Unified Goal Hierarchy and Its Enemies

- Very gritty people: most mid-level and low-level goals related to ultimate goal
- Lack of grit: less coherent goal structures

**Examples of low grit:**
1. **Positive fantasizing** (**Gabriele Oettingen**): indulging in visions of positive future without figuring out how to get there — short-term payoff (feeling great) but long-term disappointment
2. Bunch of mid-level goals with no unifying top-level goal (scattered clusters)
3. Several competing goal hierarchies unconnected with each other (multiple separate trees)

### Goal Conflict is Normal

- Some goal conflict is a necessary feature of human existence
- Author: one hierarchy for work (*use psychological science to help kids thrive*), another for being best mother to two daughters
- Working parents know: never enough time, energy, attention
- **One top-level *professional* goal, rather than any other number, is ideal**
- The more unified, aligned, and coordinated our goal hierarchies, the better

### Warren Buffett's Three-Step Prioritization

- Three steps: (1) Write down 25 career goals, (2) Circle 5 highest-priority, (3) The other 20 — **avoid at all costs**; they distract and eat away time/energy
- Author tried the exercise; realized many goals were means to one ultimate goal: *helping kids achieve and thrive*
- Additional step: **To what extent do these goals serve a common purpose?**

### Low-Level Goals and Flexibility

- Grit is NOT about stubbornly pursuing every single low-level goal
- **Don't beat your head against the wall attempting to follow through on something that is merely a means to a more important end**
- **The higher the goal, the more stubborn you should be**
- Highest-level goal gets written in ink; lower-level goals written in pencil

### Bob Mankoff's Journey to The New Yorker

- **Bob Mankoff**: attended LaGuardia High School of Music and Art; exposure to elite talent intimidated him — stopped drawing for three years
- Studied philosophy and psychology at Syracuse University
- Senior year discovered **Syd Hoff**'s *Learning to Cartoon*; created 27 cartoons, universally rejected
- Received draft notice for Vietnam; repurposed as graduate student in experimental psychology
- While running rats in mazes, drew when possible — realized his defining characteristic was being *funny*
- Tried both stand-up comedy and cartooning; dropped stand-up, devoted energy to cartooning
- After two years of submitting to *New Yorker*: "all I had to show for it were enough rejection slips to wallpaper my bathroom"
- Top-level goal crystallized: not just funny for a living, but among the best cartoonists in the world
- Went to New York Public Library, examined every *New Yorker* cartoon back to 1925
- **Key insight**: all cartoons made the reader **think**; every cartoonist had a personal, distinctive style
- Worked through various styles, developed his **dot style** (stippling) — originally tried in high school after discovering **Georges Seurat**
- After getting rejected **~2,000 times between 1974 and 1977**, first cartoon accepted
- Next year: 13 cartoons sold; then 25; then 27; then contract cartoonist

### Roz Chast and Rejection

- **Roz Chast**: celebrated *New Yorker* cartoonist with ~90% rejection rate
- Contract cartoonists collectively submit ~500 cartoons/week; only room for ~17 per issue
- **Rejection rate of more than 96%**

### Catharine Cox's Study of Historical Geniuses

- **Catharine Cox** (Stanford, 1926): catalogued characteristics of **301 exceptionally accomplished historical figures** across four centuries
- Estimated childhood IQ from biographical evidence of intellectual precocity

**IQ findings:**
- **Smartest**: philosopher **John Stuart Mill** (IQ 190) — learned Greek at 3, wrote history of Rome at 6
- **Least intelligent** (IQs 100-110): **Copernicus**, **Faraday**, **Cervantes**; **Isaac Newton** estimated at 130

**The unexpected finding:**
- Average childhood IQ of most eminent geniuses (First Ten): 146
- Average IQ of least eminent (Last Ten): 143
- Relationship between intelligence and eminence was **exceedingly slight**

**First Ten (Most Eminent)**: Bacon, Napoleon, Burke, Goethe, Luther, Milton, Newton, Pitt, Voltaire, Washington

**What did matter: "Persistence of motive"**
- Cox evaluated **67 personality traits**; most showed trivial differences
- **Cluster of four indicators** set the eminent apart — two mapped to passion, two to perseverance:
  1. Works with distant objects in view; active preparation for later life; working toward a definite goal
  2. Tendency not to abandon tasks from mere changeability
  3. Degree of strength of will or perseverance; quiet determination
  4. Tendency not to abandon tasks in face of obstacles; doggedness

> "High but not the highest intelligence, combined with the greatest degree of persistence, will achieve greater eminence than the highest degree of intelligence with somewhat less persistence." — Catharine Cox

## Chapter 5: Grit Grows

### The Nature-Nurture Question

- "How much of our grit is in our genes?" Short answer: "in part"
- **Every human trait is influenced by both genes and experience**

### Height as Analogy

- Height is heritable, yet average height has increased dramatically in a few generations
- British military: average man was 5'5" 150 years ago, now 5'10"
- Netherlands: average man now almost 6'1" — gain of more than 6 inches in 150 years
- Gene pool didn't change that dramatically; most powerful boosters: **nutrition, clean air/water, modern medicine**

### Twin Studies on Grit

- London study: Grit Scale administered to 2,000+ pairs of teenage twins in UK
- **Heritability estimates**: perseverance subscale 37%, passion subscale 20%
- On par with other personality traits
- **No single gene for grit** — almost all traits are **polygenic** (height influenced by at least 697 genes)

### Talents Are Both Nature and Nurture

- **Dan Chambliss** example: swam competitively in high school, had physical limitations (small, ankles won't plantar flex)
- Bigger impediment was coaching: "horribly bad coaches in crucial places... literally taught me zero"
- Years later with good coaching (national/Olympic coaches): swam 200-yard individual medley as fast as in high school

### The Flynn Effect

- **Flynn effect**: Named after **Jim Flynn**, New Zealand social scientist
- Startling gains in IQ scores over past century
- On most widely used IQ tests: average increased more than 15 points in last 50 years across 30+ countries
- If scored people a century ago against modern norms: average IQ of 70 (borderline mentally retarded)
- If scored today against norms from century ago: average IQ of 130 (typical cutoff for gifted programs)
- Author initially didn't believe it: "How could it be that we're all getting that much smarter so quickly?"

### Meeting Jim Flynn

- Jim flew to Philadelphia to meet author and give talk
- Tall, bony, wire-rimmed glasses — "looked like a caricature of an academic"
- Biggest gains on tests assessing **abstract reasoning**
- Example: "Dogs and rabbits: How are they alike?" — modern children say "both mammals"; century ago: "Dogs chase rabbits" (zero points)
- **As a species, we're getting better at abstract reasoning**

### The Social Multiplier Effect

- Flynn's explanation: **television made basketball popular** → more kids played → each kid enriched learning environment for others → skills multiplied socially
- Same logic for abstract reasoning: jobs/schools ask us to think analytically more; small differences trigger virtuous cycles; **effects multiplied socially through culture**

### Grit Increases with Age

- Grit Scale scores vary by age: **grittiest adults** in late sixties or older; **least gritty** in twenties

### Two Explanations for Age Trends

1. **Cultural era** ("Reverse Flynn effect" for grit): Adults in their seventh decade grittier because grew up in very different cultural era emphasizing sustained passion and perseverance — Greatest Generation grittier than millennials because cultural forces were different

2. **Maturation story**: We *develop* the capacity for long-term passion and perseverance as we get older
   - Author's experience and stories of grit paragons (Jeff Gettleman, Bob Mankoff) suggest grit grows as we figure out life philosophy, learn to dust ourselves off after rejection, distinguish between low-level goals to abandon vs. higher-level goals demanding tenacity
   - To distinguish these explanations definitively, would need longitudinal study (like George Vaillant's Harvard study)
   - Grit Scale hasn't been around long enough for a time-lapse movie over the life course

### The Maturity Principle

- Longitudinal personality research: **most of us become more conscientious, confident, caring, and calm with life experience**
- Much change happens between ages 20 and 40, but no epoch where personality stops evolving

### How Life Experiences Change Personality

1. **Learning through trial and error**: discovering that repeatedly swapping career ambitions is unfulfilling
2. **Insights from others**: John Irving — "something that was never natural becomes almost second nature"
3. **Changing circumstances**: new situations call on us to act differently; we adapt
4. **Necessity is the mother of adaptation**

### Examples of the Maturity Principle

- **Lucy's potty training**: Author's daughter reached age 3 without learning; moved to "big kid" classroom with no changing table — immediately announced she was done with diapers
- **Bernie Noe's daughter**: Headmaster of Lakeside School in Seattle; daughter late to school every day; got job at American Eagle; store manager: "The first time you're late, you're fired" — **"most immediate behavior change I've ever seen"**; suddenly setting two alarms
- **Lectures don't have half the effect of consequences**

### Summary of Maturity Principle

- Over time, we learn life lessons we don't forget, and we adapt in response to growing demands of circumstances
- New ways of thinking and acting become habitual
- Comes a day when we can hardly remember our immature former selves
- We've adapted, adaptations become durable, and finally our identity has evolved. We've matured.

### Both Stories Likely True

- Data consistent with both stories (cultural era and maturation); both likely true to some extent
- Either way: **grit is not entirely fixed**
- Like every aspect of psychological character, **grit is more plastic than you might think**

### Why People Quit — and Why Grit Paragons Don't

- People email author daily wishing they had more grit — lament never sticking with anything, feel they've squandered talents
- Common wrong answers: "I guess I'm just lazy," "I'm just a flake," "I'm congenitally incapable of sticking with things" — **all wrong**

When people drop out, they do so for *different* reasons. Any of these thoughts might go through your head before you quit:
- "I'm bored."
- "The effort isn't worth it."
- "This isn't important to me."
- "I can't do this, so I might as well give up."

Paragons of grit quit goals too — but **the higher the level, the more stubborn they are**. Most important: **paragons don't swap compasses**.

### Four Psychological Assets of Grit Paragons

Four assets develop over years in particular order:

1. **Interest**: Passion begins with intrinsically enjoying what you do. "I love what I do!"

2. **Practice**: Daily discipline of trying to do things better than yesterday. Must zero in on weaknesses, hours a day, week after month after year. "Whatever it takes, I want to improve!"

3. **Purpose**: Conviction that your work matters. Interest without purpose nearly impossible to sustain for a lifetime. "My work is important — both to me and to others."

4. **Hope**: Rising-to-the-occasion perseverance. NOT just the last stage — defines **every stage**. **If we stay down, grit loses. If we get up, grit prevails.**

**These are not "you have it or you don't" commodities:**
- You can learn to discover, develop, and deepen your interests
- You can acquire the habit of discipline
- You can cultivate a sense of purpose and meaning
- You can teach yourself to hope
- **You can grow your grit from the inside out.**

## Chapter 6: Interest

### The "Follow Your Passion" Problem

- "Follow your passion" dominates commencement speeches
- **Will Shortz** (NYT crossword editor): "Figure out what you enjoy doing most in life, and then try to do it full-time"
- **Jeff Bezos**: Left high-salary finance job to start Amazon; "if you're not passionate about it, you won't be able to stick with it"
- **Hester Lacey** (FT journalist, interviewing achievers since 2011): consistent finding is "I love what I do"

### The Counter-Argument: Practicality

- Duckworth's father advised practicality over passion — family business assigned him chemistry (not history); thrived at DuPont for 35 years

### Scientific Evidence for Interest Matching

**Two meta-analyses support passion:**
1. **Job satisfaction**: People enormously more satisfied when work fits personal interests (~100 studies)
2. **Performance**: People perform better when intrinsic interests align with work (60 studies over 60 years) — better at jobs, help coworkers more, stay longer

**2014 Gallup poll**: More than 2/3 of US adults not engaged at work; worldwide only 13% "engaged"

### The Myth of the Epiphany

- Duckworth initially assumed grit paragons had singular epiphany moments
- **Julia Child**: *sole meunière* moment was revelation, but followed by **succession of interest-stimulating experiences** — meals, friendships, cookbooks, Le Cordon Bleu classes
- Julia later: "To think it has taken me forty years to find my true passion"

### Examples of Extended Discovery

- **Rowdy Gaines**: Tried multiple sports before swimming; "kept plugging away" until finding something to fall in love with
- **Marc Vetri** (James Beard Award chef): Equally interested in music and cooking as teenager; eventually chose cooking
- **Reddit post**: Early-thirties person paralyzed by too many interests, can't commit to anything

### Barry Schwartz on Career Discovery

- **Barry Schwartz** (Swarthmore psychologist): Compares career discovery to finding romantic partner — young people hold out for perfection

> "It's really the same problem a lot of young people have finding a romantic partner. They want somebody who's really attractive and smart and kind and empathetic and thoughtful and funny."

- "Meeting a potential match — not the one-and-only perfect match, but a promising one — is only the very beginning"

### The Psychology of Interest Evolution

**Interest development is: Discovery → Development → Deepening** (not sudden epiphany)

#### Discovery Phase
1. **Childhood too early** for knowing what you want — vocational interests begin around **middle school**
2. **Interests not discovered through introspection** — triggered by interactions with outside world; process is messy, serendipitous
   - **Jeff Bezos**: "One of the huge mistakes people make is that they try to *force* an interest on themselves"
3. **Initial discovery often goes unnoticed** — asking "Have I found my passion?" every few days is premature

#### Development Phase
4. **Initial triggering must be followed by repeated encounters** that retrigger attention
   - **Mike Hopkins** (NASA astronaut): Space shuttle launches on TV sparked interest, but it was several over years plus digging for NASA info
   - **Warren MacKenzie**: Ceramics class → *A Potter's Book* by Bernard Leach → year-long internship with Leach
5. **Interests thrive with encouragement** from parents, teachers, coaches, peers

**Example: Marc Vetri**
- Never worked hard at academics: "This is kind of boring"
- Sunday afternoons at Sicilian grandmother's — by age eleven wanted to cook at home
- Part-time dishwashing job: loved kitchen camaraderie — "everyone is nice, and they like me"

### Passion as Fragile Early Interest

> "Is it 'a drag' that passions don't come to us all at once, as epiphanies, without the need to actively develop them? Maybe. But the reality is that our early interests are fragile, vaguely defined, and in need of energetic, years-long cultivation and refinement."

### Hard Work Still Required

- **Amy Chua** ("Tiger Mom"): "Just because you love something doesn't mean you'll be great. Not if you don't work"
- Duckworth's message: **Before hard work comes play** — before diligent skill-honing, must goof around triggering and retriggering interest
- At earliest stage, novices aren't obsessed with getting better — they're **having fun**

### Benjamin Bloom's Research on the Early Years

- Interviewed 120 world-class achievers plus parents/coaches/teachers
- Skill development progresses through **three stages**, each lasting several years
- **"Early years"**: Interests discovered and developed; encouragement crucial
- Best mentors at this stage: warm and supportive

> "Perhaps the major quality of these teachers was that they made the initial learning very pleasant and rewarding. Much of the introduction to the field was as playful activity." — Bloom

- **Autonomy during early years important** — overbearing parents/teachers erode intrinsic motivation
- Kids whose parents let them make own choices about what they like **more likely to develop lasting passions**

### Sports Sampling (Jean Cote)

- **Jean Cote** (sports psychologist): Professional athletes who sampled variety of sports as children **fare better long-term**
- Early breadth provides "cross-train" opportunity
- Athletes who skip this stage often enjoy early advantage but **more likely to become injured and burn out**

### Different Needs for Beginners vs. Experts

- At start: need encouragement, freedom, small wins, applause
- Can handle criticism and corrective feedback, but **not too much and not too soon**

> "Rush a beginner and you'll bludgeon their budding interest. It's very, very hard to get that back once you do."

### Case Study: Will Shortz

- Mother was "writer and lover of words"; grandmother was crossword fan
- Came across puzzle book as child, "entranced by it" — first book followed by many more
- **Mother's role crucial**: bought books, showed him how to make puzzles during bridge club, encouraged him to sell puzzles to magazines
- **Sold first puzzle at fourteen**, regular contributor to Dell puzzle magazines at sixteen
- Mother also got guitar from neighbor — sat untouched for nine months; "I had no interest in playing it"
- Mother found individualized program at Indiana University — **only person with college degree in enigmatology** (study of puzzles)

### Case Study: Jeff Bezos

- Unusually interest-filled childhood due to unusually curious mother, **Jackie**
- Jackie had Jeff at seventeen: "I didn't have a lot of preconceived notions"
- Followed children's lead: "I felt it was my responsibility to let them do deep dives into what they enjoyed"

**Examples of Jackie's approach:**
- Age three: Jeff asked to sleep in "big bed" — Jackie helped him disassemble crib with screwdriver
- Middle school: Jeff inventing contraptions, alarm on bedroom door; many RadioShack trips ("Sometimes we'd go back four times in a day")
- Jeff built **infinity cube** — Jackie listened and asked questions; friend asked if she understood — Jackie: "It's not important that I understand everything. It's important that I listen"
- High school: Jeff turned family garage into laboratory; Jackie found professor letting him experiment after skipping classes — "Okay, I got it. Now, let's see if we can negotiate a legal way to do that"
- College: computer science/electrical engineering; built Internet bookstore named after longest river (also registered www.relentless.com)

### Continuous Learning and Career Stability

- **Will Shortz**: "I'm always learning, always stretching my brain in a new way"

> "If you're bored with puzzles, you're bored with life, because they're so diverse."

- **Grittier individuals make fewer career changes** (large-scale studies)
- Some habitually throw themselves into new projects with fierce interest, only to move on after 3-5 years — "short-termers"

### Jane Golden: Novelty Within Commitment

- **Jane Golden** (Mural Arts Program director, Philadelphia, 30+ years) — 3,600+ buildings converted into murals
- When she stopped painting: discovered growing the program could itself be creative

> "I'm like an artist who looks at the sky every morning and sees a variety of really brilliant colors where other people would just see blue or gray."

### The Emotion of Interest (Paul Silvia)

- **Paul Silvia** (psychologist): Interest = desire to learn new things, explore world, seek novelty — basic drive
- Babies need this drive because unlike other animals, they know almost nothing at birth

#### Experts vs. Beginners: Different Forms of Novelty
- **Beginner novelty**: Anything not encountered before
- **Expert novelty**: *Nuance*
- Modern art example: pieces seem similar to novice, very different to expert
- Olympics commentators perceive microscopic differences invisible to untrained eye

### Advice for Fostering Passion

**If you haven't fostered passion yet, begin at discovery:**
- *What do I like to think about? Where does my mind wander? What do I really care about? How do I enjoy spending my time? What do I find absolutely unbearable?*
- Try recalling teen years (when vocational interests commonly sprout)

**Will Shortz's Rules (adapted):**
1. **Begin with the answers you're surest of** — some things you know you'd hate; some more promising
2. **Don't be afraid to guess** — trial and error inherent in discovery; many potential passions, not just one "right" one
3. **Don't be afraid to erase an answer that isn't working out** — work in pencil until you know for sure

**After discovery, development:**
- Interests must be triggered again and again and again
- Have patience; keep asking questions; seek out people who share interests; sidle up to encouraging mentor

**Finally, deepening:**
- Brain craves novelty — tempted to move on to something new
- To stay engaged for years, need to **enjoy the nuances only a true aficionado can appreciate**

> "The old in the new is what claims the attention. The old with a slightly new turn." — William James

**Conclusion**: "Follow your passion" is not bad advice, but even more useful to understand **how passions are fostered in the first place**.
## Chapter 7: Practice

### Practice Matters More Than Time on Task

- Early research: grittier kids at the National Spelling Bee **practiced more** than less gritty competitors -- extra practice hours explained superior performance
- As math teacher, Duckworth observed huge effort range -- some kids spent zero minutes/week on homework, others studied hours daily
- Gritty people stick with commitments longer -- seemed like major grit advantage was simply **more time on task**

### The Plateau Problem

- But many people rack up decades of experience and stagnate at middling competence
- Some get twenty years of experience; others get one year of experience twenty times in a row
- **Kaizen** (Japanese): "Continuous improvement" -- resisting the plateau of arrested development
- Grit paragons all exude kaizen, no exceptions

### Hard Work Isn't Enough Without Interest

- Parents sometimes misunderstand grit as just **perseverance** -- get appreciative nods
- **But**: Nobody works doggedly on something they don't find intrinsically interesting
- Heads stop nodding, cock to side when told this
- **Amy Chua**: "Just because you love something doesn't mean you'll be great. Not if you don't work. Most people stink at the things they love."
- Most people stink even *more* at what they *don't* love

> "Before hard work comes play. Before those who've yet to fix on a passion are ready to spend hours a day diligently honing skills, they must goof around, triggering and retriggering interest."

- At the earliest stage, novices aren't obsessed with getting better -- they're **having fun**

### Experts Start as Unserious Beginners (Benjamin Bloom)

- Skill development progresses through **three stages**, each lasting several years
- Interests discovered in the **"early years"** -- best mentors are especially warm and supportive

> "Perhaps the major quality of these teachers was that they made the initial learning very pleasant and rewarding. Much of the introduction to the field was as playful activity."

- **Degree of autonomy important** -- longitudinal studies confirm overbearing parents/teachers erode intrinsic motivation
- Kids whose parents let them make own choices about what they like **more likely to develop interests later identified as passion**
- Duckworth's dad in Shanghai in 1950 didn't think twice about father assigning career path -- most young people today find it difficult to "own" interests decided without their input

### Sampling vs. Early Specialization (Jean Cote)

- **Jean Cote** (sports psychologist): shortcutting relaxed, playful discovery stage has **dire consequences**
- Professional athletes like Rowdy Gaines who sampled variety of sports as children generally **fare much better in long run**
- Early breadth provides opportunity to **"cross-train" muscles and skills** that complement more focused training
- Athletes who skip this stage often enjoy early advantage but are **more likely to become injured and to burn out**

### Quality vs. Quantity of Practice

- **Journalist Hester Lacey** interviews "mega successful" people -- all demonstrate desire to excel beyond current expertise
- Not complacency but *positive* forward-looking mindset wanting to grow

> "An actor might say, 'I may never play a role perfectly, but I want to do it as well as I possibly can. And in every role, I want to bring something new.'" -- Hester Lacey

- Grit involves both quantity (more time on task) and quality (better time on task)

### Anders Ericsson and Deliberate Practice

- **Ericsson**: cognitive psychologist studying expert skill acquisition across chess, music, athletics, and more
- Studied Olympic athletes, chess grandmasters, concert pianists, prima ballerinas, PGA golfers, Scrabble champions, radiologists
- German music academy study: elite violinists accumulated ~10,000 hours of practice over ten years; less accomplished students about half
- **Dancer Martha Graham**: "It takes about ten years to make a mature dancer"
- Telegraph operators: reaching fluency in Morse code requires ten years (~1900)
- The "ten-thousand-hour rule" and "ten-year-rule" are rough averages conveying the scale of investment needed
- **Crucial insight**: experts practice *differently*, not just more -- Ericsson calls it **deliberate practice**

#### Author's Running Example

- Ericsson questioned the author about her jogging: no specific goals, no tracking, listens to NPR, no coach
- Verdict: not deliberate practice -- explains why no improvement despite running daily since age eighteen

### The Four Components of Deliberate Practice

1. **Set a stretch goal**: zero in on one narrow weakness, not what you already do well
   - **Rowdy Gaines** (Olympic swimmer): "At every practice, I would try to beat myself"
   - **Roberto Diaz** (violinist): finding "your Achilles' heel"

2. **Full concentration and great effort**: often done alone
   - **Kevin Durant**: "I probably spend 70 percent of my time by myself, just working on my game"

3. **Seek immediate feedback**: experts hunger for it, especially what they did *wrong*

4. **Repetition with reflection and refinement**: until struggle becomes fluent and flawless

#### Ulrik Christensen's Medical Training Software

- Physician-turned-entrepreneur built adaptive learning software based on deliberate practice
- Virtual reality game teaching cardiac conditions; doctor struggled, was asked to *reflect* on certainties vs. uncertainties
- After reflection, executed procedure correctly; four consecutive perfect repetitions = mastery for the day
- Then start over with a *new* stretch goal: "One by one, these subtle refinements add up to dazzling mastery"

### Deliberate Practice Beyond Sports and Music

- First studied in chess, music, athletics -- but applies universally
- Even complex creative human abilities can be broken into component skills

#### Benjamin Franklin's Writing Practice

- Improved writing by studying essays in the *Spectator*
- Read and reread, took notes, hid originals
- Rewrote essays from notes, compared to originals, corrected faults
- To improve logical arguments: jumbled notes, put them in sensible order
- To enhance language: practiced translating prose to poetry and back

> "There are no gains without pains." -- Benjamin Franklin

#### Other Examples

- **Atul Gawande** (surgeon): "People often assume that you have to have great hands to become a surgeon, but it's not true... practicing this one difficult thing day and night for years on end"
- **David Blaine** (magician, broke breath-holding world record): "It's practice, it's training, and it's experimenting while pushing through the pain"

### National Spelling Bee Study (with Ericsson)

- **Three types of spelling activities**: (1) reading for pleasure/word games, (2) getting quizzed, (3) unassisted solitary practice -- **only the third met criteria for deliberate practice**
- **Kerry Close** (winner): 13-year-old, fifth consecutive year, estimated 3,000+ hours of practice
- Winning word: "Ursprache. U-R-S-P-R-A-C-H-E. Ursprache."
- Studies independently: works with numerous spelling study guides, makes lists, labors through the dictionary
- **Key finding**: deliberate practice predicted advancing to further rounds far better than any other preparation
- Benefits of quizzing: shines light on what you *think* you know but *actually* haven't mastered
- Kerry used quizzing diagnostically to identify weaknesses, then focused efforts on mastering them
- Reading for fun had *no* relationship to spelling prowess
- Grittier spellers rated deliberate practice as significantly more effortful AND less enjoyable than other activities
- Reading books for pleasure and Scrabble rated as effortless and enjoyable as "eating your favorite food"

### The Experience of Deliberate Practice

> "Dancing appears glamorous, easy, delightful. But the path to the paradise of that achievement is not easier than any other. There is fatigue so great that the body cries even in its sleep. There are times of complete frustration. There are daily small deaths." -- Martha Graham

- Ericsson finds deliberate practice supremely effortful
- World-class performers at *peak* of careers can handle max one hour before needing break
- Total: only about 3-5 hours of deliberate practice per day
- Athletes and musicians often nap after intensive training -- rest and recovery necessary for mental work as much as physical

> "Every day is an experiment... you're concentrating and you're exhausted... It's pretty intense." -- Judd Apatow (film director)

- World-class performers who retire tend not to keep up deliberate practice schedule -- if intrinsically pleasurable, they'd keep doing it

### Ericsson vs. Csikszentmihalyi: Deliberate Practice vs. Flow

- **Csikszentmihalyi**: eminent psychologist studying **flow** -- complete concentration leading to feeling of spontaneity and effortlessness

> "You are in an ecstatic state to such a point that you feel as though you almost don't exist... I just sit there watching in a state of awe and wonderment. And the music just flows out by itself." -- Orchestra conductor describing flow

- **Key differences**:
  - Flow is spontaneous; deliberate practice is carefully planned
  - Deliberate practice is exceptionally effortful; flow is, by definition, effortless
  - Flow happens when challenge and skill are in balance; deliberate practice deliberately exceeds current skill
  - Deliberate practice is "problem solving" mode; flow is just doing

- Csikszentmihalyi's contrary view: "Even when the learning is hard, it is not bitter when you feel that it is worth having"
- Author arranged 90-minute debate between the two before ~80 educators -- both tall Europeans with slight accents, perfect gentlemen, no insults -- no resolution reached

#### Author's Resolution

- Collected data from thousands of Grit Scale takers across diverse occupations
- **Finding**: grittier adults reported experiencing *more* flow, not less
- **Conclusion**: gritty people do more deliberate practice AND experience more flow -- no contradiction
- Deliberate practice is a *behavior*; flow is an *experience* -- they don't have to happen simultaneously

> "Deliberate practice is for preparation, and flow is for performance."

### Athletes on Practice vs. Performance

- **Rowdy Gaines**: swam 20,000+ miles in eight years (in fifty-yard lap increments) for a 49-second Olympic race
  - "Did you enjoy those miles?" "I'm not going to lie. I never really enjoyed going to practice... brief moments when I'd think, 'God, is this worth it?'"
  - "So why didn't you quit?" "It's very simple. It's because I loved swimming... I had a passion for competing, for the *result* of training, for the feeling of being in shape, for winning"

- **Mads Rasmussen** (Olympic gold medalist rower):

> "It's about hard work. When it's not fun, you do what you need to do anyway. Because when you achieve results, it's incredibly fun."

- **Katie Ledecky** (world record swimmer): broke world record and said "it felt pretty easy... I was so relaxed" -- but credits the hard work in practice
  - Swimming since age six; at first swim meet, grabbed lane line repeatedly, few strokes, grabbed again; told dad "Great!" beaming ear to ear
  - Coach **Bruce Gemmell**: Katie *always* relished tough challenges; sneaks extra practice time on drills she's horrible at
  - "I'll catch her sneaking practice time to get better at it, so within some period of time, she's one of the best in the group"

### Author's TED Talk as Deliberate Practice Example

- Prepared talk, got devastating feedback from producer Juliet Blake and Chris Anderson
- Lost in jargon, too many slides, no suspense -- "like telling the punch line of a joke at the very beginning"
- Rewrote, practiced before family, got more criticism (daughters: "Why do you say 'Um' all the time?")
- Gave much better final talk: "Nobody wants to show you the hours and hours of becoming"

### Can Deliberate Practice Be Positive?

- Grittier spellers rated deliberate practice as both more enjoyable AND more effortful
- **Two interpretations**: (1) "learn to love the burn" -- develop taste for hard work; (2) "some people enjoy a challenge" naturally
- **Kerry Close**: deliberate practice was never flow, but was gratifying: "Some of my most *rewarding* studying was on my own, forcing myself to break down a big task"
- Author's guess: different kinds of positive experience -- thrill of getting better vs. ecstasy of performing at your best

### Three Suggestions for Deliberate Practice

1. **Know the science**: most people accomplish zero hours of daily deliberate practice; even motivated people may not be doing it correctly
   - **Noa Kageyama** (Juilliard): didn't start practicing deliberately until age 22; once he learned the science, quality and satisfaction skyrocketed

2. **Make it a habit**: figure out when and where you're most comfortable; do it then and there every day
   - **Daily Rituals** (Mason Currey): 161 artists/scientists -- no single rule, but all had daily rituals, all creatures of habit
   - **Charles Schulz** (18,000 *Peanuts* strips): rose at dawn, drove kids to school, went to studio, worked through lunch until children returned
   - **Maya Angelou**: by 7am delivered herself to "tiny mean" hotel room with no distractions until 2pm
   - **William James**: "There is no more miserable human being than the one for whom the beginning of every bit of work must be decided anew each day"
   - Author's personal habit: "When it's eight in the morning and I'm in my home office, I will reread yesterday's draft"

3. **Change the way you experience it**: embrace challenge without judgment
   - Coach **Terry Laughlin**: "Deliberate practice can feel wonderful... It's about in-the-moment self-awareness *without judgment*"
   - Infants and toddlers spend most of time trying things they can't do -- they don't seem embarrassed
   - **Elena Bodrova and Deborah Leong**: around kindergarten, children learn that mistakes inspire negative reactions from adults -- we inadvertently teach shame
   - Solution: teachers model emotion-free mistake-making; say "That was hard! It was great!"

## Chapter 8: Purpose

### Two Sources of Mature Passion

- **Interest** is one source of passion; **purpose** -- the intention to contribute to the well-being of others -- is another
- Common sequence: self-oriented interest first, then self-disciplined practice, then integration with other-centered purpose
- **Benjamin Bloom's** three phases: "early years" (interest), "middle years" (practice), **"later years"** when larger purpose becomes apparent

### What Gritty People Mean by Purpose

- Not just goal-oriented but oriented toward benefiting other people
- Common references: "my children," "my clients," "my students," "this country," "science," "society"

## Chapter 9: Hope

### Active Hope vs. Passive Hope

- **Japanese saying**: "Fall seven, rise eight"
- Passive hope: wishful thinking without effort
- **Active hope** (grit-enabling): expectation that *your own efforts* can improve your future
- "I have a feeling tomorrow will be better" vs. "I resolve to make tomorrow better"

### Duckworth's Neurobiology Crisis

- Failed first college neurobiology quiz, then midterm; TA suggested dropping the course
- Chose hopeful self-talk: "I won't quit! I can figure this out!"
- Resolved to *major* in neurobiology, sought extra help, practiced under pressure
- Final grade: B (lowest ever, but most proud)

### Learned Helplessness (Seligman & Maier, 1964)

- **Marty Seligman** and **Steve Maier**: dogs in cages receiving random electric shocks
- Control group: dogs could end shocks by pushing nose against panel
- No-control group: same shocks, no way to stop them
- Next day, **shuttle box test**: tone signals impending shock, dogs can jump barrier to safety
  - Dogs with control jumped to safety
  - Two-thirds of dogs without control just lay down whimpering, passively waiting
- **Key insight**: not suffering itself, but *suffering you think you can't control* leads to hopelessness
- Later research confirmed: uncontrollable stress produces clinical depression symptoms

### Learned Optimism

- About one-third of helpless dogs remained resilient -- kept trying to escape
- **Optimists vs. pessimists** differ in causal explanations:
  - Pessimists: permanent, pervasive causes ("I screw up everything")
  - Optimists: temporary, specific causes ("I mismanaged my time")
- Real-world outcomes: optimists earn higher grades, stay healthier, sell 25% more (insurance agents), perform better after setbacks (elite swimmers)

### Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (Aaron Beck)

- Challenged Freudian psychoanalysis: thoughts (not unconscious conflicts) drive feelings and behavior
- CBT: observe negative self-talk and change maladaptive behaviors -- proven longer-lasting than antidepressant medication

### Teach For America Study

- **Wendy Kopp**: TFA founder/CEO, paragon of grit (conceived TFA senior year at Princeton, built massive educational nonprofit)
- 400 TFA teachers completed Grit Scale and optimism questionnaire before classroom entry
- One year later: optimistic teachers were grittier and happier
- Grit and happiness together explained why optimistic teachers achieved more student academic progress
- **Conclusion**: when you keep searching for ways to change your situation, you stand a chance of finding them; when you stop searching, you guarantee you won't

> "Whether you think you can, or think you can't -- you're right." -- Henry Ford

### Carol Dweck and Growth Mindset

- **Carol Dweck**: psychology doctoral student, wondered why some persevere while others quit
- Studied middle school children identified as "helpless" by teachers/principal/psychologist

#### Attribution Retraining Experiment

- Divided children into two groups:
  - *Success only* program: solved math problems, always praised for doing well
  - *Attribution retraining* program: solved math problems, occasionally told "you should have tried harder"
- Result: success-only children gave up easily after difficulty; attribution-retraining children tried harder
- **Learned to interpret failure as cue to try harder** rather than confirmation they lacked ability

#### Growth Mindset Theory

- Over four decades, Carol probed deeper into people's **theories of intelligence**
- **Fixed mindset**: agree that "Your intelligence is something very basic about you that you can't change very much"
- **Growth mindset**: agree that "You can always substantially change how intelligent you are"
- Growth-oriented people assume you can get smarter with right opportunities, support, effort, and belief
- Fixed mindset becomes liability at obstacles -- setbacks interpreted as "you don't have the right stuff"
- 2,000+ high school seniors: growth mindset students grittier, earned higher grades, more likely to persist in college
- Measured across ages: always correlates with grit
- Predicts better school performance, emotional/physical health, stronger social relationships

### KIPP Schools and Praise

- **KIPP** (Knowledge Is Power Program): 70,000 students, mostly low-income; almost all graduate high school, 80%+ attend college
- Teacher training emphasizes praising effort/learning over "natural talent"
- Fixed-mindset examples: "You're a natural!" / "Well, at least you tried!"
- Growth-mindset examples: "You're a learner!" / "That didn't work. Let's talk about how you approached it."

### Research on Mindset Formation

- **Daeun Park study**: teachers who gave privileges to high performers and emphasized comparisons inculcated fixed mindset in students by year's end
- Children develop fixed mindset when parents react to mistakes as harmful -- children watch actions, even when parents *say* they have growth mindset

### Corporate Mindset Culture

- **Jennifer Chatman** (Berkeley): Fortune 1000 employees in growth-mindset companies reported 47% more trustworthy colleagues, 49% more innovation, 65% more risk-taking support

### Managing Your Inner Pessimist

- Most people have inner fixed-mindset pessimist alongside growth-mindset optimist
- Easy to say growth-mindset words without changing body language, facial expressions, behavior
- **Susan Mackie** technique: name your inner fixed-mindset character ("Controlling Claire") to acknowledge slipping without shame

### Bill McNabb (Vanguard CEO) on Growth Mindset

- Observed unsuccessful leaders believed "I can't learn anymore. I am what I am"
- Successful leaders "stayed on a growth trajectory," kept surprising with growth
- Shift to growth mindset: joined crew team in college (never rowed before), had many early failures, kept getting better
- Lesson: "When you have setbacks and failures, you can't overreact to them. You need to step back, analyze them, and learn from them. But you also need to stay optimistic."

### Nietzsche and Challenge-Building Resilience

> "What doesn't kill me makes me stronger" -- Nietzsche

- **Outward Bound**: increases independence, confidence, assertiveness -- benefits *increase* over 6 months
- **But**: dogs with uncontrollable stress showed no benefit -- most became more vulnerable
- **Urgent question**: When does struggle lead to hope vs. hopelessness?

### Neurobiology of Hope (Steve Maier)

- New experiment: adolescent rats (5 weeks old) received shocks; one group could control, other couldn't
- Effects assessed 5 weeks later as adults: both groups subjected to uncontrollable shocks
- **Results**: adolescent rats with *no control* grew up timid and helpless; rats with *control* grew up adventurous, **inoculated against learned helplessness in adulthood**
- Brain mechanism: prefrontal cortex sends inhibitory message to limbic areas ("Cool it down there! There's something we can do")
- **Plasticity in circuitry**: overcoming adversity on your own *during youth* develops different wiring for handling adversity later
- Adversity must be "pretty potent" -- brain areas need to wire together through challenge
- Can't just *talk* someone into believing -- must activate control circuitry *while* experiencing adversity

### The Poverty Concern

- Steve Maier worries about kids in poverty: "getting a lot of helplessness experiences, not enough mastery experiences"
- Need contingency between actions and outcomes early in life

### The Fragile Perfects

- Duckworth also worries about those who cruise friction-free for long before first real failure
- Little practice falling and getting up -- many reasons to stick with fixed mindset
- "Fragile perfects": high-achievers who stumble in young adulthood after first midterm failure
- Know how to succeed but not how to fail

### Kayvon Asemani: Growth Mindset Exemplar

- Penn freshman from Milton Hershey School (boarding school for severely disadvantaged children)
- Entered Hershey in fifth grade, one year after father nearly strangled mother to death
- Spring semester struggles in quantitative courses: "Numbers have always been difficult for me. But I embrace the challenge... even if it means graduating with a GPA less than what I would have earned if I just majored in something that didn't require me to manipulate numbers"

### Teaching Hope

**Fixed vs. Growth Mindset Chain**:
- Fixed mindset → pessimistic explanations → giving up on challenges
- Growth mindset → optimistic self-talk → perseverance over adversity

**Teaching hope requires**: Update beliefs about intelligence/talent, practice optimistic self-talk, ask for help

#### 1. Update Beliefs About Intelligence

- **Brain plasticity evidence**: study in *Nature* found adolescent IQ scores increased from age 14-18, not fixed
- Brain structure changes with learning: math skills strengthen math-related brain areas
- Brain is like a muscle: strengthens with use throughout life
- Myelin growth continues into adulthood: insulating sheath speeds neural signals

#### 2. Practice Optimistic Self-Talk

- Resilience training (preventative CBT): children who completed training had lower pessimism, fewer depression symptoms over 2 years
- Pessimistic college students: less anxiety over 3 years
- You can modify self-talk and prevent it from interfering with goals

#### 3. Ask for a Helping Hand

- Mathematician **Rhonda Hughes**: PhD after 89 faculty rejections

- Key insight: asking for help is how to hold onto hope

> "You actually *develop* the ability to do mathematics. *Don't give up!*" -- Rhonda Hughes and Sylvia Bozeman

## Chapter 10: Parenting for Grit

**Question asked daily**: "What can I do to encourage grit in the people I care for?"

### The Parenting Debate: Two Extreme Views

- **Adversity camp**: paraphrase Nietzsche -- scowling parents, endless criticism, chaining to instruments, grounding for A-minus
- **Unconditional support camp**: children are challenge-seeking by nature; freed from imperious parents, they'll follow intrinsic interests
- **Historical example -- John Watson** (1928): "Never hug and kiss them. Never let them sit in your lap" -- let children cope from birth, rotate caregivers
- **Reality**: neither extreme is supported by research; no research yet on parenting *and* grit specifically

### Two Case Studies

**Steve Young** (NFL quarterback, 49ers):
- Strict Mormon upbringing; father's response when Steve wanted to quit BYU (8th-string QB): "You can quit. But you can't come home because I'm not going to live with a quitter"
- Trained 10,000 spiral throws in 2 months
- Father **"Grit" Young**: built to 10,000 sit-ups in a row over years; rule: whatever you sign up for, you *had to* see through to the end
- Also tremendously supportive: mother sat in classroom for weeks when Steve had separation anxiety
- Steve's reflection: father *knew* him; "It was tough, but it was loving"

**Francesca Martinez** (British comedian, cerebral palsy, calls herself "wobbly"):
- Diagnosed at age 2; doctors said would "never lead a normal life"
- Four-hour drives each way to perform for 10 minutes, countless cold calls
- Parents (**Tina and Alex**): "Go and follow your dreams, and if they don't work out, you can reassess"
- Dropped out of high school at 16 with parents' permission
- "I don't take credit for my hard work and passion. I think these qualities came from my family, which was very loving and very stable"
- Parents also pushed her to do physical therapy exercises (years of battles, door-banging, tears)
- Father Alex (writer) on work ethic: "To finish things, you have to put the work in"
- Mother Tina (environmental activist): children need freedom AND limits -- "We taught our children to live by clear principles and moral guidelines"

### Wise Parenting (Authoritative)

- **Larry Steinberg's framework** (two dimensions): Supportive/Unsupportive x Demanding/Undemanding
- Four quadrants: **Wise** (supportive + demanding), Permissive, Authoritarian, Neglectful
- Study of 10,000 American teenagers: warm, respectful, demanding parents produced higher grades, more self-reliance, less anxiety/depression
- Pattern replicates across nations and developmental stages
- **What matters**: messages children *receive*, not messages parents *aim* to deliver
- Don't judge parents lecturing child in supermarket -- you lack context for child's interpretation
- Both Steve Young and Francesca Martinez had parents who were tough AND loving, child-centered but not deferring judgment to children

**Nancy Darling's parenting assessment** (psychologist/parenting expert):
- Supportive = Warm (count on parents for help, spend time talking, do fun things) + Respectful (right to own point of view, privacy, freedom)
- Demanding = expect to follow rules, don't let me get away with things, point out ways to improve, expect best even when hard

### Children Imitate and Emulate Parents

- Classic Stanford experiment: preschoolers who watched adults attack inflatable doll became "carbon copies"
- When parents are loving, respectful, demanding: children follow example AND revere it
- **Benjamin Bloom**: parents of world-class performers were "models of the work ethic" whose interests were communicated to children
- To bring forth grit: (1) have passion/perseverance for own goals, (2) parent in ways that encourage emulation

### Mentors Beyond Parents

- **Tobi Lutke**: dropped out of German high school at 16; apprenticed under **Jurgen** (grizzled programmer)
- Jurgen gave daily printouts with red-marker corrections: "Taught me not to tangle my ego up in the code I write"
- Sent Tobi alone to General Motors installation -- manufactured situations slightly outside comfort zone
- Founded **Shopify**: software company, $100+ million revenue

### Wise Teachers (Ron Ferguson)

- **Ron Ferguson** (Harvard economist) studied 1,892 classrooms
- Demanding teachers produce measurable academic gains; supportive teachers enhance happiness and college aspirations
- **Psychologically wise teachers** combine both

### The Power of Wise Feedback (Yeager & Cohen)

- Seventh-grade teachers gave feedback with one of two Post-it notes:
  - Control: "I'm giving you these comments so that you'll have feedback"
  - **Wise feedback**: "I'm giving you these comments because I have very high expectations and I know that you can reach them"
- Result: 40% of control students revised essays vs. **80%** with wise feedback; replication showed twice as many edits

### Cody Coleman's Story

- **Turning point**: older brother asked "Where do you want to go to college?" and said "Why wouldn't Princeton take you? If you work harder... you have nothing to lose by trying"

## Chapter 11: The Playing Fields of Grit

### Why Extracurriculars Matter

- Author's daughter Lucy struggled to open raisins box at age 4, gave up -- author worried about grit
- Enrolled Lucy in ballet as structured extracurricular activity
- **Two critical features** of activities like ballet, piano, football:
  1. Adult in charge (ideally supportive AND demanding, NOT the parent)
  2. Activities **designed** to cultivate interest, practice, purpose, and hope
- The ballet studio, recital hall, dojo, basketball court, gridiron = **"playing fields of grit"**

### Evidence on Extracurriculars

- Research: kids in extracurriculars earn better grades, higher self-esteem, less likely to get in trouble
- Longitudinal studies confirm: more participation predicts better outcomes
- **Overdosing is rare**: average American teenager spends 3+ hours/day on TV and video games, plus social media
- **Beeper studies**: in class, kids report being challenged but unmotivated; with friends, fun but not challenged; in extracurriculars: **both challenged AND having fun**
- No other experience in young people's lives reliably provides this combination

### Sustained Participation Is Key

- Extracurriculars especially beneficial when done for **more than a year**
- **Margo Gardner** (Columbia): followed 11,000 teenagers to age 26 -- kids with two+ years in extracurriculars significantly more likely to graduate college and volunteer as adults; hours per week predicted employment/earnings only for two-year participants

### Warren Willingham's Personal Qualities Project

- **ETS director** (1978): high school grades and test scores only half-decent at predicting later success
- Followed several thousand students five years, collecting 100+ personal characteristics
- One variable won "by a long stretch": **follow-through**
- **Definition**: "evidence of purposeful, continuous commitment to certain types of activities versus sporadic efforts in diverse areas"
- Top follow-through: two different high school extracurriculars for several years each, with significant advancement in both
- After controlling for grades and SAT scores, follow-through predicted: college honors, leadership positions, notable accomplishments across all domains
- **Particular pursuits didn't matter** -- what mattered was signing up, signing up again, and making progress

### Duckworth's Grit Grid Study

- Author couldn't sleep after reading Willingham: "Holy smokes! What Willingham calls 'follow-through' sounds a lot like grit!"
- Partnered with **Gates Foundation** (interested in college dropout problem); recruited 1,200 college seniors
- **Grit Grid scoring** (following Willingham):
  - 2+ years in activity = 1 grit point; 1 year only = 0
  - Multiple years with advancement (member → treasurer) = additional point
  - High advancement (president, MVP) = third point
  - Max score: 6 points (two different multiyear commitments with high achievement in both)
- Students with higher Grit Grid scores rated themselves higher in grit -- teachers agreed
- After two years: only 34% of 1,200 students still enrolled
  - **69% of students scoring 6/6** still in college
  - **16% of students scoring 0/6** still on track for degrees
- Applied to teachers: follow-through in college predicted staying in teaching AND effectiveness
  - Persistence and effectiveness had **no relationship** with SAT scores, GPAs, or interviewer ratings

### The Corresponsive Principle (Brent Roberts)

- Challenged view that personalities are "set like plaster" after childhood
- Situations and personality traits reciprocally reinforce each other: traits steer us toward situations that amplify those traits
- Hostile adolescents entered vicious cycles; agreeable adolescents entered virtuous cycles
- Applied to grit: following through both requires grit AND builds it

### The Hard Thing Rule

- **Part 1**: Everyone in the family does a hard thing requiring daily deliberate practice
- **Part 2**: You can quit, but not on a bad day -- must finish committed interval (season, tuition period)
- **Part 3**: You get to pick your hard thing
- **High school amendment (Part 4)**: must commit to at least one activity for two years minimum
- Lucy cycled through ballet, gymnastics, track, handicrafts, piano -- eventually landed on **viola** after three years

### Bill Fitzsimmons on Harvard Admissions

- Dean of admissions for 40+ years; intimately familiar with Willingham's research
- Harvard admits students who "have made a commitment to pursue something they love, believe in, and value -- and have done so with singular energy, discipline, and plain old hard work"
- Activities aren't just a signal of grit -- they're transformative

> "My sense, from being in admissions for over forty years, is that most people are born with tremendous potential. The real question is whether they're encouraged to employ their good old-fashioned hard work and their grit to its maximum." -- Bill Fitzsimmons

### The Inequality of Extracurricular Access

- Affluent students' participation steady for decades; **poor students' participation dropping precipitously** (Robert Putnam)
- Pay-to-play sports, uniform costs, transportation, private lesson fees all widening the gap
- Students qualifying for subsidized meals: Grit Grid scores **full point lower**
- **Geoffrey Canada** (Harlem Children's Zone): "What poor kids need is a lot. But you can sum it up by saying that what they need is a decent childhood"

### Learned Industriousness (Robert Eisenberger)

- **Robert Eisenberger** (University of Houston): leading authority on whether doing hard things teaches doing other hard things
- Dozens of studies: rats randomly assigned to work hard (press lever 20 times for single pellet) vs easy (press lever 2 times)
- Afterwards, all rats given different difficult task -- rats previously required to work hard demonstrated **more vigor and endurance**
- **Favorite experiment**: rats fed through wire-mesh hoppers (gnaw food through small openings) vs pellets scattered on floor
  - After month of working for food: hopper-fed rats performed better on subsequent runway task
- **Children study**: gave pennies to second/third graders for counting objects, memorizing pictures, matching shapes
  - Some children given rapidly increasing difficulty; others repeatedly given easy versions
  - Afterward, tedious copying task: children trained on difficult tasks **worked harder**
- **Learned industriousness**: association between effort and reward can be learned
- Homage to Seligman's "learned helplessness" -- Bob dubbed this **"learned industriousness"**
- Without directly experiencing the effort-reward connection, animals and people default to laziness (evolution shaped us to avoid effort)

### Why Extracurriculars Provide the Best Playing Field

- Author found herself ill-suited to be demanding with her own daughters
- Struggled to provide feedback; found herself enthusiastically praising no matter what
- **This is why extracurriculars offer superior playing fields for grit** -- coaches and teachers bring forth grit in children not their own
- Ballet teacher was equally supportive as author AND more demanding
- Late arrivals: stern lecture; forgot leotard: sat and watched; incorrect execution: endless repetitions until correct

## Chapter 12: A Culture of Grit

### Culture Shapes Grit

- **Culture = shared norms and values of a group** -- defines how "we" do things
- Creates invisible psychological boundaries; categorical allegiance (you're in or you're not)

> "If you want to be grittier, find a gritty culture and join it. If you're a leader, and you want the people in your organization to be grittier, create a gritty culture."

> "The hard way to get grit is to do it by yourself. The easy way is to use conformity -- the basic human drive to fit in -- because if you're around a lot of people who are gritty, you're going to act grittier." -- Dan Chambliss

### Culture and Identity

- Over time, norms become internalized: "The way we do things around here" becomes "The way I do things"
- **James March** (Stanford): sometimes decisions come from cost-benefit analysis; other times from identity: "Who am I? What does someone like me do in a situation like this?"

- First core value example -- *"We don't whine"* paired with George Bernard Shaw: "The true joy in life is to be a force of fortune instead of a feverish, selfish little clod of ailments and grievances"

### Verbatim Memorization at West Point

- Centuries-old tradition: "plebes" (first-year cadets) memorize Bugle Notes -- songs, poems, codes, creeds
- **Schofield's Definition of Discipline** (1879): "The discipline which makes the soldiers of a free country reliable in battle is *not* to be gained by harsh or tyrannical treatment"
- Same commands can inspire allegiance OR seed resentment
- **Origin of great leadership = commander's respect for subordinates**

### West Point's Developmental Model

- Current superintendent: **Lieutenant General Robert Caslen**
- **Beast Barracks attrition**: 170 quit (12%) forty years ago; down to less than 2% now
- Explanation: shift from **attrition model** to **developmental model**
- "When only the survivalists succeed, that's an attrition model. There's another kind of leadership -- a developmental model. Standards exactly the same -- high -- but in one case, you use fear. In the other, you lead from the front."
- Leading from the front: literally getting out in front, doing same hard work, facing same risks
- When cadets fall short: sit down, create training program, lead workouts
- "Very often, the cadet who was unable to do it on their own is now motivated, and once they start to improve, their motivation increases"
- Academic, physical, military standards grown MORE stringent over time while producing finer leaders
- Caslen: four years won't turn Grit Scale 2s into 5s, but "People change. Growth mindset. You never really know who is going to become a Schwarzkopf or a MacArthur."

## Chapter 13: Conclusion

### Growing Your Grit - Two Pathways

- **From the inside out**: cultivate interests, develop challenge-exceeding-skill practice, connect work to purpose beyond yourself, learn to hope when all seems lost
- **From the outside in**: parents, coaches, teachers, bosses, mentors, friends -- developing grit depends critically on other people

### Grit and Happiness

- Study of 2,000 American adults: **grittier people more likely to enjoy healthy emotional life**
- At top of Grit Scale, grit went hand in hand with well-being regardless of measurement
- Author acknowledges uncertainty about costs of grit paragons' devotion

### Personal Reflection

- Daughters Amanda and Lucy watched author attempt new things and cry when difficult
- They wish mother would relax more and talk about Taylor Swift
- But they don't wish mother was anything other than a paragon of grit
- They recognize **complacency has its charms, but none worth trading for fulfillment of realizing their potential**

### Can You Have Too Much Grit?

- **Aristotle**: too much or too little of any good thing is bad (too little courage = cowardice; too much = folly)
- **Adam Grant and Barry Schwartz**: inverted-U function for most traits
- **Duckworth's finding**: with grit, haven't found the inverse U predicted by Aristotle
- Strategic quitting is valuable -- the author benefited from quitting piano (no interest or talent) and becoming fluent in French (freed time for more gratifying pursuits)
- "Finishing whatever you begin without exception" is a good way to miss better opportunities
- Survey of 300 Americans: **zero people aspired to be less gritty**

### Grit Is Not Everything

- Greatness and goodness are different -- if forced to choose, author puts **goodness first**
- Grit is far from the only important aspect of character

### Three Dimensions of Character

1. **Intrapersonal** ("performance character" / "resume virtues"): includes grit, self-control -- predicts academic achievement
2. **Interpersonal** ("moral character" / "eulogy virtues"): includes gratitude, social intelligence -- predicts positive social functioning
3. **Intellectual**: includes curiosity and zest -- predicts independent posture toward learning

- **Plurality of character operates against any one virtue being uniquely important**

### Limits: Self-Imposed vs. Real

- We all face limits in talent and opportunity
- **More often, limits are self-imposed** -- we try, fail, conclude we've hit the ceiling
- Response to daughter saying "I'll never be Mozart": **"You're not practicing piano to be Mozart"**

### Final Example: Ta-Nehisi Coates

- Won MacArthur genius award; *Between the World and Me* became extraordinary bestseller
- Eight years earlier: unemployed, laid off from *Time*, scrambling for freelance, considered driving a cab
- Pushed through "extraordinary stress" of his book: "The writing was very, very different. The sentences had much more power."
- MacArthur video: **"Failure is probably the most important factor in all of my work. Writing is failure. Over and over and over again."**

> *The challenge of writing*
> *Is to see your horribleness on page.*
> *To see your terribleness*
> *And then to go to bed.*
>
> *And wake up the next day,*
> *And take that horribleness and that terribleness,*
> *And refine it,*
> *And make it not so terrible and not so horrible.*
> *And then to go to bed again.*
>
> *And come the next day,*
> *And refine it a little bit more,*
> *And make it not so bad.*
> *And then to go to bed the next day.*
>
> *And do it again,*
> *And make it maybe average.*
> *And then one more time,*
> *If you're lucky.*
> *Maybe you get to good.*
>
> *And if you've done that,*
> *That's a success.*

### Redefining Genius

- Author's father's words: "You're no genius"
- **If genius = accomplishing great things without effort**: father was right
- **But if genius = working toward excellence ceaselessly, with every element of being**: then father is a genius, author is, Coates is, **and if you're willing, so are you**
