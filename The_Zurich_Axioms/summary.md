# The Zurich Axioms — Summary

**Author:** Max Gunther
**Pages:** ~190
**Published:** 1985

## Overview
The Zurich Axioms is a collection of 12 Major Axioms and 16 Minor Axioms distilled from the speculative wisdom of Swiss bankers and financiers, passed down through Max Gunther's father Frank Henry and the "Swiss Club" of Wall Street speculators. The book rejects conventional investment wisdom (diversify, buy and hold, play it safe) in favor of an aggressive, risk-embracing approach to building wealth through speculation.

## The 12 Major Axioms

1. **On Risk** — "If you are not worried, you are not risking enough." Worry is the price of meaningful gains. Sober Sylvia (safe, diversified) vs. Mad Mary (concentrated, risky) — Mary ends up richer. Always play for meaningful stakes.

2. **On Greed** — "Always take your profit too soon." Greed makes you hold too long hoping for more. J. Paul Getty's oil lease story: the second million is never as satisfying. Decide your ending position before you begin, and get out when you reach it.

3. **On Hope** — "When the ship starts to sink, don't pray. Jump." Take small losses quickly before they become big losses. Susan Garner's office building: she held on hoping it would recover, turned a manageable loss into a catastrophe. Alice & Harry's house flip: same pattern with real estate.

4. **On Forecasts** — "Human behavior cannot be predicted. Distrust anyone who claims to know the future." Irving Fisher's 1929 prediction that stocks had reached a "permanently high plateau." All forecasters, from economists to astrologers, have terrible track records.

5. **On Patterns** — "Chaos is not dangerous until it begins to look orderly." The Gambler's Fallacy, the illusion of order in random data. Charles Wells breaking the bank at Monte Carlo (pure luck, not a system). Caroline Otero's system that "worked" until it didn't. Alfred Malabre's Citibank options disaster — seeing order where there was chaos. Correlation vs. causation traps (hemlines, Super Bowl indicator).

6. **On Mobility** — "Avoid putting down roots. They impede motion." Don't get emotionally attached to investments. Be ready to jump when something better appears or when your current position sours. Loyalty to a stock, property, or investment is irrational.

7. **On Intuition** — "A hunch can be trusted if it can be explained." Intuition is real but not magical — it's your subconscious processing stored data. Test every hunch: do you have enough knowledge in the relevant area to have generated it? Maine coast real estate woman: years of study made her hunch plausible. Never confuse a hunch with a hope (Minor Axiom XI).

8. **On Religion and the Occult** — "It is unlikely that God's plan for the universe includes making you rich." Protestant minister's swamp land story. Jesse Livermore's clairvoyance delusion. The author's own Tarot card adventure with Control Data stock. If astrology worked, all astrologers would be rich. Keep money and the supernatural strictly separate.

9. **On Optimism and Pessimism** — "Optimism means expecting the best, but confidence means knowing how you will handle the worst." Optimism is seductive but dangerous — it clouds judgment and leads to ventures with no exit. Sam & Judy's inaccessible desert land. Poker pros have confidence (constructive pessimism), not optimism. The Sirens analogy. Wall Street's systematic bullish bias.

10. **On Consensus** — "Disregard the majority opinion. It is probably wrong." René Descartes as model speculator-doubter. Princeton pencil experiment on conformity. The 1980s auto industry contrarian opportunity (GM at $34 → $80, Ford at $11 → $46). Author's wife's Ford hunch quashed by her broker's majority opinion. But contrarianism as a dogma is equally foolish — think independently, not automatically against.

11. **On Stubbornness** — "If it doesn't pay off the first time, forget it." Don't chase investments out of stubbornness. The Sears-obsessed woman who chased one stock for years. Never average down — it's self-deception that makes a bad situation look better while making it worse. An investment can't "owe" you money. The gain doesn't have to come from the same place as the loss.

12. **On Planning** — "Long-range plans engender the dangerous belief that the future is under control." George & Martha's 1940s retirement plan destroyed by inflation and unexpected events. Paula W.'s Ford dividend income decimated by the auto industry collapse. The future beyond next week is essentially unknowable. Stay light on your feet like the grasshopper, not rooted like the ant. The only long-range plan you need: the intention to get rich.

## Key Themes
- **Embrace risk** — safety is the enemy of wealth
- **Stay mobile** — don't get rooted in any position, plan, or belief
- **Distrust order** — the world is chaotic, not orderly; patterns are usually illusory
- **Control emotions** — greed, hope, stubbornness, and optimism are the speculator's enemies
- **Think independently** — the majority, the experts, and the forecasters are usually wrong
- **Act decisively** — take profits early, cut losses fast, never average down

## Swiss Club Context
The axioms originated among Swiss expatriate bankers and speculators in New York, particularly around Frank Henry (the author's father) and his associates from Wädenswil on the south shore of Lake Zurich. These were men who had fled Switzerland's limited opportunities to speculate in America's more dynamic markets, bringing with them a Swiss pragmatism about money and risk.
