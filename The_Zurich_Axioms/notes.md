## Pages 1-20: Introduction + First Major Axiom: On Risk

### Introduction: What the Axioms Are and How They Came to Be
- Switzerland puzzle: rocky, no coastline, mineral-poor, inhospitable farming — yet among world's most affluent people
- Swiss got rich by being the world's cleverest investors, speculators, and gamblers
- Book is about betting to win — not for those with philosophical bias against betting
- Axioms apply to stocks, commodities, precious metals, art, antiques, real estate, casino gambling — any situation putting money at risk
- "Zurich Axioms" coined by club of Swiss stock/commodity plungers on Wall Street after WWII
- Author's father Franz Heinrich (Frank Henry) was founding member — Swiss banker, headed NY branch of Schweizerbankverein (Swiss Bank Corporation)
- Father wanted on his tombstone: "He gambled and won"
- Frank Henry's rule of thumb: only half of one's financial energies should go to job income; other half to investment/speculation
- Author invested prudently after army, broke even. Father made bundle on Canadian uranium-mining stocks
- Father couldn't articulate the rules — they were "in the air" in Swiss banking circles, absorbed over lifetime
- Group codified rules over years at Oscar's Delmonico and other Wall Street spots
- Final form: 12 Major Axioms and 16 Minor Axioms
- Rules evolved gradually, grew shorter/tidier/more useful
- "They are not just a philosophy of speculation; they are guideposts for successful living"

### The First Major Axiom: On Risk
**"Worry is not a sickness but a sign of health. If you are not worried, you are not risking enough."**

- Story of Gerald Loeb's two friends: "Sober Sylvia" and "Mad Mary"
- Both young women at E.F. Hutton, similar financial situations starting out
- Sylvia chose safety: bank accounts, savings, CDs, bonds
- Mary chose risk: stocks, then gold
- Mary's early loss of 25% — but she said "Sylvia, I'm having an adventure"
- Gerald Loeb: veteran of 1930s bear markets and post-WWII bull markets, born poor, died rich
- His book "The Battle for Investment Survival" — most popular market strategy handbook
- Modern psychology's assumption: mental health = calm/tranquility. Zurich Axioms disagree
- Freud understood need for adventure — mocked yoga and eastern "get calm" schools
- Referenced Freud's "Civilization and its Discontents" — anyone who fully achieves inner peace "has sacrificed his life"
- Life ought to be adventure, not vegetation — worry is natural/healthy response to jeopardy
- Sports analogy: athletes and spectators deliberately expose themselves to jeopardy for satisfaction
- Adventure = what makes life worth living; way to have adventure = expose yourself to risk

**The Sober Sylvia vs Mad Mary outcome (decades later):**
- Sylvia: savings accounts, CDs, local authority bonds — bonds lost capital value in 1970s rate spike, inflation eroded rest. Best move was buying house (split equity in divorce). Not rich — working for pension in one-bedroom flat
- Mary: painful start but took risks. Did well in 1960s stocks. Magnificent speculation in gold — bought at $40-50 after Nixon severed gold/dollar link in 1971, sold around $600 when it hit $875. Got rich — owns house, holiday cottage, Caribbean island property, travels first class, quit job

**Key arguments on risk:**
- You'll never get rich on salary or wage income — economic structure rigged against you
- Only hope to lift above the "great unrich" is to take risk
- Bank account gives $100→$109, taxable — barely keeps up with inflation
- Frank Henry: "Don't just think in terms of a salary. People never get rich on salaries"
- Half your speculative ventures will go sour — but that doesn't mean dollar-for-dollar losses if you handle it right
- Risk isn't as dangerous as it sounds — degree of risk usually not very great
- All investment IS speculation — only difference is "some people admit it and some don't"
- Gerald Loeb: "All investment is speculation. The only difference is that some people admit it and some don't"
- It's like difference between "luncheon" and "lunch" — same liverwurst sandwich

**Minor Axiom I: Always play for meaningful stakes**
- If amount at risk is so small you won't miss it, it probably won't bring meaningful gains
- Don't bet amounts that won't make you worry — the worry is the point
- Rule of thumb: risk enough to generate genuine concern but never so much that a single loss would sink you

**"Investment grade" illusion:**
- IBM ("Big Blue") — bought at 1973 peak, wait 9 years to get money back
- General Motors — on all brokerage "investment grade" lists in 1971, bought at peak, you'd STILL be waiting to get money back
- 1929 crash examples: New York Central Railroad $257→$9, Radio Corporation (RCA ancestor) $574→$12, GM $1,075→$40
- Calling it an "investment" doesn't change facts: a gamble is still a gamble
- Gerald Loeb: "All investment is speculation. The only difference is that some people admit it and some don't"
- Like difference between "luncheon" and "lunch" — same liverwurst sandwich

**J. Paul Getty story (Minor Axiom I in action):**
- Most people think he inherited wealth — wrong. Made his monumental pile starting as ordinary middle-class speculator
- Father George F. was Minneapolis lawyer/self-taught speculator, struck rich in Oklahoma oil boom
- George F. had stern Work Ethic beliefs — no pampering sons, no gifts after old enough to earn own living
- Most valuable thing from father was instruction, not money — just couple modest loans on no-excuses repayment schedule
- J. Paul originally wanted diplomatic corps or writing career, but father's love of speculation was in his blood
- Drawn to Oklahoma oil — worked as roustabout and tooldresser, accumulated few hundred dollars
- Could have bought tiny $50 shares in wildcatter syndicates — but knew he'd never get rich on tiny shares
- Instead bid $500 (nearly entire wad) on half share of oil lease near Stone Bluff
- January 1916: first test well hit pay dirt, 700+ barrels/day of crude. Sold interest for $12,000
- "Of course I was lucky... But even if I had lost, that wouldn't have changed my conviction that I was right to take the chance"
- "By taking a chance — a pretty big chance — I gave myself the possibility of getting somewhere interesting"

**Jesse Livermore anecdote:**
- Tall, handsome, light-blond hair, drew crowds on Wall Street
- Reporter asked if being millionaire was worth the strife — Livermore said he liked money a lot
- "Every occupation has its aches and pains. If you keep bees, you get stung. Me, I get worried. It's either that or stay poor."
- Made and lost four huge fortunes speculating in stocks
- He and Frank Henry drinking at bar — Livermore forgot dinner party, explained he gets distracted/forgetful during chancy moves
- Frank Henry noted there was never a time Livermore wasn't in a chancy move
- Livermore: "I don't think I'd enjoy life half as much if I always knew how rich I was going to be tomorrow"
- Unfortunately Livermore didn't have all the other Axioms — his story didn't end happily

**Minor Axiom II: Resist the allure of diversification**
- Diversification = spreading money thin, lots of little speculations instead of few big ones
- Idea: if 6 investments get nowhere, maybe 6 others get somewhere — safety through variety
- Three major flaws of diversification:
  1. Forces you to violate Minor Axiom I (meaningful stakes) — more you diversify, smaller each bet gets
  2. Creates situation where gains and losses cancel each other out — back to Point Zero
  3. Become juggler with too many balls — can't take defensive action when things go wrong, get paralyzed
- Example: $5,000 diversified into 10 bets of $500. Even if Hoo Boy Computer doubles, $500 gain won't make you rich. Meanwhile boom conditions make fixed-interest stock drop $100 and gold drop $300. Net: zero.
- Author's personal rule: no more than 4 speculations at any one time, usually 3 or less, sometimes just one
- "Put all your eggs in one basket, and then watch the basket" — Wall Street saying that stands scrutiny
- Don't diversify for sake of diversity — like filling basket in supermarket shopping contest with expensive junk you don't want
- Only put money into ventures that genuinely attract you

**Speculative Strategy (First Axiom summary):**
- Put your money at risk. Don't be afraid of getting hurt a little
- Degree of risk usually not hair-raisingly high
- Price for this glorious chance is worry — but worry is not sickness, it's "hot and tart sauce of life"
- Once you get used to it, you enjoy it

## Pages 37-40+: The Second Major Axiom: On Greed
**"Always take your profit too soon."**

- Amateurs on Wall Street, in poker, everywhere — stay too long and lose. What makes them do it is greed
- If you can conquer greed, that one act of self-control makes you better than 99% of speculators
- Greed = excessive acquisitiveness, wanting more than you came in for, losing control of desire
- Acquisitiveness (healthy) vs greed (bloated, self-destructive cousin)
- Greed is built into human psyche — can't be exorcised by sermons
- Rich paradox at heart of Second Axiom: by reducing your greed, you improve chances of getting rich

**Sherlock Feldman story (casino manager, the Dunes, Las Vegas):**
- Beefy man, thick-rimmed glasses, sad good humor — observed patrons from 2am to 10am daily
- "If they wanted less, they'd go home with more" — his axiom on greed
- Made and lost several small fortunes in youth, finally learned to control himself, died comfortably rich
- Story of woman at roulette: started with $10 on single number, won $350, kept betting, built to $9,800
- She wasn't satisfied — wanted $10,000 (round number). Kept going, lost everything including original $10
- Swiss version: "Don't push your luck" / "Don't stretch your luck"

**Key greed arguments:**
- Winning streaks are rare; short modest ones vastly more common. Long winning streaks make news precisely because they're rare
- Always bet on the short and modest. Don't let greed get you — cash out and walk away
- You WILL sometimes regret having walked away — winning set continues without you. This depressing experience happens to every speculator
- But for every time quitting early turns out wrong, there'll be dozen times it turns out right
- "Always take your profit too soon" = cash out before winning events reach peak. Don't try to squeeze last dollar from a set
- Mountain on foggy night metaphor: peak is somewhere above you, sheer drop on other side. Can't see it. Only sensible course: stop climbing when you reach a good height. Stop short of peak. Stop TOO SOON.
- You may find you're only halfway up — but don't nurse regret. You ARE up. You've made a solid gain.

**Alice and Harry story (Connecticut couple, real estate greed):**
- Bought house in Fairfield County in early 1970s, stretched financially
- House value multiplied ~2.5-3x in decade. With mortgage leverage, capital had sixfolded
- Should have sold. But greed got them — heard about Marin County homes tenfolding
- Alice: "If our house tenfolded, we'd be millionaires!"
- Harry's motivation: fear of regret — "Suppose we sell and the guy I sold it to resold for triple?"
- Held on. Real estate collapsed 1981-1982. Couldn't sell for a year. Only offer was barely above purchase price
- Last seen waiting for market recovery, determined to sell "too soon" next time

**Fear of regret:**
- Carrying out Second Axiom's hardest difficulty — fear of regret
- "Never check the price of a stock you've sold" — Wall Street ancient teaching, designed to protect from "left-behind blues"
- Author's own experience: sold Gulf Oil at $31, watched it soar to nearly $60. Dumped 1,500 shares IBM at $70, stock leaped to $130
- Must not torture self — instead congratulate self on all the times selling too soon was brilliantly correct
- "The blues come creeping in the night" — no analgesic for it, must simply endure

**South American speculator story (fear of regret paralysis):**
- Frank Henry's friend, too emotional for Wall Street — always getting pockets emptied in high-stakes poker
- Had bought Wometco Enterprises (TV/movie), price rose then faltered, sensibly sold out at good profit
- Then price quadrupled due to unforeseen events — threw him into frenzy of rage and regret
- Became afraid to sell ANYTHING — fear that history would repeat, stock would zoom after he sold
- Put Wometco money into Warner Communications — stock rose, good profit, combined Wometco-Warner had doubled his money
- But couldn't pull the trigger to sell. Warner's Atari video-games division collapsed — stock lost two-thirds of value in dizzy non-stop plunge

**Minor Axiom III: Decide in advance what gain you want from a venture, and when you get it, get out**
- Purpose: answer the paralyzing question "What is enough?"
- Problem: as wealth grows, every new position feels like a starting position, not an ending
- $1,000 becomes $2,000 — doesn't feel like doubling, feels like you've always had $2,000
- Solution: before entering speculation, set a finish line. "I'm going into this with the purpose of..." Keep it modest (double in 2 years, or 50% in 1 year)
- At starting line, the target feels like a prize worth striving for. Nurture this feeling as venture matures
- Only stay past your ending position if dramatic, unforeseen upheaval makes you near-certain the winning set continues (e.g., freak freeze destroys Florida citrus crop while you hold orange juice futures)
- Such situations are rare — most of the time, arrival at ending position should signal: it's over

**Reward yourself at endings:**
- Rig up a reward: new car, coat, expensive dinner at ritziest restaurant
- Frank Henry rewarded with oysters and American steaks (hard to find in Switzerland)
- Jesse Livermore bought antique shaving mugs. Mary (Loeb's friend) bought a new dress or suit
- Gerald Loeb: "Why do you go to all the trouble of making this money? What's it there for? To look at?"
- Loeb was first counselor to say publicly that investor/speculator should SPEND some winnings
- Loeb urged spending a portion of gains in any gainful year — concept of "invading capital" is sacred cow that deserves slaughter
- Author keeps money in Merrill Lynch cash management account — hybrid margin/checking/Visa account. When hitting ending position, uses Visa card for "weekend of sinful luxury in New York"

**Speculative Strategy (Second Axiom):**
- "Sell too soon" — don't wait for peaks, don't hope for winning streaks to continue
- Don't stretch your luck. Expect winning streaks to be short
- When you reach a previously decided-upon ending position, cash out and walk away
- Only exception: dramatic unforeseen change making you near-certain of continued winning

## Pages 51-60: The Third Major Axiom: On Hope
**"When the ship starts to sink, don't pray. Jump."**

- Second Axiom = what to do when things go right. Third Axiom = saving yourself when things go wrong
- Roughly half your speculative ventures will turn sour before reaching preplanned ending positions
- Doesn't mean dollar-for-dollar losses — successful speculators know what to do when tide turns, and unhesitatingly do it
- Knowing how to get out of bad situation may be rarest of all speculative gifts — takes courage and razor-blade honesty

**Martin Schwartz:**
- Former securities analyst, now full-time commodity futures speculator
- 1983: increased his playing money by 175%, won U.S. Trading Championship (Chicago commodity brokerage contest)
- Asked how he achieved such results, told NYT: "I'll tell you how I became a winner. I learned how to lose."
- Sherlock Feldman (casino): asked what makes a good poker player, answered "Knowing when to fold"
- Amateur gambler hopes/prays cards will fall his way; professional studies how to save himself when they fall against him
- Inability to jump off sinking ship has probably cost more speculators more money than any other failing

**Susan Garner story (real estate — office building):**
- Quit Chase Manhattan Bank to speculate full time
- Paid $2,000 for fractional interest in small suburban office building
- Speculation based on planned federal-state highway that would make town a commercial center
- Highway project hit funding problems — postponed 1 year, then 2-3, then 5, then indefinitely
- Lawyer offered $1,500 for her share — she refused (couldn't bear $500 loss)
- He offered $1,600 — still no. Price fell to $1,000, then $800
- "I was angry at myself for not taking $1,500. I kept hoping the situation would improve and vindicate my judgment. The lower the price went, the more stubborn I got"
- While $2,000 was trapped, other speculations beckoned: antique furniture, stocks, stamp album
- Finally sold for $750. Lesson: when the ship starts to sink, jump

**Three obstacles to implementing the Third Axiom:**

**1. Fear of regret** (same as Second Axiom but reversed — fear loser becomes winner after you leave)
- Gold example: bought at $400, collapses to $350, sell at 12% loss — then six wars break out, gold zooms to $800
- Yes it hurts. But such sudden reversals don't happen often. Problems causing price drops tend to be long-lived
- More often than not, correct course is to bail out when price first sags appreciably

**2. Need to abandon part of an investment**
- $5,000 in Italian lira, hunch proves wrong, capital shrunk to $4,000. You abandon $1,000. That hurts.
- Instinct of typical small-time speculator: sit tight, hope to get $1,000 back. But that money is LOCKED UP — should be out chasing gains in livelier ventures
- Worse on margin — resembles poker. Must keep adding money to stay in the game (margin calls)
- Willingness to abandon is usually the more trustworthy response

**3. Difficulty of admitting you were wrong**
- Must say "I was wrong" to broker, banker, spouse, and worst of all, to yourself in the mirror
- Typical loser hangs on hoping future events will vindicate judgment — "This price drop is just temporary"
- Protects ego but bankbook records the truth: while money was stagnating, it could have been working, doubling
- "Refusing to be wrong is the wrongest response of them all"

**Minor Axiom IV: Accept small losses cheerfully as a fact of life. Expect to experience several while awaiting a large gain.**
- Ideally we should WELCOME small losses — they protect from large ones
- Small losses provide excellent protection
- Gerald Loeb's rule: sell when stock retreats 10-15% from highest price held
- Frank Henry gave himself 10-20% leeway. Most seasoned speculators use similar rules
- Key principle: "You take small losses to protect yourself from big ones"
- Example: stock at $100 drops to $85 (15% from high) → sell. Stock at $100 rises to $120 then drops to $100 (17% from high) → sell even though breakeven

**Speculative Strategy (Third Axiom):**
- When ship STARTS to sink — don't wait until half submerged
- Look for trustworthy evidence improvement is coming; if none, take action without further delay
- Jump off before everybody else starts to panic
- Stop-loss orders: standing instruction to broker to sell if stock drops to set level. Advantage: saves agony of deciding. Disadvantage: robs you of flexibility. Author prefers operating without automatic loss-taking mechanism — depend on your own capacity for hard decisions
- "All things come to him who waits" — ancient Chinese proverb. Perfect nonsense in world of money. Don't wait for sagging ventures to improve
- Think of small losses like taxes or electric bills — unpleasant but part of the cost of the speculation business
- "You and your bank account can both grow larger simultaneously"

## Pages 64-80: The Fourth Major Axiom: On Forecasts
**"Human behavior cannot be predicted. Distrust anyone who claims to know the future, however dimly."**

- 1969: CPI rose ~5%, economists predicted rise then taper in early 1970s. It doubled instead
- 1979: CPI at 11.5%, consensus said double-digit through mid-1980s. Back to 1969 levels by 1982
- Nobody has the faintest idea what will happen next year, next week, or even tomorrow

**Failed prophets:**
- Wall Street Journal, August 23, 1929: Dow Theory revealed "major upward trend" in stock prices, "outlook brighter than at any time." Couple months later, everyone went down the drain
- Joseph Granville (stock market guru): early 1981 said "Sell everything!" — market seesawed, he stayed bearish. 1982 saw spectacular bull market, biggest in living memory. Followers who stayed out regretted it earnestly
- 1983: 60% of professional money managers did WORSE than S&P 500 (which rose 22%). Three-fifths of high-paid seers made less than a novice throwing dice
- One celebrated manager predicted interest rates would fall → loaded clients with bonds. Rates rose, bonds plunged. Thought drug stocks would rise → fell. Loaded clients with MCI stock → turned out to be a dog

**The prophet's trick:**
- Dr. Theodore Levitt (economist) told Business Week: "You make twenty-five predictions and the ones that come true are the ones you talk about"
- Economists issue June/July forecasts, revise in September, re-revise in November, again in December. Each oracle prays at least one will be right. If an early one hits: "I foresaw this back in July!" — carefully avoiding mention that it was canceled and superseded
- "Accepting such a prophecy is like buying a ticket that is scheduled to expire before the play is performed"

**Nostradamus:**
- Michel de Nostredame: 16th-century French doctor, hundreds of prophecies in tangled four-line verses
- So indirect/mystical you can interpret them to prove anything. Author studied 100 prognostications: 3 correct, 18 incorrect, 79 such dense gibberish he couldn't tell what old Frenchman was driving at
- Not impressive record — yet made enormous name for himself. Wasn't often right, but was sure often

**Jeane Dixon:**
- Self-advertised psychic, famed for predicting Kennedy assassination
- Wrong predictions: Russia and China would unite, CIO chief Walter Reuther would run for President, cancer cure early in century
- Committee for Scientific Investigation of Claims of the Paranormal (SUNY Buffalo) found her record no better than ordinary person making guesses

**Self-fulfilling prophecies (Granville):**
- So many following Granville that when he said "sell," people sold → market dropped 23 points on the Dow
- But even self-fulfilling prophecies don't reliably self-fulfil. Later in 1981, Granville predicted another plunge on September 28. Instead NYSE scored one of biggest price gains in history. Europe and Japan followed suit
- His followers learned: Granville is like anybody else — wins some, loses some

**Donald L. Rogers (1970, "How to Beat Inflation by Using It"):**
- Listed stocks to buy based on land-owning companies (land = inflation hedge)
- Some picks did well (Warner Communications until mid-1983). Others like ITT were miserable
- Whether you profited depended on luck of which picks you chose — what's the sense of listening to the prophet?

**Value Line Investment Survey (Apple Computer, July 1983):**
- Listed Apple as "Selected Stocks for Performance" at ~$55
- A few months later: down to $17.25
- Every forecast has possibility of unforeseeable events. No forecast about human behavior can be 100% foreseeable events

**Long-term CDs trap (1970s):**
- Economists predicted rates would rise then taper. Banks offered 4-6 year CDs at 7-8% — seemed amazing
- People locked money up based on that forecast. Rates went to 10-11%. People stuck serving six-year sentence at 7%

**Key insight: physical vs human events**
- Tides, sunrise, moon phases = predictable (physical events governed by physical laws)
- Money world = entirely about human behavior: what people think, feel, decide. Millions of unknowable variables
- Stock prices rise/fall because of what men and women are doing, thinking, feeling — not abstract accounting ledger figures
- GNP, inflation, housing starts — all results of human interaction. Recessions, booms, good times, bad — all caused by people. All entirely unpredictable
- "To claim you can make reliable forecasts about this staggering complexity seems arrogant to the point of being ridiculous"

**Speculative Strategy (Fourth Axiom):**
- Don't build speculative program on basis of forecasts — it won't work
- Successful speculator bases no moves on what supposedly WILL happen but reacts to what DOES happen
- Design program on basis of quick reactions to events you can actually see developing
- Don't let hope harden into oracular pronouncement. Never lose sight of possibility you made a bad bet
- If speculation succeeds, stay with it. If it turns sour despite all prophets' promises, remember Third Axiom: get out

## Pages 79-80+: The Fifth Major Axiom: On Patterns
**"Chaos is not dangerous until it begins to look orderly."**

- Irving Fisher, Yale economics professor: made bundle on stock market. Announced in September 1929: "Stock prices have reached what looks like a permanently high plateau." Wiped out in worst crash in Wall Street history
- Fisher's mistake: thought he had beaten the market by being smart, when he had merely been lucky. Saw patterns amid the chaos, believed he could develop formulas to exploit them
- The illusion of order trap: catches millions of investors, speculators, gamblers for all eternity. Not just Wall Street — art galleries, realty offices, casinos, antique auctions
- Entirely understandable illusion: what is more orderly than money? Four quarters always make a buck. Seems amenable to cool, rational analysis and manipulation
- "Everybody is looking for the Formula. Unfortunately, there isn't one"
- Truth: world of money = patternless disorder, utter chaos. Patterns appear like patterns in a cloudy sky or ocean froth — ephemeral, not sound basis for plans
- Fifth Axiom may be the most important of all — the "Emperor Axiom." Once you grasp it, you'll be cleverer than Professor Fisher with all his scholarly attainments

**Art world illusion:**
- Louise Moillon (17th-century French painter): bought at rural auction for $1,500, sold in New York within a year for $120,000
- Sovereign American Art Fund: unit trust proposing to get rich by buying/selling art using expert judgment to spot patterns and emerging trends. IPO at $6/share
- Fund's purchased masterpieces looked promising first — shares traded over $30. Then gloom: obscure artists got obscurer, one expensive painting challenged as forgery. Two years after opening: trading at 75 cents

**Mutual funds prove the same lesson:**
- Best-educated, best-paid, best-equipped investment theorists in the world — platoons of assistants, libraries of financial theory, computers
- If anyone could find usable patterns and a formula, they should have done it long ago
- Sad fact: mutual fund managers win some and lose some, like everybody else
- Forbes charted fund performance in bear markets: 9 out of 10 fell as fast or faster than stocks as a whole
- Funds search doggedly for magic formula because they're paid to and genuinely believe formula exists
- Buying into funds is just as risky as buying individual stocks — same chaos, same rules of play apply

**Alfred Malabre story (WSJ editor, Citibank disaster):**
- Going overseas, gave portfolio to Citibank's management service — one of 10-15 biggest banks on earth
- Malabre's illusion: "What these guys don't know about money probably isn't worth knowing"
- Citibank bought him Avon Products at $119 → traded under $20 two years later. Sears at $110 → sagged to $41.50. IBM at ~$400 → deflated to $151
- Only by taking emergency action on his own did Malabre avert financial disaster
- Lesson: be wary of any adviser who claims to see anything but chaos. The more orderly it looks to them, the less they merit your trust

**How-to-get-rich books:**
- Authors believe they got rich because they found a winning formula. Truth: they got rich because they were lucky
- Any half-baked moneymaking scheme works when you're lucky, none works when you're not
- Zurich Axioms acknowledge luck as most powerful single factor in speculative success or failure
- Most advisers ignore luck, pretend it isn't there, or talk past it rapidly
- Two contradictory advice books: Lewis Owen's "How Wall Street Doubles My Money Every Three Years" (buy at 12-month highs — momentum) vs Samuel Greenfield's "The Low-High Theory of Investment" (buy at 12-month lows — mean reversion). Both can't be right. Neither is — it depends on luck
- "No formula that ignores luck's dominant role can ever be trusted"

**Minor Axiom V: Beware the Historian's Trap**
- Based on age-old belief that history repeats itself → orderly repetition allows accurate forecasting
- Event A followed by Event B in past → Event A happens again → "Event B is about to happen!"
- History repeats sometimes but mostly doesn't — never reliably enough to bet money on
- Consequences usually trivial in daily life but dangerous with money — can wipe you out
- Ubiquitous in financial counseling: analysts go back to last bull market, collect every fact about what was happening (GNP rising, interest rates falling, steel profitable, White Sox in cellar, President's Aunt Matilda had a cold) → wait for same configuration → "A new bull market is on the way!"
- Young woman at Swiss Bank Corporation: inherited capital, studied dollar/lira relationship historically, found pattern (lira rose when Swiss franc rising + US-Soviet relations cool + other indicators). Waited for signal, bought lira → promptly lost value against dollar
- Frank Henry urged her to sell at 15% loss. She refused — "The formula had always been right in the past!"
- "Formulas can be wrong, but markets never are. The market does what it does. It makes no predictions and offers no promises."

**Minor Axiom VI: Beware the Chartist's Illusion**
- Charts/graphs can make disorderly numbers look like a Major Trend
- Chartists believe future prices can be ascertained by charting past price movements
- Look for jiggles and wiggles before big price moves, believe patterns will repeat
- When wrong (often as not), humbly blame self for not being astute enough — can't accept simplest explanation: stock market has no patterns, almost never repeats in reliably predictable way
- "Making charts of stock prices is like making charts of ocean froth. You'll see each pattern once, and then it will be gone"
- Charts make things look orderly even when depicting chaos. Mutual fund sales brochures squeeze years together on chart page, bridge bad years, produce "honey of a chart" with soaring black line
- Can con others AND yourself — upward-sloping line showing lira's yearly highs might have downward-sloping yearly lows. Calm steady change implied by line is illusion — truth is increasing disorder
- "Life never happens in a straight line"

**Minor Axiom VII: Beware the Correlation and Causality Delusions**
- Story of man keeping giraffes away by waving arms: "Doing a good job ain't I?"
- Human mind = order-seeking organ, uncomfortable with chaos, will retreat into fantasy to sort things out
- When two events occur in close proximity, we insist on constructing causal links — makes us comfortable but also vulnerable
- Author's own experience: noticed IBM and Honeywell prices moving in opposite directions over 18 months. Constructed elaborate theory (big investors shifting money between them). Made money a couple times, then BOTH plunged together — lost 25%
- Truth: seemingly orderly price movements were caused by random events that coincided by pure chance
- "Unless you can actually SEE a cause operating, really see it, regard all causal hypotheses with the greatest skepticism"
- Republican First-Year Jinx: stock market consistently slumped in first year of every GOP President (Hoover, Eisenhower x2, Nixon x2, Reagan). Like Super Bowl Omen — chance correlations that happen to fit. Began by chance, will end by chance

**Minor Axiom VIII: Beware the Gambler's Fallacy**
- Perceived order not in the world but in the self — "I'm hot tonight!" / "This is my lucky day!"
- Belief that you're temporarily in a state where random events will be influenced in your favor — a calm island of order
- "Test your luck" advice at casinos: drop a few bucks in slot machine first. If it pays, you're hot — go big. If not, go watch TV
- Everybody believes this at some level — hot nights and cold nights seem real
- Gambler's Fallacy reinforced by stories gamblers love to tell about hot streaks and runs of luck

**Famous gambling stories (illustrating streaks are real but unpredictable):**
- Charles Wells: "The Man Who Broke the Bank at Monte Carlo" — broke the bank on three separate nights in 1891. "Breaking the bank" = exhausting house playing money at a table. Last night played single numbers (37-to-1 odds) — put money on 5, it came up five times in a row. Walked out with 100,000+ French francs (~$1M+ in modern dollars)
- Caroline "La Belle" Otero: perhaps most famous courtesan at Monte Carlo. Brought at age 18 by inept gambler who lost his wad and abandoned her. Down to her last two louis (20-franc pieces, ~$100 each). Bet both on red at roulette, too frightened to watch, walked away. Red came up 28 times in a row. Bank was broken. She was suddenly rich — Monte Carlo's acknowledged queen
- Both Wells and Otero later acted as though afflicted with grandiose Gambler's Fallacy — took long-shot chances assuming they'd stay hot forever. Caroline Otero died broke in a seedy Paris apartment. Charles Wells died broke in jail
- Stories only prove that winning streaks happen. Nothing orderly about them — can't know when they'll start or how long they'll continue. "They are merely one more part of the chaos"

**Speculative Strategy (Fifth Axiom):**
- Don't see order where order doesn't exist — doesn't mean despair of finding good bets
- Study the speculative medium you're interested in; when something looks good, take your best shot
- But don't be hypnotized by illusion of order. Your studying cannot ignore the overwhelmingly large role of chance
- Internal monologue: "I've done my homework. I think this bet can pay off. But since I cannot see or control all the random events that will affect my money, I know the chance of my being wrong is large. Therefore I will stay light on my feet, ready to jump this way or that when whatever is going to happen happens."

## Pages 106-120: The Sixth Major Axiom: On Mobility
**"Avoid putting down roots. They impede motion."**

- Rootlessness is considered bad by modern mental-health theory (like worry). Zurich Axioms disagree
- Nice to have roots — familiar place, old friends, good neighbors. But if you let it impinge on your financial life, it can cost you a lot of money
- Not just geographic mobility — it's a state of mind, a habitual method of organizing your life
- Message comes in two halves, each covered by a minor axiom

**Minor Axiom IX: Do not become trapped in a souring venture because of sentiments like loyalty and nostalgia**
- Janice Shattuck (NJ realtor) story: couple lived in house 20+ years, mortgage paid off, kids gone, biggest asset was home equity. Neighborhood declining — houses in disrepair, rented to college students. Shattuck found a buyer (absentee landlord wanting to use it as dorm). Couple couldn't sell — "We have roots here." Memories, loyalty to neighbors urging them to stay. Neighborhood kept declining, house still unsold, offered price will be drastically lower
- "Get attached to people, but not to houses or neighborhoods"
- Not to companies either: Frank Henry's friend, chief engineer at small manufacturer. Company in serious trouble from Japanese competition. Stock sagging badly. Engineer saw problems were worse than publicly known. Should have sold but felt loyalty — board chairman was buying MORE stock as PR gesture (SEC required him to disclose). Engineer sat tight out of loyalty. Both ended with worthless stock. Frank Henry later wondered if chairman had been secretly shorting the stock in a second brokerage account while publicly buying

**Minor Axiom X: Never hesitate to abandon a venture if something more attractive comes into view**
- Common trap: rooted to something you're waiting to pay off — speculation/hobby dilemma (art/stamps collection), or just waiting for a dog stock to move
- Example: $10,000 in Hoo Boy Computer aiming for $15,000. Hoo Boy goes nowhere year after year. Hey Wow Electronics looks more promising. Common reaction: "I can't sell now, I've got to wait for my payoff!" But it's the same money no matter where it's invested
- Never get rooted because of feeling investment "owes" you something
- Only thing you lose by switching = dealer/broker commission. Capital gains tax impact likely minor if stock hasn't moved
- Possibility of regret is same whether you switch or stay — so leave it out of the calculation entirely
- Decision should ride solely on: which speculation, in your judgment, offers best promise for speedy payoff?

**Speculative Strategy (Sixth Axiom):**
- Preserve your mobility. Stay footloose, ready to jump away from trouble or seize opportunities quickly
- All moves should be made after careful assessment, not for trivial reasons
- But when venture is clearly souring or something clearly more promising comes into view — sever those roots and go
- "Don't let those roots grow too thick to cut"

## Pages 118-120+: The Seventh Major Axiom: On Intuition
**"A hunch can be trusted if it can be explained."**

- A hunch = mysterious piece of feeling-stuff, not-quite-knowledge that feels like knowledge but doesn't feel perfectly trustworthy
- Speculators are hit by hunches frequently. Three approaches:
  1. **Scorn**: ignore all hunches, insist on facts only. But the "facts" (charts, forecasts) are often "pretty goofy material" — seems more trustworthy than hunches but isn't necessarily
  2. **Indiscriminate trust**: lean on hunches too hard, too often, without skepticism. "I go with my hunches" — neglecting that they quite often lead to calamity
  3. **Discriminating use** (the Zurich approach): some hunches are valuable, some are garbage. The challenge is discerning which

**What IS a hunch?**
- Not ESP or occult powers — a perfectly ordinary mental event
- Based on actual solid information stored in your mind that you don't know you possess
- Dr. Eugene Gendlin (U. of Chicago psychologist): common human experience to know something without knowing how you know it
- You take in colossal amounts of data daily — vastly more than you can store/recall consciously. Most stored in reservoir just below or behind conscious level
- Face recognition analogy: you recognize a friend instantly but can't list the specific clues. The information is in your head, but you don't know what or where it is
- "A good hunch is something that you know, but you don't know how you know it"

**Maine coast real estate story:**
- Woman speculating in real estate, renovated old seashore house in Maine, trying to sell
- One offer was close but just shy of her ending position — holding for more
- 3am: woke with powerful hunch that she should take the offer, that market for old Maine coast homes was about to soften
- She had studied Maine coast economy for years — local papers, property owners' association, realtors, Business Week reader
- Most information stored below conscious level. Hunch arose from connections in that huge nonconscious data bank — forgotten bits of data drifting together like jigsaw puzzle pieces
- Trusted the hunch, accepted the offer. A month later it was apparent she'd made a brilliantly right move — highest offer she'd see for a long time

**Testing hunches:**
- When hit by a hunch, first ask: does a big enough library of data exist in your mind to have generated it?
- If it's about Maine coast real estate and you've studied it for years → plausible, trust it
- If it's about Hoo Boy Computer and you barely know the company → not plausible, discard
- Some "hunches" are just airy nothings — brain playing with itself, like dreams. Come from nowhere, mean nothing, lead nowhere
- Internal dialogue test: "What do I really know about this?" If answer is basically nothing → ignore the hunch

## The Eighth Major Axiom: On Religion and the Occult
**"It is unlikely that God's plan for the universe includes making you rich."**

**Minor Axiom XII: If astrology worked, all astrologers would be rich.**
**Minor Axiom XIII: A superstition need not be exorcised. It can be enjoyed, provided it is kept in its place.**

**Protestant minister & swamp land story:**
- Minister from Wädenswil (Frank Henry's boyhood friend) pastored a NJ church
- Elderly parishioner moving south offered to sell 12 acres of undeveloped land at cost as a parting gift to the church
- Minister was ecstatic — real estate values soaring, land in desirable area for homesites
- Frank Henry warned it sounded too good to be true — "instant killings usually turned out to be traps"
- Minister dismissed concern as a gift from God: "Sometimes the Lord punishes us and sometimes He rewards us"
- Congregation bought the land, planned to subdivide into quarter-acre lots
- Building inspector revealed the land was pure swamp — no septic system would work, needed staggeringly expensive drainage
- The church had been had. Moral: you can't pray yourself rich

**Core argument:**
- No evidence God cares whether you die rich or poor
- Bible says maintaining a healthy soul means being better off poor
- Abraham Lincoln: God must have had special love for the poor since He made so many of them
- Leaning on supernatural help produces same result as leaning on forecasts — illusion of order, dangerously unworried state
- "God will protect me" = heavenly purse insurance. Don't count on it.

**Jesse Livermore's occult connection (extended story):**
- Born poor on Massachusetts farm, went to Boston in 1893, got job at stockbrokerage chalking prices on blackboards
- Developed uncanny ability to guess price movements from bucket shops (betting parlors for stock prices)
- Started with nickels and dimes, quickly amassed $2,500 — enormous for a young man then
- Sharpened skills until bucket shops told him to take his money elsewhere
- Moved to Wall Street, became one of cleverest speculators, famous before age thirty
- Flowing blond hair, icy blue eyes, married three times, kept mistresses across America and Europe
- Constantly gnawed by question: was he clairvoyant?
- 1906: shorted Union Pacific on a hunch during a bull market — broker was perplexed. Next day (April 18, 1906) San Francisco earthquake destroyed millions in UP track/property. Made $300,000.
- "Clairvoyant" label stuck, though it was just luck — random events crash around blindly
- When luck/clairvoyance forsook him, he'd try to build a rational system instead
- 1940: bankrupt for 4th time, wrote *How to Trade in Stocks* — a hymn to patterns, "Pivotal Points" and "Secondary Reactions." It was "perfect nonsense"
- December 1940: walked into Sherry-Netherland Hotel, drank two old-fashioneds, went to men's room, shot himself dead
- Frank Henry: "There were twenty different Livermores. I only knew one of him."
- His financial affairs were in disarray for 4th time — facing truth that his approach was decidedly fallible

**Author's Tarot card story:**
- Became proficient in Tarot readings while researching card games for a magazine article
- Godnick & Son (Wall Street broker-dealer) story: shabby man walked in, put entire $5,000 savings into calls on Control Data at $30. Why? Hot tip from Tarot cards.
- Six months later Control Data was over $100. Man cashed out with $60,000+ check. Triple-quadrupled his money.
- Author owned Control Data himself at $60, planned to sell at $120. Bert Godnick suggested consulting Tarot if unsure.
- Did a Tarot reading: Control Data had "perfectly glorious future." Never seen a layout so sure.
- Hooked by the Tarot, didn't sell at $120, just watched. Stock climbed to $155.
- At $155, second Tarot reading: "Violent change and ghastly misfortune." Immediately sold out.
- Stock struggled to $160, then plunged. Nine months later: $28. "The Tarot had saved me!"
- But came to senses: just a couple strokes of luck. Put Tarot deck away, vowed never to use it for money again.
- Lunch with self-styled Tarot master who assured Tarot was best way to achieve financial goals. When check came, Tarot master bummed cab fare off the author. "Temporary cash-flow problem."

**Charles Kellner of Hillsdale, NJ:**
- Plays superstitions perfectly — serious money in real estate/restaurants with no supernatural beliefs
- But plays NJ state numbers game using tips from dreams
- Dreamed of haunted house #283, bet it, won $500. Dreamed of mother's old house number, bet it, won again.
- Wife Dolores: "He's Charlie the Three-Digit Dreamer. He makes more money per hour asleep than he ever did awake."
- Uses superstition only for inconsequential matters (lottery), never for serious investments
- Key: do it humorously, only when situation defies rational analysis

**Speculative Strategy:**
- Money and the supernatural are an explosive mixture — keep them apart
- No evidence any occult belief produces consistently good financial results
- Most anybody can show is an occasional, isolated bull's-eye hit (proves nothing except lucky flukes happen)
- Expecting help from God or occult powers is not just useless but dangerous — lulls you into unworried state
- In handling your money, assume you are entirely on your own. Lean on nothing but your own good wits.

## The Ninth Major Axiom: On Optimism and Pessimism
**"Optimism means expecting the best, but confidence means knowing how you will handle the worst. Never make a move if you are merely optimistic."**

**Minor Axiom XIV: Never follow speculative fads. Often, the best time to buy something is when nobody else wants it.**

**Optimism vs. Confidence distinction:**
- Optimism has always had good press — cheerful souls, good company in gloomy times
- 1930s: nationwide network of Optimist Clubs — "things would get better if only people believed they were getting better"
- General buoyancy ("I'll learn. I'll do well") is fine and even necessary
- But be extremely wary of optimism applied to specific money ventures — can be dangerous state of mind
- Professional gamblers know this: optimism is one of their most effective tools for emptying amateur pockets

**Poker analogy:**
- Pro: if odds say don't bet, he folds. Abandons what he's contributed to the pot, saves himself from bigger loss.
- Amateur: befuddled by optimism. "Maybe I'll be lucky, maybe I'll draw my card, maybe the guy is bluffing..."
- Amateur beats the odds just often enough to keep crazy optimism alive. Keeps investing money in losing hands.
- Pro doesn't have optimism — he has confidence. Confidence springs from the constructive use of pessimism.

**Sam and Judy's land development disaster:**
- Young yuppie couple (advertiser + pediatric resident) in San Francisco suburbs
- Had parlayed $12,000 nest egg to $25,000 through lucky speculation
- Learned about vast land development in southwestern state — corporation had overextended, was selling outlying lots at bargain prices
- Could buy impressively big acreage cheap — if development continued, could double/triple money
- But huge "if": corporation might go bankrupt, roads/utilities might never reach their lots
- They were optimistic, thought risk worth taking. Didn't ask how they'd save themselves if cards fell against them.
- Unlike IBM stock (liquid, can sell out), their land had no exit — no buyers if corporation failed
- Could have bought higher-priced land closer to developed segment (had an exit) but optimism made them go for the big score
- Corporation no longer exists. Roads and utilities never came. Sam and Judy stuck with inaccessible wilderness land, may never sell it. Life-long trap.

**Optimism's hypnotic allure (the Sirens analogy):**
- Optimism feels so much better than pessimism — like the Sirens of Greek legend luring sailors to death on the rocks
- Any venture has limitless possible futures, good and bad, equally likely. But which feels more likely to you? The good kind.
- Optimism is altogether human and probably incurable — the very act of betting money is an optimistic statement
- Paradox: optimism, which feels so good and may be necessary, can lead to financial doom if uncontrolled

**Wall Street optimism bias:**
- WSJ and NYT business journalists phone brokers/analysts every afternoon after markets close
- Each journalist has a favorites list. Qualifications for the list: accessibility, articulateness, and optimism
- Author's count: at least 3/4 of market readings in columns are optimistic — decidedly lopsided
- Two reasons: (1) bulls genuinely outnumber bears because optimism feels better, (2) journalists prefer interviewing bulls — the song is sweeter
- One unnamed officer of a major brokerage quoted constantly, always wrong — predicted bull market through 1980-81, finally right in August 1982, bull market arrived, then petered out spring 1983. "Never mind, just a temporary pause!" Dow would top 1300. It didn't. By Q1 1984 it slumped toward 1100.
- In April after one good day (+20 pts), quoted in NYT as saying it was "the beginning of the second great leg of the bull market." Next day Dow gave up half the gain. Day after that, the rest.

**Odysseus analogy:**
- Odysseus got past the Sirens by plugging crewmen's ears with wax and having himself roped to the mast
- No such defense against the song of the optimist — you'll never block it entirely (you're human)
- Best defense: stay aware of the optimistic bias in your internal compass and stay alert to its dangers

**Speculative Strategy:**
- Never make a move if you are merely optimistic
- Before committing money to a venture, ask how you will save yourself if things go wrong
- Once you have that clearly worked out, you've got something better than optimism — you've got confidence

## The Tenth Major Axiom: On Consensus
**"Disregard the majority opinion. It is probably wrong."**

**René Descartes as the model speculator:**
- World's champion doubter — refused to believe anything until he verified it himself
- Fled France to Netherlands to escape religious authorities angered by his doubting
- Hit upon *Cogito, ergo sum* — "I think, therefore I am"
- Made major contributions to mathematics, built philosophy of unsurpassed lucidity
- Also a successful gambler-speculator: fascinated by Amsterdam's stock/commodity exchanges
- Often traveled to Paris under assumed identity to avoid arrest for heresy, and gambled
- Studied card, board, and spinning-wheel games with customary skepticism, rejecting all folk wisdom
- Seems always to have gone home from Paris richer, sometimes a lot richer
- Lived comfortably on small inheritance + gambling winnings. Died well off.
- His trick: disregard what everybody tells you until you've thought it through yourself
- "Scarcely anything has been pronounced by one [learned person] the contrary of which has not been asserted by another"
- "It would avail nothing to count votes... in the matter of a difficult question, it is more likely that the truth should have been discovered by few than by many."

**Majority opinion in financial life:**
- In democratic age, trained from school to accept majority will — "The people have spoken. It must be right."
- This spills into financial lives: we listen to economists, bankers, advisers, AND majorities
- Standard investment clichés accepted by majority as truth: "A bird in the hand..." "Build a diversified portfolio." "Only bet what you can afford to lose."
- Majority believe these are unarguable truths. Instructive to note: the majority of people aren't rich.

**Auto industry contrarian opportunity (early 1980s):**
- Massive troubles: plants shutting down, thousands unemployed, GM halved its dividend, Ford paid no dividend at all
- Majority opinion (Detroit union halls to Wall Street): auto industry in deep mud for a long, long time
- Stocks sank to dismal lows: GM at $34 in 1981-82, Ford (adjusted for 3-for-2 split) at $11
- Anybody who ignored majority and bought made out fine: GM to $80 in 1983, Ford quadrupled from $11 to $46+
- Industry troubles shorter-lived than most people thought possible

**Author's wife and Ford stock:**
- Wife had a 6-month CD coming due early 1982. Had a hunch about Ford — liked their cars, heard women praising them, believed the panic would pass
- Talked to her broker about buying Ford. He laughed at her.
- Broker was immersed in majority opinion, could document it abundantly — newspaper stories, analyst reports, the low stock price itself all sang: "Don't buy!"
- She didn't buy. Unusual for her — normally capable of independent thinking, but majority pressure was too intense.

**Princeton pencil experiment:**
- 8-10 people at a table, half dozen pencils of assorted colors in the middle
- All same length except one (red one) which was clearly shorter
- Asked to vote on pencil lengths — all but one person (coached ringers) would say all pencils were same length
- In about 1/3 of runs, the non-ringer would cave to majority opinion and agree the pencils were the same length
- Against evidence lying plainly in view — squirming, fidgeting, but finally agreeing with the clearly wrong majority

**Contrarianism warning:**
- Some make a dogma of betting automatically against the majority — "contrary thinkers" or "contrarians"
- Trouble: it starts with a good idea then hardens into a grandiose illusion of order
- Buying solely because something is unwanted is almost as silly as betting unthinkingly with the herd
- Herd isn't always wrong: everybody shunned Chrysler stock rightly — company had one foot in the grave, a long shot. Contrarian bet would have seemed foolhardy (though in hindsight Chrysler recovered to $35 by end 1983, a tenfold gain)
- Key distinction: think independently, don't automatically go with OR against the majority

**Gold fad example:**
- Early 1980s recession: gold prices jumped as people piled in (lockbox of value in uncertain times)
- Newspapers full of ads for gold bullion coins and medallions, brokers touted Homestake and gold-mining stocks
- But by end of 1983, economy rosier, gold price down — couldn't find anybody to sell you a gold medallion
- Best time to buy is when nobody else wants it; best time to sell is when everybody is screaming "Gimme!"

**Speculative Strategy:**
- Majority, though not always wrong, is more likely to be wrong than right
- Guard against betting unthinkingly either with the majority or against — but particularly the former
- Figure everything out for yourself before putting your money at risk

## The Eleventh Major Axiom: On Stubbornness
**"If it doesn't pay off the first time, forget it."**

**Minor Axiom XV: Never try to save a bad investment by averaging down.**

**Perseverance warning:**
- "If at first you don't succeed, try, try again" — good advice for spiders and kings (who are usually born rich)
- For ordinary people struggling to make a buck, this advice should be heeded selectively
- In speculation, perseverance can be useful sometimes but can also lead to financial doom

**Sears-obsessed woman (Merrill Lynch story):**
- Woman at U. of Chicago, admired Sears for its philanthropy (donated Encyclopaedia Britannica to the university)
- In college vowed to invest only in companies doing substantial social good. At age ~40, decided Sears was what she wanted.
- Bought first packet of Sears stock. Stock nose-dived — customers stayed out of stores for 12 months. Share prices of big retailers plunged.
- Acting on Third Axiom (on hope), she sold out taking 15-20% loss. Put money in bank.
- Stock went nowhere for a year, then suddenly leaped past her sell point and kept climbing.
- She was baffled and angry — "the left-behind blues." Stock "owed" her money.
- Bought back in at high price (yield below 4%, historically unusual for Sears). Broker argued against it. She stubbornly insisted.
- Stock slumped again. She chased Sears for years — over tops of bull markets and down into bear markets
- Finally in late 1982, Sears turned in a winning performance. She felt satisfied. But all those years chasing one stock, she missed other opportunities. Only slightly better off than when she started — could have been a heavy loser if not for blind luck.

**Frank Henry's real estate stubbornness:**
- Kept buying in/out of real estate near Morristown, NJ after losing money there
- Was *damned* if he was going to sit still for it — investment "owed" him

**Author's IBM confession:**
- Author had same fixation with IBM stock — "the doggone stock owes me money"
- Though he doesn't trade IBM anymore, still imagines buying IBM puts or calls, gets irritated when they rise without him
- How can an investment medium "owe" you money? It's illogical to personify stocks/metal/art with thoughts about "owing"

**Why perseverance traps us — three emotional drivers:**
1. Personification of the investment: "This investment took money from me, and by God I'm going to haunt it until it pays me back!"
2. Feelings of vengeance: "I'll teach that stock to make a fool out of me!"
3. Wish to have judgment vindicated: "I'll be proved right in the end."

**Averaging down trap (Minor Axiom XV):**
- Buy 100 shares of Hoo Boy Computer at $100 ($10,000 total). Price plunges to $50.
- Friend Numbsley Skullbone counsels: buy 100 more at $50, average cost drops to $75. Magic!
- But it's self-deception: you still paid $100 for the first 100 shares. The "average" doesn't change that.
- Real danger: encourages you to disregard Third Axiom (on hope) — "when the ship starts to sink, don't pray, jump"
- Averaging down gives an excuse for inaction: "I don't have to sell now, I'll just buy more and average down..."
- Frank Henry knew a man who talked himself into being *happy* when his speculations slumped — would buy more, average cost fell, felt smarter. Didn't make him rich — stuck in bad investments for years.
- Test: "Would I buy Hoo Boy at $50 if I didn't already own a bundle I'd bought at $100?" If no, don't throw new money in.
- Why does the gain have to come from the same investment where you lost? It'll be the same good, spendable cash no matter where it comes from.

**Speculative Strategy:**
- Perseverance is good for spiders and kings but not always for speculators
- Don't chase an investment in a spirit of stubbornness
- Reject the thought that an investment "owes" you something
- Don't buy the alluring but fallacious idea that you can improve a bad situation by averaging down
- Value the freedom to choose investments on their merits alone

## The Twelfth Major Axiom: On Planning
**"Long-range plans engender the dangerous belief that the future is under control. It is important never to take your own long-range plans, or other people's, seriously."**

**Minor Axiom XVI: Shun long-term investments.**

**George and Martha's long-range plan disaster:**
- Married in 1940s. George was an accountant at a small CA firm, Martha a secretary at an insurance agency
- Martha's father (small businessman) suggested they construct a Long-Range Plan with a financial counselor
- Plan: retire on combined pension + Social Security of $700/month ($8,400/year — peak of affluence in 1940s when top income bracket was "$7,500 and over")
- Plan to save $20,000 by age 65 to buy a small house with cash (no mortgage)
- Today $700/month barely rents a small apartment. They don't have the $20,000 either.
- In 1960s, George's employer tangled in falsified financial records scandal, CA firm went belly-up. George's job and planned pension vanished.
- Found another job but never achieved planned retirement income
- Several times George could have jumped to promising new direction (e.g., friend's CA practice — now prosperous) but the plan made him feel safe. Too risky to deviate.
- Now retired in their 60s, nearly broke, eating canned beans, wondering what happened
- "Two things happened: planning, followed by the unexpected"
- They got themselves rooted in the plan, missed opportunities, and the unexpected destroyed their assumptions

**Future visibility metaphor:**
- Can dimly see structure of next week — can make a rough financial plan for next Wednesday
- A month ahead: visibility dims markedly. A year: fogged almost to opacity.
- Ten, twenty years: no visibility at all. "Like peering into a pea-soup fog in the dead of night. Whatever is waiting for us out there is entirely unknowable."
- "If you can't know what you're planning for, how can you construct a sensible plan?"

**The illusion of order in planning:**
- Economists, financial advisers, life-insurance salesmen all talk as though the money world changes slowly and predictably, like a tree growing
- They peer into the next century and see a world that's basically like this one, only more so — more automated, more this, more that
- They observe current trends and extend them into the future. Very tidy, allows for lots of long-range plans.
- What they fail to recognize: the money world is only in a limited sense like a tree growing
- Some trends will peter out or reverse. Nobody knows which ones. New trends will spring into existence from factors nobody today dreams of.
- Unknowable events will take us by surprise: booms, busts, upheavals, wars, crashes, collapses

**Paula W.'s long-term Ford investment disaster:**
- Swiss Bank Corporation executive told the story
- Ford production-line worker who worked her way up to management, accumulated thousands of Ford shares
- Husband died in her 50s, sold house in Detroit suburb + Florida cottage, put all money into Ford stock
- Had ~20,000 shares paying $2.60 dividend = $52,000/year income. Comfortable with early retirement pension.
- Broker warned trouble was brewing in auto industry, suggested switching to utility stocks (9-15% yield). Paula refused — long-term investment, no plans to sell, didn't even check stock price.
- 1980: Ford cut dividend from $2.60 to $1.73. Paula's income down to $34,600.
- 1981: Ford cut to 80 cents. Income: $16,000.
- 1982: Ford paid no dividend at all. Paula desperate — had to sell 4,000 shares at appallingly low prices to pay living expenses. Down to 16,000 shares.
- 1983: Ford recovering, 50-cent dividend. Had to sell another 2,000 shares. Down to 14,000 shares. Income: ~$7,000.
- 1984: Dividend $1.20, 14,000 shares = $16,800. Kept her alive but wasn't what she'd envisioned.

**Jesse Livermore quote on long-term investing:**
- "I believe it is a safe bet that the money lost by [short-term] speculation is small compared with the gigantic sums lost by so-called investors who have let their investments ride."
- "From my viewpoint, the [long-term] investors are the big gamblers. They make a bet, stay with it, and if it goes wrong, they can lose it all."
- "The intelligent speculator will... by acting promptly, hold his losses to a minimum."

**Long-term investment traps (sales pressure):**
- Companies offer employee stock purchase plans — convenient payroll deductions. Roots you in one stock.
- GM stock was above $90 in 1971. Hasn't been close since. Imagine being "stuck" in a long-term GM investment.
- Monthly investment plans from brokers/mutual funds: encourage you to concoct long-range plans
- Mutual fund salespeople show charts in four colors proving how great their past performance was (or if it was terrible, show projections of how great the future will be)
- Life insurance industry: two kinds — those that root you in long-term investment (whole life/annuity) and those that don't (term). Author recommends term insurance only.
- Buy cheapest term insurance for dependents. It locks you into nothing.

**The only long-range plan you need:**
- The intention to get rich. How you will accomplish that is something you cannot know except in the most general way.
- Stay light on your feet like the grasshopper, not rooted like the ant
- React to events as they unfold in the present. When you see opportunities, go for them. When you see danger, jump out of the way.
- Re-evaluate every investment at least every three months: "Would I put my money into this if it were presented to me for the first time today?"
- Don't get stuck, don't get rooted — like the ant, a potential victim for fate's bulldozer
- The Twelfth Axiom says there is only one long-range financial plan you need: the intention to get rich. The *how* is not knowable or plannable. All you need to know is that you will do it somehow.
