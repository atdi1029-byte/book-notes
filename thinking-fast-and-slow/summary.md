# Thinking, Fast and Slow — Summary

**Author:** Daniel Kahneman
**Published:** 2011
**Pages:** 499 (+ appendices)

## One-Sentence Summary
The human mind operates through two systems — fast intuitive thinking (System 1) and slow deliberate reasoning (System 2) — and understanding how they interact, where they fail, and how they shape our choices is the key to better judgment and decision-making.

## Core Framework
Kahneman organizes decades of research with Amos Tversky and others around a central metaphor: System 1 (automatic, effortless, associative) and System 2 (controlled, effortful, rule-following). System 1 generates impressions and feelings that System 2 usually endorses with minimal scrutiny. This division of labor is efficient but produces systematic biases — anchoring, availability, representativeness, overconfidence, loss aversion, framing effects — that persist even among experts and even when people are warned about them.

## The Five Parts

**Part 1: Two Systems** — Introduces the System 1/System 2 framework through demonstrations (pupil dilation, the bat-and-ball problem, the invisible gorilla). Establishes that System 1 is the default operator and System 2 is lazy, easily depleted, and often just rubber-stamps System 1's output. Key concept: WYSIATI (What You See Is All There Is) — System 1 builds the best story from available information and ignores what's missing.

**Part 2: Heuristics and Biases** — The classic research program. Anchoring (random numbers shift estimates), availability (ease of recall substitutes for frequency), representativeness (similarity substitutes for probability), regression to the mean (misunderstood as causal), and the conjunction fallacy (Linda the bank teller). Small samples produce extreme results; people see patterns in randomness; base rates are ignored when vivid individual information is present.

**Part 3: Overconfidence** — The narrative fallacy makes the past seem predictable. Experts are systematically overconfident (CFOs' confidence intervals miss 67% of the time). Simple algorithms consistently beat clinical judgment across 200+ studies. The planning fallacy causes chronic underestimation of time and cost. The premortem technique is a practical antidote.

**Part 4: Choices** — Prospect theory replaces expected utility theory. People evaluate outcomes as gains and losses relative to a reference point, not as final wealth states. The value function is S-shaped: concave for gains (risk aversion), convex for losses (risk seeking), and steeper for losses (loss aversion ratio ~2:1). The fourfold pattern explains simultaneous lottery-buying and insurance-purchasing. Framing effects reverse preferences for identical options. Mental accounting leads to the disposition effect, sunk-cost fallacy, and narrow framing.

**Part 5: Two Selves** — The experiencing self lives in the present; the remembering self constructs the story afterward. The peak-end rule and duration neglect mean memories of experiences are governed by their most intense moment and their ending, not their length. Colonoscopy patients preferred objectively more painful procedures that ended gently. Well-being research shows experienced happiness plateaus at ~$75K income while life satisfaction keeps rising. The focusing illusion: nothing matters as much as you think it does while you're thinking about it.

## Why It Matters
This book is the definitive synthesis of behavioral economics and judgment research. It provides a vocabulary (anchoring, availability, WYSIATI, prospect theory, loss aversion, narrow framing, peak-end rule) that changes how you think about your own thinking. The insights apply to investing, negotiation, medicine, law, policy, and daily life. Kahneman's honesty about his own susceptibility to the biases he discovered makes the book both humbling and practical.
