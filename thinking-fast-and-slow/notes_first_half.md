## Introduction

### Core Goal: A Vocabulary for Judgment Errors

- Book's imagined setting: the office watercooler — gossip about others' judgments
- Goal: give readers a richer vocabulary for **cognitive biases** — systematic errors
  that recur predictably; a diagnostic label (e.g., **halo effect**) makes a bias
  easier to anticipate, recognize, and counter
- Focus on biases does NOT imply the mind is generally broken — most judgments are
  fine most of the time, but we are often confident even when wrong

### Origins: Kahneman & Tversky

- 1969: Kahneman invited **Amos Tversky** to guest lecture; seminar debate concluded
  people are NOT good **intuitive statisticians**
- Collaboration methodology: invent questions, jointly examine intuitive answers —
  each question was a small experiment. Goal: identify the **intuitive answer** (the
  first one that comes to mind, the one you're tempted by even when you know it's wrong)
- 14-year collaboration — the best work either ever did

### Heuristics Discovered: Representativeness

- **Steve the librarian problem**: "meek and tidy soul, passion for detail" — is Steve
  more likely a librarian or a farmer? Nearly everyone says librarian
- Statistically absurd: **more than 20 male farmers for every male librarian** in the US
- Participants ignored base rates entirely → **representativeness heuristic**

### Heuristics Discovered: Availability

- **Availability heuristic**: judging frequency or probability by ease of retrieval
- Classic demo: Is letter *K* more likely first or third in a word? Most say first
  (easier to generate); in reality K occurs far more often in third position
- Easy retrieval ≠ high frequency

### The 1974 Science Article

- Published in *Science*, challenged two dominant 1970s assumptions:
  1. People are generally rational
  2. Departures from rationality are caused by emotion
- K&T documented systematic errors in *normal* thinking traced to the **design of the
  cognitive machinery itself**
- One of the most highly cited works in social science: 300+ articles citing it in 2010

### Fast vs. Slow Thinking — Expert Intuition

- **Gary Klein**'s story: firefighter commander suddenly shouted "Let's get out!" —
  the fire had been unusually quiet, ears unusually hot; the fire's heart was in the
  basement. He felt danger without knowing why
- Chess master glances at board and announces "White mates in three"

> "The situation has provided a cue; this cue has given the expert access to
> information stored in memory, and the information provides the answer.
> Intuition is nothing more and nothing less than recognition."
> — **Herbert Simon**

### When Expert Intuition Fails: The Ford Stock Example

- Chief investment officer bought **Ford Motor Company** stock because he was impressed
  at an auto show. He never asked the relevant question: *Is Ford stock currently underpriced?*
- He answered an easier question (do I like Ford cars?) — the **affect heuristic**:
  judgments guided directly by feelings of liking/disliking

### The Core Framework: System 1 and System 2

- Fast thinking = **System 1**: automatic, intuitive, effortless — expert pattern
  recognition AND heuristic shortcuts, plus basic perception and memory
- Slow thinking = **System 2**: deliberate, effortful, controlled — kicks in when
  intuitive search fails
- Key principle: when faced with a difficult question, we often answer an *easier,
  related* question instead — usually without noticing the substitution

## Part 1: Two Systems

## Chapter 1: The Characters of the Story

### Two Demonstrations

- **Fast (System 1):** Looking at an angry woman's face — you instantly know she is
  angry, predict she will say unkind words, sense the threat. Effortless and automatic.
- **Slow (System 2):** 17 × 24. You must retrieve a cognitive program, execute steps,
  hold intermediate results. Pupils dilate, muscles tense, blood pressure rises.
  Pupils contract immediately upon finding the answer (408) or giving up.

### Naming the Two Systems

- Terms originally proposed by **Keith Stanovich** and **Richard West**
- **System 1**: operates automatically and quickly, no voluntary control
- **System 2**: allocates attention to effortful activities; associated with subjective
  experience of agency, choice, and concentration
- We identify with System 2 — the "conscious, reasoning self." But **System 1 is the
  hero of the book**: it originates the impressions and feelings that become beliefs

### The Invisible Gorilla

- **Christopher Chabris** and **Daniel Simons**: viewers counting basketball passes miss
  a woman in a gorilla suit crossing the court for 9 seconds — about half of all viewers
- **"We can be blind to the obvious, and we are also blind to our blindness"**

### Plot Synopsis: How the Two Systems Interact

- System 1 continuously feeds System 2: impressions, intuitions, intentions, feelings
- System 2 mostly adopts System 1's suggestions with little modification
- System 2 mobilized when System 1 hits a question it can't answer or detects a violation
  of its world model
- **Division of labor is efficient**: minimizes effort. But System 1 has **systematic
  biases** — answers easier questions and has little understanding of logic or statistics

### Illusions: Visual and Cognitive

- **Müller-Lyer illusion**: two lines appear different lengths; they are identical. Even
  after measuring, you *still see* the bottom as longer — you cannot make System 1 stop
- Key distinction: **you can change your belief (System 2) without changing your
  impression (System 1)**
- **Cognitive illusions** work the same way. Strong attraction to a patient with repeated
  failed treatments is a danger sign, not a genuine connection

> "Do not even think of taking on this patient! Throw him out of the office! He
> is most likely a psychopath and you will not be able to help him."
> — Kahneman's psychotherapy professor

## Chapter 2: Attention and Effort

### System 2's Defining Feature: Laziness

- System 2's main characteristic: **laziness** — a reluctance to invest more effort
  than strictly necessary

### Pupil Dilation as Proxy for Mental Effort

- Psychologist **Eckhard Hess**: pupils widen while viewing beautiful nature photos;
  dilate substantially during mental multiplication — dilation proportional to difficulty
- Kahneman collaborated with **Jackson Beatty** using the **Add-1 task** (hear 4 digits,
  report each incremented by 1 to a metronome beat)
- **Add-3** (increment each digit by 3): pupil dilates ~50% of original area in first
  5 seconds; heart rate increases ~7 bpm. "This is as hard as people can work."
- When overloaded: subjects gave up — pupils stopped dilating or actually shrank
- Response shape: **inverted V** — effort builds with each added digit, peaks during
  transformation, relaxes as memory unloads
- Detection of letter *K* during Add-1 followed the same inverted-V: missed ~half the
  time at peak effort, even with eyes staring straight at it

### Law of Least Effort

- **Law of least effort**: when multiple paths achieve the same goal, people gravitate
  toward the least demanding one — true for cognitive as well as physical exertion
- Skill increases → cognitive demand decreases; fewer brain regions activate
- "Laziness is built deep into our nature"

### What System 2 Can Do That System 1 Cannot

- System 1 detects simple relations; cannot handle multiple distinct topics simultaneously
  or use purely statistical information
- System 2 alone can: follow rules, compare objects on several attributes, make
  deliberate choices
- Task switching is effortful — one major reason Add-3 and multiplication are so hard
- Working memory tests requiring rapid task-switching predict general intelligence and
  Israeli Air Force pilot performance *beyond* IQ effects

## Chapter 3: The Lazy Controller

### System 2's Natural Speed

- Light cognitive tasks run simultaneously with walking at no cost; heavy cognitive
  tasks compete directly — ask someone to compute 23 × 78 while walking and they stop
- **Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi** coined **flow** — effortless concentration; sustained
  focus requires no self-control, freeing resources for the task itself

### Ego Depletion

- **Roy Baumeister**: all voluntary effort — cognitive, emotional, physical — draws on
  a *shared* pool of mental energy
- **Ego depletion**: forcing yourself to do something makes you less willing/able to
  exert self-control on the *next* challenge
- Classic demo: suppressing emotional reactions to a film → give up sooner on a handgrip
  dynamometer task afterward
- **Glucose is the physical reality**: effortful cognition depletes blood glucose; ego
  depletion can be reversed by ingesting glucose
  - Depleted subjects who drank Splenda (vs. glucose lemonade) showed expected depletion
    on a subsequent task requiring intuition override

### Israeli Parole Judges

- 8 Israeli parole judges reviewed ~35% of requests approved overall
- **Parole approval spikes to ~65% right after food breaks**, then drops to near zero
  before the next break — tired, hungry judges fall back on the safe default of denial

### The Lazy System 2: Bat-and-Ball Puzzle

- Classic puzzle (**Shane Frederick**): *A bat and ball cost $1.10. The bat costs one
  dollar more than the ball. How much does the ball cost?*
  - Intuitive answer: 10¢ (wrong — gives total of $1.20)
  - Correct answer: 5¢
- **>50% of Harvard, MIT, and Princeton students give the wrong answer**; >80% at less
  selective universities — ardent followers of the law of least effort

### Intelligence, Control, and Rationality

- **Marshmallow Test** (Walter Mischel): four-year-olds offered 1 cookie now vs. 2
  cookies if they wait 15 min — ~half managed to wait; 10–15 years later: resisters
  had higher executive control, higher intelligence, less drug use
- **Cognitive Reflection Test (Shane Frederick)**: 3 questions each inviting a compelling
  but wrong intuitive answer; low CRT scorers prefer $3,400 now over $3,800 next month
  at 63% rate; only 37% of high scorers show same preference
- **Keith Stanovich's key distinction**: **algorithmic mind** (measured by IQ) vs.
  **reflective mind** (rationality — disposition to check and question); high IQ does
  not make people immune to cognitive biases

## Chapter 4: The Associative Machine

### Associative Activation

- Reading "Bananas Vomit" triggers: unpleasant images, disgust face, physical recoil,
  elevated heart rate, sweat gland activation — an attenuated version of the real experience
- System 1 assumed causal-temporal connection, constructing a story of bananas causing
  sickness; resulted in temporary aversion to bananas
- Key property: **associative coherence** — each element supports and strengthens the
  others in a self-reinforcing pattern
- "Cognition is embodied; you think with your body, not only with your brain"

### The Florida Effect (John Bargh)

- **John Bargh** (NYU): students assembled sentences from word sets; for one group, half
  contained elderly-associated words (*Florida, forgetful, bald, gray, wrinkle*)
- After the task, students walked down a corridor: those primed with elderly words
  **walked significantly more slowly** — none noticed the common theme
- **Ideomotor effect**: the influencing of an action by an idea — reciprocal loop:
  slow walking → faster recognition of words like *forgetful*, *old*, *lonely*

### Primes That Guide Us

- **Arizona voting study (2000)**: support for school-funding propositions significantly
  higher when polling station was *in a school* — effect larger than parent/non-parent
  difference
- **Money priming** (**Kathleen Vohs**): subtle money reminders (Monopoly money, dollar
  screensaver) → persevered 2x longer before asking for help, less willing to assist
  a confused peer, picked up fewer pencils when experimenter dropped them, set chairs
  118 cm apart (vs. 80 cm) — **money primes individualism**
- **Lady Macbeth effect**: thinking of a shameful action → more likely to complete word
  fragments as WASH and SOAP; thinking about stabbing a coworker → prefer soap/disinfectant
  over batteries. Cleansing is body-part specific: lying on phone → prefer mouthwash;
  lying by email → prefer soap

### Honesty Box Experiment

- British university kitchen: rotating posters above price list — eyes watching vs. flowers
- **Eye weeks**: contributed ~70p per liter of milk
- **Flower weeks**: contributions dropped to ~15p — kitchen users paid ~3x more when
  "watched" by symbolic eyes, with no conscious awareness of the effect

## Chapter 5: Cognitive Ease

### The Cognitive Ease Dial

- Brain continuously monitors; one dial measures **cognitive ease** — range from "Easy"
  to "Strained"
- **Cognitive ease**: familiar, true, good, effortless
- **Cognitive strain**: problem detected → vigilant, suspicious, fewer errors but less creative
- Causes are interchangeable: repeated experience, clear display, primed idea, good mood
  all increase ease; you can't always identify which source is driving the feeling

### Illusions of Remembering

- **Larry Jacoby**: fabricated name shown once → days later, subjects identify it as a
  minor celebrity. The truth: seeing a word before makes it *perceptually clearer* the
  next time → ease is misattributed as memory/fame
- **Core mechanism**: cognitive ease → impression of familiarity; sources of ease are
  interchangeable and non-traceable

### Illusions of Truth

- **Illusion of truth**: statements feel true when they produce cognitive ease
- **Reliable way to make people believe falsehoods: frequent repetition**
- Bold, high-contrast statement ("Adolf Hitler was born in 1892") judged more believable
  than plain text — both are false (1889)
- **Danny Oppenheimer**: complex vocabulary signals poor intelligence and low credibility
- **Use rhyme**: aphorisms in rhyming form judged more insightful ("Woes unite foes")
- **Easy-to-pronounce source names**: reports from "Artan" trusted over "Taahhut"

### Strain and the Cognitive Reflection Test

- 40 Princeton students took CRT in normal vs. washed-out gray font
- **Normal font: 90% made at least one error; bad font: only 35% made errors**
- Cognitive strain mobilizes System 2, which rejects intuitive wrong answers
- **Making thinking harder can improve accuracy** for certain problem types

### Mere Exposure Effect and Cognitive Ease

- Stocks with **pronounceable names** outperform tongue-twisting tickers in their first week
- **Robert Zajonc**: repeated exposure to a stimulus creates mild affection — even without
  conscious awareness. Works even when stimuli are shown too briefly to be consciously
  perceived — stronger for stimuli never consciously seen
- **RAT (Remote Association Test)**: find one word linking three seemingly unrelated words
  (*cottage, Swiss, cake* → **cheese**)
  - Good mood more than **doubled** accuracy on RAT; unhappy subjects performed no better
    than chance. Unhappy = disconnected from intuition
  - When subjects were given an alternative explanation for their good feeling (music in
    earphones), the intuition of coherence disappeared — proving emotional response *is*
    the basis of the coherence judgment

## Chapter 6: Norms, Surprises, and Causes

### Assessing Normality

- System 1 maintains and updates a model of your personal world — what is **normal** in it
- Two varieties of surprise: **active** (you were waiting for something that didn't happen)
  vs. **passive** (you weren't waiting, but absence is noticed — e.g., a colleague's usual dog)
- Brain detects violations of normality within **0.2 seconds** of an odd word

### Norm Theory (Dale Miller)

- **Norm theory**: events feel normal by being linked to a prior episode retrieved from
  memory, not because they were expected in advance
- Meeting acquaintance Jon at Great Barrier Reef (surprising), then again in a London
  theater two weeks later — *less* surprised the second time despite being objectively
  more improbable. System 1 normalized it

### The Moses Illusion

- "How many animals of each kind did Moses take into the ark?" — most don't catch the
  error (it was Noah). Moses is not *expected* but is not *abnormal* in a biblical
  context; he and Noah share vowel sounds and syllable count — **associative coherence**
  suppresses detection. Replace "Moses" with "George W. Bush" and the illusion disappears

### Seeing Causes and Intentions

- "Fred's parents arrived late. The caterers were expected soon. Fred was angry." — you
  immediately know why Fred is angry; this causal connection is automatic (**System 1**)
- **Nassim Taleb** (*The Black Swan*): Bloomberg flashed two contradictory headlines on
  the day of Saddam Hussein's capture — each explained opposite bond price movements.
  A statement that can explain two contradictory outcomes explains nothing

- **Albert Michotte** (1945): a black square moves and contacts another, which immediately
  begins moving → observers experience powerful "illusion of causality." Six-month-old
  infants show surprise when cause-effect sequences are altered — we are born with
  **impressions of causality**

- **Fritz Heider** and **Mary-Ann Simmel** (1944): 100-second film of a large triangle,
  small triangle, and circle — viewers spontaneously see: large triangle bullying small
  triangle, terrified circle, the two joining forces. Only people with **autism** do not
  experience this perception of intention

- **Paul Bloom**: our inborn readiness to separate physical and intentional causality
  explains the **near universality of religious beliefs** — these two modes of causation
  were shaped separately by evolution

## Chapter 8: A Machine for Jumping to Conclusions

### Neglect of Ambiguity and Suppression of Doubt

- Figure demonstration: A B C / 12 13 14 — the middle character is identical in both
  displays, but read as "B" in letter context and "13" in number context
- **System 1 resolves ambiguity without awareness** — you pick an interpretation and
  never know alternatives existed
- **Conscious doubt is not in System 1's repertoire** — holding incompatible
  interpretations simultaneously requires mental effort; that is System 2's domain

### A Bias to Believe and Confirm

- **Daniel Gilbert**: understanding a statement requires first *believing* it; unbelieving
  is a subsequent, effortful act. Initial believing = automatic System 1; unbelieving = System 2
- When System 2 was busy (holding digits), subjects later *believed* false sentences were
  true — depleted System 2 couldn't unbelieve them
- **Implication**: people are more susceptible to persuasion when tired and depleted

### The Halo Effect

- **Halo effect**: tendency to like or dislike everything about a person based on a first impression
- **Solomon Asch** classic: same six adjectives for Alan and Ben, in reversed order
  - Alan (intelligent first): rated far more favorably — early traits change the *meaning*
    of later ones (stubbornness in an intelligent person = justifiable; in an envious person
    = dangerous)
- Kahneman's fix for grading: score all answers to question 1 first, then question 2 —
  **decorrelates errors**; the resulting inconsistency reveals prior consistency was spurious

### WYSIATI (What You See Is All There Is)

- System 1 constructs the best story from *currently activated ideas*; information not
  retrieved might as well not exist
- Knowing less makes it *easier* to fit everything into a coherent pattern
- Downstream biases from WYSIATI:
  - **Overconfidence**: confidence reflects story coherence, not evidence quantity
  - **Framing effects**: "90% survival rate" vs. "10% mortality" evoke different emotions
  - **Base-rate neglect**: Steve the librarian story — statistical fact about farmers
    vs. librarians never retrieved

- Tversky study: participants who heard only one side of a legal case were *more confident*
  in their judgments than those who heard both sides — one-sided evidence produces a more
  coherent story → higher confidence, even with less information

## Chapter 9: How Judgments Happen

### Face Reading and Voting

- **Alex Todorov** (Princeton): in a single glance at a stranger's face, we assess
  *dominance* (square chin = threatening) and *trustworthiness* (smile/frown = intentions)
- Students shown campaign portraits for 1/10 of a second, rated competence
- In ~**70% of races** for senator, congressman, and governor, the election winner was the
  candidate rated higher in competence from the brief photo — confirmed in Finland,
  England, Australia, Germany, Mexico
- Effect is ~3x larger for **politically uninformed, TV-heavy viewers**

### Sets and Prototypes

- System 1 instantly registers average length of lines — but cannot register *total* length
- **Average vs. sum distinction**: System 1 represents sets by **prototype**; handles
  averages/norms well but poorly handles **sum-like variables**
- **Exxon Valdez experiment**: three groups asked willingness to pay to save 2,000 /
  20,000 / 200,000 migratory birds
  - Average contributions: **$80, $78, $88** — nearly identical despite 100x quantity range
  - Participants reacted to a **prototype** — the image of one oil-soaked bird

### The Mental Shotgun

- System 1 runs many computations **simultaneously and automatically**
- **Mental shotgun**: you intend one computation but System 1 fires a scatter of related ones
- Experiment: participants pressed key when word pairs rhymed; VOTE–GOAT (rhyme, different
  spelling) caused slower responses than VOTE–NOTE — spelling computed even though irrelevant
- "Some jobs are jails" — literally false, metaphorically true → slower response when judging
  literal truth; metaphorical assessment was evoked and created interference

## Chapter 12: Answering an Easier Question

### Substituting Questions

- When System 1 cannot quickly answer a **target question** (hard), it finds and answers
  a related **heuristic question** (easier) instead — **substitution**
- Distinguished from Pólya's deliberate substitution — the heuristics here are *not chosen*;
  they are a consequence of the mental shotgun

| Target Question | Heuristic Question |
|---|---|
| How much to contribute to save an endangered species? | How much emotion do I feel about dying dolphins? |
| How happy are you with your life these days? | What is my mood right now? |
| How popular will the president be in six months? | How popular is the president right now? |
| How should financial predators be punished? | How much anger do I feel about financial predators? |
| How far will this candidate go in politics? | Does this person look like a political winner? |

### The 3-D Heuristic

- Three men in a corridor: figure on the right *looks* larger — measuring reveals they are
  **identical in size** on the page. System 1 interprets corridor depth cues as 3-D; that
  impression dominates judgment of 2-D printed size. Only visual artists develop the skill
  to see the drawing as flat

### The Mood Heuristic for Happiness

- German student survey: "How many dates did you have last month?" followed by "How happy
  are you these days?" → correlation very high (dates dominated happiness report)
- Reversed order: "How happy are you?" then "How many dates?" → correlation ~zero
- Students substituted "how happy am I with my romantic life?" for "how happy am I overall?"

### The Affect Heuristic

- **Paul Slovic**: people let their **likes and dislikes** determine their beliefs about
  the world — conclusions precede arguments
- Political hawks believe adversary nations are weak; doves believe they are strong
- Information about lower risk also changes perception of *benefit* upward — the underlying
  emotional valence shifted
- **System 2 as apologist, not enforcer**: searches for information *consistent* with
  existing beliefs rather than examining them

## Characteristics of System 1 (Summary List)

- Generates impressions, feelings, inclinations → endorsed by System 2 → become beliefs
- Operates automatically, quickly, with no voluntary control
- Creates coherent associative patterns; links cognitive ease to illusions of truth
- Distinguishes surprising from normal; infers and invents causes and intentions
- Neglects ambiguity, suppresses doubt; biased toward belief and confirmation
- Exaggerates emotional consistency (**halo effect**)
- Focuses on existing evidence, ignores absent evidence (**WYSIATI**)
- Represents sets by norms/prototypes; does not integrate over set size
- Computes more than intended (**mental shotgun**)
- Sometimes substitutes easier for harder question (**heuristics**)
- More sensitive to changes than states (**prospect theory**)
- Overweights low probabilities; shows diminishing sensitivity to quantity
- Responds more strongly to losses than gains (**loss aversion**)
- Frames decision problems narrowly, in isolation from one another

## Part 2: Heuristics and Biases

## Chapter 10: The Law of Small Numbers

### Small Samples and Extreme Results

- Rural counties with high kidney cancer rates — the mind invents a causal story (fresh
  air, simple life). But the low-rate counties are also rural. The real explanation:
  small samples produce extreme outcomes more often than large samples
- Marble urn analogy: draw 4 marbles vs. 7 marbles recording all-red or all-white outcomes
  — the 4-marble sampler sees extreme results **8x more often** (12.5% vs. 1.56%)
- Key asymmetry people miss: "Large samples are more precise than small samples" and
  "Small samples yield extreme results more often" are logically identical statements,
  yet the second feels much more surprising

- K&T named their article **"Belief in the Law of Small Numbers"** — the intuition
  that the law of large numbers applies to small samples too
- Even authors of two statistics textbooks showed the same flawed intuitions when surveyed

### Cause and Chance

- Six baby births: BBBGGG, GGGGGG, BGBBGB are all equally likely, yet people judge BGBBGB
  far more likely because it "looks random." GGGGGG triggers search for cause
- **William Feller**: during WWII bombing of London, maps of rocket hits showed conspicuous
  gaps — people suspected German spies. Statistical analysis showed a typical random distribution

> "To the untrained eye, randomness appears as regularity or tendency to cluster."
> — William Feller

- Kahneman during the **1973 Yom Kippur War**: two squadrons had wildly different loss
  rates; he advised the command to accept the difference as blind luck and stop the inquiry
  — an unfair search for a nonobvious cause

### The Hot Hand Fallacy

- **Tom Gilovich**, **Robert Vallone**, and Tversky: analysis of thousands of shot sequences
  showed **no hot hand** exists in professional basketball — sequences pass all tests of randomness
- **Red Auerbach** (Boston Celtics coach): "Who is this guy? I couldn't care less."
- The illusion scales: how many good years before an investment advisor is "skilled"?
  How many acquisitions before a CEO has "flair for deals"?

### Gates Foundation / Small Schools Error

- Researchers and the **Gates Foundation** found the most successful schools tended to be
  small — 6 of the top 50 were small, an overrepresentation by 4x
- Invested ~$1.7 billion in creating small schools
- **Howard Wainer** and **Harris Zwerling** pointed out the fatal flaw: if you ask about
  the *worst* schools, they also tend to be small. Small schools are not better — they are
  simply **more variable**. Large schools produce better outcomes in higher grades

## Chapter 11: Anchors

### The Wheel of Fortune Experiment

- K&T rigged a wheel (stopped only at 10 or 65), had University of Oregon students spin
  it, then estimate percentage of African nations in the UN:
  - Wheel stopped at 10 → average estimate: **25%**
  - Wheel stopped at 65 → average estimate: **45%**
- A random wheel spin yielded no useful information — yet estimates tracked the anchor

### Two Mechanisms of Anchoring

1. **Anchoring as Adjustment** (System 2): start from anchor, assess if too high or low,
   mentally "move" — but adjustment **stops too early** at the near edge of the uncertainty region
   - Driving off the highway: you remain anchored to highway speed and adjust down insufficiently
   - **Nick Epley** and **Tom Gilovich**: people instructed to shake head moved farther from anchor;
     nodders showed enhanced anchoring; cognitive load keeps people closer to anchor

2. **Anchoring as Priming Effect** (System 1): "Was Gandhi more or less than 144 years old?"
   — you don't adjust down from 144, yet the absurd number still raises your estimate
   - **Thomas Mussweiler** and **Fritz Strack**: high temp anchor (20°C) → faster recognition
     of summer words (sun, beach); low temp anchor (5°C) → faster recognition of winter words
     (frost, ski). The anchor selectively activated compatible knowledge

### The Anchoring Index

- San Francisco Exploratorium (redwood height):
  - High anchor (1,200 feet) → mean estimate: 844 feet
  - Low anchor (180 feet) → mean estimate: 282 feet
  - **Anchoring index: 55%** (ratio of estimate difference to anchor gap)
- Real-estate agents assessed a house with manipulated listing prices: index **41%** for
  professionals; **48%** for business students. Professionals denied any influence — they were wrong
- German judges rolled loaded dice (3 or 9), then sentenced a shoplifter:
  - Dice showing 9 → average sentence **8 months**; showing 3 → **5 months**; index 50%

### Uses and Abuses of Anchors

- **Arbitrary rationing as marketing**: Campbell's soup, "Limit 12 per person" → shoppers
  bought roughly twice as many as without a limit; the limit of 12 served as anchor
- **Negotiations**: first offer sets the anchor; moving first is an advantage
- Counter-strategy: don't submit a matching outrageous counteroffer (implicitly accepts
  the frame). Make a scene, reject the number loudly, refuse to continue with it on the table
- **Adam Galinsky** and **Mussweiler**: better counter is to deliberately focus on arguments
  *against* the anchor — think about the minimal acceptable offer to the opponent

## Chapter 12: The Psychology of Availability

### Schwarz's Fluency Experiments

- **Norbert Schwarz**: does judgment track the **number** of instances retrieved or the
  **ease** of retrieval?
- Participants list either 6 or 12 instances of their own assertive behavior, then rate
  how assertive they are
  - Listing 12 is hard — the last few feel forced, fluency drops sharply
  - **Result**: people who listed 12 rated themselves as **less** assertive than those who
    listed 6 — ease of retrieval dominated over quantity
- Participants listing assertive behaviors while **frowning** rated themselves less assertive
  than smilers — frowning creates genuine cognitive strain

### The Fluency Paradox in Practice

- People believe they ride bikes less often after recalling **many** rather than few instances
- Less confident in a choice after producing **more** supporting arguments
- UCLA professor: students who listed **more** ways to improve a course rated it **higher**
  (fewer suggestions = felt like a good course)

### Conditions That Increase Reliance on Availability

People follow System 1 / ease-of-retrieval more strongly when:
- In a good mood
- Engaged in a concurrent effortful task (cognitive load)
- Knowledgeable novices (vs. true experts)
- Scoring high on faith in intuition
- Made to feel **powerful** — reminding people of a time they had power raises trust in intuition

## Chapter 13: Availability, Emotion, and Risk

### Causes-of-Death Survey

- **Paul Slovic**, **Sarah Lichtenstein**, and **Baruch Fischhoff**: surveyed public
  perception of causes of death vs. actual statistics:
  - Strokes cause ~2x as many deaths as all accidents combined → **80% judged accidental
    death more likely**
  - Tornadoes judged more frequent killers than asthma (asthma is **20x deadlier**)
  - Accidental death judged 300x more likely than diabetes; true ratio is 1:4
- Media coverage is biased toward novelty and poignancy; unusual causes get disproportionate
  coverage → perceived as less unusual than they are

### The Affect Heuristic

- **Affect heuristic** (Slovic): people make judgments by consulting their emotions —
  answering the easy emotional question instead of the hard cognitive one
- Technology survey: reading a message extolling **benefits** of a technology → also
  perceived it as **less risky** (received no evidence about risk). The reverse also held

> "The emotional tail wags the rational dog." — **Jonathan Haidt**

### The Availability Cascade

- **Timur Kuran** and **Cass Sunstein** coined **availability cascade**: a self-sustaining
  chain in which a media story about a minor risk leads to public panic and large-scale
  government action, amplified by **"availability entrepreneurs"**
- **Alar scare (1989)**: chemical sprayed on apples, shown to cause tumors in rats at
  gigantic doses; media cascade → **Meryl Streep** testified before Congress; apple
  industry suffered large losses; FDA banned Alar. Subsequent research confirmed only very
  small cancer risk — **net effect on public health was probably negative** (fewer apples)
- **Probability neglect** (**Cass Sunstein**): minds either **ignore small risks or wildly
  overweight them**, nothing in between — we imagine the numerator (vivid disaster) and
  forget the denominator

## Chapter 14: Tom W's Specialty

### The Tom W Experiment

- **Tom W** personality sketch: high intelligence, lacks creativity, neat/orderly, dull
  writing, sci-fi humor, strong drive for competence, antisocial — deliberately designed
  as an "anti-base-rate" character
- 114 grad students at three universities: probability rankings were **identical** to
  similarity rankings — complete substitution of representativeness for probability
- **Robyn Dawes** — sophisticated statistician — was the first test subject; said
  "computer scientist?" then immediately caught himself when Kahneman mentioned base rate

> "The question about probability was difficult, but the question about similarity was easier,
> and it was answered instead."

- Key distinction: **similarity and probability are not governed by the same logical rules**
  — similarity can ignore base rates; probability cannot

### Moneyball Example

- **Michael Lewis**, *Moneyball*: baseball scouts predicted player success by build and look
  (representativeness). **Billy Beane** (Oakland A's) overruled scouts, selected players by
  statistical performance history — players were cheap because other teams had rejected them
  for not "looking the part"

### Bayesian Reasoning

- Named for **Reverend Thomas Bayes** (18th-century English minister)
- **Bayes's rule**: prior beliefs (base rates) must be combined with the diagnosticity of evidence
  - 3% base rate for CS grad students + description 4x more likely for CS than other fields
    → Tom W is CS with **11% probability** (not ~70%)
- Two Bayesian principles to internalize:
  - **Base rates matter**, even in the presence of individual evidence
  - **Intuitive impressions of diagnosticity are often exaggerated**

## Chapter 15: Linda: Less Is More

### The Conjunction Fallacy

- **Linda**: 31, single, outspoken, bright, philosophy major, concerned with discrimination,
  participated in antinuclear demonstrations
- Critical comparison: "Linda is a bank teller" vs. "Linda is a bank teller and is active
  in the feminist movement"
- **Logic**: feminist bank tellers are a *subset* of bank tellers — P(feminist bank teller)
  *must* be ≤ P(bank teller)
- Yet: **89% of undergraduates** violated the logic of probability
- Doctoral students at **Stanford GSB**: **85%** still ranked "feminist bank teller" as
  more probable
- **Stephen Jay Gould**: "a little homunculus in my head keeps jumping up and down, shouting
  'but she can't just be a bank teller; read the description'" — System 1 insists even when
  System 2 knows

### Coherence vs. Probability

- **Plausibility ≠ probability** — but they are easily confused by the unwary
- Forecasting trap: adding detail to scenarios makes them more persuasive but *less* likely
  to come true — "A flood in North America in which 1,000 drown" vs. "A California
  earthquake causing a flood in which 1,000 drown" — second rated more probable

### Hsee's Dinnerware Study

- **Christopher Hsee**: two dinnerware sets evaluated
  - Set A (40 pieces): includes 8 cups (2 broken) and 8 saucers (7 broken)
  - Set B (24 pieces): all in good condition; is contained in Set A
  - **Joint evaluation**: Set A priced at $32, Set B at $30 (correct direction)
  - **Single evaluation**: Set B priced at $33, Set A at $23 — reversed!
  - System 1 averages rather than adds: broken dishes drag down Set A's average quality
- **Frequency representation** reduces conjunction fallacy: "How many of the 100 participants?"
  → 25% error rate vs. 65% for "What percentage?"

## Chapter: Causes Trump Statistics

### Statistical vs. Causal Base Rates

- Classic cab problem: Green cabs = 85% of fleet, Blue = 15%; witness identified Blue with
  80% accuracy. Correct Bayesian answer: **~41% probability** the cab was Blue. Most people
  ignore base rate and anchor on witness testimony alone → answering ~80%
- **Statistical base rate**: "85% of cabs are Green" — fact about population, not causally
  linked to the individual accident; System 1 finds nothing to chew on
- **Causal base rate**: "Green cabs cause 85% of accidents" — implies a stereotype that
  applies to individuals; subjects gave much more weight to it, closer to Bayesian answer
- Key principle: **causal base rates** are treated as information about the individual case;
  **statistical base rates** are underweighted or ignored

### The Helping Experiment (Nisbett & Borgida)

- **Richard Nisbett** and **Eugene Borgida**: students told the procedure and results of
  NYU's famous helping experiment (only 4 of 15 participants immediately helped someone
  having a seizure), then shown bland interview videos — asked to predict who helped
- Students who knew the base rate (27% helped immediately) made **identical predictions**
  to those who knew only the procedure — learned nothing that changed actual beliefs
- The fix: show students the two individuals *first*, then reveal they didn't help — THEN
  ask for global results → students' guesses about global results became extremely accurate
  - Individual surprise forces the causal story; the generalization follows naturally

## Chapter: Regression to the Mean

### The Flight Instructor Insight

- Israeli Air Force flight instructors: a senior instructor argued that after praising a
  cadet for a clean maneuver, performance usually got *worse*; after screaming at a bad
  execution, performance usually *improved*. Conclusion: punishment works, praise backfires
- He was observing **regression to the mean**, not a causal effect of feedback
- **Perverse feedback loop**: we are nice when others please us (above average → next will
  regress down) and nasty when they displease us (below average → next will regress up)
  → statistically "rewarded" for being nasty and "punished" for being nice

### Talent and Luck Formula

- Kahneman's "favorite equation":
  - `success = talent + luck`
  - `great success = a little more talent + a lot of luck`

### Why the Mind Resists Regression

- Regression was discovered **200 years after** the theory of gravitation — it required
  **Sir Francis Galton** (1886) to identify it, and he struggled
- "Highly intelligent women tend to marry men who are less intelligent than they are."
  People invent causal explanations. Statistically equivalent statement: "The correlation
  between spouses' intelligence is less than perfect." No one finds this interesting.
- **Statistician David Freedman**: if regression is explained to a jury, the side doing
  the explaining will lose — that counterintuitive

### Correlation Coefficient

- Examples:
  - Self-reported height vs. weight, adult American males: **~0.41**
  - SAT scores vs. college GPA: **~0.60**
  - Income vs. education level in the US: **~0.40**
  - Family income vs. last four digits of phone number: **0**

### Regression and Illusion of Treatment Effects

- "Depressed children treated with an energy drink improve significantly" — depressed
  children are an extreme group; extreme groups regress to the mean over time regardless
  of any intervention. To test whether any treatment works, you **must** compare against
  a control group — the control group is expected to improve too, just from regression

## Chapter 18: Taming Intuitive Predictions

### Nonregressive Intuitions

- *Julie is a senior who read fluently at age 4. What is her GPA?* — People quickly
  produce ~3.7 or 3.8
- System 1 steps:
  1. Causal link constructed: evidence connected to target through shared factor
  2. Norm evaluation: evidence evaluated against reference group
  3. Substitution + intensity matching: evaluation of childhood reading skill substituted
     for the GPA prediction; percentile score directly mapped onto GPA scale
- This process ignores uncertainty entirely; predictions are **systematically nonregressive**
- Kahneman & Tversky experiment: participants could not distinguish *evaluating current
  evidence* from *predicting future performance* — judgments were identical

### A Correction for Intuitive Predictions

**Four-step procedure** (illustrated with Julie's GPA):
1. Start with the **baseline** (average GPA for the reference class)
2. Determine the GPA matching your intuitive impression of the evidence
3. Estimate the **correlation** between evidence and outcome (~0.30 for reading age/GPA)
4. Move that **fraction of the distance** from baseline to intuitive match (e.g., if r = .30,
   move 30% of the way)

- When evidence is strong (high correlation), adjustment is small; when evidence is weak,
  stay near the baseline
- **Kim vs. Jane** (hiring a professor): Kim has spectacular recommendations, no track record;
  Jane has 3-year postdoc, excellent research record, less dazzling interview. Intuition
  favors Kim — but Jane has a larger sample, so Kim's extreme impression should be regressed
  more deeply. Correct answer: you might end up choosing Jane despite feeling more impressed

## Chapter 19: The Illusion of Understanding

### Narrative Fallacy

- **Nassim Taleb**: **narrative fallacy** — explanatory stories of the past shape our view
  of the world and our expectations for the future
- Compelling narratives are: simple, concrete, assign large roles to talent/intention over
  luck, focus on striking events rather than the countless events that failed to happen

### The Google Story

- Two Stanford CS grad students develop a superior search algorithm → obtain funding →
  series of good decisions → one of the most valuable companies in America
- A year after founding, they were willing to sell for less than $1M; buyer said too high —
  this *lucky* incident is woven into the narrative, helping us underestimate how much luck mattered
- **The illusion**: understanding the Google story feels like learning a general lesson.
  But the ultimate test: would it have made the event **predictable in advance**? No story
  meets that test

> Our comforting conviction that the world makes sense rests on a secure foundation: our
> almost unlimited ability to ignore our ignorance.

### The Word "Knew" and the 2008 Crisis

- "I knew the crisis was coming" — people say this because the crisis happened, not because
  they could have proven it at the time. Many intelligent economists did *not* believe a
  catastrophe was imminent — therefore the crisis was not **knowable** in advance
- **This linguistic bias perpetuates the illusion that the world is more predictable than it is**

### Hindsight Bias

- **Baruch Fischhoff** with **Ruth Beyth**: before/after Nixon's 1972 visit to China,
  participants recalled having assigned higher probabilities to events that occurred and
  lower to those that didn't. Replicated for **O.J. Simpson** trial and **Clinton** impeachment
- **Outcome bias**: decisions judged by their outcomes, not the quality of reasoning at the time
- **Duluth Bridge Study**: students told debris had blocked the river causing major flood →
  56% said city should have hired a flood monitor (vs. 24% who knew only pre-decision evidence)
  — explicit instruction to ignore hindsight didn't help
- Social cost: decision makers judged too harshly for bad outcomes not reasonably foreseeable;
  **undeserved halos** given to lucky risk-seekers who got away with it

## Chapter 20: The Illusion of Stock-Picking Skill

### Terry Odean's Research

- **Terry Odean** (formerly Kahneman's student): studied 10,000 brokerage accounts over 7 years
- Compared returns of sold stock vs. bought stock over the following year
- Result: **unequivocally bad** — shares traders sold outperformed shares they bought by
  **3.2 percentage points per year**, above transaction costs
- "Trading Is Hazardous to Your Wealth": **most active traders had the poorest results**
- "Boys Will Be Boys": men acted on useless ideas significantly more often than women;
  **women achieved better investment results than men**

### The Financial Firm Spreadsheet — Zero Correlation

- Kahneman given a spreadsheet: 25 anonymous wealth advisers, 8 consecutive years of
  performance rankings; computed 28 year-to-year correlation coefficients
- Average correlation: **.01** — effectively zero
- The firm was **rewarding luck as if it were skill** — bonuses based on annual rankings
  that had no predictive validity
- One executive told Kahneman at the airport: "I have done very well for the firm and no
  one can take that away from me"

### Why the Illusion Persists

- Stock pickers do exercise genuine skills: analyzing data, income statements, management quality
- The skill gap: evaluating business prospects ≠ knowing whether that information is
  **already priced in**
- Illusions reinforced by **professional culture**: communities of like-minded believers
  sustain unshakable faith

## Chapter 22: Intuitions vs. Formulas

### Paul Meehl and Clinical vs. Statistical Prediction

- **Paul Meehl** — 1954 book *Clinical vs. Statistical Prediction*: reviewed 20 studies
  comparing clinical predictions vs. simple formula from a few scores
- Classic example: counselors interviewed freshmen for 45 min, had high school grades +
  aptitude tests + personal statements; formula used only high school grades + one aptitude
  test — **formula beat 11 of 14 counselors**
- Since Meehl, ~200 studies conducted — algorithms win **~60% of the time**; ties in
  remaining cases (a tie = de facto win for algorithms, which cost far less)

> "There is no controversy in social science which shows such a large body of qualitatively
> diverse studies coming out so uniformly in the same direction as this one." — Paul Meehl

### The Bordeaux Wine Formula

- **Orley Ashenfelter**: statistical formula predicts future Bordeaux wine prices from
  three weather variables: average summer temperature, harvest rainfall, prior winter rainfall
- Formula correlation with actual future prices: **above .90** — beats expert tasters who
  have tasted the wine
- French wine community reaction: "somewhere between violent and hysterical"

### Why Experts Are Inferior to Algorithms

- **Experts try to be clever** — they consider complex combinations; complexity reduces
  validity in most low-validity environments
- **Human inconsistency**: experienced radiologists contradict themselves **20% of the time**
  on the same X-ray seen on separate occasions
- Root cause: **System 1's extreme context-dependency** — unnoticed stimuli shift judgments
  moment to moment. Formulas don't suffer this.

### The Apgar Score

- Pre-1953: obstetricians used unstructured clinical judgment to assess newborn distress
- 1953: anesthesiologist **Virginia Apgar** jotted down five variables at breakfast:
  heart rate, respiration, reflex, muscle tone, color — scored 0/1/2
- Score 8+ = baby in good shape; 4 or below = immediate intervention needed
- **Apgar score** still used in every delivery room worldwide; credited with substantially
  reducing infant mortality

### Kahneman's Israeli Army Interview Experiment

- In 1955, Kahneman redesigned the IDF's recruit interview system (existing system:
  free-form 15-20 min interview → global impression → combat fitness score — proven
  "almost useless")
- Redesigned with **structured factual questions** on six independent personality traits,
  each scored 0-5 via factual questions; traits evaluated in **fixed sequence** to prevent
  **halo effect**
- Interviewers: "You are turning us into robots!" — compromise: after completing all six
  ratings, they could do a "close your eyes" exercise — imagine the recruit as a soldier
  and assign an intuitive score 1-5
- Result: structured six-trait sum **substantially outperformed** old global interview;
  the "close your eyes" intuitive score performed **just as well as the sum of six ratings**
- Lesson: **intuition adds value — but only AFTER disciplined collection of objective
  information**. System still in use 45 years later.

### Expert Intuition: When Can We Trust It?

- Two conditions for valid expert intuition:
  1. Sufficiently **regular environment**
  2. Adequate **opportunity to practice** with clear feedback
- **Anesthesiologists** (fast, clear feedback) can develop reliable intuitions; **radiologists**
  (little feedback on whether diagnoses were correct) cannot
- **Subjective confidence is not a diagnostic of accuracy** — a judgment that answers the
  wrong question can still be held with high confidence
- Kahneman–**Gary Klein** joint conclusion: you can distinguish valid intuitions from bogus
  ones by examining the environment and the learning history — *not* by consulting confidence

## Chapter 23: The Outside View

### The Curriculum Project Story

- Kahneman assembled a team to write a curriculum on judgment/decision-making; team
  estimated **~2 years** to finish (range: 1.5–2.5 years)
- Kahneman asked **Seymour Fox** (curriculum expert): how long did comparable teams take?
- Answer: ~40% of such teams **never finished**; those that did took **7–10 years**
- When asked how their team compared: "Below average, but not by much"
- The group acknowledged the information and **carried on anyway** — the project took
  **8 years** and was never used by the Ministry

### Inside View vs. Outside View

- **Inside view**: forecast based on specific circumstances of the current case (WYSIATI)
  — failed to account for "unknown unknowns" (divorces, illness, coordination failures)
- **Outside view**: look at the reference class of similar cases; use base rate as baseline
- People who have information about an individual case rarely feel the need to know statistics
  of the class — the outside view surprises them and is routinely **ignored**

### The Planning Fallacy

- **Planning fallacy** (coined by Kahneman and Tversky): plans and forecasts unrealistically
  close to best-case scenarios
- Examples:
  - **Scottish Parliament building**: estimated £40M (1997) → final cost **£431M** (2004)
  - **Rail projects worldwide (1969–1998)**: >90% overestimated passengers; average
    ridership overestimate = **106%**; average cost overrun = **45%**; accuracy did
    not improve over 30 years
  - **American kitchen renovations**: expected cost avg $18,658; actual avg **$38,769**
- Contractors knowingly lowball bids; unrealistic initial budgets are often **strategic**

### Reference Class Forecasting

- **Bent Flyvbjerg** (Oxford): "The prevalent tendency to underweight or ignore distributional
  information is perhaps the major source of error in forecasting."

> "The prevalent tendency to underweight or ignore distributional information is perhaps the
> major source of error in forecasting." — Bent Flyvbjerg

**Three-step process**:
1. Identify an appropriate reference class (e.g., large railway projects)
2. Obtain statistics of that class → generate **baseline prediction**
3. Adjust baseline using case-specific information only to the extent there are particular
   reasons to expect more or less bias than typical

## Chapter 24: The Engine of Capitalism

### Optimistic Bias

- Kahneman's claim: optimistic bias may be the **most significant** of all cognitive biases
  in terms of consequences for decisions
- Study of people who exaggerate expected lifespan beyond actuarial predictions: they work
  longer hours, more likely to remarry after divorce ("triumph of hope over experience"),
  more likely to bet on individual stocks

### Entrepreneurial Delusions

- US small business 5-year survival rate: **~35%**
- Survey of American entrepreneurs: average estimate of success odds for "any business like
  yours" = **60%** (almost double reality); **33%** said their chance of failing was zero
- **Thomas Åstebro**: Canadian inventors given D/E grades (predicted failure) — 5 of 411
  lowest-graded projects reached commercialization, none successful. After unequivocal
  failure prediction: ~47% continued, doubling their losses on average before giving up

### Overconfident CEOs

- **Ulrike Malmendier** and **Geoffrey Tate**: highly optimistic CEOs assumed more debt,
  more likely to overpay for targets, undertook more value-destroying mergers
- **Celebrity CEO problem**: award-winning CEOs subsequently underperform on stock and
  operating metrics; spend more time on outside activities; more likely to engage in
  earnings management

### Competition Neglect

- **"Above-average effect"**: 90% of drivers believe they're better than average — now
  reinterpreted as cognitive bias, not ego: people answer "Am I a good driver?" instead
  of "Am I better than average?"
- Kahneman asked startup founders: what % of your outcome depends on *you*? Answer was
  never below 80% — yet competitors and market shifts matter just as much
- **Competition neglect** (**Colin Camerer** and **Dan Lovallo**): entrepreneurs imagine
  futures where rivals barely exist

> "Hubris. Hubris. If you only think about your own business, you think, 'I've got a good
> story department, I've got a good marketing department.' And you don't think that
> everybody else is thinking the same way." — Disney Studios chairman

### Overconfidence

- Duke University survey of CFOs: 11,600 forecasts of S&P 500 returns
  - Correlation between estimates and actual values: **slightly less than zero**
  - CFOs gave **80% confidence intervals** — expected surprise rate: ~20%; actual: **67%**
  - To achieve a true 80% CI, CFOs would have needed to state "between −10% and +30%" —
    an interval **4× wider** than they actually stated
- ICU study: physicians "completely certain" of a diagnosis were **wrong 40% of the time**
- **"Bold forecasts and timid decisions"** (Kahneman and **Dan Lovallo**): overconfident
  predictions paired with risk aversion once stakes feel real

### The Premortem

- **Gary Klein's premortem** procedure:
  - When a team has nearly decided but not yet formally committed, gather knowledgeable people
  - Premise: "Imagine we are one year in the future. We implemented the plan. The outcome
    was a disaster. Take 5–10 minutes to write a brief history of that disaster."
- Two advantages:
  1. **Overcomes groupthink**: legitimizes suppressed skepticism
  2. **Unleashes imagination**: invites knowledgeable people to think freely about failure modes

## Chapter 25: Bernoulli's Errors

### Expected Utility Theory

- Dominant framework since **John von Neumann** and **Oskar Morgenstern**
- K&T's goal: document systematic violations of these axioms in real human choices

### Bernoulli's Core Insight

- **Daniel Bernoulli** (18th c.): people do NOT evaluate gambles by expected value (probability-
  weighted dollar average) but by **expected utility** — probability-weighted *psychological* value
- **Risk aversion** explained by **diminishing marginal utility of wealth**: each additional
  dollar adds less utility than the last
- Example: 80% chance of $100 + 20% chance of $10 → expected value = $82; but most prefer
  $80 for certain
- Applications: explains why poor people buy insurance and rich people sell it

### Bernoulli's Error: The Missing Reference Point

- **Jack and Jill scenario**: both have $5M today; Jack had $1M yesterday, Jill had $9M
  — Bernoulli says they're equally happy (same current wealth), but clearly Jack is elated
  and Jill is despondent
- Happiness is determined by **recent change** relative to a **reference point**, not absolute
  wealth level — this is pervasive in perception
- The model can explain risk aversion but cannot explain **risk-seeking when facing losses** —
  a behavior common in entrepreneurs and generals facing bad options

### Theory-Induced Blindness

- The mystery: why did Bernoulli's flawed theory survive so long unchallenged?
- **Theory-induced blindness**: once a theory is accepted as a tool, it becomes extraordinarily
  difficult to notice its flaws. Anomalous observations get explained away

## Appendix A: Judgment Under Uncertainty — Heuristics and Biases (Tversky & Kahneman, 1974)

### Three Heuristics

**1. Representativeness**
- People assess probability that object A belongs to class B by **degree of resemblance**
- Biases produced:
  - **Base-rate neglect**: engineers/lawyers experiment — 70 engineers, 30 lawyers vs. 30/70
    split produced essentially the **same probability judgments** — sharp violation of Bayes' rule
  - "Dick" example (uninformative description): subjects judged probability of Dick being an
    engineer as **.5 in both conditions** — ignoring base rate entirely
  - **Insensitivity to sample size**: subjects assessed distributions of average height for
    samples of 1,000, 100, and 10 men — produced **identical distributions**
  - **Hospital problem**: 45 births/day vs. 15 births/day — majority (53 of 95) chose "about
    the same" for which hospital would show more 60%+ male birth days. Small samples
    produce more extreme deviations; this fact is not part of people's intuitive repertoire
  - **Gambler's fallacy**: after a long run of red on roulette, people believe black is "due"
    — chance is wrongly viewed as a **self-correcting process**
  - **Illusion of validity**: confidence depends on degree of representativeness (quality of
    match), not on factors that limit predictive accuracy. Redundancy among inputs *increases*
    confidence while *decreasing* accuracy

**2. Availability**
- People assess frequency or probability by the ease with which instances can be brought to mind
- Biases produced:
  - **Familiarity as retrievability**: lists with more famous personalities of one sex → that
    sex judged more numerous
  - **Letter *r* experiment**: people recall words starting with *r* more easily than words
    with *r* in third position → judge first-position as more numerous; actually wrong
  - **Imaginability**: committees of 2 members from 10 feel more numerous (median estimate:
    70; correct: 45) than committees of 8 (median: 20; correct: 45) — opposite of math
  - **Illusory correlation** (**Chapman and Chapman**): clinical diagnoses + patient drawings;
    subjects markedly overestimated co-occurrence of intuitively associated pairs even when
    data did not support the link — **extremely resistant to contradictory data**

**3. Adjustment and Anchoring**
- People start from an initial value and adjust — adjustments are **typically insufficient**
- Classic demo: percentage of African nations in the UN:
  - Starting at 10 → median estimate: **25%**
  - Starting at 65 → median estimate: **45%**
  - Payoffs for accuracy did **not** reduce the anchoring effect
- Descending sequence (8×7×6×5×4×3×2×1) → median estimate: **2,250**; ascending → **512**;
  correct answer: **40,320**
- **Biases in conjunctive and disjunctive events**:
  - Conjunctive: probability of chain-like success **overestimated** (each step looks likely)
  - Disjunctive: probability of at least one failure in a complex system **underestimated**
- **Overconfident confidence intervals**: actual values fall outside stated X₀₁–X₉₉ range
  ~30% of the time; subjects state overly narrow intervals — anchoring on best estimate
  and adjusting insufficiently outward

### Why Statistical Principles Aren't Learned from Experience

- Relevant instances are **not coded appropriately** — the mind doesn't register the right features
- To self-calibrate, a person would need to track what proportion of events *actually occur*
  among those assigned the same probability — but it's **not natural to group events by
  judged probability**, so the feedback loop never closes

## Appendix B: Choices, Values, and Frames (Kahneman & Tversky)

### Prospect Theory: The Value Function

- Standard decision analysis describes outcomes in terms of **total wealth** — but people frame
  outcomes as **gains, losses, and neutral outcomes** (maintenance of status quo)
- The value function has three key properties:
  - Defined on **gains and losses** relative to a reference point (not total wealth)
  - **Concave** in the domain of gains (diminishing sensitivity to gains)
  - **Convex** in the domain of losses (diminishing sensitivity to losses)
  - Considerably **steeper for losses than for gains** (**loss aversion**)

### Loss Aversion

- **Loss aversion**: a loss of $X feels more painful than a gain of $X feels pleasurable
- Most undergraduates refused to stake $10 on a coin flip unless they stood to win **at least $30**

### Risk Seeking in the Domain of Losses

- Given: 85% chance to lose $1,000 (15% chance to lose nothing), or a sure loss of $800
- Most people prefer the **gamble** — even though its expected value (−$850) is worse than
  the sure loss (−$800). This is **risk-seeking**: people gamble to avoid locking in a certain loss

### The Asian Disease Problem (Classic Framing Experiment)

- **Gain frame** (N=152): 200 people will be saved (certain) vs. 1/3 probability 600 saved
  → **72% chose certain gain** (risk averse)
- **Loss frame** (N=155): 400 people will die (certain) vs. 1/3 probability nobody dies
  → **78% chose the gamble** (risk seeking)
- Problems are objectively identical — yet framing **reverses the majority preference**
- Violation of **invariance**: rational choice requires the same decision regardless of description
- When shown their conflicting answers, respondents are puzzled but **cannot resolve the
  contradiction** — "framing effects resemble perceptual illusions more than computational errors"

### Dominance Violation via Mental Accounting

- Concurrent decisions: (A) sure gain $240 + (D) 75% chance to lose $1,000
  vs. (B) 25% chance to gain $1,000 + (C) sure loss $750
- **73%** chose A+D (risk averse on gains, risk seeking on losses)
- But A+D is **dominated** by B+C — B+C is strictly better on both outcomes
- Separating the problems triggers framing effects; combined, the domination is clear

### Decision Weights (Figure 2)

- The value of a lottery ticket is **not a linear function of probability**
- Moving from 0% → 5% has a larger psychological impact than 30% → 35%
- Moving from 95% → 100% also has a larger impact than 30% → 35% — **category-boundary effect**
- **Low probabilities are overweighted** → simultaneous appeal of lottery tickets (low-prob
  gain) and insurance policies (low-prob loss)
- **Pseudo-certainty effect**: in sequential framing, people ignore the first phase, treating
  an uncertain outcome as if it were certain → overweighting of "certainty"

### Mental Accounting

- **Lost ticket vs. lost cash** (Richard Thaler):
  - Lost $10 theater ticket: only **46%** would buy another (perceived as doubling the cost)
  - Lost $10 cash: **88%** would still buy a ticket (cash not posted to the theater account)
- **Calculator/Jacket experiment**: willing to drive 20 min to save $5 on a **$15 calculator**:
  68%; on a **$125 calculator**: 29% — only the topical framing differs
- **Endowment effect**: reluctance to part from assets that belong to one's endowment —
  buying prices significantly lower than selling prices. Challenges standard economic theory.

### Formulation Effects

- Credit card surcharge vs. cash discount (Richard Thaler): credit card lobbyists insisted
  price differences be labeled a "cash discount" rather than a "credit card surcharge"
  — "surcharge" frames higher price as a loss; losses loom larger than gains
- A physician or advisor can influence decisions **without distorting information** — merely
  by framing outcomes
