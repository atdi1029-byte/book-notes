# Antifragile — Detailed Notes

## Prologue

### I. How to Love the Wind

- **Wind as metaphor**: Wind extinguishes a candle and energizes fire — likewise with randomness, uncertainty, and chaos, you want to use them, not hide from them
- You want to be the fire and wish for the wind
- The goal is not merely to survive uncertainty but to **dominate, domesticate, and conquer** the unseen, the opaque, and the inexplicable

### II. The Antifragile

- **Core definition**: Some things benefit from shocks — they thrive and grow when exposed to volatility, randomness, disorder, stressors, and love adventure, risk, and uncertainty. There is no word for the exact opposite of fragile — Taleb coins the term **antifragile**
- **Antifragility is beyond resilience or robustness** — the resilient resists shocks and stays the same; the antifragile gets better
- This property is behind everything that has changed with time: evolution, culture, ideas, revolutions, political systems, technological innovation, corporate survival, good recipes, the rise of cities, legal systems, equatorial forests, bacterial resistance
- **Antifragility determines the boundary between what is living and organic and what is inert** — like a stapler on your desk vs. the human body
- Antifragility allows us to deal with the unknown, to do things without understanding them — and do them well

> "Let me be more aggressive: we are largely better at doing than we are at thinking, thanks to antifragility. I'd rather be dumb and antifragile than extremely smart and fragile, any time."

- **Antifragility and fragility are degrees on a spectrum** — there are even financial contracts explicitly designed to benefit from market volatility

### Nonprediction

- By grasping antifragility we can build a guide to **nonpredictive decision making** under uncertainty
- **Fragility can be measured; risk is not measurable** (outside of casinos)
- This provides a solution to the **Black Swan problem** — the impossibility of calculating risks of consequential rare events
- **Simple test of asymmetry**: anything that has more upside than downside from random events is antifragile; the reverse is fragile

### Deprivation of Antifragility

- **Critical insight**: antifragility is the property of all natural systems that have survived — depriving these systems of volatility will harm them; they will weaken, die, or blow up
- We have been **fragilizing the economy, health, political life, education** by suppressing randomness and volatility
- Much of our modern world has been harming us with **top-down policies** (dubbed **"Soviet-Harvard delusions"**)
- **The tragedy of modernity**: those trying to help are often hurting us the most
- Everything **top-down fragilizes**; everything **bottom-up thrives** under the right amount of stress

### Upside at the Expense of Others

- The largest fragilizer of society: **absence of "skin in the game"**
- Some become antifragile at the expense of others by getting the upside from volatility and exposing others to the downside
- The rise of a new class of **inverse heroes**: bureaucrats, bankers, Davos-attending members of the I.A.N.D., and academics with too much power and no real accountability
- At no point in history have so many **non-risk-takers** exerted so much control

> "The chief ethical rule is the following: Thou shalt not have antifragility at the expense of the fragility of others."

### III. The Antidote to the Black Swan

- **Black Swans** are large-scale unpredictable events of massive consequence — the unpredictor is called the "turkey" when surprised and harmed
- **Black Swans hijack our brains** — they are retrospectively explainable, creating the **illusion of predictability**
- **Complex systems** are full of interdependencies and nonlinear responses — "nonlinear" means doubling the dose doesn't give double the effect
- Man-made complex systems develop **cascades and runaway chains** that decrease predictability and cause outsized events

> "You get pseudo-order when you seek order; you only get a measure of order and control when you embrace randomness."

## Chapter 1: Between Damocles and Hydra

### Half of Life Has No Name

- **The post office package thought experiment**: What is the exact opposite of "fragile"? Almost everyone says "robust" — but **this is wrong**
- The resilient/robust neither breaks nor improves. The exact opposite of a "fragile" parcel would be one marked **"please mishandle"** — its contents would **benefit from shocks**
- **The fragile** is *at best* unharmed; **the robust** is *at best and at worst* unharmed; **the antifragile** is *at worst* unharmed
- The neologism was necessary — no word in any major language (Indo-European or otherwise) expresses reverse fragility. Taleb checked Latin, Greek, Romance, Slavic, Germanic, Indo-Iranian, Semitic, and Turkic families

> "Half of life — the interesting half of life — we don't have a name for."

### Please Behead Me

- Three mythological archetypes illustrate the spectrum:
  - **Damocles**: A courtier enjoys luxury but with a sword hanging over his head by a single horse hair. **Damocles is fragile** — it is only a matter of time
  - **Phoenix**: The bird destroyed and reborn from its ashes, always returning to its initial state — **robustness**. Phoenix is the ancient symbol of **Beirut**, destroyed seven times in five thousand years and rebuilt seven times. Taleb saw the eighth episode during Lebanon's civil war
  - **Hydra**: Each time a head is cut off, **two grow back**. Harm is what it likes. **Hydra represents antifragility**
- **Sophistication brings fragility to Black Swans**: as societies gain complexity, they become increasingly vulnerable to collapse (archeologist **Joseph Tainter**)
- To counter success, you need a high dose of robustness or antifragility — **you want to be Phoenix, or possibly Hydra. Otherwise the sword of Damocles will get you**

### On the Necessity of Naming

- We know more than we think we do — our perceptions and intuitions can be **superior to what we know and tabulate**
- **Guy Deutscher's** *Through the Language Glass*: primitive populations can match colors they have no words for — they are **culturally, though not biologically, color-blind**
- We are intellectually, not organically, **antifragility-blind**
- **William Gladstone** first discovered limited ancient color vocabulary in the 1850s — Homer's **"wine-dark sea"** reflects this. Gladstone also insisted on **balanced fiscal budgets** — fiscal deficits have proven a prime source of fragility

### Proto-Antifragility

- **Mithridatism**: King **Mithridates IV** of Pontus built immunity by ingesting sub-lethal doses of poison. The method (*Antidotum Mithridatium*) became fashionable in Rome — until it complicated **Nero's** attempts to poison his mother **Agrippina**, who had Mithridatized herself
- Mithridatization is the approach used in **vaccination and allergy medicine** — not quite antifragility, still robustness, but a hint that deprivation of poison makes us fragile
- **Hormesis**: When a poisonous substance in some dose makes you **better off overall** — a small dose acts as medicine by triggering **overreaction**. First scientifically described in 1888 by **Hugo Schulz**
- Plants protect themselves with poisonous substances that, ingested in right quantities, **stimulate our organisms** — limited, low-dose poisoning triggers healthy benefits
- **Caloric restriction** activates healthy reactions and lengthens life expectancy — **too much regular food is bad for you**
- Hormesis lost respect after the 1930s from mistaken association with **homeopathy** — but the mechanisms are different and hormesis has ample empirical backing

### Domain Independence Is Domain Dependent

- Some people understand an idea in one domain and **fail to recognize it in another** — this is **domain dependence**
- **The Dubai banker**: carried his luggage by a uniformed porter, then fifteen minutes later lifted free weights at the gym replicating suitcase swings with kettlebells
- The **Federal Reserve** controls interest rates while Americans would reject government control of car prices — same principle, different domain. **Ron Paul** was called a crank for suggesting its abolition
- Domain dependence is why it has been difficult to realize that uncertainty, incomplete understanding, disorder, and volatility are **members of the same close family**

## Chapter 2: Overcompensation and Overreaction Everywhere

### Post-Traumatic Growth

- **David Halpern** (U.K. government advisor) introduced Taleb to **post-traumatic growth** — the opposite of PTSD, by which people harmed by past events surpass themselves
- Popular culture knows this as **"it builds character"** — but intellectuals focus on negative responses from randomness rather than positive ones

### Innovation from Necessity

- **How do you innovate? First, try to get in trouble** — serious, but not terminal
- Latin: sophistication is born from hunger (**artificia docuit fames**). Ovid: difficulty wakes up genius (**ingenium mala saepe movent**)
- **The excess energy released from overreaction to setbacks is what innovates!**
- Note the disproportionate contribution of **uneducated technicians and entrepreneurs** to technological leaps, from the Industrial Revolution to Silicon Valley
- **Cato the Censor** looked at comfort as a road to waste — **abundance is harder to handle than scarcity**. We are richer than ever yet more heavily in debt than ever

### Automation, Horse Races, and Whispering

- Automation of airplanes is **underchallenging pilots**, causing deaths — the **FAA** found pilots "abdicate too much responsibility to automated systems"
- **The best horses lose when competing with slower ones** — undercompensation from absence of challenge
- Taleb's lecturing trick: **whisper, not shout** — making the audience switch into intellectual overdrive. **Peter Drucker** and **Jacques Lacan** mesmerized crowds as the antithesis of polished speakers
- Empirical evidence of **"disfluency"**: mental effort activates more analytical brain machinery

### Antifragile Responses as Redundancy

- Antifragile hormetic responses are a form of **redundancy** — Nature likes to overinsure itself
- **Layers of redundancy** are the central risk management property of natural systems — we have two kidneys, extra capacity in many organs
- Human design tends toward **debt** — the opposite of redundancy
- **Redundancy is not defensive; it is more like investment than insurance** — tell the MBA analyst that "inefficient" is often very efficient

### The Lucretius Problem

- Taleb calls the mental defect of using past worst cases as future worst cases **the Lucretius problem** — the fool believes the tallest mountain equals the tallest he has observed
- **Pharaonic Egypt** used Nile high-water marks as worst-case estimates. The **Fukushima reactor** was built to withstand the worst past earthquake
- Nature, unlike **Alan Greenspan**, prepares for what has not happened before — **your body is more imaginative about the future than you are**

### On the Antifragility of Riots, Love, and Information

- **Seneca** on punishment: "Repeated punishment crushes the hatred of a few, stirs the hatred of all... just as trees that have been trimmed throw out countless branches"
- Political movements and rebellions are **highly antifragile** — repression feeds them

> "The higher you build your barricades, the stronger we become." — Irish revolutionary song

- Obsessive love is antifragile — in Proust's *La recherche*, Swann's obsession with Odette is fueled by her elusiveness
- **Information is antifragile** — banned books thrive. **Ayn Rand's** novels have been read for decades *thanks to* brutally nasty reviews
- **Criticism is an unfaked badge of attention** — boring is the only truly bad thing for a book
- Taleb's great-grandfather **Nicolas Ghosn**: "I never hear anything wrong said about you. You have proven yourself incapable of generating envy."
- **Heuristic for independence**: those who dress outrageously are robust in reputation; those in suits are fragile to information about them

## Chapter 3: The Cat and the Washing Machine

### The Organic vs. the Mechanical

- **Bold conjecture**: everything that has life in it is to some extent antifragile — the secret of life is antifragility
- Your bones get denser with episodic stress — **Wolff's Law** (1892). A dish or car will not
- The **phenomenon of aging is misunderstood** — much comes from **maladjustment**: too few stressors or too little recovery time. Artificial aging comes from stifling internal antifragility

### The Complex

- The effective distinction is between **noncomplex and complex systems** — many man-made things (markets, economic activities, cultural behavior) grow on their own, closer to the cat than the washing machine, but **mistaken for washing machines**
- With complex systems, **interdependencies are severe** and the notion of "cause" itself is suspect

### Stressors Are Information

- Complex systems **convey information through stressors** — your body gets information about the environment through stress, via hormones
- **Errors and their consequences are information**; for small children, pain is the only risk management information

### Equilibrium, Not Again

- For complexity theorist **Stuart Kaufman**, "equilibrium" in social science can be death
- **Dissipative structures** (Ilya Prigogine): organisms exist permanently "far from equilibrium" — normalcy requires volatility and stress

### Touristification and the Secret Thirst for Chance

- **Touristification**: the systematic removal of uncertainty and randomness from things — converting activities into the equivalent of a script
- If you are alive, something deep in your soul **likes a certain measure of randomness**
- An environment with variability does not expose us to **chronic stress injury** — unlike the gym machine forcing endless identical repetitions. **Much of modern life is preventable chronic stress injury**

## Chapter 4: What Kills Me Makes Others Stronger

### Antifragility by Layers

- **The antifragility of some comes at the expense of the fragility of others** — the fragility of every startup is necessary for the economy to be antifragile
- **Restaurants** are individually fragile but collectively antifragile — had they been individually robust, the business would deliver Soviet cafeteria food

### Evolution and Unpredictability

- There is a stronger variety of antifragility linked to **evolution** — the unit does not get stronger; **it dies**, accomplishing a transfer of benefits
- **Robert Trivers** figured out the competition between gene and organism ("selfish gene")
- An **immortal organism** would need to predict the future perfectly — by letting organisms go one lifespan at a time with modifications, nature does not need to predict
- When you drink poison in small amounts, evolutionary processes replace bad proteins with stronger ones — **autophagy** — a process that selects and kills the weakest

### Thank You, Errors / Learning from Others' Mistakes

- When you are antifragile, deviations are more helpful than harmful — error is used as a **source of information**
- **Henry Petroski**: had the *Titanic* not sunk, we would have built larger liners and the next disaster would have been more tragic
- **Every plane crash makes the next flight safer** — aviation exploits small, independent errors
- But **every bank crash makes the next one more likely** — the economic system lacks this antifragility

### National Entrepreneur Day

- **A loser** is someone who after making a mistake doesn't introspect, feels embarrassed rather than enriched with new information
- For the economy to be antifragile, **every individual business must be fragile** — exposed to breaking
- Modern society should treat ruined entrepreneurs like dead soldiers — **there is no such thing as a failed entrepreneur**
- Taleb's dream: **National Entrepreneur Day** — "Most of you will fail, disrespected, impoverished, but we are grateful for the risks you are taking. You are at the source of our antifragility."

## Chapter 5: The Souk and the Office Building

### Two Types of Professions: John and George

- **John**: bank clerk, perfectly predictable income, 25 years. Until the banking crisis when his job was "made redundant." At fifty, he would never recover
- **George**: taxi driver, extremely variable income day to day. But year after year, averages the same as John. Never had a single day without a fare in 25 years. Can continue into his eighties
- **The central illusion in life: that randomness is risky, that it is a bad thing**
- Artisanal careers harbor antifragility: small variations make them adapt continuously — **free options, the hallmark of antifragility**
- **The more variability you observe in a system, the less Black Swan-prone it is**

### Switzerland: The Most Antifragile Place on Earth

- The most stable country **does not have a government** — governed entirely bottom-up through municipal **cantons**, near-sovereign mini-states
- **"Stalin could not have existed in a municipality"** — small is more antifragile
- Biology plays a role: an administrator is shielded from the sting of **shame** for failures; lobbyists cannot exist in a municipality
- **Bruno Leoni** argued for the robustness of judge-based law (diversity) over rigid codified law
- Switzerland has traditionally had low university education compared to other rich nations — its system was based on **apprenticeship models**: **techne** (crafts), not **episteme** (book knowledge)

### Mediocristan vs. Extremistan

- **Mediocristan**: plenty of small variations that cancel out in aggregate — the taxi driver's income
- **Extremistan**: mostly stability and occasionally large chaos — the bank employee's income
- Human overintervention to smooth or control causes a switch from **Mediocristan** into **Extremistan** — this applies to health, politics, economics, even mood with and without Prozac

### The Great Turkey Problem

- **The Turkey metaphor**: A turkey is fed for a thousand days; every day confirms butchers love turkeys "with increased statistical confidence." Then comes Thanksgiving
- The surprise is a **Black Swan event** for the turkey, not the butcher
- **The mother of all harmful mistakes**: mistaking absence of evidence (of harm) for evidence of absence
- Our mission becomes **"how not to be a turkey"** — figuring out the difference between **true and manufactured stability**

### Twelve Thousand Years

- The **northern Levant** stayed perhaps the most prosperous province in human history for **twelve thousand years** — dominated by traders on the **Silk Road**
- Managed by **local elites** through decentralized methods the Ottoman retained
- The modernist **Baath Party** centralized the place and enforced utopias — Aleppo went into **instant decline**, trading families fled
- **Lebanon was a benign, smaller, disorganized state** — despite a civil war, today Lebanon has between **three to six times the wealth of Syria**
- A wealthy Aleppine merchant: **"We people of Aleppo prefer war to prison"** — "prison" meaning loss of political and economic freedoms

> "It seemed, wrote Machiavelli, that in the midst of murders and civil wars, our republic became stronger... A little bit of agitation gives resources to souls and what makes the species prosper isn't peace, but freedom." — Rousseau citing Machiavelli

### The Centralized State and War

- Neither Romans nor Ottomans allowed local autonomy out of love of freedom — they did it for **convenience**. This combination provides more stability than the centralized nation-state
- The fissiparous mass of European statelings had constant small-scale tension but **without large consequences** — **Mediocristan**
- The creation of nation-states led to the two world wars: **more than sixty million victims**
- **Steven Pinker** misread the statistical process — a devastating war would be completely consistent with his statistics; **Ben Bernanke** was similarly fooled with his "Great Moderation"

## Chapter 6: Tell Them I Love (Some) Randomness

### Tight Controls Cause Blowups

- **James Clerk Maxwell** showed mathematically that **tightly controlling steam engine speed leads to instability** — light control works; close control leads to overreaction
- In markets, fixing prices or eliminating speculators provides an **illusion of stability** with periods of calm punctuated by large jumps
- **Injecting some confusion stabilizes the system**

### Variations as Purges

- **Small forest fires** cleanse the system of flammable material — systematically preventing them makes the big one much worse
- Absence of fluctuations causes hidden risks to accumulate — the longer without a market trauma, **the worse the damage when commotion occurs**
- A **"weak hand"** is someone fragile but doesn't know it, lulled by false security — when many rush to the door, they collectively cause crashes

> *Fluctuat nec mergitur* (fluctuates, or floats, but does not sink)

### Hungry Donkeys and Stochastic Resonance

- **Buridan's Donkey**: equally famished and thirsty, equidistant between food and water — saved by a **random nudge**. Absence of randomness equals guaranteed death
- **Stochastic resonance**: adding random noise makes you hear sounds with **more accuracy** — weak SOS signals become audible with background noise
- **Annealing**: heating and controlled cooling of metal causes atoms to wander randomly through higher-energy states, finding **better configurations**
- **Simulated annealing** in mathematics; **Nathan Myhrvold** brought attention to a 1975 paper showing **random drilling was superior** to whatever search method was employed
- Chaotic systems can be **stabilized by adding randomness** — the change from chaos to order came not by removing chaos but by adding randomness

### Political Annealing

- **Athenian assemblies** were chosen by lot — protection against degeneracy
- **Alessandro Pluchino** et al. showed randomly selected politicians can improve parliamentary functioning
- For **Voltaire**, the best government was tempered with political assassination — creating needed reshuffling
- The ancients perfected random draw through **divinations** — the **sortes virgilianae** (opening Virgil's *Aeneid* at random)

### Foreign Policy and Manufactured Stability

- Artificially suppressed volatility exhibits **no visible risks** while risks accumulate beneath the surface — **volatility is information**
- Take **Egypt** before 2011, supported by the U.S. for four decades "to avoid chaos" — identical to "too big to fail"
- **Saudi Arabia**: a total monarchy propped by a superpower, in open contradiction with its purist origins — without the U.S., it would have had its revolution and eventually some stability
- The bitterness of Iranians toward the U.S. comes from installing the repressive **Shah of Iran** — the theocratic regime today is largely the result
- We need to think in **second steps, chains of consequences, and side effects**

> "One of life's packages: no stability without volatility."

### What Do We Call Here Modernity?

- **Modernity**: humans' large-scale domination of the environment, systematic smoothing of jaggedness, stifling of volatility
- Marked by **rationalization (naive rationalism)** — the idea society must be designed by humans
- The life of the lion in the **Bronx Zoo** compared to freedom — we once had free-range humans before the advent of the soccer mom
- **Violence is transferred from individuals to states. So is financial indiscipline.** At the center is the denial of antifragility
- **Modernity starts with the state monopoly on violence, and ends with the state's monopoly on fiscal irresponsibility**

## Chapter 7: Naive Intervention

### Iatrogenics

- In the 1930s, **389 children** presented to NYC doctors: each successive group of doctors recommended tonsillectomies at the same rate regardless of need — **probabilistic homicide**
- **Iatrogenics**: damage from treatment in excess of benefits (*iatros* = healer in Greek)
- **George Washington's death** hastened by bloodletting (five to nine pounds of blood). Until penicillin, going to the doctor *increased* your chance of death
- **Ignaz Semmelweis** observed more women died in hospitals than on the street — he called doctors criminals, was punished, humiliated, ended up in an asylum, and died ironically of hospital fever

> "The final lesson is that one should not expect laurels for bringing the truth."

- Medical error kills **between three and ten times** as many as car accidents in the U.S.
- **Generalized iatrogenics** should extend to political science, economics, urban planning, education — but not one consultant or academic recognized it

### Table 3: Fragilizing Interventionism

| Field | Interventionism | Iatrogenic Cost |
|-------|----------------|-----------------|
| Medicine | Overtreatment; pharma addition not subtraction | Medical error; antibiotic-resistant bacteria |
| Ecology | Micromanaging forest fires | Larger "big ones" |
| Politics | Central planning; supporting rotten regimes | Chaos after revolution |
| Economics | State interventionism; optimization | Deeper crises; big-time blowups |
| Parenting | Soccer mom: removing every random element | Touristification of children's minds |

### Not Doing Nothing / Non-Naive Interventionism

- **Alan Greenspan**: "top economic iatrogenist of all time" — ironing out the boom-bust cycle caused risks to hide and accumulate
- **Gordon Brown's** mission to "eliminate" the business cycle led to the **mother of all fragilities**
- The argument is not against intervention but against **naive** intervention — we tend to **over-intervene where benefits are minimal** while **under-intervening in emergencies**
- **The Drachten experiment**: a Dutch town removed all street signs — deregulation *increased* safety, confirming antifragility of attention whetted by danger

### In Praise of Procrastination — The Fabian Kind

- **Fabius Maximus** ("the Procrastinator") drove Hannibal crazy by avoiding engagement despite Hannibal's obvious military superiority
- The **Fabian Society** (Shaw, Wells, Woolf, Russell) — named after the Cunctator, based on delaying revolution
- Latin: ***festina lente*** ("make haste slowly"). Lao Tzu: ***wu-wei*** ("passive achievement")
- **Procrastination is our natural defense** — letting things exercise their antifragility

### The Noise-to-Signal Ratio

- **The more frequently you look at data, the more noise you get** — at yearly frequency, half signal/half noise; at daily, **95% noise**; at hourly, **99.5% noise**
- **Newspapers as iatrogenics**: they fill pages daily — should be two lines on some days, two hundred pages on others
- The media glorifies the anecdote — every day **6,200 persons die** in the U.S. from preventable causes, but the media reports sensational freak events
- The best way to mitigate interventionism: **ration the supply of information** as naturalistically as possible

### The State Can Help — When Incompetent

- **Chinese famine** (1959-1961): 30 million dead — famine was *more severe* in areas with higher food production (government distribution policy)
- **Soviet inefficiency as accidental robustness**: every town had staples growing around it — one calorie of transportation per calorie of nutrition vs. twelve-to-one in the U.S.
- **France works well** not because of Cartesian rationalism but because centralization was a **failed aim** for a long time (**Graham Robb's** *The Discovery of France*)
- **Nordic countries** (Sweden, Denmark): the state collects taxes but money is **spent in the communes** — far from the statism one assumes

## Chapter 8: Prediction as a Child of Modernity

- In Korea, Taleb raged at **Takatoshi Kato** for presenting projections without showing past performance — our track record in predicting significant rare events is **not close to zero; it is zero**
- The **Triad** was born there: **Fragility-Robustness-Antifragility** as a replacement for predictive methods
- Forecasting is iatrogenic — **Danny Kahneman** showed providing random numerical forecasts **increases risk taking** even when the person knows they're random

### The Predictive

- The robust and antifragile **don't need accurate comprehension of the world** — **redundancy** (extra cash, cans of Spam, gold bars) means you don't need to predict which event will cause difficulties
- Those **in debt** need to predict with more and more accuracy because of their fragility
- After an event, switch blame from inability to see it coming to: **"why did we build something so fragile?"**
- We cannot rely on eliminating greed — humanity has tried for thousands of years. The intelligent action is to make the world **greed-proof**

### The Idea of Becoming a Non-Turkey

- There are **two domains**: one where we can predict (physical), and the **Black Swan domain** (social, economic, cultural) where only turkeys operate
- The demarcation is as visible as **the cat vs. the washing machine**

> "Randomness in the Black Swan domain is intractable. I will repeat it till I get hoarse. The limit is mathematical, period."

- Because of modernity, the share of **Extremistan is increasing** — winner-take-all effects worsen. Success is **planetary, or nothing**
- The answer is simple: ***less is more*** — move the discourse to **(anti)fragility**

## Chapter 9: Fat Tony and the Fragilistas

### On Suckers and Nonsuckers

- Fat Tony and Nero predicted a **sucker's fragility crisis** — they were convinced that a snowballing crisis was bound to happen **simply because there were suckers**
- Fat Tony believed **nerds, administrators, and bankers** were the ultimate suckers — collectively even bigger suckers than individually
- By **betting against fragility**, they were antifragile
- Tony made a bundle from the 2008 crisis (high eight to low nine figures); Nero made less but was satisfied
- Nero's family wealth had **peaked in 1804** — money to him was not a social statement. **Excess wealth** is a heavy burden, forcing endless complications

### Ethics, Recognition, and Honor

- Fat Tony: "You will be ridiculed. Words are for sissies. A system based on verbal warnings will be dominated by non-risk-taking-babblers."
- There is a health-eroding dependence on **external recognition** — those who depend on words remain **fragile to the emotional toll** from compliments they did not get
- Nero's ritual: the **"Fat Tony bet"** — opening the envelope with his trading statement, then going on with his day

> "A man is honorable in proportion to the personal risks he takes for his opinion — in other words, the amount of downside he is exposed to."

### What the Nonpredictor Can Predict

- Fat Tony did not believe in predictions but **predicted that predictors would go bust**
- His model: **identify fragilities**, bet on the collapse, collect big after the collapse, then have lunch
- Someone who predicts will be **fragile to prediction errors** — Fat Tony is antifragile by taking the mirror image of his fragile prey

## Chapter 10: Seneca's Upside and Downside

### Seneca Solved Antifragility

- **Lucius Annaeus Seneca**: philosopher who was the wealthiest person in the Roman Empire — fortune of **three hundred million denarii**
- He walked the walk — focused on practical Stoicism: how to handle adversity, poverty, and critically, **wealth**
- **Wisdom in decision making** is vastly more important than knowledge — to become a successful philosopher king, better to **start as a king than a philosopher**
- **Professor Triffat** wrote the textbook on "rational decision making" but when facing a real decision (Harvard appointment) angrily said: "Come on, this is serious!" Seneca is nothing but "this is serious"

### Stoicism's Emotional Robustification

- Success brings asymmetry: you have **a lot more to lose than to gain** — hence fragile (Damocles' sword)
- Seneca: possessions make us worry about **downside**, acting as punishment. Dependence on circumstances induces **a form of slavery**

> "He is in debt, whether he borrowed from another person or from fortune."

- The modern Stoic sage transforms fear into prudence, pain into information, mistakes into initiation, desire into undertaking
- Stoicism is about the **domestication**, not elimination, of emotions

### How to Become the Master — Seneca's Barbell

- Seneca went beyond robustness: wealth is the **slave of the wise man and master of the fool** — he *kept the upside*
- He played a trick on fate: **kept the good and ditched the bad** — cut the downside and kept the upside

> "The bookkeeping of benefits is simple: it is all expenditure; if any one returns it, that is clear gain; if he does not return it, it is not lost, I gave it for the sake of giving."

### The Foundational Asymmetry

- The fragile has **more to lose than to gain** from volatility; the antifragile has **more to gain than to lose**
- If you have less to lose than to gain, you like volatility and you are antifragile
- If you have more upside than downside, you may be **harmed by lack of volatility**

> *Fragility implies more to lose than to gain, equals more downside than upside, equals (unfavorable) asymmetry*

> *Antifragility implies more to gain than to lose, equals more upside than downside, equals (favorable) asymmetry*

## Chapter 11: Never Marry the Rock Star

### On Irreversibility and Path Dependence

- The first step toward antifragility: **decrease downside** rather than increase upside

- The **barbell**: a combination of **extremes kept separate**, with avoidance of the middle

- In finance: **90% in boring cash, 10% in maximally risky securities** — you cannot lose more than 10%, while exposed to massive upside. 100% in "medium" risk has a **risk of total ruin** from miscomputed risks

## Chapter 12: Thales' Sweet Grapes

### The Teleological Fallacy and the Rational Flaneur

- **Teleological fallacy**: the illusion you know exactly where you are going — almost all important innovations came from **serendipity and tinkering**, not directed research
- The **rational flaneur** makes decisions at every step based on new information — optionality in its purest form. **Steve Jobs** was a rational flaneur: distrusting focus groups, relying on tinkering
- America's principal asset is **risk taking and optionality** — the culture of trial-and-error with **no shame in failing**

### Thales of Miletus

- **Thales** put a deposit on olive presses before harvest — the first recorded **option contract**. Limited loss (small deposit), large possible outcome (controlling presses in a great harvest)
- Aristotle missed the financial insight, interpreting it as philosophers proving they could be rich — missing the asymmetry of payoff

### Options Are Agents of Antifragility

- An **option** = asymmetry + rationality: keep the favorable outcomes, let the unfavorable go
- Options benefit from volatility and uncertainty — exactly like antifragile systems
- **Financial independence** gives you the ultimate option: the freedom to choose
- **Sweet grapes** (Thales) vs. **sour grapes** (Aesop's fox): someone who *could* be rich but chooses not to is fundamentally different from someone who *can't* and rationalizes

### Things That Like Dispersion

- Options care about the **favorable tail**, not the average. For authors: better to have **fanatical** supporters than lukewarm ones
- **Luxury goods** don't care about average income — they care about **dispersion**
- **Larry Summers' controversy**: males and females have equal average intelligence but males have more **variance** — the number of successful scientists depends on the tails

> "Growth in society may not come from raising the average, but from increasing the number of people in the 'tails.'"

### The Thalesian vs. the Aristotelian

- The **Thalesian** focuses on payoff (asymmetries). The **Aristotelian** focuses on being right or wrong
- Because of asymmetry, knowledge about an event and making profits from it are **not the same thing**
- If you have optionality, you don't need much intelligence — all you need is wisdom to **not do unintelligent things** and **recognize favorable outcomes when they occur**
- **Nature and options**: Francois Jacob's **bricolage** — trial and error. Nature selects even within the womb (half of embryos undergo spontaneous abortion). **Optionality is a substitute for intelligence**

### Life Is Long Gamma

- Rabbi **Anthony Glickman**: **"Life is long gamma"** — life benefits from volatility
- The largest wealth generators in America: **real estate** (option at expense of banks) and **technology** (trial and error). Banks have **negative optionality** — losing periodically every penny made in their history

- We see high research in wealthy countries and conclude research generates wealth — a classic epiphenomenal error

## Chapter 18: On the Difference Between a Large Stone and a Thousand Pebbles

### The Simple Rule

- A king swore to crush his son with a large stone. His sage advised: cut the stone into pebbles. **The difference between a thousand pebbles and one large stone** illustrates how fragility stems from nonlinear effects
- **Nonlinear**: doubling the dose gives a lot more or a lot less than double the effect. The response is a **curve, not a straight line**

> *For the fragile, shocks bring higher harm as their intensity increases.*

- Driving at 50 mph into a wall causes more than ten times the harm of 5 mph. Seven bottles of wine at once is more harmful than one per day for seven days. Jumping from thirty feet brings more than ten times the harm of three feet
- This is the expansion of **Seneca's foundational asymmetry** — asymmetry is necessarily nonlinearity

### Why Is Fragility Nonlinear?

- If jumping one millimeter caused a linear fraction of the damage of jumping thirty feet, we would be dead within hours from pacing in our rooms
- What is fragile is something **both unbroken and subjected to nonlinear effects** — and extreme events, since large impacts are rarer than small ones
- Close to eight thousand **microearthquakes** daily (below 2 on Richter) — totally harmless; shocks of 6+ make the news

> *For the fragile, the cumulative effect of small shocks is smaller than the single effect of an equivalent large shock.*

### Convexity and Concavity

- **Concave** (curves inward) = fragile, looks like a **frown**. **Convex** (curves outward) = antifragile, looks like a **smile**
- Why asymmetry maps to convexity: if for a given variation you have **more upside than downside**, the curve is convex
- The convex **likes volatility** — if you earn more than you lose from fluctuations, you want many fluctuations

### Traffic in New York

- Traffic is highly nonlinear — one can add cars with minimal impact, then a 10% increase makes travel time jump 50%
- The average number of cars doesn't matter; what matters is the **volatility** — every additional car increases travel time more than the previous one
- **Central banks** can print money with no effect, then "unexpectedly" it causes a jump in inflation
- NYC authorized a film company to use the **Fifty-ninth Street Bridge** on a Saturday — the small closure turned minutes into hours due to **multiplicative effects**

### Size and Fragility

- **P. W. Anderson**: "More Is Different" — a city is not a large village; a corporation is not a larger small business
- **A "balanced meal"** misses the hidden dimension of variability — consuming no protein Monday and catching up Wednesday causes a *better* physiological response through nonlinearity
- **Run, don't walk**: health benefits are **convex to speed** — sprinting and resting beats walking steadily

### How to Be Squeezed

- A **squeeze** occurs when people must do something immediately regardless of cost — squeezes become **nonlinearly costlier** as size increases
- **Why you should not own an elephant as a pet**: water shortage costs scale nonlinearly with size
- **Richard Roll's** "hubris hypothesis": mergers show no gain from increased size — gains are visible but **risks are hidden**
- **Jared Diamond**: large animals become extinct more easily ("Why Cats Have Nine Lives") — elephants break limbs easily; cats and mice survive falls from several times their height

### The Societe Generale Fire Sale

- **Jerome Kerviel** rogue trading forced a $70 billion fire sale — markets dropped ~10%, costing **$6 billion** in losses
- A fire sale a tenth that size would result in **no loss at all** — if ten smaller banks each had a "Micro-Kerviel," total losses would be **close to nothing**
- The problem is not "bad controls" or "greed" — it is primarily **size and the fragility that comes from size**

### Project Size, Wars, and the Planning Fallacy

- **Bent Flyvbjerg**: project cost overruns increase markedly with size — for bridges and tunnels (monolithic), not for roads (built in segments)
- Planes tend to land later, never earlier — uncertainty creates **one-way asymmetry** in delays
- The **Great War** estimated to last months — cost at least **ten times** projected financial costs. The **Iraq war** estimated at $30-60B, swelled to **more than $2 trillion**
- The **planning fallacy** is not psychological — it is **inherent in the nonlinear structure** of projects. Errors add to the right end of the timeline, not the left
- The **Empire State Building** and **London Crystal Palace** (concept to opening in nine months) were completed on time — a much more **linear economy** with fewer nonlinearities
- **Black Swan effects are necessarily increasing** due to complexity, interdependence, globalization, and "efficiency"

### Pollution, Concentration, and Wealth

- Fossil fuel harm is **concave** — a little is devoid of harm, a lot causes climatic disturbances. **Split pollution sources** to reduce total harm
- **Jennifer Dunne** on the **Aleuts** (hunter-gatherers): remarkable **lack of concentration** in predatorial behavior — whenever low on a resource, they switched to another, preserving the ecosystem
- **Globalization** made contagions planetary — the entire world became a huge room with narrow exits
- Simply by being richer, the world has additional **unpredictability and fragility** — wealth means more headaches

## Chapter 19: The Philosopher's Stone and Its Inverse

### How to Detect Who Will Go Bust

- In 2003, journalist **Alex Berenson** brought Taleb **secret risk reports of Fannie Mae** from a defector
- A move upward in an economic variable led to **massive losses**, a move downward to **small profits** — further moves upward led to even larger losses, further moves downward to even smaller profits
- Their exposures were severely **concave** — losses accelerated as economic variables deviated. Blowup was inevitable
- Taleb kept telling anyone who would listen that Fannie Mae was **"sitting on a barrel of dynamite"** — people said his opinion was wrong (the stock was going up)
- He checked similar institutions and realized a **total collapse of the banking system was a certainty**
- Things happened as planned by destiny — it just took **a bit longer than expected**

### The Fragility Detection Heuristic

- The technique: detecting **acceleration of harm** — if when traffic increases by 10K cars travel time grows 10 minutes, but the next 10K adds **30 minutes**, that acceleration shows fragility
- **Government deficits** are concave to economic conditions — every additional deviation makes deficits incrementally worse
- **Financial leverage** has the same effect — you need to borrow more and more, just as in a **Ponzi scheme**
- Taleb collaborated with **Raphael Douady** to prove the **link between nonlinearity, dislike of volatility, and fragility**
- The method applies to **Fukushima-style computations** — all small probabilities tend to be very fragile to errors; a small change in assumptions can make probability rise from one-per-million to one-per-hundred

### Positive and Negative Model Error

- Execution errors are neutral in the long run (both ways). But with **negative convexity effects**, there is a **one-way outcome** — planes land later, wars get worse, travel time only increases
- This one-sidedness brings both **underestimation of randomness** and **underestimation of harm**

### How to Lose a Grandmother (Jensen's Inequality)

- Your grandmother will spend two hours at an average of **70 degrees F** — but the first hour at **zero degrees** and the second at **140 degrees**. Result: **no grandmother**
- Temperature changes become more harmful as they deviate from 70 — the **variability** is more important than the average
- The notion of average is of **no significance** when one is fragile to variations — the **dispersion** matters more (the **second-order effect** or **convexity effect**)

> *Do not cross a river if it is on average four feet deep.*

### The Philosopher's Stone (Jensen's Inequality)

- The traffic example: 90,000 then 110,000 cars (average 100,000) produces horrendous traffic; 100,000 both periods is smooth
- Key principles: (a) The more **nonlinear**, the more the function divorces from the something; (b) The more **volatile**, the more the function divorces; (c) If the function is **convex** (antifragile), the average of the function exceeds the function of the average — **and the reverse for concave** (fragile)
- The **squaring function** example: average of the square payoff of a die (15.17) is **higher** than the square of the average payoff (12.25) — a 24% "edge," the **hidden benefit of antifragility**
- Someone with a **convex payoff** can **guess worse than random** and still outperform

> *This explains my statement that you can be dumb and antifragile and still do very well.*

- This hidden "convexity bias" is **Jensen's inequality** — what the common discourse on innovation is missing
- The **inverse philosopher's stone** (concave): the hidden harm of fragility means you need to be **much better than random** just to offset the negative effect
- If you have **favorable asymmetries** (positive convexity, options), you will outperform the average in uncertainty — the more uncertainty, the more optionality kicks in

> *Molto facile.*
