Key Takeaways

  1. Antifragility is beyond robustness. The opposite of fragile is not robust — it’s something that gains from disorder. Robust systems merely resist shocks; antifragile systems get stronger. There was no word for it, so Taleb coined one.
  2. Depriving systems of stressors kills them. Muscles atrophy without exercise, economies blow up after artificial calm, political systems explode after forced stability. Volatility is not noise — it’s information and nourishment.
  3. The Triad replaces prediction. Fragile–Robust–Antifragile is more useful than forecasting Black Swans. You can’t predict rare events, but you can measure fragility and position accordingly.
  4. Small is antifragile, large is fragile. Switzerland’s canton system, restaurant ecosystems, and artisanal careers thrive through distributed volatility. Large centralized systems accumulate hidden risks that explode catastrophically.
  5. Skin in the game is the core ethical rule. Those who gain from volatility while transferring downside to others are the greatest fragilizers — bureaucrats, bankers, academics with no personal exposure.
  6. Seneca’s asymmetry is the key. Cut your downside, keep your upside. The barbell strategy — combining extreme safety with extreme risk — dominates the “moderate middle,” which is a sucker’s game.
  7. Optionality is a substitute for intelligence. Trial and error with the rationality to keep what works outperforms directed research. Evolution, cooking, and Silicon Valley all operate this way.
  8. Naive interventionism is iatrogenic. Most attempts to “help” complex systems cause more harm than benefit — from overmedication to central banking to nation-building. Procrastination is often the wisest response.
  9. Via negativa outperforms via positiva. Subtraction beats addition. Knowledge grows by removing what’s wrong. The best risk management is eliminating fragilities, not adding “improvements.”
  10. The Lindy Effect governs the nonperishable. For technologies, ideas, and books, every additional year of survival doubles expected remaining life. Your grandmother’s heuristics beat business school theories.
  11. Nonlinearity is where fragility lives. The difference between a large stone and a thousand pebbles of equivalent weight is the essence of fragility — harm accelerates with dose.
  12. Modern “efficiency” is fragility in disguise. Optimization removes redundancy, which is the buffer against Black Swans. Soviet-Harvard rationalism treats organic systems like machines and guarantees eventual catastrophic failure.
  13. Information is antifragile. Books benefit from being banned, ideas thrive when attacked, criticism is an unfaked badge of relevance. The more you try to suppress information, the more it spreads.
  14. Practitioners trump theorists. The Industrial Revolution, medicine, cooking, and finance were all driven by tinkerers, not academics. Universities claim credit for innovations that arose from trial and error.
  15. Nature is the ultimate antifragile system. Evolution loves randomness and disorder. Organisms die so species can adapt. Hormesis, caloric restriction, and overcompensation are features, not bugs.

The Triad: Fragile–Robust–Antifragile

  • Damocles is fragile — the sword hangs by a thread; it’s only a matter of time
  • Phoenix is robust — reborn unchanged from its ashes, returning to the same state
  • Hydra is antifragile — cut one head, two grow back; it gains from harm
  • Every domain can be classified: economies, health, knowledge, politics, careers, relationships
  • Fragility implies more to lose than to gain (unfavorable asymmetry); antifragility implies more to gain than to lose (favorable asymmetry)
  • Fragility is measurable; risk is not (outside of casinos). Focus on detecting fragility rather than predicting events

Modernity as Fragilizer

  • Centralized nation-states concentrate risk — converting Mediocristan (many small variations) into Extremistan (rare but catastrophic jumps)
  • “Efficiency” removes vital redundancies — having two kidneys is “inefficient” until you need both
  • The separation of action from consequence (agency problem) — bankers get bonuses, taxpayers get bailouts
  • Soviet-Harvard rationalism: treating cats like washing machines — organic, complex systems are not mechanical
  • Touristification: the systematic removal of uncertainty and randomness from life
  • Violence transferred from individuals to states; financial indiscipline transferred from individuals to governments
  • Modernity starts with the state monopoly on violence and ends with the state’s monopoly on fiscal irresponsibility
“Wind extinguishes a candle and energizes fire. You want to be the fire and wish for the wind.”

The Barbell Strategy

  • 90% ultra-safe + 10% ultra-risky beats 100% “moderate” — you cannot possibly lose more than 10%, while you are exposed to massive upside
  • Someone with 100% in “medium-risk” securities has a risk of total ruin from miscomputation of risks
  • Applies to finance, careers (sinecure + creative work), health (sprint or walk, never jog), social life (reliable friends + wild adventurers)
  • Seneca’s barbell: kept his 300 million denarii but mentally wrote them off daily — the upside of wealth with the emotional robustness of poverty
  • Literature barbell: Kafka was an insurance executive, Spinoza a lens grinder, Trollope a postal clerk — sinecure + writing is vastly superior to full-time dependence on publishers
  • Biological barbell: monogamous birds cheat with the “rock star” — 90% accountant for stability, 10% alpha for genetic upside
  • Ray Dalio’s rule: “Make sure the probability of the unacceptable is nil.” Then take as much risk as you want on the upside
“Provide for the worst; the best can take care of itself.”

Optionality & Trial-and-Error

  • Option = asymmetry + rationality: the right but not the obligation; keep the good, discard the bad
  • Thales’ olive presses: the first option contract in history — small deposit for the right to use presses if the harvest was great; known, bounded loss; large, open-ended gain
  • The rational flâneur: unlike a tourist with a fixed itinerary, revises his route at every step based on new information
  • America’s principal asset is risk-taking culture with no shame in failing, not resources or education
  • The wheeled suitcase took 6,000 years between the invention of the wheel and putting tiny wheels on luggage — simplicity is only trivial in retrospect
  • Steve Jobs’ “Stay hungry, stay foolish” probably meant: be crazy but retain the rationality of choosing the upper bound when you see it
  • Evolution produces astonishingly sophisticated objects without intelligence — through optionality plus a selection filter, plus randomness
“If you think that education causes wealth, or that intelligent actions and discoveries are the result of intelligent ideas, you will be in for a surprise.”

Via Negativa

  • Subtractive knowledge is more robust than additive — what we know is wrong cannot turn out to be right; what we know is right might turn out to be wrong
  • Charlatans give positive advice (“Ten Steps for—”); practitioners teach what to avoid
  • Chess grandmasters win by not losing; people become rich by not going bust; religions are mostly about interdicts
  • Michelangelo on David: “I just remove everything that is not David”
  • Steve Jobs: “Innovation is saying no to 1,000 things”
  • The 80/20 rule: 1% modification of systems can lower fragility by about 99%
  • If you have more than one reason to do something, just don’t do it — obvious decisions require no more than a single reason
  • More data ≠ more knowledge — as Paul Valéry wrote: “How many things one should disregard in order to act”

Contrarian Ideas

  • Stability causes instability. Artificially suppressing volatility (in economies, political systems, forests) guarantees a larger eventual explosion. The longer the calm, the worse the storm. Small forest fires prevent catastrophic ones.
  • Education doesn’t cause wealth. Countries get educated because they’re wealthy, not the other way around. Taiwan surpassed the Philippines despite lower initial literacy. The green lumber fallacy: what matters for success is often completely different from what outsiders assume.
  • Procrastination is wise risk management. It’s not laziness — it’s your evolved self telling you the intervention isn’t worth the iatrogenic risk. Fabius Maximus defeated Hannibal by delaying. Lao Tzu’s wu-wei: “passive achievement.”
  • Greed is an epiphenomenon, not a cause. Every crisis is blamed on greed, but greed is ancient and constant. The real cause is systemic fragility — making the world greed-proof beats trying to eliminate greed.
  • Predictions are iatrogenic. Even random numerical forecasts cause people to take more risk. The solution isn’t better predictions — it’s building systems that don’t need predictions.
  • Medium risk is the most dangerous. The “golden middle” is where measurement errors are largest. The barbell’s extremes are both more knowable than the squishy center.
  • “What doesn’t kill me makes me stronger” is usually misinterpreted. It’s not the individual who gets stronger — it’s the system. Some individuals must fail for the collective to benefit.
  • The old is superior to the new. The Lindy Effect: for nonperishable things, every additional year of survival increases expected remaining life. Bet against neomania.

Author’s Key Proposals

  1. Detect fragility, don’t predict events. Use the fragility heuristic — check if harm accelerates with dose (concavity). If a small perturbation causes disproportionate damage, you’re fragile.
  2. Apply the barbell everywhere. Finance (90% cash, 10% speculative), career (stable job + creative side projects), health (intense exercise + complete rest), knowledge (deep expertise + broad exploration).
  3. Demand skin in the game. Never trust anyone whose downside is disconnected from their advice. The chief ethical rule: do not have antifragility at the expense of others’ fragility.
  4. Respect the Lindy Effect. Bet on the old over the new for anything nonperishable. Technologies, ideas, and practices that have survived are more likely to continue surviving.
  5. Practice via negativa. Remove fragilities before adding improvements. Subtract harmful things (debt, overmedication, toxic people, excessive data) rather than adding new things.
  6. Embrace redundancy. Extra cash, extra capacity, extra time buffers are investments, not waste. Redundancy is not defensive; it is more like investment than insurance.
  7. Let small fires burn. Don’t suppress small crises in complex systems. Small forest fires prevent catastrophic ones. Small business failures prevent systemic ones.
  8. Favor tinkering over planning. The rational flâneur approach — revise at every step, keep what works, discard what doesn’t. Business plans are narrative traps.
  9. Limit size and concentration. Break large entities into smaller ones. The harm from a single Kerviel ($6B loss) is nonlinearly worse than ten micro-Kerviels (≈$0 loss).

Detailed Chapter Notes

Prologue: How to Love the Wind

  • Wind extinguishes a candle and energizes fire — you want to use randomness, not hide from it
  • Core definition: some things benefit from shocks; they thrive when exposed to volatility, randomness, disorder, and stressors
  • Antifragility is beyond resilience — the resilient resists shocks and stays the same; the antifragile gets better
  • Fragility can be measured; risk is not measurable (outside of casinos). This provides a solution to the Black Swan problem
  • The chief ethical rule: thou shalt not have antifragility at the expense of the fragility of others
“I’d rather be dumb and antifragile than extremely smart and fragile, any time.”

Book I — Ch. 1: Between Damocles and Hydra

  • The post office package thought experiment: “fragile — handle with care” has no opposite. The true opposite is “please mishandle”
  • The fragile is at best unharmed; the robust is at best and worst unharmed; the antifragile is at worst unharmed
  • Three mythological equivalents: Damocles (fragile), Phoenix (robust), Hydra (antifragile)
  • Mithridatism: sub-lethal doses of poison build immunity — proto-antifragility, a step toward robustness
  • Hormesis: one step further — the poisonous substance in small doses makes you better off overall
  • Domain dependence: people grasp antifragility in one domain but fail to see it in another — the Dubai banker who has a porter carry his bags, then lifts kettlebells at the gym
“Half of life — the interesting half of life — we don’t have a name for.”

Ch. 2: Overcompensation and Overreaction Everywhere

  • Post-traumatic growth: the opposite of PTSD — people harmed by past events surpass themselves. Popular culture knows it (“it builds character”) though intellectuals miss it
  • Innovation from necessity: artificia docuit fames (sophistication born of hunger); the excess energy from overreaction to setbacks is what innovates
  • Best horses lose against slower rivals — undercompensation from absence of challenge
  • Whispering, not shouting: being slightly inaudible causes listeners to switch into intellectual overdrive (disfluency effect)
  • Antifragile redundancy: Nature likes to overinsure — two kidneys, extra capacity, overcompensation. Redundancy is not defensive but aggressive investment
  • The Lucretius Problem: the fool believes the tallest mountain equals the tallest one he’s observed. Fukushima was built to withstand the worst past earthquake. Nature prepares for what hasn’t happened
  • Information is antifragile: banned books thrive; criticism is an unfaked badge of attention; the more you try to suppress, the more it spreads
“The higher you build your barricades, the stronger we become.” — Irish revolutionary song

Ch. 3: The Cat and the Washing Machine

  • Bold conjecture: everything that has life is antifragile to some extent; the secret of life is antifragility
  • Bones get denser with episodic stress (Wolff’s Law); muscles grow from overload; a dish or car cannot
  • Stressors are information — complex systems convey information through stress, via hormones and messengers
  • For the nonorganic, equilibrium is inertia; for the organic, equilibrium is death
  • Touristification: systematic removal of uncertainty — the guilty parties include education, planning, gym machines, electronic calendars
  • Much of modern life is preventable chronic stress injury — walking on uneven terrain means no two steps are identical, unlike the gym machine

Ch. 4: What Kills Me Makes Others Stronger

  • The antifragility of some comes at the expense of the fragility of others — restaurants are individually fragile but collectively antifragile
  • The fragility of every startup is necessary for the economy to be antifragile — that’s what makes entrepreneurship work
  • Evolution only works because of its antifragility — organisms need to die for nature to be antifragile; an immortal organism would need to predict the future perfectly
  • Every plane crash makes the next flight safer; every bank crash makes the next one more likely — aviation is antifragile; banking is not
  • Taleb’s dream: National Entrepreneur Day — “Most of you will fail, but we are grateful for the risks you are taking. You are at the source of our antifragility.”

Book II — Ch. 5: The Souk and the Office Building

  • John vs. George (twins): bank clerk with predictable income vs. taxi driver with variable income — John is fragile to a single employer; George is antifragile to many small ones
  • Switzerland: the most antifragile place on earth — bottom-up cantons, no real central government; the most stable and wealthy country in history, built on apprenticeships not universities
  • The northern Levant: 12,000 years of prosperity through decentralized trading — destroyed overnight when the Baath Party centralized Syria
  • “We people of Aleppo prefer war to prison” — Lebanon, despite civil war, ended up 3–6x wealthier than centralized Syria
  • Mediocristan vs. Extremistan: bottom-up variations produce many small fluctuations (Mediocristan); centralized systems produce rare catastrophic jumps (Extremistan)
  • Steven Pinker’s error: saying nuclear bombs are safer because they explode less often — risk is in the potential, not the past

Ch. 6: Tell Them I Love (Some) Randomness

  • Maxwell’s Governors (1867): tight controls on steam engines led to instability — light control works; close control leads to overreaction and crashes
  • Small forest fires cleanse flammable material; suppressing them makes the big one catastrophic
  • Market “cleanups”: volatile markets flush out “weak hands” regularly; calm markets let fragile positions accumulate until mass panic
  • Buridan’s Donkey: stuck between food and water, only randomness can unlock it — absence of randomness equals guaranteed death
  • Stochastic resonance: adding random noise makes weak signals audible — randomness is free and nothing does better
  • Annealing: heating metal causes atoms to find better configurations — simulated annealing in math uses randomness for optimal solutions
  • Political annealing: Athenian assemblies chosen by lot; Voltaire’s best government tempered by assassination; randomize rulers, not citizens
  • Saudi Arabia: U.S.-backed forced stability creates explosive fragility — preventing noise makes the problem worse in the long run
“One of life’s packages: no stability without volatility.”

Ch. 7: Naive Intervention

  • Tonsillectomy study (1930s): 389 children shown to doctors; ~45% recommended surgery regardless of need — probabilistic homicide at work
  • Iatrogenics: literally “caused by the healer” — medical error kills 3–10x as many people as car accidents in the U.S.
  • George Washington’s death: doctors hastened it through bloodletting (5–9 pounds of blood); medicine had a negative balance sheet until penicillin
  • Semmelweis: showed hospital births killed more women than street births; called doctors criminals; punished, died in an asylum of the very hospital fever he warned against
  • Alan Greenspan: “top economic iatrogenist of all time” — ironed out the boom-bust cycle until risks exploded catastrophically
  • Non-naive interventionism: intervene to limit size, concentration, and speed — not to suppress all volatility
  • Fabius Maximus: defeated Hannibal by delaying engagement — procrastination is our natural defense against iatrogenics
  • Signal vs. noise: yearly data is 50/50; daily is 95% noise; hourly is 99.5% noise — anyone who listens to news is one step below sucker
“The final lesson is that one should not expect laurels for bringing the truth.”

Ch. 8: Prediction as a Child of Modernity

  • Our track record in predicting significant rare events in politics and economics is not close to zero; it is zero
  • Forecasting is iatrogenic — providing someone with a random numerical forecast increases his risk taking, even if he knows the projections are random
  • The robust and antifragile don’t need as accurate a comprehension of the world as the fragile — extra cash in the bank eliminates the need for prediction
  • Those in debt, because of their fragility, need to predict with more and more accuracy
  • The Fourth Quadrant: where we have high exposure to rare events and these events are incomputable — focus on getting out of it

Book III — Ch. 9: Fat Tony and the Fragilistas

  • Fat Tony (Brooklyn trader) and Nero Tulip (intellectual) — both predicted the 2008 crisis by detecting sucker fragility
  • Fat Tony made “high eight to low nine figures” from the crisis; Nero made a bit less but was satisfied
  • Fat Tony’s method: identify fragile entities, bet on their collapse, have lunch
  • You can’t predict in general, but you can predict that those who rely on predictions will eventually go bust
  • Curiosity is antifragile — magnified by attempts to satisfy it; books have a secret mission to multiply
  • By betting against fragility, they were antifragile — Fat Tony took a mirror image of his fragile prey

Ch. 10: Seneca’s Upside and Downside

  • Seneca: wealthiest person in Rome (300M denarii), tutor to Emperor Nero, prominent Stoic — walked the walk
  • Stoicism is not about indifference to fate but about antifragility from fate — no downside from Lady Fortuna, plenty of upside
  • Success brings asymmetry: you have more to lose than to gain — hence fragile. The pain of loss exceeds the pleasure of gain
  • Seneca’s method: mentally write off possessions so actual losses produce no sting — like buying insurance against emotional harm
  • He kept the wealth but cut the downside — “the bookkeeping of benefits is simple: it is all expenditure; if any one returns it, that is clear gain”
  • The modern Stoic sage: transforms fear into prudence, pain into information, mistakes into initiation, and desire into undertaking
“He is in debt, whether he borrowed from another person or from fortune.”

Ch. 11: Never Marry the Rock Star

  • The barbell strategy: combination of two extremes with nothing in the middle — extreme risk aversion on one side, extreme risk loving on the other
  • Financial barbell: 90% in boring cash, 10% in maximally risky securities — maximum known loss with exposure to massive upside
  • Path dependence: a broken vase cannot be reassembled — fragility has a ratchet-like property of irreversibility
  • Literature barbell: French writers took sinecures (civil servant jobs) then wrote freely — Kafka, Spinoza, Trollope all had day jobs
  • Biological barbell: monogamous birds cheat with the alpha; females want both economic stability and good genes
  • Health barbell: sprint or walk, never jog; maximum intensity weight lifting + complete rest
  • Just as Stoicism is the domestication of emotions, the barbell is the domestication of uncertainty

Book IV — Ch. 12: Thales’ Sweet Grapes

  • The teleological fallacy: the illusion that you know exactly where you are going — most important innovations came from serendipity and tinkering
  • The rational flâneur: makes decisions at every step to revise his schedule based on new information — pure optionality
  • Thales’ olive presses: first option contract in history — small deposit, limited loss, large open-ended gain
  • Option = asymmetry + rationality: keep what is good and ditch the bad — you need the rationality to recognize a good outcome
  • Sweet grapes vs. sour grapes: Thales chose philosophy over wealth from a position of strength — genuine choice vs. rationalized failure
  • Life is “long gamma” (Rabbi Glickman) — benefits from volatility and variability
  • Banks have negative optionality — they periodically lose every penny made in their history

Ch. 13: Lecturing Birds on How to Fly

  • The wheeled suitcase took ~6,000 years after the wheel — technology is trivial retrospectively, not prospectively
  • The Soviet-Harvard Department of Ornithology: academics lecture birds, birds fly, academics claim credit for bird flight
  • Epiphenomena: seeing wealthy countries with high research, then inferring research causes wealth (compass-on-bridge error)
  • The real process: Random Tinkering → Heuristics → Practice → More Tinkering (not Theory → Application)
  • The Green Lumber Fallacy: Joe Siegel (top lumber trader) thought “green lumber” was painted green — what matters for success is different from what outsiders assume

Ch. 14: When Two Things Are Not the “Same Thing”

  • Fat Tony’s Kuwait trade: made $18M from $300K — everyone predicted oil would rise with war, but the price had already adjusted. “Kuwait and oil are not the same ting”
  • Conflation: mistaking something for its function — the event and its price effect are not the same
  • Jim Simons (Renaissance Technologies): never hires economists; uses physicists for pattern recognition without theorizing
  • Heuristic knowledge embedded in traditions is empirically superior to business school theories — grandmother’s wisdom survives because the grandmother survived

Ch. 15: History Written by the Losers

  • Table of misattributions: jet engines (tinkering, not physics), architecture (heuristics, not Euclid), derivatives formulas (traders, not Black-Scholes), medicine (luck and side effects, not biology)
  • The Industrial Revolution: driven by uneducated, isolated tinkerers — textile machines, steam engines had nothing to do with science
  • Two main sources of innovation: the hobbyist and the English rector — both in barbell situations (secure income + free time + optionality)
  • NCI’s “war on cancer”: 144,000 plant extracts screened, zero approved drugs — major cancer drugs (Vinca Alkaloids) were discovered by chance
  • Strategic planning has no evidence of working — Coca-Cola began as a pharmaceutical, Nokia as a paper mill, DuPont as explosives
  • Rules: (i) look for optionality, (ii) prefer open-ended payoffs, (iii) invest in people not business plans, (iv) make sure you are barbelled

Ch. 16: A Lesson in Disorder

  • The ecological vs. the ludic: classroom skills stay in the classroom; chess ability doesn’t improve general reasoning
  • E. O. Wilson: the biggest hindrance to child development is the soccer mom — eliminating trial and error from children’s lives
  • Only the autodidacts are free — lions in captivity live longer but are not lions
  • Taleb’s barbell education: studied the minimum to pass exams, read 30–60 hours per week of self-directed reading
  • “The trick is to be bored with a specific book, not with reading itself” — jump from book to book when bored; trial and error is freedom
“We do not study for life, but only for the lecture room.” — Seneca

Book V — Ch. 18: A Large Stone and a Thousand Pebbles

  • The King’s Stone: a king swore to crush his son with a large stone; his advisor had it cut into pebbles — fragility stems from nonlinear effects
  • Driving into a wall at 50 mph is more than 10x the harm of 5 mph; drinking 7 bottles of wine at once is worse than 1/day for 7 days
  • Traffic is highly nonlinear: adding 10% more cars can increase travel time by 50% — the average number of cars doesn’t matter; volatility does
  • The Société Générale fire sale: $70B sold at once = $6B loss; $7B would have caused zero loss — size is the real problem, not “bad controls”
  • Projects take longer, never shorter: not a psychological bias but inherent in the nonlinear structure — errors can only extend, never compress time
  • The Iraq War: expected $30–60B, actual cost swelled to over $2 trillion
  • Pollution harm is concave: split sources among many small ones rather than concentrating in one large source
“Do not cross a river if it is on average four feet deep.”

Ch. 19: The Philosopher’s Stone

  • Fannie Mae (2003): Taleb saw their secret risk reports — moves upward caused massive losses, moves downward produced only small profits. Acceleration of harm = certain blowup
  • Fragility detection heuristic: if harm accelerates with dose (concavity), the system is fragile. Simple, measurable, tractable
  • Jensen’s inequality: the hidden benefit of antifragility — with convex payoffs, you can guess worse than random and still outperform
  • The inverse philosopher’s stone: with concave payoffs, you need to be much better than random just to break even
  • Small probability fragility: a small change in assumptions can make probability rise from 1/million to 1/hundred — 10,000-fold underestimation

Book VI — Via Negativa & The Lindy Effect

  • The apophatic method: define by what something is not — Pseudo-Dionysos, Neoplatonists, Michelangelo carving David by subtraction
  • Charlatans are recognizable: they give only positive advice (“Ten Steps for—”). Pros use the negative: chess masters win by not losing
  • Subtractive knowledge: what is wrong is robust; what is right is fragile. Knowledge grows by subtraction more than addition
  • Bergson’s razor: a philosopher should be known for one single idea, not more
  • The Lindy Effect: for the nonperishable, every additional day implies a longer life expectancy. A book in print for 40 years will be in print another 40
  • Neomania: the love of the modern for its own sake — the on-off switch heuristic: anything with an on-off switch is prone to neomania and fragility
  • Technology at its best is invisible — greatest benefit when it displaces the deleterious (barefoot shoes, tablets returning to Babylonian writing)

Medicine, Convexity, and Opacity (Chs. 20–22)

  • Iatrogenics in medicine: overmedication is a convexity problem — harm accelerates nonlinearly while benefits plateau
  • Via negativa in health: removing harmful things (sugar, smoking, excess food) is more robust than adding supplements or drugs
  • The barbell in diet: episodic fasting + occasional feasting outperforms steady “balanced” meals — caloric restriction activates healthy hormetic pathways
  • Run, don’t walk: health benefits are convex to speed (up to a point) — sprinting + rest outperforms steady jogging
  • The “balanced meal” myth: consuming no protein Monday and catching up Wednesday produces better physiological response than steady intake
  • Drug interactions: with tens of thousands of drugs on the market, interactions are underestimated by a factor of four — the pool should be shrinking, not growing

Book VII — Ch. 23: Skin in the Game

  • The largest fragilizer of society: absence of skin in the game — gaining upside from volatility while exposing others to downside
  • At no point in history have so many non-risk-takers exerted so much control — bureaucrats, bankers, academics with no personal exposure
  • In the past, people of rank took risks and bore consequences — today the reverse: inverse heroes game the system while citizens pay
  • Hammurabi’s Code: if a builder’s house collapses and kills the owner, the builder is put to death — perfect skin in the game
  • Corporate agency problem: CEOs collect bonuses in good years, taxpayers absorb losses in bad years — antifragility at the expense of others
  • The ethical rule generalized: never get into a plane with a pilot who doesn’t have skin in the game — your surgeon should share your downside

Ch. 24: Fitting Ethics to a Profession

  • Fat Tony’s ethics: “You will be ridiculed. Words are for sissies. A system based on verbal warnings will be dominated by non-risk-taking babblers”
  • The solution is not regulation but making people bear the consequences of their decisions
  • Large corporations are “too big to fail” because they have extracted themselves from the consequences of their actions
  • The problem is not capitalism but the modern version that separates entrepreneurs from liability
  • A man is honorable in proportion to the personal risks he takes for his opinion — the amount of downside he is exposed to
