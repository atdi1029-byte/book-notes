## Book VI: Via Negativa

## Chapter 20: Via Negativa

### The Apophatic Method

- The **"apophatic"** focuses on what cannot be said directly, from the Greek *apophasis* (saying no) -- leading to **via negativa**, the negative way, which does not try to express what something *is* but lists what it is *not*, proceeding by elimination
- Associated with **Pseudo-Dionysos the Areopagite**, a Near Eastern mystic confused with a judge converted by Paul -- he was the disciple of **Proclus** the Neoplatonist, who repeated the metaphor that **statues are carved by subtraction**

> *Michelangelo was asked by the pope about the secret of his genius, particularly how he carved the statue of David. His answer was: "It's simple. I just remove everything that is not David."*

- The **interventionista** focuses on positive action -- acts of **omission** are not considered acts and do not appear to be part of one's mission
- Taleb's lifelong heuristic: **charlatans give you only positive advice**, exploiting our sucker-proneness for "how to" recipes -- yet in practice it is the **negative** that's used by pros: chess grandmasters win by **not losing**; people get rich by **not going bust**; religions are mostly about **interdicts**

### Subtractive Knowledge

- The greatest contribution to knowledge consists in **removing what we think is wrong** -- **subtractive epistemology**
- Central tenet: **we know a lot more what is wrong than what is right** -- negative knowledge (what is wrong) is more robust to error than positive knowledge (what is right)
- **Knowledge grows by subtraction** much more than by addition -- what we know today might turn out wrong but what we know to be wrong cannot turn out right
- Since one observation can disprove a statement while millions can hardly confirm it, **disconfirmation is more rigorous than confirmation** -- associated with **Karl Popper**, but actually a central tenet of the **skeptical-empirical school of medicine** of the postclassical Eastern Mediterranean
- **Popper's limitation**: he missed that subtractive prediction works -- if an incompetent surgeon operates on a brain, one can safely predict serious damage; falsification of a theory should lead to the **breaking of the object** of its application

### Via Negativa in Political Systems and Decision Making

- A good political mechanism helps **remove the bad guy** -- it's not about who to put in; the bad can cause more harm than the collective actions of good ones
- **Jon Elster** wrote *Preventing Mischief*, basing negative action on **Bentham's idea** that the legislator's art is limited to preventing what might prevent liberty
- For the Arab scholar **Ali Bin Abi-Taleb**, keeping one's distance from an ignorant person is equivalent to keeping company with a wise man

> *"People think focus means saying yes to the thing you've got to focus on. But that's not what it means at all. It means saying no to the hundred other good ideas."* -- Steve Jobs

- **Subtractive knowledge** is a form of barbell -- critically, it is **convex**: what is wrong is robust; what you don't know is fragile, but you don't take it seriously so you make sure it does not harm you

### Less Is More

- Traced to **Makridakis, Dawes, Goldstein, and Gigerenzer**: **simpler methods** for forecasting work much better than complicated ones
- **Goldstein and Gigerenzer** coined **"fast and frugal" heuristics** -- good decisions despite limited time and knowledge
- The **eighty/twenty idea** (**Vilfredo Pareto**): moving toward **99/1** across many contemporary domains -- accordingly, **1 percent modification** of systems can lower fragility by about **99 percent**
- **Ezekiel Emanuel** showed half the population accounts for less than 3% of health care costs; the sickest 10% consume 64%
- **Bent Flyvbjerg** showed the bulk of corporate cost overruns come from large technology projects

> *As they say in the mafia, just work on removing the pebble in your shoe.*

### More Data, Less Predictability

- We have **never had more data** yet have **less predictability than ever** -- more data can make you miss the **big truck**
- **Convincing disciplines** (physics) use little statistical backup; **political science and economics** are full of elaborate statistics yet have never produced anything of note -- the person with the **largest number of alibis** turns out to be guilty
- The simple argument that **Black Swans and tail events run the socioeconomic world** -- and these cannot be predicted -- is sufficient to invalidate their statistics
- **Christopher Chabris and Daniel Simons** (*The Invisible Gorilla*): people watching a basketball game, diverted with counting passes, completely **miss a gorilla stepping into the middle of the court**

### The Single-Reason Heuristic

- If you have **more than one reason** to do something, **just don't do it** -- by invoking more than one reason you are trying to **convince yourself**; obvious decisions require no more than a **single reason**
- The French army rejected absenteeism excuses with **more than one reason** (death of grandmother + cold virus + boar bite)
- If someone attacks an idea using **more than one argument**, you know it is not real
- **Bergson's razor**: "A philosopher should be known for one single idea, not more"
- **Paul Valery** asked Einstein if he carried a notebook: "I never have ideas" -- he just did not have trivial ones
- Heuristic: if someone has a **long bio**, skip him -- if a resume covers "more than two or three lives," avoid like the bubonic plague

## Chapter 20: Time and Fragility

### The Lindy Effect and Prophetic Subtraction

- Antifragility implies -- contrary to instinct -- that **the old is superior to the new**
- **Time is the same as disorder** -- the fragile is harmed by both; the antifragile benefits from both
- The **via negativa method of prophecy**: what is fragile will eventually break, and we can tell what is fragile -- positive Black Swans are more unpredictable than negative ones
- **Longer-term predictions are more reliable** than short-term ones (apparent paradox): the probability of the fragile being swallowed by history increases with time

> *"Time has sharp teeth that destroy everything,"* declared Simonides of Ceos.

### Technology Constancy

- Close your eyes and imagine the future -- your imagination will **add things**; the correct approach is to **subtract**, since what is fragile will break
- Tonight's dinner at a **restaurant** (25+ centuries old) uses **silverware** (Mesopotamian), **wine** (6 millennia), **glasses** (29 centuries), food prepared with **fire** and implements unchanged since the Romans, sitting on a **chair** (3,000+ years old)
- **David Edgerton** showed we produce 2.5x as many **bicycles** as cars and invest most technological resources in **maintaining existing equipment**
- The **prime error** in imagining the future: taking the present as baseline and **adding** speculative technologies; we **over-technologize** the future

### The Blindness of Technothinkers

- Technothinkers have **uncompromising neomania** and a profound **lack of elegance** -- their absence of literary culture is a **marker of future blindness**
- Outside science fiction, **literature is about the past** -- we still read Homer, Plato, Shakespeare, but not medieval physics textbooks
- By setting foot in a **museum**, one connects with the elders -- the past is a much better teacher about the future than the present
- Technology is at its best when it is **invisible** -- greatest benefit when it **displaces the deleterious** (bureaucracy, isolated nuclear family, car-designed suburbs, media dictatorship)
- We are now using technology to **reverse technology** -- the shoe industry sells shoes that replicate being **barefoot**; the **tablet** returns us to Babylonian roots of writing

### The Lindy Effect Defined

- Separate the **perishable** (humans, single items) from the **nonperishable** (informational)
- Building on the version developed by **Benoit Mandelbrot**:

> *For the perishable, every additional day translates into a shorter additional life expectancy. For the nonperishable, every additional day may imply a longer life expectancy.*

- A book in print for **forty years** can be expected to remain in print another forty; if it survives another decade, then another **fifty** years
- The **robustness** of an item is proportional to its life
- Physicist **Richard Gott** tested this with **Broadway shows** (May 1993) -- predicted longest-running shows would last longest, proven right with **95% accuracy**; as a child he correctly guessed the **Great Pyramid** would outlive the **Berlin Wall**

### Two Common Mistakes

- **Mistake 1: The counterexample fallacy** -- the argument is about **life expectancy** (a probabilistic average), not every single case
- **Mistake 2: The "young" technology fallacy** -- a technology, being informational, **does not age organically**; the wheel is not "old" in the sense of degeneracy
- How to teach children for the future: make them **read the classics** -- "The future is in the past"

> *He who does not have a past has no future.* -- Arabic proverb

### Mental Biases and Neomania

- Information hides failures -- we confuse the **necessary and the causal**: because surviving technologies have benefits, we think all technologies with benefits will survive
- We notice **change, not statics** -- we rely more on **water** than cell phones, but cell phones change so we think they matter more
- **Treadmill effects**: we acquire new items, feel a boost, then rapidly **revert to baseline** -- studied by **Danny Kahneman** as the psychology of **hedonic states**
- **Artisanal** items cause fewer treadmill effects -- they have **antifragility** (artisanal shoes take months to become comfortable); items with an **on-off switch** have no such redeeming quality

### Architecture and Urban Planning

- **Neomania in architecture** is particularly vicious because modernistic buildings are **not fragile enough to break physically** -- they stick around to torture consciousness
- Top-down urban planning is usually **irreversible**; bottom-up is **gradual and incremental** with a positive slope
- Natural growth has a **fractal quality** (Mandelbrot's insight) -- jaggedness and self-similarity; contemporary architecture is **smooth**, top-down, and feels dead
- **Gaudi's buildings** in Barcelona are inspired by nature -- rich, jagged details that lead to inner peace; yet his idea went nowhere except promoting unnatural modernism
- **Jane Jacobs** heroically resisted **Robert Moses** who wanted to raze neighborhoods for highways -- the **machine-organism dichotomy**: for Jacobs the city is an organism; for Moses a machine
- Most expensive neighborhoods in Paris today were the ones **left alone** by renovators
- **Stewart Brand** (*How Buildings Learn*) shows buildings keep incurring mutations as if needing to evolve

### Metrification and Traditional Measures

- The campaign for **metrification** was born from the **French Revolution** -- the old system has remained **refractory** in the US and England
- Natural measurements have **logic**: a **foot** corresponds to a foot; a **mile** is a thousand paces; a **furlong** is the distance one can sprint before running out of breath; a **pound** is what you can imagine holding

## Chapter 20: Turning Science into Journalism

### Time as a Filter for Information

- The best filtering heuristic: take into account the **age of books and scientific papers**
- Taleb follows the **Lindy effect**: books around for ten years will be around for ten more; books around for two millennia will persist much longer
- Academic work can be subjected to Lindy effects -- the hundreds of thousands of papers that are just noise

### Scientific Breakthroughs vs. Noise

- Time can act as a cleanser of noise -- the work of **Judah Folkman** (cure cancer by choking blood supply via **neovascularization**) looked impeccable on paper but produced results only in **macular degeneration**, not cancer
- If we should have a prize, it should be for **"over a hundred"** -- it took 140 years to validate **Jules Regnault** who discovered optionality and mapped it mathematically
- The **textbook test**: open any elementary textbook to a random chapter -- odds are it is still relevant; try conference proceedings from five years ago -- it will feel like a stale newspaper

> "As little as feasible from the last twenty years, except history books that are not about the last fifty years."

### What Should Break

- For *The Economist* (2010), Taleb predicted the future would contain **bookshelves, telephones, artisans** -- the fragile should disappear: large corporations, nation-states, economics departments
- **City-states and small corporations** are more likely to thrive
- The classical role of the **prophet** is not to look into the future but to talk about the present -- in the Semitic tradition (*nby* = Nevi, nabi), principally someone connecting with God; the Greek *pro-phetes* meant "spokesman"
- Being a prophet has never been desirable -- **Jeremiah** was beaten, persecuted, stoned; **Cassandra** got prophecy with the curse of not being believed; **Tiresias** was blinded for revealing gods' secrets
- The **lack of recursive thinking**: if messengers have always been persecuted, one would expect intelligent people to learn -- but **nothing of the sort takes place**
- **Empedocles' dog** always sleeps on the same tile -- a natural match confirmed by long frequentation; human technologies that survive are like the tile to the dog, corresponding to something deep in our nature

## Chapter 21: Medicine, Convexity, and Opacity

### Via Negativa Decision Rule

- Only resort to medical techniques when the health payoff is **very large** and visibly exceeds potential harm -- same logic as government intervention, squarely **Thalesian, not Aristotelian** (decision making based on payoffs, not knowledge)
- The non-natural needs to prove its benefits, not the natural -- according to the principle that nature is less of a sucker than humans, **only time -- a long time -- is evidence**
- The **"do you have evidence" fallacy**: mistaking evidence of no harm for no evidence of harm -- the same as mistaking **absence of evidence for evidence of absence**

### The Emergency Room and Burden of Evidence

- After breaking his nose, Taleb challenged the doctor on applying **ice** -- swelling is the body's response to injury, and the burden of evidence falls on **us humans** to show we can do better than Mother Nature
- Confirmed via computer: **no compelling empirical evidence** for reduction of swelling outside rare threatening cases
- **Meehl and Dawes** pioneered cataloging how many things believed true by professionals don't match empirical evidence

### First Principle of Iatrogenics (Empiricism)

- We do not need **evidence of harm** to claim a drug is dangerous -- harm is in the future (the **turkey problem**)
- **Trans fat**: assumed healthier than lard, now **widely banned** as the cause of heart disease
- **Thalidomide** and **Diethylstilbestrol**: benefits appeared obvious and immediate but small; harm was delayed by decades

### Second Principle of Iatrogenics (Nonlinearity in Response)

- Iatrogenics is **not linear** -- take a lot more risks with the severely ill, not with near-healthy people
- **Hypertension**: when mild, only **5.6%** benefit from treatment (1 in 18); when severe, **72%** benefit -- treatment benefits are **convex to condition**
- Of 120,000 drugs available, one can hardly find a *via positiva* one that makes a healthy person unconditionally better
- The more ill the person, the less likely nature would have found the solution -- a condition three standard deviations from the norm is **300x rarer**; five deviations is **a million times rarer**
- **Pharmaceutical companies** scrape the bottom of the barrel, reclassifying conditions (normal blood pressure becomes **"pre-hypertensive"**) to drive more medication
- The iatrogenics is in the patient, not the treatment: if near death, **no holds barred**; if near healthy, **Mother Nature should be the doctor**

### Jensen's Inequality in Medicine

- The **volatility of an exposure** can matter more than its average -- the **"convexity bias"**
- People with **acute respiratory distress syndrome** were put on mechanical ventilators with constant pressure -- but reaction to pressure is **nonlinear**
- **J. F. Brewster** et al. found that dispensing higher pressure on occasion and low pressure at other times **decreased patient mortality** -- occasional spikes open collapsed **alveoli**
- Humans are **antifragile to lung pressure** -- this arises directly from the nonlinearity of the response

### Burying the Evidence and Radiation

- Medicine's successes were prominently displayed; its mistakes literally **buried** -- in the cemetery of history
- In the 1940s-50s, children received **radiation** for acne, tonsillitis, ringworm -- about **7 percent** developed thyroid cancer two to four decades later
- We are necessarily **antifragile to some dose of radiation** at naturally found levels; small doses may prevent injuries from larger ones (the body develops immunity)
- **Statins**: meant to lower cholesterol, but one needs to treat **fifty high risk persons for five years** to avoid a single cardiovascular event; the metric "cholesterol" may not equate to health -- it is not certain whether these indicators are **causes or manifestations** that correlate to a condition
- **Metric-lowering drugs** are vicious because of legal complexity: the doctor has incentive to prescribe (or be sued for negligence), but side effects don't appear as caused by medicine

### Verified and Potential Iatrogenics

- **Vioxx** (delayed heart problems), **antidepressants** (overprescribed), **bariatric surgery** (vs. starvation), **cortisone**, **disinfectants** (autoimmune diseases), **hormone replacement therapy**, excessive **cesarean births**, **lobotomies**, **whitening of rice/wheat**, **hygiene beyond a certain point** (denying hormesis), **antibiotics** (transferring antifragility from body to germ)
- Surgery had a better track record than medicine because results were **visible** -- surgeons were **blue-collar workers**, closer to artisans; in England and France, surgeons' guilds merged with **barbers'**
- **Back surgery** for sciatica: often useless -- evidence shows six years later it is on average equivalent to doing nothing; yet **spinal cord fusion** is still practiced liberally as it is lucrative for the doctor

## Chapter 22: To Live Long, but Not Too Long

### Life Expectancy and Convexity

- Life expectancy increased due to **sanitation, penicillin, drop in crime, life-saving surgery** -- not routine medical treatment
- **Hospital strikes** reveal that when only urgent cases are treated, life expectancy either increases or does not drop
- Conditional life expectancy data: in the last 50 years, only **four months** added to expected life span of a person already 60

### Subtraction Adds to Your Life

- The via negativa approach: what you **don't do** matters more than what you do
- Remove painkillers, vitamin pills, food supplements, the family doctor; avoid fruits without ancient Greek or Hebrew names
- **Personal rule on liquids**: drink nothing that isn't at least a thousand years old -- wine, water, coffee; orange juice is the most deceitfully noxious "healthy" drink
- **Oliver Wendell Holmes Sr.**: "if all the medications were dumped in the sea, it would be better for mankind but worse for the fishes"

### The Iatrogenics of Money

- Construction laborers with ham and cheese baguettes seem **happier** than billionaires at Michelin restaurants -- food tastes better after exertion
- Romans understood that anything that "softens" or "mollifies" is negative -- they saw comfort's side effects
- **Desert vs. city culture**: the desert is full of poetry, contemplation, frugality; the city breeds gossip, moral decay, decadence -- Christ fasted forty days in the desert; Saint Simeon spent forty years on a column in Northern Syria
- Medical iatrogenics results from **wealth and sophistication** -- money has its own iatrogenics
- **Treadmill effect**: you need more and more to stay in the same place; greed is antifragile though not its victims; **we have never been richer in history, and never more in debt** (for the ancients, debt was bondage)
- **Tantalus class**: permanently tantalized, socialized into a milieu -- you move to Greenwich, Connecticut, then become a pauper next to twenty-million-dollar mansions
- Romans circumvented treadmill effects through **patron-client relationships** -- private charity, no welfare, no church; provincial landowners maintained open tables

### Religion, Fasting, and Convexity Effects

- Religion **protects us from scientism** -- going to church for mild cases helps by limiting intervention
- Taleb follows the **Greek Orthodox calendar** with nearly **200 days of fasting per year** -- the Paleo vs. vegan debate misses that episodic variation is the key
- **Jensen's inequality** applied to nutrition: humans are **omnivorous** in response to variegated environments with unplanned availability of sources
- When we are herbivores we eat steadily; when predators we eat randomly -- **proteins need to be consumed randomly**; there is a big difference between getting all nutrients together at every meal versus **serially**
- The **Cretan diet fallacy**: researchers looked at Cretan longevity, cataloged what they ate, then naively inferred the food types caused longevity -- missing the **second-order effect** of Greek Orthodox fasting and its massive variations in intake

> Breaking a fast feels like the exact opposite of a hangover.

### Autophagy and Intermittent Deprivation

- **Biological mechanisms are activated by food deprivation** -- researchers rationalize with **autophagy** (eating oneself): cells break down proteins and recombine amino acids when deprived
- **Valter Longo** found mice in initial starvation phases can withstand high doses of chemotherapy without visible side effects
- The idea of **breakfast as a main meal** has been progressively shown to be harmful -- no benefits unless one has worked for it; we are **not designed to receive food from the delivery person**
- Walking may be as necessary as **sleep** -- all ancestors until the automobile spent much time walking

### I Want to Live Forever

- For the ancients, the worst outcome was not death but **a dishonorable death**
- The modern illusion: we should live as long as we can, as if each individual were the end product -- before, we were part of the **collective and future progeny**
- The **gene is antifragile** (information), but the carrier is fragile -- Nietzsche's *aut liberi, aut libri* (either children or books)
- Taleb's **anti-me**: **Ray Kurzweil**, who pops close to 200 pills daily; while Taleb proposes removing offensive elements, Kurzweil works by **adding**

> Then say goodbye, have a nice funeral, and, as the French say, *place aux autres* -- make room for others.

## Chapter 23: Skin in the Game: Antifragility and Optionality at the Expense of Others

### The Agency Problem and Disappearance of Heroism

- The worst problem of modernity: **malignant transfer of fragility and antifragility** from one party to the other -- one gets benefits, the other gets harm
- The main difference between older surviving societies and ours: the **disappearance of heroism** -- a shift away from those who take downside risks for others
- **Heroism** is the exact inverse of the agency problem: accepting deprivation for the sake of others
- Power now goes to **bankers, corporate executives, and politicians** who steal a free option from society

### Courage and Its Evolution

- The **robustness -- even antifragility -- of society** depends on heroes; if we are here today, it is because someone took risks for us
- In traditional societies, a person is only as respectable as the **downside he or she is willing to face** for others -- knights, generals, commanders, even mafia dons accept that rank makes them most exposed
- Heroism evolved: from Homeric **physical courage**, to Lacedaemonian **renunciation and sacrifice**, to the **Socratic Plato** form -- courage to stand up for an idea and enjoy death in thrill
- No one has had more prestige than **two thinkers who sacrificed their lives for their ideas** -- one Greek, one Semite
- We should pause when we hear **happiness** defined as an economic condition -- Taleb is distraught by heroism-free **"middle class values"**: enshrining "hard work" for a bank, diligent newspaper reading, obedience, reliance on stock market investments, suburban life
- A **half-man** is not someone without opinions, just someone who does not take risks for them
- Historian **Paul Veyne** showed gladiators were mostly **volunteers** who wanted the chance to become heroes by risking their lives
- Taleb's greatest lesson in courage from his **father**: at a Lebanese war roadblock, he refused to comply with a militiaman; shot in the back as he drove away, the bullet stayed in his chest for life
- If you take risks and face your fate with dignity, nothing can make you small; if you don't, nothing can make you grand -- the notion of **megalopsychon** (Aristotle), or *Shhm* in Arabic: **nonsmall**

### Hammurabi's Rule

> If a builder builds a house and the house collapses and causes the death of the owner -- the builder shall be put to death.

- They were **much more advanced 3,800 years ago** -- the builder knows more about hidden risks than any inspector
- The foundation with **delayed collapse** is the best place to hide risk -- the object is not to punish retrospectively but to **provide up-front disincentive**
- These asymmetries are most severe with **small-probability extreme events** (Black Swans) -- the most misunderstood and easiest to hide

### Fat Tony's Heuristics

- **First**: never get on a plane if the pilot is not on board (addresses transfer of fragility)
- **Second**: make sure there is also a copilot (redundancy)
- **Ralph Nader's rule**: people voting for war need at least one descendant exposed to combat
- For the **Romans**, engineers spent time under the bridge they built -- every opinion maker needs skin in the game
- **Forecasts induce risk taking** -- more toxic than any other form of human pollution

### The Talker's Free Option

- Society grants **mere talkers a free option** -- we need entrepreneurs and risk takers at the top of the pyramid, and talkers at the bottom
- Fat Tony had **something at risk** and would have been harmed by an adverse outcome -- no agency problem; this makes his profiting permissible
- It is profoundly **unethical to talk without doing**, without exposure to harm -- transferring fragility is much more acute now under modernity's connectivity and the newfound invisibility of causal chains

> If you see fraud and don't say fraud, you are a fraud.

- **Thomas Friedman** helped cause the Iraq war through influential op-eds, paying no price; his "earth is flat" globalization idea ignores that globalization brings **fragilities and extreme events**
- Natural systems work by **penalties** -- but there is **no penalty for opinion makers** who harm society; the Obama administration after 2008 was populated with people who drove the bus blindfolded

### Privilege, Obligation, and Skin in the Game

- In the old days, **privilege came with obligations** -- you want to be a feudal lord, first to die; Caesar, Alexander, Hannibal were on the battlefield; **Napoleon** showing up during battle was equivalent to adding 25,000 troops
- **Raymond Aron** was right about Communism but remained colorless; his enemy **Sartre** got everything wrong but looked radiant -- the coward's books survived
- **Champagne socialism**: never listen to a leftist who does not give away his fortune -- **Francois Mitterrand** emulated monarchical pomp on a socialist platform while conservative **de Gaulle** led a life of old-style austerity
- **Ralph Nader**: aside from astonishing courage, exhibits absolutely **no divorce between what he preaches and his lifestyle** -- a secular saint

### Soul in the Game and Doxastic Commitment

- Beyond skin in the game: those who have their **soul in the game** -- a prophet is not someone who first had an idea, but the one to first **believe in it** and take it to its conclusion
- **Doxastic commitment** (from Greek *doxa*, belief): a costly commitment beyond words -- the originator of a theory is someone who believed in a **doxastic way**, not the first to mention it over dessert wine

### The Ancients, the Stiglitz Syndrome, and Decimation

- The ancients understood the **Stiglitz syndrome** with sophisticated mechanisms -- Romans forced engineers to **spend time under the bridge they built**
- Roman soldiers signed a **sacramentum** accepting punishment in the event of failure
- **Decimation**: if a legion lost a battle with suspicion of cowardice, **10 percent** were put to death by random lottery -- the magic number: putting more than 10% to death would weaken the army; too little and cowardice dominates
- On burning one's vessels: Arab commander **Tarek** crossed Gibraltar in 711, burned his ships, then told his small army: *"Behind you is the sea, before you, the enemy. All you have is sword and courage"* -- he conquered Spain; same heuristic used by **Cortes in Mexico**

### The Problem of Insulation

- **Myles Burnyeat** on skeptics who are skeptical in one domain but not another -- a philosopher puzzles about the reality of time but applies for next year's sabbatical grant
- **Harry Markowitz** does not use his own portfolio theory for his own portfolio -- he uses simpler cabdriver methodologies closer to Mandelbrot
- Heuristic: does the researcher whose ideas apply to the real world **apply them to his daily life?** If so, take him seriously; otherwise, ignore him
- The greatest Arabic poet **Almutanabbi** died for his poetry -- when fleeing enemies, his companion recited his own boastful verse back at him; he turned around to certain death rather than face **dishonor of running away**

### Karl Popper's Missed Point

- Popper was under the illusion that ideas compete with each other -- he missed that it is **people who have the right ideas, or societies with the correct heuristics**, that survive
- He missed the **Thalesian effect**: a wrong idea that is harmless can survive; those with wrong heuristics but small harm in the event of error will survive
- **"Irrational" behavior can be good if it is harmless** -- which is more dangerous: mistaking a bear for a stone, or a stone for a bear? Those who overreact had a **survival advantage**

### Options, Antifragility, and Social Fairness

- The stock market: the greatest **transfer of antifragility** in history -- managers have **incentives without disincentives**
- Two scenarios where the market averages the same but follows different paths: the more volatile path is **more profitable to managers** (they cash stock options); society has the **exact opposite payoff**
- **Robert Rubin** earned $120 million from Citibank in bonuses; when it collapsed, he kept his money -- taxpayers compensated him; **clawback provisions** don't solve the problem because managers still have no net downside
- Banks have **lost more than they ever made** in their history, with managers paid billions

### Hammurabi's Rule and Historical Punishments

- Bankers used to be subjected to Hammurabi's rule -- the tradition in **Catalonia** was to behead bankers in front of their own banks (the fate of **Francesco Castello**, 1360)
- In modern times, only the **mafia** executes these strategies: "Vatican banker" **Roberto Calvi** of Banco Ambrosiano was killed by the mafia for losing their money; **Bugsy Siegel** met the same fate for running an unprofitable casino
- In some countries like **Brazil**, top bankers are made unconditionally liable to the extent of their own assets

### Which Adam Smith?

- Many right-wingers who cite **Adam Smith** as patron saint of capitalism (a word he never uttered) do so without reading him
- In *The Wealth of Nations* Book IV, Smith was wary of giving someone **upside without downside** and had doubts about limited liability:

> The directors of such companies, being the managers rather of other people's money than of their own, it cannot well be expected that they should watch over it with the same anxious vigilance.

- The version of capitalism you need is with the **minimum number of people in the left column of the Triad** -- the Soviet system's central problem was putting everyone in charge of economic life in that fragilizing left column

### The Ethics of Corporations

- While **corporations sell junk drinks, artisans sell cheese and wine** -- there is a transfer of antifragility from small to large
- Companies like **Coke or Pepsi** sell sugary water that messes up biological signaling, *causing* diabetes
- A corporation **does not feel shame**, does not have honor or generosity -- only self-serving actions are acceptable; all defects result from **absence of skin in the game**
- **Anything that requires marketing** carries side effects -- word of mouth is the only naturalistic filter
- The mechanism of **cheapest-to-deliver-for-a-given-specification** pervades corporate products

## Appendix I: Technical Companion

### The Conflation of Events and Exposure to Events

- **f(x)** is *exposure* to variable **x** -- f(x) can be made more predictable than x:
  - **Earthquake**: x = intensity; f(x) = deaths -- controllable through building standards
  - **Falling**: x = height pushed from; f(x) = physical condition -- you can manage the effect
  - **Traffic**: x = number of cars; f(x) = travel time -- take the subway, or walk
- Some people talk about f(x) thinking they are talking about x -- the **conflation of event and exposure**
- One can become **antifragile to x without understanding x**, through convexity of f(x)
- The answer to "what do you do in a world you don't understand?" is: **work on the undesirable states of f(x)**
- **Robustification rather than forecasting Black Swans**

### Convex Transformation (the Barbell)

- Modify f(x) to make knowledge of x on the left side irrelevant -- this operation is the **convex transformation**, nicknamed **"barbell"**
- **Green lumber fallacy** (technical): confusing f(x) for another function g(x) with different nonlinearities

### The Fourth Quadrant

- **Tail events are not computable** in fat-tailed domains, but we can assess exposure
- The **Fourth Quadrant** danger zone: fat-tailed distributions + concave-convex exposure functions that exacerbate remote outcomes
- **Nothing is open-ended in nature** -- things end up convex on one end, concave on the other; boundedness of harm brings convexities somewhere

### Medical Nonlinearities

- **Iatrogenics** occurs with small identifiable gains and exposure to **Black Swans with delayed invisible large side effects** -- like selling a financial option against tiny immediate gains
- The dose-response pattern: at **low levels** convex (increasingly effective), **additional doses** become concave (ineffective or harmful) -- applies to anything bounded on both sides, including happiness
- **Kahneman-Tversky prospect theory** models a similar shape empirically
- **Hormesis**: initial benefits with increasing dose, then harm -- a bounded worst-case scenario in biology

### The Inverse Turkey Problem

- The unseen rare event is **positive** -- when looking at a positively skewed (antifragile) time series, you miss the good stuff and **underestimate the benefits**

## Appendix II: Where Most Economic Models Fragilize and Blow People Up

### The Markowitz Incoherence

- If someone says probability is "estimated" to be zero, there is a problem -- something estimated needs an **estimation error**, so probability cannot be zero
- **Markowitz** started his paper with "Assume you know E and V" -- then accepted they need to be estimated; if parameters need estimation with error, the **derivations should be written differently**
- **Economic models are extremely fragile to assumptions** -- slight alterations lead to extremely consequential differences

### Government Deficits and Convexity Bias

- A government estimates unemployment at 9% and forecasts a 200 billion deficit -- but unemployment is **stochastic** (fluctuates ~1%)
- At 8%, deficit = -75bn; at 10%, deficit = -550bn -- the average is **-312bn, not -200bn**
- The **concavity bias** of -112.5bn is the **inverse philosopher's stone**: variability systematically worsens outcomes under concavity

### Ricardian Comparative Advantage

- **David Ricardo's** model assumes prices and costs remain **constant** -- but if the price of wine drops 90%, the specialized country faces disaster
- The **Irish potato famine** (1850): **monoculture** proved one of the most lethal strategies -- one bad crop killed a million and displaced another million
- Taleb is not against specialization but says it should emerge **organically through tinkering**, not imposed by bureaucrats using models
- **Probability matching** (betting proportional to probabilities rather than all-in on the favorite) is considered an "error" by academics but is actually sound when probabilities are **unstable** -- similar to redundancy as a buffer

### How Fat Tails Come from Parameter Uncertainty

- The more **parameter uncertainty** in a model, the more **small probabilities tend to be underestimated** -- small probabilities are **convex** to errors of computation
- A "**three sigma**" event: probability rises by **60%** with just 5% error in standard deviation
- A "**six sigma**" event: rises **five times more**; a "**ten sigma**" event: more than **a billion times**
- The smaller the probability, the more precision needed -- the slightest uncertainty causes **mayhem**
- This explains **Fukushima** (held to happen once per million years, actually once per ~30 with proper uncertainty) and **Fannie Mae**
- **Fat tails mean incomputability of tail events**, little else

### Portfolio Fallacies

- Markowitz users claim diversification is "better than nothing" -- **wrong**: it pushes people to **optimize and overallocate**, making them vulnerable to **model error and tail events**
- The lower the perceived correlation, the worse the exposure; if correlation thought to be -1, **maximally overallocated**
- With leverage, this becomes **Long-Term Capital Management** (LTCM)
- **Kelly's method** requires no joint distribution or utility function -- in practice just the ratio of expected profit to worst-case return, dynamically adjusted to **avoid ruin**; compatible with bounded trial and error

### How Fat Tails Emerge from Leverage and Feedback Loops

- If agents buy in response to wealth increase and sell to maintain leverage (**concave exposure**), and securities respond nonlinearly to purchases/sales, then **CLT no longer holds** -- fat tails are an immediate result of feedback and leverage
- Forced selling is exactly like hedging a **short option** -- negative skewness
- Multiple agents compound the effect through **contagion**
