## Chapter 1: What Is Economics?

### Definition
- **Economics** = the study of the use of scarce resources which have
  alternative uses. (Lionel Robbins' classic definition)
- **Scarcity**: what everybody wants adds up to more than there is.
  Even middle-class Americans in a swimming pool complain they're
  "just getting by" — a Harvard sociologist called them
  "budget-constrained." But it's not the budget that constrains them:
  reality constrains them. Scarcity is universal.
- Garden of Eden was NOT an economy — everything available in
  unlimited abundance. Without scarcity, no need to economize.
- The NY Times ran a feature on middle-class Americans "just getting by"
  with the photo showing a family in their own swimming pool. The
  irony illustrates that scarcity is relative and universal.

### Productivity
- Economics studies how decisions about land, labor, capital, and other
  resources determine a country's standard of living.
- Poor countries can have rich natural resources: Venezuela and Uruguay
  have several times the natural resources per capita of Japan and
  Switzerland — yet Japan/Switzerland have more than double the
  real income per capita.
- Soviet industries used more electricity than American industries
  but produced less output. Resource abundance does not
  automatically create prosperity — efficiency does.
- From petroleum comes not just gasoline but plastics, asphalt, Vaseline.
  From iron ore: paper clips to automobiles to skyscraper frames. How
  much to allocate to each use? That's what economics is about.

### The Role of Economics
- NOT about making money, running a business, or predicting stocks.
- Economics studies cause and effect: what happens when you do
  specific things in specific ways. Methods used by Marxist economist
  Oskar Lange did not fundamentally differ from conservative Milton
  Friedman — it's the principles, not the politics.
- Key lens: **incentives** created by decisions, not just goals pursued.
  "Consequences matter more than intentions — and not just
  immediate consequences, but longer run repercussions."
- Good intentions + bad economics = disaster. Many economic
  catastrophes resulted from well-intentioned policies.
- 1930s Great Depression: probably caused more deaths worldwide than
  Hiroshima and Nagasaki combined — from bad economic policy.
- India and China in late 20th century made fundamental economic
  policy changes → India: 20 million rose out of destitution in a decade.
  China: people living on $1/day fell from 374 million (1990) to 128
  million (2004) — nearly a quarter billion lifted out of poverty.
- Military medical team on battlefield: classic economic problem —
  allocating scarce resources (doctors, medications) with alternative
  uses. Not a dime changes hands. Still pure economics.

## PART I: PRICES AND MARKETS

## Chapter 2: The Role of Prices

### How Economies Allocate Resources
- Feudal economy: lord of the manor gave orders. Soviet Union: central
  planners issued orders for steel, wheat, dams — same principle.
- Market economy: no one at top controls. How does it work?
- Mikhail Gorbachev reportedly asked Margaret Thatcher: "How do you
  see to it that people get food?" Answer: "I don't. Prices did that."
  And the British were better fed than Soviets, despite not producing
  enough food to feed themselves for a century — prices bring food
  from other countries.
- Without prices, imagine the army of bureaucrats needed just to supply
  London with food each day. Prices do the same job faster, cheaper,
  better — freeing those would-be bureaucrats for productive work.
- China with 1/5 of world's population and only 10% of arable land now
  feeds itself through prices attracting food from abroad. KFC by early
  21st century was making more sales in China than in the U.S. One-
  quarter of Chinese adults were overweight — remarkable for a country
  that once had recurring famines.

### How Prices Convey Information
- Prices convey information throughout the whole economic system —
  across the world — without any planning commission issuing orders.
- If someone in Fiji makes better shoes at lower cost, those shoes appear
  in U.S. stores without Washington officials even knowing.
- After WWII, Americans bought Japanese cameras without knowing
  Japan made cameras. A modern economy has millions of products —
  too many for any leader to know.
- Prices coordinate all of this: who gets what resources, in what amounts,
  directed to their highest-valued uses.

### Prices and Decision-Making
- Prices convey not just INFORMATION but INCENTIVES to act.
- When a crop fails, "greedy" food suppliers rush food to the hungry
  region at maximum speed — probably faster than salaried government
  employees on a humanitarian mission. "Greedy" suppliers may drive
  through the night; humanitarians proceed at comfortable pace.
- Rising price of oil: signals both to cut consumption AND to seek
  alternatives AND to explore for more oil. One price change
  coordinates millions of independent decisions.
- Each price change forces everyone who uses that resource to
  reconsider alternatives, including those who never heard of whatever
  caused the price change.

### Supply, Demand, and "Real" Value
- There is NO objective "real" value. Water vs. diamonds: both buyer and
  seller only transact because each values what they get MORE than
  what they give up. Transactions are positive-sum — not zero-sum.
- If grapes are in surplus, wine prices fall, farmers shift to other crops,
  eventually prices recover. If new crop is popular, production expands
  until profits are competed away.
- Prices not only ration existing supplies but create incentives to
  expand supplies. Higher prices attract more resources to production;
  lower prices cause resources to shift elsewhere.
- "Real value" as a concept leads to fallacies. What IS the "real value"
  of a house after a major employer leaves town? The price IS the value
  under current conditions.

### Systemic vs. Intentional Causation
- Systemic causation = reciprocal interactions produce results no one
  intended. As Friedrich Engels said: "what each individual wills is
  obstructed by everyone else, and what emerges is something that no
  one willed."
- Stock market at 14,367 on a given day: result of millions of
  interactions, no one intended that number.
- Primitive peoples attributed wind to spirits. Modern people attribute
  price rises to "greed." Both explain systemic results by personal
  intentions — both are wrong.
- Higher prices in poor neighborhoods: not greed. Systemic explanation:
  higher insurance costs, higher security costs due to crime, higher per-
  dollar cost of doing business. Lending $100 each to 50 people costs
  more than lending $5,000 to one person. Armored car to a ghetto
  check-cashing store costs as much as one to a suburban bank.
  About 10% of Americans lack checking accounts — higher in low-
  income areas.

### Competition
- Competition is the crucial factor explaining why prices cannot be held
  at arbitrary levels for long.
- Like water seeking its own level — but with waves and tides. Prices
  fluctuate as they seek equilibrium; they are never frozen.
- Competition forces prices toward equality and causes capital, labor,
  and resources to flow toward highest returns — until returns equalize
  through competition.

## Chapter 3: Price Controls

### What Price Controls Do
- Price controls create a mismatch between quantity supplied and
  quantity demanded — always.
- Ceiling below market price → shortage. Floor above market price →
  surplus.
- Soviet Union tried to maintain price controls on bread. Result: bread
  available but meat disappeared from stores.
- When prices cannot rise to ration limited supplies, non-price
  rationing emerges: waiting in lines, personal connections,
  bureaucratic allocation.

### Rent Control
- New York rent control: classic example. Reduced supply of available
  apartments. Landlords convert to condos, let buildings deteriorate,
  reduce maintenance to save money.
- Stockholm rent control: apartments became hard to find, with waiting
  lists of years. Those lucky enough to have rent-controlled apartments
  hung onto them even when circumstances changed.
- New York Congressman Charles Rangel had FOUR rent-controlled
  apartments, one used as an office. Mayor Ed Koch kept his during
  his 12 years in Gracie Mansion. Hollywood stars kept Manhattan
  rent-controlled apartments for occasional visits.
- Under rent control, vacancy rates in New York fell dramatically.
  Same policy in Cairo, Hong Kong, Stockholm, Melbourne all led to
  similar consequences — same economic principles apply everywhere.

### Quality Deterioration
- When price controls prevent higher prices, quality decreases instead.
- Consumer Reports 1943: 19 out of 20 candy bars were smaller than
  4 years earlier. Canned food quality deteriorated.
- Under Soviet controls: a third of all cars came off assembly lines
  with parts missing.
- In housing: maintenance falls, buildings deteriorate.

### Hoarding
- Price controls below market price → hoarding of the price-controlled
  good. During 1970s gasoline controls: motorists drove to filling
  stations with half-full tanks to top off, rather than waiting until
  nearly empty.
- With millions of motorists keeping tanks fuller than normal, vast
  amounts of gasoline disappeared into individual inventories —
  leaving less at filling stations. A small national shortage became a
  very severe local shortage. Conspiracy theories about oil tankers
  circling offshore emerged — when in fact the "missing" gasoline was
  in everyone's own tank.
- Hoarding varies with perishability: strawberries can't be hoarded;
  haircuts definitely can't. Apartments can be hoarded indefinitely
  (keeping unused apartments as Mayor Koch did).

### Black Markets
- Price controls almost invariably produce black markets where prices
  exceed even free-market levels (risk premium added).
- In Russia, a local food embargo was called the "150-ruble decree" —
  the cost of bribing police at checkpoints.
- In Soviet Russia, even when black market food trading was
  punishable by death, black markets still existed. Soviet economists:
  "speculators and food smugglers at the risk of their lives brought as
  much grain into the cities as all the state purchases."
- During WWII U.S. price controls: employment in meat-packing plants
  declined as meat was diverted to black markets — empty counters
  in butcher shops.

## Chapter 4: An Overview of Prices

### Prices as Knowledge
- Prices represent the distilled judgment of millions of people about
  the relative scarcities and values of goods and services.
- Friedrich Hayek's insight: the knowledge needed to coordinate a
  modern economy is dispersed among millions of individuals and
  cannot be gathered into any central authority. Prices collect and
  transmit this dispersed knowledge automatically.

### Price Coordination vs. Central Planning
- Systemic analysis shows why central planning cannot replicate prices:
  1. Knowledge problem: no one can gather all relevant information
  2. Incentive problem: no one has same incentives to use info efficiently
  3. Speed problem: prices respond instantly; bureaucracies don't
- Soviet Union: used more electricity per unit of output than the U.S.
  Not because of bad intentions but because prices did not convey
  accurate costs of electricity to producers.

### Prices and Social Benefits
- Producer who best matches consumer desires grows rich —
  not necessarily wiser than competitors, just more responsive.
- Society benefits: scarce resources directed toward what millions
  of people actually want, not what planners think they should want.
- Entrepreneurial profits are signals — they attract resources to
  where demand is highest. Losses are equally important signals —
  they direct resources AWAY from less-wanted production.

## PART II: INDUSTRY AND COMMERCE

## Chapter 5: The Rise and Fall of Businesses

### Business Failure is Normal and Necessary
- About 1/3 of new businesses fail within 2 years; more than half fail
  within 4 years. Only 35% of new companies survive 10 years.
- This is not a problem — it's how markets work. Failed businesses
  free up resources for more productive uses.
- A & P grocery chain: once 15,000 stores, largest retailer in the world
  in 1929. Now virtually unknown — shrunk to a fraction of its size.
- Fortune 500 list: 26 companies dropped off between 2010-2011
  alone, including Radio Shack and Levi Strauss.
- Financial centers have shifted: 1780s-1830s, U.S. financial center
  was Chestnut Street, Philadelphia. Then Wall Street. Then Wall Street
  replaced the City of London as world financial center.

### Profits and Losses as Signals
- Sun Microsystems' profit in early 2007 was "its first since mid-2005."
- Japanese CD player makers "thrived" in late 1980s, then within a
  few years "only offered manufacturers razor-thin margins."
- U.S. Steel: founded 1901 as world's largest steel producer, built
  the Panama Canal and Empire State Building steel. By 2011: ranked
  13th, lost $53 million that year and $124 million the next.
- High profits attract investment → more supply → prices fall →
  profits shrink → sometimes losses → some firms exit → supply falls
  → equilibrium restored.

### The Personal Factor
- Lenin declared that running businesses required only "the four rules
  of arithmetic." After a few years in power, he admitted economic
  ruin, starvation, and peasant uprisings were common. He then
  sought "people versed in the art of administration" from the old
  capitalist class — the very people he had overthrown.
- Study of Danish CEOs: death in the family led on average to 9%
  decline in corporate profitability. If it was a child, 21% decline.
  Death of only child: steepest decline. Corporations are personal.
- McDonald's first really successful franchisees: working-class couple
  who drained their life savings, had trouble coming up with $100 for
  the cash register on opening day. They ended up millionaires.
  Company-owned restaurants did not succeed as well as owner-
  operated ones — the personal financial stake mattered enormously.

### Hostile Takeovers as Discipline
- A poorly managed company is worth more to outside investors who
  can improve it than to its existing owners.
- Investors buy stock at $60 when it's trading at $50, replace
  management, stock rises to $100. Everyone gains.
- This process keeps even large corporations under pressure to perform.
  Not surprisingly, many corporate managers lobby state legislatures
  for laws impeding hostile takeovers.

## Chapter 6: The Role of Profits—and Losses

### What Profits Signal
- Profits are NOT just income for rich shareholders. They signal that
  resources are being used to create value consumers want.
- Losses signal the opposite — resources are being mis-allocated.
- Profits also fund investment for future production. A business that
  earns no profit cannot maintain or expand.

### Income "Distribution" Fallacies
- Income statistics often compare people at different life stages, not
  different social classes.
- Three-quarters of Americans in the bottom 20% of income in 1975
  were in the top 40% at some point in the next 16 years.
- European pattern same: half of Greeks below poverty line in a given
  year had risen above it within two years. Two-thirds of Dutch poor
  had risen within two years. Britain: nearly two-thirds of those in
  bottom 10% rose out within 5 years.
- New Zealand study: income inequality over a working lifetime was
  LESS than income inequality in any given year — because movement
  between brackets is so common.
- It is impossible to stay in the same age bracket for life, but people
  don't stay in the same income bracket for life either — yet statistics
  treat income brackets as permanent castes.

## Chapter 7: The Economics of Big Business

### Why Big Businesses Exist
- Economies of scale: producing a million of something costs less per
  unit than producing a thousand. Large up-front fixed costs spread
  over more units.
- Henry Ford's moving assembly line cut the price of a Model T from
  $850 (1908) to $290 (1925), making cars affordable to ordinary
  Americans for the first time.

### The Optimal Size Problem
- Restaurants are smaller than steel mills: successful restaurant
  operation requires constant on-site monitoring by someone with
  strong financial incentives (i.e., the owner).
- Tractor drivers in Soviet collective farms could exceed their "norms"
  by going faster but plowing shallower. Owner-operators would never
  do this — it's their own land they're damaging.
- Howard Johnson pioneered restaurant franchising in 1930s: he
  supplied half the capital, the local manager the other half — giving
  franchisees a financial stake in success, not just a salary.

### Costs and Capacity
- Cruise ships: must price to cover overhead even when sailing at
  partial capacity in off-season. That's why seniors get large off-
  season discounts — lower variable cost, same fixed cost.
- General principle: price discrimination by time, location, or customer
  type is often efficient, not predatory.

### Limited Liability and Corporations
- Limited liability: investors can lose only what they invest, not their
  entire personal wealth. This was a revolutionary innovation.
- Before limited liability, investing in businesses meant unlimited
  personal exposure. Few would invest in strangers' businesses.
- With limited liability, vast pools of capital from millions of investors
  can be organized. The cost of building railroads, steel plants, and
  power grids was only possible with corporate finance.
- Creditors know about limited liability ("Ltd." or "Inc." warns them)
  and can price risk accordingly.
- Separation of ownership and management: stockholders don't want
  to manage daily operations. They want returns. They delegate to
  professional managers monitored by boards of directors.
- Mutual funds oppose giving rank-and-file shareholders more control
  over executive compensation — not because they like excessive pay,
  but because they know most shareholders lack the expertise and
  incentives to monitor management effectively.

## Chapter 8: Regulation and Anti-Trust Laws

### The Deregulation Experience
- Airlines before deregulation: regulated fares, regulated routes,
  no fare competition. Deregulation → new airlines, lower fares,
  higher seat utilization, more choices.
- Trucking deregulation: fewer empty trucks driving around. More
  efficient delivery of goods. Prices fell.
- European airlines deregulated in 1997: Ryanair and other discount
  carriers forced British Airways, Air France, Lufthansa to cut fares.
- General pattern: regulation originally justified to prevent excessive
  prices, but over time evolved into protecting existing businesses
  from price competition. The original rationale inverted.

### Anti-Trust: Competition vs. Competitors
- A & P grocery chain: charged with anti-trust violations when it had
  MORE than four-fifths of American grocery sales handled by
  competitors. A & P was prosecuted for having LOW prices.
- The fundamental confusion: what is detrimental to COMPETITORS
  is not the same as what is detrimental to COMPETITION.
- Famous anti-trust cases have often involved companies charging
  LOWER prices than competitors. Competitors complained; government
  acted — at consumers' expense.
- Buying in carload lots uses fewer scarce resources than buying in
  small individual quantities. Chain stores that do this are more
  efficient — yet they have been prosecuted for "unfair" competition.

## Chapter 9: Market and Non-Market Economies

### Why Businesses Displaced Other Producers
- Market (business) production displaced feudal, communal, and
  government production over centuries because of cost advantages.
- Marx and Engels in The Communist Manifesto: "The cheap prices of
  its commodities are the heavy artillery with which it batters down
  all Chinese walls."
- Government enterprises typically operate as monopolies. Monopoly
  is the enemy of efficiency in any system — it's just that under
  socialism, monopoly is the NORM.

### FEMA vs. Private Insurance
- Hurricane/disaster response: FEMA notoriously slower and less
  efficient than private insurance companies.
- An insurance company that lags behind competitors in paying
  disaster claims loses customers AND word spreads, causing millions
  elsewhere to switch. Billions of dollars in business at stake.
- FEMA faces no such competitive pressure, regardless of how much
  it's criticized. It will survive and receive future appropriations.

### The Property Rights Foundation
- Without property rights: Soviet agricultural produce shipped carelessly;
  spoiled fruit left to contaminate other fruit in same boxes.
  American farmers were appalled — in the U.S. such carelessness
  would cause personal bankruptcy.
- In the USSR, food was owned "by the people" — meaning no one
  had incentive to safeguard it. Those handling food received fixed
  salaries regardless of whether food spoiled.
- "The only animals threatened with extinction are animals not owned
  by anybody. Colonel Sanders was not about to let chickens become
  extinct. Nor will McDonald's stand idly by and let cows go extinct."
- The commons: sheep grazing on unowned land left it overused and
  patchy while adjacent privately owned land was in far better
  condition. Same principle worldwide.
- Soviet forests: "forested areas that are cut down are not reseeded,"
  according to Soviet economists — financial suicide in a capitalist
  economy, but normal in a system without property rights.

## PART III: WORK AND PAY

## Chapter 10: Productivity and Pay

### What Determines Wages
- Wages determined by productivity — the value of what workers
  produce. This is not just theory but economic necessity.
- An employer who pays workers more than they produce will go
  bankrupt. One who pays less will lose them to competitors.
- Software engineers in Silicon Valley earn far more than software
  engineers in India — not because Americans are more deserving,
  but because American engineers can create more value in the
  American economic environment (with more capital, better
  infrastructure, more developed markets).

### Capital and Productivity
- Workers using more capital are more productive. A ditch digger
  with a backhoe can do in an hour what 100 workers with shovels
  take all day to do.
- Soviet railway workers vs. American: Soviet boxcars moved more
  freight per year (they worked around the clock with scarce capital).
  This showed NOT Soviet efficiency but their relative SCARCITY of
  capital — a sign of poverty, not efficiency.
- Rich countries junk equipment faster than poor countries — not
  waste, but because their workers' time has more productive
  alternative uses. Japan exports used cars to UAE, Dubai, Africa.
  Cameroon taxis: "beaten-up old Toyotas, carrying four in the back
  and three in the front." Dubai mechanics repair dented cars cheaply
  for export to poorer countries.

### Human Capital
- India: government controls were so pervasive that large businesses
  had to set up entire bureaucracies in Delhi just to navigate the
  permissions process. Relaxing controls in 1991 → economy growth
  rate increased dramatically, foreign investment rose from $150 million
  to $3 billion (20x increase).
- More schooling is not automatically more human capital. If schooling
  redirects people from productive activities to credentialism, it can
  reduce actual productivity.

## Chapter 11: Minimum Wage Laws

### The Basic Economics
- Minimum wage laws are price floors on labor. Economic principle:
  fewer units demanded at higher prices.
- Most affected: teenagers, low-skilled workers, minorities — exactly
  those the laws intend to help.

### Evidence and Experience
- United Mine Workers under John L. Lewis: successfully won very high
  wages for miners. Consequence: coal companies invested massively
  in machinery to replace miners. Mining employment collapsed.
  Mining communities devastated.
- Lewis knew this would happen. He prioritized keeping his remaining
  members employed at high wages over employment of more members
  at lower wages — rational for the union, not for the unemployed.
- By 1950, U.S. produced 3/4 of world's cars, Japan under 1%.
  Twenty years later, Japan produced almost 2/3 as many as U.S.
  By 1990, one-third of U.S. car sales were Japanese. The UAW's
  gains for its members may have cost 200,000 auto jobs.
- Detroit automakers laying off thousands while Toyota hired American
  workers by thousands in non-union transplant factories. Workers in
  these factories voted against unionization in secret ballot elections.

## Chapter 12: Special Problems in Labor Markets

### Unemployment Statistics
- Unemployment rate measures percentage of people IN the labor force
  who cannot find work. But some people drop OUT of the labor force.
- When many give up looking, the unemployment rate falls even though
  the number of jobless has not fallen.
- After the early 21st century downturn: unemployment peaked at 10%,
  then declined — but labor force participation rate fell to levels
  not seen in decades as millions stopped looking.
- From mid-2009 to early 2013: 3.7 million workers went on Social
  Security disability — "the fastest enrollment pace ever."
- Alternative measure: what percentage of working-age adults outside
  institutions are actually working. More accurate signal.

### Collective Bargaining
- Steelworkers: high wages and benefits → steel companies invested
  $35 billion in machinery replacing workers → 200,000+ jobs lost
  in a decade, steel towns devastated.
- "Give-backs" became common as unions faced going-out-of-business
  threats: GM, Ford, DaimlerChrysler, Goodyear, Verizon all saw unions
  agree to relinquish previous gains. NY Times: "Keeping their employers
  competitive, they have concluded, is essential to keeping unionized
  jobs from being lost to nonunion companies."

## PART IV: TIME AND RISK

## Chapter 13: Investment

### What Investment Really Means
- Investment: foregoing current consumption in hopes of larger future
  consumption. Individually measured in money; socially measured in
  real resources diverted from consumption to capital formation.
- Buying government bonds for an individual = investment. For the
  nation as a whole: no new real capital — just a transfer from future
  taxpayers to current bondholders. Fallacy of composition.
- Total financial institution investments worldwide in 2009: $60 trillion.
  American institutions owned 45%.

### Present Value
- Present value = anticipated future benefits, discounted for the delay.
- A 90-year-old planting fruit trees that won't mature for 20 years:
  rational, because the land's value rises immediately to reflect those
  expected future benefits. He can sell the land next month.
  "Part of the value of his wealth today consists of the value of food
  that has not yet been grown — and which will be eaten by children
  who have not yet been born."
- Politicians face no "present value" mechanism making them account
  for consequences beyond the next election.
- Standard & Poor's downgraded California bonds in 2001 even with
  no defaults and billions in surplus — because the electricity crisis
  commitments increased future risk. Present value responds to
  future expectations, not just current state.

## Chapter 14: Stocks, Bonds and Insurance

### Long-Run Returns
- Long-run: a dollar invested in stocks in 1801 was worth over $500,000
  in 1998 (real terms). Same dollar in bonds: ~$1,000. In gold: $0.78.
  "As good as gold" and "money in the bank" are misleading phrases
  for long-run planning.
- 1930s: stocks had real return of only 3.6% while bonds returned 6.4%.
  But in 1940s-70s: bonds had NEGATIVE real returns (inflation stole
  value) while stocks had positive returns. Relative risk of stocks vs.
  bonds varies enormously with time horizon.
- $100,000 in Vanguard S&P 500 index on Jan 1, 2000: worth $89,072
  in nominal terms by mid-December 2009 — or $69,114 in Jan 2000
  dollars. A decade of zero real returns.

### Risk
- Risk: probabilities can be calculated (actuarial tables).
- Uncertainty: outcomes not predictable with confidence.
- "Windfall gains" are viewed suspiciously — but they compensate for
  the risk of windfall losses. Cannot have one without the other.

## Chapter 15: Special Problems of Time and Risk

### Inescapability of Risk
- The most important thing about risk is its inescapability. Particular
  individuals can be sheltered — only by having someone else bear
  the risk. For society as a whole, there is no someone else.
- Most people today are employees with guaranteed pay — they've
  transferred their risks to others. This produces the illusion that
  risk has been eliminated.
- "Speculator" is not a term of endearment. "Windfall gains" are viewed
  suspiciously. But both are the flip side of windfall losses — which
  no one wants to discuss.

### Present Value and Resource Conservation
- Private owners protect forests because depletion reduces present
  value of their land. Government-owned forests lack this incentive.
- Oil reserves: owners have incentive to extract at the pace that
  maximizes present value — neither too fast nor too slow.
- "Future generations" argument often wrong: high prices for scarce
  resources today automatically create incentives to use less and find
  alternatives — protecting future generations better than mandates.

## PART V: THE NATIONAL ECONOMY

## Chapter 16: National Output

### The Fallacy of Composition
- What's true for one part of the economy is NOT necessarily true
  for the whole. Especially dangerous in macroeconomic thinking.
- Sports stadium: any individual can see better by standing up, but
  if everyone stands, no one sees better.
- Burning building: any individual escapes faster by running, but mass
  running creates deadly stampedes — which is why fire drills train
  orderly evacuation.
- "Saving jobs" in one industry: government spending can save 10,000
  jobs in widgets. But the money comes from elsewhere — the 10,000
  jobs saved may cost 15,000 jobs lost in other industries.
  The saved jobs are visible; the destroyed jobs are scattered, invisible.
- 1990s: massive layoffs made headlines in steel, autos, telecom.
  Yet overall unemployment in the U.S. was at its lowest in years
  and total jobs were at record highs. What was true for visible parts
  was opposite for the whole.

### The Great Depression
- 1929: unemployment was 3%. After stock market crash, unemployment
  peaked at 9% two months later, then began declining.
- Then came unprecedented government interventions:
  - Smoot-Hawley Tariffs of 1930: 1,000 economists signed a public
    letter warning against them; Congress and Hoover ignored it.
  - Five months later: unemployment reversed and hit double digits.
  - Stayed above double digits for EVERY month of the entire decade.
  - Germany: unemployment hit 34% in 1931, setting the stage for
    Nazi electoral triumph in 1932, Hitler in power in 1933.
- Comparison to 1920-21 recession: sharp downturn, government
  did virtually nothing. Economy recovered rapidly by 1922.
- Conclusion: the Great Depression was not the result of market failure
  but of massive government intervention that turned a normal
  recession into a catastrophe.

## Chapter 17: Money and the Banking System

### What Money Is
- Money = any widely accepted medium of exchange: gold, silver, sea
  shells, tobacco warehouse receipts (colonial America), gin bottles
  (British West Africa), cigarettes (WWII POW camps — generating
  interest rates and Gresham's Law), 12-foot doughnut-shaped rocks
  in Yap Islands (ownership transferred electronically like modern
  banking, without physical movement), modern paper currency.
- When Argentina's monetary system broke down in 2002: barter clubs
  (trueque) emerged — bread traded for firewood, antique china for
  prime beef. Demonstrated how much wealth money enables.
- During U.S. Great Depression: money supply contracted drastically →
  150 barter systems in 30 states emerged as substitutes.
- Money is NOT wealth for society as a whole — just an artifact for
  transferring wealth and creating incentives to produce it.

### Banking and Money Multiplication
- Banks keep only a fraction of deposits as reserves, lending the rest.
- "Fractional reserve banking": works fine normally. Vulnerable when
  many depositors want cash simultaneously (bank runs).
- Bank robbery could cause a run far more damaging than the robbery.
  The money robbed was small; the subsequent panic could close
  the bank and wipe out all depositors.
- A perfectly solvent bank can fail in a bank run: assets (mortgages,
  buildings) cannot be instantly converted to cash. Liquidity = ability
  to convert to cash WITHOUT losing value.
- Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation (FDIC): insures deposits,
  eliminating bank runs by reassuring depositors their money is safe.
  But deposit insurance creates moral hazard: banks take more risk
  knowing depositors don't bear the consequences.
- Inflation is a monetary phenomenon: too much money chasing too
  few goods. When government prints money to pay its bills, it in
  effect taxes savers by reducing purchasing power of their savings.

## Chapter 18: Government Functions

### Legitimate Roles
- Government has legitimate roles: enforcing property rights and
  contracts, providing national defense, building infrastructure with
  large external benefits, correcting genuine market failures.
- External costs (pollution) and external benefits (education, basic
  research) are cases where private decisions don't align with
  social optimum.
- Property rights foundation: without them, the invisible hand cannot
  work. Contracts must be enforceable.

### Government Failures
- Government interventions often produce consequences opposite to
  their stated goals.
- Regulations cost about $7,800 per employee in large businesses,
  $10,600 in small businesses — giving large businesses a competitive
  advantage (they can spread compliance costs over more output).
- Powers created for one crisis survive long after the crisis is over.
  FDR took U.S. off gold standard in 1933 using wartime powers
  created during WWI to prevent trading with enemy nations.
  The war had been over for 14 years. The powers were still there.

### Property Rights and Environment
- Animals threatened with extinction: ONLY those not owned by anyone.
  No one is letting chickens or cows go extinct.
- Pollution: air and water — things not owned by anyone — are
  what gets polluted.
- Environmental regulations often have unintended consequences:
  fuel efficiency standards → more miles driven → more fuel used.
  Underground storage tanks regulations → above-ground tanks with
  fire risks. Government air bags → killed small children.

## Chapter 19: Government Finance

### Taxes
- Higher tax RATES do not always produce higher tax REVENUES.
  Revenue depends on rates × taxable activity. Higher rates reduce
  taxable activity.
- 2005: U.S. tax revenues ran 15% higher than 2004. Corporate taxes
  up 40%. No increase in tax rates.

### Government Borrowing
- The burden of the debt cannot be fully passed to future generations:
  the real resources consumed must be sacrificed by the current
  generation. Only the obligation to repay creditors can be shifted.
- WWII: paid by taxes AND bonds. But real sacrifice (foregone civilian
  goods) was borne by the WWII generation regardless. Wartime
  inflation then stole part of the real value of the bonds when cashed.
- Farm subsidy bill in 2002: bipartisan support estimated to cost the
  average American family $4,000+ in inflated food prices over the
  next decade.
- Brazil: generous pensions for government employees → people can
  retire BETTER OFF than when they were working.
- India's licensing system: so pervasive that businesses needed parallel
  bureaucracies just to navigate permissions. State governments
  didn't want to "alienate government servants' vote bank" by removing
  thousands of inspectors.

## Chapter 20: Special Problems in the National Economy

### Inflation
- Inflation: too much money chasing too few goods.
- Germany's hyperinflation 1920s: Weimar Republic printed money to
  pay WWI reparations. By 1923, wheelbarrow of currency couldn't buy
  a loaf of bread. Middle class savings wiped out. Set stage for Hitler.
- Zimbabwe 21st century: hyperinflation so severe people used
  foreign currencies instead.

### The Great Depression (Revisited)
- Unemployment never reached 10% in the year following the 1929
  crash, then soared past 20% for 35 consecutive months after
  government interventions began.
- "It is virtually impossible politically for any American government
  to allow a recession to run its course, as American governments
  once did for more than 150 years prior to the Great Depression."
- Very seldom does anyone compare what actually happens when
  the government "does something" with what has happened when
  the government did nothing.

## PART VI: THE INTERNATIONAL ECONOMY

## Chapter 21: International Trade

### The Balance of Trade Fallacy
- "Favorable" balance of trade (export surplus) vs. "unfavorable"
  (import surplus): terminology goes back centuries when gold was
  seen as wealth.
- Adam Smith in 1776: the real wealth of a nation is its goods and
  services, not its gold supply.
- During the Great Depression: U.S. had an export surplus ("favorable"
  balance of trade) EVERY YEAR of the decade — yet it was a catastrophe.
- When U.S. trade deficit narrowed sharply in spring 2001:
  BusinessWeek celebrated. But it narrowed because imports fell
  during an economic slowdown — alongside falling stocks, rising
  unemployment, declining output.
- U.S. became record-breaking "debtor nation" during the booming
  prosperity of the 1990s. The words cannot be taken at face value.

### Why Both Countries Gain from Trade
- **Absolute advantage**: growing bananas in the Caribbean is far
  cheaper than in the U.S. Growing coffee requires specific climate
  conditions found in few places.
- **Comparative advantage**: even if one country is better at producing
  EVERYTHING, trade still benefits both. The U.S. should focus on what
  it's relatively MOST better at producing.
- **Economies of scale**: specialization allows much larger scale
  production, reducing costs far below what each country could
  achieve producing for itself alone.

### Tariffs and Protectionism
- Smoot-Hawley: 1,000 economists warned against it; Congress passed
  it anyway. Result: exacerbated the Depression.
- Import steel tariffs: saved some steel jobs → raised costs of
  American manufacturers using steel → cost far more jobs than saved.
- Countries often claim foreign competitors are being "unfair." True —
  virtually all countries impose "unfair" restrictions. Responding in
  kind deprives both countries of even more benefits.

### Outsourcing and Comparative Advantage
- Taiwan by early 21st century made "the vast majority of the world's
  computer components" — allowing U.S. to specialize in software
  design, where its comparative advantage was greatest.
- "If a million new jobs are created in scattered companies and half
  a million jobs are lost in one visible industry where unions can
  raise a clamor, the million winners don't count politically."
- Result: laws passed restricting free trade for vocal concentrated
  constituencies at cost of far larger but diffuse benefits.

## Chapter 22: International Transfers of Wealth

### Immigration
- After Moriscos expelled from Spain in 17th century: "Who will make
  our shoes now?" A cleric asked after supporting the expulsion.
- "Brain drain": 60%+ of college graduates from Fiji, Trinidad, Haiti,
  Jamaica, and Guyana emigrate to OECD countries. For Guyana: 83%.
  Massive loss of national wealth.
- Nazi Germany's antisemitic policies: many Jewish scientists fled to
  America, played major role in making U.S. first with atomic bomb.
  Germany's ally Japan paid a huge price for this emigration.
- Immigrant welfare rates: Japan-origin immigrants to U.S. — 2% on
  welfare. Laotian-origin — 46%. No single pattern applies to all
  immigrants; everything depends on which immigrants, which
  countries, which periods of history.

### Imperialism
- Cecil Rhodes made a personal fortune in Africa — but the costs to
  British taxpayers exceeded even his fabulous wealth.
- Britain at empire's height: invested MORE in the United States than
  in all of Asia and Africa combined. Wealth flows to where wealth
  already exists.
- Slavery: concentrated in American South and northern Brazil —
  both of which remained the LESS prosperous, less technologically
  advanced regions of their countries.
- Serfdom in Russia "simply put much wealth in the hands of a
  spendthrift nobility" — contributing little to economic development.

## Chapter 23: International Disparities in Wealth

*[The longest chapter in the book, new to the 5th edition]*

### The Core Question
- Was there ever any realistic chance that nations would have similar
  economic development? Given vast differences in geographic,
  demographic, and cultural factors, equal outcomes would be a
  "staggering coincidence."

### Geographic Factors
- **Soil fertility**: Mollisols (richest agricultural soils) found in
  American Midwest/plains extending into Canada, across Eurasia from
  Eastern Europe to northeastern China, and in temperate South
  America. NOT in tropics or sub-Saharan Africa. African soils have
  multiple severe deficiencies; crop yields are a fraction of China's
  or the U.S.'s. Shallow topsoil limits root growth; dryness inhibits
  fertilizer use; wetlands breed malaria and river blindness.
- **Rainfall**: Western Europe has rain spread throughout the year.
  Sub-Saharan Africa: long dry spells followed by torrential downpours
  that wash away topsoil. Spain's annual rainfall ranges from <300mm
  to >1,500mm within the same country.
- **Rivers and transportation**: Rhine flows from Switzerland to North
  Sea — continuous access to the world. African rivers like the
  Zaire/Congo are magnificent in length and volume but interrupted
  by cataracts and waterfalls — ships can't get far inland. The Nile
  was too shallow for large Roman-era ships. European rivers:
  - Western: flow to open seas (Atlantic access to whole world)
  - Eastern: flow to Black Sea, landlocked inland seas, or Arctic
  - Southern (Mediterranean): "torrents in winter and almost dry in
    summer" — little navigation value.
  - Distinguished geographer: "The rivers which flowed north of the
    Alps were incomparably more useful than those of the
    Mediterranean basin."
- **Coastlines**: Africa is more than twice the size of Europe but has
  a shorter coastline — less twisting means fewer natural harbors.
  African coastal waters are often too shallow for large ships, which
  must anchor offshore and unload to smaller boats — far more
  expensive. Europe trade with Asia went around Africa for centuries
  without stopping.
- **Climate**: Athens has nearly double London's annual sunshine hours;
  Alexandria has more than double. Even within Spain, climate varies
  enormously.

### Cultural and Demographic Factors
- Isolated peoples (in Balkan mountains, African rift valleys) tend
  to be culturally and technologically behind peoples who have traded,
  traveled, and encountered other civilizations.
- Geographic isolation preserves culture but retards economic
  development — because contact with the wider world brings not
  just trade goods but also ideas, technologies, and competitive
  pressures.

### The Imperialism Explanation (Critique)
- Common argument: rich countries are rich because they exploited
  poor countries. But:
  - There were large disparities BEFORE conquest; these pre-existing
    disparities facilitated the conquests.
  - The most successful economies (Japan, South Korea, Taiwan,
    Switzerland, Hong Kong) were either not colonized or derived
    minimal benefit from colonialism.
  - Slavery: regions with the most slaves are the LESS prosperous
    parts of their countries (American South, northern Brazil).
  - Britain at height of empire invested more in the U.S. than all
    of Asia and Africa combined.

### Implications
- Equal economic outcomes across nations were "unlikely in principle
  and rare empirically." This is not a judgment — it's an observation
  about the extraordinary complexity of factors required to align.
- Geography is not destiny — human knowledge changes what
  constitutes a natural resource. But geography creates initial
  conditions that persist across centuries.
- The question "Why are some nations rich and others poor?" cannot
  be answered by pointing to exploitation or virtue. The combination
  of geographic, historical, cultural, and institutional factors is
  staggering in its complexity.

## PART VII: SPECIAL ECONOMIC ISSUES

## Chapter 24: Myths About Markets

### The Market Is People
- The biggest myth: we think of "the market" as a thing — impersonal,
  mechanical. But a market IS people engaging in economic transactions
  on mutually agreed terms.
- This misconception allows third parties to claim they're "rescuing"
  people from the "dictates of the market" when in fact they're
  subjecting people to the dictates of third parties.

### Different Prices for "Same" Things
- Northern California consumer magazine: same grocery list, same
  brands — ranged from $80 to $125 across stores. Three different
  Safeway stores: $98 to $103. Differences reflect real estate costs,
  inventory levels, service.
- The cheapest store had only 49% of items in stock at any time;
  more expensive stores had 75%+ in stock. Price differences reflect
  real differences in what you're getting.
- Christmas cards cheaper on Dec. 26 than Dec. 24 — physically
  identical cards have different values at different times.

### "Greed" as Explanation
- Wall Street "faceless financial analysts" demanding profit
  requirements: in fact, they handle pension funds and savings of
  millions of ordinary people.
- If "vagaries of newspaper journalism" make it hard to earn market
  returns, why should teachers and mechanics subsidize editors and
  reporters? The less wealthy subsidizing the more wealthy.

## Chapter 25: "Non-Economic" Values

### What "Non-Economic" Values Actually Mean
- Indian minister: didn't want "multinational companies getting rich
  selling face creams to poor Indians." When entrepreneur explained
  that lowering excise duty would lower prices and raise tax revenues,
  the minister replied: "How can you decide what she wants?" without
  realizing that was exactly what he himself was doing.
- The things called "luxuries of the rich" have a habit of becoming
  amenities for all as markets and technology advance. In the 20th
  century: cars, phones, refrigerators, TVs, air conditioning, personal
  computers all went from luxury to commonplace.
  First VCRs: $30,000 each. Eventually affordable for most Americans.
  Oranges, sugar, and cocoa were once luxuries in Europe.
- Clean water: private water companies in England comply with
  environmental regulations a higher percentage of the time than
  government-run systems in Scotland.
- Lofty rhetoric about non-economic values often amounts to selfish
  attempts to have one's own preferences subsidized by others.

## Chapter 26: The History of Economics

### Adam Smith and the Classical School
- Adam Smith (1723-1790): Wealth of Nations (1776) — foundational text.
- Smith: the "invisible hand" — individuals pursuing their own interests
  inadvertently promote the public good.
- David Ricardo: comparative advantage in international trade.
- Thomas Malthus: population grows geometrically; food supply grows
  arithmetically → inevitable mass poverty. Wrong because he didn't
  foresee technological progress.

### The Labor Theory of Value and Marx
- Marx took the labor theory of value to its logical extreme: if labor
  creates all value, then capitalist profits represent surplus value
  extracted from workers — exploitation.
- The problem: capital also contributes to production. Without
  machinery, workers produce far less.
- Marx predicted increasing misery for workers and inevitable revolution.
  In practice, real wages rose dramatically under capitalism.

### The Marginalist Revolution
- 1870s: Carl Menger (Austria) and W. Stanley Jevons (England)
  independently developed marginal utility theory — simultaneously.
- The key insight: value is determined not by total utility but by
  MARGINAL utility — the utility of one more unit.
- Water vs. diamonds: Smith said water is more useful than diamonds
  yet diamonds cost more. Answer: we already have ample water, so
  the marginal value of another glass is low. We have few diamonds,
  so the marginal value of another carat is high.
- This unified price theory around supply (costs) and demand
  (marginal utility) and ended the labor-theory-vs.-utility debate.
- Alfred Marshall integrated both sides: supply + demand determine
  price. Introduced elasticity and consumer surplus.

### Keynes and Friedman
- Keynes: markets don't automatically return to full employment.
  Government spending can stimulate demand (fiscal policy).
  "In the long run we are all dead." Dominated policy 1940s-1970s.
  Stagflation of 1970s contradicted Keynesian models.
- Friedman: money supply is the key driver. Great Depression was
  primarily caused by the Fed allowing money supply to contract by
  one-third. Stable monetary policy better than activist intervention.
- Hayek: price system as information-processing mechanism.
  Central planning cannot replicate what prices do — the knowledge
  problem.

## Chapter 27: Parting Thoughts

### The Three Key Economic Fallacies
1. **Zero-sum thinking**: believing economic transactions benefit one
   party only at the expense of the other. If transactions were zero-
   sum, rent control and minimum wage laws might make sense.
   But both parties gain from voluntary transactions; changing the
   terms by force reduces the number of transactions.

2. **Ignoring competition**: central planning seems appealing because
   the alternative looks like "chaos of uncoordinated activity."
   But competition is what coordinates the chaos productively.

3. **Not thinking beyond initial consequences**: import steel tariffs
   do save jobs in the steel industry — but raise costs for all
   manufacturers using steel, costing far more jobs than saved.

### Bread and Champagne
- Wartime price controls on bread and butter (not champagne/caviar).
  Goal: keep essentials affordable for the poor.
- Consequence: bread/butter producers earn lower profits. Competition
  for scarce resources means champagne producers can bid workers
  and ingredients away from bread producers.
- Result: less bread and butter produced; more champagne and caviar.
  The price controls on essentials caused LESS of the essentials
  to be produced. Exactly opposite of the goal.

### Economics as a Tool
- "Seldom do people think things through foolishly. More often, they
  do not bother to think things through at all, so that even highly
  intelligent individuals can reach untenable conclusions because
  their brainpower means little if it is not deployed and applied."
- The fundamental principles are not hard to understand — "but they
  are easy to forget, especially amid the heady rhetoric of politics
  and the media."

## Key Concepts and Definitions

- **Scarcity**: what everyone wants exceeds what there is
- **Economics**: study of scarce resources with alternative uses
- **Price controls**: cause shortages (ceiling) or surpluses (floor)
- **Opportunity cost**: value of the best alternative foregone
- **Comparative advantage**: even if less efficient at everything, can
  still gain from specializing in least-disadvantaged activity
- **Absolute advantage**: simply producing something at lower cost
- **Economies of scale**: lower per-unit costs at higher output
- **Present value**: future expected benefits, discounted for delay
- **Marginal utility**: utility of one more unit (not total utility)
- **Fractional reserve banking**: banks keep fraction of deposits in
  cash, multiply the money supply through lending
- **Fallacy of composition**: what's true for part may not be true
  for the whole
- **Systemic causation**: outcomes not intended or caused by any
  single actor; result of complex interactions
- **Human capital**: skills, knowledge, experience embodied in people
- **Limited liability**: investors can only lose their investment amount
- **Externalities**: costs or benefits imposed on non-participants
- **Gresham's Law**: bad money drives out good (referenced in context
  of cigarette money in WWII POW camps)
