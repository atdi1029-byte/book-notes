# The Rise and Fall of American Growth
## Robert J. Gordon — 912 pages (2016)

---

## Core Thesis

American economic growth is not a steady, reliable process — it was a
one-time miracle that may never be repeated. Between 1870 and 1970,
the United States underwent a transformation so radical and so complete
that no subsequent period of innovation can plausibly match it. The
inventions of that century — electricity, indoor plumbing, motor vehicles,
antibiotics, the telephone, radio, and air travel — addressed every basic
human need: food, shelter, clothing, health, transportation, communication,
and entertainment. They could happen only once, because you can only
transition from darkness to electric light once, from horse to automobile
once, from high infant mortality to low infant mortality once.

Since 1970, by contrast, innovation has been real but narrow. The
Information Age revolutionized entertainment, communications, and
information — roughly 7% of GDP. It barely touched food, clothing,
housing, health, transportation, and the physical world. The result is
slower productivity growth and, compounded by four structural headwinds
(inequality, education stagnation, demographic drag, and government debt),
a future of near-zero real income growth for median Americans.

---

## The Three Big Ideas

1. **The Special Century (1870–1970)** was unique in human history.
   Annual western-world growth before 1820 was 0.06% per century.
   The 50 years of 1920–1970 alone accounted for 66% of all cumulative
   TFP growth across 124 years.

2. **Post-1970 innovation was "dazzling but disappointing"** — channeled
   only into ICT; TFP grew at barely one-third the 1920–1970 rate.
   Counterfactual: if post-1970 productivity had matched 1920–1970,
   real GDP per person in 2014 would be $97,300 instead of $50,600.

3. **Four structural headwinds** push growth of real median disposable
   income to only 0.3%/year for 2015–2040, vs. 2.1%/year for 1920–2014.

## Book Structure

**Part I (Chapters 2–9): 1870–1940 — The Great Transformation**
- Life in 1870: horses, outhouses, 45-year life expectancy, 60-hour weeks
- The networked home: electricity, gas, water, sewer, telephone
- Transportation: railroads → electric streetcars → motor vehicles
- Communications: telegraph → telephone → radio → talkies
- Public health: how clean water (not medicine) saved millions of lives
- Work, wages, and hours; consumer credit; government's expanding role

**Entr'acte: The Midcentury Shift**
- What real GDP misses; why IR #2 covered all of human wants;
  why IR #3 covers only ~7% of GDP

**Part II (Chapters 10–15): 1940–2015 — Progress Continues, Then Stalls**
- Supermarkets, appliances, suburbs, urban decay
- Automobiles and the interstate; air travel peaks in 1958 and stagnates
- TV, home electronics, the internet
- Medicine: antibiotics and vaccines accomplish more pre-1960 than after
- The feminization of the labor force; retirement and Social Security
- Why output per person growth held up longer than productivity growth

**Part III (Chapters 16–18): Causes and Prospects**
- The Great Leap Forward: how the Depression and WWII created the
  productivity miracle; learning-by-doing; government-financed capital
- Why IR #3 cannot match IR #2; Moore's Law breakdown after 2006
- Inequality, education, demography, fiscal debt — the four headwinds

**Postscript:** Policy reforms that could help, but only marginally

## Key Statistics

- Life expectancy at birth 1870: 45 years. Today: 78.
- Infant mortality 1880: 188/thousand. Today: ~6/thousand.
- Share of "unpleasant" jobs: 87% (1870) → 22% (2009)
- Model T price: $950 (1908) → $269 (1923)
- Home electricity: 0% (1880) → 96% urban (1940) → 100% (1950)
- Real wages grew FASTER than productivity 1870–1940 (2.48% vs 1.51%/yr)
- TFP growth: 1.89%/yr (1920–70) vs. 0.40%/yr (2004–14)
- Manufacturing jobs: ~30% of labor force (1953) → <10% (2015)
- CEO pay: 20× average worker (1973) → 257× (2013)
- Black male HS dropouts who have been in prison: 14.7% (1979) → 68% (2009)

## Why This Book Matters

Gordon challenges the tech-optimist consensus that assumes innovation
will always drive prosperity. The book is simultaneously a deep history
of American life (what it actually felt like to live in 1870 vs. 1940 vs.
today) and a rigorous macroeconomic argument about why the great
inventions cannot be repeated. It forces us to distinguish between
innovations that address every human need (IR #2) and those that address
only one slice of human experience (IR #3). The headwinds chapter is
among the most sobering economic forecasts written before COVID —
and most of its predictions have since proven correct or conservative.
