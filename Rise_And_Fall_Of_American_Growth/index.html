Glossary

Core Framework Terms

Total Factor Productivity (TFP): Output divided by a weighted average of labor (0.7) and capital (0.3) inputs. The best available measure of innovation and technological change — everything that makes an economy more efficient beyond simply adding more workers or machines. Gordon's TFP data shows a "one big wave" shape: slow before 1920, surging 1920–1970, slow again after.

General Purpose Technology (GPT): An invention so broad in its applications that it spawns cascading sub-inventions for decades. Electricity and the internal combustion engine are GPTs — they enabled elevators, appliances, streetcars, suburbanization, and air travel. Software may be a GPT, but its effects are confined to a narrow slice of GDP.

Industrial Revolution #1 (IR #1): Steam power, cotton textile machinery, iron and railroads — roughly 1770–1870. Britain-centered; the U.S. absorbed its gains by 1870.

Industrial Revolution #2 (IR #2): Electricity, the internal combustion engine, chemicals, running water, and sanitation — roughly 1870–1970. The subject of this book. Covered every basic human need: food, shelter, clothing, health, transportation, communication, and entertainment.

Industrial Revolution #3 (IR #3): Computers, the internet, and smartphones — roughly 1960 to the present. Revolutionized information, communication, and entertainment. Barely touched food, clothing, housing, health, or physical transportation. Gordon's central claim is that IR #3's narrow scope explains why it cannot replicate IR #2's productivity miracle.

The Special Century: Gordon's term for 1870–1970, when the great inventions of IR #2 transformed every dimension of American life. The term captures both the uniqueness and the non-repeatability of that era.

Productivity and Measurement

New Product Bias: A form of price index understatement that occurs when new goods appear but aren't immediately added to the CPI basket. Room air conditioners first sold in 1951 but not indexed until 1967; VCRs sold from 1978 but not indexed until 1987. All price declines during those gaps go uncounted.

Early-Adoption Price Drop Bias: The Model T fell from $950 (1908) to $269 (1923), yet no car price index existed until 1935. That 73% quality-adjusted price collapse is simply missing from the historical record.

Quality Bias: When a product improves but the CPI records no price change because the nominal price held steady — e.g., a TV offering a larger screen at the same dollar price.

Outlet Substitution Bias: When consumers shift to lower-price retailers (A&P in the 1920s, Walmart today), the CPI doesn't record that as a real price reduction because it tracks prices within existing store categories.

Consumer Surplus: The gap between what a good is worth to buyers and what they actually pay. The electric light created enormous consumer surplus — Nordhaus calculated that $20 bought 5,500 candle-hours in 1800 but 4.4 million candle-hours by 1920. That 800× improvement in real purchasing power is mostly invisible to GDP statistics.

Solow Paradox: Robert Solow's 1987 observation that "we can see the computer age everywhere but in the productivity statistics." Gordon's resolution: computers are not everywhere — they barely touch food, clothing, housing, or physical transportation, which together represent the majority of consumption.

Labor Market and Inequality

Great Compression: The 1945–1975 period of relative income equality, when a unionized blue-collar worker could live next door to an accountant or teacher. Goldin and Margo's term for the wartime and postwar leveling of wages.

Great Divergence: Post-1970 widening of income inequality. Average income in the top 0.01% jumped from 50 to 250 times the economy-wide average between 1970 and 1998.

Labor Force Participation Rate (LFPR): The share of the working-age population that is employed or actively seeking work. Women's LFPR rose from 34.9% (1948) to a peak of 76.8% (1999), masking slower productivity growth by adding hours to the denominator.

Learning by Doing: The productivity gains that come from accumulated experience on a production task, not from deliberate research. Kaiser shipyard workers reduced Liberty freighter construction from 8 months (1942) to a few weeks (1943) purely through practice and worker-suggested improvements.

Policy and Institutions

Wagner Act (1935): Made union organizing substantially easier; led to rapid wage increases in the late 1930s. Gordon argues this forced firms to substitute capital for labor, paradoxically boosting productivity during the Depression.

Rural Electrification Administration (REA): New Deal agency that brought electric power to rural America. One Wyoming ranch woman called the day electricity arrived "my Day of Days because lights shone where lights had never been, the electric stove radiated heat, the washer turned."

FDIC (Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation): Created by the Glass-Steagall Act (1933). Nearly eliminated bank failures — 10,000 of 25,000 U.S. banks had failed between 1931 and 1933. One of the most effective single policy interventions in American economic history.

Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC): Federal tax credit for low-income workers. Gordon cites evidence that it reduces low-birthweight births, raises math and reading scores, and boosts college enrollment — making it one of the most cost-effective policy tools available.

The Four Headwinds

Income Inequality Headwind: Because productivity gains flow disproportionately to the top, median disposable income grows slower than mean income. The bottom 90% saw essentially zero real income growth from 1972 to 2013.

Education Headwind: U.S. high school graduation stagnated after 1970; college costs rose at triple the inflation rate; the U.S. fell from 1st to 12th in college completion globally.

Demographic Headwind: Baby boomer retirement (2008–2034) subtracts an estimated 0.4%/year from output per person as retirees exit the labor force.

Fiscal Headwind: Rising entitlement costs and government debt require future tax increases or spending cuts, both of which reduce real disposable income. Medicare trust fund projected to reach zero by 2030.

Chapter 1: Introduction — The Ascent and Descent of Growth

Growth is not a steady, reliable process. For nearly all of recorded human history, it was essentially absent. From AD 1 to AD 1820, annual per-capita growth in the western world averaged 0.06% per century — so slow that a person's material standard of living was virtually identical to that of their grandparents and great-grandparents. As economic historian Steven Landsburg put it: "For the next 99,800 years or so, nothing happened. Almost everyone lived on the modern equivalent of $400 to $600 per year." Then, beginning around 1770, something changed — and the pace of change accelerated dramatically after 1870.

Gordon's central framework organizes economic history into four phases: virtually no growth before 1770, slow growth from 1770 to 1870 as the first industrial revolution spread, remarkably rapid growth from 1870 to 1970, and slower growth since 1970. The 50 years from 1920 to 1970 alone — only 40% of the 124-year span from 1890 to 2014 — accounted for fully 66% of all cumulative TFP growth across that period. The "one big wave" interpretation of American economic history is not a poetic metaphor. It is what the data show, decade after decade.

The book opens with a thought experiment that grounds the abstraction in lived experience. When Hurricane Sandy knocked out power below 34th Street in Manhattan in 2012, New Yorkers were pushed back to the conditions of the 19th century. No elevators, no subway, no refrigeration, no lighting. New Jersey drivers couldn't pump gasoline because the pumps required electricity. The storm lasted only a few days, but it illustrated how completely the infrastructure of daily life rests on the great inventions of the Special Century — and how quickly civilized urban existence collapses without them.

The argument for uniqueness rests on the concept of one-time transitions. You can only transition from darkness to electric light once. You can only transition from the horse to the motor vehicle once. You can only transition from 22% infant mortality to 1% infant mortality once. You can only replace the one-room schoolhouse with universal high school once. Each of these transitions produced an enormous, unrepeatable gain in welfare. The era of great inventions was precisely the era when most of these one-time transitions occurred simultaneously, producing a compression of welfare gains across a single century that had never happened before and cannot happen again.

The transportation example is particularly vivid. In 1830, no one could travel faster than a horse. By 1958, the Boeing 707 flew at 0.85 times the speed of sound. That journey — from horse speed to near-sonic speed — represents 100% of the possible improvement in travel velocity over any ordinary range of distance. It happened in roughly 130 years. The improvement from the 707 to today's aircraft? Essentially zero. Planes today are slightly slower than in 1960 because fuel efficiency became more important than speed.

The price index problem is fundamental to understanding why conventional GDP statistics understate how much better life became during the Special Century. Four systematic biases work together to make official measures too pessimistic about past progress and, by implication, too optimistic about more recent progress. New products aren't indexed until years after introduction. Early price drops from mass production are missed entirely. Quality improvements captured at stable nominal prices register as no price decline. And the shift of consumers toward lower-price retailers is recorded as no real gain. The Model T fell from $950 in 1908 to $269 in 1923 — a 73% real price reduction — but because no car price index existed until 1935, this enormous improvement in living standards simply does not appear in the historical record.

Beyond what GDP misses in measurement, there are entire categories of welfare improvement that GDP simply doesn't attempt to value. Indoor plumbing replacing outdoor privies and outhouses doesn't count. The value of the lives saved by penicillin doesn't count. The value of Pasteur's germ theory changing behavior and preventing disease doesn't count. Urban sanitation infrastructure — the clean water systems that eliminated typhoid, cholera, and yellow fever — doesn't count. William Nordhaus calculated that the value of improvements in life expectancy from 1900 to 1950 was as large as all other sources of real GDP per capita growth combined. With health capital included, annual per-capita growth from 1900 to 1950 was 4.2%, not the conventional 2.05%.

The counterfactual Gordon poses is arresting. If productivity growth from 1970 to 2014 had simply maintained its 1920–1970 pace, real GDP per person in 2014 would be $97,300 instead of the actual $50,600. That missing $46,700 per person — for every man, woman, and child in the United States — represents the cost of the productivity slowdown. It is not a small adjustment at the margin. It is the difference between the America we have and a dramatically more prosperous one that seemed to be within reach in 1970.

"Innovation since the 1970s has been real, dazzling, and disappointing — real and dazzling because the proliferation of personal computers, cell phones, the internet, and e-commerce has changed the way we live and work; disappointing because its impact on total factor productivity has been so little." — Robert J. Gordon

The book's third big claim concerns not just growth but distribution. Even if productivity were to recover to its 1920–1970 pace, four structural headwinds would prevent that growth from translating into rising living standards for most Americans. Rising inequality means that productivity gains flow increasingly to the top. Education stagnation means the workforce is less prepared than it was in 1970. Baby-boomer retirement is mechanically reducing labor force participation. And government debt constrains the fiscal tools available to respond. Gordon's projection for 2015–2040: real median disposable income grows at only 0.3% per year, compared to 2.1% per year for the near- century from 1920 to 2014. The rise of American growth may have already given way to its fall.

Chapter 2: The Starting Point — Life and Work in 1870

To understand how much changed, you have to understand what you are starting from. In 1870, the United States had a population of exactly 40 million, a median age well under 25, and an average household of 5.3 persons. Eighty percent of adults lived in married-couple households, prime-age male labor force participation was 98%, and retirement essentially did not exist — men worked until they dropped. The country had absorbed the British First Industrial Revolution and was wealthier than most of Europe: per-capita income at 74% of British levels, 128% of German. McCormick's reaper at the London Crystal Palace in 1851 had demonstrated what the "American system" of interchangeable parts could do. Yet daily life remained almost medieval in its material deprivation.

Housing in 1870 was defined by isolation, darkness, and physical labor. Zero percent of homes had electricity. Buildings were poorly insulated — rags stuffed into cracks to keep out wind. There was no indoor plumbing, no running water, no flush toilet. The 1893 Commissioner of Labor report found that 88% of Baltimore families had access only to outdoor privies. An 1899 survey of New York City workers found them "housed worse than any city in the civilized world." The dumbbell tenement — five to seven stories high, with four apartments per floor sharing a five-foot-wide air shaft that quickly became a garbage dump — was the dominant form of working-class housing in the nation's largest city, and it was banned only in 1901.

The horse defined urban life in ways that are almost impossible to grasp from the modern vantage point. Boston in 1870 had 250,000 citizens sharing streets with 50,000 horses. Each horse produced 20 to 50 pounds of manure and a gallon of urine daily, which works out to 5 to 10 tons of manure per square mile per day. Dead horses — averaging 1,200 pounds each — were left in the street to decompose enough to be cut apart for removal. The fall 1872 horse flu epidemic paralyzed northeastern cities: streetcars were suspended, there was no milk or ice or groceries, saloons ran out of beer, and construction halted entirely. The horse was not a charming anachronism but a dominant, expensive, messy, dangerous, and essential piece of urban infrastructure that employed hundreds of thousands of people simply managing its waste and remains.

Life expectancy at birth in 1870 was only 45 years, and that figure is misleading in the optimistic direction — it reflects the enormous drag of infant and child mortality. Infant mortality was 176 per thousand live births. Memphis in September 1873 was struck simultaneously by yellow fever, cholera, and smallpox; 2,000 of 7,000 yellow fever cases died. Charles Eliot, the new president of Harvard, wrote in 1869 that "the ignorance and general incompetency of the average graduate of American Medical Schools at the time of his entry upon practice is something horrible to contemplate." There was no physician licensing in 1870. The profession was largely indistinguishable from quackery, which was itself a thriving industry.

Consumer spending in 1870, translated to 2010 dollars, came to roughly $2,808 per person annually — about $54 per week. Food consumed 51.9% of that spending. By 2013, food at home had fallen to only 7.6% of consumer expenditures, and services had expanded to 66.5% — much of it for categories that didn't even exist in 1869. The diet itself was monotonous and tied to whatever could be raised locally. The dominant foods were pork and corn. Refrigerated railcars hadn't been invented yet. Beef arrived in cities "on the hoof" — cattle were shipped live, starved, and "virtually dead on arrival." Americans averaged 3,029 daily calories per capita in 1870, more than in Britain, but the quality and variety were desperately limited.

Work in 1870 was predominantly agricultural, dangerous, and unrelenting. Agriculture accounted for 46% of jobs. If you aggregate farming, blue-collar work, and domestic service into "unpleasant" employment, then 87% of all jobs in 1870 fell into that category — compared to 60% by 1940 and only 22% by 2009. A North Carolina housewife in 1886 carried water 8 to 10 times per day; over a year, she walked 148 miles and transported more than 36 tons of water. Steel mills ran 12-hour days at an average of 72.6 hours per week. An English steelmaker visiting America observed that "the bosses drive the men to an extent that employers would never dream of attempting in this country." There was no legal protection for injured workers — courts ruled that employees "assumed the risk" by accepting employment.

The 1870 starting point matters not just as background but as the foundation of the argument. The changes that followed — the networked home, the automobile, the health revolution, the shorter work week — can only be understood as departures from this baseline of horse-manure streets, outhouses, kerosene lamps, and back-breaking physical labor. Every subsequent chapter in the first half of the book measures the distance traveled from here. And the distance is almost incomprehensibly large.

Chapter 3: What They Ate and Wore, and the Revolution in Retail

Food spending consumed more than half of the average household budget in 1870, and it stayed stubbornly high — at 41 to 43% — through the BLS budget surveys of 1888 to 1919, even as real incomes rose. This apparent paradox reflects not satiation but the genuine scarcity and cost of food before the great inventions of food preservation, distribution, and retail transformed the sector. Total calorie consumption per capita was essentially flat from 1800 to 1980, meaning that what changed was not how much Americans ate but what they ate, how it was preserved, how it reached them, and what share of their incomes it required.

The transformation of food began with canning, invented by Nicolas Appert in 1809 originally for Napoleon's armies. Gail Borden was horrified by the fate of the Donner Party and invented condensed milk, patented in 1856. By 1870, less than one can per person per year was consumed; by 1910, the figure was 33 cans per person. John Harvey Kellogg accidentally invented corn flakes in 1894 at his sanitarium in Battle Creek, Michigan, as a digestive aid for patients. His former patient, C.W. Post, launched Grape-Nuts in 1897 and became his commercial competitor. Clarence Birdseye discovered flash-freezing by observing Inuit fish preservation in Labrador in 1912, but had to wait until postwar America — when refrigerators finally had adequate freezer compartments — to commercialize it.

Food safety before regulation was genuinely appalling. A 1902 survey found that 52.8% of New York City milk samples were adulterated — dealers added molasses, chalk, or plaster of Paris to mask diseased cattle milk or extend volume. The first pasteurized milk supply didn't appear until Pittsburgh in 1907. Upton Sinclair's The Jungle (1906) was intended as "the Uncle Tom's Cabin of the labor movement," documenting exploitation of immigrant meatpacking workers, but readers fixated on the food safety revelations instead. After government investigators confirmed his findings, "trade in lard, sausage, and canned goods came almost to a standstill." President Theodore Roosevelt signed the Pure Food and Drug Act on June 30, 1906, creating the ancestor of today's FDA.

The biometric record of American heights offers a counterintuitive finding about the relationship between nutrition and health. Native-born American males born in 1830 averaged 68.3 inches. That figure declined to 66.6 inches for those born in 1890, then recovered to 69.6 inches for those born in 1940. This hump shape occurred despite rising per-capita calorie availability. The explanation is sanitation, not food: urbanization packed people into dense proximity, school attendance created new transmission vectors for disease, and water contamination set children's immune systems back at critical developmental stages. People were eating more but growing less because their bodies were fighting constant infection. It was clean water, not more food, that eventually allowed the genetic potential for height to be realized.

The retail revolution transformed how Americans bought food and clothing. Aaron Montgomery Ward launched the first mail-order catalog in 1872, promising money-back guarantees and risk-free transactions — radical innovations in an era when buyers had to trust local merchants without recourse. Sears Roebuck was fulfilling 100,000 orders per day by 1900. Rural Free Delivery, implemented starting in 1901, and Parcel Post, added in 1913, created the physical infrastructure for catalog commerce. Catalog penetration among households went from 3.6% in 1902 to 25.7% by 1928. The A&P grocery chain grew from 7,500 outlets in 1920 to 30,000 by 1930; a Pittsburgh survey in 1911 found chain store prices 21.3% lower than neighborhood stores on average, and 35% lower on meat items. The outlet substitution bias this created — the real price reduction from switching to cheaper stores — was not captured in official price indexes, just as Walmart's lower prices are not captured today.

The urban department store was a different kind of retail revolution — aspirational rather than economical. Marshall Field's in Chicago featured a Tiffany glass ceiling with 1.6 million pieces, the first and largest ever built, and an entire floor of restaurants on the seventh floor designed to draw shoppers upward. "Shopping in these stores became an adventure in high art. Department stores became museums." This democratization of luxury — the ability of an ordinary factory worker's wife to spend an afternoon among chandeliers and marble floors, surrounded by goods from around the world — was itself a form of welfare gain that GDP could never capture.

The clothing sector followed a parallel trajectory: from domestic production to industrial manufacture to retail variety. In 1870, most clothing was made at home or by local tailors and dressmakers. The share of consumer expenditures devoted to clothing stood at 13.1% in 1900. By 2013, that share had fallen to 3.1%, not because people were less clothed but because industrial production, supply chains, and synthetic fabrics had dramatically reduced the real cost of apparel. The liberation of women from the labor of sewing their own and their families' clothing — accomplished largely by 1940 — was an enormous quality-of-life improvement that appears nowhere in conventional growth measures.

Chapter 4: The American Home — From Dark and Isolated to Bright and Networked

"Networked" is the single word that summarizes the interior revolution in urban American homes between 1870 and 1940. Five networks — electricity, natural gas, telephone, clean running water, and sewers — transformed the home from an isolated unit that produced its own light, heat, and water to a node in a metropolitan infrastructure system. Gordon emphasizes an insight about equality that is easy to overlook: "Networking inherently implies equality. Everyone, rich and poor, is plugged into the same electric, water, sewer, gas, and telephone network." In 1870, the rich had servants to haul water, carry fuel, and empty privies. The networked home eliminated that labor and gave everyone the same basic infrastructure.

Diffusion of these five networks across the urban housing stock was remarkably rapid. Electricity went from 0% of homes in 1880 to 96% of urban homes by 1940. Running water went from perhaps one-third of homes in 1900 to essentially universal urban coverage by 1940. Indoor flush toilets, virtually nonexistent in 1870, reached 83% exclusive use among urban households by 1940. Central heating, natural gas, and telephone access followed similar trajectories. The key statement Gordon makes about this process: "Today's houses and apartments are much more similar to those of 1940 than those of 1940 are to their predecessors of 1900." The big transition happened in a single 40-year span.

The transformation of lighting illustrates the magnitude of change. A single modern 100-watt bulb produces approximately 1,200 lumens. The best kerosene lamp of the 1870s produced 80 to 100 lumens. The best town gas fixtures produced more light than kerosene but emitted ammonia, sulfur dioxide, and carbon monoxide, and the gasworks that supplied them were located in poor neighborhoods and belched toxic smoke around the clock. On October 22, 1879, Thomas Edison's cotton-thread filament glowed for 14.5 hours. The cultural response was immediate and visceral. One contemporary wrote: "Here was a little click that meant light was contained in a glass vacuum and need never again be linked with a flame… A child could be left alone with it." By 1920, electric light was ten times brighter than kerosene and a hundred times brighter than a candle.

"$20 per year in 1800 bought 5,500 candle-hours of illumination. By 1890, that same $20 bought 73,000 candle-hours from kerosene and gas. By 1920, electric power produced 4.4 million candle-hours for the same $20. That is an 800-fold real improvement in one of humanity's most basic needs." — Economist William Nordhaus, cited by Gordon

The electric refrigerator illustrates the pattern of dramatic price decline and rapid diffusion that characterized almost all of the great consumer appliances. A refrigerator cost $775 in 1919. By 1931, Sears was selling one for $137 — an 82% price reduction in twelve years. Ownership went from essentially zero in 1920 to 8% of homes in 1930 and 44% by 1940. One million refrigerators were sold in the entire decade from 1919 to 1929; then 2.5 million were sold in 1932 alone, even during the Depression, because the product had become affordable enough that even hard-pressed families made it a priority. The washing machine reached 40% of homes by 1940; a 1928 Sears model sold for $79 to $92, about three weeks of median Muncie income.

Running water required more than a pipe connection — it required an entirely new set of indoor fixtures that didn't exist in 1870. The total number of waterworks in the United States grew from 244 in 1870 to 9,850 by 1924. Muncie, Indiana had no running water before 1885 and fewer than two dozen complete bathrooms in the entire city in 1890. A 1908 Sears catalog offered a complete bathroom set — tub, toilet, sink, and fixtures — for $43.80, with a shipping weight of 480 pounds. That set cost roughly three weeks of a working-class wage, yet the value was enormous: a full bathroom raised apartment rents by 82%, and central heating raised rents by 28%, revealing just how much people would pay for these amenities when they could afford them.

The bungalow movement of 1910 to 1930 democratized home ownership in ways that are easy to underestimate. Developed in Los Angeles and spreading to Chicago by 1905, the bungalow was a modest, practical house designed to be affordable to working-class families while incorporating all the new amenities. Some 80,000 bungalows were built in Chicago between 1910 and 1930. Sears sold complete construction kits for $750 to $2,000, covering all materials; the kit could be assembled in 352 carpenter-hours. The real price of nails had fallen by a factor of ten between 1830 and 1930, making construction materially cheaper in ways that don't appear in nominal housing price series.

Lynd and Lynd's landmark 1925 Muncie, Indiana survey — Middletown — provides the most vivid contemporaneous evidence of how people weighed the new amenities. When they surveyed 26 "run-down houses" that lacked bathtubs, they found that all 26 had automobiles. An Indiana farm housewife explained simply: "You can't go into town in a bathtub." A Tennessee farmer who had just received electricity through the REA stated: "The greatest thing on earth is to have the love of God in your heart, and the next greatest thing is to have electricity in your house." These are not the sentiments of people marginally pleased with incremental improvements. They are testimony to transformative change.

Chapter 5: Motors Overtake Horses and Rail

The motor vehicle's conquest of the United States between 1900 and 1930 was the fastest adoption of any technology in human history to that point. Motor car registrations went from 8,000 in 1900 to 9 million in 1920 to 23 million in 1929. "The total power created by automobiles exceeded that of all farm animals as early as 1910." By 1930, the United States accounted for 78% of all motor vehicles registered worldwide. The Model T Ford alone went from 0 to capturing 55% of the U.S. new car market. "Nothing in the millennia of human history rivals the speed with which automobile ownership spread in a mere two decades between 1910 and 1930."

To understand the automobile's arrival, you have to understand the railroad's dominance before it. The U.S. had a 60,000-mile rail network by 1870, and mileage grew fivefold between 1870 and 1900 — nearly twenty new miles of track per day for thirty years. By 1893, seven transcontinental lines criss-crossed the continent. But railroads were inherently inflexible: they went where tracks had been laid, and they served stations, not doorsteps. Abraham Lincoln's 1860 trip from Springfield to New York for his Cooper Union speech covered 1,200 miles across five trains, two ferries, four days, and three nights. By 1940, the Denver Zephyr averaged 64.6 mph over the Chicago–Denver run on a diesel engine that was four times more fuel-efficient than steam and fully air-conditioned. The railroad reached its peak and perfected its form — but it remained a fixed-track technology.

The internal combustion engine's origin story is almost perfectly parallel to Edison's light bulb. Karl Benz's first successful engine test occurred on New Year's Eve, 1879 — just ten weeks after Edison's light bulb on October 22. He wrote of that moment: "My heart was pounding. I turned the crank. The engine started to go 'put put put,' and the music of the future sounded with regular rhythm." The first practical horseless carriage appeared in 1886. "The automobile is European by birth, American by adoption" — the U.S. market, the scale of production, and the genius of Henry Ford turned the European invention into a mass product.

The Model T price collapse is one of the most dramatic examples in economic history of how mass production transforms affordability. The introductory Model T in 1908 cost $950. After the introduction of the moving assembly line in 1913, the price dropped steadily: $950, $780, $600, $440, $360, $295, $269 by 1923, after 15 million had been produced. The ratio of purchase price to annual disposable income per person fell from 3.16 in 1910 to 0.43 in 1923 — from more than three years' income to less than six months'. By 1926, 75% of new automobiles were financed on installment plans. By that same year, 93% of Iowa farmers owned automobiles — a transition from essentially zero in 1910 that had taken barely fifteen years.

"Social contacts were few and commonly limited within a radius of a few miles... All this has been revolutionized by motor vehicles. The farmer is no longer isolated. He drives to town in his car, his wife shops by automobile, his children attend high school in the county seat." — Contemporary rural survey, cited by Gordon

The automobile's adoption swept away an entire urban ecology. Horse-drawn streetcars had peaked at more than 100,000 horses, 18,000 streetcars, and 3,000 miles of track in American cities. Chicago alone removed 7,000 horse carcasses per year from streetcar service. Heaps of manure seven feet high accumulated on Liberty Street in New York. The electric trolley replaced all of this within a decade of its 1888 introduction in Richmond, Virginia: within two years, there were 200 electrified systems nationwide, and by 1902, 98% of all streetcar trackage was electrified. Public transit peaked at 383 rides per person per year in cities over 25,000 by 1925. But the fixed-fare economics of transit — the five-cent fare could not survive the 74% price inflation of World War I — combined with automotive competition to devastate it. By the end of the war, one-third of all transit companies were bankrupt.

The automotive revolution had social costs that contemporaries already recognized. Government policies favored cars by building roads with public funds while forcing urban transit companies to operate as self-sufficient private enterprises — even though transit riders paid the same local property taxes that financed road construction. Highways enabled suburban sprawl; suburban sprawl demanded more highways. The automobile destroyed the social texture of urban street life that had developed over centuries. Disappearing from American streets were street vendors, delivery boys, the casual pedestrian encounter, the corner drugstore, the local café, the neighborhood store, sidewalk displays, and eventually the sidewalks themselves. "Along with these things," one contemporary observer wrote, "a subtle weave of human relations disappeared."

The chapter's conclusion is precise: the transportation revolution of 1870 to 1940 was literally unrepeatable. The transition from a society dependent on the horse to one dependent on the motor vehicle is a transition that can happen only once. The increase of intercity train speeds from 25 mph to 60 mph covered the practical range from slow to fast — it cannot happen a second time. The replacement of horse manure from city streets, the freeing of a quarter of agricultural land previously used to grow horse feed, the elimination of the workforce employed to collect and process animal waste — all of this happened once, and its welfare effects were captured once. No subsequent transportation improvement has come close to matching this magnitude.

Chapter 6: From Telegraph to Talkies

The communications revolution of 1844 to 1940 compressed the most radical improvement in human communication speed in all of recorded history into a single century. Before the telegraph, the fastest information could travel was as fast as a ship, a railroad, or a horse — roughly 25 miles per hour at best. On May 24, 1844, Samuel Morse sent "What hath God wrought?" from the U.S. Capitol to a railroad depot in Baltimore. Within two years, nine telegraph companies had strung 2,000 miles of wire from Portland, Maine, to Chicago and New Orleans. By 1855, the network was ubiquitous east of the Mississippi. The transcontinental telegraph appeared in 1861; an undersea cable linked Britain and America in 1866. The social impact was immediate: financial arbitrage opportunities that had existed for weeks closed to minutes; single-track railroads could be safely operated where double-track had previously been required, saving enormous capital; newspapers could publish war coverage while battles were still being fought.

The telephone followed the telegraph's example of radical speed improvement, but extended communications to ordinary households. Alexander Graham Bell was not a professional inventor but a professor of vocal physiology whose expertise lay in human speech — his grandfather had founded a London school of elocution, and his father had developed methods for teaching the deaf to speak. Bell filed what has been called "the most valuable patent application in history" on February 14, 1876, arriving at the patent office hours ahead of rival Elisha Gray. His breakthrough came on March 10, 1876 — "Mr. Watson, come here — I want to see you" — and that same night he wrote his father that telegraph wires would one day be "laid on to houses just like water or gas." He was exactly right, though it took fifty years to reach 50% of households, compared to radio's twenty.

The phonograph illustrates how even the great inventors misjudged what their inventions were for. Thomas Edison invented it in July 1877 — he recited "Mary Has a Little Lamb" into the tin-foil cylinder — and called it a "talking machine" intended as a business dictation device. He explicitly did not conceive of it as an entertainment medium. Early phonographs appeared as coin-slot machines in soda fountains and saloons in the 1890s, which is how the entertainment application was discovered commercially. Emile Berliner perfected flat-disc technology by 1888; the Victor Talking Machine Company followed; by 1929 Victor had merged with RCA. The famous dog Nipper listening to the gramophone — "His Master's Voice" — was registered as a trademark in 1900. It took a full fifty years from Edison's invention to achieve accurate music reproduction through the microphone and vacuum tube technology developed in 1925.

"No single event in the history of invention before 1940 brought about a more striking egalitarianism than the spread of radio — within twenty years of the first commercial broadcast, more than 80 percent of American homes were tuned in to the same programs, hearing the same music and the same news, regardless of income or geography." — Robert J. Gordon

Radio diffused faster than any previous technology — faster than electricity, motor vehicles, telephone, or phonograph. The first commercial broadcast was KDKA Pittsburgh on November 2, 1920, when announcer Leo Rosenberg asked "Is anyone listening out there?" and broadcast election returns. Receiver sales went from $60 million in 1922 to $843 million in 1929. By 1940, 80% of American homes had radios. The Depression accelerated radio ownership — from 35% of homes in 1929 to 82% by 1940 — while telephone usage actually fell, because radios were bought outright while telephones were rented. The cultural integration radio produced was extraordinary: the Seabiscuit vs. War Admiral match race in June 1938 drew 40 million listeners in a population of 129 million. Franklin Roosevelt kept his advisors waiting until the race ended. A survey found that Americans would sooner sell their refrigerators, bathtubs, telephones, and beds to cover rent than give up the radio.

Motion pictures capped the pre-1940 communications revolution by adding visual imagery to mass entertainment. Nickelodeons — five-cent storefront theaters — spread rapidly after 1905; by 1908 there were more than 8,000 nationwide drawing 4 million customers per day. Some viewers had never traveled more than a few miles from home, and early accounts describe front-row customers panicking and running out when a film showed a train approaching the camera. Al Jolson's The Jazz Singer (1927) made talkies an overnight reality; by 1930, advertisements read "100% talking." Weekly movie attendance as a percentage of population reached 73% in 1929 and stayed above 60% through 1948. Four of the American Film Institute's top ten movies of the 20th century were made between 1939 and 1942.

Gordon makes a claim about cultural significance that deserves serious engagement: by some measures, the information and entertainment advances of the 1870 to 1940 period made more cultural difference than those of the postwar era. The transition from isolation — from a rural farm with no newspaper, no telegraph, no telephone, no radio — to full connection with national culture, politics, and entertainment happened in this period. What followed was deeper penetration and improved quality of the same basic services, not a comparable qualitative leap. A farm family in 1940 could listen to the same radio broadcasts as a family in Manhattan. No subsequent communications technology has produced an equivalent democratization of access.

The telegraph also reshaped commerce and finance in ways that permanently altered the structure of American capitalism. Alexander Field estimates the telegraph contributed approximately $1 billion in social benefit as of 1890 — roughly 7% of 1890 nominal GDP — primarily by allowing single-track instead of double-track railroad lines. Together, the telegraph and railroad created a single integrated national market for the entire eastern United States by the 1870s, enabling large national multifunctional firms, the rise of the urban department store, and the emergence of the national financial press. The Associated Press, founded in 1846 by New York newspapers sharing costs to cover the Mexican-American War, was a direct telegraph creation.

Chapter 7: Nasty, Brutish, and Short — Illness and Early Death

The most counterintuitive finding in the entire book concerns the pace of health improvement. The annual rate of life expectancy improvement was twice as fast in the first half of the 20th century as in the second half — despite the fact that antibiotics, cancer treatment, organ transplants, CT scanners, and MRI machines all belong to the second half. Life expectancy at birth improved at 0.32 years per year from 1890 to 1950 but only 0.15 years per year from 1950 to 1998. Out of every 1,000 live births, 188 lives were saved by improvements between 1880 and 1950; only 21 additional lives were saved per 1,000 births between 1950 and 2010. The era of the medical miracle was not the era of the fastest mortality improvement.

The explanation is clean water, not medicine. Before 1900, the leading causes of death were infectious diseases transmitted through contaminated water supplies: typhoid, cholera, dysentery, and waterborne diarrheas. Urban households with filtered water went from 0.3% in 1880 to 25% in 1910 to 93% by 1940. David Cutler and Grant Miller estimated that clean water alone explains 50% of the overall mortality reduction from 1900 to 1936 and 75% of the decline in infant mortality. Memphis's 1878 yellow fever epidemic — which killed 10% of the city's population — was the most powerful single argument for massive water infrastructure investment. The cities that invested earliest in filtration and chlorination saw the sharpest mortality drops, in a pattern that cannot be explained by any other variable.

Medicine's contribution to the great mortality decline was surprisingly small. John McKinlay and Sonja McKinlay studied the decline of infectious disease mortality from 1900 to 1973 and found that most of the decline in tuberculosis and influenza mortality had already occurred before medical interventions were available. Their estimate: "3.5 percent probably represents a reasonable upper-limit estimate of the total contribution of medical measures to the decline in mortality since 1900." Virtually all the decline in infectious disease mortality was achieved by 1955 — before medical care spending began its rise from 4% of GDP in the mid-1950s to 17% by 2010. The great irony is that Americans have since spent the equivalent of a small economy on healthcare to produce much smaller improvements than clean water achieved at a fraction of the cost.

"In 1900, nearly 18 percent of males born in the United States died before their first birthday; today, cumulative mortality does not reach 18 percent until age 62." — Economists Kevin Murphy and Robert Topel

The racial dimension of the health gap exposes the mechanisms with brutal clarity. In 1900, life expectancy for nonwhite males was 33 years; for white males, 48 years — a 15-year gap. Jim Crow policy explicitly denied nonwhite neighborhoods clean running water and sanitary sewerage. The differential mortality between Black and white Americans in this period was not primarily genetic or cultural — it was a direct consequence of government-enforced infrastructure inequality. Where Black communities were denied the same water and sewer connections that white communities received, they died at much higher rates from waterborne diseases. The link between infrastructure inequality and health inequality is one of the book's most powerful historical demonstrations.

Medical education in 1870 was a scandal. There were only 120 hospitals in the entire country in 1873. Surgery was reliably fatal — postoperative sepsis killed roughly 50% of major surgery patients. Joseph Lister's antiseptic methods using carbolic acid, developed in Glasgow, were the essential breakthrough; Listerine was named after him in 1879. The most vivid illustration of pre-antiseptic medicine is President Garfield's death in 1881: the assassin's bullet passed through his back and would have been survivable. Physicians probed the wound with unwashed fingers and unsterilized instruments, introduced massive infection, and Garfield died from the subsequent sepsis after 79 days of agony. The 1910 Flexner Report devastated for-profit medical schools — Flexner found that "touted laboratories consisted of a few vagrant test tubes squirreled away in a cigar box; corpses reeked" — and the closure of most for-profit schools dramatically raised standards while restricting supply and raising fees.

The valuation of health improvements changes the entire picture of 20th-century economic progress. William Nordhaus calculated that the value of the improvement in life expectancy from 1900 to 1950 was as large as the growth rate of real GDP per capita from all other reasons combined. Including health capital, the true annual GDP growth rate from 1900 to 1950 was approximately 4.2% per year, compared to the conventional measure of 2.05%. This is not a minor adjustment — it roughly doubles measured progress. And it implies that the great public health investments of the early 20th century — water filtration, sewerage, milk pasteurization, food safety regulation — were among the highest-return investments ever made, producing welfare gains that dwarfed the cost.

Gordon's hierarchy of what drove the health revolution runs in this order: urban sanitation (clean water explaining 75% of infant mortality decline), uncontaminated milk and food regulation, Pasteur's germ theory changing hand-washing and behavioral norms, window screens blocking mosquitoes and flies, the horse-to-automobile transition eliminating horse waste that bred disease vectors, and only then basic medical tools. Hospitals became genuinely useful between 1900 and 1940: 5% of women delivered in hospitals in 1900; 50% of all women and 75% of urban women by 1939; 95% by 1960. The hospital of 1940 was a fundamentally different institution from the hospital of 1870 — transformed from a place to die into a place to recover.

Chapter 8: Working Conditions and the Labor Revolution

Work in 1870 was long, dangerous, and almost entirely physical. The standard industrial work week was ten hours per day, six days per week — 60 hours total. Steel mills in the 1880s averaged 72.6 hours per week. Courts refused to protect workers injured on the job, ruling that employees "assumed the risk" of employment. Women made up only 21% of the labor force in 1870, boys aged 14 to 15 made up 50%, and elderly men worked until they literally could not. By 2009, the average industrial work week had stabilized at 40 hours, where it has remained for 70 years — a threshold that Keynes famously and wrongly predicted in 1931 would fall to 15 hours by the end of the 20th century.

The electrification of manufacturing between 1900 and 1929 was the most important single force reducing both the difficulty and danger of industrial work. Half of the manufacturing productivity surge of the 1920s can be attributed to the diffusion of electric motor capacity into factories. But productivity was only half the story. Electrification also transformed working conditions by eliminating the overhead shafts and leather belting that had driven pre-electric machinery — systems that "swirled dust and grease through the factory atmosphere" and created constant entanglement hazards. Electric motors meant "lighter, cleaner workshops made possible by skylights" and a fundamental improvement in the physical experience of factory work, independent of productivity gains.

The Triangle Shirtwaist Factory fire of March 25, 1911, is the period's most lasting symbol of the cost of unregulated industrial work. The fire killed 148 employees — mostly young immigrant women — in a New York City garment factory. Workers were locked in rooms to prevent theft and unauthorized breaks; scores were found piled against closed, locked doors. The fire produced immediate legislative response in New York State and contributed to the Progressive Era push for factory safety laws. Gordon draws the explicit parallel to the Bangladesh factory fires of 2012 to 2013, where the same combination of locked exits and locked-in workers produced the same outcome, demonstrating that the Triangle disaster reflected not unique American cruelty but the universal economic logic of unregulated employment.

"No other era in human history, either before or since, combined so many elements in which the standard of living increased as quickly and in which the human condition was transformed so completely." — Robert J. Gordon

Real wages grew faster than productivity from 1870 to 1940 — 2.48% per year versus 1.51% — a reversal of the pattern that has held since. The most dramatic single example is Henry Ford's January 5, 1914, announcement of the $5 day — doubling the standard manufacturing wage of $2.04 overnight. Ford's stated motivation was partly to reduce the enormous turnover and absenteeism rates in his assembly plants. His 200-member "Sociological Department" visited the homes of all 13,000 employees to ensure they were spending their new wages appropriately. The episode illustrates the ambiguity of welfare gains in the industrial era: workers were materially far better off, but employers still claimed wide latitude over their private lives.

The high school graduation rate is perhaps the most important single metric of the human capital transformation. In 1910, only 9% of Americans graduated from high school. By 1940, that figure was 51%; by 1970, 76.9%. The growth rate was 14 percentage points per decade from 1910 to 1940, then 8.7 percentage points per decade from 1940 to 1970. After 1970, the progress essentially stopped — the graduation rate has been essentially flat for 50 years. The education revolution of the first half of the 20th century was not a natural progression that continued indefinitely. It was a specific historical achievement that stalled, with profound consequences for both income inequality and economic growth.

Ruth Schwartz Cowan's paradox — that consumer appliances didn't actually reduce total home production hours — deserves attention because it complicates the simple welfare-gain narrative. As washing machines, vacuum cleaners, and electric ranges arrived, standards for cleanliness and meal quality rose to match. Houses that had been cleaned weekly were now cleaned daily. Female home production fell from 50.4 hours per week in 1900 to 31.1 hours by 2005, but male home production rose from 3.7 to 17.3 hours, and the total declined far less than the technological inputs would suggest. The appliances didn't liberate women by reducing housework — they liberated women by enabling them to enter the paid labor force while maintaining equivalent household output, and by redistributing domestic labor somewhat more evenly between men and women.

The labor market transformation from 1870 to 1940 changed the composition of the workforce as profoundly as the hours and conditions. The share of workers in "unpleasant" jobs — agriculture, blue-collar industry, and domestic service — fell from 87.2% in 1870 to 60.4% in 1940, and would continue falling to 21.6% by 2009. Women's labor force participation went from 12% in 1870 to 26% by 1940, on its way to 72% by 2000. Boys aged 14 to 15 went from 50% labor force participation in 1870 to 10% by 1940 as high school expanded. Elderly males aged 65 to 74 went from 88% participation in 1870 to 53% by 1940 — retirement was being invented before Social Security made it financially possible.

Chapter 9: Consumer Credit, Insurance, and the Role of Government

The popular narrative that consumer credit was invented in the 1920s is wrong. Credit was pervasive in rural America from the earliest settlement, with farmers routinely carrying year-long debts at their local general stores, waiting for harvest to settle accounts. What changed in the 1920s was the application of installment credit to durable goods. By 1929, installment financing covered 61% of new cars, 65% of used cars, 80 to 90% of furniture, and 75% of washing machines. GMAC, established in 1919, pioneered modern auto finance. Effective interest rates on 1920s car loans typically ran around 34% annually, though this was not disclosed in the advertising. The purchase of the Model T on a Ford Weekly Purchase Plan — saving $5 per week before receiving the car — failed completely; consumers preferred GMAC's approach of getting the car immediately and paying later.

For agricultural sharecroppers in the South, the credit system was not an opportunity but a trap. Interest rates of 40 to 60% were standard. Unpaid debts accumulated year after year; sharecroppers often legally could not leave their plots without voiding debt obligations. This debt peonage — functionally a continuation of bound labor after Emancipation — affected millions of Black and poor white families in the South and operated with the explicit or implicit support of local legal systems. The same era that produced the Model T and the electric refrigerator for northern urban workers produced debt bondage for southern agricultural workers. The geographic and racial divergence in the benefits of American growth is impossible to understand without this contrast.

Life insurance was one of the most important financial innovations of the period, providing the first systematic mechanism for managing the catastrophic economic risk of early death. Life insurance company assets as a percentage of GDP went from 4.5% in 1875 to 37.0% by 1933. By 1915, roughly 25 to 30% of the population had some form of life insurance coverage. The fraternal insurance plans that dominated early coverage had a fatal structural flaw: they enrolled young healthy members at actuarially cheap rates, failed to build adequate reserves for aging memberships, and eventually collapsed as mortality claims accelerated. Their share of the insurance market fell from 33% to 15% between 1915 and 1925 as commercial companies offering actuarially sound pricing displaced them.

The role of government in the 19th-century American economy is systematically underestimated by the laissez-faire mythology that surrounds the era. Government intervened extensively and consequentially: the Homestead Acts distributed western land; the Morrill Act of 1862 created land-grant colleges that by 1980 enrolled 18% of all U.S. college students and produced 66% of annual PhDs; the Sherman Antitrust Act of 1890 eventually broke up Standard Oil in 1911; Herbert Hoover as Commerce Secretary in the 1920s standardized the sizes of nuts, bolts, automobile tires, and plumbing parts — an obscure act that enabled the Arsenal of Democracy in World War II, because standardized parts could be manufactured by 23 additional companies with no prior industry experience.

"Bismarck figured out this greatest of all economic policy issues in the 1880s, but even now an efficient system of the provision of medical care as a right of citizenship has thus far eluded the American political system." — Robert J. Gordon

The New Deal's lasting institutional contribution was the elimination of bank failures as a routine feature of American economic life. Between 1931 and 1933, 10,000 of the 25,000 banks in the United States had failed. The FDIC, created by the 1933 Glass-Steagall Act, made depositor panics irrational by insuring deposits — and bank failures essentially ceased. Glass-Steagall also decoupled commercial from investment banking; that decoupling was repealed by the Clinton administration in 1999 and "blamed in part for excess financial leverage" leading to the 2007 to 2009 financial crisis. The New Deal's other great institutional innovation, Social Security, was established in 1935 and would eventually reduce the poverty rate among the elderly from 35% in 1959 to 10% by 2003.

The Rural Electrification Administration illustrates how infrastructure investment creates welfare gains that conventional economics systematically ignores. REA-financed electricity arrived on farms that had been lit by kerosene since settlement. One Wyoming ranch woman's description of the day it arrived is among the most evocative passages in the book: "My Day of Days because lights shone where lights had never been, the electric stove radiated heat, the washer turned, and an electric pump freed me from hauling water." The REA didn't just provide electricity — it provided liberation from physical labor that had defined farm life for generations. The economic value of that liberation, multiplied across millions of farms, was enormous. None of it appears in any investment return calculation.

The mortgage market of the 1920s illustrates both the possibilities and the dangers of financial innovation applied to housing. Mortgage debt as a share of nominal GDP surged from 16% in 1919 to 41% by 1930 as homeownership approached 50% nationwide. But the mortgages of the 1920s were typically five-year balloons requiring full refinancing at maturity — with no long-term fixed-rate option. When the Depression struck and banks stopped lending, these balloon mortgages could not be refinanced, and foreclosures swept through urban neighborhoods. New construction fell to near zero and stayed there through World War II. The FHA and the 30-year fixed-rate mortgage, New Deal innovations, resolved the structural flaw in the 1920s system by creating the financing instrument that would fuel the postwar suburban boom.

Entr'acte: The Midcentury Shift from Revolution to Evolution

Gordon places an interlude between the two halves of his historical survey to make explicit what the data have been implying throughout: the great transformation was essentially complete by 1940. The home was networked. The automobile was universal. Life expectancy had improved more in the previous fifty years than in the previous five hundred. The question the second half of the book addresses is not whether progress continued after 1940 — it clearly did — but whether it could possibly match the pace of the first half. The answer is no, and this interlude explains why in structural terms.

The critical distinction is scope. Industrial Revolution #2 — electricity, internal combustion engine, chemicals, plumbing, sanitation — addressed virtually the entire span of human needs. Food: refrigeration, pasteurization, the supermarket. Clothing: industrial production, synthetic fabrics. Shelter: central heating, electric light, running water, the elevator. Health: clean water, germ theory, vaccines, antibiotics. Transportation: railroads, automobiles, early air travel. Communication: telegraph, telephone, radio, cinema. IR #3 — computers, the internet, smartphones — revolutionized information, communication, and entertainment. That is roughly 7% of GDP in 2014. "This single fact, the narrowness of the effects of IR #3, is enough to explain why growth slowed after 1970."

The problem with conventional GDP measurement as a welfare gauge is nowhere more apparent than in this comparison. Real GDP does not value the transition from kerosene danger to electric safety. It does not capture the 73% quality-adjusted price drop of the Model T between 1910 and 1923. It does not measure the difference between a silent nickelodeon flickering in a storefront and a color sound film in an air-conditioned palace. It completely misses the reduction of infant mortality from 22% of births in 1890 to 1% by 1950 — which Nordhaus estimates created more welfare value than all other sources of consumer welfare combined. It doesn't account for the change from 72-hour steel mill weeks "accompanied by heat, sweat, and danger" to 40-hour weeks in climate-controlled offices.

The TFP chronology tells the story with precision. Growth ran at 2.72%/year from 1953 to 1964, fell to 1.44%/year from 1977 to 1994, briefly revived to 2.05%/year from 1995 to 2004 during the internet investment boom, then collapsed to 0.6%/year through end of 2014. By 2013 to 2014, trend output per person growth had fallen to essentially zero. The brief 1995 to 2004 revival — driven by the adoption of the web, e-commerce, and office computing — lasted only ten years before fading back to the post-1970 baseline. Paul David's dynamo analogy from 1990 predicted exactly this kind of delayed payoff and then tapering: the question is whether the internet's structural change to the economy was as broad and deep as electrification's, and the TFP data increasingly say no.

Chapter 10: Food, Clothing, and Housing, 1940–2015

By 1940, the food system had already accomplished its most important structural transformations. Clarence Saunders had opened the first self-service grocery under the Piggly-Wiggly name in 1916; the word "supermarket" entered the language in 1933; the first shopping cart appeared in 1937. Supermarket share of total food sales went from 28% in 1946 to 69% by 1963 as smaller stores were rapidly displaced. The number of items stocked in a typical supermarket went from 300 to 600 in the 1920s to 17,500 by 1985. These were quantitative extensions of the self-service, chain-store model that had already been established. The fundamental revolution — the shift from local, seasonal, preserved-at-home food to nationally distributed, industrially processed, commercially refrigerated food — had happened before 1940.

The postwar food story contains genuine advances alongside some regressions. Frozen foods were the only meaningfully new food technology — Birdseye's 1912 discovery finally reaching mass application after 1950 when refrigerators acquired substantial freezer compartments. The poultry-to-red-meat ratio went from 14% in 1940 to 89% by 2010, reflecting both cost reduction in chicken production and growing awareness of heart disease risks from red meat. Food eaten away from home as a share of food at home went from 20% in 1929 to 37% in 1970 to 70% by 2013. But after a century of stability from 1870 to 1970, total daily calorie consumption increased more than 20% after 1970, converting a lean and fit America into the most obese of all developed countries. U.S. obesity rates in 2000 reached 27%; no other of 23 countries studied exceeded 20%. The inequality dimension is stark: "Poor children sit in idleness in front of TV screens and eat cheap fast foods. Upper-middle-class children eat kale salads before their trip to participate on the soccer team."

The postwar appliance story is one of quality improvement, reliability improvement, and energy efficiency — not of new appliances. Gordon's 1990 study using Consumer Reports evaluations from 1938 to 1986 traces these improvements with precision. Refrigerators showed quality-adjusted price declines of –1.66% per year from 1952 to 1983, cumulating to 76% over 34 years — all missed by the CPI because it didn't measure energy efficiency improvements. Early postwar models suffered from "slipping belts, poor piping and shaft seals, trouble with expansion valves, and thermostat trouble." By 1971, Consumer Reports noted that quality had converged at a high level — and after 1971, there were no further quality comments, indicating saturation. Room air conditioners saw energy efficiency improve by two-thirds between 1953 and 1983. The microwave oven was the only genuinely new appliance introduced after 1950; it showed the fastest quality-adjusted price decline of any appliance, at –6.7% per year.

The postwar housing story divides sharply into two phases. From 1945 to 1975, the great appliance adoption brought central air conditioning, automatic washers and dryers, dishwashers, and color television to the mass market. The modern suburban house reached near-saturation of basic amenities by the mid-1970s. Levittown on Long Island — 80,000 people eventually housed; a four-room Cape Cod selling at $7,990 in 1949, often requiring no down payment for GI Bill veterans — was the template. Interior size was only 750 square feet, roughly a third of the median new house built after 1995. The postwar houses were small and well-equipped. Their successors were large and no more equipped.

The suburbanization that followed WWII differed from European postwar development in five ways that explain American sprawl. First, U.S. population more than doubled from 1950 to 2015 while the UK grew only 15% and Germany 18%. Second, the U.S. had essentially no zoning equivalent to British green belts. Third, American car diffusion was further advanced than anywhere else. Fourth, government policy actively subsidized urban expressways while starving public transit. Fifth, the mortgage interest deduction — "the mansion subsidy" — grew with house size, incentivizing larger rather than more conveniently located homes. The productivity paradox of American sprawl: big-box stores near suburban interstate junctions generate genuine economies of scale. European land-use regulations protecting pedestrian inner-city districts impose "substantial costs in reducing economy-wide productivity and real output per capita."

Urban decay in the Rust Belt was the shadow side of suburban expansion. From 1950 to 2010, Chicago lost 25.5% of its city population, Philadelphia 26.3%, Cleveland 56.1%, Detroit 61.4%, St. Louis 62.7%. Parts of central Cleveland, Detroit, and St. Louis came to "resemble ghost towns." During the 1940s and 1950s, the Great Migration brought southern Black sharecroppers north in enormous numbers — as many as 2,000 migrants per week arrived at the Illinois Central Station in Chicago in the late 1940s, recruited by Ford and other war industries desperate for any available labor. They found good manufacturing jobs during the great age of U.S. manufacturing from 1941 to 1972, then lost them as globalization eliminated the unionized blue-collar employment that had been the foundation of northern prosperity.

The Federal Housing Authority's "red-lining" policy made northern segregation as systematic as southern Jim Crow. FHA designated any neighborhood where Black families moved in as "unstable," which triggered denial of conventional mortgages, which triggered white flight, which confirmed instability — a self-fulfilling cycle of disinvestment. Most Black families buying homes in Chicago in the 1950s relied on "contract financing" — inflated purchase prices on high-interest contracts that did not permit building home equity. The federal government that passed the Civil Rights Act of 1964 was simultaneously sustaining urban segregation through its housing finance policy. The consequence — concentrated poverty, underfunded schools, crumbling infrastructure — persisted for generations.

Chapter 11: Automobiles and Air Travel, 1940–2015

The motor vehicle's great transformation of American society was essentially complete by 1970. Motor vehicles per 100 households went from 89 in 1929 to 113 in 1950 to 207 by 2010, but the qualitative change — the replacement of horse and transit by automobile as the primary mode of personal transportation — had already happened. Vehicle miles per person grew at 13.5% per year from 1919 to 1929, at 8% from 1950 to 1980, and at only 1.1% from 1980 to 2012. Vehicle miles in 2012 were 6.4% lower than in 2004 — no higher than in 1997. The automobile culture had matured to saturation.

The Model T's affordability relative to income is a fact that most people find surprising. The 1923 Model T cost 11% of per-household annual consumption — roughly six weeks of income. A 1950 Plymouth cost 33% of per-household annual consumption. A typical 2014 car costs even more in income-relative terms. The Model T was twice as affordable relative to incomes as the average vehicle today. The great democratization of the automobile happened in the 1910s and 1920s, not in the 1950s or since. Quality improvements since 1950 are real and substantial — better safety, reliability, fuel economy, electronics — but they came at higher income-relative prices, not lower ones.

The Interstate Highway System, concentrated in the period from 1958 to 1972, was among the most productive public investments in American history. Of 35 industries examined, all but three showed significant cost reductions attributable to interstate access. Interstate spending contributed roughly 31% of total productivity gain in the 1950s, 25% in the 1960s, and only 7% in the 1980s as the network neared completion. The economy-wide TFP slowdown after 1972 partly reflects the end of the highway construction productivity bonus. Total ultimate cost of the system: $450 billion in 2009 dollars. Few public investments in any era have generated returns at this scale.

"The golden age of air travel was the 1950s, not the 2010s. A round-trip New York to Los Angeles sleeper flight in 1936 cost $285 in 1936 dollars — almost exactly the same as today's business class fare in nominal terms. And the total travel time was the same: then and now, roughly three hours and fifty minutes from curb to curb." — Robert J. Gordon

Air travel followed a trajectory of rapid improvement followed by stagnation and regression that is, in retrospect, a template for the entire post-1970 story. The period from 1926 to 1936 was the greatest decade of commercial aircraft advances in history. The DC-3 (first flight June 26, 1936) carried 21 passengers across the continent in 15 hours. The Boeing 707 jet, entering service in 1958, cut Los Angeles to New York to 4.8 hours. Since then — in 60 years of technological development — the trip now takes 5.6 hours, because fuel-saving speeds and runway congestion have reversed the jet age's speed advantage. The annual rate of elapsed-time improvement for air travel since 1958 is negative 0.2% per year. Flying is literally slower than it was in the Kennedy administration.

The deregulation of 1978 is often cited as a success story of reduced airfares, but the reality is more complex. Real prices declined most rapidly from 1940 to 1960 — at 8.1% per year — before the jet age and before deregulation. After 1980, real average prices showed no decline at all. Deregulation increased price variance — cheaper for advance purchases, much more expensive for business travel — without reducing the average. The post-9/11 security apparatus wastes an estimated 200 million hours per year in passenger time, equivalent to roughly $8 billion annually in lost economic value. Gordon's assessment is blunt: the actual security failure of 9/11 was the flimsy cockpit door, which was fixed within days of the attacks. Everything else is security theater consuming enormous resources to defend against threats that rational terrorists have abandoned.

Chapter 12: Entertainment and Communications, 1940–2015

Television was the fastest-diffusing major consumer technology in American history. Household penetration went from 9% in 1950 to 64.5% in 1955 — a diffusion rate that exceeded even the automobile and radio. The cultural impact was immediate and total. On January 19, 1953, the CBS broadcast of Lucy Ricardo having a baby drew 68.8% of all the TV sets in the country — more Americans watched that episode than watched Eisenhower's inauguration the following morning. Milton Berle's Texaco Star Theater was so dominant that Detroit reservoir pressure dropped sharply when the show ended, because the entire city went to the bathroom at once. Television didn't just entertain — it synchronized American culture in a way that nothing since radio had achieved.

The political consequences of television were profound and largely unplanned. The 1960 Nixon-Kennedy debate established the visual primacy of television in American politics: viewers who watched overwhelmingly picked Kennedy as the winner while radio listeners gave it to Nixon. On March 7, 1965, television broadcast the Selma to Montgomery march and the police attacks on peaceful protesters — footage that is widely credited with directly enabling the Voting Rights Act of 1965. Television didn't merely report politics; it changed which arguments were persuasive and which politicians were viable. This is a welfare effect that has no dollar value and no place in GDP.

The measurement problem in consumer electronics is extreme. A 1950 9-inch black-and-white RCA table model television cost $350. A 2014 40-inch HD LED color television cost $418. The true annual price decline from 1952 to 1983 was –4.3% per year; the CPI recorded only –1.0% per year. The cumulative unmeasured consumer surplus from TV from 1952 to 1983 was 278% of what was actually spent on sets — meaning for every dollar Americans spent on televisions, they received $3.78 worth of value beyond the purchase price. For every dollar of GDP spending on TV sets from 1952 to 1999, quality improvements delivered five dollars of unmeasured benefits. The VCR showed an even sharper pattern: prices fell from $1,200 in 1978 to under $250 by 1987, but the CPI didn't include VCRs until 1987 — all price decline before that date is completely absent from any price index.

"A pedicure is a pedicure regardless of whether the customer is reading a Kindle or surfing the web on a smartphone. The great services sector — which now accounts for two-thirds of consumer spending — is largely untouched by digital transformation." — Robert J. Gordon

The recorded music industry went through multiple format revolutions after 1980, each producing quality improvements that GDP barely captured. The compact disc (1982) outsold vinyl by 1988 and cassettes by 1991, peaking at 95% of sales in 2002. The iPod (2001) replaced the portable CD player. Spotify now offers 30 million songs for $10 per month — less than one CD per year. The quality improvement in music access and portability from 1980 to 2015 was massive and real, and the price signal was approximately zero or negative (music became cheaper, not more expensive). This creates a genuine paradox for welfare measurement: the most dramatic improvements in entertainment quality since 1940 appear in GDP as economic contraction rather than growth, because prices fell even as quality soared.

Long-distance telephone communication followed a path of steady and eventually total commoditization. The first AT&T Direct Distance Dialing, introduced in 1951, allowed the Mayor of Englewood, New Jersey, to reach the Mayor of Alameda, California, in 18 seconds — compared to the 23 minutes required with five operators in 1915. By 2013, 91% of American adults had cell phones, and the incremental cost of a call had fallen to approximately zero. The telephone had evolved from a luxury item accessible to 45% of households in 1929 to a universal utility that cost essentially nothing per use. The welfare gain was enormous; the GDP signal was declining, not rising.

Chapter 13: Computers and the Internet

The history of computing is a history of almost incomprehensible performance improvement compressed into six decades. The ENIAC in 1946 weighed 30 short tons, occupied 1,800 square feet, consumed 175 kilowatts — "when ENIAC was turned on, the lights in Philadelphia dimmed." A 2014 Walmart Lenovo laptop at $449 had 750 times the memory and 1,000 times the speed of the Cray-1 supercomputer that cost $8.8 million in 1976. Computing performance relative to price improved at 44% per year from 1976 to 2014. Moore's Law — the doubling of transistor density roughly every two years — actually accelerated to 60.1% per year from 1997 to 2006, then collapsed to 11.5% per year from 2006 to 2014. "Moore's Law has gone off the rails" since 2006, reflecting fundamental physical limits in silicon transistor miniaturization.

The key structural constraint on ICT's economic impact is its share of GDP. ICT spending in 2014 was only 7% of GDP. Even at 20% annual performance gains — which are no longer being achieved — a 7% GDP share generates at most 1.4 percentage points of GDP growth. The other 93% of the economy — food, clothing, housing, health, transportation, personal services — produces no comparable performance improvement. A haircut in 2015 takes the same time and requires the same skilled human labor as a haircut in 1955. A physical education class occupies the same gymnasium with the same ratio of teachers to students. A road trip still requires a driver in the seat. ICT is transformative within its domain, but its domain is not the economy.

The commercial internet unfolded with extraordinary speed. The World Wide Web appeared in December 1991. Mosaic — the first graphical browser and the true killer app of the internet age — launched in September 1993. Windows 95 arrived in August 1995; one observer captured the moment: "The Internet is slow, superficial, chaotic, nerdy, hostile, and largely a waste of time. You just gotta try it." Amazon was founded in 1994; Google in 1998; Wikipedia in 2001; iTunes in 2001; Facebook in 2004. All of these transformative platforms were more than a decade old when Gordon wrote, and their structural economic effects — on retail, media, advertising, information search — had been substantially realized by 2005.

Gordon's diagnosis of the post-2005 stasis is pointed. "Throughout the world, the equipment used in office work and the productivity of office employees closely resembles that of a decade ago." The smartphone, introduced in 2007, and the tablet, in 2010, produced no measurable response in TFP growth statistics whatsoever. These devices improved individual user experience — more convenient access to the same applications — without transforming how organizations produce output. The proportion of workers visiting recreational websites during workday hours may actually have lowered office productivity by providing constant entertainment alternatives to work. The productivity data are unambiguous: labor productivity grew at only 0.5% per year in the five years ending Q2 2015, compared to 2.3% per year in 1994 to 2004.

The firm-level evidence reinforces the aggregate picture. Young firms — those five years old or younger — fell from 14.6% of all firms in 1978 to 8.3% by 2011, nearly halving, and this decline began well before the 2008 financial crisis. The share of people under 30 owning stakes in private companies fell from 10.6% in 1989 to 3.6% by 2014 — a collapse in entrepreneurial activity that directly contradicts the Silicon Valley narrative of tech-driven dynamism. Net investment — new investment minus depreciation — ran at a 3.2% long-run average but fell to 1.0% in 2013, less than a third of historical norms. These are not indicators of an economy being energized by transformative technology; they are indicators of an economy slowing down.

Chapter 14: Health and Medicine, 1940–2015

By 1945, two-thirds of all deaths in the United States came from chronic diseases — heart disease, cancer, stroke — a complete reversal from 1920, when two-thirds of deaths came from infectious diseases. The great 20th-century health transition was essentially complete. Life expectancy at birth had gained 25 years from 1880 to 1950. The gain from 1950 to 2008 was only 9 years. The era of penicillin, bypass surgery, cancer treatment, and MRI scanners produced, in aggregate, less improvement in life expectancy than the era of clean water and pasteurized milk.

The FDA drug approval record tells a story of declining pharmaceutical productivity. The FDA approved 50% more new drugs in the two decades from 1940 to 1960 than in the next 51 years combined. Penicillin — discovered by Alexander Fleming in 1928, available in American pharmacies by summer 1945 at 6.5 cents per dose — cured pneumonia, rheumatic fever, and syphilis, which had been major causes of death and disability. The Salk polio vaccine of 1955 was tested in the largest clinical trial in history, immunizing more than 400,000 children; by the late 1960s, polio had been entirely eliminated from the United States. The birth control pill, approved by the FDA in 1960, has been called "after penicillin the most important pharmaceutical development of the 20th century" — it ended the baby boom, enabled women to plan careers, and fundamentally altered the economics of female labor force participation.

"It's like a Greek tragedy. Everybody plays his individual part to perfection — the surgeon, the nursing staff, the diagnostic equipment manufacturers — and the total just doesn't work." — An oncologist on cancer treatment, cited by Gordon

Cancer is the starkest example of expensive but modest progress. The cancer mortality rate rose from 64 per 100,000 in 1900 to 200.5 per 100,000 in 2000 — a tripling. The five-year cancer survival rate improved from approximately 50% in 1971 to 63% by 2004 despite roughly $200 billion spent on cancer research over those decades. Heart disease followed a more successful trajectory: mortality peaked in 1963 at 55% of all deaths, then declined with better blood pressure control, emergency rooms, and coronary care units. But even cardiac progress has slowed: CHD mortality declined at roughly 4% per year in the late 1970s and only 0.5% per year in the 2000s.

Healthcare cost growth is the system's most serious failure. Healthcare expenditures as a percentage of GNP went from 4.5% in 1950 to 9.4% in 1980 to 17.7% by 2010. The U.S. spends 55% more than Canada per capita on healthcare and has 2.5 years shorter life expectancy. Detailed comparison attributes U.S. excess spending: 39% to higher administrative costs, 31% to higher physician incomes. Nearly a sixth of the U.S. population still lacked health insurance when Gordon wrote. The book's indictment is precise: Bismarck solved this problem in the 1880s. Germany has had universal healthcare for 140 years. The United States has spent four generations and trillions of dollars arriving at a system worse on most measures than those of its peer countries.

The inequality in health outcomes mirrors the inequality in income with disturbing precision. White women without high school diplomas lost 5 years of life expectancy between 1990 and 2008 — "a measure of decline last seen among Russians after the fall of the Soviet Union." This is not a marginal statistical artifact; it is a reversal of life expectancy for a large demographic group in a wealthy country during a period of overall prosperity. The mechanisms are well-documented: stress from poverty and inequality, worse access to both preventive and acute care, dietary deficiencies, environmental exposures, and the despair that economist Anne Case and Angus Deaton would later document as "deaths of despair" from suicide, drug overdose, and alcoholism concentrated among non-college-educated white Americans.

Chapter 15: Work, Youth, and Retirement, 1940–2015

The feminization of the American labor force was the most important structural change in the postwar economy, and it happened slowly, against fierce institutional resistance. Women were 6% of doctors and 3% of lawyers in 1960. A medical school dean said in 1961: "Hell yes, we have a quota. We don't want them here." Sandra Day O'Connor graduated third in her class from Stanford Law School and was offered positions by only one firm — as a legal secretary. Women's prime-age labor force participation rate went from 34.9% in 1948 to a peak of 76.8% in 1999, then fell back to 73.9% by 2014. Female wages as a percentage of male wages went from 58.8% in 1975 to 77.4% by 2010 — meaningful progress that stopped well short of equality.

Manufacturing's decline from 30% of the labor force in 1953 to under 10% by 2015 is the central fact of postwar labor economics. The $20-per-hour manufacturing wage allowed millions of Americans with no more than high school education to enter the middle class — to buy houses, save for retirement, send children to college. That wage structure is "on its way to extinction" (Uchitelle). The Great Compression from 1945 to 1975 — when a unionized blue-collar worker lived next door to an accountant or teacher in roughly equivalent material comfort — was both historically unprecedented and apparently unrepeatable. Between 1970 and 1998, average income in the top 0.01% jumped from 50 to 250 times the economy-wide average, replacing the Great Compression with the Great Divergence.

"The book's title has become a statement of fact. Since 2000, labor productivity growth has slowed, and hours worked per person have fallen — leaving output per person in genuine decline. The Rise and Fall of American Growth is no longer a historical judgment. It is a description of what is happening now." — Robert J. Gordon

Claudia Goldin's analysis of the remaining gender wage gap identifies the mechanism with precision. Occupations where one specific person must always be available — trial lawyers, M&A bankers, surgeons, investment managers — penalize time flexibility severely. A client who needs a lawyer at 11 pm on a Sunday will pay enormous premiums for the specific person they know and trust. Occupations where any qualified professional can substitute — pharmacists, physicians in group practices, veterinarians — generate far smaller penalties for time flexibility. The gender wage gap is not, primarily, about discrimination; it is about the structure of jobs that demand complete temporal availability, which disproportionately conflict with caregiving responsibilities that women still carry disproportionately.

The education picture after 1970 is one of stagnation layered on inequality. The high school graduation rate peaked at 76.9% in 1970 and then fell — actually fell — to 74% by 2000, before partial recovery. The U.S. college completion rank fell from 1st in the world in 2000 to no better than 12th. University costs rose at more than triple the overall inflation rate since 1972. State funding for higher education declined by a third from 2001 to 2012. Total student debt reached $1.2 trillion. The underemployment rate for 22-year-old college graduates was 56%. The most pernicious aspect of the education inequality is its self-perpetuation: college completion among top-quartile income households rose from 40% in 1970 to 77% by 2013, while completion for bottom-quartile households rose from only 6% to 9%. The educational elite is becoming increasingly self-reproducing.

A key empirical finding about post-1970 output per person resolves what had appeared to be a puzzle. Labor productivity growth slowed significantly after 1970 — yet output per person did not slow by as much until after 2000. The explanation: women entering the labor force in the 1970s through 1990s raised hours worked per person even as productivity per hour stagnated. Women's entry was a one-time buffer. Since 2000, both labor productivity growth and hours worked per person have declined sharply together. The two drivers of output per person — how much each worker produces and how many people work — are both deteriorating simultaneously for the first time since the 1930s.

Chapter 16: The Great Leap Forward — 1920s to 1950s

The productivity explosion of the 1928 to 1950 period is one of the most puzzling phenomena in all of economic history, and Gordon dedicates his most analytical chapter to explaining it. The puzzle: if TFP growth from 1870 to 1928 had simply continued at its historical rate, output per hour in 1950 would have been 52% higher than in 1928. The actual increase was 99% — nearly double the expected pace. The puzzle deepens when you note that the great inventions had already reached most urban households by 1928: electricity, gas, telephone, running water, and sewers were all connected; 93% of Iowa farmers owned motor vehicles. What caused productivity to surge at precisely the moment when the visible transformation seemed complete?

TFP growth rate by decade, 1890–2014. The 1940–50 bar represents the "Great Leap Forward." Source: Gordon's analysis of Bureau of Labor Statistics data.

The New Deal's most underappreciated contribution to productivity was its effect on real wages. The Wagner Act (1935) made union organizing substantially easier; the Fair Labor Standards Act (1938) codified the 40-hour week with mandatory overtime pay. Together these drove up real wages sharply in the late 1930s. Higher wages forced firms to substitute capital for labor — to invest in machines that would do what previously required humans. Private equipment investment surged: the ratio of equipment investment to equipment capital rose from 13.6% in 1928 to 17.1% by 1941. This capital deepening raised labor productivity permanently, independent of any subsequent technology adoption. The mechanism was paradoxical: protective labor legislation intended to benefit workers also, through its pressure on firms, drove a productivity improvement that benefited the whole economy.

World War II's contribution is the most novel and counterintuitive of Gordon's arguments. Military spending in 1944 was equivalent to 80% of the entire 1939 economy. The federal government financed new productive equipment from 1941 to 1945 equal to roughly 50% of the privately owned equipment stock that existed in 1941 — and it was all more modern than the existing capital. The number of machine tools in the United States doubled from 1940 to 1945. The government-financed "Big Inch" and "Little Big Inch" petroleum pipelines, 1,300 miles from Texas to New Jersey, were built in 1942 to 1944 to move oil safely past German submarines. Both are still in use today. Traditional productivity studies compared private output to private capital input — systematically omitting government-financed capital operated by private firms, creating an apparent productivity miracle where the true cause was government investment.

Learning by doing — the productivity gains from accumulated production experience — was perhaps the most powerful wartime mechanism. Kaiser shipyards began building Liberty freighters on an eight-month completion schedule in 1942. By 1943, completion time was a few weeks. In a contest between two yards, an entire ship was assembled from prefabricated parts in four days, made possible by more than 250 employee-suggested efficiency improvements. Henry Ford's government-financed Willow Run factory built the first B-24 bomber in May 1942; production reached 75 per month by February 1943, 150 by November 1943, and peaked at 432 per month — essentially one per hour — in August 1944. Workers employed 50,000 people at peak. Cost reductions spread through the entire war economy: Pontiac cut production costs on its Oerlikon gun by 23%; Chrysler cut radar system costs by 57% in eight months; gyrocompass costs fell to 55% of original.

The breadth of wartime manufacturing involvement is crucial to understanding the permanence of the learning gains. Jewelry makers produced artillery fuses. Lawn mower manufacturers made shrapnel shells. Postal meter manufacturers made bomb mechanisms. Shoe makers made helmet liners. Vacuum cleaner makers made gas mask parts. Wheelbarrow makers made ammunition carts. Every postwar consumer goods manufacturer had been deeply embedded in military production for four years. The efficiency lessons — in materials handling, in quality control, in process engineering — translated directly into the postwar consumer goods boom. The refrigerators, washing machines, and automobiles of 1946 were built by companies and workers with wartime production experience that had no peacetime parallel.

The closed economy that resulted from the restrictive immigration acts of 1921 and 1924, combined with the Ford-McCumber (1922) and Smoot-Hawley (1930) tariffs, had multiple effects on the Great Leap. Annual immigration fell from 1.0% of population per year in 1909 to 1913 to 0.25% from 1925 to 1929. Reduced labor competition made union organizing easier, supporting the wage pressure that drove capital-labor substitution. High tariffs forced all available innovation into U.S.-based factories without the option of offshoring. The wartime income compression — the Great Compression — owed partly to protection of workers from immigrant and import competition. The same closed-economy conditions that economists today regard as harmful to efficiency may have contributed, in a very specific historical context, to the productivity miracle Gordon is explaining.

The innovations of the 1930s themselves deserve recognition as a direct cause of the productivity leap. Kleinknecht counted the 1930s as the decade with the greatest number of important inventions. The plastics explosion of that decade alone was extraordinary: seven new plastics were introduced between 1933 and 1939 — polyvinylidene chloride, low-density polyethylene, acrylic methacrylate, polyurethanes, polystyrene, Teflon, nylon, and neoprene. Chemical innovations produced coatings that doubled railroad tie life and quick-drying lacquers that cut car painting from weeks to hours. The east Texas oil field discovery in October 1930 — "the largest and most prolific oil reservoir in the contiguous United States" — made cheap fuel available precisely when motor vehicle adoption was accelerating. Chain stores developed self-service in the 1930s, cutting the employees needed to run a store by half or more. The Great Leap Forward was not a miracle; it was the convergence of multiple independent causes at a single historical moment.

Chapter 17: Can the Future Match the Great Inventions?

Peter Thiel's epigraph sets the chapter's tone: "We wanted flying cars; instead we got 140 characters." The innovation optimists who predict that artificial intelligence, robotics, driverless cars, and the internet of things will replicate the productivity miracle of the Special Century face a simple empirical problem: TFP growth from 1970 to 1994 averaged only 0.57% per year; from 1994 to 2004, the period of maximum internet investment, it reached only 1.03% per year; from 2004 to 2014, it fell to 0.40% per year. All three intervals fall far below the 1.89% per year of 1920 to 1970. The "third industrial revolution revival" of the internet era lasted exactly ten years and then dissipated.

Gordon's resolution of the Solow Paradox — the famous observation that computers are everywhere except in productivity statistics — is that computers are not, in fact, everywhere. "We don't eat computers or wear them or drive to work in them or let them cut our hair." ICT spending is only 7% of GDP. The other 93% — food preparation, housing construction, medical care, education, personal services, physical transportation — produces no comparable performance improvement from computing. A pedicure requires the same human time and skill as in 1955. A construction site still needs workers who pour concrete and swing hammers. A long-haul truck still needs a driver watching the road. Technology optimists focus on where change is happening and extrapolate to the whole economy, but the sectors where change is slowest are also the largest sectors.

The specific innovations most often cited as transformative collapse on inspection. Robots at competition fall "every which way." Amazon's Kiva robots in warehouses do not touch merchandise — they lift shelves and bring them to human packers who still do the fundamental work. ATMs created more bank branches, not fewer, because they reduced the cost of branch operation below the break-even threshold, enabling expansion into locations that previously couldn't support a teller. Google's driverless car can navigate only on roads that have been mapped in detail; any deviation causes the software to blank out. These are impressive incremental achievements. They are not the electric motor, the automobile, or the antibiotic.

"We wanted flying cars; instead we got 140 characters." — Peter Thiel, epigraph to Chapter 17

The office work comparison is particularly damaging to the transformation narrative. "Throughout the world, the equipment used in office work and the productivity of office employees closely resembles that of a decade ago." The word processor replaced the typewriter once, in the 1980s. Email replaced postal mail once, in the 1990s. The web replaced physical reference materials once, in the 2000s. Each of these was a genuine one-time transition that generated real productivity improvements. But the smartphone, introduced in 2007, did not produce a measurable productivity improvement in office work. Instead, research suggests that 90% of workers visit recreational websites during the workday, and that smartphone notifications fragment concentration in ways that may impose a net productivity cost. The office of 2015 is not more productive per hour than the office of 2005.

The decline in business dynamism compounds the innovation story. Young firms — those five years old or younger — fell from 14.6% of all firms in 1978 to 8.3% by 2011, and this collapse began well before the 2008 financial crisis. The share of people under 30 owning stakes in private companies fell from 10.6% in 1989 to 3.6% by 2014. Net investment — real investment minus depreciation — ran at 3.2% of GDP as a long-run average but fell to 1.0% in 2013. The venture capital and startup culture celebrated in the financial press affects a narrow slice of the technology sector. The aggregate data on investment, firm creation, and entrepreneurship tell a story of declining dynamism that is hard to reconcile with a narrative of transformative technological change.

Chapter 18: Inequality and the Four Headwinds

Even if productivity growth were to recover to its 1920 to 1970 pace, four structural headwinds would prevent that growth from translating into rising living standards for most Americans. Gordon presents them as an arithmetic identity: output per person growth, minus the demographic drag from aging, minus the inequality drag from concentration of gains at the top, minus the fiscal drag from entitlement obligations, yields real median disposable income growth. Each headwind subtracts from the others' effects mechanically. Together they convert historically remarkable innovation into near-zero income growth for the median household.

Gordon's headwinds arithmetic: even with optimistic labor productivity growth, structural drags reduce median real income growth to near zero through 2040.

The inequality headwind is the largest and most clearly documented. Piketty and Saez identify three distinct eras in American income distribution. From 1917 to 1948, incomes became more equal — the bottom 90% grew at 1.43%/year while the top 10% grew at only 0.58%. From 1948 to 1972, all groups grew at roughly the same rate — the golden age. From 1972 to 2013, the Great Divergence: average real income in the bottom 90% was actually lower in 2013 than in 1972, peaking at $37,053 in 2000 and falling 15% to $31,652 by 2013. The top 10% average income doubled from $161,000 in 1972 to $324,000 by 2007. CEO pay went from 20 times the average worker wage in 1973 to 257 times by 2013. The Target CEO received a $47 million retirement package after a credit card data breach — roughly 1,000 times the average Target worker's 401(k) balance.

The causes of the Great Divergence are well-documented and mutually reinforcing. Union membership fell from 27% of workers in 1973 to 13% by 2011; the decline in collective bargaining power directly reduced the share of productivity gains going to workers. Import share of GDP went from 5.4% in 1970 to 16.5% by 2014; David Autor and colleagues calculate that China imports alone accounted for roughly a quarter of the decline in manufacturing employment from 1990 to 2007. The minimum wage in constant 2011 dollars fell from $8.38 in 1979 to $4.68 by 2006. Automation destroyed routine jobs in manufacturing and clerical work while creating high-skill jobs and service jobs, hollowing out the middle of the income distribution. Each of these forces operated independently; together they restructured the wage distribution in ways that may be difficult or impossible to reverse.

The prison statistics are among the most damning in the book. The share of white male high school dropouts who had been in prison rose from 3.8% in 1979 to 28.0% by 2009. For Black male high school dropouts, the figures went from 14.7% to 68.0% — two-thirds of all Black male high school dropouts spent at least one spell in prison by age 40. The United States incarcerates at a rate 8 to 10 times higher than the largest European countries, at an annual cost of $74 billion, and 60% of released prisoners are unemployed one year later. Incarceration at this scale is not primarily a criminal justice phenomenon; it is an economic and social one — the consequence of mass unemployment, destroyed family structures, inadequate education, and the disappearance of manufacturing jobs that once provided livable wages to workers without college degrees.

Charles Murray's Fishtown data — tracking the bottom third of the white population — shows the social breakdown in its starkest form. The share of children born to mothers aged 40 who were living with both biological parents fell from 95% in 1960 to 34% by 2010. This collapse in family stability is not primarily a cultural phenomenon in the sense of values or choices; it is a structural consequence of male wage stagnation, mass incarceration, and the disappearance of the stable employment that historically made marriage an economically rational and socially supported institution.

The four headwinds together produce Gordon's central forecast. Optimistic labor productivity growth of 1.20%/year, minus the demographic drag of 0.40%/year, yields 0.80% output per person. Subtract the 0.43%/year inequality headwind — the gap between median and mean income growth — and add the 0.10%/year fiscal headwind from rising entitlement costs, and real median disposable income grows at only 0.3%/year from 2015 to 2040. For comparison, the comparable figure for the near-century from 1920 to 2014 was 2.1%/year. The compression from 2.1% to 0.3% is Gordon's most precise statement of what "the fall of American growth" means in practical terms for ordinary households.

Postscript: America's Growth Achievement and the Path Ahead

Gordon insists the book's conclusion is not about failure. "The book's message is not about failure but about unrepeatable and unique past achievements." The United States retains real advantages over its competitors: eight of the ten most valuable companies in the world are American; the venture capital ecosystem has no peer; DARPA created the internet; shale oil and gas technology reversed the country's energy trajectory; the top 30 universities in the world are dominated by American institutions; immigration and higher fertility rates compare favorably to aging Europe and Japan. These advantages are real. They simply cannot replicate the one-time gains of the Special Century.

The policy agenda Gordon advocates is explicitly aimed at reducing the headwinds rather than boosting the fundamental innovation rate, which he regards as largely outside policy control. On inequality: tax progressivity should be expanded, with proposed "super-bracket" rates for earners above $1 million and $10 million; capital gains should be taxed as ordinary income. The minimum wage at $7.25 in 2014 was 12% below its 1960s average in real terms, despite real compensation per hour rising 115% over fifty years. Expanding the EITC is justified by evidence that it reduces low birthweight, raises math and reading scores, and boosts college enrollment — among the highest-return public investments available.

On incarceration: the U.S. rate at 8 to 10 times European levels costs $74 billion annually, and drug prohibition costs an additional $88 billion. Both are policy choices, not natural phenomena, and both could be reversed. On education: James Heckman's research demonstrates that preschool programs yield 7 to 10% annual return per dollar invested; by age 5, the brain has already built the cognitive and character skills critical for future success, making early intervention the highest-leverage point in the educational system. The U.S. enrolls only 69% of four-year-olds in preschool, ranked 26th in the OECD. Australia's model of free college with income-contingent repayment through taxes addresses both access and the student debt crisis simultaneously.

Gordon's honest final assessment is that even if all proposed policies were implemented, the boost to median disposable income would be "no more than a few tenths of a percent above the rate without policy changes." The arithmetic of the headwinds cannot be fully overcome by policy alone. But the society created by those policies — more equal, better-educated, less incarcerated, less indebted — would be one in which the modest growth that remains was more broadly shared. The fall of American growth is not inevitable to the same degree for all Americans. The distribution of whatever growth occurs is very much a matter of political choice.

Trading & Investing Lessons

Lesson 1: Identify One-Time Transitions Before They End

Gordon's most powerful framework for investors is the concept of the one-time transition — improvements so large that they can happen only once. The automobile replacing the horse was a one-time transition. Electric light replacing kerosene was one-time. The urbanization of a rural society was one-time. When you identify a genuine one-time transition in progress, the growth trajectory of the dominant industry is secular and powerful — but it ends, and it ends completely. Investors who rode the railroad boom of 1870 to 1900 had to recognize when track mileage saturation was approaching. Investors in consumer appliances had to recognize when the networked home reached saturation in the 1970s. The discipline is identifying both the takeoff and the inflection point.

Lesson 2: Sector Scope Determines Economic Impact

Gordon's critique of IR #3 — that it covers only 7% of GDP — is a direct investing lesson: the addressable market of a technology determines its economic ceiling. Electricity addressed everything. The automobile addressed everything. The internet addressed information, communication, and entertainment. Investors who priced internet companies in 2000 as if they were reinventing the whole economy were committing the same error as investors who would have priced telegraph companies as if they were replacing all human communication, not just long-distance text. Know the sector share.

Lesson 3: Price Index Bias as a Hidden Alpha Source

Gordon's four types of price index bias — new product, early adoption, quality, and outlet substitution — are not merely academic quibbles. They represent systematic understatement of real purchasing power improvement in specific sectors. Industries where quality improvement runs far ahead of nominal prices — consumer electronics, pharmaceuticals in their early decades, telecommunications — offer returns that official price indices understate. The patient investor who held semiconductor companies through the entire Moore's Law era captured gains that conventional GDP accounting made look impossible in advance.

Lesson 4: The Headwinds Are Structural, Not Cyclical

Gordon's four headwinds — inequality, education stagnation, demographic drag, fiscal constraints — are not business cycle phenomena. They don't reverse in recoveries. Investors treating U.S. consumer spending as a cyclical, mean-reverting series underweight the structural constraint that median real income growth may genuinely be approaching zero for an extended period. Asset allocation built on historical real income growth rates of 2%/year may be systematically miscalibrated for a 0.3%/year environment.

Lesson 5: Infrastructure as Compounding Productivity

The Interstate Highway System contributed 31% of total productivity gain in the 1950s. Water and sewer infrastructure explained 75% of infant mortality decline and — through Nordhaus's valuation — produced as much welfare gain as all other sources of GDP growth combined. Government infrastructure investment at scale creates compounding productivity benefits across the entire private economy. Sectors that benefit most from infrastructure — trucking, retail, manufacturing, agriculture — tend to outperform when major infrastructure buildouts occur. The absence of comparable infrastructure investment since 1980 is itself a structural explanation for slowing growth.

Lesson 6: Saturation Curves and the End of Rapid Growth

Every technology follows an S-curve from invention to saturation. Radio reached 80% of homes in 20 years. Television reached 64.5% in five years. Internet adoption followed similar curves. Gordon's framework suggests that once a technology reaches saturation — once the one-time transition is complete — growth in that sector shifts from volume-driven to quality-driven. Quality improvements generate real but unmeasured welfare gains; they don't necessarily generate equivalent stock price appreciation. The investor who bought television manufacturers at peak diffusion captured the volume growth; the investor who bought at saturation bought into a mature commodity market.

Key Takeaways

1. Growth is historically exceptional, not the default

Annual growth before 1820 was 0.06% per century — so slow as to be perceptible only across generations. The assumption that modern growth rates are natural or inevitable is historically illiterate. The 1870 to 1970 miracle was the anomaly, not the baseline.

2. The Special Century's inventions could happen only once

You transition from darkness to electric light once. From horse to automobile once. From 22% infant mortality to 1% infant mortality once. From rural isolation to networked home once. All of these transitions happened between 1870 and 1970. None can happen a second time.

3. IR #2 covered every human need; IR #3 covers only one slice

Electricity, indoor plumbing, the automobile, and antibiotics addressed food, shelter, clothing, health, transportation, and communication — the entire span of human wants. The internet addressed information, communication, and entertainment — approximately 7% of GDP.

4. Clean water, not medicine, drove the great mortality decline

Clean water explains 50% of overall mortality reduction and 75% of infant mortality decline from 1900 to 1936. Medical care spending has risen from 4% of GDP in the 1950s to 17% today, producing far smaller mortality improvements than water infrastructure achieved at a fraction of the cost.

5. The Depression and WWII caused the Great Leap Forward

Paradoxically, the two greatest economic disruptions of the 20th century — the Great Depression and World War II — directly caused the productivity miracle of 1928 to 1950. The New Deal forced capital-labor substitution; the war generated learning-by-doing and government-financed manufacturing capacity that permanently raised productivity.

6. Four headwinds push median income growth toward zero

Inequality (gains concentrated at top), education stagnation (flat graduation rates, soaring costs), demographics (baby boom retirement), and fiscal constraints together reduce projected real median disposable income growth to 0.3%/year for 2015 to 2040 — compared to 2.1%/year for 1920 to 2014.

7. Moore's Law has stalled; the ICT productivity revival was brief

Computing performance improvement accelerated to 60.1%/year from 1997 to 2006, then collapsed to 11.5%/year from 2006 to 2014. The TFP revival of 1994 to 2004 lasted one decade; TFP growth returned to its post-1970 average afterward.

8. Price indices systematically understate past progress

New product bias, early-adoption price drop bias, quality bias, and outlet substitution bias together mean that real welfare improvement in the Special Century was substantially larger than official GDP statistics indicate — and the gap between past and present is larger than conventional measures suggest.

9. The fall is not inevitable for everyone

Policy choices — tax progressivity, minimum wage, EITC, preschool, prison reform, immigration — can partially offset the headwinds and improve the distribution of whatever growth occurs. The structural challenge is real; the policy response is still a matter of political choice.

Contrarian Ideas

Conventional: Technology keeps accelerating; the future will be more innovative than the past

Gordon's counter: The data show the opposite. TFP growth in 2004 to 2014 was 0.40%/year — one-fifth of the 1920 to 1970 rate. The smartphone and tablet produced no measurable TFP response. Young firms as a share of all firms nearly halved from 1978 to 2011. Net investment fell to one-third of its historical average. The burden of proof lies with the optimists to explain the productivity data, not with Gordon to explain away Silicon Valley.

Conventional: Modern medicine is responsible for longer lives

Gordon's counter: Most of the great mortality decline was complete before modern medicine. McKinlay and McKinlay's upper-limit estimate is that medical interventions explain 3.5% of mortality decline since 1900. Clean water explains 50% of overall mortality reduction. The U.S. spends 55% more than Canada on healthcare and has 2.5 years shorter life expectancy. Healthcare cost growth since 1950 has far outrun any corresponding mortality improvement.

Conventional: The Depression was purely destructive

Gordon's counter: The Depression paradoxically contributed to the Great Leap Forward in productivity. By forcing the New Deal, it produced the Wagner Act and high real wages that drove capital-labor substitution. By creating fiscal capacity for the war mobilization, it enabled the government-financed manufacturing transformation of 1941 to 1945. Without the Depression, probably no New Deal; without the New Deal, probably no wartime wage-compression that permanently changed the income distribution.

Conventional: The internet is as transformative as electricity

Gordon's counter: Electricity addressed 100% of the economy — lighting, heat, manufacturing, transportation, all consumer appliances. The internet addresses 7% of GDP. A pedicure in 2015 takes the same time as in 1955. A construction site still requires workers who pour concrete. Food still requires cooking. The structural ceiling on internet-driven growth is its sector share, and that share is far smaller than electricity's.

Conventional: The 1950s and 1960s prosperity reflected special postwar conditions

Gordon's counter: The prosperity of the 1948 to 1972 period was the normal consequence of inventions made between 1880 and 1940 reaching their full diffusion across the economy. The Special Century's inventions were already in place; the postwar decades were the harvest. What was special about the era was not postwar reconstruction but the depth of the underlying technological transformation that had been building for 70 years.

Proposals & Frameworks

The Three Industrial Revolutions Framework

Gordon organizes all of modern economic history into three industrial revolutions. IR #1 (steam, textiles, iron, railroads: 1770–1870) was Britain-centered and created the foundation. IR #2 (electricity, ICE, chemicals, sanitation: 1870–1970) was the subject of this book and the most transformative period in human history. IR #3 (ICT: 1960–present) was real but narrow. The framework's predictive power: scope of coverage → ceiling on growth impact. IR #2 covered all human needs; IR #3 covers one sector. This is the structural explanation for why IR #3 cannot replicate IR #2's productivity miracle.

The Four Headwinds Arithmetic

Gordon's most policy-relevant framework is a simple subtraction. Take optimistic labor productivity growth (1.20%/year). Subtract demographic drag from baby-boom retirement (0.40%). Subtract the inequality headwind — the gap between median and mean income growth (0.43%). Subtract fiscal drag from rising entitlement costs (0.10%). Remainder: 0.30%/year real median disposable income growth. This is not a pessimistic scenario — it is an optimistic one that assumes no productivity slowdown from current trend. The conclusion is structural, not cyclical.

The Price Index Bias Taxonomy

Gordon identifies four systematic forms of price index understatement: (1) New product bias — products not in the index when first introduced. (2) Early-adoption price drop bias — rapid price declines before official tracking begins. (3) Quality bias — improvements at stable nominal prices. (4) Outlet substitution bias — consumer shifts to lower-price retailers. Each applies most powerfully during periods of rapid innovation and technology adoption — precisely the periods when growth appears slowest in official statistics.

Policy Agenda (in order of estimated effectiveness)

Gordon's policy recommendations rank by mechanism and evidence: (1) EITC expansion — highest demonstrated return, affects child outcomes, labor supply, and income simultaneously; (2) Preschool investment — Heckman's 7–10%/year return per dollar, highest-leverage educational intervention; (3) Immigration reform — raise annual legal immigration from 0.3% to Canada's 0.8%, end denial of residency to foreign-born U.S. university graduates ("self-imposed brain drain"); (4) Incarceration reform — $74 billion cost, 60% recidivism, disproportionate damage to low-income communities; (5) Drug legalization — $88 billion saved; (6) Progressive tax reform — taxing capital gains as ordinary income, super-brackets for incomes above $1M and $10M; (7) Carbon tax — $20/ton CO2 raises $115 billion annually while correcting an externality.

What Didn't Fit

The Biometric Height Mystery

American male height at birth year 1830 averaged 68.3 inches, then declined to 66.6 inches for those born in 1890, then recovered to 69.6 for those born in 1940. The decline occurred despite rising per-capita calorie availability — a puzzle that reveals sanitation as the dominant variable rather than nutrition. Urbanization packed children into dense proximity with disease vectors, school attendance created new transmission chains, and water contamination suppressed immune development at critical growth stages. The recovery after 1890 directly tracks the water filtration and sanitation investments of that era, providing a direct biometric measure of public health infrastructure's biological impact. The pattern demolishes any simple "more food equals better health" narrative.

The Horse Flu Epidemic of 1872

The fall 1872 horse flu epidemic paralyzed northeastern American cities for weeks and provides the clearest possible illustration of urban dependence on horse power. Streetcars were suspended. Milk and ice and groceries could not be delivered. Beer ran out in saloons because barrels couldn't be moved. Construction halted. Fire departments couldn't respond effectively. Chicago removed 7,000 horse carcasses per year in normal times from streetcar service alone; during the epidemic, dead animals simply blocked streets. The episode reveals that the horse was not a charming historical feature but an essential and fragile piece of urban infrastructure — one whose replacement by the automobile and electric trolley was not merely a convenience but a fundamental improvement in urban resilience.

The Sears Catalog as Infrastructure

The Sears Roebuck catalog, fulfilling 100,000 orders per day by 1900, was not merely a retail convenience — it was a critical piece of the national market infrastructure that the telegraph and railroad had made possible. Aaron Montgomery Ward's money-back guarantee was a genuine institutional innovation: it made purchasing from an anonymous national seller rational for rural customers who had previously relied entirely on face-to-face transactions with local merchants they could hold accountable. The catalog penetration going from 3.6% of households in 1902 to 25.7% by 1928 tracks almost perfectly with Rural Free Delivery implementation and the spread of the Model T. These three innovations — RFD, the automobile, and the catalog — together ended rural isolation as a permanent feature of American life.

ENIAC's Debut and the Computing Origin Story

ENIAC (1946) weighed 30 short tons, occupied 1,800 square feet, consumed 175 kilowatts — and when it was turned on, the lights in Philadelphia dimmed. The IBM Personal Computer (1981) broke with IBM tradition by using an Intel chip and Microsoft operating system and releasing its technical specifications publicly, enabling an entire ecosystem of compatible manufacturers. IBM expected to sell 250,000 units in 5 years and sold 1 million in under 4 years. The World Wide Web appeared in December 1991; the Mosaic browser in September 1993; Windows 95 in August 1995. The entire arc from 30-ton room-filling computer to pocket supercomputer took 70 years — faster than the railroad or the automobile, but producing a narrower scope of economic transformation because it was confined to information processing rather than extending to physical work.

Glass-Steagall's Unraveling

The 1933 Glass-Steagall Act did two things: it created the FDIC, which nearly eliminated bank failures; and it decoupled commercial banking from investment banking, preventing deposit institutions from speculating with customer funds. Gordon notes that the Clinton administration's repeal of the investment banking separation in 1999 was "blamed in part for excess financial leverage" in the 2007 to 2009 financial crisis. This is a direct historical irony — one of the New Deal's two most successful financial institutions was partially dismantled 66 years later, and the consequences arrived within a decade. The comparison between the 10,000 bank failures of 1931 to 1933 and the relative resilience of FDIC-insured deposits in 2008 demonstrates how much the remaining half of Glass-Steagall continues to matter.

About the Book

Author: Robert J. Gordon

Published: 2016 (Princeton University Press)

Pages: 912

Summary: A sweeping economic history of the United States from 1870 to the present that argues the great inventions of 1870–1970 were so transformative and so unrepeatable that no subsequent wave of innovation can match them. About the Author

Robert J. Gordon is the Stanley G. Harris Professor of the Social Sciences at Northwestern University, where he has taught since 1973. He received his PhD from MIT in 1967, where his dissertation — on the productivity puzzle of the 1920s to 1950s — was supervised by Robert Solow, who later won the Nobel Prize in Economics partly for his work on growth and productivity. Gordon's PhD research identified the same puzzle that became the foundation of this book, making The Rise and Fall of American Growth in some sense the culmination of a 50-year intellectual project.

Gordon is one of the foremost authorities on productivity measurement and business cycle analysis in the United States. His previous work includes seminal papers on the relationship between the computer revolution and productivity growth — his 2000 paper "Does the New Economy Measure Up to the Great Inventions of the Past?" was among the earliest and most rigorous challenges to the tech-optimist consensus. He is a research associate of the National Bureau of Economic Research and has been described as one of the most important macroeconomists working on long-run growth. His willingness to challenge fashionable narratives about technology and progress — his argument that the internet and smartphone do not come close to matching electricity and the automobile in economic impact — has made him both controversial and, as the TFP data have confirmed his predictions, increasingly influential.

Gordon's analytical approach combines historical narrative — deep archival research into consumer budgets, mortality records, patent data, and contemporary accounts — with rigorous quantitative analysis of productivity, price indices, and income distribution. The result is a book that succeeds simultaneously as popular history and technical economics, accessible to the general reader while grounded in data that withstands professional scrutiny. His Northwestern colleague and PhD advisor Solow noted that Gordon had been thinking about this productivity puzzle since 1965. The book's dedication is to Gordon's wife Julie of 52 years, and the title itself was suggested by Seth Ditchik of Princeton University Press.

Why This Book Matters

The Rise and Fall of American Growth arrived in 2016 as the most rigorous challenge to the tech-optimist consensus that had dominated economic policy discussion for two decades. Silicon Valley, venture capital, and most mainstream economics treated the digital revolution as a continuing acceleration of human progress — Moore's Law as destiny, the smartphone as the new electricity. Gordon's answer was grounded not in prediction but in measurement: TFP data, life expectancy trajectories, productivity statistics, and income distribution records that had been accumulating for 150 years. The data showed a "one big wave" of progress that peaked around 1950 and had been declining ever since, interrupted only briefly by a ten-year internet productivity revival from 1994 to 2004. The burden of proof, Gordon argued, lay with the optimists to explain why the measured data should be ignored.

The book's second dimension of importance is as history. In 800 pages of original research, Gordon reconstructed what life actually felt like at the starting point of the Special Century — the horse manure, the outhouses, the 45-year life expectancy, the 60-hour work weeks in steel mills — and traced every dimension of its transformation over the following century. This is not economic history as abstraction. It is economic history as lived experience: the Tennessee farmer receiving electricity for the first time, the 40 million Americans listening to Seabiscuit run, the Levittown veteran buying a house with no down payment. The book reminds us what the economy is actually for — not GDP growth as an end in itself, but the elimination of darkness, disease, isolation, and back-breaking labor that defined most of human history.

The headwinds chapter proved to be almost prophetically accurate. Gordon's 2016 projection of near-zero real median income growth anticipated the political conditions that produced the populist wave of 2016 to 2020. When median real incomes stagnate for decades, when inequality concentrates gains at the top, when the good manufacturing jobs disappear and the educational ladder becomes accessible only to the already-advantaged — the social contract that postwar prosperity built begins to fray. Gordon did not predict the specific political outcomes. But he provided the best economic explanation of the structural conditions that made them possible. That is the highest purpose of economic history: not to predict the future, but to understand the present well enough to see what is genuinely at stake.
