## Preface

Gordon's intellectual origin: as 24-year-old MIT grad student (1965),
noticed the ratio of sales to capital jumped sharply between the 1920s
and 1950s — economy-wide, per Kendrick's 1961 *Productivity Trends*.
His PhD found that WWII factories paid for by government were never
recorded in private capital data, creating an artificial productivity
surge. His two 2000 papers ("One Big Wave" and "Does the New Economy
Measure Up?") became this book's foundation.

## Chapter 1: Introduction — The Ascent and Descent of Growth

**The Special Century 1870–1970**
Gordon's central thesis: growth is not steady. From AD 1 to AD 1820,
annual western-world growth = 0.06%/year — 6% per century. Landsburg:
"For the next 99,800 years or so, nothing happened. Almost everyone
lived on the modern equivalent of $400–600/year." Then suddenly,
a couple of centuries ago, people started getting richer.

Framework: virtually no growth until 1770 → slow growth 1770–1870 →
remarkably rapid growth 1870–1970 → slower growth since 1970.

**Three Big Ideas**
1. The 1870–1970 century was unique in human history
2. Post-1970 advances were "dazzling and disappointing" — channeled
   into entertainment/ICT only; TFP grew at barely one-third the
   1920–1970 rate
3. Four structural "headwinds" — inequality, education, demography,
   fiscal debt — push growth of real disposable income for bottom 99%
   "little above zero"

**Why Great Inventions Cannot Be Repeated**
In 1870, working-class families bathed monthly in a kitchen tub filled
with hand-carried cold water. One-time transitions: electric light
(flip a switch once), electric elevator (changed urban density forever),
motor vehicles replacing horses (freed a quarter of agricultural land
that had fed horses; eliminated horse-waste workforce), transportation
speed (from 1830s railroads to Boeing 707 at 0.85× speed of sound in
1958 — 100% of potential speed gain in ~130 years).

**Starting Point: 1870**
The Battle of New Orleans was fought 3 weeks after its war ended because
news traveled only as fast as ships. The telegraph ended that world
permanently — single most radical speed improvement in communication
history. Transcontinental railroad joined at Promontory Summit, Utah,
1869.

**Networked Housing by 1940**
0% of homes wired for electricity in 1880 → ~100% of urban homes by
1940. By 1940: 94% of urban homes had clean running water and sewers;
80%+ had interior flush toilets; 73% had gas; 58% central heating;
56% mechanical refrigerators. The 1870 home was isolated; the 1940
home was "networked" with five connections: electricity, gas, telephone,
water, and sewer. Urban population share: 24.9% (1870) → 73.7% (1970).

Hurricane Sandy 2012 illustration: New Yorkers below 34th Street
were pushed back to the 19th century — no elevators, no subway, no
refrigeration, no lighting; NJ drivers couldn't pump gas because pumps
required electricity.

**Post-1970: Narrower Palette**
All electronic entertainment, communications, and IT = only ~7% of
GDP in 2014. Food: only greater variety. Clothing: style changes,
near-complete offshoring. Kitchen: microwave oven the only significant
post-1970 appliance. Air travel: actually worse by 2015.

**Price Index Problems (systematic GDP understatement)**
Four forms of bias: (1) New product bias — room AC first sold 1951
but not in CPI until 1967; VCR first sold 1978, not indexed until 1987.
(2) Early-adoption price drop bias — Model T went from $950 (1908)
to $269 (1923) — a 3× real improvement missed because no car price
index existed until 1935. (3) Quality bias — new TV models offer
bigger screens at same price; not recorded as a price decrease.
(4) Outlet substitution bias — Walmart charges less for identical eggs
than supermarkets — not recorded as a price reduction. This bias
repeated across every retail transition.

Items not in GDP at all: indoor plumbing replacing outhouses; value
of lives saved by penicillin; Pasteur's germ theory; urban sanitation.

**TFP: The "One Big Wave"**
Three periods: labor productivity (TFP) grew 2.8%/yr in 1920–70 vs.
1.8% and 1.7% in adjacent periods. The 50 years 1920–70 = 40% of the
124-year span 1890–2014, but accounted for 66% of cumulative TFP growth.

Paul David's Dynamo Analogy (1990): ~40-year lag between Edison's Pearl
Street (1882) and electrification of manufacturing in 1920s. Solow
(1987): "We can see the computer age everywhere but in the productivity
statistics." Productivity soared 1996–2004 then broke down; returned to
slow rates despite proliferation of laptops and smartphones.

Counterfactual: if productivity growth 1970–2014 had matched 1920–70
rates, real GDP per person in 2014 would be $97,300 instead of actual
$50,600 — a $46,700 difference for every man, woman, and child.

**The Great Leap Forward: Depression and War (preview)**
Both the Depression and WWII directly contributed to the productivity
leap. No Great Depression = no New Deal = no unionization = no rise
in real wages + no shrinkage of average hours → no productivity boost.
WWII "learning by doing" taught firms and workers efficiency. Federal
government financed an entirely new manufacturing sector: new plants
and equipment during the war equal to fully half the stock of privately
owned equipment that existed in 1941 — and it was more modern.

## Chapter 2: The Starting Point — Life and Work in 1870

**American Standing**
U.S. had absorbed the British First Industrial Revolution. By 1870:
74% of British and 128% of German per-capita income. At the London
Crystal Palace 1851: McCormick's reaper could do the work of 40 men;
Colt's revolver made from interchangeable parts — "the American system."
Population: exactly 40 million in 1870.

**Demographic Profile of 1870**
Average household: 5.3 persons (vs. 2.6 in 2010). 80.6% of adults in
married-couple households (vs. 48.4% in 2010). 59% of population under
age 25. Only 3.0% aged 65+. Fertility: white women 4.6; Black women
7.7. Prime-age male labor force participation: 98%. Female: only 21%.
Teenage male: 76%. Retirement essentially didn't exist — men "worked
until they dropped."

**Consumer Spending 1870**
Per-capita consumption = $2,808 in 2010 prices = $54/week. Food:
51.9% of spending. By 2013: services expanded to 66.5%; food at home
fell from 44.3% to 7.6%. By 2013: nearly 60% of consumer spending
was on goods and services that didn't even exist in 1869.

**Food in 1870**
Diet monotonous and tied to what could be raised locally. Dominant
foods: pork and corn. Icebox not yet common. Refrigerated railcar not
invented until 1871. Before then, beef arrived in cities "on the hoof"
— cattle shipped live, starved, "virtually dead on arrival." Americans
averaged 3,029 daily calories per capita in 1870 (vs. ~2,500 in UK).

**Housing in 1870**
0% of homes had electricity. Buildings poorly insulated: "rags stuffed
into cracks." No indoor plumbing, running water, flush toilets. 1893
Commissioner of Labor report: 88% of Baltimore's families had access
only to outside privies. NYC workers "housed worse than any city in
the civilized world." Dumbbell tenements: 5–7 stories, 4 apartments per
floor, 5-foot-wide air shaft that became garbage dump; banned in 1901.

**Horses and Urban Life**
Boston: 250,000 citizens shared streets with 50,000 horses. Each horse
produced 20–50 lbs of manure and a gallon of urine daily = 5–10 tons
of manure per square mile per day. Fall 1872 horse flu epidemic
paralyzed northeastern cities: streetcars suspended, no milk/ice/
groceries, no beer in saloons, construction halted. 8.6 million horses
in 1870 = roughly one horse per five people.

**Health and Disease 1870**
Life expectancy at birth: only 45 years (vs. 78 recently). Infant
mortality: 176 per thousand. Charles Eliot (new Harvard president,
1869): "The ignorance and general incompetency of the average graduate
of American Medical Schools… is something horrible to contemplate."
No physician licensing in 1870. Memphis, September 1873: yellow fever,
cholera, and smallpox struck simultaneously; 2,000 of 7,000 yellow
fever cases died.

**Work in 1870**
Agriculture: 46% of jobs. If farming + blue-collar + domestic service
= "unpleasant": 87% of 1870 jobs were unpleasant → 60.4% in 1940 →
21.6% in 2009. North Carolina housewife (1886): carried water 8–10
times/day; over a year, she walked 148 miles carrying water and
transported more than 36 tons.

## Chapter 3: What They Ate and Wore

**Food Transitions**
Budget studies (BLS surveys 1888–1919): food share of spending stayed
41–43% — barely moved even as real incomes rose. Total calories per
capita: essentially flat 1800–1980. Total meat consumption fell by
one-third 1870–1929; pork down half.

**Rise of Processed Foods**
Canning invented 1809 by Nicholas Appert. Gail Borden: shocked by
Donner Party disaster; invented condensed milk (patented 1856). By
1870: less than 1 can per person/year; by 1910: 33 cans per person/year.
Kellogg's corn flakes: accidentally invented 1894 at a sanitarium.
Post Grape-Nuts 1897 (by Post, a former sanitarium patient of Kellogg).
Cracker Jack: Chicago World's Fair 1893. Popsicle: Frank Epperson,
age 11, left a powered-soda drink outside overnight in 1905; children
renamed "Epsicles" to "Pop's 'sicles." Coca-Cola invented 1886 but
only a soda fountain drink; first bottling plant 1899. By 1900: U.S.
food processing = 20% of manufacturing output.

Clarence Birdseye discovered flash-freezing in Labrador 1912, observing
how Inuits preserved fish. Had to wait until postwar America (by 1950,
refrigerators featured substantial frozen food compartments).

**Food Safety**
1902 NYC: 52.8% of milk samples adulterated. Dealers added "molasses,
chalk, or plaster of Paris" to mask diseased cattle milk. First
pasteurized milk: Pittsburgh, 1907.

Upton Sinclair's *The Jungle* (1906): intended as "the Uncle Tom's
Cabin of the labor movement." After government confirmed conditions:
"trade in lard, sausage, and canned goods came almost to a standstill."
June 30, 1906: Pure Food and Drug Act signed — established ancestor of
today's FDA.

**Biometric Surprise**
Native-born American male height: 68.3 inches (birth year 1830) →
declined to 66.6 inches (birth year 1890) → recovered to 69.6 (birth
year 1940). Despite rising per-capita calories — urbanization and school
proximity created faster disease contagion. Not food — sanitation was
the culprit.

**Retail Evolution**
A&P: 7,500 outlets by 1920, 30,000 by 1930. Pittsburgh 1911 price
survey: chain store prices 21.3% lower than neighborhood stores on
unweighted average — meat items ~35% cheaper. Outlet substitution bias
not captured in CPI — just as Walmart's lower prices aren't captured
today.

Montgomery Ward: first catalog 1872. Aaron Montgomery Ward: money-back
guarantees, risk-free transactions. Sears Roebuck: fulfilling 100,000
orders/day by 1900. Rural Free Delivery implemented by 1901; Parcel
Post 1913. Catalog circulation: 3.6% of households (1902) → 25.7%
(1928).

Marshall Field's (Chicago): Tiffany glass ceiling with 1.6 million
pieces — first and largest ever built. Entire floor of restaurants on
7th floor to draw shoppers upward. "Shopping in these stores became an
adventure in high art. Department stores became museums."

## Chapter 4: The American Home — From Dark and Isolated to Bright and Networked

**The Central Thesis**
"Networked" is the single word summarizing the interior revolution in
urban American homes 1870–1940. Five networks: electricity, natural
gas, telephone, clean running water, and sewers. Key insight on
equality: "Networking inherently implies equality. Everyone, rich and
poor, is plugged into the same electric, water, sewer, gas, and
telephone network." In 1870, the rich had servants to do the hauling;
middle and working classes had to do it themselves.

Diffusion: 0% electricity (1880) → 96% urban (1940); running water
one-third of homes (1900) → universal urban (1940); indoor toilets
~15% (1900) → 83% urban sole use (1940).

"Today's houses and apartments are much more similar to those of 1940
than those of 1940 are to their predecessors of 1900."

**Light Before Electricity**
A single 100-watt bulb = ~1,200 lumens. Best 1870 lamp = 80–100 lumens.
Kerosene (invented 1850s): burned 10× brighter than candles. Town gas:
brighter but emitted ammonia, sulfur, CO2; explosion-prone; gasworks
located in poor neighborhoods producing "furnaces belching foul smoke."

Edison's light bulb October 22, 1879: cotton-thread filament glowed
14.5 hours. "Here was a little click that meant light was contained in
a glass vacuum and need never again be linked with a flame… A child
could be left alone with it." By 1920: electric light was 10× brighter
than kerosene and 100× brighter than a candle.

William Nordhaus: $20/year in 1800 bought 5,500 candle-hours. By 1890
(kerosene/gas): $20 bought 73,000 candle-hours. By 1920 (electric):
$20 bought 4.4 million candle-hours.

**Electric Appliances**
Electric refrigerator: $775 (1919) → $568 (1926) → $137 (1931 Sears);
only 8% of homes (1930), soared to 44% by 1940. 1 million sold for
entire decade 1919–29, then 2.5 million in 1932 alone.

Washing machine reached 40% of homes by 1940. 1928 Sears: $79–$92
(about 3 weeks of Muncie median income). Electric iron first sold 1893.

**Running Water and Plumbing**
Total waterworks in U.S.: 244 (1870) → 9,850 (1924). Muncie, Indiana:
no running water before 1885; in 1890, fewer than two dozen complete
bathrooms in entire city. 1908 Sears: complete bathroom set for $43.80
= about 3 weeks' working-class income; shipping weight 480 lbs.
Value: full bathroom raised rent by 82%; central heating raised rent
by 28%.

Central heating: steam boiler danger — Mark Twain noted people boarding
a riverboat in St. Louis had only a 50–50 chance of making it to New
Orleans (246 boiler explosions in 1888 alone; 2 deaths per 3 explosions).

**Bungalow Movement (1910–1930)**
Developed in Los Angeles; spread to Chicago by 1905. 80,000 bungalows
built in Chicago 1910–1930. Sears sold complete kits: $750–$2,000 for
all materials; could be assembled in 352 carpenter-hours. Real price
of nails fell by a factor of TEN between 1830 and 1930.

Indiana farm housewife 1925: "You can't go into town in a bathtub."
Lynd survey of 26 "run-down houses" — only 5 had bathtubs, but all 26
had automobiles. Tennessee farmer: "The greatest thing on earth is to
have the love of God in your heart, and the next greatest thing is to
have electricity in your house."

## Chapter 5: Motors Overtake Horses and Rail

**Motor Vehicle Growth**
Motor cars registered: 8,000 (1900) → 9 million (1920) → 23 million
(1929). "The total power created by automobiles exceeded that of all
farm animals as early as 1910." 78% of the world's automobiles were
registered in the United States in 1930. "Nothing in the millennia of
human history rivals the speed with which automobile ownership spread
in a mere two decades between 1910 and 1930."

**Railroads**
U.S. had 60,000-mile rail network by 1870. Railroad mileage grew 5×
between 1870 and 1900 — "nearly twenty new miles of track a day for
thirty years." By 1893: 7 transcontinental lines. Abraham Lincoln's
1860 trip from Springfield to New York for Cooper Union speech: 1,200
miles, 5 trains, 2 ferries, 4 days, 3 nights.

Speeds: 1870: fastest intercity trains under 25 mph. 1940: Denver
Zephyr: 64.6 mph average on Chicago-Denver run (diesel engine, 4×
more fuel-efficient than steam, with air conditioning). Passenger rail
peak: 1911–20, average 38.1 billion passenger-miles/year = only 379
miles per person. After WWII, rail traffic per person declined steadily;
by 1958 had fallen below 1882 level.

**Urban Transit: Horse to Electric**
Horse-drawn omnibuses: NY 1833. NY Herald (1864): "Modern martyrdom
may be succinctly described as riding in a New York omnibus." Peak of
horse-drawn streetcars: 100,000+ horses, 18,000 streetcars, 3,000
miles of track. "Chicago alone removed 7,000 horse carcasses per year
from streetcar service." Horse manure heaps: 7 feet high on Liberty
Street in New York.

Frank J. Sprague invented electric trolley for Richmond, VA. Within 2
years: 200+ electrified systems nationwide. By 1902: 98% of trackage
electrified. Nearly 5 billion passengers/year in 1902. Public transit
peak: 383 rides per person per year in cities 25,000+ by 1925.

Streetcar decline: 5-cent fixed fare couldn't survive 74% price
inflation 1913–1923. By end of WWI: one-third of all transit companies
were bankrupt.

Boston first underground subway: 1897; 50 million passengers in first
year. NYC first subway: 1904; express trains at 40 mph. By 1940: NY
subway carried 3 billion of 5 billion annual transit journeys — still
only 5 cents from Coney Island to the Bronx.

**Karl Benz and the Internal Combustion Engine**
Karl Benz's first success: New Year's Eve 1879 — just 10 weeks after
Edison's light bulb (October 22, 1879). First horseless carriage: 1886.
"The automobile is European by birth, American by adoption." Benz:
"My heart was pounding. I turned the crank. The engine started to go
'put put put,' and the music of the future sounded with regular rhythm."

**Model T Ford — The Price Revolution**
Model T: first produced late 1908; introductory price $950. Moving
assembly line: 1913. Network of almost 7,000 dealers by 1913; 65% in
rural areas. Price data:
- 1909–10 Model T: $950 → 1923 Model T: $269 (after 15 million produced)
- Quality-adjusted price fell 73%; captured 55% of U.S. new car market
  at peak (1923)
- Ratio of purchase price to disposable income per person:
  3.16 (1910) → 0.43 (1923)
- By 1926: 75% of new automobiles financed on installment

Farm adoption: by 1926: 93% of Iowa farmers had automobiles (from 0%
in ~1910). "Social contacts were few and commonly limited within a
radius of a few miles... All this has been revolutionized by motor
vehicles." Casualties of the automobile: crossroads general store,
local banks, one-room schoolhouse. Government policies encouraged urban
sprawl and undermined urban transit. Disappearing from American streets:
street vendors, delivery boys, the casual walk, sidewalk displays,
and even the sidewalks themselves.

**Chapter 5 Conclusion**
The transition of America from rural to urban inherently could happen
only once. The retirement of horses and their attendant street waste
could happen only once. The increase of intercity train speeds from 25
mph (1870) to 60 mph (1940) could happen only once.

## Chapter 6: From Telegraph to Talkies

**Print Media**
1870: white literacy rate 88.5%; Black only 20.1% (slavery). Daily
newspaper circulation: 7 million (1870) → 96 million (1940). Average
household bought 3.1 different newspapers by 1910–30. 1880–1905: age
of "yellow journalism" — Hearst on Cuba: "You provide the pictures;
I'll provide the war." Henry Luce: *Time* founded 1923; *Life* launched
1937.

**Telegraph**
May 24, 1844: Morse — "What hath God wrought?" sent from U.S. Capitol.
Never so radical an increase in communication speed: before 1844 telegraph,
limited to railroad/horse/sailing ship speed.

**Telephone**
February 14, 1876: both Elisha Gray and Bell arrived at patent office
— Bell arrived hours earlier; Gray was forgotten. Bell's prediction on
the very night of his first successful experiment: his invention would
join home utilities "just like water or gas." American exceptionalism:
telephones per person in 1900 — 4× more than England, 20× more than
France. Limitations in 1940: NYC→SF 3-minute call = $46 (2005 dollars);
NYC→SF didn't drop below $10 until 1966.

**The Phonograph**
July 1877: Thomas Edison invented the phonograph; recited "Mary Has a
Little Lamb." Edison did NOT originally intend it for entertainment —
called it a "talking machine," intended as a business dictation device.

Early phonographs appeared in 1890s as coin-slot machines in soda
fountains and saloons. Emile Berliner perfected flat disc technology
by 1888 → Victor Talking Machine Company → merged with RCA in 1929.
Dog Nipper listening to gramophone, "His Master's Voice" — registered
1900. Both phonograph and telephone took fully 50 years from invention
to presence in 50% of American homes. 1925: microphone + vacuum tube
technology finally achieved accurate music reproduction.

**Radio**
Diffusion speed exceeded electricity, motor vehicles, telephone, and
phonograph: >80% of homes within 20 years of first commercial broadcast
(1920). November 2, 1920: KDKA Pittsburgh — first commercial broadcast.
Announcer Leo Rosenberg: "Is anyone listening out there?" — broadcast
election returns. Receiver sales: $60 million (1922) → $843 million
(1929). By 1940: 80% of homes had radios — "No single event in the
history of invention before 1940 brought about a more striking
egalitarianism."

During Depression: radio ownership soared from 35% (1929) to 82%
(1940) while telephone usage in households fell from 45% to 33%
(radios purchased vs. phones rented).

Seabiscuit vs. War Admiral match race, June 1938: 40 million listeners
in population of 129 million — FDR kept advisors waiting until race
ended. Survey finding: "Americans would rather sell their refrigerators,
bath tubs, telephones, and beds to make rent payments, than to part
with the radio box."

**Motion Pictures**
Nickelodeons ~1905: five-cent admission. 1908: 8,000+ nationwide; 4
million customers/day. Some viewers had never traveled beyond a few
miles from home; early feature of train approaching camera caused
front-row customers to panic and run out.

Al Jolson in *The Jazz Singer* (1927): first talkie — near-instant
industry conversion; by 1930, ads read "100% talking."

Weekly attendance as % of population: 73% (1929) → stayed above 60%
through 1948 → collapsed to 10% in 1960s as television took over.
Four of AFI's top 10 movies of the 20th century made between 1939–42:
*Wizard of Oz* (1939), *Citizen Kane* (1941), *Casablanca* (1942).

Gordon argues information/entertainment advances before 1940 could be
argued to have made MORE cultural difference than those after 1940.

## Chapter 7: Nasty, Brutish, and Short — Illness and Early Death

**The Counterintuitive Central Finding**
Annual rate of improvement in life expectancy was TWICE as fast in the
first half of the 20th century as in the last half — counterintuitive
given antibiotics, cancer treatment, CT/MRI.

Life expectancy at birth improved at 0.32 years/year (1890–1950) vs.
0.15 years/year (1950–98). Out of every 1,000 births: 188 lives saved
by improvements 1880–1950 vs. only 21 additional lives saved 1950–2010.

Bizarre coincidence: John Adams (age 90) and Thomas Jefferson (age 83)
both died July 4, 1826 — 50th anniversary of Declaration of
Independence; Americans who survived childhood always had potential
to live long.

Kevin Murphy and Robert Topel: "In 1900, nearly 18 percent of males
born in the U.S. died before their first birthday; today, cumulative
mortality does not reach 18 percent until age 62."

Racial gap: 1900 life expectancy nonwhite males = 33 years; white males
= 48 years (15-year gap). Jim Crow policy explicitly denied nonwhite
areas running water and sanitary sewage — direct cause of shorter life.

**The Critical Finding: Medicine Had Little to Do with It**
Infectious diseases: 37% of deaths (1900) → less than 5% (1955) →
only 2% (2009). McKinlay and McKinlay: most decline in tuberculosis
and influenza mortality had already occurred BEFORE medical interventions.
"3.5 percent probably represents a reasonable upper-limit estimate of
the total contribution of medical measures to the decline in mortality
since 1900." All decline in infectious disease mortality achieved by
1955 — BEFORE medical care spending began its rise from 4% of GDP
(mid-1950s) to 17% (2010).

**Water and Sewers: The Central Explanation**
Clean water filtration/chlorination likely "the most important public
health intervention of the twentieth century." Urban households with
filtered water: 0.3% (1880) → 25% (1910) → 93% (1940). Cutler and
Miller: clean water explains 50% of overall mortality reduction
1900–1936, 75% of decline in infant mortality. Memphis yellow fever
epidemic 1878: killed 10% of city's population — major impetus for
water infrastructure investment.

"Pasteur Revolution" 1880–1900: pathogenic organisms discovered at
one per year. NYC diphtheria antitoxin program: death rate from 785
per 100,000 (1894) to 1.1 per 100,000 (1950) — a major cause of death
completely wiped out within 60 years.

Gordon's hierarchy of causes: (1) urban sanitation — clean water explains
75% of infant mortality decline; (2) uncontaminated milk, food regulation;
(3) Pasteur germ theory → behavioral change; (4) window screens;
(5) horse-to-automobile transition; (6) improved surgery; (7) basic
medical tools.

**Hospitals and Medical Education**
1870: hospitals more like nursing homes; only 120 hospitals in 1873.
1870–1880s: hospital = "repositories of filth and infection"; postoperative
sepsis killed ~50% of major surgery patients. Key: Joseph Lister
(Glasgow) → antiseptics using carbolic acid; 1879 Listerine named after
him. Symbol of pre-antiseptic era: President Garfield (1881) — assassin's
bullet survived; doctors probed with fingers and unsterilized instruments;
Garfield died from secondary infection.

1910 Flexner Report: devastated for-profit medical schools. Flexner:
"touted laboratories consisted of a few vagrant test tubes squirreled
away in a cigar box; corpses reeked." Most for-profit medical schools
shut down; reduced supply → higher fees.

Childbirth: only 5% of women delivered in hospitals (1900) → 50% of
all women / 75% of urban women (1939) → 95% (1960).

**Accidents and Homicides**
Railroad deaths in 1898–1900 ≈ British Army losses in the three-year
Boer War. Motor vehicle fatality rate per 100 million miles: 45 (1909)
→ 1.3 (2008) — 40× improvement.

U.S. homicide rate 7–10× higher than any European country. Two peaks:
1933 (end of Prohibition) and 1980 (drug violence). Pinker: democracy
"came too early" to America — state never fully acquired monopoly on
violence.

**Valuing Life Expectancy (Nordhaus)**
Value of improvements in life expectancy 1900–1950 was AS LARGE AS the
growth rate of real GDP per capita from all other reasons combined.
With health capital included: 1900–1950 annual GDP growth per capita
= 4.2% (vs. conventional 2.05%).

Gordon's indictment: "Bismarck figured out this greatest of all economic
policy issues in the 1880s, but even now an efficient system of the
provision of medical care as a right of citizenship has thus far eluded
the American political system."

## Chapter 8: Working Conditions

**Labor Force Changes**
Adult women (ages 25–64): 12% (1870) → 26% (1940) → 72% (2000).
Boys 14–15: 50% in labor force (1870) → 10% (1940) as high school
expanded. Elderly males (65–74): 88% (1870) → 53% (1940) → 24% (1990).
Retirement invented before Social Security — decline in elderly male
participation had begun well before the New Deal.

**Hours and Conditions**
1870: 10 hours/day, 6 days/week = 60 hours/week standard.
By 1940: 40 hours/week — and has remained stable for 70+ years.
Keynes 1931 prediction that turned out quite wrong: "Three-hour shifts
or a fifteen-hour week may put off the problem for a great while."

Share of workers in "unpleasant" jobs: 87.2% (1870) → 21.6% (2009).

Steel mills in 1880s: 12-hour days, 72.6 average hours/week. English
steelmaker visiting America: "The bosses drive the men to an extent
that employers would never dream of attempting in this country." No
legal protection for injured workers: courts ruled workers "assumed
the risk" by accepting employment.

Electrification of manufacturing: half of 1919–1929 manufacturing
productivity surge from electric motor capacity. Key benefit: also
improvement in conditions — "lighter, cleaner workshops made possible
by skylights" and elimination of overhead shafts and belting that
"swirled dust and grease through the factory atmosphere."

**Triangle Shirtwaist**
Triangle Shirtwaist Factory fire, NYC, March 1911: 148 employees died
— mostly young immigrant women. Workers were locked in rooms; scores
found piled against closed doors. Directly parallel to Bangladesh
factory fires of 2012–13.

Ruth Schwartz Cowan paradox: consumer appliances didn't reduce overall
home production hours — rising standards (cleaner houses, better meals)
absorbed the time savings. Female home production: 50.4 hours/week
(1900) → 31.1 (2005). Male: 3.7 hours/week (1900) → 17.3 (2005).

**Real Wages vs. Productivity**
1870–1940: real wages grew FASTER than productivity — 2.48% vs.
1.51%/year. 1940–present: reversed — productivity (2.25%/yr) outpaces
real wages (1.56%/yr). Henry Ford's $5/day (January 1914): doubled the
standard manufacturing wage of $2.04/day. Ford's 200-member
"Sociological Department" visited all 13,000 employees' homes.
Post-1980 divergence: large share of productivity gains going to top 1%.

HS graduation rates: 9% (1910) → 51% (1940) → 76.9% (1970); grew
by 14 percentage points/decade 1910–1940, then 8.7 per decade
1940–1970, zero after 1970.

**Chapter 8 Conclusion**
"No other era in human history, either before or since, combined so
many elements in which the standard of living increased as quickly and
in which the human condition was transformed so completely."

## Chapter 9: Consumer Credit, Insurance, Government

**Consumer Credit**
Credit was NOT invented in the 1920s — pervasive in rural America from
the beginning. By 1929: installment credit financed 61% of new cars,
65% of used cars; 80–90% of furniture, 75% of washing machines.
Car loan effective interest rates in 1920s: typically 34%.
GMAC established 1919 — beginning of modern auto finance.

Sharecroppers: interest rates 40–60%; debt peonage — unpaid debts
accumulated year after year; sharecroppers often couldn't leave without
voiding debt obligations.

Mortgage debt soared: ~$12B (1919) to $43B (1930) = 16% → 41% of
nominal GDP. Homeownership near 50% nationwide by 1930. Depression:
near zero new construction through WWII.

**Life Insurance**
Life insurance company assets as % of GDP: 4.5% (1875) → 37.0% (1933).
By 1915: ~25–30% of population had life insurance. Fraternal insurance
plans' fatal flaw: enrolled young, healthy members at cheap rates but
failed to build assets for aging members — share fell from 33% to 15%
between 1915 and 1925.

**Government's Role**
Late 19th century "laissez-faire" is misleading — government intervened
extensively: Homestead Acts; Morrill Act of 1862 creating land-grant
colleges (by 1980: 18% of U.S. college students, 66% of annual PhDs);
Sherman Antitrust Act (1890) led to 1911 Standard Oil breakup; Herbert
Hoover as Commerce Secretary standardized sizes of nuts, bolts, auto
tires, plumbing parts — little-known factor enabling Arsenal of Democracy
in WWII; New Deal FDIC nearly eliminated bank failures (10,000 of 25,000
banks had failed 1931–33); Social Security 1935.

REA: Wyoming ranch woman called the day electricity arrived "my Day of
Days because lights shone where lights had never been, the electric stove
radiated heat, the washer turned, and an electric pump freed me from
hauling water."

## Entr'acte: The Midcentury Shift from Revolution to Evolution

**Three Industrial Revolutions**
IR #2 (electricity + ICE): inventions late 19th century, maximum effect
1920–70. IR #3 (ICT): began 1960, continues today. Critical distinction:
IR #2 covered virtually the ENTIRE span of human wants — food, clothing,
housing, transportation, entertainment, communication, health, medicine,
working conditions. IR #3 revolutionized only entertainment,
communications, and information. "This single fact, the narrowness of
the effects of IR #3, is enough to explain why growth slowed after 1970."

**What Real GDP Misses**
Real GDP does NOT value: brightness and safety of electric light vs.
kerosene danger; 80% price drop of Model T 1910–1923; the difference
between a nickelodeon with silent images vs. Gone With the Wind with
color and sound; reduction of infant mortality from 22% of births (1890)
to 1% (1950) — by some estimates, this created more welfare value than
all other sources of consumer welfare combined; the change from 72-hour
weekly steel mill work "accompanied by heat, sweat, and danger" to
40-hour week in an air-conditioned office.

Consumer surplus estimates — railroads vs. canals: 3% of real GDP/year;
tractors: 6% of real GDP/year. "It is not difficult to imagine an
omitted value to economic growth amounting to 100 percent of GDP."

Productivity growth chronology: 2.72%/yr (1953–64) → 1.44%/yr (1977–
94) → 2.05%/yr (1995–2004) → 0.6%/yr (end of 2014). 2013–14: trend
output per person growth = ZERO.

## Chapter 10: Food, Clothing, Housing, 1940–2015

By 1940, food and its marketing were much closer to 2015 conditions
than to 1870 conditions. Clarence Saunders, founder of Piggly-Wiggly,
opened first self-service market in 1916. "Supermarket" term first used
in 1933. First shopping cart 1937. Supermarket share of total food
sales: 28% (1946) → 69% (1963). Supermarket items stocked: 300–600
(1920s) → 17,500 (1985).

Postwar food: poultry-to-red-meat ratio: 14% (1940) → 89% (2010).
Food eaten away from home as share of food at home: 20% (1929) →
37% (1970) → 70% (2013). After century of stability (1870–1970), total
daily calorie consumption increased >20% after 1970. 2000 obesity rates:
U.S. 27%; no other of 23 countries exceeded 20%. "Poor children sit
in idleness in front of TV screens and eat cheap fast foods. Upper-
middle-class children eat kale salads before their trip to participate
on the soccer team" — obesity is a social/inequality problem.

Clothing share of consumer expenditures: 13.1% (1900) → 3.1% (2013).
650,000 apparel jobs disappeared 1997–2007. Synthetic fabrics are the
only genuine quality improvement — eliminated ironing.

Housing: modern conveniences reached 100% coverage: electric power by
1950; running water/exclusive flush toilet by 1970; central air
conditioning from 37% (1970) to 89% (2010). Median new single-family
home: 1,900 sq ft (1990) → 2,364 sq ft (2013). Microwave oven: 8%
(1980) → 96% (2010) — fastest appliance adoption in postwar history.

## Chapter 11: Automobiles and Air Travel, 1940–2015

Motor vehicles per 100 households: 89 (1929) → 113 (1950) → 207
(2010). Great transformation to motorized society essentially over by
1970. Vehicle miles per person growth: 13.5%/year (1919–29) → 8.0%
(1950–80) → only 1.1% (1980–2012). Vehicle miles in 2012 were 6.4%
lower than in 2004 — no higher than 1997. Combined 1906–2012 quality:
14× total improvement.

Model T affordability paradox: 1923 Model T price = 11% of per-household
consumption. 1950 Plymouth = 33% of per-household consumption. Model T
was TWICE as affordable relative to incomes as today's typical vehicle.

Interstate Highway System (1956–1992): Of 35 industries examined, all
but 3 experienced significant cost reductions; interstate spending
contributed ~31% productivity gain (1950s), ~25% (1960s), ~7% (1980s).
Economy-wide TFP slowdown after 1972 at least partly reflects end of
highway construction productivity bonus.

**Air Travel**
1926–1936: greatest decade of commercial aircraft advances in history.
Since first jet flight 1958: NO improvement in speed or comfort.

DC-3 (first flights June 26, 1936): 21 passengers; transcontinental
in 15 hours. Round-trip sleeper fare: $285 in 1936 = $3,940 in today's
prices — almost exactly current business class fare of $3,600 NY–LA.
Speed comparison NY–Chicago: 3h55m in 1936 vs. 2h35m today — but
1936: arrived 10 minutes before departure, no security. Total travel
time 1936 vs. today: 3h50m vs. 3h50m — virtually identical.

Boeing 707 jet (1958): LA–NY in 4.8 hours. REVERSAL: Today's LA–NY
= 5.6 hours (fuel-saving speeds + runway congestion). Annual rate of
elapsed time improvement: –0.2%/year since 1958 — NEGATIVE.

Air crash fatalities per 100 billion passenger miles: 580 (1950) →
10 (1990) → complete safety achieved by ~2006.

Deregulation 1978 did NOT lower average prices — increased variance
(lower for advance travel, higher for business). Real price of air
travel: fastest decline 1940–60 (–8.1%/year) BEFORE jets; no decline
at all 1980–2013. Security theater: 200 million hours/year wasted
(~$8 billion annual waste); 9/11 issue was flimsy cockpit doors (fixed
within days).

## Chapter 12: Entertainment and Communications, 1940–2015

TV diffusion: 9% (1950) → 64.5% (1955) — fastest diffusion of any
appliance or device in history. January 19, 1953: CBS broadcast Lucy
having a baby — 68.8% of country's TV sets tuned in. Milton Berle's
Texaco Star Theater so popular that Detroit reservoirs dropped sharply
when show ended (everyone went to bathroom at once).

1960 Nixon–Kennedy debate: TV viewers overwhelmingly picked Kennedy.
March 7, 1965: Selma to Montgomery march; TV broadcast of police
attacking protesters → Voting Rights Act of 1965.

TV quality improvements: 1950: $350 for 9-inch black-and-white RCA
table model. 2014: $418 for 40-inch HD LED color. True annual price
decline 1952–83: –4.3%/year vs. CPI's –1.0%/year. Total unmeasured
consumer surplus 1952–83: 278% of what was spent on TV sets. For every
dollar of GDP spending on TV sets 1952–1999, quality improvements
delivered $5 of unmeasured benefits.

VCR price: $1,200 (1978) → under $250 (1987) — –17%/year. CPI didn't
include VCR until 1987 — all price decline before then completely missed.

CD (1982): outsold vinyl by 1988, outsold cassettes by 1991, peaked
2002 at 95% of sales. iPod (2001): replaced portable CD player.
Spotify: 30 million songs, $10/month.

First AT&T Direct Distance Dialing (1951): Mayor of Englewood NJ to
Mayor of Alameda CA took 18 seconds vs. 23 minutes with 5 operators
in 1915. By 2013: 91% of adults had cell phone.

## Chapter 13: Computers and Internet

Cray-1 supercomputer (1976): $8.8 million. 2014 Walmart Lenovo laptop
($449): 750× memory, 1,000× speed of Cray-1 — performance/price
improved 44%/year since 1976. Moore's Law pace: accelerated to 60.1%
/year (1997–2006), then collapsed to 11.5%/year (2006–2014). "Moore's
Law has gone off the rails" since 2006.

Key constraint: ICT spending (2014) = only 7% of GDP. Even 20%/year
performance gains × 7% share = only 1.4% GDP growth boost.

ENIAC (1946): 30 short tons, 1,800 sq ft, 175 kilowatts; "when ENIAC
was turned on, the lights in Philadelphia dimmed." First credit card:
Frank McNamara forgot wallet at business lunch 1949 → founded Diners
Club. Barcode UPC: first scan June 26, 1974 — 67-cent pack of Wrigley's
Juicy Fruit gum.

IBM PC (1981): broke IBM tradition by using Intel chip and Microsoft OS;
released specs publicly; expected 250,000 sales in 5 years, sold 1
million in less than 4 years.

World Wide Web (December 1991). Mosaic browser (September 1993) =
"killer app." Windows 95 launch (August 24, 1995): observer: "The
Internet is slow, superficial, chaotic, nerdy, hostile, and largely a
waste of time. You just gotta try it." Amazon founded 1994.

Key criticism of tech optimists: ICT barely touched food, clothing,
motor vehicles, furniture, appliances, rent, health care, education,
personal care — two-thirds of consumption is services, mostly untouched.
"A pedicure is a pedicure regardless of whether the customer is reading
a Kindle or surfing the web on a smartphone."

## Chapter 14: Health and Medicine, 1940–2015

Life expectancy gain 1880–1950: +25 years. Gain 1950–2008: only +9
years. By 1945: 2/3 of all deaths from chronic diseases (heart, cancer,
stroke); only 25 years earlier 2/3 had been from infectious diseases.

Heart disease: mortality rate actually ROSE between 1900 and 1940;
peaked 1963 at 55% of all deaths. Decline after 1963 with better blood
pressure control, emergency rooms, coronary care units. Recent slowdown:
CHD mortality rate declined ~4%/year in late 1970s → only 0.5%/year
in 2000s. Risk of resurgence.

Cancer mortality: 64/100,000 (1900) → 200.5 (2000) — 3× increase.
5-year cancer survival rate: 50% (1971) → 63% (2004) despite $200
billion spent. "It's like a Greek tragedy. Everybody plays his
individual part to perfection… and the total just doesn't work."

Penicillin: discovered 1928, available in U.S. pharmacies summer 1945
at 6.5 cents/dose. Cured: pneumonia, rheumatic fever, syphilis. FDA
approved 50% more new drugs 1940–60 than in the next 51 years combined.

Salk polio vaccine (1955): largest clinical trial in history — 400,000+
children immunized. By late 1960s: polio virus entirely eliminated in
United States.

Healthcare expenditures as % of GNP: 4.5% (1950) → 9.4% (1980) →
17.7% (2010). U.S. vs. Canada: 55% higher healthcare spending, 2.5
years shorter life expectancy. U.S. excess spending vs. Canada:
39% administrative, 31% higher physician incomes.

Birth control pill: FDA approval 1960; called "after penicillin the
most important pharmaceutical development of the 20th century." Pill
ended baby boom; enabled women to enter labor force.

White women without high school diploma lost 5 years of life expectancy
between 1990–2008 — "a measure of decline last seen among Russians after
the fall of the Soviet Union." Healthcare share of GDP: only 4% between
1930–60; reached 18% by 2013; sixth of population still lacked insurance.

## Chapter 15: Work, Youth, Retirement, 1940–2015

Manufacturing jobs peaked at ~30% of labor force (1953) → fell to 10%
by 2015. "$20/hour wage allowed masses of Americans with no more than
high school education to rise to the middle class... on its way to
extinction" (Uchitelle).

Great Compression (1945–1975): Goldin and Margo — blue-collar union
worker could live next door to accountant or teacher. Between 1970–1998:
average income in top 0.01% jumped from 50 to 250 times the economy-
wide average — Great Compression replaced by "Great Divergence."

WWII: 5.2 million women entered labor force. 1960: women = 6% of
doctors, 3% of lawyers, <1% of engineers. Medical school dean 1961:
"Hell yes, we have a quota. We don't want them here." Sandra Day
O'Connor: graduated Stanford Law, turned down by 40 law firms, initially
worked as legal secretary. Prime-age female LFPR: 34.9% (1948) → peak
76.8% (1999) → 73.9% (2014). Female wages as % of male wages: 58.8%
(1975) → 77.4% (2010).

Claudia Goldin's insight: occupations where ONE specific person must
always be available (trial lawyers, M&A bankers, surgeons) penalize time
flexibility; pharmacists and physicians in group practices do NOT.

HS completion: 10% (1900) → 75% (1970) → little progress since.
GI Bill paid college for millions of WWII veterans. College completion
rank: fell from 1st in world to no better than 15th.

Social Security (1935): poverty rate among elderly fell from 35% (1959)
to 10% (2003). Defined-benefit pension plans: 30% of workers (1983) →
15% (2013). 2030 projection: 2 workers per retiree (vs. 5 in 1960).

Key finding: output per person = labor productivity × hours per person.
After late 1960s: labor productivity growth slowed; but output per
person did not similarly slow until after 2000 — because women entering
labor force raised hours worked per person. Since 2000: sharp decline
in BOTH labor productivity growth AND hours worked per person — "the
book's title, The Rise and Fall of American Growth, has become a
statement of fact."

## Chapter 16: The Great Leap Forward (1920s–1950s)

**The Puzzle**
"One of the greatest achievements in all of economic history." Had
1870–1928 growth rate continued, by 1950 output per hour would have been
52% higher than 1928; ACTUAL 1928–50 increase: 99% — nearly doubled
the expected pace. Puzzle: Great Inventions had already reached most
urban households by 1928 — electricity, gas, telephone, running water,
sewers all connected; 93% of Iowa farmers owned motor vehicles by 1926.

**Quantitative Evidence**
1870–1928 trend: output per hour and output per person both grew at
1.9%/year. Post-1928 divergence: productivity barely fell during Great
Depression but soared to 32% above trend by 1957.

**Growth Accounting**
Growth decomposition: (1) labor quality — educational attainment;
(2) capital per unit of labor; (3) capital quality; (4) TFP — "the
measure of our ignorance." Key finding: educational attainment rose at
STEADY pace before and after 1928 — so it CANNOT explain the Great Leap.

**New Deal and Unions**
Wagner Act (1935): made union organizing much easier; pushed up real
wages. Fair Labor Standards Act (1938): codified 40-hour week with
mandatory 1.5× overtime. Mechanism: higher wages → firms economized
on labor → substituted capital for labor → higher productivity per worker.
Denison: shorter work hours reduced fatigue → directly improved efficiency.

**WWII as Economic Miracle**
Most novel thesis: WWII itself was perhaps the most important contributor.
Military spending in 1944: 80% of the entire economy's 1939 size.
Federal government purchased productive equipment 1941–45 equal to
roughly 50% of entire pre-war privately owned equipment stock — and it
was ALL more modern than existing capital. Personal savings rate peaked
1943–44 at 27% (vs. normal 5–10%) — nothing to buy, rationing.

Two overriding conclusions: (1) WWII saved U.S. from secular stagnation;
(2) Great Inventions of late 19th century continued to transform
production in 1930s and 1940s, not just 1920s. Alex Field's claim that
1930s were the "most progressive decade" validated.

## Chapter 17: Can the Future Match the Great Inventions?

Peter Thiel epigraph: "We wanted flying cars, instead we got 140
characters."

TFP chronology: 1970–94 = 0.57%/yr; 1994–2004 = 1.03%/yr; 2004–14 =
0.40%/yr — all far below 1.89%/yr of 1920–70. IR #2 TFP surge lasted
50 years; IR #3 revival lasted only 10 years.

Gordon's resolution of the Solow Paradox: computers are NOT everywhere
— "We don't eat computers or wear them or drive to work in them or let
them cut our hair." ICT barely touched food, clothing, motor vehicles,
furniture, appliances, rent, health care, education, personal care.

**1994–2004 Revival Was Unique**
Amazon (1994), Google (1998), Wikipedia (2001), iTunes (2001), Facebook
(2004) — all more than a decade old by writing. Office transformation
from typewriters to web-linked PCs essentially complete by 2005.
"One-time-only jump in the level of productivity."

Post-2005 stasis: "Throughout the world, the equipment used in office
work and the productivity of office employees closely resembles that of
a decade ago." Smartphones in office: 90% of workers visit recreational
websites during workday — dragging down productivity.

**Decline in Business Dynamism**
Young firms (≤5 years old) as share of all firms: 14.6% (1978) →
8.3% (2011) — nearly halved, started BEFORE 2008 crisis. People under
30 owning stakes in private companies: 10.6% (1989) → 3.6% (2014).

Net investment ratio: 3.2% long-run average; fell to 1.0% in 2013 —
less than a THIRD of historical average.

**Future Innovations: Assessment**
Robots at competition "fell every which way." Amazon warehouses: Kiva
robots do NOT touch merchandise — only lift shelves and bring to human
packers. ATMs created MORE bank branches — complementarity theme.
Driverless cars: benefits minor compared to the invention of the car
itself. Google car doesn't drive on unmapped roads — any deviation
causes software to blank out.

Key paradox: quantity of electronic data rising exponentially for
decades WITHOUT pushing TFP out of post-1970 lethargy, except
temporarily 1994–2004. Smartphone (2007) and smart tablet (2010)
introductions: NO measurable response in TFP growth whatsoever.

Numbers don't lie: unemployment fell 10.0% (Oct 2009) → 5.3% (Jun
2015); but labor productivity grew only 0.5%/yr in five years ending
Q2 2015 vs. 2.3%/yr in 1994–2004.

## Chapter 18: Inequality and the Four Headwinds

**First Headwind: Inequality**
Piketty and Saez three eras:
- 1917–48: incomes became MORE equal — due to Great Depression, WWII,
  New Deal; bottom 90% grew 1.43%/yr, top 10% grew only 0.58%/yr
- 1948–72: GOLDEN AGE — all three groups grew at roughly SAME rate;
  average growth 2.58%/yr; 5× the income growth of 1972–2013
- 1972–2013: GREAT DIVERGENCE — average real income bottom 90%
  actually LOWER in 2013 than 1972; peak bottom-90% real income was
  $37,053 (2000); by 2013 had fallen 15% to $31,652; top 10% average
  income DOUBLED from $161,000 (1972) to $324,000 (2007)

Pew Research 1983–2013 wealth: upper 21% doubled ($318K → $639K);
middle 46% stagnated ($94K → $97K); bottom 33% FELL ($11K → $9K).

CEO pay: 20× average worker pay (1973) → 257× (2013). Target CEO
received $47 million retirement package after credit card data breach
— roughly 1,000× the average Target worker's 401(k) balance.

Causes of the Great Divergence: unionization reversal (27% (1973) →
13% (2011)); import share of GDP (5.4% (1970) → 16.5% (2014));
China imports accounted for about a QUARTER of the decline in
manufacturing employment 1990–2007 (David Autor et al.); automation
destroying routine jobs (manufacturing share 30% (1953) → <10%);
minimum wage (constant 2011 dollars): $8.38 (1979) → $4.68 (2006).

**Second Headwind: Education**
College completion rank: U.S. 2nd in world (2000) → now 12th.
University costs rose at MORE THAN TRIPLE the overall inflation rate
since 1972. State funding for higher education declined by a THIRD
(2001–2012). Total student debt: $1.2 trillion. Underemployment rate
for 22-year-old college graduates = 56%.

High school graduation: increased from <10% (1900) to 80% (1970) →
FELL to 74% (2000). PISA 2013: U.S. ranked 27th in math. Property
tax financing creates vicious circle. Self-perpetuating educational
elite: college completion top-quartile income households rose from 40%
(1970) to 77% (2013); bottom-quartile rose from only 6% to 9%.

**Third Headwind: Demographics**
Baby boom (1946–1964) retirement: started 2008, extends to 2034 →
scholars estimate –0.4%/yr drag on output per person. Labor force
participation rate: 66.0% (2007) → 62.6% (June 2015) = loss of 8.5
million jobs, most permanent.

**Fourth Headwind: Government Debt**
CBO baseline forecast: debt-to-GDP ratio rises to 100% by 2038.
Medicare trust fund reaches zero: 2030; Social Security trust fund
zero: 2034.

**Social Breakdown**
White male high school dropouts who have been in prison: 3.8% (1979)
→ 28.0% (2009). Black male high school dropouts in prison: 14.7%
(1979) → 68.0% (2009) — TWO-THIRDS of all black male high school
dropouts spend at least one spell in prison by age 40.

Charles Murray's "Fishtown" data (bottom third of white population):
children living with both biological parents (mothers aged 40): 95%
(1960) → 34% (2010).

**Chapter 18 Conclusion: The Forecast**
Gordon's projection for 2015–2040:
- Labor productivity growth: 1.20%/yr (optimistic)
- H/N change (baby-boom retirement): –0.40%/yr
- Output per person: 0.80%/yr
- Inequality headwind: median grows 0.43%/yr slower than average
- Median real income growth: 0.40%/yr
- Fiscal headwind: additional –0.10%/yr
- BOTTOM LINE: real median disposable income grows only 0.3%/yr
  for 2015–2040 — compared to 2.1%/yr for the near-century 1920–2014

"An exercise in subtraction" — innovation slowdown + four headwinds
convert historically remarkable growth to "barely positive."

## Postscript: America's Growth Achievement and the Path Ahead

"The book's message is not about failure but about unrepeatable and
unique past achievements." U.S. strengths: top 8 of 10 most valuable
companies in world are American; venture capital; DARPA Internet roots;
shale oil/gas; top 30 universities dominated by U.S. institutions;
higher fertility + immigration vs. aging Europe/Japan.

**Policy Recommendations**
- Tax progressivity: top 1% income share tripled from 8% (1974)
  to 22% (2014); proposed "super-bracket" tax rates for earners >$1M
  and >$10M; make capital gains taxed like regular income
- Minimum wage: currently $7.25 = 12% LESS than the 1960s average in
  2014 prices; real compensation per hour rose 115% over 50 years
- EITC: reduces low birth weight, raises math/reading scores, boosts
  college enrollment; expand and make more generous
- Incarceration: U.S. rate 8–10× higher than largest European countries;
  costs $74 billion/year; 60% released from prison unemployed one year
  later
- Drug legalization: cost of prohibition = $88 billion/year
- Preschool: James Heckman — preschool programs yield 7–10%/yr return
  per dollar invested; by age 5 brain already "built the cognitive and
  character skills critical for future success" — too late to intervene
  later. U.S. only 69% of 4-year-olds enrolled (ranked 26th OECD)
- Higher education: Australia model — free college, repay through income
  tax based on % of income; no payment if unemployed
- Regressive regulation: occupational licensing rose from 10% (1970)
  to 30% (2008) of jobs; restrictive zoning inflates housing prices
  in productive metro areas (Glaeser's "regulatory tax")
- Immigration reform: U.S. annual legal immigration = 0.3% of population
  vs. Canada's 0.8%; end policy of denying residency to foreign-born
  graduates of U.S. universities ("self-imposed brain drain")
- Carbon tax: CBO estimates $20/ton CO2 tax would raise $115 billion/yr

Gordon's final assessment: even if ALL proposed policies were
implemented, boost to median disposable income would be "no more than
a few tenths of a percent above the rate without policy changes" — but
would create "more equal, better-educated society."
