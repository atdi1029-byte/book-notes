## Chapter 10: ALCO Governance, Reporting, and Treasury Organisation

### ALCO Governance Model
- ALCO must have formal Terms of Reference (ToR) — succinct, distributed to all management
- Minimum meeting frequency: **once every four weeks** (same day/time each month)
- **Membership** (medium-sized bank): CEO or Deputy CEO; CFO (Chair); Head of Treasury (Deputy Chair); business line heads; CRO; Head of Research; guests = ALM desk head, market/liquidity risk, product control
- CFO chairs (not CEO or business line head); Head of Treasury chairs if Treasury is pure cost centre
- Head of Credit NOT on ALCO — handled at Executive Credit Committee level

### ALCO Operating Agenda (Figure 10.1)
- Review gap limits, actual gaps, sensitivities, recommendations to amend limits
- Review liquidity ratio and other liquidity metrics; adherence to regulatory/internal limits; assess forecasted values
- Review and discuss deposit and funding trends: concentrations, programs, promotion campaigns, forecasts
- Review exceptions/excesses to internal and regulatory policies/limits (reported by Head of Financial Planning)
- Review capital allocation and profit contribution by business line; regulatory capital adequacy forecasts
- Review market environment and potential impact on branch's IRR and trading activities
- Review and approve authorised instruments and permissible hedging/position-taking strategies
- Review recommendations to change policies, objectives, limits
- Review internal FTP arrangements; consider if changes required
- Set standards for measuring/monitoring quantitative limits on all trading activities
- In liaison with CRO, set and review stress testing scenarios
- Review new trading activities under New Products & Services policy
- Act as centre of excellence for all ALM-related policy and governance issues

### ALCO Reporting
- **Monthly pack**: key risk report (liquidity, FX, capital, large exposures); prepared by Middle Office; in larger banks by specialist Finance or Risk Management team
- **Traffic light system**: Green = everything in order, no limits breached; Amber = advance warning, potential problem, keep under watch; Red = urgent issue needing immediate attention (should rarely appear if ALCO process functioning effectively)
- Pack usually sent in PDF electronically; reviewed in hardcopy at meeting
- Board summary: 2-slide PowerPoint — ALM, Liquidity, Capital risk — produced given time constraints at senior level
- Documents circulated **at least one week before** meeting; for extraordinary meetings: documents available to circulation list ASAP
- Each agenda item must be responsibility of a **named individual** who will present

- **Table 10.2**: Cash inflow/outflow report — Sight: inflows 594m, outflows (847m), net (252m); cumulative mismatch grows to significant shortfall by 1–3 year bucket
- **Table 10.3**: Maturity transformation ratio = 14 (limit 24); Top 5 counterparty concentration: Commercial Bank of ABC = 14% (exceeds 10% limit)

### Group ALCO (GALCO) Structure
- **GALCO** recommended for multinational/multi-entity banks
- Structure: Group Treasury → Regional ALCOs (if set up) → Local ALCOs → Local Treasuries
- Branch (no separate legal entity): no local ALCO; Head of Treasury reports direct to Group
- Subsidiary/partnership: local ALCO required; ToR identical to domestic except local does NOT set policy
- **Table 10.4**: Capital approval limits: GALCO = any amount; Group FD = $100m; Group Head of Treasury = $50m; Local ALCO = $10m; Local HoT = $1m

### Treasury Organisation Models
- **Cost centre Treasury**: reports to CFO; manages LAB portfolio, liabilities, balance sheet, ALM policy, internal FTP, Treasury MI
- **Profit centre Treasury**: adds money markets and repo desk, term funding, securitisation; reports to CEO or MD Front Office
- **Four models** (Figures 10.9–10.12):
  1. Front office (P&L + policy) → reports to CEO; financial reporting separates external from internal activities; internal FTP reported in ALCO book, NOT Treasury P&L
  2. Middle office (policy only) → reports to CFO/CRO; no market-facing function; governance may include regulatory reporting
  3. Market-facing with governance (term liabilities, no money markets) → includes Investor Relations; reports to CFO or CEO; risk of policy implementation problems
  4. Large front office (policy + money markets) → DCM/securitisation placed elsewhere in investment bank or DCM division
- **Author's recommendation**: Option 1 — critical capital and liquidity management best placed in strong central Treasury
- **Principal decision**: markets-facing profit centre vs cost centre "middle office"; well-governed institutions benefit from Treasury as markets-facing profit centre
- **Securitisation note**: only true-sale cash securitisation counts as funding; synthetic securitisation has no value as funding transaction
- **Table 10.8**: Bank Operating Functions matrix — strategic management (capital, liquidity, NII/NIM); market-facing (term liabilities, money markets, swaps, collateral, counterparty risk, investor relations); governance (finance and risk management)

### UK FSA Best Practice Guidelines (January 2011)
- ALCO is most important senior management committee in a bank; must always be value-added
- Must be forward-looking, proactive, show strong challenge and debate
- Minutes must summarise extent of discussion — NOT just action points
- **Committee Membership**: CEO or Deputy CEO + CFO chair; all business group heads; CRO; Head of Research/Economics; Head of Internal Audit
- **Nature of discussion**: forward-looking on future plans/strategy at business line level; takes proactive decisions, does not just note risks; delegates authority to sub-committee where appropriate
- ALCO pack should include: market/economic outlook + impact on ALM; liquidity/funding metrics by currency with forecast; stress test results; IRR analysis (modified duration, VaR, NII/NIM sensitivity, basis risk); current funding composition; refinancing risk stress points; liquidity stress scenarios of varying severity; CFP update; required level of granularity to invite challenge from members

### Regional ALCO Reporting
- Banks with multinational presence: best practice to incorporate regional entities into regional ALCO; care — no dilution or duplication of governance (either regional or legal entity ALCO, not both)
- **Table 10.5**: Regional Liquid Asset Buffer summary — per legal entity and currency (AUD, BDT, HKD, IDR, MYR, NZD, TWD, USD); shows assets available in liquidity stress by country
- **Table 10.6**: Regional LTD ratios vs targets — Australia 109% (target 100%), Bangladesh 68% (target 85%), Malaysia 115% (target 100%); shows reliance on parent funding by entity
- **Table 10.7**: Regional currency exposure report — cash flows by currency across maturity buckets (0–1m through >5yr) for AUD, BDT, CNY, HKD, IDR, JPY, MYR, SGD, NZD, TWD, USD

### BSMCO (Balance Sheet Management Committee)
- Recommended for larger banks when ALCO lacks sufficient time to review all policies
- Reviews more technical items; feeds up to ALCO where necessary
- Membership: Head of Balance Sheet Management; Deputy Head of Treasury; Regional/Subsidiary Treasury delegates; IR Risk Management delegates; Chief Economist; Chair = usually Deputy Head of Treasury
- Role: review IRR and liquidity risk from macro perspective; recommend action before expected stress events; overlap membership with ALCO

### Stress Testing and Scenario Analysis
- Stress testing now a **significant part** of bank risk management (post-2007–09); moved from peripheral to central; required by UK FSA as part of ICAAP and liquidity reporting
- **Choudhry paradox**: "the next financial crisis will be different in shape and form to any previous ones — by definition it is not possible to plan for it completely"
- Cannot stress test unknown events; stress testing helps prepare for *types* of scenarios, not specific future events
- Must be embedded in general risk management — NOT become "a procedures manual consigned to a filing cabinet"
- Two practical purposes: (1) inform limit-setting; (2) reduce sensitivities before event occurs
- **Scenario analysis**: hypothetical event applied to current portfolio → measures impact on P&L, capital, liquidity
- **Stress test**: moves market factors by defined amount (e.g. ±200 bps on IR) → measures sensitivity
- **VaR back-testing**: >5% exceedances (at 95% CI) = model mis-specified; 1-day VaR for trading limits; 99% VaR for regulatory capital
- VaR assumes lognormal returns, i.i.d. time series, constant portfolio — all unrealistic; VaR should NOT be sole risk indicator
- Banks scale 1-day VaR to 10-day by multiplying by √10

### Stress Test Policy Framework
- 7 objectives: understand sensitivities; complement VaR; evaluate tail risk; assess firm-wide impact; test assumptions/hidden concentrations; inform limits/capital allocation; meet regulatory requirements (FSA/Basel II)
- **Factors to test**: interest rates (parallel shifts, curve steepening/flattening, basis risk); FX rates (devaluations, peg breaks); security prices; PD migration; LGD/recovery rate changes; concentration risk (single-name, sector, country); funding risk (markets closing); operational risk; capital planning risk; strategic risk
- **Figure 11.15**: Market risk stresses = IR parallel shift ±200bps, twist, FX ±20%; Credit risk stresses = internal ratings migration one/two-notch, four recovery rates; Results measured on P&L, capital, and liquidity impact
- **Table 11.14**: Daily tests (34) cover ±25/50/100/200/300 bps parallel shifts + twist; Monthly capital tests (9): credit migration, recovery rate scenarios; Monthly liquidity tests (12): funding closure 1 week/1 month/3 months, depositor runs
- Credit stress recovery rates: 0%, 20%, 40%, 60%; **most likely = 40%** (Basel II standard assumption)
- **Table 11.15**: Stress test output — change in book PV across named scenarios; breakdown by Bond, Swap, Loan, Deposit, MM-CD, FX
- **Table 11.16**: Treasury P&L impact summary — **Treasury total maximum loss: –EUR 1.409 million** under worst-case stress
- **Economic Downturn Stress Test (Figure 11.17)**:
  - Unstressed CAR: 17.98%
  - Iteration 1 (moderate recession): CAR → 16.62%; P&L impact –£47.5m; survives (>15% internal min); factors: credit migration losses, increased provisions, spread widening on securities, NII compression
  - Iteration 2 (severe recession): CAR → 14.66%; P&L impact –£121.8m; **FAILS** (below 15% minimum); additional factors: higher default rates, further spread widening, funding cost increases
  - Conclusion: bank survives moderate downturn but requires capital action under severe scenario
- **Stressed VaR (SVaR)**: Basel II formula — MRC_t = max(k/60 × ΣVaR) + max(m/60 × ΣSVaR) + STC_t; both k ≥ 3, m ≥ 3; SVaR = static risk measure from 1-year stress observation period; does not incorporate market movements
- SVaR critique (Max Wong, *Bubble Value at Risk*, 2011): stress tests based on pre-specified events and long observation periods over-influenced by past economic cycles; will not accurately indicate impact of next stress event (by definition different in nature)

## Part III: Bank Liquidity Risk Management

## Chapter 12: Principles of Bank Liquidity Management

### Core Thesis
- "Liquidity management is the most important risk management function in banking" — Choudhry
- 2007–08 was as much a **crisis of bank liquidity** as capital erosion
- Government intervention (taxpayer guarantees) prevented systemic collapse after Lehman bankruptcy
- "A bank that has to resort to central bank for funding has failed; it is a failure of management"

### FSA Failures Analysis
- Excessive reliance on short-term wholesale funding and securitisation
- Funding long-dated illiquid assets with short-term wholesale liabilities
- Overseas branches excessively reliant on parental support
- Running down pool of genuinely liquid assets; abolishing liquidity buffers

### Historical Context: Boyd and Gertler (1994)
- Paper on US banking crisis of 1980–82; findings remain fully current; published in Journal of Monetary Economics
- US banking crisis of 1980–82: **larger banks** contributed disproportionately to loan losses
- Two causes: deregulation/financial innovation → increased competition; regulatory environment inadvertently subsidised risk-taking by large banks via TBTF implicit guarantee
- **"Too-big-to-fail"**: well-intentioned (failure of large bank could be contagious, disturb entire financial system) but unfortunate side-effect of subsidising large bank risk-taking
- **"Greenspan Put"**: Fed chairman's actions over 10 years to 2007 assisted markets → subconscious belief large banks could not fail → ever-larger risk-taking during cheap liquidity period
- Balance sheet: rise in share of assets in loans; fall in liquid securities and cash/reserves; reduced reserve requirements → banks reduced precautionary holdings; increased access to short-term money markets → reduced need for liquidity buffers
- Large bank liability structure: money market instruments ~42% of liabilities; **money center banks ~54%**; >half of purchased funds from abroad → smaller NIMs, riskier assets, lower capital/assets ratios
- Northern Rock: precisely this pattern — aggressive expansion relying on non-core deposits
- Boyd & Gertler: Basel I (1988) confronted the implicit subsidy problem; large banks held less capital than they might have otherwise; "the main stress on the system has not been the raw number of failures; rather it has been the poor performance of large banks"
- Choudhry conclusion: liquidity crises are endemic; history repeats itself; banks not learning lessons are at high risk of repeating same mistakes

### 12 Strands of Liquidity Risk Management (Board must understand all)
1. Definition of liquidity risk
2. Role of liquidity risk management
3. Board responsibilities
4. Liquidity strategy, policy and processes
5. Regulatory requirements and reporting obligations
6. Funding strategy and policy
7. Liquidity risk tolerance
8. Institution-specific and market-wide stress scenarios and stress testing
9. Forecasting funding cash flows over different time horizons
10. The liquidity buffer
11. Intra-group and cross-border group lending
12. Liquidity contingency funding plan and stress testing

### Ten Principles of Bank Liquidity Risk Management (HSBC Model)
1. **Fund illiquid assets with core customer deposits** — founding principle; Northern Rock violated this textbook
2. **Where unavailable, use long-term (>1yr) wholesale funding** — appropriate tenor = function of asset maturity
3. **No over-reliance on wholesale funding; more long-term (>1yr) than short-term** — author recommends max 20–30% of balance sheet in <3 month funding; **"ALM smile"**: matching asset/liability tenor profile; abandoning this was "effortless, non-value-added" carry trade with catastrophic post-Lehman results
4. **Maintain liquidity buffers of instantly liquid assets** — sovereign risk-free securities only; bank CDs, FRNs, corporate bonds became illiquid in Q4 2008
5. **Establish a contingency funding plan** — tested regularly; avoid concentration risk
6. **Know and test central bank facilities** — operational procedures must be practised
7. **Be aware of all liability-side exposures** — including reputational obligations (ABCP conduits)
8. **Liquidity risk is an array of metrics — calculate them all** — single indicator (LTD) insufficient
9. **Internal funds pricing (FTP/TLP) must be set correctly** — key driver of risky balance sheet growth pre-crisis
10. **Liquidity risk framework owned by Group Management Board; centralised policy** — no exceptions without Board pre-authorisation

### Additional Principles
- **Type A depositors** (financial institutions, governments, local authorities): higher withdrawal risk in stress; FSA deemed higher likelihood of withdrawal in stressed environment
- **Type B depositors** (retail customers, small businesses, clients >3yrs standing): more stable; lower withdrawal risk
- All legal entities must be "stand-alone" re: liquidity
- Traditional principle: bank creates deposit base (ideally retail) before starting to lend → overseas entities self-sufficient
- Be aware of collateral liquidity under stress: derivatives collateral funded on overnight/daily basis; if short-term markets become illiquid, bank faces funding risk on collateral obligations

### UK FSA Liquidity Framework
- FSA CP08/22 + CP08/24 (2008) → formalised in PS09/16 (Oct 2009): most demanding regulatory response globally
- FSA requirements more onerous than Basel Committee's December 2010 document; NOT new — reiteration of standard practice in commercial banking in previous decades
- Main FSA changes: increased gap/mismatch limits; increased supervisory oversight; increased reporting obligations; revoking behavioural adjustments previously allowed; requirement to hold genuine liquid asset buffers; requirement to increase average tenor and diversify sources
- **ILAA (Individual Liquidity Adequacy Assessment)**: bank assesses own liquidity adequacy; done at least annually; updated whenever strategy/operating model/product/geographic base changes
- ILAA must cover firm-specific, sector-specific, and market-wide stresses; sent to regulator as liquidity report and to Board
- Three FSA-prescribed stress tests: (1) **name-specific shock** — market perceives firm potentially insolvent short-term; long-term: multi-notch credit rating downgrade; (2) **market-wide dislocation** — widespread solvency concerns; certain asset classes remain illiquid for extended period; (3) **combination**
- New regime implications: higher governance; decreased short-term wholesale; greater incentive for retail time deposits; higher LAB (more government bonds); reduced expansion during favourable conditions (countercyclical capital and macroprudential rules per Basel III)

### Liquidity Risk Drivers
- **Money market funds** (CD/CP): most volatile; most likely to disappear in firm- or market-wide stress; may dry up completely
- **Wholesale deposits with rating constraints**: local authorities, government agencies, central banks, money market funds, insurance companies all have strict credit rating guidelines; **downgrade triggers** — deposit withdrawn immediately if bank downgraded below minimum threshold
- **Rate-sensitive depositors**: where no customer relationship exists, highly volatile if deposit rates fall below competition
- **Large corporates with sophisticated Treasury functions**: more volatile than smaller corporates or passive Treasury functions
- **Internet depositors**: least resilient retail funds
- **Secured funding (repo)**: most resilient in stressed conditions; may be only source of funds available in crisis; highly dependent on collateral quality — sub-IG and structured finance collateral may become difficult to repo
- **Derivatives**: mark-to-market deterioration in stress → higher collateral requirements; undrawn commitments drawn at worst possible time — exacerbates bank's funding shortage
- **Cross-currency**: foreign currency shortfalls may lack central bank access; Asian crisis 1997 imposed convertibility restrictions; FX funding exposure reported separately in liquidity MI

### Liquidity Policy Statement
- **Definition**: "the ability to ensure the bank will always be able to maintain or generate sufficient cash resources to meet its payment obligations in full as they fall due, on acceptable terms, under all market conditions"
- Two templates provided (Wiley website): (1) small-to-medium commercial bank; (2) larger banking group with multiple jurisdictions
- **Objectives of liquidity policy**: set policy for measuring/monitoring/managing liquidity risk; set liquidity risk tolerance governance; set approval and review arrangements; document pricing of liquidity risk and FTP/TLP; document intra-day liquidity risk; document collateral cash flow liquidity risk; document relationship to group entity; set diversity requirements; document stress testing; document contingency funding plan (timing, responsibility, extent of use)
- Five pillars: Short-term liquidity / Structural liquidity / Reporting / Stress testing / Contingency plan — all feeding into Liquid Assets Buffer
- **Figure 12.3**: governance structure — Board (ultimate responsibility, defines risk appetite) → ExCo → ALCO (structural + operational liquidity) / Risk (CRO — independent oversight, stress testing) / Treasury (day-to-day operational liquidity) / Finance (reporting and limit-monitoring)
- **Risk appetite**: assets must be transformable to liquid cash within [X] days or (i) fundable from contingency source, (ii) securitisable, (iii) eligible as central bank collateral; BAU funding limited to retail deposits, corporate deposits, long-dated capital market funds
- **Target metrics**: LTD 100%; long-dated funding (>1yr) minimum 25%; minimum LAB size
- **Liquidity buffer requirement in policy**: bank must hold buffer of truly liquid, unencumbered assets funded with long-dated liabilities; cost of buffer passed to business lines proportional to funding requirement and balance sheet liquidity stress created

### Liquidity Value of Liabilities (Table 12.2)
- Each liability type has different liquidity value; behavioural aspect may render it more stable/long-term than contractual maturity implies
- Regulators review liquidity on both contractual AND behavioural basis
- **Retail current accounts**: contractually 1-day but historically customers maintain stable balances → treat proportion as term funding
- Table 12.2 — ranked from highest to lowest liquidity value:

  2. Retail deposit accounts

- Lesson: maximise funding from higher-value liabilities; minimise reliance on wholesale inter-bank funding

### Policy on Intra-group Lending (IGL)
- IGL = credit equivalent exposure between connected group entities
- UK regulatory approach: IGL limit = **25% of lending bank's capital base**; example: EUR 500m capital → EUR 125m IGL limit
- **Integrated Group (IG)**: opt-in; no IGL limit, 0% risk-weighting; requires majority board control, consolidation scope, no legal impediments to capital transfers
- **Non-integrated group (NIG)**: connected counterparties failing IG criteria; aggregate NIG exposures must remain within 25% of large-exposure capital base
- Best practice: all new IGLs authorised by Group Treasury/GALCO

### The Liquid Asset Buffer (LAB)
- Historical practice: held government bonds as standard until 21st century; abandoned as markets took liquidity for granted
- **Key 2008 lesson**: investment-grade bank FRNs, bank CDs — many became illiquid in Q4 2008; **LAB must comprise sovereign bonds only**
- Added benefit: positive regulatory capital treatment; forces tighter liquidity management
- LAB delivers zero or negligible positive return → firms want it as small as possible → argues for sound liquidity regime in first place
- Choudhry debunks myth that conservative LAB is only for large SIFI banks: every bank can become systemically important (Northern Rock; Dunfermline Building Society)
- **UK FSA PS09/16**: LAB of high-quality government bonds, central bank reserves, multilateral development bank bonds — mandatory for all UK banks
- **Eligible LAB securities**: UK gilts; bonds ≥Aa3 from EEA, Canada, Japan, Switzerland, US; reserves at BoE, Fed, ECB, SNB, BoC, BoJ; designated multilateral development banks (ADB, AfDB, EIB, IBRD, etc.); Choudhry also includes Singapore, Hong Kong, Taiwan
- **Two-tier structure**: Level 1 = sovereign (no cap, no haircut); Level 2 = agency/AA-rated corporate/covered bonds (max 40% of LAB; 15% haircut; rated <A– gets 100% haircut i.e. excluded)
- **LAB cannot be funded in repo** — must be funded by long-term unsecured funds (>90 day, ideally >1yr)
- LAB must be demonstrably liquid — bank should use it to generate liquidity periodically; three benefits: (i) demonstrates liquidity; (ii) removes market perception of stress when selling (routine activity); (iii) ensures familiarity with operational processes
- LAB size = function of **90-day stressed funding cash outflow** (FSA) or 30-day (Basel III)
- Regulators insist on higher LAB for firms with structural liquidity problems or following poor liquidity policy
- Multi-jurisdictional banks: must hold multiple LABs (one per jurisdiction/currency) — local authority may impose restrictions on foreign currency movements in crisis

### Central Bank Facilities
- Bank of England: Discount Window Facility (DWF) — gilt lending against eligible collateral; 4 collateral levels: A (sovereign/supranational), B (liquid third-party), C (illiquid third-party), D (own-name securitisations/covered bonds)
- Operational standing facilities, reserve accounts, open market operations also available
- ECB: regular operations + post-2008 extended facilities (3m, 6m, 1yr) — all as repo
- **Critical**: dealing room must practise accessing central bank facilities operationally

### Contingency Funding Plan (CFP)
- Most important element of liquidity risk management; in some jurisdictions a regulatory requirement; contains the "know-how" for rare stress events that bank staff are largely unaccustomed to dealing with
- Must describe: array of viable contingency measures with amounts/lead times/business impact; governance structure and communication plans; steps for critical intra-day payments; MI for Board
- CFP allows bank to: (i) offset management actions against cash outflows in FSA stress scenario; (ii) set a lower LAB size requirement; (iii) generate customer confidence and positively influence credit rating agency review
- **Table 12.7**: CFP action list by business line:
  - Banking/Loans: collateral enablement £100m (High prob); pledging £50m (High); withdrawal of unsettled lines £100m (High); stop/delay pipeline £250m (High); selling £300m (Low)
  - Debt Securities: sell securities £500m; additional repo lines £100m; sell high-yield assets £150m; lengthen repo £100m; diversify repo counterparties £50m
  - ABS/Structured Finance: repo eligible ABS/CMBS/RMBS £200m (Medium prob)
  - Derivatives/MM Trading: cash settle in money market positions at discount £25m (Low)
  - Banking/Loans (Loans): corporate loan line withdrawal £200–250m
  - Treasury Services: delay operational payments £200m (Low); optimise payment systems collateral £150m (Medium)
- FSA ILAA: bank required to hold LAB to offset combined 3-month stress scenario (idiosyncratic in first 2 weeks + market-wide after 3 months); Board-approved CFP required
- Newer alternative funding sources (internet deposits, CD/CP programmes, credit unions, sovereign wealth funds, foreign central bank reserves) are the **most unstable in crisis**; regulators concerned about inadequate deposit growth and over-reliance on non-core funding

### Liquidity Management and Collateral
- ISDA survey 2010: cash formed **83%** of all collateral posted; remainder = eligible sovereign bonds
- Derivatives collateral management = aspect of funding and liquidity management; should be under Treasury function, not treated separately
- Key considerations: policy centralised for whole bank; maximise ability to net margin calls; monitor collateral concentration risk; ensure collateral calls accurate and timely; cross-business-line collateral use requires centralised management
- "In-house securitisation" (common in European banks 2009–2011): repackage loan assets into structured note form → use as collateral at ECB; maximises efficiency but difficult to deploy in market stress

## Chapter 13: Liquidity Risk Metrics

### Six Key Baseline Liquidity Metrics (all banks should calculate)
1. **Loan-to-deposit ratio (LTD)**
2. **1-week and 1-month liquidity ratios**
3. **Cumulative liquidity model**
4. **Liquidity risk factor (LRF)**
5. **Concentration and funding source report**
6. **Inter-entity lending report**

### Metric Details

**LTD Ratio**
- >100% = early warning of excessive asset growth / reliance on wholesale funds
- <70% = excessive liquidity, potentially inadequate return
- **Best-practice range: 95%–105%**
- NOT predictive; does not account for tenor, concentration, volatility — must be used with other measures
- **LTD gap (Customer Funding Gap)** = customer lending minus customer deposits; max aggregate gap limit set per risk appetite

**1-Week and 1-Month Liquidity Ratios**
- Table 13.2 example (4 subsidiaries): Country G: 1-week +53.27% vs 25% limit — **BREACH**; Country F: 1-month –39.11% vs –50% limit — within
- Worsening ratio approaching limit = must drive change in funding strategy

**Cumulative Liquidity Model**
- 12-month forward-looking model of expected cash flows (contractual + behavioural)
- Plots cumulative surplus/deficit vs counterbalancing capacity (LAB + contingent)
- Intersection = stress horizon — must not occur before minimum survival period

**Liquidity Risk Factor (LRF)**
- **LRF = Average assets tenor / Average liabilities tenor**
- Table 13.4 example: avg liabilities 19 days; avg assets 262 days; LRF = **14** (limit 24)
- Rising LRF = growing structural mismatch; trend most important indicator

**Concentration Reports**
- Table 13.5: Commercial Bank of Surrey = 14% of funding vs 10% limit — **BREACH**
- Sector report: Customer-Private 37.4% vs 30% limit — near-breach flagged for monitoring

**Inter-entity Lending Report**

- Regulators may ring-fence local assets during crisis — entity must have standalone adequacy

### Strategic Level Metrics: LCR and NSFR

**LCR (Liquidity Coverage Ratio)**
- **Formula**: Stock of HQLA / Stressed net cash outflows (30 days) > 100%
- UK FSA extends to **90 days** — Choudhry recommends 90-day standard as best practice
- Run-off assumptions: retail stable deposits 5%; retail less stable 10%; unsecured wholesale non-financial operational 25%, all others 75%; financial entities 100%; all secured funding backed by non-Level-1 assets 100%
- 3-notch credit downgrade: 100% additional collateral posting assumed
- ABCP conduits callable within 30 days: 100% drawdown assumption

**NSFR (Net Stable Funding Ratio)**
- **Formula**: Available Stable Funding / Required Stable Funding > 100%
- **ASF weights**: 100% = Tier 1/2 capital, liabilities >1yr; 85% = stable retail/SME <1yr; 70% = less stable retail <1yr; 50% = large corporate <1yr; 0% = wholesale financial <1yr
- **RSF weights**: 0% = cash, short-term instruments; 5% = unpledged HQLA sovereign bonds; 20% = AA-rated corporate/covered bonds; 50% = other corporate bonds, loans to corporates <1yr; 85% = retail loans (non-mortgage) <1yr; 100% = loans >1yr + 10% of undrawn committed lines

**Long-term Liquidity Limits**
- Liabilities/assets maturing >1yr: minimum **98%**
- Liabilities/assets maturing >3yr: minimum **85%**
- Liabilities/assets maturing >5yr: minimum **70%**

### Tactical Level Metrics

**CCP (Cash Capital Position)**
- CCP = TLA – TVL – CTL
- TLA = total liquid assets; TVL = total volatile liabilities; CTL = committed transaction liabilities
- Positive CCP = can survive loss of all volatile liabilities using liquid assets alone

**Market Lock-out Horizon / Survival Period**
- Number of business days bank can remain cash-flow positive with **zero access to unsecured funding**
- Table 13.10: Date 1/11/2010 — survival horizon 23 days vs target 14 days (**Within target**); Date 1/10/2010 — survival horizon 9 days vs target 14 days (**BREACH** — triggered management action)
- Most intuitive metric for senior management and regulators

**Encumbered Asset Ratio**
- Rising encumbrance = fewer unencumbered assets for contingency liquidity
- Figure 13.5: group encumbrance rose from ~5–6% (Jun 2010) to ~12–14% (Nov 2010) — warning sign

### Example 13.1: Tactical and Strategic Metrics at Multi-entity UK Bank
- **Tactical Ratios**: (1) Maximum Cumulative Outflow (MCO): net cash in/outflow over 14/30/90 days under BAU; (2) Wholesale Borrowing Limit: max Treasury can raise in wholesale/interbank; (3) Swapped Funds Limit: FX liquidity limit for non-domestic funds swapped to local currency
- **Strategic Ratios**: (1) LTD ratio; (2) Commitments guideline limit; (3) Medium-term funding ratio (liabilities >1yr / assets >1yr); (4) Liquid assets ratio (cash + marketable securities / total assets); (5) Net inter-bank lending limit — can be set "always positive" so bank is always net lender, never borrower

### Example 13.2: Liquidity Risk Appetite (Real Bank Data)
- Customer deposits: £1,905m (48% of funding — meets >40% target)
- Maturity transformation: avg assets 1,192 days; avg liabilities 39 days; ratio 30x (limit 24x)
- 1-week regulatory limit: >0%; actual 13.95%

### External and Comparative Metrics
- **Market-related monitoring**: equity prices, debt yields and spreads, FX rates (market-wide); equity price, CDS spreads, money-market trading prices, bank-specific funding rates (bank-specific)
- Key example: 3-month Euribor vs EONIA spread — near-zero pre-Aug 2007; spike to ~80 bps following 2007 crisis; difficult to spot until well into it

### Key Conclusion
- No single metric suffices; liquidity management is as much **qualitative as quantitative**
- "Finance is a people/relationship business" — maintain stakeholder relationships during normal times; impossible to build during stress

## Chapter 15: Internal Funds Pricing Policy (FTP)

### Core Concept
- Treasury acts as internal counterparty for all business lines; centralises IR/basis/funding mismatch risk
- FTP also called: **Funds Transfer Pricing (FTP)**, **Firm Liquidity Pricing (FLP)**, **Term Liquidity Premium (TLP)**
- **Key driver** of capital allocation and business decisions; poor FTP was central cause of risky balance sheet growth pre-crisis
- CEBS (2008): "Institutions should have an adequate internal mechanism incorporating all costs of liquidity from short to long term, including contingent risk"

### Pre-Crisis Failures
- **UBS CDO desk**: funded itself at below-market internal rates; management viewed realistic funding as "constraint on growth"; reported "super profits" that were actually **funding arbitrage**; profits were illusory — borrowing at low internal rate, investing in illiquid structured finance
- **KBC FP**: received liquidity from AA-rated parent at Libor + **12.5 bps** uniform rate; funded leveraged structured products (2yr+ maturities) on short-term rolling basis → severe stress post-Lehman
- Common pre-crisis approach: Libor + 15 bps for ALL business lines and ALL tenors — failed to account for differing liquidity profiles

### FTP Framework
- **Best practice**: Gross approach at marginal cost — most effective for risk management
- **Two key options**: (1) gross vs net approach (gross = all assets/liabilities charged/credited; net = only net position charged); (2) marginal vs actual cost
- Gross approach at marginal cost preferred: individual business lines can't employ funds they raise themselves; discipline most rigorous; risk management most effective
- FTP charge should include: direct costs (interest cost of raising funds) AND indirect costs (CFP + LAB costs)
- Indirect costs: (i) mismatch liquidity cost; (ii) cost of contingent liquidity risk (LAB); (iii) other (country risk)
- Formal objectives: consistent liquidity pricing; remove IR risk from business lines; include cost of liquidity in product pricing
- Policy owned by Board, delegated to ALCO, reviewed semi-annually
- **Author's critique of CEBS approach**: setting 5-year loan FTP at 5-year funding cost ignores that banks are in the maturity transformation business and would rarely undertake matched funding; more practical to have graduated TLP curve reflecting actual rollover risk

### FTP in a Group Structure
- Group Treasury may have more than one pricing curve; one baseline funding curve in reporting currency; overseas businesses pass relevant FTP charge from Group Treasury; individual businesses may each have different COF (retail bank raises term funds below group marginal COF; investment bank raises at COF but lends below COF)
- **Dynamic FTP** (Figure 15.2): (A) neutral ALM smile → FTP = COF curve; (B) funding gap at long end → steeper FTP curve to incentivise long-term liability raising; (C) surplus long-term funding → flatter curve; (D) blended WACC rate
- Behavioural analysis: if term deposit shown 80% sticky at 3 years → 3-year FTP applies to 80%, shorter-term FTP to remaining 20%

### FTP Curve (Table 15.1)
| Tenor | GBP (bps) | EUR (bps) | USD (bps) |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0–6mo | 0 | 0 | 5 |
| 6–12mo | 5 | 5 | 10 |
| 1–2yr | 15 | 10 | 20 |
| 3yr | 35 | 20 | 40 |
| 5yr | 50 | 40 | 75 |
| 10yr | 100 | 70 | 125 |
| >10yr | 135 | 90 | 150 |

- **Key insight**: beyond ~5 years, incremental liquidity stress per additional year becomes minor; FTP curve can be **flat beyond 5 years** (Table 15.5: 5yr=50 bps, 6yr=51, 7yr=52... >10yr=60)
- Trading book assets: FTP charged at **expected holding duration**, not legal maturity; Tier 1 (G7 bonds) = 1-week/1-month FTP; Tier 3 (below A-/A3) = 6-month FTP
- Securitisable assets: **50% of relevant tenor FTP** (asset is partially liquid)
- Committed back-up lines: 35–75 bps depending on rating and tenor; highest because drawn at worst possible time
- ABCP conduits: FTP at tenor of **longest dated asset** in conduit

### Dynamic FTP
- Figure 15.2: FTP curve adjustments:
  - A: ALM smile → FTP = bank's COF curve (neutral)
  - B: Funding gap at long end → FTP curve steeper than COF (incentivise raising long-term liabilities)
  - C: Surplus long-term funding → FTP curve flatter than COF
  - D: Blended WACC rate — where business line asset pricing straddles DCM and Retail Bank COF

### Derivatives Collateral Funding
- Pre-2008: OIS–Libor spread averaged 2–3 bps short-dated, 12–15 bps long-dated; banks paid little attention; post-2008: FRA/OIS basis reached **364 bps in USD (Oct 2008)**; EUR/USD OIS-Libor averaged 35–40 bps in 2011 (peaked ~175 bps late 2008/early 2009); 3mo–6mo Libor spread avg 25–30 bps
- **Key rule**: collateralised derivatives → discount at **OIS rate** (not Libor); LCH also adopted OIS discounting
- Multi-currency CSA: collateral poster will choose currency with lowest funding cost → creates **collateral option**; bank must construct hybrid curve comparing OIS, EONIA, SONIA; adjust for cross-currency basis
- **Figure 15.4**: EUR vanilla IRS vs 6mo Euribor: 1yr=1.369, 5yr=2.575, 10yr=3.388; EUR EONIA: 1yr=0.811, 5yr=2.074, 10yr=2.963; difference material even at short tenors
- Uncollateralised derivatives: discount at bank's **TLP/COF curve** (bank COF = issuer zero-coupon bond price × derivative payoff)
- **Bank COF splits into four categories**: (1) secured ST (OIS — lowest, irrelevant for uncollateralised); (2) secured LT (covered bonds, MBS); (3) short-term unsecured COF (0–12 month); (4) long-term unsecured COF (**TLP** — 2–10 year)
- **CVA formula**: CVA = (1–R) × Σ qᵢvᵢ; DVA = ENE × Credit Spread; Funding Cost = (EPE + ENE) × Derivative Funding Spread
- **Recommendation**: uncollateralised derivative assets discounted at relevant tenor TLP; fund collateral postings substantially in term funding (not wholly short-term); if funded short-term → liquidity squeeze risk
- Table 15.3: net collateral position report — total EUR 2.3 billion; GBP largest (EUR 1.47bn, dominated by cash EUR 850m)

### Proxies for TLP
- (1) Difference between funded rate (bond ASW) and unfunded rate (swap) at same tenor
- (2) Difference between risk-free rate and swap rate
- (3) Difference between fixed rate on term IRS vs OIS swap of same tenor
- (4) Incremental increase in bank's COF per tenor step; e.g. 1yr=20 bps, 2yr=30, 3yr=35, 4yr=40, 5yr=50; take increase at each step (credit risk element theoretically near-identical at each step, so increment ≈ liquidity premium)
- (5) CDS–ASW basis (should not be used in isolation)
- **Recommendation**: base FTP on average of all above; element of judgement always required; FTP reviewed every 6 months

### CEBS FTP Guidelines (5 Key Guidelines)
- **Guideline 1**: Liquidity cost benefit allocation is an important part of the whole liquidity management framework; FTP integral to bank ALM; rate must be commensurate with correct allocation of costs; each bank must have clearly articulated definition of risk tolerance re: internal funds pricing
- **Guideline 2**: Must have proper governance structure; "owned" by senior management, delegated to ALCO, explicit approval and annual review process; CEBS: *"the function ultimately charged with implementing FTP should be service-oriented and not have a profit target for this specific role"*; if Treasury is P&L centre, FTP setting should be under Finance
- **Guideline 3**: Output must be actively and properly used; applied at "coal face" by transaction managers; rationale understood by everyone; "Liquidity pricing methodology should compensate providers of liquidity and charge users" — same TLP grid applies to both
- **Guideline 4**: Scope must be comprehensive — covers all significant assets, liabilities, off-balance sheet items; retail call deposits — if bank can show >50% stability over 5 years including crisis → can treat half as long-term; committed credit lines charged to reflect cost of supplying funds at worst possible time; FTP rates reviewed regularly
- **Guideline 5**: Determined by robust methodologies; bank sets internal curve with respect to Libor/Euribor; for >1yr tenors refers to swap curve; bank should note own CDS curve; if no secondary market debt at some tenors, construct via term structure model; fundamental principle: product approval process must be closely integrated with internal pricing

### Template FTP Policy
- FTP policy facing inertia/resistance from business lines; must be Board-owned, ALCO-delegated, Finance-implemented
- Example FTP matrix: liabilities <6mo = Libor; 6-12mo = Libor+10 bps; 1-5yr = Libor+30 bps; >5yr = Libor+50 bps
- No bid-offer spread in principle (internal pricing function should operate to zero P&L target); if both ways, spread should be 1–2 bps maximum

## Part IV: Bank Strategy and Governance

## Chapter 16: Bank Strategy I — Formulating Strategy and Direction

### The Sustainable Bank Business Model
- Five fundamentals (unchanged since banking began): (1) managing capital (leverage); (2) managing liquidity mismatch (maturity transformation); (3) the gap (fund short, lend long); (4) liquidity assumption (ability to roll over funding); (5) credit/default risk management
- Pre-crash: banks targeted 22–25% RoE; not tenable long-term; new, lower RoE targets must be set
- **Market share is NOT a value-added KPI for banks** — emphasis on market share drove Northern Rock and B&B to demise

### Leverage Ratios
- Basel III introduces explicit leverage ratio limit
- **Lehman Brothers**: leverage ratio rose from 24:1 (2003) to **31:1+ (2007)**; 3–4% asset value decline would eliminate equity — and did
- European banks: ~48x leverage in 2007; US banks: ~44x
- Canada and Australia: imposed leverage ratio limits and did NOT suffer bank crashes in 2008
- Figure 16.3: Canada limit = Tier 1+2 ≥5% of on-BS assets; Switzerland = Tier 1 ≥3–4%; US = Tier 1 ≥3–4%

### Capital Structure Recommendations
- **Countercyclical capital management**: treat capital as scarce at all times; build up during bull markets; capital must cushion "stress events" — outlier events that normal distributions don't capture
- **Contingent capital (CoCo)**: converts to equity when Tier 1 falls below pre-specified trigger; should be the only sophisticated instrument in capital structure; promotes investor transparency
- Simple capital structure: common equity + retained profits; long-dated vanilla debt acceptable but should also be vanilla
- BoE (2009): average **8.5% Tier 1** needed to avoid breaching 4% minimum during last crisis; for some firms up to **18%**; even with 8.5% Tier 1, 40% of sample banks would still have breached minimum
- **Lloyds ECNs (2009)**: GBP 13.5bn; 12% coupon; converts at 5% Tier 1 trigger at 50% of par — first real-world CoCo; "pre-funded rights share issue"
- **FSA Bail-In regime**: senior unsecured debt absorbs losses after equity eroded; investors demand higher premium; removes taxpayer liability from automatic bailout
- Future capital structure (Figure 16.5): Equity → Senior unsecured debt → Customer deposits (above guarantee level) → Secured funding (covered bonds, term repo, ABS/RMBS)

### Core Competence: "Know Your Risk"
- BoE Financial Stability Report (June 2009): considerable decline in cross-border lending flows after Lehman; banks pulled back from overseas markets irrespective of whether peripheral or not
- **Cross-border bank lending volumes** (USD billion): grew from ~$500bn (2000) to ~$2,500bn (2007 peak); crashed to approximately –$1,500bn (2009) — negative flow
- **UK corporate lending**: UK banks lending fell ~16% (2002–2009); foreign subsidiaries fell 10.5–20%; foreign branches fell 17–**46%** — most dramatic retreat; foreign banks have less depth of corporate relationships
- Cross-currency swap market in USD came under pressure post-Lehman → many banks struggled to obtain dollar funding even domestically
- Lesson: **during expansionary phase, remain focused on core competencies** where bank has actual knowledge and competitive advantage; concentrating on strengths makes it less likely loan origination standards will decline
- Technical reason for focus on domestic: overseas lending often in foreign currency, more dependent on external markets and wholesale counterparties → more vulnerable to market downturn

### UK ICB (September 2011)
- **Ring-fence**: retail/SME/overdraft inside; corporate banking/trade finance/EEA business permitted inside; trading/wholesale/proprietary derivatives/non-EEA outside
- Each part meets capital/liquidity requirements **stand-alone basis**; inside/outside can deal up to 25% Tier 1 large exposure limit
- **Capital regime**: Basel III core Tier 1 (7%) + ring-fence buffer (3%) + Basel III other T1 (3.5%) + PLAC bail-in/CoCo (3.5%) + resolution buffer (3%) = up to **20% total**
- ICB compliance deadline: **2019**

### Strategy Setting and Performance Parameters
- Vision statement elements: stable commercial bank; **RoE 12–14%** through cycle; **AA–/Aa2** rating; revenue from customer business; robust risk management; cost base limit at [x]% of revenue
- This vision statement almost explicitly restricts proprietary trading business
- Bank strategic plan: business focus — home market, eurozone, GCC; customer base — corporate/FI and HNWI retail; wholesale funding share limit: **20%**; management focus: cost base limit, explicit balance sheet usage metric, RoC target 12–14%
- **Base KPIs**: Capital (RoC, RoE, RAROC, assets/capital); Liquidity (LTD, liquidity ratio, wholesale funding ratio); Cost base (front/back-office ratio, cost-income ratio); Risk appetite (provisions/lending, NPLs/lending, VaR); Growth (asset growth, liability growth)
- Note: some KPIs are maxima (control targets), not minima; market share is explicitly NOT a KPI
- "Sustained 12% RoE over 10–15 years is infinitely preferable to several years of 22% RoE followed by losses"
- **Table 16.2** (quarterly performance report example): Core Tier 1 actual 7% vs 3yr target 10%; RoE 9.80% vs 11%; wholesale funding 33% vs 20%; leverage 23:1 vs 15:1; cost-income 66% vs 50%
- 5-year targets only useful in broadest terms (at vision statement level) due to dynamic markets; 3-year rolling plan is optimal

### Countercyclical Funding
- Lesson: banks should take advantage of "benign" conditions to improve funding structures
- **Sterling Libor–OIS spread**: near-zero in 2007; rose to ~130 bps mid-2007; dipped; spiked to ~245 bps peak late 2008; fell to near zero by Oct 2009 — low spread periods are opportune moments to extend maturity and take on longer-term funding
- UK banks reduced reliance on ≤1-week funding from **15%** (Dec 2008) to **9%** (Oct 2009) of unsecured wholesale
- Aggregate customer funding gap fell from **GBP 842bn** (Q4 2008) to **GBP 610bn** (Q2 2009) = 18% of loans (lowest since 2003)
- Canada/Japan: **negative** customer funding gaps (deposits > loans) — did not suffer 2008 crashes; Figure 16.9: Spain +40%, Italy +42%, Germany +38%, UK +24%, France +22%, US +5%, Japan –25%, Canada –30%
- ~50% of UK bank aggregate wholesale funding was <6 months maturity in 2009
- UK retail deposit spread: rose from below Libor (pre-2008) to almost **200 bps over Libor** by 2009; risk: paying excessively above market rates conveys impression bank is in difficulty
- Bank funding strategy must target: (1) reduce reliance on wholesale funding; (2) increase average tenor of liabilities

### Early Warning Indicators
- **Stock and Watson (1989) LEI model** recommended; principal forward-looking metric; should be reported monthly at ALCO, ExCo, and BSMCO
- 9 LEI inputs (UK): building societies new commitments, CBI order book volume, CBI expected output, FT All Share Index, gilt 10yr–3mo yield spread, productivity (whole economy), total gross operating surplus of corporations, M2, CEI
- 4 CEI inputs: index of production, unemployment rate, household disposable income (real), retail sales
- Figure 16.15: all four LEI versions successfully flagged 1990–91 and 2008–09 UK recessions; LEI2 and LEI3 produce smoother time series
- When indicator signals overheating: tighten loan origination standards, increase capital buffers, secure more term funding
- Also monitor: private sector credit/GDP ratio; credit-to-GDP gap; rate of increase in retail lending; RoE on bank capital vs long-run averages; **Baltic Dry Index** (bulk carrier prices — reached all-time high May 2008 = start UK/EU recession); **world trade volumes** month-on-month growth rate

### CCB Formula (Example 16.4)
- **Formula**: CCB = Σ(W_i × CCB_i) where W = weighting (proportion of total capital exposure), CCB_i = countercyclical buffer for each jurisdiction i
- CCB example: 50% domestic/20% France/20% US/10% GCC at 2%/2.5%/1.5%/2% = **CCB 2.000%**
- Individual bank should run more conservative: LCR at 90-day instead of 30-day; NSFR at 110–120% instead of 100%; leverage ratio own stricter limit

### Funding Tools (Chapter 16)
- Beyond vanilla (deposits, CDs, MTNs): **Tri-party repo**; **TRS (Total Return Swaps)**; **existing assets "collateral upgrade"**; **liquidity option transactions**
- **Liquidity Option Facility** (Example 16.5): bank pays premium granting option to swap portfolios with counterparty at any time; counterparty holds AAA sovereign bonds in third-party custody; on exercise, bank puts overcollateralised portfolio as collateral, receives high-quality bonds for use in repo or as LAB; counts as term funds for liquidity metrics equal to tenor of option; bank pays: option premium (life of trade) + upgrade fee on exercise

### Liquidity as Asset Class
- Capital is necessary but **not sufficient** for sustainable banking — liquidity equally important
- Two benefits of preserving liquidity: (i) viewed as better quality counterparty → reduces funding costs; (ii) able to generate revenue from liquidity strength
- Proxy measures: (1) repo margin/asset spread ratio — was ~1:5 (20%) during 2005–2007; increased to ~1:10 by Q1 2009; (2) iBoxx collateralised bonds spread: stable ~20 bps 2003–07; rose to **200+ bps** Q1 2009; (3) CDS–ASW basis — contains factor for cash liquidity premium; increasing basis indicates higher premium for liquidity

### Chapter 16 Conclusions
- Financial markets are indispensable to economy and well-being; regulation must preserve stability while allowing innovation
- Increasing capital levels is necessary but NOT sufficient — liquidity equally important
- **Simplest capital structure = most efficient**: pure equity + retained profits + senior unsubordinated bonds + deposits
- New liability model: (i) less reliance on wholesale funding; (ii) less reliance on short-term funding; (iii) more diversified funding base; (iv) genuine self-sufficiency in funding
- Through-the-cycle strategy cannot include obsession with maximising RoC, market share, or league table positioning

## Chapter 17: Bank Strategy II — Capital and Funding Management

### Capital Management
- Core Tier 1 capital: must be parked in central bank deposit or sovereign bonds — only qualifying zero-risk assets
- Capital income from government bonds goes to **central ALCO book** (not business lines — would skew RoE)
- Business lines do NOT utilise capital to fund operations → no explicit cost to allocate; but they cover the cost of maintaining capital through their rate of return
- RoE target (set by shareholder) = hurdle rate for each business line; can be uniform minimum or modified for different business requirements
- Subordinated debt: three cost elements — (1) Libor base; (2) term premium → Treasury P&L, reallocated to business lines; (3) subordinated premium → consistent with RoE on initial capital
- Share capital benefit (zero real interest cost): allocated to central ALCO book, NOT to individual business lines; treatment must be consistent with retained earnings — both zero real cost
- Capital management policy objectives: (1) meet regulatory obligations; (2) maintain capital resources and buffer in line with risk profile; (3) manage capital planning efficiently

### Capital Approval Process
- ALCO = unlimited authority; Finance Director = [x]m; Head of Treasury = [x]m; Business line head = [x]m
- RWA balances reported weekly to Finance, Treasury, and business lines; inconsistency with Finance forecast reported to ALCO
- Business restructuring (any transfer or disposal impacting regulatory capital) must be approved in advance by ALCO

### Systemic Risk and Moral Hazard
- **FSA definition**: "when its collapse would impair provision of credit and financial services with significant negative consequences for the real economy"
- Three types: (1) by size; (2) by interconnection (inter-bank/clearing systems); (3) by association — market views one firm as representative of a group; failure of one seen as potential failure of all
- Author: "virtually all banks are potential areas of systemic risk" — implications for all banks, not just large ones
- Bear Stearns had large exposure to hedge funds with low-grade assets — demonstrated that non-TBTF banks can create significant turbulence
- **LoLR moral hazard**: (1) signals to depositors that central bank will back private banks → no need to withdraw; (2) encourages depositors to seek highest rates → banks compete by taking more asset-side risk
- Northern Rock: paid higher deposit rates than "high street" banks due to more aggressive credit portfolio
- LoLR is nonetheless necessary: October 2008 — without government guarantees, asset prices would have fallen further, investment/consumption drop would have been more severe
- **1907 banking crisis precedent**: J.P. Morgan and other banks injected own private money to guarantee market liquidity — historical example of private sector LoLR alternative; led to founding of US Federal Reserve six years later
- **Industry-funded liquidity mechanism** (Choudhry 2009): IMF-style pool funded by all banking licence holders; biggest inter-bank users contribute most; private trade association operates and manages; removes taxpayer liability; incentivises banks toward conservatism
- **Lehman Brothers capitalised at 11% Tier 1 at collapse** — acceptable to regulators; demonstrates capital alone is insufficient

### Macroprudential Regulation
- When one bank fails, entire industry is potentially at risk → must oversee soundness of industry as whole
- **Pro-cyclicality**: lower loan standards in growth → eases credit supply → boosts asset prices; then amplifies recession when credit withdrawn; this was the error of Northern Rock, Bradford & Bingley, HBOS
- Macroprudential tools: (1) adjust Tier 1 equity requirement during excess growth; (2) adjust liquidity ratio to reduce short-term wholesale reliance; raising capital requirements targeting specific loan types (otherwise banks shift to higher-risk activities)
- **Liquidity ratio as key macroprudential tool**: more conservative LCR for banks with large asset-liability gaps forces them not to originate beyond their ability to fund
- Addressing interconnection: (1) strict liquidity ratio limits; (2) global central clearing for OTC derivatives (Dodd-Frank); (3) "International Money Exchange" clearing house for inter-bank (Choudhry 2009) — would eliminate bilateral counterparty risk; (4) reduce leverage; (5) higher capital ratios; (6) loss-absorbing capital instruments
- **Living wills**: TBTF banks must have orderly wind-down plan; overseas operations separately capitalised

### Countercyclical Capital Buffer (CCB) in Strategy
- Bank should set its own procedures to build CCB during excess growth without waiting for regulatory pressure
- Figure 16.12: aggregate UK banks customer funding gap grew from near-zero (1997) to ~£650bn (2008) — textbook herd mentality
- What should NOT be part of strategy: ever-greater risk taking and leverage; over-reliance on wholesale funding; exposure to higher-risk products (100 LTV mortgages, buy-to-let); emphasis on market share and high RoE as KPI targets

### Rating Agency Frameworks
- **Moody's BFSR (Bank Financial Strength Rating)**: measures likelihood bank requires assistance to avoid default; 4 factors: (1) structure of assets/liabilities — how illiquid assets funded by stable liabilities; (2) extent illiquid assets funded by wholesale/inter-bank funds; (3) ability to access short-term market during disruption; (4) effectiveness of internal liquidity risk management (A to E scale)
- **Moody's liquidity ratio** = (Market funds – Liquid assets) / Total assets; where Market funds = long + short-term debt + inter-bank liabilities
  - Excellent: <–10%; Very good: >–10% to 0%; Satisfactory: 0% to 10%; Modest: >10% to 20%; Poor: >20%
- BFSR scoring: 50% qualitative (management framework) + 50% financial; of financial half, liquidity = 8%; management framework (5.1%) weighted MORE than ratio itself (2.8%) — reflects that process matters as much as outcome
- Figure 17.4 peer group example: Our Bank –15% = Excellent; B1 –5% = Very good; B2 0% = Satisfactory; B3 3% = Satisfactory; B4 11% = Modest
- **Standard & Poor's**: does not publish separate liquidity rating; assesses within overall bank rating; uses survival horizon model under "run on the bank" scenario; standard asset haircuts and liability run-off rates; Level 1 (primary: cash, bank deposits, govt securities, ABS, IG corporate bonds); Level 2 (secondary: securitisation, syndications, loan sales, contingent facilities)
- **Fitch**: 5 structural metrics — (1) loans/deposits; (2) loans/(deposits + ST funding); (3) liquid assets/total assets; (4) liquid assets/wholesale funding; (5) wholesale funding/total funding and capital

### Integrated Capital and Liquidity Management Strategy
- Basel III environment requires integrating capital and liquidity at highest strategy level
- Strategy level capital considerations: review business model for Basel III compatibility (growing/maintaining/exiting); ensure appropriate incentive scheme; ensure infrastructure enables timely capital reporting; review if existing models should be restructured for capital efficiency (branch vs subsidiary)
- Tactical capital: business lines charged correctly for capital costs; Basel III implications considered for all new proposals; review long-dated business from capital efficiency/RoA viewpoint; regular (not one-off) review exercise
- **Figure 17.7**: Formulating capital management strategy — external factors (regulatory, investors, rating agencies) + risk-reward profile (risk appetite, earnings volatility preference) + economic capital → Bank 3-year capital and funding plan + stress testing → business lines (targets, incentives, limits); ROE/EPS flows both ways
- Capital management framework must communicate: how risk appetite aligns to stakeholder needs; how capital allocation integrated with funding; tolerance for earnings volatility; framework for business line risk exposure

### Basel Pillar III / ICAAP
- ICAAP undertaken at least annually; presented to ExCo and Board; submitted to FSA; describes how risks assessed, controlled, monitored, mitigated and reported
- Example Pillar III (BANK plc): standardised approach to credit, market, and operational risk; primary objectives: comply with externally imposed requirements; maintain healthy ratios to support business
- Board set capital buffer of **10% above regulatory minimum**
- **Key note (footnote)**: Lehman Brothers was capitalised at **11% Tier 1 at time of collapse** — a level acceptable to regulatory authorities; demonstrates capital alone is insufficient

### Capital vs Liquidity Trade-off (Example 17.2)
- Bank A: 5% equity, 6-month avg debt, 10% liquid assets → better liquidity position
- Bank B: 20% equity, 1-month avg debt, 5% liquid assets → better capital but worse liquidity
- Paradox: if Bank A holds high-risk assets, it may be riskier because capital may fall below minimum; but in a liquidity crisis, Bank A is in better position (longer tenor liabilities)
- "A liquidity event will finish a bank as a going concern if it cannot roll over liabilities — the riskier bank is actually Bank B (well-capitalised but short-dated liabilities)"
- Lehman Brothers was capitalised at 11% Tier 1 at time of collapse — acceptable to regulators; demonstrates capital alone is insufficient
- **Author's recommended position**: 10% equity; 12-month avg debt; 10% liquid assets; loans avg 24 months

### Liquidity Management Strategy (Chapter 17)
- Closely integrated with capital management; policy document must include CFP, liquidity risk tolerances, and tactical plan for diversifying funding sources
- Specific metric targets example: maintain liquidity buffer at 115% of 90-day regulatory stressed cash outflows; LTD ratio 95%; LAB of $10 billion; maximum short-term wholesale funding of $1 billion
- Liquidity risk appetite must be made explicit; reported at ExCo and ALCO level
- Regulatory requirements always considered when assessing profitability on existing and new business
- Robust and wide-ranging stress testing + scenario planning; results acted on through mitigating actions and enhanced CFP

### Contingent Capital Instruments
- Must be loss-absorbing on going-concern basis; coupons cancellable; principal write-down capable
- Up to 50% Tier 1 for convertibles with no call feature; up to 35% for write-down hybrids; up to 15% for step-up/SPV-issued
- **Lloyds ECNs**: GBP 13.5bn; 12% coupon; converts at 5% Tier 1 trigger at 50% of par

## Chapter 18: Principles of Corporate Governance

### Core Thesis
- Author: if one must identify a single factor for the 2007–09 crash, it was a **failure of corporate governance**
- "Poor judgement, allied with not inconsiderable incompetence, led to the financial crash being much worse than it need have been"

### Introduction: Why Governance Is Central
- 2007–09 crisis: 10–12 significant causal factors interacted; but if one must identify a single factor specific to banking, it was a **failure of corporate governance**
- "Poor judgement, allied with not inconsiderable incompetence, led to the financial crash being much worse than it need have been"
- Banks are critical infrastructure; when banks mobilise and allocate funds well, cost of capital is lowered for all market participants → increases capital formation and productivity growth
- Chapter reviews: conduct of boards and senior management; governance infrastructure; lessons for policy; sources of risk as determinants of governance structure; recommends more effective framework

### BIS (1999) Bank Corporate Governance (5 elements)
1. Setting bank's objectives including target RoE
2. Control framework for daily operations
3. Protecting customer deposits
4. Strategy accounting for all stakeholders
5. Maintaining bank as going concern through the business cycle
- Crash: at many banks, objectives (iv) and (v) failed completely; in some cases all five

### Academic Evidence
- **Saunders et al. (1990)**: bank ownership and management structure influence risk-taking
- **Levine (2004)**: importance of strengthening private investor ability to exert governance control; shareholder education vital; **debt holders on Board** would improve governance — they don't enjoy upside from risk-taking but suffer on downside; state-owned banks are NOT a solution to inadequate governance
- **Laeven & Levine (2007)**: traditional regulatory policy does NOT drive risk aversion; Basel II capital requirements and supervisory oversight do NOT appear to reduce risk taking; bank shareholders are NOT a risk control device — rather the opposite; large shareholders have incentives to increase risk exposure after collecting deposits and debt
- **House of Commons Treasury Committee**: NEDs lacked relevant banking expertise; "cosy club"; combined Board roles with full-time employment and other directorships; recommendations: (i) enlarge talent pool; (ii) restrict NEDs to limited directorships; (iii) Board supported by dedicated secretariat; (iv) all board members demonstrate sufficient expertise before appointment
- Most significant finding: "bank shareholders do NOT subject boards to sufficient scrutiny; shareholders have incentive to increase risk exposure, particularly after period of increasing returns"
- Average European bank board: 11.8 members; Belgium 12.7, UK 8.5 — both suffered crises; **board form does NOT prevent failure**
- Heidrick and Struggles (2009): UK banks rated highest on corporate governance yet suffered possibly the most serious banking crisis; Denmark (low governance rating) experienced fewer problems → board form uncorrelated with outcomes

### Case Studies: Governance Failures

**Northern Rock**
- CEO Adam Applegarth: aggressive growth; H1 2007 originated GBP 10.7bn mortgages (+47% y-o-y) = **19% of ALL new UK mortgages**
- Products: 100% LTV and 125% LTV mortgages — assumed house prices never fall
- Two governance failures: (1) market share emphasis over funding; (2) asset growth funded by wholesale/securitisation markets
- **Chairman Matt Ridley**: background as zoologist and science writer; **no prior banking experience** — fundamental qualification failure
- When rumours reached inter-bank market → credit lines withdrawn Aug–Sep 2007 → nationalised

**HBOS**
- CEO **Andy Hornby**: consulting → manufacturing (Blue Circle) → Asda → George (clothing) → Halifax Retail → HBOS CEO 2006; **no recession experience in banking**
- Corporate loans: GBP 12bn (2005) → **GBP 70bn** (2008); losses GBP 6.7bn from corporate lending out of GBP 10bn total
- Market share is valid KPI in retail; NOT in banking — emphasis drove unsustainable growth

**Dunfermline Building Society**
- Conservative Scottish building society; from **2004**: diversified into commercial property and MBS assets
- Purchased MBS from GMAC and **Lehman Brothers subsidiary**; write-downs over **50% of notional** in 2008
- Chairman Jim Faulds to parliament: "the responsibility is solely the responsibility of the Board" — yet claimed "no toxic assets"
- IT project: GBP 31.4m invested (vs capital base <GBP 300m) in "Dunfermline Solutions"; wrote off GBP 9.5m; reduced 2007 operating profit from GBP 11.5m to GBP 2m; described as "excellent progress" in annual report — parliamentary commission called this "disingenuous"
- Nationalised 2009; deposit business → Nationwide

**UBS**
- USD 59bn government bailout end 2008 + USD 5.2bn capital injection
- Flawed internal funding: structured credit desk funded at artificially low internal transfer price; management viewed realistic funding model as "constraint on growth strategy"
- Board lacked expertise to supervise derivatives subsidiary; did not identify the fundamental flaw

**KBC Bank**
- EUR 2.5bn + EUR 2bn equity injections + EUR 20bn guarantees from Belgian/Flemish governments
- Board unaware of "Atomium" SPV (USD 5bn ABS structure including sub-prime); FP CDO business had grown to **>EUR 20bn** — Board unaware
- 5 years of RoE exceeding **22%** → management complacency; trades below 20% RoR rejected
- Transfer price: **Libor + 12.5 bps uniform** for all instruments/tenors; funded leveraged structured products at short-term rolling rates → severe post-Lehman stress

**Lehman Brothers**
- Cult of personality: CEO Dick Fuld and president Joe Gregory — "remote" and "denial" frequently used in descriptions; megalomania, ego, and envy drove decisions
- Risk management head asked to **leave the room** during high-risk strategic acquisitions discussions
- Fuld's envy of ex-Lehman MDs at Blackstone (Peterson, Schwarz) drove investments in hedge funds, energy, commodities, leveraged mortgages
- Leverage: 24:1 (2003) → 31:1+ (2007)

### Board Structure Observations
- Three board structures globally: (1) **Unitary** (UK, Spain) — non-exec chair + NEDs + CEO + executive directors; (2) **Two-tier** (Germany, Switzerland, Holland) — separate supervisory and executive boards; (3) **Mixed** (Belgium)
- Critical observation: all these countries except Germany suffered banking crises in 2008; many German landesbanks required federal government rescue
- Heidrick and Struggles (2009): UK banks rated highest on corporate governance yet suffered possibly the most serious banking crisis
- Average board size in European banks: 11.8 members; Belgium 12.7, UK 8.5 — both suffered crises
- VIX spiked from stable low volatility to **80.06** on 10/27/08 within weeks; Morgan Stanley CDS spiked from ~200 bps to **1,108 bps** on 10/8/08; pace of events confirms monthly board meetings were inadequate
- Solution: "Alpha committee" sub-structure enabling rapid senior management response to changing events
- "A rubber stamp Board is not acceptable"

### Conclusions on Governance Failures
- Common thread across all failed firms: failure of corporate governance despite diversity in size, culture, market participation, and strategy
- Senior management acted in ways that suggest firm failure would follow inevitably in any market correction
- **Transparency** critical: opacity of balance sheets (off-balance sheet exposures, securitisations) was major factor
- Muradoglu: *"Herding and underestimation of low probability high impact events are all parts of human nature. Human nature will not change. Thus, we need better regulations."*
- De Bondt et al. (2010): in pursuit of short-term super profits, governance culture was not sufficient to prevent management from "bending financial reports in their favour"
- **CEOs with consulting/retail background**: HBOS and Bradford & Bingley CEOs both had consulting industry backgrounds; "not best practice to appoint CEOs with background in retail where market share is an important KPI"

### Policy Recommendations
1. **Debt holder representation**: at least one Board member must be representative of major debt holders; "short-term quarterly reporting culture forces boards to focus on share price and peer comparison → lower risk discipline"
2. **Expert knowledge**: financial services expertise prerequisite for all board members (NEDs and executive directors including CEOs); regulator approves only suitably qualified persons; expertise reviewed annually
3. **Clear raison d'être**: Board must articulate clear, coherent strategy explicitly outlining areas of business competence; Northern Rock and DBS were drawn into higher-risk sectors without such articulation
4. **Board size**: 6–10 persons more effective than 12–20; larger boards reduce personal accountability; each member takes refuge in collective position — makes it harder to restrain management and cult of personality
5. **Alpha-team**: CEO, CFO, one business line head, Treasury head, one or two NEDs — meets 1–2 days every month for agile crisis response; full Board meeting once every 4–6 weeks is administratively difficult to increase beyond that
6. **Expertise of sub-committees**: Board Audit & Risk committees only composed of those with proven bank experience and expertise gained over the business cycle
7. **Transparency**: regulators enforce annual requirement that management communicate to shareholders in precise and clear fashion the extent of bank's risk and losses; DBS described a GBP 9.5m loss as "excellent progress"

### Governance Structure and Committee Overview
- Figure 18.4: Board → CEO, COO, CRO, Finance, Treasury, Business line heads, Alpha Team; CRO independent — "dotted line" to CEO only for communications; Internal Audit reports to Board
- Figure 18.5: Board → Alpha Committee → ExCo → (Board Audit & Risk; Executive Credit; Executive Risk; Management Committee) → ALCO → BSMCO

**Table 18.1 — Committee Terms of Reference**:
- **Board of Directors**: sets medium/long-term strategy; defines high-level business model; sets target ROE/ROC/ROA; monitors risk exposure and stress scenarios; assumes overall responsibility for liquidity risk and specifying tolerance
- **Alpha Committee**: smaller committee of principal executives + NEDs; executive authority from Board; meets during crisis or stress periods; meets 1–2 days per month; responds to fast-moving events where monthly board meeting is too slow
- **ExCo**: implements Board-approved strategy across business lines; manages risk within tolerance; sets limits at business line level; manages regulatory relationships; approves capital allocation; responsible for stewardship of capital
- **Board Audit & Risk Committee**: delegated Board authority over bank finance, accounting policies, internal control, and regulatory reporting; investigates bank business with recommendations; reports on all risk and control issues; undertakes audit committee role
- **Executive Credit Committee**: reviews capital allocation and credit limit approvals; delegates to ALCO and Credit Committees; approves credit and loss provisions
- **Executive Risk Committee**: delegated authority to oversee risk and control at strategic and tactical level; reviews risk tolerances for regulatory, funding, and liquidity risk
- **ALCO**: as discussed in Part II; meets more frequently than Board; weekly if Alpha Committee is meeting
- **BSMCO**: technical advisory committee feeding into ALCO; reviews strategic, external market, peer group, and regulatory developments; monitors capital state, requirements, customer deposits, NIM, net IR risk; monitors external macroeconomic indicators; meets weekly when Alpha Committee is meeting

### Social Contract
- **Paradox of aggregation**: what one bank can do, all cannot; during market corrections all banks trying to raise liquidity simultaneously causes asset prices to fall — this is a systemic risk feature requiring minimum standards for all banks (efficient market hypothesis does not hold during market corrections)
- Banks receive banking licence, LoLR, deposit insurance — in exchange for managing business to prevent bank runs and systemic instability
- "Banks should not need to be required by law to operate in a way that is sensible; rather, they should seek to ensure they do in any case"
- "The right thing is not exclusively a profit target"
