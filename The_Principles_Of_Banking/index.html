Part I: A Primer on Banking

The opening section of The Principles of Banking serves as both orientation and manifesto. Choudhry opens his preface with a reference to Frank Herbert’s Dune, where the Fremen of the desert planet Arrakis revere water as “the water of life” — the most precious substance in their world. For banks, he argues, that substance is liquidity. Every institution that failed during 2007–2009 had lost sight of this elemental truth.

The preface lays out five deceptively simple principles that Choudhry considers the irreducible foundation of sound banking: secure your funding base before lending beyond your deposit base; fund the majority of loans from core, stable customer deposits; lend prudently and with genuine expertise; maintain capital sufficient to absorb both expected and unexpected losses; and — in case anyone missed the point — see principle one again. The fifth principle being a repeat of the first is not a joke. It is the thesis of the entire book.

Chapter 1: A Primer on Bank Business and Balance Sheet Risk

Choudhry begins at the beginning: what is a bank, and how does it make money? The answer is deceptively simple. A bank takes deposits and makes loans, earning the spread between the interest it pays on funding and the interest it charges on lending. This net interest income accounts for sixty to eighty percent of revenue at traditional retail and commercial banks, and can reach eighty percent or more at building societies. Even at the most complex multinational banking groups, the fundamental business model has not changed since ancient Babylon.

Beyond net interest income, banks earn from fees and commissions (valued by management for their stability and low capital consumption) and trading income (the most volatile of the three). The proportions vary dramatically:

Bank Type Net Interest Fees Trading
UK building society 80%18%2%
European regional bank 62%27%11%
US investment bank (London) 22%52%26%
But the principle holds across all types: revenue always flows from the interaction of assets and liabilities, and risk accompanies every revenue stream.

The chapter introduces the gap — the difference in maturity between a bank’s assets and its liabilities — as the central concept of the entire book. A UK building society advancing a twenty-five-year mortgage funds it with deposits that rarely have a contractual maturity beyond two or three years. This maturity transformation is not an unfortunate side effect of banking; it IS the business of banking. It is also the source of every form of liquidity risk the rest of the book will examine. Choudhry calls understanding the gap and meeting its needs “the cornerstone of banking risk management.”

The gap naturally produces a reliance on a positively sloping yield curve: banks borrow at shorter, lower rates and lend at longer, higher ones. When the curve inverts, or when short-term rates rise faster than long-term lending rates can be repriced, the spread compresses and sometimes turns negative. In a low or declining rate environment, banks may suffer regardless of their maturity profile, because it is difficult to pass rate cuts through to depositors who are already earning minimal returns.

Bank capital is introduced as the equity cushion that absorbs losses and allows the bank to continue as a going concern. Average equity is only eight to twelve percent of total balance sheet, making banks among the most leveraged institutions in any economy. Choudhry is emphatic on a principle that he says too many banks violate: capital should never be lent to customers. It exists solely as a loss buffer and should be invested only in risk-free assets. The income from that capital goes to the central ALCO book and ultimately to shareholders — it must not be credited to individual business lines, as that would distort their return calculations.

John Hicks’s concept of the “constitutional weakness” of financial markets frames the theoretical foundation: investors want liquidity and instant access, while borrowers want the longest possible term. Banks bridge this gap through intermediation, accepting the twin risks of bank runs (many depositors withdrawing simultaneously) and credit losses (many borrowers defaulting simultaneously). They manage these risks by diversifying across wide pools of assets and liabilities — a principle that works until correlations spike toward one during a systemic crisis.

The chapter presents detailed ratio analysis:

Ratio Formula Benchmark
Pre-tax ROE Pre-tax income / Avg equity >20% = strong
ROE Net income / Avg equity >10% = strong
ROA Net income / Avg assets >1% = strong
Cost-income Non-interest costs / Net revenues Lower = better
Net interest margin NII / Avg earning assets Yield minus funding cost
Choudhry makes a pointed argument that return on assets is a superior measure to return on equity, because ROE rewards dangerous leverage. A bank can inflate its ROE simply by increasing its balance sheet relative to equity — exactly the behavior that brought down Lehman Brothers, whose leverage ratio climbed from 24:1 in 2003 to over 31:1 by 2007.

The RAROC (risk-adjusted return on capital) framework is introduced as the proper way to compare business lines with different risk profiles. If a business line’s RAROC falls below the bank’s cost of equity, that line is destroying shareholder value and should reprice or exit. Worked examples demonstrate how thin the margins in banking actually are.

Scenario Assumptions Break-Even Loan Rate
Simple (no defaults) Loan 100, Equity 10, Deposits 90, Deposit rate 5%, Target ROE 10%, Tax 20% 5.75% (only 75 bps of margin)
With credit risk Same + 5% default probability in Year 2, 40% recovery 11.1% (more than doubles)

The jump from 5.75% to 11.1% when credit risk is introduced demonstrates how dramatically default assumptions affect loan pricing. A bank that ignores default probability in its pricing — or uses optimistic assumptions during a boom — will discover too late that its apparently profitable loans were actually under-priced. “One would be surprised just how many banks do not observe this very basic principle,” Choudhry observes.

The chapter closes with a taxonomy of bank risks: credit risk (default and deterioration), market risk (particularly interest-rate risk, the dominant form for banking books), liquidity risk (both funding and market varieties), operational risk (citing Nick Leeson and Kweku Adoboli as examples), and model risk, which Choudhry calls literally fatal based on 2007–2009 experience. He introduces the ALCO (asset-liability committee) as “the most important committee in a bank — more important than the Board itself” — a provocative claim he will defend across multiple chapters.

Chapter 2: Bank Regulatory Capital

Regulatory capital is the framework that governments impose to ensure banks hold enough cushion to absorb losses without requiring taxpayer bailouts. Choudhry traces this framework through three iterations: Basel I (1988), Basel II (2004), and Basel III (2010), each responding to the failures of its predecessor. The evolution reveals a recurring pattern: regulators create rules, banks find ways to game them, a crisis exposes the gaming, and new rules emerge.

Basel I established the now-familiar eight percent minimum capital requirement against risk-weighted assets. Risk weights were deliberately crude:

Risk Weight Asset Type
0% Cash; own sovereign bonds; Zone A (OECD) government debt
20% Interbank lending; multilateral development bank claims
50% Residential mortgages; residential MBS
100% ALL corporate loans (regardless of rating)
Tier 1 capital (shareholders’ equity and non-cumulative preference shares) had to constitute at least half the total, with Tier 2 (subordinated debt, cumulative preference shares, and hybrid instruments) making up the balance.

The blanket one hundred percent weight for all corporate loans created a perverse incentive that Choudhry highlights as Basel I’s critical flaw: since a loan to a AAA-rated corporation consumed exactly the same amount of capital as a loan to a junk-rated borrower, banks had a powerful economic incentive to lend to riskier borrowers, where the higher interest rate produced a superior return on the same capital charge. This single design flaw drove much of the credit risk accumulation that would later prove catastrophic.

The chapter works through detailed numerical examples. ABC Bank PLC holds a portfolio of Treasury bills (zero percent weight), interbank loans (twenty percent), mortgages (fifty percent), and commercial loans (one hundred percent). Total assets of 2,536 million pounds produce risk-weighted assets of 1,298 million, requiring capital of 103.84 million. The contrast between fully collateralised repo (zero capital charge) and unsecured lending to the same counterparty (800,000 pounds) demonstrates how dramatically risk weighting affects capital consumption.

Basel II introduced three pillars: minimum capital (Pillar 1), supervisory review (Pillar 2), and market disclosure (Pillar 3). The standardised approach replaced blanket weights with ratings-based differentiations:

Asset AAA–AA A BBB BB–B <B Unrated
Sovereign 0%20%50% 100%150%100%
Banks 20%20%20% 50%150%20%
Corporates 20%50%100% 100%150%100%
The Internal Ratings-Based approach allowed sophisticated banks to use their own models, theoretically producing more accurate capital charges. But Basel II carried its own anomalies: unrated corporates attracted one hundred percent weight, lower than the 150 percent for below-B borrowers, creating an incentive to avoid rating altogether.

The chapter covers the mechanics of trading book capital, off-balance-sheet derivatives (which attract lower capital charges because principal is not exchanged), and operational risk charges. Basel III is presented as the crisis response: stricter definitions of qualifying capital, higher minimum ratios, a leverage ratio cap, and for the first time, explicit liquidity requirements through the LCR (Liquidity Coverage Ratio) and NSFR (Net Stable Funding Ratio). The countercyclical capital buffer — zero to 2.5 percent additional capital that regulators can impose during periods of excessive credit growth — represents an attempt to break the pro-cyclical pattern that amplified every previous crisis.

Choudhry presents a worked capital calculation and introduces the concept of return on regulatory capital, showing how different business lines can be compared on a risk-adjusted basis. He emphasises that the evolution from Basel I through III reveals a fundamental truth: crude but universal rules (Basel I) are gamed through securitisation; more risk-sensitive but complex rules (Basel II) create new opportunities for regulatory arbitrage; and comprehensive rules addressing both capital and liquidity (Basel III) represent the most promising approach, though they too will inevitably be tested by the next crisis.

A single data point anchors the entire discussion: Lehman Brothers was capitalised at eleven percent Tier 1 at the moment of its collapse — a level that was perfectly acceptable to its regulators. Capital requirements alone, no matter how sophisticated, cannot prevent bank failure if liquidity management is neglected. This observation anticipates the liquidity-focused chapters that form the book’s technical core.

Chapter 3: Banking and Credit Risk

Credit risk is the oldest and most fundamental risk in banking: the possibility that a borrower will fail to repay. Choudhry examines it through three lenses — default probability, loss given default, and the credit value-at-risk framework that attempts to quantify portfolio-wide credit exposure. The chapter is especially valuable for its treatment of how credit risk was systematically mispriced in the years before the crash.

The discussion begins with credit ratings, tracing the parallel scales of Moody’s, Standard & Poor’s, and Fitch. Investment-grade ratings (Baa3/BBB- and above) are distinguished from speculative grade, with detailed transition matrices showing the probability of migration between categories. A Moody’s matrix reveals that an A-rated credit has a 91.05 percent probability of remaining A after one year, a 5.87 percent chance of being upgraded to Aa, and a 0.06 percent probability of defaulting — small but not zero.

Internal bank rating systems are presented as a necessary complement to external agencies. The crisis demonstrated that rating agencies were subject to conflicts of interest (paid by issuers) and methodological failures (historical correlation assumptions that broke down under stress). Banks must develop their own credit assessment capabilities, mapping internal scores to agency equivalents but not relying on them blindly.

Credit Value-at-Risk extends market VaR concepts to credit portfolios. The variance-covariance approach applies, but with a critical complication: credit loss distributions are heavily skewed, with a long tail of large losses that normal distributions fail to capture. Choudhry works through the mechanics of expected loss (probability of default times loss given default times exposure at default) and unexpected loss (the tail risk that capital must absorb).

The treatment of credit limit setting is practical and specific. Single-name limits, sector limits, country limits, and maturity limits must all be set and monitored. The author emphasises that limits should reflect the bank’s actual risk appetite, not merely regulatory requirements, and that ALCO should receive regular reporting on credit concentration.

Loan origination standards receive pointed attention. Choudhry describes SVA (shareholder value added) as the most appropriate metric for evaluating whether a loan is properly priced: if the RAROC exceeds the cost of capital, the loan creates value; if not, it should be repriced or declined. “That banks do not do this on a regular basis is a failing of senior management,” he writes.

The discussion of CDS recovery rates is particularly prescient. The standard assumption of forty percent recovery (later revised to fifty percent) drove CDS pricing models, yet actual recovery rates varied wildly across industries and rarely matched the assumed value. Choudhry’s recommended approach for hedging: assume zero percent recovery, because in default, accounting rules require immediate write-down of the full par value, and actual recovery payments can take months to years to materialise.

The market-implied default probability approximation — CDS spread divided by one minus recovery rate — works reasonably when default probability is low but becomes unreliable precisely when accuracy matters most: during recessions and bear markets when defaults cluster. This is a recurring theme in Choudhry’s treatment of quantitative methods: models work until they are needed most.

Chapter 4: A Primer on Securitisation

Securitisation — the process of packaging loans into tradeable securities — is presented as both a legitimate funding tool and a mechanism that, when misused, contributed directly to the financial crisis. The chapter traces the process from origination through to the creation of special purpose vehicles, the tranching of cash flows, and the credit enhancement structures that allow senior tranches to achieve higher ratings than the underlying assets.

The Airways No. 1 Limited case study illustrates a real securitisation structure in detail. An airline securitises its future ticket receivables: a special purpose vehicle purchases the right to future cash flows, issues rated notes to investors, and uses the ticket revenues to service the debt. The structure requires credit enhancement (over- collateralisation, reserve accounts, liquidity facilities) to achieve the target rating. This worked example demonstrates how securitisation can genuinely transfer risk and create new funding sources for originators.

The post-2008 securitisation market receives substantial treatment. The market effectively shut down during the crisis, as investors lost confidence in the complexity and opacity of structured products. Rating agencies came under severe criticism for assigning AAA ratings to tranches that subsequently suffered catastrophic losses. Choudhry notes the irony that securitisation was originally developed to reduce bank risk through diversification, but in practice it concentrated risk in opaque vehicles that even their creators did not fully understand.

Synthetic securitisation — using credit default swaps rather than true sale to transfer risk — is distinguished from cash securitisation. The critical distinction for ALM purposes: only true-sale cash securitisation counts as funding, because synthetic structures do not remove assets from the balance sheet or provide new cash. This distinction became important as banks increasingly used synthetic structures to manage regulatory capital rather than genuine risk transfer.

The chapter addresses the Basel III treatment of securitisation, including the significant risk transfer test (banks must demonstrate genuine economic risk transfer, not merely regulatory capital reduction) and the tension between retention requirements (originators must retain a portion of the risk) and the SRT test. Choudhry also distinguishes securitisation from covered bonds, noting that covered bonds remain on the originator’s balance sheet and benefit from dual recourse — both to the cover pool and to the issuing bank — making them a more conservative funding instrument.

In-house securitisation, where banks package their own loans into structured form solely for use as collateral at central banks (common in European banks during 2009–2011), is flagged as a practice that increases balance sheet interconnection with the central bank without creating genuine diversification. The lesson from the crisis is clear: securitisation is a powerful funding tool when used transparently and with genuine risk transfer, but becomes dangerous when used primarily for regulatory arbitrage or to generate illusory profits from mispriced funding.

Part II: Bank Asset and Liability Management

Chapter 5: The Yield Curve

This is the book’s technical centrepiece — a sprawling, densely mathematical chapter with three parts and seven appendices that Choudhry considers essential reading for every senior banker. His reasoning is straightforward: the yield curve drives everything. A bank sets the price of its assets and the cost of its liabilities from what its own internal risky yield curve implies. Without understanding the curve, a banker cannot properly value any instrument on the balance sheet.

Part I covers the foundations. The yield to maturity curve is the simplest and most commonly observed, but it has a fundamental flaw: it assumes all cash flows are reinvested at the same rate, which is almost never true. The zero-coupon (or spot) curve eliminates this assumption by pricing each cash flow at its own discount rate. Bootstrapping — the technique of deriving spot rates sequentially from observed bond prices — is worked through step by step. The process starts with the shortest maturity, where the spot rate is directly observable, then uses each derived rate to solve for the next one along the curve.

A simplified bootstrapping example illustrates the logic. Suppose a 1-year zero-coupon bond trades at 96.15, implying a 1-year spot rate of 4.0%. A 2-year bond with a 5% coupon trades at par (100). Its first coupon of 5 is discounted at the known 1-year spot rate (4.0%), leaving a present value of 4.808 for the first cash flow. The remaining 95.192 must equal 105 discounted at the unknown 2-year spot rate, which solves to approximately 5.03%. Each successive maturity is bootstrapped from the rates already derived.

Maturity Coupon Price Spot Rate Discount Factor
1 year 0% (zero) 96.15 4.00% 0.9615
2 year 5% 100.00 5.03% 0.9065
3 year 6% 101.50 5.80% 0.8453

The discount factor column is particularly important for practitioners: it represents the present value of one unit of currency received at that maturity. Multiplying any future cash flow by its discount factor gives the present value directly. The entire pricing framework for bonds, swaps, and derivatives rests on this discount function.

The forward curve is derived from spot rates and represents the market’s implied future interest rates. The critical insight: forward rates are not predictions of where rates will go. They are the rates at which an investor is indifferent between investing for the full period at the spot rate or investing for a shorter period and rolling at the implied forward rate. This break-even principle is the mathematical foundation of the no-arbitrage framework that underlies all fixed-income pricing.

A concrete example makes this tangible. If the 1-year spot rate is 4.0% and the 2-year spot rate is 5.03%, the implied 1-year forward rate starting in one year can be calculated: (1.0503)² / (1.04) − 1 = approximately 6.07%. This means that an investor earning 5.03% per year for two years earns exactly the same total return as an investor earning 4.0% for one year and then 6.07% for the second year. If the actual 1-year rate in one year turns out to be lower than 6.07%, the 2-year investor did better; if higher, the 1-year-and-roll strategy wins. The forward rate is the knife edge between these two outcomes.

Choudhry emphasises that this does not mean the market “expects” rates to be 6.07% next year. The expectations hypothesis claims this, but the liquidity preference theory argues that forward rates contain a term premium — compensation for the additional risk of holding longer-dated bonds. In practice, forward rates systematically overestimate future spot rates, suggesting that liquidity preference effects dominate pure expectations in most market conditions.

A normal (upward-sloping) curve reflects liquidity preference and expectations of economic expansion. An inverted curve is historically one of the most reliable recession predictors.

Part II examines yield curve theories. The expectations hypothesis holds that forward rates equal expected future spot rates, implying that the yield curve shape reflects nothing more than market expectations for rates. The liquidity preference theory (Hicks) argues that investors demand a premium for holding longer-dated bonds, creating a natural upward bias in the curve. The market segmentation hypothesis suggests that different maturity sectors are dominated by different institutional participants (pension funds at the long end, money market funds at the short end) whose supply and demand conditions are independent.

Choudhry presents evidence that none of these theories alone explains observed curve behavior. The most practical framework combines elements of all three: expectations drive the general level, liquidity preference explains the typical upward slope, and segmentation effects create local supply and demand distortions. An inverted yield curve — where short rates exceed long rates — has historically been one of the most reliable recession indicators, reflecting expectations of future rate cuts by central banks in response to anticipated economic weakness.

Part III addresses curve fitting — the mathematical challenge of constructing a smooth, continuous yield curve from discrete market observations. Nelson-Siegel and its extension Svensson are parametric models that fit the curve with a small number of parameters, producing smooth results but sometimes struggling at the very short end. Spline methods (cubic polynomial, B-splines, and cubic spline interpolation) fit the curve piecewise, offering greater flexibility but requiring careful smoothing to avoid spurious oscillation.

The seven appendices cover Jensen’s inequality and the shape of the yield curve, testing the unbiased expectations hypothesis, ordinary least squares regression, forward rate structure proofs, regression splines, cubic spline interpolation, and a summary of collateral classes. These appendices constitute a self-contained graduate-level treatment of curve mathematics, reflecting Choudhry’s academic credentials and his belief that senior management must understand the mathematics driving their balance sheets.

The chapter also covers the swap spread — the difference between swap rates and government bond yields at the same maturity — as a measure of credit and liquidity conditions. In normal markets, the swap spread is positive (swap rates above government yields) reflecting the credit risk of the banking sector embedded in Libor-based swaps. Choudhry examines the historical relationship between swap spreads and the VIX volatility index, finding a strong positive correlation: when market fear rises, swap spreads widen as banks are perceived as riskier relative to sovereigns.

The relationship between government borrowing and swap spreads is counterintuitive. When governments issue large volumes of debt, bond yields rise, but swap rates may not move as much because the banking sector’s credit risk has not changed. This can compress or even invert the swap spread, as occurred in certain maturities during periods of heavy government issuance. Understanding these dynamics is essential for ALM practitioners who use swaps to hedge interest-rate risk on government bond portfolios.

The OIS (overnight indexed swap) discounting framework represents perhaps the most significant technical shift in fixed-income pricing since the crisis. Before 2008, the Libor–OIS spread averaged two to three basis points at short maturities and twelve to fifteen basis points at longer ones — banks treated them as essentially interchangeable. In October 2008, the FRA–OIS basis reached 364 basis points in USD, revealing that Libor and OIS were fundamentally different rates reflecting different credit and liquidity conditions.

Period USD Libor–OIS Spread
Pre-August 2007 2–3 bps
August 2007 (crisis onset) ~80 bps
October 2008 (peak) 364 bps
2011 (new normal) 35–40 bps

Post-crisis, the market consensus shifted: collateralised derivatives must be discounted at the OIS rate, not Libor. LCH Clearnet adopted OIS discounting, and banks that continued using Libor-based curves for collateralised trades were systematically mispricing their portfolios. Multi-currency CSA (Credit Support Annex) arrangements added further complexity: the collateral poster chooses the currency with the lowest funding cost, creating an embedded collateral option that must be valued separately.

The secured funding curve is introduced as a practical alternative for pricing collateralised transactions, reflecting the post-crisis reality that unsecured and secured funding markets have permanently diverged.

Chapter 6: Asset-Liability Management I

With the theoretical foundations laid, Choudhry turns to the mechanics of what he considers the core function of banking. Asset-liability management is defined as the practice of managing the balance sheet to generate adequate, stable returns while maintaining liquidity and capital within acceptable risk parameters. It is not a profit centre; it is a survival function.

The liquidity gap is the fundamental tool. A gap report arrays all assets and liabilities by maturity bucket and calculates the net position in each. A negative gap at a given tenor means the bank has more liabilities maturing than assets — it will need to find replacement funding. A positive gap means excess liquidity that needs redeployment. The cumulative gap profile reveals the bank’s overall funding position and where the pressure points lie.

Choudhry introduces the ALM smile — the ideal shape of a bank’s maturity profile where asset and liability tenors are reasonably well matched, with a controlled degree of transformation that generates spread without creating excessive rollover risk. Banks that abandoned the ALM smile before the crisis, running massive maturity mismatches to capture the carry between short-term funding and long-term lending, were engaged in what he calls an “effortless, non-value-added carry trade” that generated apparent profits until Lehman’s collapse shut the wholesale funding markets overnight.

The chapter introduces behaviouralisation — the practice of treating deposits with short contractual maturities as having longer effective maturities based on historical behavior. Retail current accounts that are contractually withdrawable on demand may, in practice, show stable balances for years. The proportion that can be treated as “core” (long-term) versus “volatile” (short-term) is a critical assumption that regulators and ALM practitioners debate continuously.

Detailed case studies work through real-world ALM policy implementation. Case Study 6.1 presents a full ALM policy for a securities firm, including gap profiles, enhanced policy frameworks, and the debate between weighted average cost (WAC) and pool funding approaches. Case Study 6.2 examines an FRN (floating-rate note) liquidity book portfolio. Case Study 6.3 constructs a UK gilt portfolio specifically designed for interest-rate hedging. These case studies demonstrate that ALM is not a theoretical exercise but a daily operational discipline requiring specific policies, limits, and reporting structures.

The liquidity ratio — liquid assets divided by short-term liabilities — is presented as a simple but essential monitoring tool. Choudhry emphasises that no single metric captures liquidity risk adequately; a battery of measures is required, each illuminating a different dimension of the bank’s funding position. The maturity gap report, the cumulative gap profile, the liquidity ratio, and the funding concentration report together provide senior management with the minimum information needed to manage the balance sheet responsibly.

Chapter 7: Asset-Liability Management II

The second ALM chapter dives deeper into interest-rate risk — the risk that changes in market rates will affect the bank’s income or the economic value of its equity. Interest-rate risk is the dominant market risk for most banks, far more significant than foreign exchange or equity price risk, because interest rates affect every asset and liability on the balance sheet simultaneously.

Two complementary measures are presented: earnings sensitivity (how NII changes when rates move) and economic value sensitivity (how the present value of equity changes). These can give conflicting signals — a bank might see NII increase from a rate rise while simultaneously suffering a decline in economic value due to duration mismatches. Senior management must monitor both and understand the trade-offs.

The ALM desk is described as the operational centre of risk management, responsible for implementing ALCO decisions, managing the hedging programme, and running the internal funds transfer pricing mechanism. Choudhry outlines two models: the ALM desk as pure cost centre (managing risk to zero and passing all funding costs to business lines) and the ALM desk as a constrained profit centre (permitted to take limited positions within ALCO-approved risk parameters while still performing its core risk management function).

Gap analysis, while fundamental, has limitations that the chapter addresses directly. It assumes parallel yield curve shifts (all rates move by the same amount), ignores basis risk (the possibility that different rates on the balance sheet move by different amounts), and treats each time bucket as independent. More sophisticated approaches — duration gap analysis, simulation models, and option-adjusted frameworks — address these limitations but add complexity and model risk.

The cost of funding section introduces the concept that not all liabilities have the same economic cost, even when they carry the same nominal interest rate. A demand deposit paying two percent has different behavioral characteristics than a two-year fixed deposit paying two percent. The demand deposit might be withdrawn tomorrow; the fixed deposit is locked in. The internal funds transfer pricing framework must capture these differences to ensure that business lines are charged accurately for the funding they consume and the liquidity risk they create.

Securitisation is revisited from an ALM perspective. When a bank securitises assets, it removes them from the balance sheet (in a true-sale structure) and replaces illiquid loans with cash. This reduces the gap, improves the liquidity profile, and frees up capital — but only if the securitisation achieves genuine risk transfer. Retained interests, first-loss tranches, and implicit support arrangements can negate much of the ALM benefit. The chapter emphasises that securitisation should be viewed as a funding and risk management tool, not as a profit centre.

The chapter concludes with a discussion of middle-office treasury procedures, emphasising that ALM requires robust operational infrastructure: daily position reporting, limit monitoring, exception handling, and independent valuation. The middle office must be genuinely independent of the front office and report either to the CFO or CRO, never to the Head of Trading. This organisational principle was violated at several institutions that subsequently failed.

Chapter 8: ALM III — Trading and Hedging Principles

The third ALM chapter is the most technical, covering the mechanics of how banks actually hedge their interest-rate and credit risk exposures. The trading approach — taking outright positions based on a view of future rates — is distinguished from the hedging approach, where the objective is to neutralise existing risk rather than profit from directional bets. For the ALM desk, hedging is the primary mandate, though controlled position-taking may be permitted within strict limits.

Repo market specials trading receives detailed treatment. A security trading “special” in the repo market can be borrowed at rates significantly below general collateral rates, creating profit opportunities for those who hold it. The mechanics of general collateral (GC) repo, special repo, and the relationship between repo rates and bond yields are worked through with specific market examples. Understanding repo markets is essential for ALM practitioners because repo is the primary mechanism for funding bond portfolios and generating short-term liquidity.

Interest-rate hedging tools are surveyed comprehensively: forward rate agreements (FRAs), interest-rate futures, interest-rate swaps, and options. Each instrument is evaluated in terms of its effectiveness at neutralising specific types of interest-rate exposure. FRAs and futures hedge short-term rate risk; swaps transform fixed-rate exposures to floating or vice versa across the full maturity spectrum; options provide asymmetric protection at the cost of a premium.

The futures strip hedge is worked through as a detailed case study. A bank expecting to borrow at a future date sells a strip of futures contracts across successive quarterly delivery dates to lock in its effective borrowing rate. The “strip rate” is calculated as the geometric mean of implied forward rates, and the example demonstrates how the hedge converges to the target rate as the futures contracts settle against actual Libor fixings. The concept of the “tail” — the P&L effect from the timing mismatch between futures settlement and the underlying borrowing — is explained as a practical consideration that textbooks often overlook.

Interest-rate swap hedging applications cover the most common ALM use case: converting fixed-rate assets funded by floating-rate liabilities (or vice versa) to eliminate the interest-rate gap. Choudhry works through swap mechanics including the fixed-rate calculation, day count conventions, and the distinction between par swaps, off-market swaps, and amortising swaps.

Bond futures contracts and their use in hedging are covered with specific reference to the cheapest-to-deliver (CTD) mechanism and the delivery option embedded in government bond futures. Modified duration and PV01 (present value of a basis point) are presented as the primary hedge ratio measures, with the relationship between the two made explicit.

The chapter closes with credit risk hedging using credit default swaps. A CDS provides insurance against default, with the protection buyer paying a periodic premium to the protection seller in exchange for a contingent payment if a credit event occurs. The basis between CDS spreads and cash bond spreads is examined, along with the practical challenges of basis risk in hedging credit exposure.

Chapter 9: ALM IV — The ALCO

Choudhry devotes an entire chapter to the committee he considers the most important in any bank. The ALCO (asset-liability committee) is responsible for overseeing the entire balance sheet: setting risk limits, approving hedging strategies, establishing internal funds pricing, monitoring liquidity and capital adequacy, and ensuring that the bank can survive stressed conditions.

The chapter presents a complete ALCO policy framework, beginning with the terms of reference that must be formally documented and distributed to all management. ALCO should meet at minimum once every four weeks, on the same day and time, with a fixed agenda that covers gap limits, liquidity metrics, deposit and funding trends, exceptions to limits, capital allocation, market conditions, and FTP arrangements.

Membership is specified precisely: the CEO or Deputy CEO, the CFO (who should chair), the Head of Treasury, all business line heads, the CRO, and the Head of Research or Economics. Notably, the Head of Credit is not on ALCO — credit decisions are handled by a separate Executive Credit Committee. The CFO chairs rather than the CEO or a business line head, ensuring independence from both operational management and commercial interests.

The chapter draws on UK FSA best practice guidelines to specify what effective ALCO governance looks like. Discussions must be forward-looking, proactive, and show strong challenge and debate. Minutes must summarise the extent of discussion, not merely list action points. Documents must be circulated at least one week before the meeting, and each agenda item must be the responsibility of a named individual. The FSA found that common failures included ALCO being merely a rubber stamp, insufficient expertise among members, inadequate forward planning, and a tendency to note risks rather than take decisions to address them.

The ALCO reporting pack is described in detail: traffic light systems (green, amber, red) for key risk indicators, gap profiles by currency and maturity, liquidity ratios and forecasts, capital adequacy projections, and stress test results. For larger banks with multiple entities, a Group ALCO (GALCO) structure is recommended, with regional ALCOs feeding into the group level but maintaining local autonomy on operational matters.

Chapter 10: ALCO Terms of Reference and Treasury Operating Model

This chapter extends the ALCO framework into a full operating blueprint for the Treasury function. Four models are presented, ranging from a pure cost centre (Treasury manages risk but has no market-facing function) to a large front-office operation (Treasury runs money markets, repo, term funding, and securitisation as profit centres while also maintaining governance responsibilities).

Choudhry’s recommended model is the front-office option where Treasury is a markets-facing profit centre with governance responsibilities. The critical insight is that capital and liquidity management — the most important strategic functions in a bank — are best placed in a strong central Treasury that has both the market expertise to execute and the governance authority to enforce policy. Separating these functions across different departments creates coordination gaps that can prove fatal in a crisis.

The chapter provides detailed reporting templates including the ALCO monthly pack, cash flow maturity reports, concentration reports, and inter-entity lending summaries. Real examples show how reporting should look: maturity transformation ratios, top counterparty concentrations, and cross-entity exposure. A concentration breach — where a single counterparty provides fourteen percent of total funding against a ten percent limit — is flagged as requiring immediate ALCO attention.

The BSMCO (Balance Sheet Management Committee) is introduced as a technical advisory body that feeds into ALCO, handling the more granular analytical work that ALCO lacks time to address. Its membership overlaps with ALCO but includes more technical specialists, and it reviews interest rate risk and liquidity from a macroeconomic perspective. During periods of stress, BSMCO should meet weekly and its recommendations should flow directly to the Alpha committee (the crisis response body introduced in the governance chapter).

Capital approval authorities are hierarchically structured: GALCO has unlimited authority, the Group Finance Director up to a specified limit, the Head of Treasury a smaller amount, and local ALCOs and Heads of Treasury the smallest. This ensures that significant capital decisions always reach an appropriate level of seniority while allowing routine transactions to proceed without bureaucratic delay.

Chapter 11: Risk Reporting, Risk Policy, and Stress Testing

The final chapter of Part II integrates everything into a comprehensive risk management framework. A risk map assigns each material risk category to a primary oversight committee and identifies the first and second lines of defence. Credit risks flow to the Executive Credit Committee; liquidity and market risks to ALCO; operational and legal risks to the Risk and Compliance committee; and strategic risks to the Executive Management Committee.

Portfolio credit risk reporting receives detailed treatment through a worked example of a twenty-asset portfolio. Total exposure of 255 million pounds produces aggregate expected loss of 1.612 million (0.63 percent), aggregate unexpected loss of 14.557 million, and a 95th percentile loss of 16.05 million (6.29 percent). The RAROC for this portfolio is 14.73 percent against a cost of capital of ten percent, generating positive SVA (shareholder value added) of 683,500 pounds.

The SVA framework is presented as the most appropriate tool for portfolio management. Individual assets are evaluated against the portfolio average SVA per unit. Assets below average may be under-priced (needing repricing or divestment) or may serve as loss leaders generating other business for the bank. Portfolio evaluation by business line enables senior management to identify which parts of the bank are the most efficient users of capital.

Risk policy templates for the banking book, trading book, and market risk are presented as practical documents that specify permitted instruments, authorised strategies, and quantitative limits. An approved product list classifies instruments as “permitted for trading and investment,” “fully covered sales only,” or “not permitted” — with explicit exclusions preventing unauthorised activities. Market risk policy makes adherence a Board directive and part of each employee’s performance review.

The treatment of stress testing is extensive and practical. Choudhry presents a seven-objective framework for stress testing: understanding sensitivities, complementing VaR, evaluating tail risk, assessing firm-wide impact, testing hidden concentrations, informing capital allocation, and meeting regulatory requirements. The distinction between scenario analysis (hypothetical event applied to current portfolio) and stress testing (systematic variation of market factors by defined amounts) is made precise.

Daily stress tests (thirty-four scenarios covering parallel rate shifts from twenty-five to three hundred basis points plus curve twists), monthly capital tests (nine scenarios of credit migration and recovery rate variation), and monthly liquidity tests (twelve scenarios of funding closure and depositor runs) are specified in detail. A worked economic downturn scenario shows an unstressed capital adequacy ratio of 17.98 percent declining to 16.62 percent under moderate recession (still above the fifteen percent internal minimum) but falling to 14.66 percent under severe recession — a breach that would require corrective action.

VaR limitations are addressed directly. VaR assumes lognormally distributed returns, independent and identically distributed time series, and a constant portfolio — all unrealistic assumptions. Scaling one-day VaR to ten-day by multiplying by the square root of ten compounds these errors. Choudhry cites Max Wong’s Bubble Value at Risk in arguing that stress tests based on historical observation periods will not accurately predict the next crisis, which will by definition differ from all previous ones. VaR should never be the sole risk indicator.

Part III: Bank Liquidity Risk Management

Chapter 12: Principles of Bank Liquidity Management

This is the heart of the book and the chapter that Choudhry’s foreword writers single out for special praise. “Liquidity management is the most important risk management function in banking,” he writes. The 2007–2008 crisis was as much a crisis of bank liquidity as of capital erosion, and government intervention prevented systemic collapse only because central banks acted as emergency liquidity providers. “A bank that has to resort to the central bank for funding has failed; it is a failure of management.”

The historical analysis draws on Boyd and Gertler’s 1994 study of the US banking crisis of 1980–1982, finding that larger banks contributed disproportionately to loan losses due to two factors: deregulation and financial innovation increased competition, while the too-big-to-fail implicit guarantee inadvertently subsidised risk-taking by the largest institutions. The Greenspan Put — a decade of Federal Reserve interventions that created a subconscious belief that large banks could not fail — amplified risk-taking during the cheap liquidity era. These findings, published fifteen years before the 2008 crisis, described almost exactly the dynamics that produced it.

Choudhry presents ten principles of bank liquidity risk management, adapted from the HSBC model, that constitute his prescription for sound practice. Each principle addresses a specific failure mode observed during the crisis.

The first and foundational principle is to fund illiquid assets with core customer deposits. Northern Rock violated this textbook rule, funding long-dated mortgage assets with short-term wholesale funding and securitisation proceeds. When securitisation markets seized up in August 2007 and wholesale counterparties withdrew credit lines, Northern Rock had no deposit base to fall back on and was nationalised within months.

Where customer deposits are insufficient, the second principle directs banks to use long-term wholesale funding with maturities exceeding one year. The appropriate tenor should match the maturity profile of the assets being funded — a five-year loan should not be funded with three-month commercial paper. The third principle sets an explicit cap: no more than twenty to thirty percent of the balance sheet should be funded by sub-three-month wholesale borrowing.

The fourth principle — maintaining liquidity buffers of instantly liquid, sovereign risk-free securities — was the one most systematically violated before the crisis. Banks had abandoned the traditional practice of holding government bonds as a liquidity reserve, because the opportunity cost (lending the money at higher rates instead) seemed wasteful in an environment where wholesale funding was assumed to be always available. The key 2008 lesson was brutal: investment-grade bank FRNs, bank CDs, and even some corporate bonds that were previously considered liquid became completely impossible to sell or repo in the fourth quarter. Only sovereign bonds and central bank reserves retained genuine market liquidity.

Principles five through seven address contingency planning. Banks must establish and regularly test a contingency funding plan (CFP) that specifies exactly which actions to take, in what order, with what amounts and lead times, if normal funding channels close. Central bank facility access must be practised operationally — the dealing room must know how to access the discount window before a crisis forces them to learn on the fly. And banks must be aware of all liability-side exposures, including reputational obligations: ABCP conduits and structured investment vehicles that the bank might feel compelled to support even though it has no contractual obligation to do so.

The final three principles address governance. A comprehensive battery of liquidity metrics must be calculated (not just LTD), internal funds pricing must be set correctly (the subject of an entire chapter), and liquidity risk policy must be centralised under Group Management Board authority with no exceptions permitted without Board pre-authorisation.

The UK FSA’s liquidity framework (PS09/16, October 2009) is presented as the most demanding regulatory response globally, exceeding even the Basel Committee’s December 2010 requirements. The FSA mandated genuine liquid asset buffers (government bonds and central bank reserves, not bank FRNs or corporate bonds), required banks to demonstrate survivability under combined firm-specific and market-wide stress scenarios, and imposed detailed reporting obligations including daily and weekly submissions.

The liquid asset buffer (LAB) receives extended treatment. Before the crisis, many banks had abandoned the traditional practice of holding government bonds as a liquidity reserve, taking market liquidity for granted. The key lesson of 2008 was that investment-grade bank FRNs and CDs — previously considered liquid — became completely illiquid in the fourth quarter, leaving banks with no genuine cushion. The LAB must comprise only sovereign bonds and central bank reserves: Level 1 assets with no cap and no haircut, supplemented by up to forty percent Level 2 assets (agency and high-quality corporate bonds) with a fifteen percent haircut.

The contingency funding plan (CFP) is described as the most important element of liquidity risk management. It must describe viable contingency measures with specific amounts, lead times, and business impact; governance and communication protocols; intra-day payment procedures; and management information for the Board. The loan-to-deposit ratio (LTD) is the simplest: above one hundred percent signals reliance on wholesale funding; below seventy percent signals excess liquidity and potentially inadequate returns. Best practice is ninety-five to one hundred and five percent. But LTD is not predictive and does not account for tenor, concentration, or volatility — it must always be read alongside other measures.

The liquidity risk factor (LRF) — average asset tenor divided by average liability tenor — captures the degree of maturity transformation in a single number. A sample bank with average liabilities of nineteen days and average assets of 262 days has an LRF of fourteen against a limit of twenty-four. The trend matters more than any single reading: a rising LRF signals growing structural mismatch requiring management attention.

Concentration reports identify dangerous dependencies on individual counterparties or funding sources. When a single depositor provides fourteen percent of total funding against a ten percent limit, ALCO must act — either by diversifying the funding base or by accepting the concentration risk as a conscious decision with appropriate monitoring. The inter-entity lending report identifies which entities within a group are net borrowers and which are net lenders, a critical concern because regulators may ring-fence local assets during a crisis, preventing capital and liquidity transfers within the group.

The survival period is highlighted as the most intuitive metric for senior management and regulators: when a bank’s survival horizon falls from twenty-three days to nine days in a single month, as one example shows, immediate management action is triggered.

The chapter closes with external and comparative metrics, including monitoring the bank’s own CDS spread, secondary market debt yields, and peer-group funding cost comparisons. A bank whose term liquidity premium diverges significantly from its peer group is receiving a market signal that should not be ignored. “Finance is a people and relationship business,” Choudhry observes. Maintaining stakeholder relationships during normal times is essential because it is impossible to build them during a crisis.

Chapter 14: Liquidity Risk Reporting and Stress Testing

This chapter translates metrics into actionable management information. The deposit tracker monitors LTD monthly with year-end forecasts, flagging breaches before they become critical. A sample shows LTD at 86.08 percent against an eighty-five percent Board limit — a breach requiring immediate action to either increase deposits or reduce lending.

Daily liquidity reports present the full balance sheet maturity profile: assets of 3.8 billion with average remaining duration of 44.59 months versus liabilities of 4.4 billion with average duration of only 7.72 months. The mismatch is stark and drives the cumulative gap that the bank must manage on a continuous basis.

Funding maturity gap reports reveal that without securities adjustment (the ability to repo or sell liquid assets), one sample bank has a survival horizon of only seven days. Adding liquid securities extends this to twenty-seven days — still below Basel III requirements and demanding corrective action. Funding concentration reports identify specific counterparties exceeding group limits, while wholesale pricing and volume analysis reveals cliff risk where heavy concentrations in short maturity buckets create sudden rollover exposure.

Choudhry is emphatic about presentation: liquidity management information for senior management should fit on one page. “Keeping to one side of A4 will increase the chance that it will actually be read.” The monthly liquidity snapshot combines cumulative gap charts, key ratios (LRF, LTD, inter-group lending), and a qualitative template covering eight items from ratio changes to retail deposit trends to average daily opening cash position.

Stress test reports show unstressed survival horizons (forty-nine days in one example) extended to ninety-three days under FSA-specified stresses when securities sales and repo are included as counterbalancing measures. Individual shock scenarios range from light retail deposit withdrawal (five percent, seventy percent probability, moderate impact) to severe reputational damage (combined scenario, 0.5 percent probability but one hundred percent impact when it occurs).

The FSA’s observed behavioural forecasting (OBF) assumptions provide a regulatory baseline: non-interest-bearing liabilities assumed to run off at one hundred percent every three days; current accounts at negative 5.66 percent every thirty-six days; retail time deposits at negative 0.41 percent every 344 days. These assumptions, combined with scenario analysis, produce the stressed cash flow profiles that determine whether the bank’s LAB is adequate or needs to be increased.

Chapter 15: Internal Funds Pricing Policy

Funds transfer pricing (FTP) is arguably the chapter with the greatest practical impact. Poor FTP was a central cause of the excessive risk-taking that produced the crisis, and fixing FTP is one of the most effective reforms a bank can implement. Treasury acts as internal counterparty for all business lines, centralising interest-rate, basis, and funding mismatch risk, and charging each business line for the liquidity it consumes.

The pre-crisis failures are damning. UBS’s CDO desk funded itself at below-market internal transfer prices; management viewed realistic funding costs as a “constraint on growth.” The reported “super profits” were actually funding arbitrage — borrowing cheaply internally and investing in illiquid structured products. When the music stopped, the profits evaporated and the losses proved catastrophic. KBC Financial Products received liquidity from its AA-rated parent at a uniform Libor plus 12.5 basis points regardless of tenor or instrument, funding leveraged structured products on a short-term rolling basis. Post-Lehman, this proved lethal.

The recommended framework is the gross approach at marginal cost: every asset and liability is individually charged or credited, and the rate reflects current market funding costs rather than historical averages. Business lines cannot deploy funds they raise themselves; all funding flows through Treasury at market-based transfer prices. This is the most rigorous approach and provides the most effective risk management, because it ensures that no business line can generate profits simply by exploiting a mispriced internal funding subsidy.

FTP charges include both direct costs (the interest cost of raising funds at the appropriate tenor) and indirect costs: the mismatch liquidity premium, the cost of maintaining the contingent liquidity buffer (LAB), and where applicable, country risk premiums. These indirect costs are what distinguish properly structured FTP from the simple Libor-plus-spread approaches that failed before the crisis.

The chapter covers derivatives collateral funding in detail, reflecting the post-crisis reality that collateral management is an aspect of liquidity management that can no longer be treated separately. The OIS–Libor spread blew out from near zero to 364 basis points in October 2008, making the distinction between secured and unsecured funding rates a first-order concern. Collateralised derivatives must be discounted at the OIS rate, not Libor, and multi-currency CSA arrangements create collateral optionality — the collateral poster will choose the currency with the lowest funding cost, creating additional pricing complexity.

Five proxies for the term liquidity premium (TLP) are presented, and Choudhry recommends basing FTP on an average of all five, acknowledging that professional judgement is always required. The CEBS (Committee of European Banking Supervisors) guidelines specify that FTP must be comprehensive, properly governed, actively used at the transaction level, and determined by robust methodologies. A critical governance point: if Treasury is a profit centre, the FTP-setting function should sit under Finance, not Treasury, to avoid the obvious conflict of interest.

Part IV: Bank Strategy and Governance

Chapter 16: Formulating Strategy and Direction

The strategy chapters shift from technical risk management to the boardroom decisions that determine whether a bank survives or fails. Five fundamentals of banking have not changed since the profession began: managing capital (leverage), managing the liquidity mismatch (maturity transformation), funding the gap, maintaining the assumption that funding can be rolled over, and managing credit risk. Banks that forgot these fundamentals during the boom — targeting ROE of twenty-two to twenty-five percent, prioritising market share, and leveraging beyond prudent limits — were the ones that failed.

Leverage ratios receive pointed attention. Lehman Brothers rose from 24:1 leverage in 2003 to over 31:1 by 2007 — meaning a three to four percent decline in asset values would eliminate its entire equity.

At 31:1 leverage, Lehman needed only a 3.2% decline in asset values to eliminate its entire equity base.

European banks averaged approximately 48:1 leverage in 2007; US banks approximately 44:1. Canada and Australia imposed explicit leverage ratio limits and did not suffer bank crashes in 2008. This natural experiment provides compelling evidence for the Basel III leverage ratio, which Choudhry considers one of the regulation’s most important innovations.

The sustainable bank business model is built around a vision statement that explicitly constrains risk-taking: target ROE of twelve to fourteen percent through the cycle (not twenty-two percent in good years followed by losses); maintain an AA-/Aa2 rating; derive revenue from customer business rather than proprietary trading; and set an explicit limit on wholesale funding (twenty percent of the balance sheet maximum). Market share is explicitly excluded as a KPI. “Sustained twelve percent ROE over ten to fifteen years is infinitely preferable to several years of twenty-two percent ROE followed by losses.”

Countercyclical funding principles are illustrated with post-crisis data. The Sterling Libor–OIS spread spiked from near zero to 245 basis points during the crisis before falling back. Banks should exploit benign conditions to extend liability maturities and build buffers. UK banks reduced reliance on sub-one-week funding from fifteen to nine percent of unsecured wholesale between December 2008 and October 2009, while the aggregate customer funding gap fell from 842 billion to 610 billion pounds. Canada and Japan, which had negative customer funding gaps (deposits exceeding loans), did not experience banking crises.

The chapter introduces early warning indicators based on the Stock and Watson leading economic indicator model. Nine inputs — including building society new commitments, CBI order book volume, the FT All Share Index, and the gilt ten-year-to-three-month yield spread — are combined into a composite indicator that successfully flagged both the 1990–1991 and 2008–2009 UK recessions. The Baltic Dry Index (bulk carrier shipping prices) reached an all-time high in May 2008, marking the start of the UK and EU recessions. When these indicators signal overheating, banks should tighten loan origination standards, increase capital buffers, and secure more term funding.

The UK Independent Commission on Banking (ICB, September 2011) ring-fence proposals are described: retail and SME banking inside the fence, trading and wholesale activities outside, with each part meeting capital and liquidity requirements on a standalone basis. Total capital requirements could reach twenty percent:

Pre-crisis banks held ~8% total capital. Post-ICB UK requirements could reach 20%.

Liquidity as an asset class is a provocative concept: banks that preserve strong liquidity positions benefit from lower funding costs (counterparties view them as higher quality) and the ability to generate revenue from their liquidity strength during periods when competitors are struggling. Proxy measures including repo margin ratios, collateralised bond spreads, and CDS-ASW basis all confirm that the premium for liquidity increases dramatically during stress, creating opportunities for well-prepared institutions.

A liquidity option facility is described as an innovative funding tool: a bank pays a premium for the right to swap its portfolio for AAA sovereign bonds held by a counterparty in third-party custody. On exercise, the bank receives high-quality bonds for use as repo collateral or in the LAB. The facility counts as term funding for liquidity metrics for the life of the option, providing contingent liquidity without the balance sheet cost of holding sovereign bonds outright.

This chapter integrates capital and liquidity management at the strategic level, arguing that Basel III makes it impossible to treat them separately. Core Tier 1 capital must be invested in central bank deposits or sovereign bonds — the only qualifying zero-risk assets. The income from these investments belongs to the central ALCO book, not individual business lines, preventing distortion of line-level return calculations.

Contingent capital instruments (CoCo bonds) are presented as the appropriate sophisticated element in an otherwise deliberately simple capital structure. Lloyds Banking Group’s Enhanced Capital Notes — 13.5 billion pounds, twelve percent coupon, converting to equity at fifty percent of par when Tier 1 falls below five percent — represent the first real-world CoCo and effectively function as a pre-funded rights issue. The FSA’s bail-in regime, where senior unsecured debt absorbs losses after equity is eroded, removes the automatic taxpayer guarantee and forces investors to price bank failure risk more accurately.

Systemic risk receives a nuanced treatment. Three types are identified: by size (too big to fail), by interconnection (inter-bank and clearing system dependencies), and by association (market perception that one institution’s failure implies risk for all similar institutions). Choudhry argues that virtually all banks are potential sources of systemic risk — not just the largest ones. Bear Stearns, though not technically too-big-to-fail, demonstrated that a midsized institution with concentrated exposures to hedge funds and low-grade assets could create significant market turbulence.

The 1907 banking crisis precedent is cited: J.P. Morgan and other private bankers injected their own money to guarantee market liquidity, providing a historical example of private-sector emergency lending that ultimately led to the founding of the Federal Reserve six years later. Choudhry proposes a modern equivalent — an IMF-style industry-funded liquidity pool where all banking licence holders contribute, with the largest inter-bank users contributing the most. This would remove taxpayer liability while incentivising banks toward conservatism.

The capital-versus-liquidity trade-off is illustrated through a striking comparison. Bank A has five percent equity, six-month average debt maturity, and ten percent liquid assets. Bank B has twenty percent equity, one-month average debt maturity, and five percent liquid assets. Bank B appears stronger on capital metrics, but in a liquidity crisis, Bank A is better positioned because it has longer-dated liabilities and more liquid assets. A liquidity event will finish a bank as a going concern if it cannot roll over its liabilities, regardless of how much capital it holds. Choudhry’s recommended position: ten percent equity, twelve-month average debt maturity, ten percent liquid assets, and average loan maturity of twenty-four months.

The chapter provides detailed rating agency frameworks from Moody’s, S&P, and Fitch, showing how each evaluates bank liquidity. Moody’s Bank Financial Strength Rating allocates fifty percent to qualitative factors (management framework) and fifty percent to financial metrics, with the management framework weighted more heavily than the liquidity ratio itself — reflecting the view that process matters at least as much as outcome. Peer-group benchmarking scorecards enable banks to compare their positioning against competitors on LTD, leverage, NSFR, LAB ratio, and weighted average maturity of debt in issue.

Chapter 18: Principles of Corporate Governance

The final substantive chapter is Choudhry’s most impassioned. If one must identify a single factor responsible for the 2007–2009 crisis, he argues, it was a failure of corporate governance. “Poor judgement, allied with not inconsiderable incompetence, led to the financial crash being much worse than it need have been.”

The academic evidence is sobering. Laeven and Levine (2007) found that traditional regulatory policy does not drive risk aversion, that Basel II capital requirements and supervisory oversight do not appear to reduce risk-taking, and that bank shareholders are not a risk control device — rather the opposite. Large shareholders have incentives to increase risk exposure after collecting deposits and debt. Heidrick and Struggles (2009) found that UK banks rated highest on corporate governance in Europe yet suffered possibly the most serious banking crisis, while Denmark, rated low on governance, experienced fewer problems. Board structure and governance form are uncorrelated with outcomes.

The case studies are devastating. Northern Rock’s CEO Adam Applegarth grew mortgage origination by forty-seven percent year-on-year in the first half of 2007, capturing nineteen percent of all new UK mortgages, including products at one hundred and one hundred and twenty-five percent loan-to-value. The chairman, Matt Ridley, was a zoologist and science writer with no prior banking experience. When interbank rumours reached the market, credit lines were withdrawn and the bank was nationalised.

HBOS’s CEO Andy Hornby came from Asda’s George clothing line. Corporate loans exploded from twelve billion pounds in 2005 to seventy billion in 2008, producing losses of 6.7 billion from corporate lending alone. Market share — a perfectly valid KPI in retail clothing — proved catastrophic as a banking strategy driver. Dunfermline Building Society, a conservative Scottish institution, diversified into commercial property and MBS assets from 2004, purchasing securities from GMAC and a Lehman Brothers subsidiary that suffered write-downs exceeding fifty percent. Its chairman described a 9.5-million-pound IT loss (against a capital base under 300 million) as “excellent progress” — a characterisation that a parliamentary commission called “disingenuous.”

UBS’s board lacked the expertise to supervise its derivatives subsidiary and did not identify the fundamental FTP flaw that allowed the CDO desk to generate illusory profits. KBC Bank’s board was unaware of a five-billion-dollar ABS structure called “Atomium” that included sub-prime exposure, or that the bank’s CDO business had grown to over twenty billion euros. Five years of ROE exceeding twenty-two percent produced management complacency; trades below twenty percent return on capital were rejected.

Lehman Brothers is presented as the extreme case of governance failure. CEO Dick Fuld and president Joe Gregory were described as “remote” and in “denial.” The risk management head was asked to leave the room during discussions of high-risk strategic acquisitions. Fuld’s envy of former Lehman managing directors who had become wealthy at Blackstone drove investments in hedge funds, energy, commodities, and leveraged mortgages. Leverage climbed from 24:1 to over 31:1 as accountability disappeared.

Choudhry’s policy recommendations are specific and actionable. At least one Board member must represent major debt holders, who have direct downside exposure that shareholders can diversify away. Financial services expertise must be a prerequisite for all board members, including NEDs and CEOs, with regulatory approval required and annual review. Board size should be limited to six to ten members to maintain individual accountability. An Alpha committee — CEO, CFO, one business line head, Treasury head, and one or two NEDs — should meet one to two days per month for agile crisis response, since monthly full Board meetings proved far too infrequent when the VIX went from twenty to eighty in weeks.

The paradox of aggregation frames the systemic dimension: what one bank can do, all cannot. When every bank simultaneously tries to raise liquidity during a market correction, asset prices fall and funding markets freeze. This is a systemic risk feature requiring minimum standards for all banks, because the efficient market hypothesis does not hold during stress events. Banks receive banking licences, lender-of-last-resort access, and deposit insurance in exchange for managing their businesses responsibly. “Banks should not need to be required by law to operate in a way that is sensible; rather, they should seek to ensure they do in any case.”

Chapter 19: Applications Software, Policy Templates, and Teaching Aids

The final chapter describes the supplementary materials available on the Wiley website: Excel spreadsheets for yield curve calculations and Monte Carlo simulation; PowerPoint lectures on ALCO policy, FTP, liquidity risk, and ALM; policy document templates for ALCO terms of reference, banking book policy, liquidity contingency plans, and market risk policy; reporting templates for the ALCO pack, Treasury reporting, and cash flow survival analysis; and stress testing report templates.

The centrepiece is the YCF Cubic B-Spline Yield Curve Application, an Excel VBA tool developed by Zhuoshi Liu and Choudhry that derives spot and forward curves from bond prices or money market rates, calculates fair prices for bonds and swaps, and implements the cubic spline methodology used by the Bank of England and the US Federal Reserve. These materials transform the book from a theoretical treatise into a practical toolkit that treasury professionals can deploy directly in their operations.

Key Takeaways

Liquidity Is Primary

Capital adequacy is necessary but not sufficient. Lehman Brothers was capitalised at eleven percent Tier 1 when it collapsed. Liquidity management is the most important risk management function in banking, and the two must be integrated at the strategic level. A bank can survive a capital shortfall if it has liquidity; it cannot survive a liquidity crisis no matter how much capital it holds.

The Five Principles Are Non-Negotiable

Secure funding before lending. Fund from deposits. Lend prudently. Hold sufficient capital. See principle one. Every bank that failed in 2007–2009 had violated at least one of these elementary rules.

FTP Is a Root Cause and a Fix

Mispriced internal funding allowed business lines at UBS, KBC, and others to generate illusory profits from cheap internal borrowing. Properly structured FTP — gross approach at marginal cost, including both direct and indirect costs — is one of the most effective reforms a bank can implement.

ALCO Supremacy

The ALCO is more important than the Board. It must meet monthly at minimum, be chaired by the CFO, include all business line heads, receive comprehensive reporting, and make binding decisions — not merely note risks.

The Gap Is the Business

Maturity transformation is banking. Understanding the gap between asset and liability maturities, managing it within prudent limits, and maintaining the ALM smile is the cornerstone of risk management.

Governance Determines Survival

Board expertise, independence, size, and meeting frequency matter more than board structure or governance ratings. CEOs without banking experience, boards unaware of massive exposures, and risk managers excluded from strategic discussions are the common thread across every failed institution.

Models Fail When Needed Most

VaR assumes normal distributions, constant correlations, and stable portfolios. Credit risk models assume historical default correlations that spike toward one during crises. Stress tests based on past events cannot predict the next crisis, which by definition differs from all previous ones. Senior judgement must override model output in extremis.

Conservatism Is Not Optional

Liquidity crises are rare enough that a banker can spend an entire career without experiencing one. The temptation to relax conservative principles during the intervening years is powerful. But because of banks’ role in the economic system, they must be able to survive these rare events without taxpayer support. Sustained twelve percent ROE is infinitely preferable to several years of twenty-two percent followed by catastrophic losses.

Contrarian Ideas

ROA Over ROE

ROE is the standard performance metric for banks, but Choudhry argues it incentivises dangerous leverage. ROA is a superior measure because it cannot be inflated by simply increasing the balance sheet relative to equity.

ALCO Over the Board

A bank can survive an incompetent Board but not an incompetent ALCO. This inverts the usual hierarchy and reflects Choudhry’s conviction that operational risk management is more critical to survival than strategic direction.

Market Share Is Not a Bank KPI

Market share is a valid performance measure in retail, manufacturing, and technology. In banking, pursuing market share drives exactly the behavior that destroys banks: loosening credit standards, pricing below risk-adjusted cost, and growing the balance sheet beyond funding capacity. Northern Rock, HBOS, and Bradford & Bingley all prioritised market share.

Capital Requirements Alone Cannot Prevent Failure

This is contrarian within the regulatory community, which spent enormous energy on Basel III capital reforms. Choudhry does not oppose higher capital — he supports it — but insists that without equally rigorous liquidity requirements, capital adequacy provides a false sense of security.

Shareholders Are Not a Risk Control

Conventional corporate governance theory treats shareholders as the ultimate check on management excess. Academic evidence cited by Choudhry shows that bank shareholders actually have incentives to increase risk exposure after collecting deposits and debt, making them a source of risk rather than a restraint on it.

Governance Form Is Uncorrelated With Outcomes

UK banks rated highest on governance in Europe suffered the worst crisis. Two-tier German boards did not prevent landesbank failures. Unitary boards, two-tier boards, and mixed boards all presided over collapses. What matters is expertise, challenge, and genuine engagement — not structure.

Proposals & Frameworks

Ten Principles of Liquidity Risk Management

Adapted from the HSBC model: fund illiquid assets with deposits; supplement with long-term wholesale; limit short-term wholesale; hold sovereign-only liquidity buffers; establish contingency funding plans; test central bank access; monitor all liability exposures; calculate comprehensive metrics; set FTP correctly; centralise under Group Board authority.

ALCO Governance Framework

Complete operating blueprint: formal terms of reference; monthly meetings chaired by CFO; specified membership; traffic light reporting system; one-week document circulation; named individual ownership of each agenda item; Group ALCO for multinational structures; BSMCO as technical advisory sub-committee.

FTP Reform Framework

Gross approach at marginal cost; direct plus indirect costs (LAB, CFP, mismatch premium); dynamic curve adjustment reflecting funding position; trading book charged at expected holding duration; securitisable assets at fifty percent; committed facilities at full tenor; governance under Finance, not Treasury; semi-annual review.

Alpha Committee

Crisis-response governance body: CEO, CFO, one business line head, Head of Treasury, one or two NEDs; meets one to two days per month; executive authority delegated from Board; designed for agile response when events move faster than monthly board meetings can accommodate.

Industry-Funded Liquidity Mechanism

Choudhry’s original proposal (2009): an IMF-style pool funded by all banking licence holders; largest inter-bank users contribute most; operated by private trade association; removes taxpayer liability; incentivises conservatism; modelled on J.P. Morgan’s private intervention during the 1907 banking crisis.

Corporate Governance Reform

Seven specific proposals: debt holder board representation; mandatory financial expertise for all directors; clear strategic articulation of competence boundaries; board size limited to six to ten; Alpha committee for crisis response; sub-committee expertise requirements; enforced annual transparency on risk and losses.

What Didn’t Fit

The Sherif Choudhry Leadership Test

In the acknowledgements, Choudhry quotes his brother Sherif (Vice President at CapGemini) on leadership: “How does one define leadership? Simple. Look at the people sitting around you at the Boardroom table, your senior leadership team. How many of them would follow you to your next job for a cut in pay? If the answer is none of them, then you’re not a leader. A true leader commands respect and loyalty in equal measure, and that goes far beyond a wage packet.” This test, applied to the CEOs described in Chapter 18, is devastating. Dick Fuld at Lehman, whose staff described him as “remote”; Adam Applegarth at Northern Rock; Andy Hornby at HBOS — none of them would likely pass the Sherif Choudhry test.

The Derek Taylor Trading Floor Vignettes

Two anecdotes from Choudhry’s early career at Hoare Govett and KBC capture the culture shift between old-school gilt trading and modern bank treasury. Young Moorad, trying to build a position: “I like the long gilt right now, I’ve just bought fifty lots…” Derek, shouting to the floor: “Sell 1,000 gilt! I don’t care what it costs, just get it done!” Years later, Moorad as treasury head: “I’m looking to diversify our funding sources, do you know any counterparties who might be long cash?” Derek: “Try Mervyn King at the Bank of England, I hear he’s a lender of last resort!” The humour masks a serious point about the evolution from proprietary risk-taking to prudent liability management.

The In-House Securitisation Arbitrage

European banks during 2009–2011 developed a practice of repackaging their own loan assets into structured note form solely for use as collateral at the ECB. This “in-house securitisation” did not transfer risk or create genuine diversification; it simply increased the bank’s interconnection with the central bank. Choudhry flags this as a practice that maximises balance sheet efficiency on paper while creating precisely the kind of hidden systemic risk that contributed to the original crisis.

The Canada-Japan Natural Experiment

Countries with negative customer funding gaps (deposits exceeding loans) did not experience banking crises in 2008.

Countries where deposits exceeded loans (negative gap) avoided the 2008 crisis entirely.

This cross-country comparison provides perhaps the strongest empirical evidence for Choudhry’s first principle: secure your funding base before lending.

The Dunfermline IT Fiasco

Dunfermline Building Society invested 31.4 million pounds in a bespoke IT project called “Dunfermline Solutions” against a capital base of under 300 million. The project consumed over ten percent of capital, required a 9.5 million pound write-off that reduced operating profit from 11.5 million to 2 million, and was described in the annual report as making “excellent progress.” The parliamentary commission’s verdict: “disingenuous.” This story captures everything wrong with pre-crisis governance in a single data point: a 300-million-pound building society burning ten percent of its capital on a failed IT project while simultaneously diversifying into Lehman Brothers MBS.

The KBC Atomium Structure

KBC Bank’s board was unaware of a USD 5 billion ABS structure called “Atomium” — named, with unintentional irony, after Brussels’ famous atomic structure monument — that contained sub-prime exposure. The CDO business had grown to over EUR 20 billion without board knowledge. Five consecutive years of ROE exceeding twenty-two percent had produced such complacency that trades below twenty percent return were rejected as insufficiently profitable. The internal funding rate of Libor plus 12.5 basis points for all instruments and all tenors provided the financial mechanism that made this growth possible. Belgium’s taxpayers provided EUR 2.5 billion plus EUR 2 billion in equity injections and EUR 20 billion in guarantees.

Why This Book Matters

The Principles of Banking matters because it was written by someone who was inside the system when it broke. Choudhry was not an outside observer or a journalist reconstructing events after the fact; he was the treasurer of one of the banks that required government rescue. This gives the book an urgency and specificity that academic treatments cannot match. When he writes about FTP failures, he is describing mechanisms he encountered firsthand. When he prescribes ALCO governance frameworks, he is documenting what he built and operated in practice.

The book’s significance extends beyond its immediate post-crisis context. The principles Choudhry articulates — fund before you lend, match your maturities, hold genuine liquidity buffers, price risk correctly, govern competently — are not new. They were standard practice for most of banking’s history and were abandoned only during the brief period of cheap liquidity and regulatory complacency that preceded the crash. The fact that these principles needed restating in a thousand-page book endorsed by the Deputy Governor of the Reserve Bank of India, the Group Treasurer of RBS, and a former Executive Director of the Bank of England tells us something important about institutional memory and the tendency of financial systems to forget their own lessons.

As Ian Plenderleith writes in his foreword: “If reading it keeps bankers awake at night, this admirable book will have served its purpose.” The motto that opens and closes the book — Dictum Meum Pactum, My Word Is My Bond — is both a reminder of what banking was built upon and a challenge to the profession to deserve that trust again.
