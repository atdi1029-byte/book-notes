How to Use This Guide

This book is a 1,040-page textbook on how to run a bank properly. It was written by a career banker — someone who sat at the Treasury desk at RBS, JPMorgan, and KBC — right after the 2008 financial crisis. It covers yield curve mathematics, capital regulations, liquidity metrics, stress testing, and corporate governance. That’s a lot of dense material.

This plain English version explains every chapter’s core concepts as if a patient colleague is walking you through it on your first day in Treasury. No jargon without explanation. No formulas without a worked example in everyday numbers. Diagrams where they help. Tables wherever they make comparisons clearer.

If you want the full treatment with all the math and citations, read the main index.html. If you want to genuinely understand what banks do, why they fail, and what “good banking” looks like — this is your guide.

Part I: Banking 101

Chapter 1: How Banks Actually Make Money

The Basic Business

A bank does something deceptively simple: it takes your money (deposits) and lends it to someone else (loans). The difference between the interest it charges the borrower and the interest it pays you is called net interest income (NII). That’s it. That’s the core business that has not changed since ancient Babylon.

Think of it like renting out an apartment. You have a mortgage (your cost of funding) and a tenant (your income). The spread between what the tenant pays you and what you pay the bank is your profit. For a bank, deposits are the mortgage and loans are the rental income.

For most normal banks — the ones on your high street — NII is sixty to eighty percent of all revenue. The rest comes from fees (charging for services like wire transfers, account maintenance, advisory work) and trading income (buying and selling securities for profit).

Bank Type Interest Income Fees Trading
UK building society 80%18%2%
European regional bank 62%27%11%
Investment bank 22%52%26%

Notice the pattern: the more “boring” the bank, the more it earns from interest. Building societies earn almost nothing from trading. Investment banks earn most of their money from fees and trading. But even Goldman Sachs still earns twenty-two percent from plain old interest income.

The Gap — Why Banking Is Inherently Risky

Here’s the fundamental tension of banking. When you put money in a savings account, you want to be able to take it out whenever you like — tomorrow, next week, right now. But when a bank lends money for a mortgage, that money is locked up for twenty-five years.

This difference in timing is called the gap. Your deposits might have a “maturity” of one day (you can withdraw anytime). The mortgage has a maturity of twenty-five years. The bank has borrowed short and lent long.

This isn’t a bug — it’s the actual business model. The technical term is maturity transformation. Banks transform short-term savings into long-term investment. This is valuable to the economy because borrowers need long-term money and savers want instant access. Banks sit in the middle and make the magic happen.

But the gap is also the source of every risk the rest of this book will examine. What happens if all the depositors want their money back at once? The bank can’t recall twenty-five-year mortgages overnight. That’s a bank run, and it’s the nightmare scenario that drives everything banks do (or should do) to manage their balance sheets.

Deposits are short-term; loans are long-term. The bank bridges this gap and earns the spread. This IS banking — and it’s also the source of liquidity risk.

How Banks Measure Performance

Banks use several ratios to measure how well they’re doing. Here are the main ones in plain terms:

Ratio What It Means Good =
ROE (Return on Equity) How much profit per dollar of shareholder money >10%
ROA (Return on Assets) How much profit per dollar of total balance sheet >1%
Cost-to-Income How much you spend to earn a dollar Lower is better
Net Interest Margin The spread between what you earn on loans and pay on deposits Higher is better

Choudhry makes a sharp point here: most banks obsess over ROE, but ROA is a better measure. Why? Because ROE can be gamed by simply borrowing more money. If you have $10 of equity and make $1 profit, your ROE is 10%. But if you borrow $90 more (increasing your balance sheet to $100), and make $2 profit on the bigger pile, your ROE jumps to 20%. You look like a genius, but you’ve actually just taken on nine times more risk.

This is exactly what Lehman Brothers did. Their leverage went from 24:1 to 31:1 between 2003 and 2007. Their ROE looked fantastic. Then a 3% drop in asset values wiped out their entire equity.

The Five Risks Every Bank Faces

Risk What It Means Real Example
Credit risk Borrowers don’t pay you back Sub-prime mortgages defaulting en masse
Market risk Interest rates, bond prices, or FX rates move against you Bank holds fixed-rate bonds; rates rise; bonds lose value
Liquidity risk You can’t get cash when you need it Northern Rock: couldn’t roll over wholesale funding Aug 2007
Operational risk Fraud, system failures, human error Nick Leeson at Barings; Kweku Adoboli at UBS
Model risk Your mathematical models give wrong answers VaR models said losses were “impossible” right before 2008 losses happened

Of these five, Choudhry argues that liquidity risk is the one that actually kills banks. Credit losses hurt. Market losses sting. But running out of cash — being unable to pay your obligations when they come due — ends a bank overnight. Northern Rock was profitable on paper. Its loans were mostly performing. But when wholesale markets froze in August 2007 and it couldn’t roll over its short-term borrowing, it was finished within weeks.

ALCO: The Most Important Committee You’ve Never Heard Of

Every bank has a Board of Directors. But Choudhry says the ALCO (Asset-Liability Committee) is actually more important. His exact quote: “It is possible to have an incompetent Board and still survive a market crisis; however, it is not possible for a bank to have an incompetent ALCO and survive the same crisis.”

ALCO is the committee that manages the balance sheet day-to-day: setting interest-rate risk limits, approving hedging strategies, monitoring liquidity, and deciding how much to charge business lines for the funding they use. We’ll cover it in detail in Chapters 9 and 10.

Chapter 2: Why Banks Need Capital (and How Much)

What Is Bank Capital?

Capital is the bank’s own money — as opposed to money it has borrowed (deposits, bonds, wholesale funding). Think of it as the bank’s savings account. If loans go bad and the bank loses money, capital absorbs the losses so depositors don’t get hurt.

Here’s a simple example. A bank has $100 of assets (loans) funded by $92 of deposits and $8 of capital (equity). If $5 of loans go bad, the bank writes them off. Assets drop to $95, but depositors are still owed $92. The loss comes out of capital, which drops from $8 to $3. The depositors are whole. The shareholders took the hit.

But what if $10 of loans go bad? Now assets are $90 and depositors are owed $92. The bank is insolvent — it doesn’t have enough to pay everyone back. That’s why regulators require banks to hold a minimum amount of capital.

Basel Rules: The International Rulebook

The Basel rules are set by a committee of global banking regulators in Basel, Switzerland. They’ve gone through three versions, each one trying to fix problems the previous version created.

Version Year Key Feature Main Problem
Basel I 1988 8% capital minimum; simple risk weights All corporate loans weighted the same → banks lent to riskier borrowers for higher returns
Basel II 2004 Risk weights based on credit ratings; 3-pillar framework Banks gamed it through securitisation; rating agencies conflicted
Basel III 2010 Higher capital; stricter definitions; NEW liquidity rules (LCR, NSFR); leverage ratio cap TBD — hasn’t been fully tested yet

How Risk Weights Work (Basel I)

Under Basel I, not every dollar on a bank’s balance sheet requires the same amount of capital. A loan to the government is considered safe (zero risk), so it needs zero capital backing. A loan to a corporation is considered risky (100% weight), so every dollar needs eight cents of capital.

Let’s work through an example. Imagine a bank with this portfolio:

Asset Amount Risk Weight Risk-Weighted
Government bonds £250m 0% £0
Interbank loans £790m 20% £158m
Home mortgages £652m 50% £326m
Corporate loans £814m 100% £814m
Total £2,536m £1,298m

Total risk-weighted assets = £1,298m. Capital requirement (8%) = £103.8m. Even though the bank holds £2.5 billion in assets, it only needs £104 million in capital because the risk weights reduce the effective exposure.

The Basel I Flaw

Here’s the fatal flaw that Choudhry highlights: under Basel I, ALL corporate loans got the same 100% risk weight. A loan to Apple (basically zero risk of default) consumed exactly the same capital as a loan to a startup that might not exist next year.

Since the capital cost was the same but risky borrowers paid higher interest rates, banks had an economic incentive to lend to worse borrowers. Better returns, same capital charge. This perverse incentive drove huge amounts of risky lending.

The Number That Changes Everything

Here is the single most important data point in the entire book: Lehman Brothers was capitalised at 11% Tier 1 when it collapsed.

Eleven percent! That was above the regulatory minimum. The regulators were happy with it. And the bank still went bankrupt.

Why? Because capital requirements alone cannot save you if you can’t get cash when you need it. Lehman had capital. It didn’t have liquidity. No one would lend to it. No one would roll over its short-term funding. Game over.

This is why Choudhry spends hundreds of pages on liquidity management. Capital is the seatbelt. Liquidity is the steering wheel. You need both, but if you can only choose one, choose the steering wheel.

Chapter 3: When Borrowers Don’t Pay You Back

Credit Risk in Plain Terms

Credit risk is the oldest risk in banking: you lend someone money and they don’t pay it back. It sounds simple, but measuring and managing it is enormously complex. Three numbers drive everything:

Parameter What It Means Example
PD (Probability of Default) Chance the borrower fails to pay AAA corporation: ~0.01% per year; BB corporation: ~1.5% per year
LGD (Loss Given Default) How much you lose IF they default Secured loan (collateral): ~30%; Unsecured: ~60–80%
EAD (Exposure at Default) How much they owe you when they default Drawn balance on a credit line

Expected loss = PD × LGD × EAD. That’s the loss you should budget for in normal times. The scary part is unexpected loss — the tail risk that comes from correlations. In good times, defaults are scattered and uncorrelated. In a recession, suddenly everyone defaults at once, and your “diversified” portfolio turns out to be much riskier than your models predicted.

Credit Ratings

Rating agencies (Moody’s, S&P, Fitch) assign letter grades to borrowers. The higher the grade, the lower the expected default rate. But the 2008 crisis showed that ratings can be dangerously wrong — especially for structured products where the agencies were paid by the issuers (a massive conflict of interest).

Choudhry’s advice: use agency ratings as one input, but develop your own internal credit assessment capabilities. Never outsource your risk judgement to someone with a conflict of interest.

Recovery Rates — What You Get Back

When a borrower defaults, you usually get something back eventually. The standard assumption in CDS (credit default swap) pricing is 40% recovery. But actual recovery varies wildly and can take months to years.

Choudhry’s recommendation for hedging: assume 0% recovery. Why? Because when a borrower defaults, your accountants will write down the entire loan to zero immediately. Even if you eventually recover 40 cents on the dollar, that might take years. In the meantime, your balance sheet has taken the full hit. Better to plan for the worst and be pleasantly surprised.

Chapter 4: Securitisation — Turning Loans Into Bonds

The Basic Idea

Securitisation is the process of taking a bunch of individual loans (mortgages, car loans, credit card receivables), bundling them together, and selling bonds backed by the cash flows from those loans.

Imagine you’re a bank with 1,000 mortgages, each paying you monthly interest and principal. You create a special company (called an SPV — special purpose vehicle) that buys those mortgages from you. The SPV then issues bonds to investors. The monthly mortgage payments flow through to the bondholders. You get your cash back immediately and can make new loans.

Why It Went Wrong

Securitisation is a perfectly legitimate funding tool. The problem before 2008 was that banks used it to:

(1) Remove risky loans from their balance sheets to dodge capital requirements, while secretly retaining most of the risk through first-loss tranches and implicit guarantees. (2) Create CDOs (collateralised debt obligations) so complex that even the people selling them didn’t understand the underlying risks. (3) Get AAA ratings on instruments that contained sub-prime mortgages, because rating agencies trusted correlation assumptions that broke down during the crisis.

The key lesson: securitisation works when there is genuine risk transfer and transparency. It becomes dangerous when used for regulatory arbitrage (gaming capital rules) or to generate illusory profits from mispriced funding.

Part II: Managing the Balance Sheet

Chapter 5: The Yield Curve — The One Chart That Rules Everything

What Is the Yield Curve?

The yield curve is a graph showing the interest rate (yield) you earn on government bonds at different maturities. A 1-year bond might pay 3%. A 5-year bond might pay 4%. A 10-year bond might pay 4.5%. Plot those points and connect them — that’s the yield curve.

Why does it matter? Because the yield curve drives everything in banking. It determines what banks pay for funding, what they charge for loans, what bonds are worth, and what derivatives cost. If you don’t understand the curve, you can’t understand banking.

The Four Shapes

The normal curve slopes upward: longer maturities pay higher rates. This makes intuitive sense — you demand more compensation for locking up your money for longer. Banks love this shape because they borrow short (cheap) and lend long (expensive).

The inverted curve is when short rates exceed long rates. This has historically been one of the most reliable recession indicators. When markets expect the central bank to cut rates in the future (because the economy is weakening), long-term rates fall below current short-term rates.

Spot Rates vs. Yield to Maturity

The yield to maturity (YTM) you see quoted on a bond has a hidden assumption: it assumes every coupon payment gets reinvested at the same rate. In reality, this almost never happens.

Spot rates (also called zero-coupon rates) fix this problem. A spot rate is the yield on a bond that makes only one payment — at maturity. No coupons, no reinvestment assumption. Each cash flow from a bond should be discounted at its own spot rate.

The technique for extracting spot rates from observed bond prices is called bootstrapping. You start with the shortest maturity (where the spot rate equals the YTM because there’s only one cash flow), then work your way out to longer maturities, using each new spot rate to solve for the next one. It’s like peeling an onion one layer at a time.

Forward Rates — The Market’s Implied Prediction

Forward rates answer the question: “Based on today’s curve, what rate is the market implying for a loan that starts in the future?”

For example, if the 1-year spot rate is 3% and the 2-year spot rate is 4%, you can calculate the 1-year rate, 1 year forward — the implied rate for a 1-year investment starting one year from now. The answer is about 5%, because you need to earn enough in year two to make the 2-year investment equivalent to two consecutive 1-year investments.

Critical insight: forward rates are not predictions. They are break-even rates. They tell you the rate at which you would be indifferent between investing for the full period at the spot rate or investing short and rolling over. Markets are not trying to forecast where rates will be; they are pricing the curve so there are no arbitrage opportunities.

Curve Fitting — Making the Data Smooth

Real bond markets give you a handful of data points (specific bonds at specific maturities). But you need a continuous curve covering every maturity. Several methods exist to fill in the gaps:

Method How It Works Pros / Cons
Nelson-Siegel Fits entire curve with 4 parameters Smooth & elegant; can struggle at very short end
Svensson Extends Nelson-Siegel with 2 extra parameters More flexible; risk of overfitting
Cubic splines Fits curve piecewise between data points Very flexible; needs careful smoothing to avoid wiggles

The Bank of England and the US Federal Reserve both use spline-based methods. Choudhry even provides an Excel VBA tool implementing cubic B-splines in the book’s supplementary materials.

Chapters 6–7: ALM — Matching What You Owe With What You Own

What Is ALM?

Asset-Liability Management is the practice of making sure the two sides of a bank’s balance sheet (what it owns and what it owes) are reasonably well matched in terms of maturity, interest rate sensitivity, and currency. It’s not a profit-making activity — it’s a survival function.

The Gap Report

The fundamental ALM tool is the gap report. You lay out all your assets and liabilities by maturity bucket and see where the mismatches are.

Imagine a simplified bank:

Bucket Assets Maturing Liabilities Maturing Gap
0–1 month £200m £500m −£300m
1–3 months £150m £300m −£150m
3–12 months £400m £200m +£200m
1–5 years £600m £100m +£500m
5+ years £500m £50m +£450m

The negative gaps in the short end tell you this bank has a lot of liabilities coming due soon (deposits, wholesale borrowing) and not many assets maturing to generate cash. It needs to constantly roll over its short-term funding or find new sources. That’s where the risk lives.

The ALM Smile

Choudhry introduces a concept called the ALM smile — the ideal shape of a bank’s maturity profile where assets and liabilities are reasonably well matched, with a controlled degree of maturity transformation.

Banks that abandoned the ALM smile before the crisis ran massive mismatches — funding long-term mortgages with overnight or weekly wholesale borrowing — because the carry (the profit from the spread) was enormous. Choudhry calls this an “effortless, non-value-added carry trade.” It generated huge apparent profits until Lehman collapsed and overnight funding markets froze. Then it destroyed the banks that relied on it.

Behaviouralisation

Here’s a tricky concept. Your checking account is contractually withdrawable on demand — you could empty it today. But in practice, most people leave a stable balance in their checking accounts for years.

Behaviouralisation is the practice of treating deposits based on their actual behavioral maturity rather than their contractual maturity. If data shows that 70% of checking account balances remain stable for three or more years, a bank might classify 70% as “core” (long-term) and only 30% as volatile. This matters enormously for ALM because it changes how much short-term rollover risk the bank actually faces.

But it’s also dangerous. Those behavioral assumptions were tested during 2007–2008 and in some cases failed. When Northern Rock’s reputation was damaged, depositors queued around the block to withdraw — behavioral assumptions went out the window.

Interest Rate Risk

The dominant market risk for most banks is interest rate risk. It comes in two flavors:

Earnings sensitivity: How does your net interest income change when rates move? If you have fixed-rate loans funded by floating-rate deposits and rates rise, your funding cost goes up but your loan income stays flat. Margin gets squeezed.

Economic value sensitivity: How does the present value of your equity change? When rates rise, the present value of long-dated fixed-rate assets falls more than the present value of short-dated liabilities. Your economic net worth drops even if your accounting income doesn’t show it yet.

These two measures can give conflicting signals. A rate rise might increase your NII (good!) while simultaneously reducing your economic value (bad!). This is why ALCO needs to monitor both.

Chapter 8: Hedging — Insurance for Your Balance Sheet

The Hedging Toolkit

Banks use four main tools to hedge interest rate risk. Think of them as different types of insurance policies for your balance sheet:

Instrument What It Does Best For
FRA (Forward Rate Agreement) Locks in a future borrowing rate Short-term (1–12 months)
Futures Exchange-traded rate lock; daily margin settlement Short-term; standardised amounts
Interest Rate Swap Converts fixed to floating (or vice versa) Medium/long-term; custom amounts
Options (caps, floors, swaptions) Protection with upside preserved; costs a premium When you want insurance, not a lock

How a Swap Works (Simple Example)

Imagine your bank made a bunch of 5-year fixed-rate loans at 6%. You funded them with floating-rate deposits that reset every 3 months based on Libor. Right now Libor is 4%, so your spread is 2%.

But if Libor rises to 6%, your spread is zero. If Libor hits 7%, you’re losing money on every loan. To fix this, you enter an interest rate swap: you agree to pay fixed (say 5%) and receive floating (Libor).

Now your position is: Receive 6% from loans + receive Libor from swap − pay 5% on swap − pay Libor on deposits = 6% + Libor − 5% − Libor = 1% locked in. The Libor terms cancel out. No matter where rates go, you earn 1%. Less than the original 2%, but guaranteed. That’s the price of certainty.

The Strip Hedge

A futures strip hedge locks in a borrowing rate for multiple future periods by selling a chain of futures contracts across successive quarterly delivery dates.

If you know you need to borrow for 12 months starting next quarter, you sell four quarterly futures contracts. Each contract locks in one quarter’s funding rate. The average of all four — the “strip rate” — becomes your effective borrowing cost.

Choudhry works through a detailed example where the strip rate comes out to 6.53%. The actual effective borrowing rate after the hedge settles is 6.53% — almost exactly matching the target. This is the power of futures hedging: it works.

Chapters 9–10: ALCO — The Most Important Meeting in the Bank

What ALCO Does

ALCO (Asset-Liability Committee) is where the balance sheet gets managed. It’s the committee that:

• Sets limits on how much interest rate risk the bank can take. • Approves hedging strategies. • Monitors liquidity position and funding concentrations. • Sets the internal transfer price for funds (FTP — more on this in Chapter 15). • Reviews capital adequacy. • Decides what to do when limits are breached.

Who Sits on ALCO

Role Why They’re There
CFO (Chair) Independent from business lines; owns the numbers
CEO or Deputy CEO Strategic authority; can make immediate decisions
Head of Treasury Manages the balance sheet daily; technical expertise
Business line heads Their activities create the risks ALCO manages
CRO (Chief Risk Officer) Independent risk perspective
Head of Research/Economics Market outlook; forward-looking input

Notice who is NOT on ALCO: the Head of Credit. Credit decisions have their own committee. ALCO is purely about the balance sheet — interest rate risk, liquidity, capital, and funding strategy.

What Good ALCO Looks Like

The UK FSA published a list of what they found at failing banks: ALCO was a rubber stamp. Members lacked expertise. Discussions were backward-looking. Minutes recorded action points but no evidence of actual debate. Risks were “noted” but no decisions were taken to address them.

Good ALCO, by contrast: Is forward-looking — what’s coming, not what already happened. Shows genuine challenge — members push back and debate. Takes decisions — not just notes risks. Documents are circulated one week before the meeting. Each agenda item has a named owner who presents. Uses a traffic light system: green (all clear), amber (watch closely), red (act now).

The Treasury Operating Model

Choudhry presents four models for how Treasury can be organized, from pure cost centre (just manages risk, no trading) to full market-facing profit centre (runs money markets, repo, term funding, AND manages governance).

His recommendation: Treasury as a market-facing profit centre with governance responsibilities. The logic is that capital and liquidity management are the most important strategic functions in a bank, and they need to sit in a team that has both market expertise and governance authority. Splitting these across departments creates coordination gaps that can kill you in a crisis.

Chapter 11: Stress Testing — What Happens When Everything Goes Wrong?

Why Stress Test?

VaR (Value at Risk) tells you the most you might lose on a normal day. Stress testing tells you what happens on the days that aren’t normal — the days when everything breaks at once.

Choudhry makes a sharp observation: “The next financial crisis will be different in shape and form to any previous ones — by definition it is not possible to plan for it completely.” You can’t stress test the unknown. But you can stress test types of scenarios and build resilience against them.

The Three Types of Tests

Type What You Do Example
Sensitivity test Move one variable by a fixed amount Shift all interest rates +200 bps
Scenario analysis Create a hypothetical event and measure the damage “Severe recession: GDP −4%, unemployment doubles, rates cut 300 bps”
Reverse stress test Start with “bank fails” and work backward to find what causes it “What would have to happen for our capital to breach the minimum?”

A Worked Example

A sample bank starts with a capital adequacy ratio of 17.98%. Under a moderate recession scenario (credit losses increase, spreads widen, NII compresses), the ratio falls to 16.62% — still above the 15% internal minimum. The bank survives.

Under a severe recession (higher defaults, further spread widening, funding cost spikes), the ratio falls to 14.66% — below the 15% minimum. The bank needs corrective action: raise capital, cut dividends, reduce risk assets, or sell non-core business.

This is why banks stress test: to discover the breaking points before they happen and take action while they still can.

Why VaR Is Not Enough

VaR has three assumptions that are all wrong: returns are normally distributed (they’re not — fat tails exist), returns are independent day-to-day (they’re not — volatility clusters), and the portfolio is static (it isn’t).

The practice of scaling 1-day VaR to 10-day by multiplying by √10 compounds all these errors. VaR told Lehman its losses were “impossible.” Then they happened.

Part III: Liquidity — The Water of Life

Chapter 12: Why Liquidity Matters More Than Capital

The Central Argument

This is the heart of the entire book. Choudhry’s thesis in one sentence: capital is the seatbelt; liquidity is the steering wheel. You need both, but a car without a steering wheel crashes every time.

His reasoning: Capital absorbs losses gradually over time. You can survive with less capital than you’d like for a while — regulators might give you a few quarters to rebuild it. But if you can’t pay your obligations today — if you can’t generate cash to meet withdrawals and maturing debts — you’re finished overnight.

Northern Rock had adequate capital. Lehman Brothers had 11% Tier 1. Both died of liquidity failure.

The Ten Commandments of Liquidity

Choudhry presents ten principles adapted from the HSBC model. Here they are in plain English:

# Principle Plain English
1 Fund illiquid assets with deposits If you’re making 25-year loans, fund them with stable customer deposits, not overnight borrowing
2 Use long-term wholesale if deposits aren’t enough At least 1 year maturity; don’t borrow short to lend long
3 Don’t over-rely on wholesale Max 20–30% of balance sheet in sub-3-month funding
4 Hold a liquidity buffer of truly liquid assets Government bonds only — bank CDs and corporate bonds became illiquid in 2008
5 Have a contingency funding plan Know exactly what you’d do if markets shut; test it regularly
6 Know your central bank facilities Practise accessing the discount window BEFORE you need it in a panic
7 Know all liability-side exposures Including reputational obligations (off-balance-sheet conduits you’d feel obligated to support)
8 Calculate ALL the metrics LTD ratio alone isn’t enough; you need a dashboard of six or more measures
9 Set FTP correctly Charge business lines the true cost of the liquidity they consume (see Ch 15)
10 Centralise under Group Board Liquidity policy set at the top; no exceptions without Board approval

The Liquid Asset Buffer (LAB)

Before the crisis, many banks had stopped holding government bonds as a reserve. Why would you hold bonds earning 3% when you could lend that money at 6%? When wholesale markets froze in 2008, those banks had nothing to sell for cash.

The LAB is a stockpile of instantly liquid, unencumbered government bonds and central bank reserves. Key rules:

• Only sovereign bonds and central bank deposits qualify. Bank CDs, corporate bonds, and structured products are NOT eligible — they all became illiquid in Q4 2008. • The buffer CANNOT be funded by repo. It must be funded by long-term unsecured liabilities (ideally over one year). • The bank should periodically sell and repurchase LAB securities — this proves they’re genuinely liquid and keeps the operational process fresh. • The cost (typically 100–150 bps of negative carry) gets recharged to business lines through FTP.

A Real LAB Calculation

Component Amount
Wholesale funding gap (peak 90 days) €1,240m
Retail deposit stress (10% Type B + 20% Type A) €91m
Credit pipeline (committed undrawn lending) €44m
Total LAB required €1,375m

At 150 bps negative carry, a €1.375 billion buffer costs about €20 million per year. That sounds like a lot. But Northern Rock’s nationalisation cost UK taxpayers billions. The buffer is cheap insurance.

Chapter 13: The Dashboard — Metrics That Keep You Alive

The Six Essential Metrics

No single number captures liquidity risk. You need a dashboard. Here are the six metrics every bank should calculate:

Metric What It Tells You Target Watch Out
LTD Loans vs deposits 95–105% >100% = wholesale dependent; <70% = lazy capital
1-wk / 1-mo ratio Short-term liquidity Positive Approaching zero = alarm
Cumulative model 12-month cash flow outlook No deficit crossover Crossover = survival horizon breach
LRF Avg asset tenor / avg liability tenor <24 Rising trend = growing mismatch
Concentration Single-name funding dependency <10% per name 14% from one counterparty = immediate action
Inter-entity Who owes whom within the group Standalone adequacy Regulators may ring-fence in crisis

The Survival Period

Perhaps the most intuitive metric: how many business days can the bank stay cash-flow positive with zero access to unsecured funding?

In one example, a bank’s survival period drops from 23 days to 9 days in a single month. The 14-day target is breached. This triggers immediate management action: extend debt maturities, raise new term funding, reduce asset origination.

When a regulator or board member asks “how safe are we?” — this is the number that answers the question in terms anyone can understand.

LCR and NSFR — Basel III’s New Rules

Basel III introduced two strategic liquidity ratios:

LCR (Liquidity Coverage Ratio): Do you have enough liquid assets to cover 30 days of stressed cash outflows? The UK FSA extends this to 90 days, and Choudhry agrees — 30 days is not long enough.

NSFR (Net Stable Funding Ratio): Are your assets funded by stable, long-term liabilities? Available stable funding must exceed required stable funding. Retail deposits get high stability weights; wholesale interbank funding gets zero.

Chapter 14: Reporting — Keeping Score Daily

The One-Page Rule

Choudhry is emphatic: liquidity MI (management information) for senior management should fit on one page. “Keeping to one side of A4 will increase the chance that it will actually be read.”

The monthly snapshot should show: the cumulative gap chart, key ratios (LRF, LTD, inter-group lending), and a brief qualitative commentary covering what changed and why.

Stress Test Outputs

The stress test reports translate abstract scenarios into concrete survival numbers. A sample bank shows:

Scenario Probability Impact Score
Light retail withdrawal (5%) 70% 20 (low)
Severe retail withdrawal (15%) 5% 40 (moderate)
Corporate withdrawal (70%) 1% 100 (severe)
3-notch credit downgrade 1% 90 (severe)
Severe reputational damage 0.5% 100 (catastrophic)

The low-probability, high-impact scenarios are the ones that matter most. A 0.5% probability event that produces catastrophic impact is exactly the kind of tail risk that killed Lehman and Northern Rock. You can’t prevent it, but you can build a buffer large enough to survive it.

Chapter 15: FTP — Charging Business Lines for the Money They Use

Why FTP Is So Important

Funds Transfer Pricing (FTP) is how Treasury charges each business line for the funding it consumes. If your mortgage team makes a 5-year loan, Treasury charges them the cost of 5-year funding. If your trading desk needs overnight cash, they pay the overnight rate.

This sounds boring. It is not. Bad FTP was one of the primary causes of the 2008 crisis.

The Horror Stories

Bank What Went Wrong With FTP Result
UBS CDO desk funded at artificially low internal rate; management called realistic pricing a “constraint on growth” $59bn government bailout
KBC Uniform Libor + 12.5 bps for ALL instruments and ALL tenors €24.5bn government support

At UBS, the CDO desk was borrowing internally at maybe Libor + 10 bps and investing in structured products yielding Libor + 200 bps. On paper, that’s 190 bps of “profit.” But the true cost of funding those illiquid, long-dated assets was much higher. The “profit” was an illusion created by cheap internal funding. When the music stopped, the losses were real and the profits vanished.

How Good FTP Works

The recommended approach: gross at marginal cost.

Gross means every asset and every liability gets individually priced. Business lines cannot deploy funds they raise themselves — everything flows through Treasury.

Marginal cost means the charge reflects today’s market funding cost, not historical averages. If it costs 4% to raise 5-year money today, that’s what the mortgage team pays — even if Treasury locked in 3% funding last year.

The FTP charge must include: (1) Direct cost: the interest rate for that tenor. (2) Liquidity premium: the extra cost of funding illiquid assets. (3) LAB cost: the business line’s share of maintaining the liquid asset buffer. (4) Contingency cost: the cost of keeping emergency funding capacity available.

A key insight: beyond about 5 years, the incremental liquidity stress per additional year becomes minor. So the FTP curve can be essentially flat beyond 5 years. A 7-year loan doesn’t cost much more in liquidity terms than a 5-year loan. But it costs dramatically more than a 6-month loan.

Part IV: Strategy & Governance

Chapters 16–17: Running a Bank That Survives

The Sustainable Business Model

Before the crisis, banks targeted ROE of 22–25%. This wasn’t sustainable — it required excessive leverage, aggressive lending, and tolerance for concentrated risks. Choudhry’s target: 12–14% ROE through the cycle.

“Sustained twelve percent ROE over ten to fifteen years is infinitely preferable to several years of twenty-two percent ROE followed by losses.”

The Natural Experiment

Countries where deposits exceeded loans avoided the 2008 crisis entirely. The first principle of banking — fund before you lend — predicted the outcome perfectly.

What NOT to Do

• Don’t chase market share — Northern Rock captured 19% of all new UK mortgages in H1 2007, including 125% LTV products. Market share is a valid KPI for selling clothes at Asda. It’s not valid for banking.

• Don’t max out leverage — Lehman went from 24:1 to 31:1. Canada imposed leverage caps and didn’t have a crisis.

• Don’t target 22% ROE — that requires dangerous leverage or risky lending. Aim for 12–14% through the cycle.

• Don’t rely on wholesale — when markets freeze, wholesale funding disappears in days. Customer deposits are sticky.

Capital vs Liquidity: A Paradox

Consider two banks:

Bank A Bank B
Equity 5% 20%
Avg debt maturity 6 months 1 month
Liquid assets 10% 5%

Bank B looks stronger — four times more capital! But in a liquidity crisis, Bank A is better off. Its debts have longer maturities (6 months vs 1 month) and it holds more liquid assets (10% vs 5%). Bank B might have plenty of capital but if it can’t roll over its 1-month liabilities, it dies.

Choudhry’s recommended position: 10% equity, 12-month average debt maturity, 10% liquid assets. Balance both sides of the equation.

Chapter 18: Governance — Why Banks Actually Fail

The Core Thesis

If you could identify one single factor that caused the 2008 crisis, it would be failure of corporate governance. Not sub-prime mortgages. Not CDOs. Not deregulation. Those were symptoms. The disease was boards and management teams that either didn’t understand, didn’t care, or were actively excluded from the decisions that destroyed their banks.

The Rogues’ Gallery

Bank CEO/Chair Background What They Did
Northern Rock Chairman = zoologist/science writer (no banking experience) 19% of all new UK mortgages; 125% LTV products; wholesale-funded
HBOS CEO from Asda clothing line (no recession experience in banking) Corporate loans £12bn → £70bn in 3 years
UBS Board lacked derivatives expertise CDO desk funded at fake-low internal rates; “super profits” = illusion
KBC Board unaware of €20bn CDO book FTP = Libor + 12.5 bps flat for everything; 22% ROE = complacency
Lehman CEO “remote” and in “denial” Risk head asked to leave room; leverage 31:1; envy-driven strategy
Dunfermline BS Building society board Bought Lehman MBS; £31m IT fiasco described as “excellent progress”

The Counterintuitive Finding

Here’s the surprising thing: UK banks rated highest on corporate governance in Europe suffered possibly the worst banking crisis. Denmark, rated low on governance, had fewer problems. Two-tier German boards didn’t prevent landesbank failures.

What does this tell us? Board structure doesn’t matter. Board expertise does. You can have the fanciest governance framework in the world, but if the chairman is a zoologist and the CEO comes from a clothing line and the board has never seen a recession — the framework is window dressing.

Choudhry’s Seven Reforms

1. Put a debt holder on the board — shareholders have incentives to increase risk (they keep the upside); debt holders have direct downside exposure. They’re better risk monitors.

2. Require banking expertise for all directors, including NEDs and CEOs. No more zoologists or clothing executives. Regulator must approve.

3. Articulate clear competence boundaries — the board must say explicitly what the bank is good at and stay within those boundaries.

4. Limit board size to 6–10 — larger boards dilute personal accountability.

5. Create an Alpha committee — CEO, CFO, Treasury head, one or two NEDs — meets 1–2 days per month for rapid crisis response. Monthly full board meetings are too slow when the VIX goes from 20 to 80 in weeks.

6. Expertise requirements for sub-committees — audit and risk committees only staffed by people with proven bank experience.

7. Enforce transparency — require management to communicate risk and losses to shareholders in clear language. No describing a £9.5m loss as “excellent progress.”

The Social Contract

Banks receive extraordinary privileges: a banking licence, access to the central bank as lender of last resort, and deposit insurance for their customers. In exchange, society expects them to manage their businesses so they don’t blow up and require taxpayer bailouts.

Choudhry’s closing words on governance: “Banks should not need to be required by law to operate in a way that is sensible; rather, they should seek to ensure they do in any case.”

The motto that opens and closes the book — Dictum Meum Pactum, “My Word Is My Bond” — is both a reminder of what banking was built upon and a challenge to the profession to deserve that trust again.
