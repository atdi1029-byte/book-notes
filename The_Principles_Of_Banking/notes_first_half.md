### Five Basic Principles of Banking
1. Secure funding base (liquidity) for ALL contingencies BEFORE lending beyond deposit base
2. Establish sound base of customer deposits; majority of lending funded from core stable deposits
3. Lend prudently with sound judgement (not following the herd), diverse range but not outside expertise
4. Maintain strong capital base of equity, sufficient to absorb all losses including unexpected ones
5. See (1) — liquidity first, always

**Core message**: CONSERVATISM. Liquidity crises are rare — possible to spend entire career without one.
Temptation to relax conservative principles is strong. Banks must survive liquidity crises without taxpayer support.

## PART I: A PRIMER ON BANKING

### Chapter 1: Bank Business and Balance Sheet Risk

#### Income Types
- **NII** (net interest income): 60–80% of income for retail/building society banks; interest earned minus cost of funding
- **Fee income**: less volatile, no market risk, does carry operational risk; uses less capital
- **Trading income**: most volatile; high market + credit risk; past profit no guarantee of future performance
- UK building society: NII 80%, Fee 18%, Trading 2%
- UK branch of US investment bank: NII 22%, Fee 52%, Trading 26%
- NII sensitive to both credit risk AND market risk (maturity structure of loan book and match/mismatch)
- In low/declining rate environment: banks may suffer negative NII regardless of maturity profile (hard to pass on rate cuts to depositors)

#### Capital and Leverage
- Bank capital = equity = cushion absorbing unreserved losses; enables bank to continue as going concern
- Average equity base = only 8–12% of total balance sheet
- Loss absorption order: reserves → dividends frozen → equity written down
- Capital creditors (including equity holders) SUBORDINATED to senior creditors and deposit holders
- **Tier 1** ("core capital"): equity + reserves + retained earnings — highest quality, not obliged to be repaid
- **Tier 2**: preference shares + hybrid instruments + sub debt — lower quality, repayable, shorter term
- Best-practice: capital NEVER lent to customers (unless risk-free); capital invested in risk-free assets only

#### The Capital Markets and Banking Intermediation
- **John Hicks' "constitutional weakness"**: investors want liquidity and ready access; borrowers want longest-term funding possible → inherent tension
- Banks as intermediaries: accept deposits (short-term, liquid) and lend to borrowers (long-term, illiquid)
- Two primary risks: (1) bank run — large number of depositors withdraw simultaneously; (2) credit risk — large number of borrowers default
- Bank reduces risk by spreading/pooling across wide asset and liability base
- Corporate capital-raising methods: reinvestment of profits; selling equity (gives ownership + voting rights); bank loans (short-term overdraft or 2–5+ year loans); issuing debt securities (bills, CP, bonds)
- Bank loans often inflexible, rate high for all but top corporates — but often first source of corporate finance
- **Regulators** more concerned with systemic risk: failure of one bank endangering entire banking system via knock-on effects
  - Did NOT arise when Barings collapsed (1995) — not large enough part of monetary system
  - BUT in 2008: integrated global industry → banks closely entwined → failure of one creates risk for all lenders → reasoning behind bail-outs of Lloyds TSB, UBS, KBC Bank

#### The Gap (Maturity Transformation)
- **The Gap** = difference in maturity between legal contractual maturity of assets and liabilities
- UK building society: mortgage final maturity 20–25 years; deposits < 2–3 years behavioural maturity
- Maturity transformation IS the basic business of banking — and IS the source of liquidity risk
- "Understanding the Gap and meeting its needs = cornerstone of banking risk management"
- Bank advancing a 25-year mortgage must find funds to match entire loan life — failure = dissolution
- Gap naturally produces reliance on positively sloping yield curve (borrow at shorter/lower rates, lend at longer/higher)
- Note 9: "Gap" = preferred term; Continental Europe prefers "mismatch" — synonymous

#### Key Ratios (Table 1.6)
| Ratio | Formula | Benchmark |
|-------|---------|-----------|
| Pre-tax ROE | Pre-tax income / Avg equity | >20% = strong |
| ROE | Net income / Avg equity | >10% = strong |
| ROA | Net income / Avg assets | >1% = strong |
| Cost-income | Non-interest costs / Net revenues | Lower = better |
| Net interest margin | NII / Avg earning assets | Yield minus funding cost |
| Loan loss provision | Provision / Pre-provision pre-tax income | Credit cost |

- **Author recommends ROA as preferred measure** (ROE incentivises dangerous leverage)
- **RAROC** = Risk-Adjusted Return on Capital = business line profit / business line equity allocation; must exceed cost of equity
- RAROC framework: measure business line returns AFTER adjusting for risk; enables comparison across lines with different risk profiles; high-volume/low-margin and low-volume/high-margin businesses compared on same basis
- If RAROC < cost of equity → destroying shareholder value → ALCO should instruct business line to reprice or exit
- Capital allocation: each line allocated equity capital proportional to its risk; risk-free return on capital NOT included in business line return (paid to shareholders directly)

#### Loan Pricing Examples
- **Example 1.1**: Loan 100, Deposit 90, Equity 10, target ROE 10%, deposit rate 5%, tax 20%
  - Break-even loan rate = **5.75%** (75 bps above funding); below this = zero value creation
- **Example 1.2**: Same + default prob 0% Year 1, 5% Year 2, recovery 40%
  - Break-even = **11.1%** (introduces subjectivity — pricing is "as much an art as a science")
- **Example 1.3**: Loan loss provision = 0.695 on Example 1.2 loan
  - Attributable profit = Post-tax interest margin − fall in loan value = 5.28 − 0.695 = **4.585**
- "One would be surprised just how many banks do not observe this very basic principle"

#### Capital Requirement
- Capital must absorb ALL expected + certain unexpected losses AND still maintain regulatory minimum
- If capital = regulatory minimum and bank suffers expected losses → under-capitalised → loss of confidence → bank run
- **EaR** (Earnings-at-Risk): observe historical distribution of earnings; volatility sets capital requirement
- Starting point = regulatory requirement; measured via: loan revenue, interest margin, MTM volatility, accounting profit

#### Main Bank Risks
- **Credit risk**: default or credit deterioration; parameters = PD, LGD, correlation
  - **Sovereign risk**: country default or moratorium
  - **Portfolio risk**: correlation approaches unity in bear markets; diversity disappears
- **Market risk**: MTM losses from price moves; KEY bank market risk = interest-rate risk
  - Even banking book (no MTM) exposed: fixed-rate assets produce lower profit when funding rates rise
- **Liquidity risk** (two types):
  1. Funding/rollover risk: bank cannot raise funds when needed (Northern Rock Aug 2007; Lehman Sep 2008)
  2. Market liquidity risk: asset cannot be sold/bought at fair price
- **Operational risk**: fraud, system failure (Nick Leeson, Kweku Adoboli examples)
- **Country risk**: war, revolution, sovereign downgrade, BoP crisis, high inflation
- **Model risk**: incorrect model → extensive losses; over-reliance on models literally fatal (2007–09)

#### ALM and ALCO
- "The art of banking is the art of asset and liability management" = maturity transformation
- **ALM desk**: manages IR risk, liquidity risk, FX risk, internal funds pricing
- Responsibility overseeing ALL aspects; individual business lines CANNOT run IR/liquidity risk independently
- **ALCO**: THE most important committee — more important than the Board itself
- "It is possible to have an incompetent Board and still survive a market crisis; however, it is not possible for a bank to have an incompetent ALCO and survive the same crisis"
- Also known as ALMAC or ALPCO; covers balance sheet management, hedging policy, risk measures/limits
- Key principle: liquidity risk and market risk (incl. IR risk) are INTER-RELATED and INTER-DEPENDENT
- Setting up ALM desk as profit centre does NOT obviate need for required levels of ALM control
- **FTP** (funds transfer pricing): cost at which Treasury lends funds to business lines — important discipline feeding into return calculation

#### Bank Capital Structure
- Three requirements: (1) capital must preserve going-concern through losses; (2) must be at least 2× regulatory minimum; (3) must cover EL + contingency reserve + regulatory minimum
- If capital falls to regulatory minimum after losses → non-viable → confidence lost → bank run → taxpayer bailout
- Best-practice: capital invested in risk-free assets ONLY; income from capital → ALCO book → shareholder reserves; NOT added to business line returns

### Chapter 2: Bank Regulatory Capital

#### Basel I (1988)
- Minimum capital = **8%** of risk-weighted assets; set by BIS (carries no legislative weight, but followed universally)
- Risk weights: 0% (own/Zone A sovereign), 20% (inter-bank), 50% (residential mortgage), 100% (corporate)
- **Tier 1**: perpetual loss-absorbing capital (equity + non-cumulative pref shares); minimum = **4%**
- **Tier 2**: upper (perpetual sub debt, cumulative prefs) + lower (fixed maturity sub debt); max = 100% of Tier 1
- Lower Tier 2: cannot exceed 50% of Tier 1; amortises on straight-line basis in last 5 years

**Example 2.2: ABC Bank PLC Capital Adequacy (Basel I)**
| Asset | Weight | Value (£m) | Risk-Weighted (£m) |
|-------|--------|-----------|-------------------|
| T-Bills | 0% | 250 | 0 |
| Inter-bank loans | 20% | 790 | 158 |
| Mortgage book | 50% | 652 | 326 |
| Commercial loans | 100% | 814 | 814 |
| **Total** | | **2,536** | **1,298** |
- Capital charge (8%) = £103.84m; Tier 1 (£456m) well above minimum

**Table 2.1: Basel I Risk Weightings**
| Weighting | Asset Type |
|-----------|-----------|
| 0% | Cash; Zone A sovereign bonds; claims on own sovereign in domestic currency |
| 20% | Inter-bank lending; claims on multilateral development banks; Zone A regional govts; Zone B bank claims <1yr |
| 50% | Residential mortgage loans; residential MBS |
| 100% | All other claims (corporate loans, commercial mortgages, etc.) |

- Zone A = OECD members + countries with special IMF lending arrangements; Zone B = all others
- Banking book assets: booked at cost, NOT revalued; Trading book: MTM daily
- **Example 2.1**: Repo: cash proceeds £50m, OECD bank counterpart (20% RW)
  - Fully collateralised → Capital charge = **£0** (max(50m − 50m) × 8% × 20% = 0)
  - Same-size unsecured loan to same OECD bank → Capital charge = **£800,000** (50m × 8% × 20%)
- Trading book formula (2.2): CA = max(((C_mv − S_mv) × 8% × RW), 0) where C_mv = cash proceeds, S_mv = securities MV
- FRAs and swaps with maturity <1 year: **NO capital requirement at all** under Basel I
- Off-balance-sheet derivatives: attract LOWER capital charge than cash instruments (principal not at risk)
- EAD for derivatives (2.3): EAD = Σmax(0, MTM) + PFE − Collateral + Collateral Haircut; PFE = Contract Value × Add-On

- **Basel I perverse incentive**: blanket 100% for ALL corporate loans → higher return on capital from lending to LOWER-rated corporates

#### Basel II (2004) — Three Pillars
- **Pillar 1**: Minimum capital (credit + operational + market risk)
- **Pillar 2**: Supervisory review — banks must set aside capital ABOVE Pillar 1 minimum
- **Pillar 3**: Disclosure — capital types, adequacy ratios, risk profiles

**Table 2.5: Basel II Risk Weights (Standardised)**
| Asset | AAA–AA | A | BBB | BB–B | Below B | Unrated |
|-------|--------|---|-----|------|---------|---------|
| Sovereign | 0% | 20% | 50% | 100% | 150% | 100% |
| Banks (opt.2 <3m) | 20% | 20% | 20% | 50% | 150% | 20% |
| Corporates | 20% | 50% | 100% | 100% | 150% | 100% |

- Anomaly: low-rated companies attract HIGHER charge than non-rated borrowers
- Undrawn short-term credit facilities: 0% under Basel I → **20%** under Basel II

**Key IRB Formula (2.9)**:
K = [LGD × N((1−R)^0.5 × G(PD) + [R/(1−R)]^0.5 × G(0.999)) − (PD × LGD)] × [1/(1−1.5×b)] × [1 + (M−2.5) × b]
- R = correlation factor (0.12–0.24); b = maturity adjustment; N() = cumulative normal; G() = inverse
- RWA = K × 12.5 × EAD
- Foundation IRB: LGD prescribed at 45% (senior unsecured), 75% (sub unsecured), M=2.5
- Advanced IRB: bank's own PD, LGD, EAD, M; scaling factor 1.06

**Sovereign comparison (Turkey vs. Malaysia)**:
- Turkey (BB−, OECD): Basel I = 0% (OECD member); Basel II standardised = 100%; Foundation IRB = 66%
- Malaysia (A−, non-OECD): Basel I = 100%; Basel II standardised = 20%; Foundation IRB = 41%
- Higher-rated non-OECD sovereigns GAIN under Basel II; lower-rated OECD members face HIGHER charges

**Corporate examples**: AA utility: Basel I = $800k → IRB Foundation = $120k; BB− industrial = $800k → $760k

**Retail under Basel II**: 75% (down from 100%); residential mortgages: 35% (down from 50%); commercial mortgages: 50% (down from 100%)

**ABS tranches (IRB Ratings-Based Approach)**: AAA senior = 7%; AA = 8%; A− = 20%; BBB− = 100%; BB+ = 250%; BB = 425%; BB− = 650%; below = deduction from capital

**Northern Rock / Bradford & Bingley**: used securitisation to back rapid mortgage growth — flawed and failed strategy; covered bonds more attractive than RMBS under Basel II IRB

#### Operational Risk
- BIS definition: "risk of loss from inadequate or failed internal processes, people and systems or from external events"
- Examples: fraud (Barings/Leeson; UBS/Adoboli), system failures, employment litigation, physical damage
- Three methods: **BIA** (15% × 3-year avg gross income), **Standardised** (beta factors per business line 12–18%), **AMA** (internal data, 5-year history required)
- Beta factors: Corporate finance/Trading 18%; Commercial banking 15%; Retail banking 12%
- Insurance can offset max **20%** of op risk capital charge (policy must have ≥1yr term, 90-day notice)

#### Basel III (December 2010)
- 27-country BCBS release; addresses capital insufficiency revealed by 2007–09 crisis
- Minimum core Tier 1 = **4.5%** of RWA (up from 2%)
- Capital conservation buffer = **2.5%** (not compulsory BUT: failure → restricted from dividends/bonuses → effectively mandatory → minimum core Tier 1 = **7%**)
- Countercyclical capital buffer = 0–2.5% (national regulator discretion when credit bubbles forming)
- Leverage ratio = **3%** Tier 1 → balance sheets cannot exceed **33× capital**; mandatory 2018, disclosure 2015
- **LCR**: Stock of high-quality liquid assets / Net cash outflows over 30 days > **100%**
- **NSFR**: Available stable funding / Required stable funding > **100%** (mandatory Jan 2018)
- Risk-weighting methodology UNCHANGED from Basel II
- **Capital Conservation Buffer**: common equity only; phased Jan 2016, fully Jan 2019; failure → restrict payouts

**Table 2.17: Basel III Capital Ratios**
| | Core Tier 1 | Tier 1 | Total Capital |
|---|-------------|--------|---------------|
| Minimum | 4.5% | 6.0% | 8.0% |
| Plus conservation buffer | 2.5% | — | — |
| Minimum + buffer | 7.0% | 8.5% | 10.5% |

- **Non-core Tier 1**: CoCo bonds and preference shares; must have conversion-to-equity or write-down features
- **Tier 3 abolished** under Basel III (was available only for trading book market risk)

#### Return on Capital (Portfolio Analysis)
**Table 2.18**: M=1000, Revenue=100, EL=30, Max loss=500
| Approach | Capital | RoC |
|----------|---------|-----|
| C = L | 500 | 20.0% |
| C = L−EL | 470 | 14.9% |
| C = L−(R−EL) | 430 | 16.3% |

- Third approach is most appropriate return measure for bank (net revenues absorb unexpected losses)
- Capital invested in risk-free assets → income → ALCO book → shareholder reserves
- Including risk-free returns from capital in RoC = BAD PRACTICE; artificially inflates RoC
- "Capital base NOT designed to generate revenue"

### Chapter 3: Banking and Credit Risk

#### Credit Risk Overview
- Credit risk as old as banking; banks become less aware during growth periods
- Evidence of pre-crisis complacency: blue-chip syndicated loans as low as **10–12 bps** over Libor (1992–99, 2002–07); banks moved to lower-rated credits to maintain margins

#### Credit Spread Risk
- Credit spread = excess premium over risk-free rate required by market for assumed credit exposure
- Credit spread risk = risk of MTM losses from changes in observed credit spreads (portfolios where spread is traded/MTM)
- **Name recognition** approach: relying on issuer's reputation rather than formal ratings — Barings Bank (1995) example of failure; Barings had sub-Libor borrowing status (on par with top clearing banks) yet collapsed
- Ratings process: issuer bears cost of rating; investors may refuse to buy unrated paper; rating = on the ISSUE not the organisation (though market commonly refers to "a triple-A company")
- **Credit watch**: precursor to possible downgrade/upgrade; usually results in downgrade; during watch phase, bond prices fall as investors sell

#### Credit Risk Principles
- "Credit risk management is a judgement call. The one single factor that most assists effective credit risk management is knowing one's market."
- **Never originate loans or invest in assets you do not understand** — irrespective of complexity
- 2007–09: impossible to accurately model credit risk for all eventualities; over-reliance on "black box" literally fatal
- Ultimate question: "What is the chance the investment will incur losses, and how much will the lender lose? And can the lender survive this potential loss?"

#### Recovery Rates and CDS
- Recovery rate parameter: **assumed** (actual may not be known for years)
- CDS market standard: recovery usually set at **40%** (Bloomberg CDSW default); post-crisis more commonly **50%**
- Market-implied default probability (flat term structure): P(default) ≈ CDS spread / (1 − RR)
- Best approach for risk management: assume **0% recovery rate** (bank written down fully; recovery received later)
- Market gives little weight to RR when default probability low — becomes risky assumption during recession
- Despite being key CDS pricing parameter, RR doesn't appear to heavily influence CDS premiums in practice

#### Credit VaR (3-step methodology)
1. **Exposure Profiles**: exposure stated on equivalent basis for all products (bonds, loans, off-balance sheet)
2. **Volatility from Up/Downgrades**: probability of migration to any rating; revalue at risk horizon using either recovery rate (default) or forward zero-coupon curve (migration); calculate expected value + standard deviation
3. **Correlations**: individual distributions combined; requires correlation estimates in credit quality changes
- Standard horizon: **1-year** (fits annual budget; matches credit agency data)

#### Credit Limit Setting
- **Author's reverse principle**: bank determines strategy based on risk expertise → drives capital level → capital allocated as credit limits (opposite of standard textbook "capital drives limits")
- **Five essential principles**:
  1. Any single exposure default (0% recovery) must be containable within capital base without endangering going-concern
  2. Diversify by sector/geography/product — but ONLY within bank's expertise
  3. Minimum internal (and external) rating criteria established (e.g., investment-grade only)
  4. Don't over-extend borrower (know customer's leverage ratio, debt service coverage)
  5. Limits set to avoid concentration by borrower rating
- **Three overlapping limit categories**: individual obligor (by product/currency), geographical region, industrial sector
- **Limit excess** = serious breach → report to ALCO; corrective actions: cease business, transfer via secondary market/securitisation/CDS, increase limit, transfer capacity, asset disposal

#### Loan Origination Standards
- Standards must be **through-the-cycle** — NO reduction during economic growth
- Failed approach examples: Northern Rock/Bradford & Bingley (100%/125% LTV mortgages); HBOS "black box" tick-box credit scoring
- Most common default time: **45–55 months** after loan start date (exception: loans at top of cycle)
- Default statistics lag actual economy → danger of continuing at lower standards when bank should be more conservative

**FICO Score (Example 3.1)**: range **350–850**; based on payment history, account balances, credit type, credit history length, new applications; NOT income, location, age, occupation

#### Collateral Enablement
- Bank funding models moving toward greater secured funding → assets should be repo-able or securitisable
- Collateral enablement template: legal framework, transferability, disclosure, currencies, vanilla vs. exotic
- Portfolio uniformity matters: 100 bullet loans same type/jurisdiction = much easier to manage than 100 heterogeneous loans in 10 countries

#### SVA and Credit Strategy
- Common error: mistaking **artificial funding profit** for genuine SVA (e.g., SIVs buying long-dated assets funded with short-dated CP → unsustainable when CP market disappeared Aug 2007)
- Lending may be lowest-ROCE business line; discipline essential
- Undertaking loss-making transactions to generate subsequent profitable business = tenuous strategy at best

### Chapter 4: Securitisation

#### Definition and History
- **Securitisation**: sale of assets (generating cash flows) from originator to SPV; SPV issues notes collateralised by asset cash flows
- Introduced US 1970s (residential mortgages); expanded to credit cards, auto loans, equipment leases
- **Synthetic securitisation** from **1997**: assets NOT sold; credit risk transferred via credit derivatives
- US ABS issuance 2007: **USD 863bn** → collapsed to **USD 230bn** in 2008–09; majority via TALF

#### Three Main Reasons Banks Securitise
1. **Funding**: diversify sources, reduce cost, reduce maturity mismatches; SPV notes typically rated HIGHER than originating bank → lower funding cost; de-links originator credit rating from notes
2. **Balance sheet capital management**: reduce/release regulatory capital; improve ROE; SPV not subject to Basel rules → needs only economically required capital (significantly below 8%)
3. **Risk management and credit risk transfer**: if first-loss not retained → credit risk removed entirely; can remove NPLs freeing capital + potentially profiting from future recovery

#### Key Structures
- **Amortising (pass-through)**: principal and interest paid throughout life; priced on WAL (weighted-average life)
- **Revolving**: collections buy new receivables during revolving period; used for credit cards, auto loans
- **Master Trust**: allows multiple securitisations from same SPV; used by frequent RMBS/credit card issuers
- **Sequential pay**: senior bonds fully redeemed before junior; **pro-rata**: simultaneous with triggers reverting to sequential if performance deteriorates

#### Cash Flow Waterfall
- All cash paid in strict priority order; independent agent runs coverage tests; failure → vehicle pays down senior notes first
- Only when senior obligations met can junior obligations receive cash

#### Basel II/III Capital Implications
- **Basel I**: blanket 100% for all ABS except AAA RMBS (50%) → drove securitisation as capital management tool
- **Basel II IRB-RBA**: AAA senior = 7%; AA = 8%; A− = 20%; BBB− = 100%; BB+ = 250%; BB = 425%; BB− = 650%; below = deduction
- Sharp cliff from BBB− to BB+ (8% → 20%+) → strong incentive to minimise unrated tranches; equity piece should be as small as possible
- **Basel III**: originator must retain **5% economic interest** (cannot hedge); FSA requires "Significant Risk Transfer" to external investor for capital relief — these are somewhat conflicting requirements

#### Airways No. 1 — Airline Ticket Receivables (Hypothetical Case Study)
- EUR 200m, 3-tranche floating-rate notes; airline ticket credit card receivables
- DSCR target > **4.0** (vs ~2.5–3.0 for residential mortgages) due to higher volatility of airline earnings
- Process: 3–12+ months from inception to closure
- Key modelling: growth projections, inflation, tax; multiple scenarios; DSCR = primary indicator

#### ECB-Led In-House Deals (Post-2008)
- Banks originate ABS using own illiquid assets, buy ALL notes themselves, repo at ECB
- ECB minimum requirements: **AAA** rating, senior tranche only, no synthetic content, listed in Europe (e.g., Irish Stock Exchange), Euroclear/Clearstream eligible
- Haircuts: CLO securities in EUR = **12%** regardless of maturity; USD = standard + additional **10–20%** for FX
- **Red Sea Master Series** case study (EUR 2bn corporate loans): 3 SPVs (XYZ Bank plc, Trust, Issuer); all notes purchased by XYZ Bank; NO accounting/capital relief (bank retains substantially all risks/rewards); continuing recognition as loans and receivables on balance sheet

#### Synthetic Securitisation
- Assets NOT sold; credit risk transferred via credit derivatives (typically CDS on reference portfolio)
- Originating bank remains legal owner; retains relationship; reduces regulatory capital via credit risk transfer
- Credit events: bankruptcy, failure to pay, restructuring (definitions vary by jurisdiction/ISDA definitions)
- Introduced 1997 — much quicker to execute than cash securitisation (no "true sale" legal process)
- **True sale** test: must be a genuine legal transfer of ownership; courts assess whether SPV is at risk → if bank retains substantially all risks/rewards (as in Red Sea deal) → no off-balance-sheet treatment
- **Significant Risk Transfer** (SRT): FSA/PRA requirement for capital relief; SRT must be demonstrated to regulator; conflicts with Basel III 5% retention requirement → tension between two objectives

#### Secured Funding Post-2008
- More secured funding → more assets **encumbered** → higher LGD for unsecured creditors in bankruptcy
- Generic covered bonds: funding level significantly lower than unsecured bank debt, especially at longer tenors
- Key distinction: covered bond = bank's obligation (dual recourse — to bank AND cover pool); ABS = SPV's obligation only (non-recourse to originator after true sale)
- Covered bonds preferred by central banks as collateral; RMBS accepted but subject to haircuts

### Chapter 5: The Yield Curve

#### Why It Matters for Bankers
- "The long-term government bond yield is the single most important variable in any market economy." (Kaletsky, *The Times*)
- Changes in rates directly impact NII; curve captures term rates and market expectations
- IR gap reflects state of bank borrowing/lending; gaps sensitive to curve shape/slope
- Balance sheet from both asset and liability sides sensitive to curve changes
- Balance sheet management and valuation requires accurate, liquid-market-reflecting yield curve

#### Types of Yield Curve
- **YTM curve**: most common; plots yield-to-maturity against term; weakness = assumes constant reinvestment rate
- **Coupon yield curve**: same coupon across maturities; high-coupon bonds generally trade at higher yields (reinvestment risk, tax treatment); rarely observed in practice
- **Par yield curve**: bonds trading at par; used in primary market for new issue coupon determination
- **Zero-coupon (spot) curve**: TRUE term structure of interest rates; no reinvestment risk; stated yield = actual annual return
- "Zero-coupon rate" and "spot rate" used synonymously

#### Bootstrapping — Spot Rates from Coupon Bonds
**Key formula**: df_N = (1 − rm_N × A_{N-1}) / (1 + rm_N)

**Example 5.1**: Given zero-coupon yields: 1y=5.000%, 2y=5.271%, 3y=5.598%, 4y=6.675%, 5y=7.213%
- Discount factors: 1y=0.95238, 2y=0.90237, 3y=0.84924, 4y=0.77223, 5y=0.70593
- 6% 2-year bond price = **101.365** (YTM 5.263%); 7% 5-year bond = **99.869** (YTM 7.05%)

**Par yield formula**: rp = (1 − df_N) / Σ(n=1 to N) df_n

#### Forward Rate Formulas and Interpretation
- **Equation 5.8**: (1 + ₍n₋₁₎rf_n) = (1 + rs_n)^n / (1 + rs_{n-1})^{n-1} = df_{n-1} / df_n
- From 1y=5.00%, 2y=5.256%: 1rf₂ = **5.514%**; from 3y=5.784%: 2rf₃ = **6.848%**
- **Spot rate = geometric average of forward rates** (discrete time)
- **Spot rate = arithmetic average of forward rates** (continuous time) — key difference

**Table 5.1: Coupon yield, zero-coupon yield, forward rate (illustrative)**
| Year | Coupon yield | Zero-coupon yield | Forward rate |
|------|-------------|-------------------|--------------|
| 1 | 5.000% | 5.000% | 5.000% |
| 2 | 5.250% | 5.257% | 5.514% |
| 3 | 5.750% | 5.784% | 6.848% |

- When coupon yield curve is positively sloping: spot curve > par curve; forward curve > spot curve
- Forward rate = **marginal return**; spot rate = **average return**
- An increasing spot rate curve does NOT necessarily mean increasing forward curve
- Forward curve must turn DOWN BEFORE spot curve reaches its maximum

**Example 5.2a — Spot from forwards**: 1y cash = 5.00%; implied 1y rates in 1y = 5.95%, in 2y = 7.25%
- 3y spot = (1.05 × 1.0595 × 1.0725)^(1/3) − 1 = **6.062%**

**Annualised forward rate (Equation 5.9)**: ₐrf_b = [(1 + rs_b)^b / (1 + rs_a)^a]^(1/(b-a)) − 1

**Practical application**: To find coupon on new 3-year par bond given spot curve (5%, 5.257%, 5.784%):
- 100 = C/(1.05) + C/(1.05257)² + (C+100)/(1.05784)³ → C = **5.75%**

**Verification of spot/forward relationship**: [(1.05257)² × (1.06848)]^(1/3) − 1 = 5.784% (matches 3y spot) ✓

**Bootstrapping discount function (Table 5.3)** — semi-annual bonds:
| Coupon | Maturity | Term (yr) | Price | Discount Factor |
|--------|---------|-----------|-------|----------------|
| 7% | 7/6/2001 | 0.5 | 101.65 | 0.98213 |
| 8% | 7/12/2001 | 1.0 | 101.89 | 0.94194 |
| 6% | 7/6/2002 | 1.5 | 100.75 | 0.92211 |
| 6.50% | 7/12/2002 | 2.0 | 100.37 | 0.88252 |
- d(0.5): 101.65 / 103.50 = 0.98213; d(1.0): (101.89 − 4 × 0.98213) / 104 = 0.94194; and so on
- Practical complication: bonds rarely spaced exactly 6 months apart and priced at par → smooth curve fitting required

**Critical insight on forward rates**:
- Forward rates reflect everything known in the market up to that point
- **INCORRECT** to view forward curve as forecast of future spot rates
- In semi-strong form efficient markets: only ONE set of forward rates from a given spot curve (market equilibrium); forward rates change instantly when new market knowledge arrives
- Forward rates are important for quoting prices for future dealing; they are the best IMPLIED expectation only

**Case Study 5.1 (July 14, 2000 — full discount function)**:
- Sources: money market rates + Eurodollar futures + swap rates
- 1-month (4.21875%): Df=0.99643; 3-month (4.25%): Df=0.98917; 6-month (4.50%): Df=0.97770
- 1-year swap (4.95%): Df=0.95283; 2-year (5.125%): Df=0.90480; 5-year (6.00%): Df=0.74421
- Critique: three data source types produce kinks; money market + swap rates include credit premium; futures rates do not (exchange-traded); bootstrapping errors compound at longer maturities

**Example 5.3 (break-even principle)**: 1y spot=10%, 2y spot=12%
- Forward-forward rate (1×2): R = (1.12)² / (1.10) − 1 = **14.04%**

**Example 5.4**: Want 2-year zero-coupon rate in 3 years; Treasuries: 3y=7.00%, 5y=7.25%
- R₃×₅ = √[(1.0725)⁵ / (1.0700)³] − 1 = **7.63%**

**Example 5.5 (money market forward)**:
- Borrowing £100m 30 days at 5.875%; loan £100m 60 days at 6.125%
- 30v60 day forward rate = **6.3443%**

#### Yield Curve Theories
1. **Pure/Unbiased Expectations Hypothesis**: forward rates = unbiased estimators of future spot; long-term rate = geometric average of expected future short-term rates; positive curve → market expects rates to rise
   - Equation 5.12: (1 + rs_N)^N = (1 + rs₁)(1 + ₁rf₂)...(1 + ₍N₋₁₎rf_N)
   - Criticism (Fama 1976): forward rates are BIASED upward; empirically q in test ₁rs₁ − ₀rs₁ = p + q(₀rf₂ − ₀rs₁) is positive but less than one → biased expectations theory

2. **Liquidity Preference Theory**: longer maturity = more risky; lenders demand liquidity premium
   - Equation 5.15: forward rate > E(future spot) by liquidity premium L_i
   - Premium increases with maturity at DECREASING rate → curve positively sloped with decreasing slope
   - Explains why positive curve even when expected future rates are constant (Example 5.7: expected rates flat at 4.5%, but liquidity premium creates upward-sloping curve from 5% to 7%)

3. **Segmentation Hypothesis**: banks at short end, pension funds at long end; no interrelationships
   - Cannot be used to INTERPRET curve (no information content about future rates)

4. **Preferred Habitat Theory**: investors have preferences but can be induced to other maturities by sufficient incentive; more realistic than pure segmentation

5. **Combined Theory**: combination of expectations + liquidity preference
   - Equation 5.16: ₀rf_i = E(₍i₋₁₎rs₁) + L_i

**Inverted curves and recessions**: Every US recession since 1955 (bar one) preceded by inverted Treasury curve; US curve inverted early 2007 → signalled 2008–09 recession. UK gilt curve inverted July 1997–June 1999 despite no recession — special factors: BoE independence + expected euro convergence.

#### Swap Spreads
- Swap spread = swap rate minus equivalent government bond yield (same basis)
- Indicator of overall health of the economy; understanding determinants important for ALM
- Swap spreads respond to: bank credit quality (Libor-GC repo spread), curve level/slope, corporate bond supply, market volatility (flight to quality), government borrowing levels
- VIX correlation with 10y USD swap spread: generally positive; exception = 9/11 period (−0.17) because Fed cut 175 bps, overwhelming geo-political factors
- **Key**: even during high tension, swap spreads also respond to micro-level financial factors

#### Yield Curve Fitting
- **Linear interpolation**: common but NOT recommended — produces bumpy spot/forward curves with kinks
- **Cubic polynomial**: d̂(t) = 1 + a₁t + a₂t² + a₃t³; too limited; non-local effects between maturities
- **Cubic spline (formal)**: ri(t) = a_i + b_i×t + c_i×t² + d_i×t³ over [t_i, t_{i-1}]; 4n unknown coefficients solvable because: fit observed data; twice differentiable at knot points; instantaneously straight at start and end (r''(0) = 0)
- **B-spline formula (5.59)**: B_p(τ) = Σ(j=p to p+4) [∏(1/(X_i − X_j))] × (τ − X_p)³; regression equation δ̂ = B'λ; solved via OLS minimising ε'ε
- **Nelson-Siegel (1987)**: rf(m) = β₀ + β₁×exp(−m/t₁) + β₂×(m/t₁)×exp(−m/t₁); 4 parameters; long-end asymptote = β₀; short-end value = β₀ + β₁
- **Svensson extension (5.64)**: rf(m) = β₀ + β₁×exp(−m/t₁) + β₂×(m/t₁)×exp(−m/t₁) + β₃×(m/t₂)×exp(−m/t₂); 6 parameters; preferred in banks — less oscillating at long tenors; advantage: long-date forward curve forced into horizontal asymptote; user need not specify knot points
- Disadvantage of Nelson-Siegel/Svensson: less flexible than splines; inaccurate for curves with both a hump AND a trough
- **Smoothness penalty (5.67)**: ∫(f''(t))² dt — integral of squared second derivative; spot rates = averages of forward rates → will always be smoother than forward curve
- **Three criteria for curve selection**: (1) accuracy — reasonable fit, flexible for variety of shapes; (2) model consistency — compatible with theoretical model (Vasicek, CIR); (3) simplicity — tractable computation
- **Key problem with bootstrapping**: minor errors in discount factors compound → spot rates; compound again → forward rates → wildly oscillating forward curve

#### Bank's Internal Funding Curve (TLP Procedure — 4 steps)
1. Fit selected method to market data
2. Extract bank's risky yield curve — baseline for vanilla fixed-rate bond issued at par
3. Construct par-par asset swap curve
4. Adopt as **Term Liquidity Premium (TLP) curve** — bank's baseline pricing curve (unsecured)
- Use **14-day or 30-day moving average**; switch to 60-day during extreme volatility (ALCO sub-committee decision)
- Loans with long gestation: use **3-month moving average**, updated quarterly
- UK FSMA Section 166 review on FTP highlighted need for consistent pricing curve across all business lines

#### Multi-currency Yield Curves
- Post-2008: single-currency curves insufficient; need baseline currency curve + foreign curve + basis adjustment
- For collateralised swaps: use local currency OIS rate (EUR → EONIA; GBP → SONIA)
- **Hybrid yield curve**: take highest daily forward rate from three reference currencies at each point; not smooth (kinks at changeover points)
- Purpose: always discount at most conservative rate → reduce probability of underpricing a swap

#### Secured Funding Curve
- OIS curve = lower bound; bank's unsecured COF = upper bound
- Jumbo Pfandbrief curve = tighter lower bound (liquid, transparent, secured)
- At 100% OC: secured curve → OIS; at 0% OC: secured curve → COF curve
- **Table 5.10**: GBP generic RMBS spreads over 3m Libor: 1y=147bps, 2y=158bps, 3y=157.5bps, 5y=167bps, 7y=163bps
- **Covered bond spreads** (specific issuer): 18m=145bps, 3.5y=181bps, 6.25y=185bps, 9y=180bps

## PART II: BANK ASSET AND LIABILITY MANAGEMENT

### Chapter 6: ALM I — Liquidity Gap and Funding Policy

#### ALM Definition
- ALM = high-level management of bank's assets and liabilities — a **strategy-level discipline**, not tactical
- Implemented in Treasury division; overall policy set by ALCO
- Principal function: manage IR risk and liquidity risk; also sets overall credit risk policy
- Must have remit overseeing ALL aspects of bank's operations
- Key principle: liquidity risk and market risk are INTER-RELATED — cannot be managed/monitored separately

#### Interest-Rate Risk (Two Strands)
1. Risk from changes in asset-liability value due to changes in rates (cash flows and present values)
2. Risk associated with **optionality** (early redeemable loans, embedded options)
- These manifest as: (1) **liquidity gap** — mismatch between different terms of A&L; (2) **interest-rate gap** — mismatch between different interest rates struck

#### Liquidity Gap
- **Liquidity gap** = difference in maturity of assets and liabilities at each point along term structure
- Amount of A&L maturing at any one time also won't match (though overall must balance)
- Risk = bank cannot refinance assets as liabilities become due
- Manage by: (1) dispersing liability profile — **no >20% in any single bucket**; (2) planning large funding in advance; (3) holding significant liquid assets (T-bills, CDs, sovereign bonds)
- **ALM "Smile"**: liability profile humped — avoids concentration in any one maturity bucket
- **Marginal gap**: difference between change in assets and change in liabilities during specified period; positive = asset change exceeds liability change

**Table 6.1** (Regional European bank, EUR millions):
| Bucket | 1W | 1M | 3M | 6M | 9-12M | >12M |
|--------|----|----|----|----|-------|------|
| Assets | 10 | 90 | 460 | 710 | 520 | 100 |
| Liabilities | 100 | 380 | 690 | 410 | 220 | 90 |
| Gap | −90 | −290 | −230 | +300 | +300 | +10 |

- Classic pattern: short-funded at short end (need to rollover); long positions at medium/long

#### Undated Assets/Liabilities
- NIBLs (non-interest-bearing liabilities): current/checking accounts, instant-access deposits
- Three treatments: (1) place in shortest bucket; (2) place in longest bucket (stable over time); (3) split into core (long-dated) + unstable (shortest bucket)
- **Behaviouralisation analysis** required to determine core balance

#### ALM Desk — Cost Centre vs. Profit Centre
- **Three stages of development**:
  1. **Cost centre**: minimises costs, hedges risk; profit target = zero
  2. **Minimising cost of funding**: maintains element of IR risk based on rate view; credited with savings vs. full-hedge costs
  3. **Profit centre**: optimises funding within limits (gap, VaR, earnings volatility)
- Profit centre status does NOT eliminate required levels of ALM control

### Chapter 7: ALM II — Balance Sheet Management

#### Historical Development
- Before Bretton Woods breakdown: ALM straightforward; mostly asset management within regulatory constraints
- Citibank's negotiable CD (1960s) → concept of interest margin and interest gap
- Rising rate volatility + higher absolute levels made gap management vital
- Derivatives (FRAs, swaps, early 1980s): allowed traditional "borrow short, lend long" to continue while hedging

#### Four Key ALM Concepts
1. **Liquidity**: ease of converting assets to cash; most liquid = overnight funds; least liquid = long-dated syndicated loans
2. **Money market term structure**: yield curve shape/expectations impact ALM significantly
3. **Maturity profile**: matched = locked-in spread; unmatched + yield curve shape = total IR risk
4. **Default risk**: risk of non-payment

#### Interest-Rate Risk Sources (Five Types)
1. **Gap risk** (mismatch risk): yield curve level and shape changes reduce NII
2. **Yield curve risk**: non-parallel or pivotal shifts; barbell structure vulnerable to inversion
3. **Basis risk**: assets priced off one rate, funding priced off another (e.g., US prime rate vs. Libor)
4. **Run-off risk**: NIBLs in rising rate environment → depositors withdraw, funds may leave banking system
5. **Option risk**: customers terminate arrangements ahead of maturity (CDs, demand deposits, mortgages)

#### Gap Formulas
- **Gap (7.1)**: Gap = A_ir − L_ir (interest-rate-sensitive assets minus liabilities)
- **ΔNII (7.2)**: ΔNII = Gap × Δr
- Positively gapped (A > L): NII rises with rates; negatively gapped: NII rises with rate falls
- **Duration gap (7.4)**: = Duration_A − w × Duration_L (where w = liabilities/assets ratio); higher = more IR exposure
- **Gap ratio (7.3)**: A_ir / L_ir; >1 = more rate-sensitive assets → rise in rates increases NPV

**Key distinction — liquidity gap vs. interest-rate gap**:
- Liquidity gap: ALL assets and liabilities; interest-rate gap: fixed-rate instruments only
- Interest-rate gap cannot be calculated without specifying a period (depends on fixed/variable distinction + maturity period)
- Primary purpose of gap report: determine sensitivity of interest margin to rate changes
- Gap measurement is always "behind the curve" — historical snapshot; actual gap is dynamic

**Portfolio NPV (Formulas 7.5/7.6/7.7)**:
- NPV = A − L ± OBS (market value of assets minus liabilities, plus/minus off-balance-sheet)
- MD_NPV = MD_A − MD_L ± MD_OBS (modified duration of net portfolio)
- ΔNPV = NPV × MD_NPV × Δr (change in market value for rate change)
- Note: modified duration accurate for small changes; >50 bps shifts produce significant errors
- Duration gap = convenient single summary number; drawback = assumes parallel yield curve shift

#### Detailed UK Bank Gap Profile (Table 7.3)
- Total A&L = £40,533m; short-funded in 0–3 months (−£2,586m combined)
- Key assets: variable mortgages £14,850m, fixed mortgages £4,181m, unsecured/leasing £3,720m
- PVBP grand total: −£1,293,155 (net short duration position)
- Maturity buckets: 14 buckets from "up to 1 month" to "10+ years"

#### Gap Analysis Assumptions and Weaknesses
- Assumes parallel shift in yield curve; contractual repayments are met; re-pricing at mid-point of time bucket
- Weaknesses:
  1. Repricing intervals arbitrary — book of liabilities repricing in 1 month + equal assets repricing in 11 months → 1-year gap = zero (no risk), but NII significantly at risk from a rate rise
  2. Parallel shift assumption — in reality, different maturity rates change by different amounts
  3. Cannot incorporate embedded options (mortgages, convertibles): only first-order exposure captured
- Despite weaknesses, widely used: simple, data already compiled for regulatory reporting, spreadsheet-based, explainable to senior management

#### ALM Policies by Bank Size
- **Bank S (£10bn)**: ALCO meets monthly minimum; IR sensitivity ratio target 90–120% (0–90 days), 80–110% (90–365 days)
- **Bank M (£50bn)**: 6m and 12m cumulative A_ir/L_ir ratio 90–110%; loan-to-deposit ratio 85–95%; scenario analysis: if potential earnings impact >10% → instructions to alter book shape
- **Bank L (£200bn)**: target ROC 14–18%; 100bp change should not impact NII >10%; gap-to-earnings-assets ratio limit ±15% to ±20%; ALCO meets fortnightly; simulation modelling standard scenarios: ±100bp, ±250bp parallel shift, ±25bp steepening/flattening, combination shifts

#### Securitisation Economics (Example 7.4)
- ABC Bank: £100m mortgages, Basel I 50% weight → capital requirement = **£4m**
- Weighted funding cost F: 96% × 10% + (8% × 50%) × (25% × 50% + 10.20% × 50%) = **10.30%**
- Post-securitisation: 90% senior at 9.80% + 10% sub at 10.61% + 0.20% servicing = **10.08%** funding cost
- Securitisation funding cost 22 bps LOWER than balance sheet funding → quantified economic benefit

#### Cost of Borrowing and FTP
- **Blended cost of funds**: USD 100 = 40 borrowed 2y + 60 borrowed 1y; rw = weighted average nearer to r2 (longest-dated, largest weight per year) but pulled toward r1 (larger amount)
- Large commercial banks: calculate WACC as daily interest payment on each loan outstanding, summed, divided by total nominal
- Business line funding: each desk has different liquidity profile; market-making = O/N to 1 week; investment portfolio = longer-dated; Treasury charges one WACC rate to all

- O/N $200m at 1.05%; 1-week $200m at 1.07%; 3-month $100m at 1.15%

**"Cash" vs. "Balance Sheet" funding approaches**:
- Cash funding: business owns portion of entity's cash (including realised P&L); historical profits reduce future funding requirement
- Balance sheet funding: Treasury owns all cash; lends to businesses only to fund current portfolio value; businesses pay opportunity cost of unrealised P&L → incentivises not carrying unnecessary unrealised P&L

### Chapter 8: ALM III — Trading and Hedging Principles

#### Yield Curve Trading
- **Funding short** (positive curve): borrow 1-week, lend 3-month = creates tail = IR gap exposure; bank rolls over funding at 1-week intervals; risk = short-dated rates rise
- **Funding long** (inverted curve): borrow 4-month, lend 2-week; bank expects rates to fall; inverted curve signals benign inflation environment
- **Credit intermediation**: repo out GC (e.g., 5.83%), buy bank CDs (e.g., 6.00%) → earn spread risk-free; capital: gilt repo 0% risk-weighted, bank CD ~20% risk-weighted

#### Repo Specials Trading
- When stock in demand → repo rate falls below GC; natural holder obtains cheap funding by lending stock and re-lending cash at GC rate
- Example: issue at 5.5% in 1-week repo while GC=7% → earns 150 bps spread
- Positive correlation: extent of "special" ↔ extent of richness in cash market

#### Bond Spread Trade (Example 8.1 — UK Gilt Market, Nov 2000)
- Inverted curve; trader believes 2y–5y spread will widen
- Long £10m 7% 2002 gilt (2yr) at 5.732%; short £3.23m 8.5% 2005 gilt (5yr) at 5.565% — BPV-weighted
- Current spread: 16.71 bps; breakeven at 1-month: **16.50 bps**; at 3-month: **16.62 bps**
- Repo determines net funding cost = breakeven cost; disciplined trader has time horizon + stop-loss

#### Interest Rate Futures Hedging

**Example 8.2 — Hedging Forward 3-month Lending (£10m)**:
- 1 June: expect £10m inflow 1 September; expects rates to fall; buy 20 Sep short sterling futures at 93.22
- Result: Libor fell to 6.25%; futures rose to 93.705; P&L = +97 ticks; effective rate = **6.735%** (vs locked-in 6.78%)

**Example 8.3 — Hedging Forward 6-month Borrowing (£30m)**:
- 16 June: sell 30 Sep + 30 Dec short sterling futures; strip rate = **6.53%**
- Result: 3m Libor rose to 6.4375%; 6m Libor 6.8125%; futures P&L = +56 ticks; effective rate = **6.5325%** (close to strip rate of 6.53%)

#### Bond Futures Hedge Ratios
1. **Conversion factor method**: Contracts = (M_bond × CF) / M_fut — deliverable bonds only; transparent; constant
2. **Modified duration / BPV method**: Contracts = (M_bond / M_fut) × (BPV_bond / BPV_fut)
   - BPV_fut = BPV_CTD / CF_CTD; must be continuously updated as BPV changes with price
   - **BPV (PV01/DV01)**: change in bond price for 1 bp parallel shift in yield curve

#### Credit Derivatives for ALM
- Bank can buy CDS protection on loan while keeping loan on balance sheet → maintains client relationship while hedging credit risk
- Credit derivatives separate ownership from management of credit risk; enable risk transfer even for illiquid assets
- Example: Haarman & Reimer loan (Euribor+225bps, A3); Akzo Nobel CDS at 28bps (A3 chemicals) → bank buys protection at 28bps without obligor knowing
- **Approaches for CDS notional sizing**:
  - **Par-for-par**: same nominal; simple but inaccurate when bond away from par
  - **DV01**: set notional relative to actual bond price; recommended when bond trading away from par
  - **Formula (8.10)** for bond above par: Hedge = N + [((P − 100)/(1 − R)) × N]; bond at 110 with 30% recovery = **$11,428,571** notional for $10m bond
- Standard assumed recovery rates: IG corporates 40%; insurance 30%; sub-IG 20%

#### Yield Curve Trading — Further Detail
- **Strip hedge**: using futures contracts across multiple maturities to hedge a forward position; each maturity "strip" locks in a specific forward rate
- Strip hedge for 6-month forward borrowing: sell near-dated + far-dated contracts; the combined strip rate approximates the actual 6-month forward rate
- **Tail**: difference between futures price lock-in and actual cash market settlement; "funding the tail" = managing this residual position
- Repo rate for GC: baseline funding cost; "special" stocks trade below GC; natural holder of a special can earn spread by lending stock in repo and reinvesting cash proceeds at GC rate
- **Credit intermediation trade**: buy credit-risky instrument (e.g., bank CD) funded by repo of government stock; profit = credit spread minus haircut cost; regulatory capital treatment = critical (0% weighted gilt repo vs. ~20% bank CD)

#### Formal IRR Policy (Example 8.9)
- Banking book policy: all assets match-funded OR hedged via internal swaps with Treasury
- IRR measured via both DV01 and VaR at: group, country/subsidiary, ALCO book, money markets, trading book, customer book
- **Customer Book**: commercial loans and deposits; IRR policy = no IRR risk via matched funding
- **ALCO Book**: equity capital, Liquid Asset Buffer, term placements, IRR hedges; equity capital assigned average duration of **10 years**
- Sensitivity tests: parallel and pivotal shifts; typically ±100bp, ±250bp, ±25bp steepening/flattening

### Chapter 9: ALM IV — The ALCO

#### ALCO Mission (Table 9.1)
| Area | Function |
|------|----------|
| Management and reporting | Formulating ALM strategy; management reporting; ALCO agenda; liquidity/gap/IRR reports; scenario planning; income projection |
| Asset management | Managing bank liquidity book (CDs, bills, FRNs); managing FRN book; investing bank capital |
| ALM strategy | Yield curve analysis; money market trading |
| Funding and liquidity management | Liquidity policy; managing funding and liquidity risk; ensuring funding diversification |
| Risk management | Formulating hedging policy; IRR exposure management; hedging using cash and derivatives |
| Internal treasury function | FTP system and level; funding group entities; cost of capital |

- ALCO meets **once a month** minimum; extraordinary meetings during stress or urgency
- Typical composition: CEO, heads of business lines, head of Treasury and Risk Management, Finance Director
- Credit committee NOT typically on ALCO — credit decisions better at Executive Credit Committee level

#### ALCO Agenda Items
- **Management reporting**: lending margin, interest income, variance from projection, customer business, future direction
- **Business planning**: future business direction; performance from risk-reward perspective; new business assessed for projected returns, risk exposure
- **Hedging policy**: FRAs much less commonly used than might be expected; many ALM desks meet hedging needs with plain vanilla IRS + exchange-traded futures; exposures left unhedged when cost prohibitive
- Hedging considerations: rate expectations, balance between risk control and revenue maximisation, level of acceptable risk

#### ALCO Reporting
- Main report: overall ALM book composition; also: business line returns, product breakdown, gap, DV01/VaR, credit risk
- **ALM Smile** visible in dynamic gap chart; ALCO can direct hedging if income at risk
- Gap breakdown by product type (interbank, corporate lending, leasing) and by rate type (fixed vs. floating)
- **Income reports**: breakdown by business line; spread in bps (average per time bucket)
- ALCO prefers **low volumes + high margin** (consumes less capital); some high-volume business (interbank MM) operates at low margin
- ALCO sets level of acceptable risk-return trade-off and bank strategy; scenario planning = expected performance under various conditions
- ALCO also responsible for bank's **FTP policy**: ensuring business lines are correctly charged for their funding; any change in FTP methodology = ALCO approval required
- ALCO must act PROACTIVELY: direct that book shape be changed before losses crystallise (FSA guideline); minutes must summarise EXTENT of debate, not just action points
- **ALCO is the VENUE for bank policy** — hedging policy, liquidity policy, capital policy all originate here; execution by Treasury; monitoring by CRO's office

### Chapter 10 (Summary): ALCO Terms of Reference and Group Structure

#### ALCO Governance
- Must be set up with governance structure and authority befitting its importance
- Meets minimum **once every 4 weeks** (same time/day embeds it in risk culture)
- Extraordinary meeting called at short notice during market stress or firm-specific urgency
- **Chaired by CFO** (not CEO; not Head of Treasury if Treasury is profit centre — conflict of interest)
- All business line heads and CRO must be represented
- Documents circulated **at least one week before** meeting for member review

#### ALCO Operating Agenda (ToR items, Figure 10.1)
- Review gap limits, actual gaps, sensitivities, recommendations to amend limits
- Review liquidity ratio, regulatory/internal limit adherence, forecast values
- Review deposit and funding trends: concentrations, programs, promotions, forecasts
- Review exceptions/excesses to policies/limits (reported by Head of Financial Planning and Control)
- Review current capital allocation and profit contribution by business line; regulatory capital adequacy forecasts
- Review market environment and impact on IRR and trading activities
- Review and approve authorised instruments and permissible hedging/position-taking strategies
- Review FTP arrangements and whether changes required
- Set and review stress testing scenarios (in liaison with CRO's office)
- Act as centre of excellence for all ALM-related policy and governance issues

#### ALCO Monthly Pack (Traffic Light System)
- Green = in order; Amber = advance warning, keep under watch; Red = urgent, immediate action
- Red should **rarely** appear if ALCO process functioning effectively
- Board-level summary: **2-slide PowerPoint** (ALM, Liquidity, Capital) given board time constraints

#### Group ALCO (GALCO) Structure
- Group Treasury → Regional ALCOs (if needed) → Local ALCOs → Local Treasuries
- Capital approval limits: GALCO = any; Group FD = up to $100m; Group Treasury Head = up to $50m; Local ALCO = up to $10m; Local Treasury Head = up to $1m
- Overseas **branch**: no local ALCO needed; Head of Treasury reports direct to Group Treasury
- Overseas **subsidiary/partnership**: local ALCO required with own ToR; operates within Group framework
- **BSMCO** (Balance Sheet Management Committee): technical sub-committee; reviews more granular items; feeds up to ALCO; Chair = Deputy Head of Treasury
- Group Treasury responsibilities: managing capital base; monitoring aggregate balance sheet risk; compiling liquidity risk appetite and funding policy; managing Group's non-traded interest-rate risk; setting FX exposure policy

#### UK FSA Good Practice Guidelines (January 2011)
- ALCO = most important senior management and risk committee in a bank
- Must be forward-looking; proactive decisions (not just noting risks)
- Strong degree of challenge; minutes must summarise extent of debate — NOT just action points
- Pack must include: market/economic outlook, liquidity/funding metrics by currency with forecasts, stress test results, IRR analysis (duration, VaR, NII/NIM sensitivity, basis risk)
- Committee Membership: CEO or Deputy CEO + CFO chair; all business group heads; CRO; Head of Research/Economics; Head of Internal Audit
- **Common failures identified by FSA**: ALCO not meeting frequently enough; chair not challenging; pack too backward-looking; stress tests not severe enough; no escalation from amber to red before crisis; FTP not reviewed regularly; liquidity metrics not broken down by currency
- ALCO charter must be reviewed and approved by Board annually; ALCO minutes reviewed by Board Risk Committee
- **Conflict of interest**: if Head of Treasury is ALCO chair AND Treasury is a profit centre → Head of Treasury's profit targets may conflict with conservative risk management; better: CFO chairs, Head of Treasury is member

#### Key Cross-Chapter Themes (Summary)
- **Liquidity first, always**: Five principles reduce to one: ensure liquidity before growth. Every chapter reinforces this.
- **Conservatism as competitive advantage**: Banks that maintained conservative principles through 2003–07 boom were best-positioned in 2008–09; "star bankers" in bull markets are not necessarily skilled bankers
- **ALCO as the nerve centre**: Regulatory capital (Ch 2), credit risk (Ch 3), securitisation (Ch 4), yield curve (Ch 5), ALM (Ch 6–8) all feed back to ALCO (Ch 9–10); ALCO synthesises all risk dimensions into coherent balance sheet management
- **Maturity transformation is the business**: The Gap is fundamental; hedging it completely = zero profit; the skill = managing Gap within risk appetite while earning the spread
- **FTP as discipline**: Internal pricing of funds to business lines ensures each business covers its true cost of funding (including term premium and liquidity premium), preventing artificial profit from mismatched books
- **Basel evolution**: Basel I (1988) → crude but universal; Basel II (2004) → more risk-sensitive but gamed via securitisation; Basel III (2010) → addresses liquidity (LCR/NSFR), leverage, and capital quality simultaneously
- **Model limitations**: Correlation approaches unity in bear markets; VaR fails in tails; gap analysis assumes parallel shifts; all models are approximations — senior banker judgment must override model output in extremis
