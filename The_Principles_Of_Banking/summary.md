# The Principles of Banking — Summary
## Moorad Choudhry (2012, Wiley Finance, 1,040 pages)

### What This Book Is
A comprehensive practitioner's guide to bank management written in the aftermath of the 2007-2009 financial crisis. Choudhry — Treasurer at RBS's Corporate Banking Division and a career banker since 1989 — argues that the crisis was fundamentally a failure to observe basic, time-tested principles of banking. The book covers everything from balance sheet mechanics to yield curve mathematics to corporate governance, but its core message is simple: **conservatism in banking is not optional, it is existential.**

### The Five Principles
1. Secure your funding base for ALL contingencies BEFORE lending beyond your deposit base
2. Fund the majority of lending from core, stable customer deposits
3. Lend prudently with sound judgement — not following the herd
4. Maintain capital sufficient to absorb all losses including unexpected ones
5. See principle 1 — liquidity always comes first

### Key Themes

**Liquidity Is the Water of Life**
Choudhry's central metaphor (borrowed from Dune) runs throughout: liquidity is to banks what water is to life on Arrakis. The 2007-2008 crisis was as much a liquidity crisis as a capital crisis. Banks that abandoned conservative liquidity management — holding genuine liquid asset buffers, maintaining prudent loan-to-deposit ratios, funding long-term assets with long-term liabilities — were the ones that failed. Northern Rock, HBOS, and Lehman all violated these basic principles.

**The Gap Is the Business**
Maturity transformation — borrowing short and lending long — IS banking. Understanding and managing "the gap" between asset and liability maturities is the cornerstone of risk management. Every chapter connects back to this fundamental tension.

**ALM and ALCO Are Everything**
The ALCO (Asset-Liability Committee) is described as more important than the Board itself. "It is possible to have an incompetent Board and still survive a market crisis; it is not possible for a bank to have an incompetent ALCO and survive the same crisis." Four chapters are devoted to ALM mechanics, and the ALCO governance framework is laid out in extraordinary detail.

**FTP Was a Root Cause**
Internal funds transfer pricing (FTP) — how Treasury charges business lines for funding — was a central driver of the crisis. UBS's CDO desk funded itself at artificially low internal rates, generating illusory "super profits." KBC charged a uniform Libor + 12.5 bps regardless of tenor or risk. Proper FTP disciplines business lines to price risk correctly and prevents the hidden leverage that destroyed banks.

**Capital Alone Is Insufficient**
Lehman Brothers was capitalised at 11% Tier 1 at the time of its collapse — a level acceptable to regulators. This single fact demolishes the argument that higher capital requirements alone prevent bank failure. Liquidity management is equally critical, and the two must be integrated at the strategic level.

**Governance Failed**
If one must identify a single cause of the crisis, it was corporate governance failure. Northern Rock's chairman was a zoologist with no banking experience. HBOS's CEO came from retail consulting. KBC's board was unaware of a USD 5 billion ABS structure on their books. Lehman's risk management head was asked to leave the room during high-risk strategy discussions. The pattern is universal across failed institutions.

### Structure
- **Part I** (Ch 1-4): Banking primer — balance sheets, regulatory capital (Basel I/II/III), credit risk, securitisation
- **Part II** (Ch 5-11): The technical core — yield curve theory and fitting (massive Ch 5 with 7 appendices), four chapters on ALM mechanics, ALCO governance, risk reporting and stress testing
- **Part III** (Ch 12-15): Liquidity risk management — principles, metrics (LCR, NSFR, LRF), reporting, FTP policy
- **Part IV** (Ch 16-18): Strategy and governance — sustainable bank business model, capital/funding management, corporate governance failures and reform
- **Part V** (Ch 19): Supplementary materials (Excel models, policy templates)

### Distinctive Contributions
- Ten Principles of Bank Liquidity Risk Management (adapted from HSBC model)
- Detailed FTP framework with worked examples and critique of pre-crisis failures
- Complete ALCO governance template with Terms of Reference, reporting packs, and traffic light system
- Comprehensive stress testing framework with actual sample outputs
- Corporate governance reform proposals including debt holder board representation and "Alpha committee" for crisis response
- Yield curve fitting methods (Nelson-Siegel, Svensson, cubic splines) with full mathematical treatment

### Who Should Read This
Senior bank management, treasury professionals, risk managers, regulators, and anyone who wants to understand what went wrong in 2007-2009 and what "good banking" actually looks like. As Plenderleith writes in the foreword: "If reading it keeps bankers awake at night, this admirable book will have served its purpose."
