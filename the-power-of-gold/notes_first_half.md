## Prologue: The Supreme Possession

- **John Ruskin** posed the paradox: when a man drowned with gold strapped to his waist, "Had he the gold? Or had the gold him?"
- Gold has intoxicated, obsessed, haunted, humbled, and exalted humanity throughout history
- It has motivated entire societies, torn economies apart, determined fates of kings, inspired great art, and provoked atrocities

> "Oh, most excellent gold! Who has gold has a treasure [that] even helps souls to paradise." — **Christopher Columbus**

- Historical figures consumed by gold: **Perseus** (Golden Fleece), **Croesus** (fingering coins), **Crassus** (murdered by molten gold), **Basil Bulgaroctonus** (200,000+ pounds of gold), **Pizarro** (slain by own men), **Sutter** (destroyed by his own gold rush discovery), **de Gaulle** (deluded that gold would make France's economy superior)

### Gold's Unique Properties

- Symbol AU from "aurora" (shining dawn); chemically inert, radiance lasts forever
- One ounce can be drawn into wire 50 miles long or beaten into sheet covering 100 square feet
- Hammered to five-millionths of an inch for gilding
- **King Ptolemy II of Egypt** (285-246 BC): led processions with gilded phallus 180 feet tall
- **Stanley Jevons** (1875): £20 million in daily London transactions would weigh 157 tons in gold coin, requiring 80 horses
- Total supply: ~125,000 tons (fits aboard one oil tanker), worth ~$1 trillion
- U.S. steel industry produces that volume in hours — yet we call gold "precious"
- Gold in Egyptian tombs (4,500 years old) remains in good condition

> "They wonder much to hear that gold, which in itself is so useless a thing, should be everywhere so much esteemed." — **Sir Thomas More**, *Utopia*

### Biblical Gold

- Over 400 biblical mentions; **Abraham** "rich in cattle, and in silver, and in gold"
- Golden Calf incident: Jews had ample gold as slaves but never thought to bribe freedom — gold not yet perceived as money
- God's tabernacle specifications (Exodus 25-28): 80 paragraphs demanding pure gold overlay
- **Job**: "If I have made gold my hope... This also were an iniquity to be punished by the judge"

### Ancient Egypt and Nubia

- Only pharaohs could possess gold; they adorned themselves like their gods
- **Hatshepsut** (c. 1482-1458 BC): "First great lady of the world" (**James Henry Breasted**)
- Held ~80 titles including Golden Horus; fond of gilding face with gold/silver dust
- Planned 100-foot gold pillars for **Amon Re** at Karnak; chancellor convinced her to use granite, covering only peaks with gold
- Her declaration: "Their height pierces to heaven... You who after long years shall see these monuments will say, 'We do not know how they can have made whole mountains of gold.'"
- "Nub" = Egyptian word for gold; **Nubia** supplied gold to Western world into 16th century
- Nubian mine output "far exceeded the quantity drawn from all the mines of the then known world"

### Mining Horrors

- **Diodorus** (Greek visitor during Caesar's Rome) described Egyptian mines in detail
- Shafts fetid, constantly depleted by tiny candles providing barely sufficient light
- Intense heat, earth frequently collapsed, subterranean water constant hazard
- Fires cracked quartz, releasing arsenic fumes causing excruciating deaths
- Slaves worked on back or side, literally worked to death or crushed by falling rocks
- Guards were multilingual mercenaries — prevented slaves from conspiring for escape
- Kings enslaved not just criminals but "kindred and relations" — men, women, children; no housing or care provided
- Roman **hydraulicking** in Spain: water jets up to 30,000 gallons/minute from tanks elevated 400-800 feet above site
- Wonderfully efficient but environmentally catastrophic — washed away mountains, destroyed farmland, silted rivers
- California Gold Rush (1852) faithfully reproduced Roman techniques until angry citizens had it outlawed (1884)
- Modern South African mines: 12,000 feet deep, 130°F; one ounce requires 38 man-hours, 1,400 gallons water, chemicals including cyanide; labor force exceeds 400,000 men (~90% black)

> "Get gold, humanely if possible, but at all hazards — get gold." — **King Ferdinand of Spain** (1511)

## Chapter 1: Get Gold at All Hazards

- **South Africa**: annual ~500 tons requires raising 70 million tons of earth (more material than Cheops pyramid)
- **Will Rogers**: "There is a big difference between prospecting for gold and prospecting for spinach"
- Gold doesn't mix with other metals; thin veins in quartz/granite; high density separates it in streams
- Gold more plentiful in ancient times than since Roman era
- Egyptian mining produced only ~1 ton annually
- Before coinage, gold owned by monarchs/priests for ceremony and advertising power

## Chapter 2: Midas's Wish and the Creatures of Pure Chance

### Gold's Transition from Adornment to Money

- Route from golden calf to money was probably inevitable
- Crowns of gold weigh heavy, but no monarch ever chose zinc or plastic
- Everlasting radiance plus scarcity suggested exceptional value
- Gold played most important monetary role in cultures holding business in highest repute
- Tension between gold as adornment and gold as money developed early, continues to present

### Jason and the Golden Fleece

- **Phryxus** escaped stepmother on winged ram with golden fleece, gift from **Hermes**
- Sister **Helle** fell into sea (landing spot named **Hellespont**)
- Delivered Golden Fleece to **Colchis** on Black Sea; King **Aeetes** nailed it to tree guarded by dragon
- **Jason** sent by King **Pelias** to fetch it (expecting him to perish)
- Won fleece with help of **Medea** (struck by Eros's dart); she sang dragon to sleep
- Jason betrayed Medea for princess of **Corinth**; Medea's revenge: poisoned gown of cloth of gold killed bride; Medea killed own sons, Jason threw himself on sword
- Moral: gold promised power, power gained a princess, in the end gold snuffed out both bride and future

### Money vs. Store of Wealth

- Value alone doesn't qualify something as money — most effective money develops from **useless objects** (paper, computer blips)
- Early Britain: cattle and slaves served as money; hoarding cattle continues in Africa
- Post-WWII Germany: cigarettes as currency — eventually went up in smoke
- **Electronic blips** are best modern money — no other use, infinitely transferable/divisible
- **Yap Island**: stone wheels (**fei**) as money, from limestone quarries on **Babelhuap** (400 miles away)
- Wealthiest family owned enormous fei on ocean bottom no one had ever seen — ancestor lost it in storm; community never doubted testimony; "purchasing power remains valid as if it were leaning against the owner's house"
- Germans (1898) marked valuable fei with black crosses as "fine" — natives immediately repaired highways; crosses erased = "happy failus resumed possession, rolled in wealth"
- Author: "In other times and places, we call this taxation and government spending"

### Federal Reserve Gold Vaults

- Author's 1940 experience at **Federal Reserve Bank of New York** research department
- Gold five stories underground; ponderous airtight cylindrical door; scale so sensitive a pea would rock it
- 100,000+ gold bars stacked to ceiling; each brick 30 lbs, 400 troy ounces, worth $14,000
- **Earmarking**: England loses gold to France = guard trundles dolly between closets, changes mark
- "Movements of just a few feet often reflected major changes in wealth between countries"
- If gold sunk into Hudson River instead of bookkeeping transfer, economic consequences identical
- **Fei parallel**: transfers of assets that never moved

### Gold Measurement and Coinage

- **Carat** from Greek keration (carob seed, each pod weighs 1/5 gram); 24-carat = 100% pure
- **Grain**: barley/wheat grains from middle of ear have standard weight regardless of ear size
- **Troy ounce** from French town of **Troyes**: 480 grains, 12 troy ounces = 1 pound
- Egyptians casting gold bars by 4000 BC stamped with pharaoh **Menes**' name
- Egyptians set silver = 10% of gold; beginning of gold-silver "complex, incestuous, occasionally violent cohabitation"
- **Assyrians/Babylonians**: stamped lions on heavy bars (~30 lbs), ducks on smaller bars
- **Mesopotamians** broke gold into **talents, minas, shekels** — shekel survived to this day in Israel
- Until ~600 BC people still insisted on weighing each piece rather than accepting stamped values

### The Lydians and Coinage

> "When money was just pieces of metal of varying weights, it had no nationality... ancient forebears functioned with one form of money acceptable everywhere. Modern experts dream of supranational currency but have no idea how to implement it."

### The Legend of King Midas

- **Phrygia** (eastern Asia Minor): capital **Gordium** on banks of **Pactolus River**
- First **King Midas** was poor but generous; **Bacchus** granted him any wish
- Everything touched turned to gold — including food and beloved daughter
- Bathed in Pactolus to reverse wish; transferred golden touch to river
- Pactolus became rich gold source for Phrygians and neighboring Lydians
- Midas ended with best of both worlds: gold in river made him rich, able to eat and touch loved ones
- **Cimmerians** later overthrew Midas; he committed suicide by poison
- 300 years later, **Alexander** cut the Gordian knot in Midas's temple (334 BC)

### Herodotus and Lydia

- **Herodotus's _Histories_**: first extended prose narrative in Western civilization
- **Lydia** occupied center of Asia Minor; capital **Sardis** sat on alluvial gold, ~200 miles inland from Aegean
- **Electrum** ("white gold"): ~2/3 gold, 1/3 silver; from Greek "he who shines"; root of word "electric"
- Lydians honored **Cybele** (goddess of mountains, guardian of ores/metals) with wild orgiastic dances
- Dynasty began when bodyguard **Gyges** killed King **Candaules** (forced to choose: kill king or die after queen caught him observing her undress)
- Oracle at **Delphi** declared for Gyges (influenced by gold/silver gifts weighing ~1,800 lbs); predicted dynasty would perish in fifth generation
- Three descendants (**Ardys, Sadyattes, Alyattes**) ruled 118 years
- **Mermnadae** refrained from destroying conquered peoples' homes — wanted monetary tribute, not revenge

### Croesus

- **Croesus** (568 BC), great-great grandson of Gyges — the fateful fifth generation
- Visit by **Solon** (just written code of laws for Athens, taking ten years off sightseeing)
- Croesus impatient to show immense wealth; asked who was most fortunate
- Solon named war heroes and prizewinning athletes instead

> "When you ask me about human affairs, you ask someone who knows how jealous and provocative god is.... Humans are the creatures of pure chance." — **Solon**

### Lydian Monetary Innovation

- Lydians "first people to mint and use gold coins and silver coins, and first retail tradesmen" — **Herodotus**
- **Sardis** sat astride great east-west highway linking Aegean to Euphrates (~1,100 miles)
- Money created demand for goldsmiths, money changers, ultimately bankers
- **Touchstone**: black jasper-like stone; gold rubbed against it, compared with 24 needles of varying purity
- Early money: bean-shaped electrum lumps called **dumps** — too heavy, no uniformity
- **Gyges** established **state monopoly over money issuance** — principle persisted throughout history
- US Constitution Article I, Section 8: "Congress shall have power to coin money"
- **Ardys** stamped ingots with marks guaranteeing weight; **lion's head** logo
- 1951 archaeology at **Ephesus**: 3,000+ items under **temple of Artemis** confirmed first true coins ~635 BC

### Croesus's Reforms

- Called in all electrum coins, melted, minted pure gold and silver — **first imperial currency in history**
- 1964 archaeologists found fire-resistant pots where Croesus's men extracted impurities using lead and salt — method not found elsewhere
- Coins stamped with **lion and bull** (arms of Sardis); opposite side incused with punch marks
- **Stater** subdivided into thirds, sixths, twelfths (carried forward into troy ounce and English shilling)
- Launched **bimetallic system** (gold-to-silver 10:1) prevailing in most countries for subsequent history
- Bimetallic systems seldom stable — changing supplies caused relative values to fluctuate
- **Achievement equivalent to establishment of euro** — increased trade, mobility, economic growth

### Croesus and the Persians

- Consulted oracle about attacking **Cyrus**; tested oracles by asking what he was doing on specific day: boiling turtle and lamb together in bronze pot
- **Delphic oracle** guessed correctly; Croesus lavished gifts: 117 ingots of pure gold (150 lbs each), golden lion (600 lbs), golden vat (522 lbs, holding 5,000 gallons)
- Received "rights of first consultation without fee, front-row seats at Pythian games"
- Oracle predicted he would "destroy a great empire"
- **Cyrus** put horsemen on camels — horses afraid of camels' sight and smell; Lydian cavalry routed
- 14-day siege; destroyed empire was Croesus's own
- Croesus on funeral pyre called out **Solon's** name three times; Cyrus moved by the story, ordered fire extinguished
- Croesus's final quip watching looting: "It's not my city or my wealth they are looting... What they are looting belongs to you."

### Croesus's Legacy

- **J. Kenneth Galbraith**: "Men possessed of money have infallibly imagined that the awe money inspires were really owing to their own wisdom"
- Croesus may be the exception: "demonstrated you don't have to be bad just because you are rich"
- Greeks, Persians, Romans all walked in footsteps of Lydians — most with far heavier tread
- **Gold was probably necessary even if not sufficient condition** for Lydian ascendancy
- Lydians achieved empire because they were rich — "as rich as Croesus"

## Chapter 3: Darius's Bathtub and the Cackling of the Geese

### Gold from Adornment to Money

- Coinage **democratized gold** — no longer royal prerogative; common citizens could touch, hoard, buy with it
- **Feedback loop**: demand for gold as money became infinite (unlike limited demand for adornment); provoked wild deeds in search of new sources
- **Phidias** clothed Athena statue in gold cloak — Athens's official treasure, visible to all (unlike Fort Knox)
- **Darius** (521-485 BC) stamped own likeness on coins called **darics**; found from Baltic to Africa
- **First government to collect taxes in coin rather than in kind** — shaped acceptable money throughout history
- **Alexander** found Darius's golden chariot, throne, and bathtub: "So this is what it meant to be a king"

### Philip II and Alexander

- **Philip II** (359 BC) opened gold/silver sources in Macedonia/Thrace (modern Bulgaria)
- Agricultural revolution: irrigation, canals, flood control turned plains into breadbasket; enhanced military power
- Built slave economy; hired **Aristotle** to tutor Alexander (ages 13-16)
- Set gold coin value at 10:1 ratio to silver; decorated coins with chariot (Olympic victory) and Zeus head
- After Philip's assassination (336 BC), Alexander kept all mints busy plus conquered mints
- Established 70+ towns from Egypt to India; needed common currency for vision of prosperous trade
- **Hercules** on coins bore striking resemblance to Alexander himself
- System prevailed 150+ years until **Quinctius Flaminius** defeated Philip V (197 BC) — first living person on Roman coins

### Roman Monetary Development

- **390 BC**: geese cackling alerted Romans to Gaul attack; shrine to **Moneta** = derivation of "money" and "mint"
- Romans gave us **pound-libra** (£), **denarius** (d for penny), **solidus** (shilling)
- English system: pound = 20 shillings, shilling = 12 pence — lasted from Norman times to 1980s

### Roman Challenges

- Growing empire + military spending + bread and circuses (**alimenta**) + gold draining to India for spices
- **Gibbon**: "The golden eagle which glittered in front of the legion, was the object of their fondest devotion"
- **Emperor Domitian** (81-96 AD) raised legionary pay to 12 pieces of gold (~$2500 equivalent)
- **Caesar** conquered Gaul: imported 100,000+ slaves for Italian mines
- **Julius Caesar** allied with **Crassus** (fire brigade extortion racket — bought burning buildings at fraction of value) and **Pompey**
- **Battle of Carrhae** (53 BC): Parthians attacked with 10,000 horse archers + 1,000 camels; only 10,000 of 40,000 Romans escaped
- Parthians executed **Crassus** by pouring molten gold down his throat — disdain for money-mad Rome

### Three Solutions to Metal Shortage

1. Live with insufficient supply (deflation, defaults — Great Depression example)
2. Import gold by plunder or trade
3. **Debasement**: reduce metal content, maintain face value
- **Nature sets ceiling on gold/silver supply** — cannot create metal from nothing (alchemists learned repeatedly)

### Debasement

- **Dionysius of Syracuse** (405-367 BC): ordered all coins brought under penalty of death, restamped one-drachma as two drachmas
- **Nero** first emperor to debase; by **Gallienus** (AD 260): silver content cut to mere 4%
- Prices rose 0.4%/year over 250 years, then **9%/year** for 34 years; by AD 304 prices **20x higher**
- Copper coins so thin imprint could only go on one side
- **Diocletian** spent 20 years trying reform under bewildering regulations
- AD 296: ordered alchemy books destroyed, "apprehensive lest opulence of Egyptians should inspire them to rebel"
- Retired voluntarily to Dalmatian palace (305)

### Constantine's Bezant

- **Constantine** (306-337) issued **gold solidus (bezant)**: 4.55 grams, **98% pure** — heavier than any existing gold coin
- **Remained in production ~700 years** with unchanging weight/purity — Guinness record
- Gold sources: eastern tributes, new taxes payable only in gold/silver
- Richest source: **conversion to Christianity** (313 AD) and stripping **pagan temples** throughout empire
- Vision of shining cross: "In hoc signo vinces" (In this sign, I shall conquer)
- Wore bejeweled golden crown at all times
- ~1200 years later: **Henry VIII** copied by pillaging Catholic churches after divorce and Protestant conversion

## Chapter 4: Byzantine Empire: Gold as Power

- Eastern Roman Empire ruled Constantinople 1,000+ years — "decadent, corrupt, conspiratorial, cruel bunch"
- **Irene** (780): had son Constantine VI blinded to secure power; reigned as emperor (not empress)
- **Basil II** ("Slayer of the Bulgars"): blinded entire Bulgarian army except every 100th man (as guides); Bulgarian tsar died of heart attack from shock

> "The bezant outstripped the dollar in stability and intrinsic value. Indeed, its record has never been equaled." — **Lopez**

### Byzantine Opulence

- **Justinian** used inherited 300,000 pounds of gold to build Saint Sophia, attempting to surpass Solomon
- **Emperor Theophilus**: gold tree shading gold throne, golden birds/lions/griffins that roared when visitors arrived
- Goldsmiths = principal artists during Dark Ages; created mosaics at Saint Mark's, San Vitale, Monreale
- **Saint Eloi** (641-660): patron saint of goldsmiths, monk and mintmaster from Gaul who learned skills in Constantinople
- **Basil II**: 200,000+ pounds hoarded in subterranean chambers; **Anastasius**: 300,000 pounds; **Theodora**: 100,000 pounds

### Byzantine Gold Diplomacy

- Paid gold tribute to keep enemies at bay — "outsourcing security"
- **Lombards** invaded Italy (568-569), 100,000 strong under King **Alboin**
- **Alboin** killed by wife **Rosamund** after trying to make her drink from skull of her murdered father
- Byzantines bribed Lombard factions to fight each other, recruited **Franks** as allies
- 754-756: Frankish invasion "greased by 50,000 gold solidi"; Franks collected 12,000 solidi annual tribute
- **Charlemagne** (773-774) conquered Lombardy, crowned emperor (800)

### Sources and Loss of Nubia

- No domestic gold; Pactolus exhausted; main source: old Nubian mines in southern Egypt/Sudan
- **Arabian Muslims** conquered territories in 7th century, cut off Nubia in one swift move

> "The empire that amazed the world by the profusion of its riches was constantly threatened with exhaustion of its stock of precious metals." — **Lopez**

- Only means of sustaining golden passions: trade, military campaigns, taxation, raw plunder

### Byzantine Trade

- Triangular pattern: sold luxury textiles to Europe (earning gold), used to offset chronic import surplus with Muslims
- Muslims maintained positive trade balance with Europe (luxuries for timber, iron, slaves)
- Imposed severe restrictions on gold export; failed to develop systematic plan for industry or trade tracking

### The Bezant's Supremacy and Decline

> "The gold money of the empire 'is accepted everywhere from end to end of the earth. It is admired by all men and in all kingdoms, because no kingdom has a currency that can be compared to it.'"

- **Justinian II** (685-711) angered Arabs by striking coins with own portrait; Muslims responded by replacing portraits with Koran quotations
- Age of **Iconoclasm**: emperors stripped golden icons from churches, filling coffers
- Became official policy (730) under **Leo III**; 100+ years of savage persecution
- After Crusaders took Constantinople (1204), bezant lost purity to debasement
- By mid-14th century, Byzantines paying taxes in Venetian ducats
- **Constantine IX** (1453, fall to Turks): only emperor who never issued coins — "empire disappeared when it had spent its last sou"

### Lopez's Critical Question

- "Were advantages of stable currency worth the sacrifices made to keep it stable?"
- Financial rectitude never a sure road to prosperity
- Britain's 1925 gold return "smothered economy" for six years; leaving EMS (1992) immediately improved exports and growth
- Merchants have greater flexibility; kings associate purity of coinage with own dignity
- **Henry VIII**: one of great debasers yet highly successful king

## Chapter 5: Gold, Salt, and the Blessed Town

### Arab Gold Culture

- Despite Mohammed's warning ("He who drinks from gold drinks the fire of Hell"), caliphs had immense appetite for gold displays
- **Harun al-Rashid's** wedding: gold balls thrown for guests, 5,000 gold pieces for a poet, 400,000 pieces for a robe of honor
- Golden trees and singing golden birds in Baghdad inspired Theophilus's throne in Constantinople
- One king's sister left 2.7 million dinars and 12,000 gold-thread robes
- Arabs ravaged enemies, outsmarted competitors at trade, reopened Egyptian/Nubian mines
- Swept from Persia to Spain, halted by **Charles Martel** at Poitiers (732)
- Dominated trade from Africa to Russia to India/China; even traveled through Russian river highways to Scandinavia
- **Dinar** issued by Caliph **Abd el-Melik** at Damascus: 97% pure, gradually displaced bezant internationally
- First dinars imitated Byzantine coins for immediate acceptance

### West African Gold

- Arabs contacted gold source that had fed Carthage 1,000+ years earlier; enjoyed virtual buyers' monopoly for centuries
- Gold area: ~600 square miles below Sahara, bordered by Ivory Coast to Nigeria (also called **Gold Coast**)
- Romans/Byzantines occupied North African coast but ignored riches to south; Arabs "meant business"
- Set up trading posts: **Tunis** (sea), **Fez** and **Marrakesh** (inland), ultimately in heart of Sahara
- **Sijilmesa**: "gateway to the Sahara... whither traders take goods of no value and return with camels laden with coarse gold"
- **Timbuktu**: major commercial center on Niger River, 1,000+ miles south of Gibraltar

### The Camel Revolution

- Camel appeared in Sahara ~AD 100, probably introduced by Roman legions
- Carries 2-3x bullock's load, covers 2x daily mileage; equivalent of automobile/airplane introduction
- Paradox: the wheel had to be "disinvented" to link Sudan and Mediterranean

> "[The camel] marked the dawn of a new era... gave man freedom of movement he had never known before." — **Bovill**

### Dumb Barter and Salt Trade

- **Herodotus** described Carthaginian silent trade: merchandise arranged on shore, smoke raised
- Natives come, leave gold beside goods, withdraw; Carthaginians inspect; process repeats until both sides satisfied — never face-to-face, never exchange a word
- Persisted for centuries; possibly protection against enslavement
- Around 750: Arabs launched military expedition from Morocco to conquer gold territories — failed completely, suffered serious casualties, never discovered where gold came from
- Thereafter obtained gold by trade instead of conquest
- **Salt** was product most desperately in demand; humans can never do without it
- Gold miners lived in one of few spots where nearest salt was ~1,000 miles away in land where nobody traveled faster than 10 miles/day
- Salt miners (many slaves) often blinded by desert winds, lived 20-day journey from nearest towns, sometimes starved from delayed food deliveries
- In many transactions, **ounce of gold exchanged for ounce of salt**
- Salt transported south by camel caravans; where pasturage too scarce for camels, great slabs broken into small pieces and placed on men's heads

## Chapter 6: The Legacy of Eoba, Babba, and Udd

### The Question of Value

- For Africans: **salt standard** governed well-being while gold satisfied only "pride and pleasure"
- 15th-century Europeans called gold-bearing areas **Guinea**; **guinea coin** (1662) minted from West African gold
- **Jenne**: remarkable 13th-century trading post, rare navigable waterways, commercial and intellectual hub
- **Es-Sadi**: called Jenne "a blessed town"
- **Saint Thomas Aquinas**: money "subordinated to something else" yet contains all material goods "by its power" — "some likeness to beatitude"

### Medieval Monetary Challenges

- When all money is hard, supplies determined by nature, not human control
- Mines exhaust, plunder shifts supplies, trade imbalances drain gold across borders
- People **hoard** as hedge against chaos; changing gold/silver ratios caused one metal to disappear from circulation
- Despite obstacles, medieval monarchs invented monetary systems that evolved into today's financial world
- Europe had minimal indigenous gold but became most vital economic force
- Focus shifted to **which nation would accumulate and employ available gold**

### Europe's Gold Scarcity and Religious Art

- First 1,000 years post-Rome: gold's role much less important than in Byzantium or Muslim domains
- Europeans exported **human slaves** to Muslims (one of few goods easterners wanted)
- Religious objects sometimes melted for coinage; **Fulbert of Chartres** justified selling sacred vessels
- **Romanesque/Gothic churches** lacked Byzantine gold mosaics; color from stained glass and small goldsmith work
- **Abbe Suger** (1137): defended gold: "If ancient law ordained cups of gold for blood of rams, how much rather for blood of our Lord?"

### Chrysography

- Gold calligraphy in illuminated manuscripts; **Charlemagne** launched European tradition via **Alcuin of York**
- **Godescalc Gospels** (783) and **Saint-Methard Gospel Books**: latter written entirely in gold calligraphy on purple ground
- Modern **cursive writing** descended from Alcuin's golden script; one initial letter took more than a full day

### Offa's Monetary Innovation

- **Offa** (757-796), king of **Mercia**: found three moneyers in Kent — **Eoba, Babba, and Udd**
- Inaugurated England's longstanding coinage leadership using Cornish silver
- Silver **penny** became core of English monetary system for 500+ years
- Smaller denominations created by cutting pennies into halves/quarters; **shilling** = "a piece cut off"
- Offa added 18 additional moneyers; production reached millions of pennies
- By year 1000: England's coinage most advanced in Europe, 70+ local mints
- **William the Conqueror** (1066) rejected debasement for English money

### Richard I's Ransom

- **150,000 marks** (£100,000) = **24 million pennies** — enough for 40,000+ skilled carpenters' annual pay
- Remarkable sacrifice for a king who spent little time in England
- **Francis I** (1529): counting/testing ransom coins took **four months**; Spaniards rejected 40,000 below standard
- 1662: 100 chests required to transfer 500,000 large-denomination French coins

### Charlemagne's Gold

- Modeled on Byzantine emperors; possibly **Irene** stimulated coinage interest; also friends with Offa
- Worked on golden desk with universe map; had goldsmith at each villa
- Buried **embalmed, sitting on gold and ivory throne** from Constantinople, with gold scepter, shield, sword
- Defeated **Avars** (796): needed **15 wagons, each pulled by 4 oxen** for captured gold
- Set pound = 20 shillings, 240 pence — system lasted centuries
- Evidence suggests recycling old gold for money reached a limit — couldn't breach without new supplies

### Quality Controls

- **Touchstone** unchanged from Lydian times; **Goldsmiths Company of London** (700+ years as official arbiter)
- **Trials of the Pyx**: public jury of "twelve discreet and lawful citizens" + twelve goldsmiths examined Mint coins
- Began under **Edward I** (1282); 700th anniversary (1982) attended by **Queen Elizabeth II**
- Coins compared against **special trial plate of the king's gold** stored in **Chapel of the Pyx, Westminster Abbey**
- **Henry I** (1124): punished nearly half of ~200 mint-masters by **chopping off right hands**
- **Seignorage**: kings profited from premium of authenticated coins over raw metal
- Monarchs had vested interest in maintaining purity; wholesale coin recalls every 3-5 years

## Chapter 7: The Great Chain Reaction

### Post-1000 Cultural Flourishing

- Year 1000 celebrated not just for millennium but end of ~500 years of barbarian raids
- Gold became preeminent tool of economic power; struggle to obtain sources would motivate great deeds and tragic treachery
- 1100-1200: France built **80+ cathedrals** (Notre Dame, Chartres), 500 abbeys, 10,000 parish churches
- Universities founded: Paris, Oxford, Bologna, Salerno
- **Paris** grew from small town to 50,000 by 1215
- Most popular role models: **knights** skilled in chivalry and **Saint Louis** for moral leadership

### The Crusades

- 1095 to ~1450: waves of Europeans (sometimes women and children) walked or sailed to Holy Land
- Inspired by faith, escape from tedium, and **vivid dreams of riches**
- 1300s: massacring infidels became less compelling as trade flourished; Arabs provided windmill, compass
- Imposed massive financial requirements; mercenary armies needed gold acceptable in occupied territories
- **Tancred** removed 20,000 mithgals of golden lamps from Jerusalem temple
- **Templars** bought Cyprus for 100,000 bezants; Saint Louis's army redeemed for 800,000 dinars
- Christians struck gold coins using captured dies with **Arabic inscriptions praising Mohammed** — continued 125 years
- **Pope Innocent IV** (1250) excommunicated those involved
- **Saint Louis** created **Agnus Dei** coin: "Christus vincit, Christus regnat, Christus imperat"

### Frederick II and the Augustalis

- **Frederick II** (Sicily, 1194-1250): built robust administrative state, welcomed intellectuals, taxed clergy, prohibited them from civil office
- Founded **University of Palermo** (1224) — first European university with royal charter
- Launched Sixth Crusade (1227), reclaimed Jerusalem temporarily, survived two papal excommunications
- Sicily had dual currency: **tari** (Arabic-rooted, stabilized at 16⅔ carats) and Frederick's **augustalis**
- **Augustalis** (after 1231): 20⅔ carats, 5.28 grams, classical eagle and laureate head — "a startling advertising medium" (**Lopez**)
- Dramatic advance eclipsed tari; strong demand across western Europe and Near East

### Genoa's Economic Power

- **Genoa** benefited from Pope **Innocent IV's** election (1241) and Frederick's defeat/death (1248-1250)
- By 1250: prosperous textile industry, massive shipyards, advanced credit operations
- Produced most ships for Saint Louis's crusade (1248); served as bankers to Saint Louis and Innocent IV
- African gold flowed via favorable North African trade balance; also remelted Islamic/Byzantine coins
- Within 10 years of gold coinage: persuaded Latin rulers of Constantinople to transfer Venetian trading privileges to Genoa

> "Good money adds its luster to the world's image of a nation."

### The Gold Coinage Chain Reaction

- **1252**: **Genoa** issued **genovino** (24-carat, ~3.5 grams); **Florence** issued **fiorino d'oro (florin)** months later
- **1284**: **Venetian ducat** — served as European standard until Napoleon (1797)
- Shakespeare's Shylock: "Thou stick'st a dagger in me! I shall never see my gold again! Fourscore ducats at a sitting!"
- Lopez: "touched off one of the greatest chain reactions in monetary history"
- All coins: 3.5 grams, 24-carat gold — standard persisted 600+ years
- First medieval gold coins minted in **commercial centers** (Genoa, Florence), not capitals

### England's Struggles

- **Henry III's** gold penny (1257): failed within 3 months — valued wrong against silver; poor couldn't use high denominations
- **Edward III's** florin (1343): rejected abroad (Florentines wanted Saint John the Baptist)
- **Noble** (1344): appropriate weight, celebrated Hundred Years' War victories (Crecy, Sluys) — became basic gold coin into 17th century
- 13th-century London far less cosmopolitan than Paris, Italian city-states; population ~50,000
- Mid-15th century: aliens still controlled 40% of English overseas trade
- Florentine bankers "achieved a financial despotism which London has never had"

## Chapter 8: The Disintegrating Age

### 14th-Century Catastrophe

- **Barbara Tuchman**: "a violent, tormented, bewildered, suffering and disintegrating age, a time of Satan triumphant"
- 20th century had peace periods; 14th century had no such relief

### Great Famine (1314-1316)

- Summer 1314: uncommonly cold/wet; crops rotted; 1315 worse — floods destroyed villages
- Food prices rose 5x; 1316 worst famine in European history — people ate cats, rats, insects, corpses
- Epidemics, violent crime, bloody self-flagellation widespread

### Black Death (1347-1349)

- Genoese defended Crimean colony **Kaffa** against Tartar siege; Tartars catapulted plague-infected corpses over walls
- Genoese fled to Italy; one ship's fleas/rats launched Black Death at Palermo
- Killed ~1/3 of population (20+ million); Europe didn't regain 1300 population until mid-16th century
- Victims: kings, queens, **Petrarch's Laura**, Sienese painters **Lorenzetti brothers**, **Andrea Pisano**
- 1348 earthquake from Naples to Venice, aftershocks to Germany/Greece

### Political Violence

- Pope imprisoned (1303), died "of humiliation"; successor murdered
- **Clement V** moved papacy to **Avignon** for 73 years; **Petrarch**: papal horses "dressed in gold, fed on gold"
- **Edward II** murdered with hot poker (1327); **Hundred Years' War** began (1338)
- **Jacquerie** (1358), **Watt Tyler's rebellion** (1381), **Henry Bolingbroke** deposed Richard II (1399)

### Economic Consequences

- Physical possessions remained as bodies disappeared; most Europeans far richer per capita
- **Albi**: fortunes >100 livres rose from 11% to 20% of population; those with <10 livres fell from 31% to 18%
- Labor scarcity + monetary plethora = sharp wage rises; consumption of meat, wine, spices expanded even among lowest classes
- 1375 Florentine chronicler indignant at "popolo minuto" refusing old trades, dressing above station

### Gold Shortages and Sumptuary Laws

- 1373-1411: English gold coin production averaged 9,500/year (1/10 of pre-plague output)
- **Edward III's Statute of Labourers** (1351): set maximum pay at preplague levels — ultimately led to Watt Tyler's rebellion
- **Sumptuary laws**: prohibited gold adornment by class (rustics limited to blanket cloth, servants forbidden gold, knights prohibited gold rings)
- "Gold, like liquor, satisfies too many needs to survive prohibition"

### Royal Ransoms

- **Jean II** (Jean le Bon): expert debaser (18 alterations in first year, 70+ in decade)

> "Money and currency are very strange things. They keep on going up and down and no one knows why."

- **Battle of Poitiers** (Sept 19, 1356): **Black Prince** with 8,000 troops overpowered Jean's 16,000-man army
- Jean fought valiantly, lost helmet, bled from two wounds; English archers' aim too good — deprived troops of more ransom prisoners
- Treaty of London (1359): 4 million gold ecus, yielding all western France; French rejected as intolerable
- Extraordinary hailstorm near Chartres (April 1360): "celestial warning" forced Edward to negotiate
- **Treaty of Bretigny** (1360): ransom scaled to **3 million gold crowns**; ~1/3 of France ceded
- 40 hostages including king's sons and brother; payments in semiannual installments
- Jean returned to captivity voluntarily (1363), died April 1364; 1 million crowns still owed
- 1.5 million crowns paid = full year's pay for ~6,000 laborers, 300,000 sheep, 1.6 million gallons of ale

### Margaret of Anjou's Ransom (1478)

- **Edward IV** abandoned invasion of France for 75,000 crowns + 50,000 crowns annual pension
- France ransomed **Margaret of Anjou** (Henry VI's widow) for 50,000 crowns in five installments
- **Christopher Challis**: if all payments made, total = 517,000 crowns (£103,400) vs. Mint's total gold output of £185,684 for 1474-1482
- **Shakespeare's Richard III**: "thou hateful withered hag" — Margaret "reputed a great beauty" but succumbed to disfiguring skin ailment after return to France

## Chapter 9: The Sacred Thirst

### 15th-Century Recovery

- Europe lost 6 million people (1350-1400) then gained 15 million (1400-1450), another 9 million (1450-1500)
- **Venice** controlled cities within 100-mile radius; territories remitted 1 million ducats annually, funded palaces including **Ca d'Oro** (exterior once covered with gold)
- **Ferdinand and Isabella** married 1469, united Spain, expelled Moors and Jews, launched dynasty spanning Europe and Americas
- **Portugal's** **King John I** (crowned 1385): transformed nation of 1 million into world power
- Son **Henry the Navigator**, ascetic who never married, drove exploration leading to Cape of Good Hope, America, Magellan's circumnavigation

### The Great Bullion Famine

- 1400: domestic European gold production ~4 tons (~1 million ducats)
- **Venetians** exported ~1 ton gold/year in ducats; **Kindleberger**: 5% of coinage/year disappeared
- Total European gold in 1500 could fit in cube only 2 meters per dimension
- **John Day**: Paris money-changers (1409) had no bullion at any price
- Tower Mint fell from £5,000/year (1460s) to virtually halted by 1490s
- Day estimates total bullion reserves shrank **50%** between ~1340 and 1460
- **Pepper** worth more than its weight in gold; German princes called bankers "peppermen"
- Paper currencies began circulating but even mid-18th century England: minted coin = 90% of money

### Deflation and Rising Gold Prices

- Commodity prices fell 20-50% (1400-1500); gold price rose from 23 to 40 shillings/ounce
- Gold's purchasing power **doubled**; one of few periods gold was spent instead of hoarded

### What Motivated Exploration?

- Rising gold prices + falling commodities = tempting rewards for finding new sources
- Also Renaissance spirit: bold experimentation, progress in navigation and mathematics
- Dreams of glory and Christian proselytizing played roles, but **burning hunger for gold remains the critical stimulant**

> "So your highness should resolve to make them Christians... without doubt there is in these lands a very great amount of gold." — **Columbus** to **Ferdinand and Isabella**

### Portugal's Exploration

- Narrow country, first in Europe for coastline relative to population; one of poorest
- **Prince Henry** captured **Ceuta** (1415): immensely wealthy Moorish port, primary for African gold caravans
- Sea route vs. camel: camel covers 35 miles/day; caravans 300-3,500 camels; ship covers 2x+ daily distance
- Manpower efficiency: man responsible for 0.7 tons cargo on camel vs. 3-14 tons on ship
- **Lateen sail**: triangular, positioned vertically, enabled tacking — without it Columbus couldn't have discovered America
- **Alvise da Cadamosto** (Venetian, 1454): left invaluable journal; first to report silent bartering; unforgettable encounter with **King Budomel** who requested help satisfying many wives
- Portuguese established **San Jorge de Mina** trading post; by early 1500s ~700 kg gold/year flowing to Portugal
- **Bartholomeu Dias** (1487): first European to round Africa; king named it **Cape of Good Hope**
- **Vasco da Gama** reached India (1497)

### Columbus

- Son of weaver, expert navigator (**Samuel Eliot Morison**: one of best of his day)
- Carried copy of **Marco Polo** — confident Japan's palaces roofed with gold
- Turned down by **Portugal** (King John II), **Spain** (commission: trip too long, no lands to discover), **England** (Henry VII's advisors ridiculed), **France** (Charles VIII)
- Columbus demanded: knighthood, admiral of Ocean Seas, viceroy/governor, 10% of all profits including gold
- **Queen Isabella** summoned him back; horseman caught him on mule path (April 1492)
- Diary mentions gold **65+ times** from Oct 12 to Jan 17; "I was attentive and took trouble to ascertain if there was gold"
- Encouraged by dark skins — Europeans believed dark skins signaled gold; "From the great heat... the country must be rich in gold"
- Found tobacco in Cuba but held no interest — only gold would satisfy

### Balboa and the Pacific

- **Balboa** (1513): led 190 Spaniards through hostile Indians, insects, snakes to discover Pacific
- Waded into surf with drawn sword, claimed "the great South Sea" for Spain
- **Keats** had wrong man but right ocean: "stout Cortez... stared at the Pacific... Silent, upon a peak in Darien"
- Later executed for treason; executioner was **Francisco Pizarro**
- **Pizarro** when asked to convert natives: "I have not come for any such reasons. I have come to take away from them their gold"

### Pizarro's Conquest of Peru

- **200 Spaniards** subdued empire of **3.5 million** spanning Ecuador to Argentina
- Scaled Andes passes — paths barely wide enough for horse, flanked by thousand-foot drops
- Defeated 30,000 Inca soldiers trained for high altitude; Spanish had cannons, muskets, horses, shining armor
- Incas had never seen the wheel; relay runner system on roads as good as Roman roads delivered fresh fish from coast to mountains
- November 1532: **Atahualpa** ("Child of the Sun") on golden litter, golden throne, collar of emeralds
- Threw Bible on ground; priest commanded: "Set on at once; I absolve you"
- Indians made no resistance; thousands fell; Spaniards suffered one wound (Pizarro protecting Atahualpa)

### The Ransom Room

- Room **17 x 22 feet, filled to 9 feet** with gold within two months
- Spaniards ripped **700 gold plates** from temple of the Sun at **Cuzco**; packed 200 loads on Indians' backs
- **Nearly 5 tons of gold** — more than total annual European output, equivalent to 20 years of Peruvian mines
- Gold art by 500 BC: diadems, earrings, ceremonial beakers; some show Chinese/Vietnamese influences
- Woolen tunic containing **30,000 minuscule plaques of sheet gold**
- Not a single piece survived — all melted into uniform bars by same Indian goldsmiths who created them
- Produced **1,326,539 pesos d'oro** (~$270 million modern equivalent)

### Atahualpa's Execution (August 29, 1533)

- Kangaroo court: charges of usurping throne, adultery, idolatry, insurrection
- Atahualpa's plea: "What have I done that I should meet such a fate? And from your hands, too?"
- Pizarro turned away without reply
- Accepted baptism as **Juan de Atahualpa** (after Saint John the Baptist) for quick death by garroting

## Chapter 10: The Fatal Poison and Private Money

### The Treasure Fleet System

- European gold/silver stock at century's end nearly **5x its 1492 size**
- Armed convoys averaged **60 ships**, occasionally 100; ships carried 200-400 tons
- Loaded at **Vera Cruz**, **Nombre de Dios**, **Cartagena**; Caribbean = **the Spanish Main**
- 2-8 armed galleons accompanied each **flota** against pirates
- Triple-locked treasure chests at **House of Trade** in Seville — each key carried by different official
- **Charles V** cried with joy at every safe flota arrival

### Piracy

- **Sir Francis Drake**: plundered gold from Catholics for nearly 25 years; Spanish called him "master-thief of the unknown world"
- 1572: seized pack train with £20,000 in coins at **Nombre de Dios**
- **Golden Hind** voyage (1577-79): snared 10+ tons of gold, silver, jewels; landed at **Point Reyes** (Marin County), claimed for **Queen Elizabeth**
- 1595: conquered by fatal dysentery in Panama; buried at sea
- Whole fleets intercepted only 3 times in the century (twice English, once Dutch admiral **Piet Heyn** in 1628)

### Spain's Paradox: Wealth Without Prosperity

- Gold came in one end, went out the other "like a dose of salts"
- 5/6 of outgoing cargoes consisted of goods from other countries
- **Cortes (Parliament)**: "The more of [gold] that comes in, the less the Kingdom has... they are only a bridge"
- **Pedro de Valencia** (1608): "So much silver and money has always been fatal poison to republics"
- "Agriculture laid down the plough, clothed herself in silk, and softened her work-calloused hands"
- Popular saying: "Spain is the foreigners' Indies" (paying for "puerilities" — baubles, playing cards)
- 1492 **expulsion of Jews and Muslims** destroyed Spain's educated merchant class
- Jews/Muslims: leaders in math/science, immune from usury strictures, skilled administrators
- **Cadiz and Seville** filled with foreigners; almost all massive borrowing financed by foreigners
- Spain not on natural trade routes; geographic isolation compounded by loss of cosmopolitan population

### Spain's Sovereign Defaults

- Greatest waste: **monarchical delusions of grandeur** — convinced American gold could bend world to their will
- **Fuggers** lent 850,000 florins for Charles V's imperial election against **Francis I**
- Charles engaged in 27 years of warfare with Francis I; left **Philip II** the fruitless 80-year Netherlands campaign
- **Charles V's** debt (37 million ducats) **exceeded total precious metals from America**
- July 1576: Philip owed troops 17.5 million florins; stopped payments, forced debt conversion
- First major sovereign default in Western history — introduced concept to modern world
- Repeated crises: **1596, 1607, 1627, 1647**
- **Fugger banking house** nearly wiped out; army mutiny, captain general couldn't afford lunch
- Spanish Armada (1588) against Elizabeth; Turkish campaigns in Balkans over 100 years

### Francis I and the Field of Cloth of Gold

- **Francis I** declared to **Cellini**: "I will choke you with gold!"; also famous for amours: "A court without women is a year without spring"
- **Battle of Pavia** (1525): Francis captured — second French king imprisoned; languished in Madrid cell writing songs
- Sons ransomed for **2 million gold crowns**; counting took 4 months; children prisoners for 4 years
- **Meeting on Field of Cloth of Gold** (1520): 2,800 tents covered in gold; archers' weapons sheathed in gold

> "Today the French / All clinquant, all in gold, like heathen gods, / Shone down the English; and to-morrow they / Made Britain India; every man that stood / Show'd like a mine." — **Shakespeare**, Henry VIII

- Two fountains spouted red wine; Wolsey rode mule with golden stirrups, 50 men with gold maces
- Henry wrestled Francis and ended up on grass, purple with rage; three years later at war

## The Price Revolution of the Sixteenth Century

### Pattern of Inflation

- First visible in Italy/Germany ~1470; took hold in England/France 1480s; Iberia 1490s; eastern Europe early 1500s
- Persisted **100+ years** — **no other inflation in history so stubborn**
- England: wood, livestock, grain rose **5x to 7x** (1480-1650); manufactured goods merely tripled
- 700% increase over 170 years = only 1.2%/year, but wages rose less than half as fast as necessities
- No prior experience, no economic theory to explain it, no established policy rules

### Causes

- Population doubled 1400-1600 (45 million to 90+ million); food supply couldn't keep pace
- Shift from arable to pastoral farming (England); labor drifting to cities
- Not a single year of complete peace on European continent 1551-1651
- **Tacitus**: "Pecunia nervus belli" (Money is the sinew of war)
- Fiscal deficits and exploding debt; Spain's **asientos** and France's **Grand Parti** = capital markets borrowing
- **Henry VIII's Great Debasement** (1540s): "worked the Mint for all it was worth"
- Inflation generates self-sustaining urgency: hoarding and speculation intensify upward pressure

### American Treasure and Monetarism

- **Adam Smith**: "The discovery of the abundant mines of America seems to have been the sole cause"
- **Jean Bodin** (1568): demonstrated rising gold/silver associated with higher prices — **spiritual father of monetarism**

> "Spain is rich, haughty, indolent... It is the abundance of gold and silver that causes the dearness of things." — **Bodin**

- **Milton Friedman**: "inflation is always and everywhere a monetary phenomenon"
- But timing doesn't fit: prices started rising ~1470; **American gold didn't arrive significantly until 1520**
- Gold/silver imports peaked ~1590; prices kept climbing; England's prices **doubled 1600-1650**
- Seville data questionable: increasing supply unloaded at Cadiz/Lisbon, growing illegal diversions
- Even **Anna Schwartz** (Friedman's colleague): Price Revolution was "contradiction of the basic hypothesis"
- Alternative: rising prices magnified demand for money, arousing efforts to bring more gold — expanding money supplies may be **result** of inflation, not cause

### Gold Value During Price Revolution

- Gold price rose only 85% (England, 1492-1611) — far below wages, food, clothing increases
- **Adam Smith**: gold's high value and small bulk make it easy to transport, explaining price stability
- "No vigilance of government can prevent their exportation... All the sanguinary laws of Spain and Portugal are not able to keep their gold at home"
- Francis I's ransom (2 million crowns) stimulated spending in Spain; prices soon higher than France; resulting price disparity produced French export surge; gold ran back to France

### Trade Fairs and Financial Innovation

- Trade fairs: essential institution in world without banks, telephones, or internet
- **48 different kinds of gold coins** circulating in Europe in 16th century (11 Italian, 9 Netherlands, 6 English)
- Key locations shifted over time: Champagne, Antwerp, Geneva, Lyons, Besancon, Piacenza
- **Lyons** held fairs 4 times/year; **Medina del Campo** (Spain): trading in bills of exchange was only activity
- **Bill of exchange**: developed by Italians in 13th century; enabled international payments without moving coins
- Example: Franco (Italy) buys wool from Berthold (Flanders); David (Flanders) buys wine from Carlo (Italy) — bills settle both without gold crossing borders
- **Million livres tournois changed hands without single penny disbursed**
- By settling differences rather than gross amounts, bill markets significantly **reduced need for coins**
- Entire process couldn't have functioned without trade fairs as central gathering places
