# The Power of Gold: The History of an Obsession

**Author:** Peter L. Bernstein
**Published:** 2000
**Pages:** 432 content pages (603 PDF pages)

## Overview

Peter Bernstein traces humanity's relationship with gold from
ancient Egypt to the collapse of the gold standard in the
twentieth century, arguing that gold's power derives not from
any intrinsic utility but from an unbroken chain of collective
belief in its supreme value. The book weaves together economics,
military history, and human psychology to show how gold has
repeatedly driven exploration, conquest, financial innovation,
and catastrophic policy errors. Bernstein's central insight is
that gold worked as a monetary anchor only when economies were
already healthy -- the metal was a consequence of prosperity,
never its cause.

## Part I: A Metal for All Seasons (Chapters 1-9)

Gold's story begins with Egyptian pharaohs who monopolized the
metal as a divine symbol of power, through mines worked by
slaves under horrific conditions. The Lydians under Croesus
transformed gold from ornament to money by minting the first
standardized coins, democratizing access to wealth and
launching international commerce. Alexander the Great, the
Romans, and the Byzantine Empire each wielded gold as an
instrument of imperial power, with Constantine's bezant
maintaining its weight and purity for an astonishing 700 years.

The Arab conquests redirected gold flows through West Africa,
where camel caravans carried salt south and gold north in a
trade so secretive that buyers and sellers never met face to
face. Medieval Europe, starved of domestic gold, developed
financial innovations -- trade fairs, bills of exchange, the
florin and ducat -- that allowed commerce to flourish even as
the physical metal remained scarce. The Black Death killed a
third of Europe's population but paradoxically made survivors
richer per capita, while the Great Bullion Famine of the
fifteenth century helped motivate the Age of Exploration.

Columbus, obsessed with gold (mentioning it 65 times in his
diary), and Pizarro, who extracted nearly five tons from the
Inca Empire only to melt priceless art into uniform bars,
represent the "sacred thirst" that drove European expansion.
The Spanish treasure fleets brought unprecedented wealth to
Europe, yet Spain itself was impoverished by monarchical
delusions of grandeur, sovereign defaults, and the expulsion
of its educated Jewish and Muslim merchant class.

## Part II: The Path to Triumph (Chapters 10-16)

The Price Revolution of the sixteenth century -- over 100 years
of stubborn inflation -- defied simple monetary explanations,
since prices began rising decades before American treasure
arrived in volume. Bernstein shows that population growth,
warfare, debasement, and the self-reinforcing psychology of
inflation all played roles, while bills of exchange and trade
fairs reduced the need for physical coins.

Gold's journey to Asia proved a one-way trip: India and China
absorbed precious metals as commodities for hoarding and
adornment, not as money, defying David Hume's theory that
trade imbalances would self-correct. Kublai Khan's paper money
system -- backed by the threat of execution -- demonstrated
that state power could substitute for metallic backing, a
lesson that would echo through centuries to Nixon's closing of
the gold window in 1971.

The Great Recoinage of 1696 brought Newton to the Mint and
established the Locke doctrine that the gold content of money
must never be altered -- a principle that would shape policy
through the Great Depression. The 1797 crisis, when Welsh women
in festival costumes helped repel a French invasion while the
Bank of England suspended gold payments for 24 years, proved
that paper money could function without gold backing. Ricardo
and the Bullion Committee codified the intellectual framework
for the gold standard, which Britain formally adopted in 1821.

South African mining, the cyanidation process, and the
"Crime of 1873" that demonetized silver set the stage for
gold's triumph as the sole international monetary standard.
The system worked through central bank cooperation, British
hegemony, and an unquestioning commitment to credibility --
but it demanded that domestic economies be sacrificed to
defend gold reserves, with no safety nets for workers or
businesses.

In America, the gold standard survived Bryan's "Cross of Gold"
campaign and J.P. Morgan's dramatic rescue of the Treasury in
1895, but the country's bimetallic tradition and powerful
silver interests kept it a reluctant participant until 1900.

## Part III: The Descent from Glory (Chapters 17-20)

World War I destroyed the conditions that made the gold
standard viable, yet leaders like Montagu Norman devoted the
1920s to restoring it at prewar parities. Churchill's 1925
decision to return Britain to gold at the old rate -- which he
later considered "the greatest blunder of his life" -- crushed
British exports, triggered a coal crisis and general strike,
and exported deflation across Europe.

The interwar gold standard became a deflationary machine:
France hoarded gold aggressively while Norman and Benjamin
Strong's easy-money intervention in 1927 inadvertently fueled
the Wall Street bubble. After the 1929 crash, adherence to
gold standard orthodoxy -- raising interest rates during
deflation, liquidating rather than stimulating -- transformed
a financial panic into the Great Depression. The Creditanstalt
crisis of 1931 triggered a chain reaction from Vienna to
Berlin to London, and Britain abandoned gold in September 1931
to widespread relief. Roosevelt's 1933 gold seizure and 1934
devaluation to $35 per ounce finally broke the deflationary
spiral in the United States.

The "golden avalanche" of the 1930s sent 60% of the world's
monetary gold flooding into America as European nations fled
political chaos, proving that gold migrates toward safety
rather than creating it. Bretton Woods established a new
dollar-gold system after World War II, but de Gaulle's
challenge and Nixon's 1971 closure of the gold window
completed gold's descent from monetary anchor to commodity.
Norman's deathbed reflection -- "we achieved absolutely
nothing" -- stands as the epitaph for an era.

## Key Themes

- **Gold as collective illusion**: Gold's value depends
  entirely on the shared belief that it is precious. Its
  physical properties (beauty, durability, scarcity) sustain
  the illusion, but the power comes from human psychology,
  not chemistry.

- **The Disraeli principle**: The gold standard was always a
  consequence of prosperity, never its cause. Leaders who
  tried to use gold to restore economic health (Churchill in
  1925, Hoover in 1931) caused immense suffering.

- **Debasement as eternal temptation**: From Dionysius of
  Syracuse to Henry VIII to modern central bankers, the
  impulse to stretch the money supply beyond its metallic
  backing has recurred in every civilization.

- **Innovation born of scarcity**: Bills of exchange, trade
  fairs, banking, and paper money all emerged because gold
  was too scarce or too heavy to serve a growing economy's
  needs -- financial progress has been a story of escaping
  gold's physical limitations.

- **Gold migrates to power**: Whether flowing to Byzantium,
  Baghdad, or the U.S. Treasury, gold has always gravitated
  toward the dominant economic and military power of the age,
  reinforcing existing advantages rather than creating new
  ones.

- **The human cost of "sound money"**: Maintaining gold
  convertibility required sacrificing employment, wages, and
  growth -- costs borne disproportionately by workers and the
  poor, who had no voice in the system until Bryan and the
  silver populists.

- **Asia as gold's graveyard**: For centuries, precious metals
  flowed east and never returned, absorbed by cultures that
  valued gold as ornament and insurance rather than as money,
  defeating every European theory about self-correcting trade
  balances.
