# The Power of Gold - Compacted Notes (Second Half)

## The Transformation of Money: Bills of Exchange

- **Bills of exchange** eliminated need to ship coins across borders—ingenious merchant innovation
- Example: Berthold (Flemish) sends wool to Carlo (Italian); Carlo draws bill on David (Flemish buyer of wine). Franco pays Carlo instead of Berthold, David pays Berthold instead of Carlo. Wine, wool, and bills move across borders but **no money goes from Italy to Flanders or vice versa**
- By 1585: bills drawn on Amsterdam merchants trading in Antwerp, Cologne, Danzig, Hamburg, Lisbon, Lubeck, Rouen, Seville
- **Lively market in trading bills of exchange** built up to handle differences
- Settlement by differences rather than gross amounts: **one million livres tournois changed hands without single penny disbursed**
- Trade fairs enabled dealers and money changers to meet, buy/sell bills, work out foreign exchange payments
- Merchants no longer had to travel to settle accounts—operations centralized at trading fairs
- Private money (credit instruments) now shared circulation with prince's coins
- **Unit of account** became abstract concept—"You cannot see dollars a check transfers, nor feel them, bite into them, weigh them"
- Only concern: that prince regulate public money supply so unit of account integrity remains stable
- Merchant firms diversified into great family firms: **Fuggers** (Holy Roman Empire), **Medici** (Florence), later **Rothschilds** and **Baring Brothers**
- Over time: gold coins circulated less frequently, gold bullion served only for very large transactions or covering unfavorable trade balance with Far East

## The Asian Necropolis and Paper Money

### One-Way Flow of Precious Metals

- New World gold/silver barely paused in Europe before continuing to Asia
- Evidence: outflow to Far East may have exceeded total imports from America between 1600-1730
- First 25 years of **East India Company** (est. 1600): bullion was 75% of all cargo shipped eastward
- **Asia as a sponge**: precious metals accumulated there, almost none returned to Europe
- **Earl Hamilton**: "The East [was] a necropolis of European treasure even in Roman days"
- India remains largest gold buyer today—Indians spend more on gold than cars, transport, refrigerators, TVs combined
- **Modern Japan parallel (1986-1998)**: exported 60-70% more than imported; reserves from <$30B to ~$200B

### Why Asia Defied David Hume's Theory

- **Hume's 1752 essay "Of the Balance of Trade"**: money accumulating in one country creates spending and rising prices, shifting demand to reverse the flow

> "It is impossible to heap up money, more than any other fluid, beyond its proper level." — **David Hume**

- **Francis I ransom**: perfect illustration—gold from France to Spain, then treasure flowed back as Spanish spending increased
- Yet precious metals accumulated indefinitely in China, India, Japan
- **Thomas Mun** (East India Company executive): defended shipping precious metals eastward—"If those nations which send out their monies do it because they have few wares of their own, how come they have so much Treasure?"
- Europeans needed Asian spices, tea, silk desperately—had no alternative but to pay in precious metals
- Spices can only grow in Asia (monsoon rains); silkworms/tea require specific climate
- **Key explanation**: Asians did not perceive gold/silver as money but as **commodities** with genuine use value
- Gold for decorating brides, baubles, ornaments, and most important: **hoarding**
- **Hoarding similar to buying insurance policy**—idle metal earns nothing, but you sleep better knowing you hold hedge against catastrophe
- Gold-consuming nobility aware of no loss shipping goods to Europe; class so small their consumption could be as conspicuous as desired
- **Adam Smith**: described Canton poverty—people "eager to fish up the nastiest garbage thrown overboard from any European ship"
- Per capita wealth in Asia close to Europe's average, but **averages obscure reality**: tiny number lived in luxury while masses existed at levels "horrible even by European standards"
- Gold and silver had **limitless market in Asia for both ostentation and insurance** against uprisings and war
- What looked irrational at first glance becomes understandable when viewed through **class structure and tastes**
- **Strangest feature of gold: has never ceased to be scarce**—steel sells for 26¢/oz; gold more than 10,000 times as much
- Mystique as store of wealth (dependent on scarcity) adds lustre as symbol of power—whether it accents halo of saint or covers palace roof
- **Insurance policy works only as long as everyone continues to consider gold item of highest value**
- In long run, **cupidity and stupidity of human beings** motivate the drama; nature contributes by distributing gold arbitrarily around globe

### Marco Polo's Evidence

- Traveled Venice to China 1271, served **Kublai Khan** for 20 years
- Khan's palace: "nothing to be seen anywhere but gold and pictures"
- Khan kept 30-40 concubines per biennial selection, four official wives with 300 ladies each, 10,000 persons in court
- **Japan**: gold "in measureless quantities," palace roofed with fine gold—passage spurred **Columbus** when he reached Cuba
- **Vochan**: 1 ounce gold = 5 ounces silver (Europe ratio: 1:10+)
- **Zar-dandan**: men made gold casts of teeth to cover entire mouthful
- **Tibet**: "rivers and lakes and mountains in which gold-dust is found in great quantity"
- **India**: gold exchanged 1:6 for silver; imported large amounts of both in 1500s-1600s
- Asian rulers held gold too important to circulate among "dirty and ignoble" hands—releasing gold would dilute state power

### Chinese Monetary Innovation

- **Most Chinese money fabricated from materials of little value**
- **Qin Shu Huangdi** (255 BC): unified feudal states, replaced awkward bronze pieces with **cast-bronze round coins with square hole** ("cash"—Tamil word for money)
- Holes allowed stringing together for carrying; low-value material meant many coins needed
- **WWII China crisis**: **Chiang Kai-shek's** government in Chongqing—all paper money printed in U.S., shipped by air through Japanese fire. Currency orders never kept pace with inflation, wasting precious cargo space
- **Hien Tsung** (806-821 AD): severe copper shortage led to paper money—"inadvertent innovation" ranking with printing, gunpowder, compass
- Successors paved way for inevitable route: **overissue and uncontrollable inflation**
- **Ma Twan-lin** (1149): "Paper should never be money [but] only employed as a representative sign of value existing in metals or produce"
- 600 years passed (until 1455) before Chinese reverted to metallic coins; another 300 years before banknotes common in Europe

### Kublai Khan's Paper Money

- Marco Polo: Khan "mastered the art of alchemy"—paper from mulberry tree bark, various sizes for denominations
- Note for 1,000 coins measured 9x13 inches, light as feather; actual 1,000 coins weighed 8 pounds

> "The procedure of issue is as formal and authoritative as if [the notes] were made of pure gold or silver." — **Marco Polo**

> "No one dares refuse it on pain of losing his life." — **Marco Polo**

- Khan required surrender of all treasure (pearls, gold, silver, precious stones)—received paper "willingly"
- System worked because China's huge landmass made economy self-sufficient

> "The Great Khan acquires all the gold and silver and pearls and precious stones of his territory... has more treasure than anyone else in the world."

- Khan accumulated assets accepted anywhere (gold/silver); people held paper accepted only in Khan's dominions—**his paper money turned into gold at his palaces**

### State Power and Money

- **Nixon parallel**: 1933 U.S. prohibited monetary gold ownership; August 15, 1971 Nixon shut gold window—"Richard Nixon had mastered the art of alchemy"
- **Japan**: used gold for adornment, not money; goldsmithing developed late, never as notable as pottery/sculpture
- Copied Chinese monetary practice but **clung to metallic money** rather than paper
- When China changed to paper (9th century), Japan imported superfluous Chinese metal coins
- Trade considered demeaning; Marco Polo: no traders ventured from Japan to mainland
- Japanese gold ingots so valuable (one worth ~1 ton copper coins) used mostly for **ceremonial transactions among nobility**—"Stones of Yap would have functioned more efficiently!"
- Portuguese tried to convert Japanese to Christianity; Japanese cut off profitable triangular trade
- Even after Admiral Perry, Japanese coinage continued copying foreign coins
- **Yen**: "round coin" in Japanese; also word for dollar. **Sen**: word for copper coin (cent)
- **Lydia, Genoa, Florence, Venice** were economic powerhouses before issuing famous gold coins—"Who ever heard of gold coin issued by Manchester or Liverpool?"
- **Pound sterling**: longest uninterrupted monetary history, like English monarchy
- After Soviet Union breakup, Russians had hard time establishing ruble acceptability
- **People demand their money must have value**—valueless money is not even money

## The Great Recoinage and Newton

### Revolution in Coin Production

- **King Charles II** (1661): mandated machinery to replace hand-hammering
- Coinage hand-hammered since Lydians—astonishing no faster methods developed
- Clipping profitable and punishment inadequate: 1270, 280 Jews beheaded for clipping
- **Eloy Mestrell**: experimented with horse-powered machines under Queen Elizabeth; hanged 1578 for counterfeiting
- **Nicholas Briot** (1620s): developed anti-clipping technique (graining/inscriptions on edges), moved from Paris to England
- Briot's output minimal vs. conventional methods; evidence suggests hand-finished coins
- **1645**: France eliminated hammering at Paris Mint
- **Pierre Blondeau** invited to England; **Charles II's Order-in-Council (1661)**: "All coin to be struck as soon as possible by machinery"
- **Guinea** (Christmas Eve 1663): new mechanized gold coin from West African gold, ~8 grams, edges inscribed **"Decus et Tutamen"** (Ornament and Safeguard)—from Cardinal Richelieu's purse clasp

### The Clipping Crisis

- New milled coins contrasted sharply with worn coins circulating since 1601 recoinage
- **Hopton Haynes** (Assay Master): 1695 bag of coins had half the weight of 1686 bag
- **Samuel Pepys** anecdote: Mint workman stamped counterfeit groats superior to genuine—"neither hanged nor burned because the cheat was so ingenious"
- Silver drained to India: 9-10 oz silver = 1 oz gold there vs. 15 oz in England
- January 1690-December 1695: only £19,383 in silver coinage produced
- Guinea surged: 20 shillings → 22 (March 1694) → 25+ (1695) → 30 (June 1695)
- Charles II's reforms served little purpose: most new silver disappeared into hoards or shipped to Asia

### Locke vs. Lowndes

- **William Lowndes** (Secretary of Treasury, September 1695): published **"An Essay for the Amendment of the Silver Coins"** tracing 629 years of coinage history
- Proposed new coins with 80% silver of old (25% Mint price increase)—pragmatic recognition of reality
- Warned: "does but postpone the Cure of a Disease which may destroy us before such remedy can take effect"
- **John Locke** (supported by **Charles Montagu**, Chancellor of Exchequer): coin reading "one shilling" = one shilling regardless of clipping
- Silver weight defines pound sterling, should continue at same weight eternally
- Satirically called Lowndes's proposal "public clipping"
- Locke's austerity doctrine: reduce import demand, the "Country Farmer" metaphor—lives within compass, never in debt
- **Doctrine invoked during Napoleonic Wars, became standard 19th-century gold standard response, motivated Great Depression policies (1931)**
- **Montagu and Locke won**: clipped shillings exchanged at original face value, loss borne by taxpayer

### The Great Recoinage (1695-1697)

- Carried out during major war against **Louis XIV**—most aggressive European leader since Romans
- By 1697, **William III** over £20 million in debt; taxation, lotteries insufficient
- **Bank of England** established (1694): lent government £1.2M at 8% interest; first private **limited-liability corporation**
- Became "Old Lady of Threadneedle Street"; steadily increased influence over banking system and gold stock
- **Glorious Revolution of 1688** resolved religious uncertainties; credit expanded rapidly, inevitable price inflation followed
- **Daniel Defoe** on stock speculation: "Compleat System of Knavery... founded in Fraud, born of Deceit"
- December 19, 1695 proclamation: clipped coins unacceptable; immediate panic, business halted
- Mint overwhelmed; metallic money shortage disrupted retail trade
- Horses powering presses so busy £700 paid just to haul manure
- Riots, petitions; **Edward Bohun** (July 1696): "Our tenants can pay no rent... Many self murders happen in small families for want"
- £6.8 million new milled silver issued; experts estimate another million clipped coins owned by poor never exchanged
- Restored English money weight to pre-Great Debasement levels (~150 years prior)
- **Locke vs. Lowndes dispute still resonating**: 1821 gold standard, Bryan's "cross of gold," Churchill's 1920s, throughout 20th century

### Isaac Newton at the Mint

- **March 1696**: took post as **Warden of the Mint** at invitation of friend **Montagu**
- Most distinguished scientist of age; spent most of life as "total nerd"—also passionate believer in alchemy

> "He was the last of the magicians, the last of the Babylonians and Sumerians, the last great mind which looked out on the visible and intellectual world with the same eyes as those who began to build our intellectual inheritance rather less than 10,000 years ago." — **Keynes**

- Born Christmas Day 1642 to farmer's widow; baby so small "he'd fit in quart pot"
- Entered Cambridge as **subsizar**: cleaning rooms, emptying bedpans of wealthier classmates
- Full professorship 1669 (age 27); no one showed for any Newton lectures over seventeen years
- Cut lectures from half-hour to fifteen minutes but punctilious about appearing
- **1685**: anti-Catholic crisis (James II forced admission of Benedictine monk to Cambridge) transformed Newton politically
- Ran for Parliament, began functioning "as a man about town," met **Montagu** and **John Locke**
- At the Mint: four days after appointment, broke with past, moved to London
- First weeks in tiny dank room next to clanking presses; work began 4 AM
- Used secret agents around countryside to root out clippers; frequented lowest public houses for informant meetings
- Attended hangings, kept detailed accounts of everything
- **December 1699**: became **Master of the Mint** after Thomas Neale died

## The 1797 Crisis and the Bullion Committee

### Battle of Fishguard and Bank Run

- **February 22, 1797**: three French frigates landed ~1,200 soldiers at Fishguard, southwest Wales
- Confronted by local militia; French saw approaching troop in red cloaks/tall black hats
- "Fearsome band" was actually **Welsh women in traditional festival costumes**
- **Jemima Nicholas** wielded pitchfork so adeptly she captured 14 French soldiers
- French invasion excitement launched rush to withdraw gold from banking system
- Bank of England gold draining at **£100,000/day**: from £7M (1794) to £5M (1795) to £2M (1797)

### The Restriction Bill

- **Sunday meeting** at Bank: King George III, PM Pitt, Lord Chancellor, Bank Governor
- **February 26**: Order-in-Council forbade Bank from issuing gold
- **March 9**: Restriction Bill passed; Bank notes deemed "payments in cash"

> "It is indispensably necessary for the public service that the Directors of the Bank of England should forbear issuing any cash in payment." — Order-in-Council

- Leading bankers/merchants at **Mansion House** passed unanimous resolution accepting Bank notes; 4,000 additional signatures
- **First time markets attacked paper currency freely convertible into gold**—right existing over a century
- Bank Directors issued notice: Bank in "most affluent and prosperous situation" with £4M excess of liabilities plus £12M government debt
- **Crisis unique**: governments had debased coinages before, Chinese had made mess with paper, but never had paper currency freely convertible into gold at £1 billion been attacked
- **Sir William Forbes** (Scotch banker): "I certainly did think the nation was ruined beyond redemption"
- Yet within short time: "how quietly the country submitted to transact all business by bank notes"
- **Embargo endured 24 years, until 1821**—far longer than anyone anticipated

### The English Monetary System

- System evolved by trial and error, without legal stipulations characterizing French/Napoleonic system
- **Public and private money circulated together and reinforced each other**
- Government's money = coinage (mostly golden guinea); private paper money substituting for coins 100+ years before 1797
- Bills of exchange endorsed from holder to holder; goldsmiths issued receipts redeemable in gold
- Total gold output during century averaged only **20 tons/year**
- Banking grew rapidly: banks paid out loan proceeds as promissory notes that circulated as money
- Bank of England (1694): original £1.2M loan paid partly as Bank notes → government used to buy war supplies
- As banknotes replaced coinage: **money supply joined directly to volume of credit provided by privately owned banking system**
- **Private sector overtook public sector as primary engine of money creation**
- Gold clearly defined by weight: **129.4 grains** in guinea = ounce of gold = **£3 17s 10½d**
- Before February 26, 1797: anyone with £210 in notes could receive 200 golden guineas
- Most of time nobody bothered converting; but when business booming and gold price rising, 200 guineas could command more than £210
- **1783**: Bank refused additional credits → sharp interest rate rise → immediate improvement in sterling exchange rate
- **Gold transformed from ultimate form of wealth into powerful vehicle of checks and balances**—role it would continue for 174 years

### Inflation and Money Supply Explosion

- **Napoleon** as ruthless accumulator: "Two millions of gold are en route [from Italy]... with a hundred carriage horses, the most beautiful one can find in Lombardy"
- Napoleon remained steadfast **"hard-money" man**—nightmare of assignat experience would have provoked immediate flight of gold
- **"Probably only major war in history conducted without currency depreciation"**
- Where did French gold come from? Much was **return of capital that fled during assignat regime**—gold earlier transferred to England now returning, driving Bank reserves from £6M to £2M
- **Napoleon inadvertently laid groundwork for panic ensuing from Battle of Fishguard**
- In Britain: Bank's commercial loans quadrupled 1797-1810; Bank note issue: ~£10M to £25M
- Prices doubled from 1797 level by Waterloo (1815)
- Inflation climbed 30% between 1802-1807, another 15% next 3 years
- By 1809: gold fetching £4 10s/ounce vs. £3 17s 10½d official
- Pound 16-20% below par in Hamburg, Amsterdam, Paris

### David Ricardo and the Bullion Committee

- **August 29, 1809**: 38-year-old stockbroker **David Ricardo** published first letters in **Morning Chronicle**—first time appeared in print
- Born 1772, Jewish merchant banker's son; broke with family at 21 to marry Quaker girl **Miss Wilkinson**
- Enormous success as stock jobber before becoming famous economist
- Major underwriter of government securities during Napoleonic Wars; held position through Waterloo for spectacular profit
- Friend **Malthus** couldn't stand strain, pleaded to take profit early—Napoleon's defeat = "good news and bad news"
- Published **The Principles of Political Economy and Taxation** (1817)
- After Ricardo's death, Malthus: "I never loved anybody out of my own family so much"

### The Bullion Committee (1810)

- 22 members, 31 days of hearings, 29 witnesses
- Mystery witness: "Mr. —" believed to be **N. M. Rothschild**
- Report leaders: **Francis Horner** (barrister, Edinburgh Review founder), **Henry Thornton** (banker, banking theory textbook), **William Huskisson** (later killed by train at Liverpool-Manchester Railway opening, 1830)
- Despite Horner's demurrer about literary quality, report was **best-seller**—sophistication matching **Milton Friedman** at his best

### The Bullion Committee's Core Doctrine

> "Gold in Bullion is the standard to which the Legislature has intended that the coin should be conformed"

- Gold not only domestic standard: **"Bullion is the true regulator both of the value of a local currency and of the rate of Foreign Exchange"**

> "When the currency consists entirely of the precious metals, or of paper convertible at will into the precious metals, the natural process of commerce adjusts, in every particular country, the proportion of circulating medium to its actual occasions... If the natural system of currency and circulation be abandoned, and a discretionary issue of paper money substituted in its stead, it is vain to think that any rules can be advised for the exact exercise of such a discretion."

- Bank Directors stubbornly denied connection between note issue and gold price
- **Mr. Pease** (Governor): "I cannot see how the amount of Bank notes issued can operate upon the price of Bullion"
- **Ricardo**: characterized them as "indeed a very ignorant set"
- Committee's most important conclusion: "first and perhaps most authoritative justification for establishment of gold standard"

### Parliamentary Defeat and Lord King's Protest

- Homer's sixteen resolutions debated four days; first fifteen defeated 150 to 75; final resolution 181 to 47
- **Chancellor of Exchequer**: Committee recommendations equivalent to "a declaration that we must submit to any terms of peace rather than continue the war"
- **Three camps in opposition**: (1) wartime priority—brakes on money supply unthinkable during government deficit spending; (2) **Thornton's group** worried returning to old pound/gold relationship when prices much higher would be devastating (the very issue that would cause 1920s crisis); (3) defenders of Bank management outraged at attack
- **Lord King**: informed tenants he would no longer accept Bank notes at face value—proposed payment in gold equivalent
- Caused such furor Parliament passed legislation declaring face value of contracts inviolate

### Return to Gold Standard (1821)

- Post-war deflation drove price level back to 1797 level by 1821; distortion that bothered Lord King washed out
- Full cash payments restored; new coin: the **sovereign** (twenty shillings)
- 35 million sovereigns minted in preparation
- **Gold standard** enshrined in official legislation—British arrangements became model for nearly one hundred years
- System put metal above man in managing money supply
- Nobody ever thought it easy to figure right amount of money—most people want more, but easy money leads to inflation
- **Baron Rothschild**: rumored to know only three people who understood money, none had very much of it
- Act of faith: free markets, expressed by changes in gold price, would do better job than policymakers
- Gold expected to support checks and balances so money supply—like Goldilocks's chair—never too large or too small
- **Fundamental problem**: what happens when quantity of ultimate standard itself begins to grow? Gold is product of earth, not construct dreamed up by economists

## Chapter 15: The Badge of Honor

### South African Gold Mining

- **George Harrison** (1886): discovered gold near Johannesburg digging stone for a widow's house
- Different from California/Australia: embedded in **reefs** one foot thick, deep underground
- One ton contained no more than an ounce of pure gold, difficult to separate
- Rush brought 2,000 immigrants/week at peak but required massive capital
- Within three years, boom seemed over—stocks crashed 95%, slag heaps accumulated
- **Cyanidation process**: **John Stewart MacArthur** (Glasgow chemist) and physicians **Robert and William Forrest**
- Worked late nights in "glory-hole under consulting rooms" using "kid-reviver" mixture
- Turned South African gold into high-tech industry of late 19th century
- Gold production: less than 1 ton (1886) → 14 tons (1889) → nearly 120 tons (1898)
- Patents declared void after extraordinary two-year lawsuit (verdict November 1896): processes "not novel and had been anticipated"
- **C.E. Fivaz** (1988): "most significant technological development in extractive-metallurgical industry of past century"
- MacArthur died poor in 1920—ruined by too much greed (Sutter by too little)

### Michel Chevalier's Inflation Warning (1857)

- Only economics professor in France, advisor to **Napoleon III**; jailed 1832 for "outrage to morals" (women's equality advocacy)
- Predicted gold rushes would cause devastating inflation
- Late 1850s wholesale prices ~35% above pre-California/Australia levels
- Calculated: financial system improvements (like steam engine cost dropping from £4000 to £1000) reduce gold demand
- London Clearing House settling 1,500-2,000 million sterling annually: "not a shilling is wanted"
- British banknote circulation barely budged: £30.9M (1846) → £31.0M (1856)
- Gold's malleability: one ton would gild ~179 acres; gold piece of 20 francs = thread from Calais to Marseilles

> "Mankind is not rich enough, nor will it soon be, to pay so dearly for so large a mass [of gold]."

- **Chevalier was wrong**: last 40 years of 19th century saw no sustained inflation
- Grossly underestimated economic growth: U.S. industrial output at century's end **5½ times** 1865 level
- Energy consumption: 7.3% per year from Sutter's Mill discovery
- Railroad track more than doubled in 1870s; golden spike linking Atlantic to Pacific driven 1869
- Missed central bank hoarding: Bank of England, Bank of France, U.S. Treasury accumulating reserves in buildings "resembling Greek temples"

### Why Gold Triumphed Over Silver

- Silver's two fatal flaws: **lack of glamour** (tarnishes faster, never drove people to greed extremes) and **excessive bulk** (1,000 guineas = ~18 lbs gold vs. 280 lbs silver)
- Transportation costs for silver much higher—acted as tax on international movements
- French word for money (argent) = silver; "sterling" defined English money for centuries even when gold-backed
- **Alexander Hamilton** (1791): invented American monetary system in remarkable document—bimetallic standard, decimal structure (radical break from Offa's 240-pennies-to-pound tradition)
- Hamilton's key insight: "To annul the use of either of the metals, as money, is to abridge the quantity of circulating medium"—supply concern recurred throughout 19th century
- Both **Ricardo** and **Locke** favored bimetallism; Ricardo opted for silver in 1816 based on "greater regularity of supply and demand"
- **Marc Flandreau**: elimination of silver in 1870s was "blatant failure of international cooperation"—narrow French decision
- **Irving Fisher** (Yale, 1911): growing gold shortage would have driven world to silver standard if not for California/Australia discoveries "as though to save the day"
- **Ludwig Bamberger**: "We chose gold, not because gold is gold, but because Britain is Britain"
- Germany seized opportunity from 1871 French victory indemnity; France retaliated **September 5, 1873** by limiting silver coinage to 280,000 francs/day
- Silver collapsed: over 60 pence/oz (1860s) → ratio of 30:1 vs. gold by century's end
- As ratio diverged from mint prices, profitable arbitrage: buy silver cheap, bring to mint for coinage—rocked gold standard foundations
- **Only defense**: eliminate silver as monetary metal except for small coins
- By 1893: even Indian mints closed to silver coinage
- Gold discoveries, Indian silver appetite, American Civil War, Germany's ambitions effectively backed world into gold standard—system displayed remarkable durability for next half-century

### The Gold Standard System

- Establishment during 1870s was no guarantee of smooth sailing
- Limited magnitude of crises but offered no government safety nets; gold outflow = danger signal → central bank interest rate increases
- If rate increases failed, central banks aided one another, frequently with large private banking institutions
- Major nation running out of gold was unthinkable—credibility beyond question, credits forthcoming
- Countries could suspend convertibility in wartime (Britain in Napoleonic Wars, U.S. 1862-1879) and reestablish at old parity
- These precedents had **profound influence on Britain's 1925 decision** to return to gold after WWI
- France took orthodox course in 1920s by resetting franc-gold relation to recognize price rise—lived more happily than British for a while
- **Peripheral countries** (including U.S.) often had to fend for themselves and did run out of reserves

### The Baring Crisis (1890)

- Ill-advised Argentine investments by London house of **Baring Brothers** nearly brought Bank of England down
- **Barings** among most respected families: primary role financing British war effort 1792, led European financing of U.S. **Louisiana Territory purchase**, led U.S. foreign trade financing to Civil War
- **Alexander Baring** (later Lord Ashburton): negotiated Maine-Canada border with Daniel Webster
- Descendants: Chancellor of Exchequer, Viceroy of India, de facto ruler of Egypt (1883-1907)
- **Henry Dana Noyes**: "Into no foreign state had English capital rushed with such reckless eagerness"
- 1882-1889: Europeans poured **$1 billion into Argentina**; Argentine immigration doubled population
- Barings underwrote **£42 million of Argentine securities** in 1880s
- Bank of England gold reserve: as low as £9 million; Barings needed at least £4 million immediately
- **Rothschilds** + Bank put up £15 million; Bank of France lent £2 million gold at 3% interest
- Another £1.5 million German gold from Russia; France offered additional £1 million
- Most remarkable: discretion and secrecy—by the 14th, "entire deed was done"
- **Kindleberger**: rescue "seen as measure of strength of London financial system more than Baring failure was taken as sign of weakness"
- Crisis cited 25 years later as proof Bank was "leader of most colossal agglomeration of financial power which the world has so far witnessed"
- Central bank cooperation well established: Bank of England drew on Bank of France 9 times (1826-1920)
- **1861**: Bank of France drew on Bank of England, Russian State Bank, Bank of Amsterdam
- **1898**: France and England helped Reichsbank; **1906-1907**: France and Reichsbank aided England
- All assistance predicated on assumption that preservation of convertibility at fixed rates was bedrock of policy
- 1890 emergency brought home necessity of much larger gold reserves
- **German war reserve** accumulated after 1900, held separate from Reichsbank—became object of envy

### Why the Gold Standard Succeeded

- **Barry Eichengreen**: critical reason was **unquestioning attachment to credibility**—rejection of any possibility nation would go off gold—and cooperation such credibility warranted
- **Kindleberger**: Bank of England hegemony kept system functioning, serving as "conductor of the international orchestra" (Keynes's phrase)
- All experts agree: system operated in congenial economic and political environment—no major European wars 1870-1914
- **Disraeli** (1895): "Our gold standard is not the cause, but the consequence of our commercial prosperity"
- Political requirement: domestic economy subordinated to gold stock defense; no safety nets of any kind
- Bad news for workers who lost jobs/saw wages cut, businesses whose profits slashed—but those were the rules
- Only in underworld of **Karl Marx** were macroeconomic concerns expressed
- Success might have been more symptom than cause—growth bailed out errors, international debts manageable, cooperation taken for granted

## American Financial Provincialism

### America's Unique Position

- U.S. waited until **1900** to legally commit to gold standard, remaining on **Hamilton's bimetallic standard**
- **Silver interests** wielded enormous political power; silver became more than metal—a **symbol of the Little Man**
- Democratic impulses and universal suffrage amplified equality themes far beyond Europe
- Foreign skepticism of American gold commitment was justified given this political climate
- U.S. situated far from Europe's 2,000 years of shared history and old-boy network
- Most wealth was "new wealth" rather than ancestral fortunes like Rothschilds or Barings
- **1791**: Hamilton set mint ratio at 15:1; gold-silver ratio shifted to 15.625:1 by 1834
- **Michel Chevalier** visiting 1833: "I have not seen there one piece of gold money, except on the scales at the Mint"
- **Congress (1834)**: set ratio at **16:1**, creating gold price of **$20.67/ounce** (prevailed for 99 years)
- Political factors: desire to "do something for gold" + **Andrew Jackson's** war against Second Bank of U.S.

### The Greenback Era (1861-1879)

- Civil War suspended dollar-gold convertibility; Treasury issued **greenbacks**—convertible into nothing, still legal tender
- Name persists as generic term for American dollars today
- ~$300M in circulation from 1863-1971; gold coins fell from $200M (1860) to $65M (1875)
- Government honored debt in gold but required customs duties in gold
- During war: $1 gold cost ~$1.30 in greenbacks; **Jay Gould and Jim Fiske** corner: $310,000 for ~$100,000 gold
- **1865 Treasury Secretary report**: urged end to greenbacks and return to "constitutional currency"—House support: 144-6
- Post-war deflation made consensus fall apart; prices doubled during war, fell 15% by 1868
- Experts never considered **devaluing gold content** of dollar—"not part of nineteenth-century view"
- **January 1875**: Congress pledged full resumption by January 1, 1879
- **May 1879**: catastrophic weather in France and England rescued U.S.—"almost as though Joseph were on hand making weather forecasts to Pharaoh"
- Pennsylvania oil pipeline completed, swelling exports; mid-autumn: $60M gold flowed into U.S.
- **First of several occasions nature rescued U.S. gold stock at last minute**

### The Crime of 1873

- **Coinage Act of 1873**: listed gold/silver coins to be minted—passed overwhelmingly (110-13 House, 36-14 Senate)
- **Startling omission**: no mention of historical **371.25-grain silver dollar** dating to Hamilton
- Had it been included, demand for silver coinage would have brought huge inflows, gold would have left
- Under those conditions, 1879 resumption would likely have been to **silver standard, not gold**
- **Senator Stewart**: "crime of the nineteenth century"; **Senator Reagan**: "greatest legislative crime and most stupendous conspiracy"
- Act debated in Congress three years, ordered printed thirteen times, occupied 144 columns in Congressional Globe
- Decision-makers knew consequences: **H.R. Linderman** (mint director) warned of silver depreciation
- **John Sherman** (Senate Finance Committee chair) drafted demonetization bill end of 1869
- Wholesale prices fell **30% by 1878**, stock prices followed
- **Wizard of Oz connection** (**Hugh Rockoff**): Oz = ounce of gold, Dorothy = little people, Emerald City = Washington, Wizard = humbug
- California/Australia discoveries had flooded gold supply; **1850**: ~3x as much money in silver as gold; **1860**: roughly even
- Silver was primary factor preventing gold from becoming market glut; silver traveled to Asia in high volume
- **Chevalier** noted silver exports acted like "parachute" to cushion fall in gold price
- **Comstock Lode** discovered in Nevada (1859)—when huge silver deposit emerged, glut clearly switched to silver

### The 1890s Gold Crisis

- **Sherman Silver Purchase Act (1890)**: Treasury required to buy $50M silver annually
- Named after same Sherman who wanted to demonetize silver twenty years earlier
- **Baring Crisis** triggered European liquidation of American securities → gold hemorrhage
- 1891: Nature intervened—South Russian wheat failure, French harvest worst since 1879; U.S. produced largest grain crop in history
- **Financial Panic of 1893**: Philadelphia & Reading Railway bankruptcy → National Cordage (147 to 10) → whole market panic
- Interest rates on shortest-term loans hit **74%**; 15,000 companies and 500 banks failed
- Unemployment exceeded 10% every year 1893-1898
- **Coxey's Army** (1894): unemployed marched on Capitol; **Eugene Debs** directed Pullman strike, jailed six months
- **Theodore Roosevelt** had to sell four acres at Sagamore Hill to stay solvent

### J. Pierpont Morgan Saves the Gold Standard (1895)

- Treasury gold at **$9 million**, sinking **$2 million/day**
- **Cleveland**: "endless chain in operation, constantly depleting Treasury's gold"
- Morgan declared: **"I have come down to see the President and I am going to stay here until I see him"**
- Told Cleveland: **"It will be all over before three o'clock"**
- Cleveland: **"What suggestions have you to make, Mr. Morgan?"**
- Syndicate: ~$65M bonds to European investors; ~3.5M ounces gold coin (about 100 tons), at least half from Europe
- Three critical elements: contract protecting Treasury, syndicate lending to Americans in European currencies, every major New York banking house bound together
- **New York World**: described syndicate as "bloodsucking Jews and aliens"
- **Bryan** asked clerk to read Shylock's bond from Merchant of Venice
- Cleveland unmoved: "never had slightest misgiving"
- Pound dropped from $4.89 to par $4.86; gold arriving at $5M/month
- **February 1896**: $100M public loan received bids of **$568M**
- **Treasury gold reserve never again fell below $100M**

### Bryan's Cross of Gold (1896)

- **Only election in American history where monetary system was central focus**
- Bryan (36): "boy orator from Platte," "Great Commoner"
- On J.P. Morgan: "Whenever he doesn't like it, he can give it back"
- "Raise less corn and more Hell.... Wall Street owns the country"

> "You shall not press down upon the brow of labor this crown of thorns, you shall not crucify mankind upon a cross of gold."

- McKinley won: Electoral College majority of 95, popular vote plurality of 602,000
- Financial markets impact: Bryan's campaign provoked steep stock break; call money at one point **125%**
- Week after election: stock prices soared, money rates down to 4%, gold flowing back into Sub-Treasury
- October wheat prices jumped from 53¢ to 94⅜¢ in election week—"It's the economy, stupid!"
- Although bimetallism was traditional for centuries, many 1890s voters perceived it as newfangled—gold had been effective single standard for 20+ years
- **Milton Friedman**: ascribed Bryan's defeat to **MacArthur-Forrest cyanide process** promising gold supply increase
- Bryan's legacy: launched battles for railroad regulation, eight-hour day, income tax, woman suffrage, temperance
- Died 1925, only 65 years old

## Chapter 17: The Norman Conquest

### Post-WWI: Return to Gold

- Over 8 million combat deaths; 1.8M Russians, 1.4M French; one-third of German males 19-22 gone
- French devastation: half the roads, hundreds of bridges, 9,000 factories, half textile industry destroyed
- National debts swelled by multiples; Allied debts to U.S.: nearly $2 billion
- Period resembles "a horror movie... with no bad guys. The good guys do enough damage on their own"

### The Cunliffe Committee (January 1918)

- **Lord Cunliffe** (Bank of England Governor): "imperative that conditions necessary for maintenance of an effective gold standard should be restored without delay"
- *The Economist*: "Back to Sanity"
- **Keynes** (age 36) dismissed by Cunliffe as having no "knowledge or experience in practical Exchange or business problems"
- No one recalled **Disraeli's warning**: gold standard was symptom, not cause, of prosperity

### Montagu Norman: High Priest of Gold

- **Governor of Bank of England**: record 24 years (1920-1944)
- "The high priest of the City's dogma that the power of Britain had been founded on gold"
- Gaunt, elegant, small beard; alternately severe, charming, seductive; periodic nervous breakdowns
- Gold was "a mystical symbol of all that was finest in the struggle of mankind to better its lot on earth"
- **"Dear money"** strategy: high interest rates → unemployment → wages in check → currency strengthens
- 1920: nearly 1 million men laid off within 12 months; Norman: "a matter for the government to worry about"

### The Price Level Problem

- 1920: pound trading at ~$4.00, lowest $3.40; Parliament mandated return to gold by end of 1925
- British prices by 1925: close to double 1914 levels; national debt from £0.5B to over £7B
- 1924: British exports 25% below prewar
- **Sir Charles Addis**: sacrifice "not too high a price to pay for recovery by the City of London of its former position"

### Churchill's Agonizing Decision (April 1925)

- Churchill: "When I held other offices under the Crown, I could always find out where I was. Here I'm lost"
- Complained Norman "perfectly happy in spectacle of Britain possessing finest credit simultaneously with million and a quarter unemployed"
- **Otto Niemeyer**: "None of the witch-doctors see eye to eye and Winston cannot make up his mind"
- **Benjamin Strong** (New York Fed): shared Norman's values; warned failure to resume gold would lead to "a long period of unsettled conditions too serious really to contemplate"
- Strong warned Fed might raise rates to discourage stock speculation—prediction proved accurate
- **Churchill's Exercise**: circulated memo with six objections, including: "Gold Reserve and Gold Standard are survivals of rudimentary and transitional stages in evolution of finance and credit"
- Radical suggestion: ship £100M gold to New York to pay war debts, provoking U.S. inflation
- Decision: March 20, 1925; announced April 25
- Strong arranged $200M standby credit; Morgan added $100M
- **J. P. Morgan**: "centuries of moral authority would go down the tube"
- **Leffingwell**: "sell the shirt to help England out... lock arms for honest money"

### Gold Standard Act of 1925

- Bank notes no longer convertible into gold coin; gold sold only in 400-ounce bars (~£1700/bar)
- **Keynes** (1930): "Gold is out of sight—gone back again into the soil. But when gods are no longer seen in a yellow panoply walking the earth, we begin to rationalize them"
- Wag's comment: "The Norman Conquest of $4.86"

### Aftermath: Coal Crisis and General Strike (1925-1926)

- **Grimmest problem**: failure of vaunted international trade revival to appear on schedule
- Coal industry losing £1M/month; sharp drop in coal exports already underway; miners refused pay cut
- **A. J. Cook**: "Not a penny off the pay, not a minute on the day"
- **Baldwin**: "All workers of this country have got to take a reduction in wages"
- **General Strike of 1926**: paralyzed country; miners eventually accepted lower wages on verge of starvation
- **Beatrice Webb**: referred to miners' leaders as "mental defectives"
- **Keynes** attacked Churchill: "Why did he do such a silly thing? Partly, perhaps because he has no instinctive judgement"
- Admitted Churchill "deafened by clamorous voices of conventional finance"—primarily Niemeyer and Norman

### French Contrast: A Study in Realism

- Striking contrast: cold-blooded English quietly discussing with professors vs. hot-headed French politicians making so much noise experts drowned out
- French lurched crisis to crisis; **ten Ministers of Finance** September 1924-July 1926
- **"Le Boche paiera"** (the Hun will pay); Ruhr invasion (January 1923) fruitless
- Franc at worst: 49 to dollar (10% of 1918 value); wholesale prices climbing 15% monthly
- **Poincare** (July 1926): lowered income taxes on rich, raised consumer taxes—capital poured back
- France back on gold at value irresistible to foreigners—wiser than Britain's decision

## Chapter 18: The End of the Epoch

### Norman vs. Moreau Rivalry

- **Emile Moreau** (Bank of France): patriotic, provincial, spoke no foreign languages
- Norman patronized Moreau, insisted on English, partial to Germans
- Warm friendship with Reichsbank president **Hjalmar Schacht** (who ended German hyperinflation, later served Hitler, acquitted at Nuremberg)
- Bank of France sterling balances: £5M (Nov 1926) → £160M (May 1927)
- French gold holdings: equal to Britain's (1926) → 5x Britain's (1931)
- When Moreau crossed Channel "to offer Norman war or peace," Norman claimed indisposed—then repudiated agreements made with staff

### Long Island Conference and Easy Money (1927)

- **Benjamin Strong** intervened: Fed lowered rates, provided gold to French for sterling sales
- Hindsight: easy money fueled violent stages of Wall Street boom
- **Leffingwell** (1929): "Monty and Ben sowed the wind. I expect we shall have to reap the whirlwind"
- **Hoover**: referred to Strong as "mental annex to Europe"
- **October 1928**: Strong died of tuberculosis

### American Stock Market Boom and Crash

- Dow doubled end 1924 to beginning 1928; jumped another 50% in second half of 1928
- **John J. Raskob** (GM director, Ladies' Home Journal): "Everybody Ought to Be Rich"
- Brokers' loans from nonbank sources: $1.0B → $6.6B at peak
- Federal Reserve internal battle: Washington wanted "direct pressure"; New York wanted sharp rate hike
- Discount Rate: 3% (1925) → 5% (1928) → 6% (August 1929)—zenith of bull market
- Industrial production already falling before October crash
- American lending abroad dried to zero; European capital flowed to Wall Street
- Britain, Germany, Italy, Austria already entering depression when crash hit

### Post-Crash Responses

> "Liquidate labor, liquidate stocks, liquidate the farmers, liquidate real estate... purge the rottenness out of the system." — **Andrew Mellon**

> "The remedy is for people to stop watching the ticker, listening to the radio, drinking bootleg gin, and dancing to jazz." — **Russell Leffingwell**

- Conventional wisdom: sins of speculators to be laid upon children
- **George Harrison** (New York Fed): proposed open-market purchases; Washington and other Fed banks blocked him
- **Hoover** (February 1931): "Victory will be won by the resolution of our people to fight their own battles"

### The Deflationary Spiral

- Wholesale prices 24% below 1929; unemployment climbing past 15%; 3,000 banks failed
- No deposit insurance in those days—depositors lost every dollar; money simply disappeared into thin air
- When U.S. began losing gold (autumn 1931), even Harrison capitulated
- **Eugene Meyer**: sharp rate increase "called for by every known rule, and foreigners would regard it as lack of courage if rate not advanced"
- Meyer correct by "every known rule"—reveals how gold standard mindsets dominated even as **Herbert Hoover** recognized crisis never before confronted
- Federal Reserve drove rates higher; prices dropped another 10%, unemployment 25%, 3,000 more banks failed
- Epidemic of disasters only **strengthened** obstinate faith in traditional approach
- As trade shriveled, failure to restrain demand would lead to most dreaded result: losses of gold
- Britain's deflationary path appeared sole available roadmap—though deflationary pressures so severe **Adolf Hitler** would come roaring into power
- "These horrors served only to strengthen determination to preserve gold reserves above all else"

### First Year After Crash (1930)

- **March 7**: Hoover reported "worst effects of Crash upon unemployment will have passed during next sixty days"—not bad forecast under circumstances
- Domino effect hit end of 1930: **Caldwell & Co.** (Tennessee bank) failure brought down companies, banks in wake
- Then failure of small New York bank with big name: **Bank of United States**; 2,300 banks followed

### The Creditanstalt Crisis (May 1931)

- Fuses in place, lit **May 11** when failure of **Creditanstalt Bank** in Vienna stunned world
- Austria's largest commercial bank by far, holding more than half all Austrian bank deposits
- Too large to fail in 1931 too—Austrian government had to bail it out, but to no avail
- **Ralph Hawtrey** (British Treasury): "sent terrible spasm of panic through financial centers of world"
- Spasm ignited run on Austrian banks, then panic hit schilling in foreign exchange
- Panic spread: Hungary, Czechoslovakia, Romania, Poland, then Germany
- Austrian National Bank tried to borrow from other central banks
- Bank of France insisted no loans unless Austrians renounced customs union with Germany—Austrian government refused
- Meanwhile Bank of France acquired $539M gold—about 25% more than entire year's worldwide production
- **Norman** infuriated French by going ahead with Bank of England loan to Austrians—too late, merely transitory stopgap
- German Chancellor **Bruning**: slashed spending, proclaimed reparations limit → flight from reichsmark
- **Hoover** proposed one-year moratorium on reparations and war debts; French furious

### Britain's Sterling Crisis (July-September 1931)

- Crisis in central Europe rapidly communicated itself to pound, largely due to French actions
- London financiers had borrowed at low rates in Paris, lent proceeds to Germany at much higher rates
- Now French demanding repayment—shocking sum of £750 million involved
- **Churchill** (on vacation): "I hope we shall hang Montagu Norman if it does"
- Norman: "I cannot explain this drop. It was sudden and unexpected"
- Norman asked Bank of France for immediate loan; French willing only if British taxpayers guaranteed it—Cabinet refused
- **July 28**: Norman went home "feeling queer," sailed to Canada—last act played without him
- We'll never know whether he was too sick or just unable to face total failure
- Sterling crisis all more remarkable because prices already fallen 38% from 1925 level
- Unemployment: one in four workers by August (up from one in six a year earlier)
- MacDonald formed National Government; King offered to forgo £50,000/year

> "It is far, far better for all of us to go with tight belts into austerity than with loose ones into confusion." — **MacDonald**

- Bank Rate leapt to 6% from 2½%; combination of higher taxes, reduced spending, higher rates was lethal
- **Keynes** (January 1931): "Oh patriotic housewives, sally out tomorrow early into the streets and go to the wonderful sales"—logic had no effect on leaders

### Invergordon Mutiny and Britain Off Gold (September 1931)

- Sailors at Invergordon struck against pay cuts; foreigners feared revolution
- £40 million gold swept from Bank in single week; £200 million lost since July
- **September 21**: Britain suspended gold convertibility
- **The Economist**: "The End of an Epoch"
- **Keynes**: "There are few Englishmen who do not rejoice at the breaking of the golden fetters"
- **Tom Johnson**: "They never told us they could do that"
- Norman sailing home, received cable: "Old Lady goes off on Monday"—thought it referred to his mother's vacation
- 24 of 47 gold standard nations suspended convertibility within days
- Six years later: not a single country permitted citizens to convert currency into gold

## The Final Crisis (1931-1933)

### America's Gold Hemorrhage

- September 22, 1931: Belgium withdrew $106M in one day; France $50M same day, $70M more weeks later
- September-October: $755M gold flowed out; one in seven gold bricks departed Federal Reserve
- Fed doubled Discount Rate from 1.5% to 3%; manufacturing fell 25% over nine months
- Unemployment doubled from 10% to 20%; wholesale prices dropped 25%
- Total banks: 30,000 (1925) → under 10,000 (1933)

### Hoover's Failed Attempts

- Spring 1932: Reserve banks bought $1B government securities; triggered fresh gold outflow
- Fiscal deficit: $2B (over 3% of national output)—"astonishing for its time"

> "Ever since the storm began in Europe, the United States has held staunchly to the gold standard.... We have maintained the one Gibraltar of stability in the world." — **Hoover**

- **November 1932**: Roosevelt defeated Hoover in landslide; four-month lame-duck paralysis
- **Bernard Baruch**: "If you start talking about [devaluation] you would not have a nickel's worth of gold in the Reserve System"

### Pre-Inauguration Panic

- February 1933: $160M gold left; first four days of March: another $160M
- **Professor Dewing** (Harvard Business School): withdrew account in gold coin → crowd stormed bank
- Called on carpet for "unpatriotic behavior"; left faculty

> "The System was demoralized... The leadership which an independent central banking system was supposed to give was conspicuous by its absence." — **Friedman and Schwartz**

### Roosevelt's Gold Seizure and Devaluation

- **March 9, 1933**: Emergency Banking Act—authorized regulation of gold, required surrender of all gold coin/bullion
- **April 18**: power to reduce gold content of dollar; **Lewis Douglas**: "This is the end of Western civilization"
- June 5: gold clause abrogated in all contracts
- **July 3**: Roosevelt's "bombshell"—gold stabilization efforts were "old fetishes of so-called international bankers"
- **Keynes**: "Roosevelt magnificently right!" **Raymond Moley**: "Magnificently left, Keynes means"
- Daily gold price-setting in Roosevelt's bedroom each morning

> Roosevelt suggested 21-cent rise: "It's a lucky number, because it's three times seven." — **Morgenthau**

- **Norman**: "This is the most terrible thing that has happened"
- **Keynes**: dollar gyrations "like a gold standard on the booze"

### January 30, 1934: Gold Fixed at $35/oz

- 69% increase from $20.67; price prevailed for **37 years**
- 1933-1937: industrial production jumped 60%, wholesale prices 31%, unemployment 25% to 14%
- European holdouts (France, Switzerland, Netherlands) caught deflationary disease
- French prices fell 25% (1931-1935), national income dropped by a third
- **September 1936**: France broke from gold under **Tripartite Agreement** with Britain and U.S.

## Chapter 19: The Transcending Value

### Postwar Recovery Without Gold

- **1945**: Europe and Asia in ruins; only Britain, Soviet Union, China had surviving governments
- **Churchill** voted out by Labour landslide within months
- Yet within **20 years**, every vestige of war's ravages vanished
- West Germany, Soviet Union, Japan were vibrant economies competing with United States

### The Golden Avalanche (1933-1939)

- One ounce of gold in mid-1930s could buy **twice as many goods/services** as in 1929—bonanza for miners
- Asia dishoarded gold for first time in history, shipping $100M westward
- U.S. gold imports 1934-1939: **$9.6 billion**; 20% from France, even India exported
- By WWII outbreak: **60% of world's monetary gold** in U.S. (over 15,000 tons = twelve years' production)
- Atahualpa's ransom chamber: only six tons—exceeded total annual European gold output in early 1500s
- Dollar/gold relationship "like a fixed star in the heavens"
- Money "heaped up" without inflation—**"liquidity trap"**: banks and households hoarded cash, defying Hume

### Why the Migration?

- Mountain of new production raised questions about whether gold price might fall—best strategy: sell to U.S. at $35 while opportunity persisted
- More significant motivation was **political**: frightening days in Europe
- **Hitler** rejected Treaty of Versailles, sent troops into Rhineland (1935), invaded Austria (1938), Czechoslovakia (1939)—made off with gold reserves
- **Italy invaded Ethiopia (1935)**; Communist system thriving in Russia through 1930s
- U.S. alone stood ready to buy gold in unlimited amounts at fixed $35/oz

### Death of the Victorian Gold Standard

- Great Victorian gold standard died "an ugly, painful, and protracted death"
- Process reached back to WWI prohibitions on convertibility; old structure completely revived after 1918
- Remarkable how many believed they could revive system in world war had altered beyond recognition
- Easy to understand nostalgia, desire for system "whose simplicity and elegance was unmatched in history of money"
- Easy to understand fascination "that had never wavered from times of Moses and Jason and Croesus and Pizarro right up to times of Montagu Norman"
- Not easy to understand "men could make such mighty decisions on basis of obsolete visions rather than objective analysis"

> "The notion that gold would make everything come out right was upside down: gold would work only when everything was already right."

- **Disraeli's** real meaning: gold standard was **consequence, not cause**, of prosperity
- **Churchill's retrospective**: "almost come to believe that the decision to go back on gold was the greatest blunder of his life"—striking given Gallipoli

### Norman's Final Reflection (1944)

> "How we labored together, you and Ben Strong!, my partners and I, to rebuild the world after the last war—and look at the damned thing now!" — **Leffingwell**

> "As I look back, it now seems that, with all the thought and work and good intentions which we provided, we achieved absolutely nothing.... By and large nothing that I did, and very little that old Ben did... produced any good effect." — **Montagu Norman**
