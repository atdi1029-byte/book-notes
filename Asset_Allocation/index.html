Key Takeaways

  1. Asset allocation explains 91.5% of return variation. The Brinson study of 91 large pension plans found that the strategic asset allocation decision dominates results — market timing contributed 1.8% and security selection 4.6%.
  2. Two fundamental risks — inflation and volatility. Every portfolio trades one for the other. Short horizons should fear volatility; long horizons should fear inflation. Time horizon determines which risk is more dangerous.
  3. Market timing is systematically value-destroying. Sharpe showed a timer must be right ~75% of the time to match buy-and-hold. Ellis found 89 of 100 pension funds lost value from timing. Jeffrey showed missing just the seven best years out of 73 collapsed wealth from $2,351 to $202.
  4. Time transforms the nature of risk. Over 1-year periods, stocks ranged from -43% to +54%. Over 20-year rolling periods, the worst compound return was still positive. Time is the great risk reducer.
  5. Diversification is the “third dimension.” Markowitz showed that baskets must be distinctly different from each other. When asset correlations are low, portfolio volatility falls below the weighted average of component volatilities.
  6. Multiple-asset-class investing raises the efficient frontier. Adding international bonds, international stocks, REITs, and commodity-linked securities improves risk-adjusted returns even when individual standalone returns are lower.
  7. Portfolio optimization is garbage-in, garbage-out. Small changes in inputs produce dramatically different “optimal” allocations. Always use constrained optimization and run sensitivity analyses.
  8. Manage expectations before managing money. Without proper education, clients will abandon sound strategies at the worst possible moment. Frame-of-reference risk — comparing a diversified portfolio to the S&P 500 — is the greatest threat to discipline.
  9. The equity risk premium (7.4%) is compensation for volatility. Large stocks returned 11.2% vs. T-bills at 3.8% (1926–1998). This premium exists because most investors cannot stomach short-term losses.
  10. Passive rebalancing beats buy-and-hold. Rebalancing forces contrarian discipline — selling winners and buying losers. Buy-and-hold lets equity exposure drift to maximum at market tops and minimum at market bottoms.
  11. The IPS is the cornerstone of fiduciary management. A formal Investment Policy Statement prevents emotional decisions by documenting objectives, allocations, ranges, and benchmarks in advance.
  12. Think total return, not income yield. Restructuring toward bonds for income sacrifices total return and increases inflation risk. Better to spend a little principal from a higher-total-return portfolio.
  13. Money management is simple but not easy. The principles are few and clear, but the psychology of living with uncertainty makes consistent execution extremely difficult.

Contrarian Ideas

  1. Most money managers destroy value. The very talent creating market efficiency makes outperformance nearly impossible. Past outperformance gives no better than a coin flip for the future.
  2. “Never spend principal” is dangerous advice. Taken literally, it pushes clients toward low-total-return portfolios that lose purchasing power faster than occasional principal withdrawals would.
  3. Commodity-linked securities belong in portfolios. Despite lower standalone returns, the GSCI’s negative correlation with stocks (-0.35) makes it a powerful portfolio improver.
  4. The single balanced manager is obsolete. Asset allocation design and security selection are fundamentally different skills requiring different organizational structures.
  5. Paper losses are economically real. “I haven’t lost money because I haven’t sold” is self-deception. Market price is consensus value regardless of transaction.
  6. Volatility is not the enemy for long-horizon investors. The “safe” choice (T-bills, CDs) guarantees purchasing power erosion. Volatility is the price of admission to real wealth building.
  7. Ultraconservative investors holding only bonds are missing a free lunch. A portfolio of 93% bonds and 7% stocks has lower volatility and higher return than 100% bonds — adding stocks actually reduces risk.
  8. The “ideal investment” is a myth. No liquid investment alternative exists with stable guaranteed principal values that can provide real returns by consistently beating the combined impact of inflation and income taxes.

Author’s Key Proposals

  1. Build portfolios from seven asset classes: Short-term debt, domestic bonds, international bonds, domestic stocks, international stocks, real estate, and commodity-linked securities.
  2. Let time horizon drive portfolio balance. Short horizons favor interest-generating stability; long horizons favor equity growth.
  3. Educate before investing. Walk clients through historical returns, volatility ranges, the equity risk premium, and frame-of-reference risk before committing money.
  4. Formalize every relationship with an IPS. Document objectives, allocations, ranges, rebalancing rules, and benchmarks.
  5. Rebalance passively to strategic targets. Quarterly review; restore ranges when breached. This is inherently contrarian and superior to buy-and-hold.
  6. Use index funds where active managers cannot demonstrate consistent value-add.
  7. View all portfolios holistically. Include non-discretionary assets; adjust discretionary holdings to compensate for employer plans and other locked positions.
  8. Use dollar-cost averaging for implementation when clients face big-dollar anxiety — 12–18 months for stocks, 6–12 for bonds.

Relevance to Trading & Investing

  1. Asset allocation should consume the majority of analytical energy — not stock picking or market timing.
  2. Match portfolio aggressiveness to time horizon, not market outlook. The broad balance between equities and fixed income is the single most consequential decision.
  3. Low/negative correlation assets improve risk-adjusted returns even with mediocre standalone performance. The GSCI paradox proves this decisively.
  4. Systematic rebalancing is a built-in contrarian discipline: sell winners, buy losers. It prevents the drift that makes buy-and-hold vulnerable at extremes.
  5. Define benchmarks before implementation to avoid chasing performance. Use a weighted average of asset class indices, not the S&P 500 alone.
  6. Total return thinking prevents the yield trap. Distorting a portfolio toward bonds for income sacrifices long-term purchasing power.
  7. Sensitivity-test all assumptions. Fragile backtests produce fragile strategies — constrained optimization beats unconstrained elegance.
  8. Apply the hypothetical cash test to every position: “Would I buy this today at this price in this size?” If not, sell it.
  9. The 7.4% equity risk premium rewards those who can tolerate short-term volatility. The daily premium is negligible; it only materializes over years.
  10. Written plans and systematic rules beat willpower in the face of uncertainty. The IPS is the written plan.

Foreword by Sir John M. Templeton

Drawing on 49 years of professional investing, Templeton distills his core principles:

Value Investing as Foundation. Successful investing is mainly common sense — searching for the best asset value for each dollar you pay. If you buy a share for a small fraction of its intrinsic value, there is less risk of a major price decline and more opportunity for a major price increase.

Diversification. Do not put all your assets in any one type of investment, one company, one industry, or one nation. By searching across all nations, you find more bargains and reduce risk because bear markets and recessions occur at different times in different countries.

Thrift. An investor seeking to build wealth must adhere to a budget that includes a large amount of savings. During Templeton’s first 15 years after college, he saved 50 cents out of every dollar earned.

“Those who are thrifty will grow wealthy, and those who are spendthrifts will become poor.”

Dollar-Cost Averaging. Invest the same amount at regular intervals in a fluctuating investment. Your average cost will be below the average price, because your dollars buy more shares when prices are low and fewer when prices are high.

Be a Lender, Not a Borrower. If you never borrow, interest will always work for you, and you will have peace of mind through bear markets and recessions that occur in most nations about twice every ten years.

Prepare for Bear Markets — Financially and Psychologically. Experts do not know when each bear market will begin, but you can be certain there will be many during your lifetime. Financially, never be forced to sell at the wrong time. Psychologically, expect the cycles so you do not act on emotion rather than facts.

Contrarian Investing. Templeton’s founding principle:

“To buy when others are despondently selling and to sell when others are avidly buying requires the greatest fortitude and pays the greatest reward.”

If you go to 10 doctors who all agree on the proper medicine, you should take that medicine. But if 10 security analysts all agree you should buy a particular share, you must do the opposite — because if 100% are buying and even one changes his mind, you have already passed the peak.

Long Time Horizons. Judge investment advisors over at least one full market cycle, preferably several. An advisor who takes the most risk will have top performance in a bull market and the opposite in a bear market.

The Power of Starting Early. If you begin at age 25 to invest $2,000 annually in an IRA compounding tax-free at 10% annually, you will have nearly a million dollars at age 65.

Chapter 1: The Importance of Asset Allocation

Ancient Roots, Modern Practice

The Talmud (circa 1200 B.C.–500 A.D.) counseled dividing money into thirds: land, business, and reserve. Asset allocation is not new — it is approximately 2,000 years old.

How Markets Became Efficient

Historically, full-time professionals competed mostly against amateur investors and could “beat the market” through stock selection. Today, institutional trading accounts for more than 80% of exchange activity. On the New York Stock Exchange, millions of transactions occur between willing buyers and sellers — both typically institutions with nearly instantaneous access to publicly available information, staffed by well-trained analysts who have carefully evaluated the same data and reached opposite conclusions.

The irony: the same high-powered talent that creates marketplace efficiency also makes achievement of above-average returns exceedingly difficult. Research repeatedly shows that most money managers underperform the market on average over time, and those who outperform in one period do not have better than even odds of outperforming in the next.

The growth of index funds is tangible evidence of this efficiency. In an efficient market, active management with its transaction costs is expected to underperform on average over time.

Markowitz and the Birth of Modern Portfolio Theory

Harry M. Markowitz published “Portfolio Selection” in the Journal of Finance in 1952, developing the first mathematical model showing how combining investments with different return patterns reduces portfolio volatility — nearly a half century before the advent of modern computers. He was awarded the Nobel Prize in Economics in 1990.

Before Markowitz, investment management was two-dimensional: focusing on the volatility and return of individual securities. Modern portfolio theory added a third dimension — the diversification effect — evaluating a security’s impact on the overall portfolio. Optimal diversification goes beyond spreading eggs across baskets; the baskets must be distinctly different from one another.

ERISA (1974) and the prudent investor rule codified this portfolio-level thinking into law, stressing the importance of diversification within a broad portfolio context.

The Three-Step Portfolio Design Process

  1. Decide which asset classes will be represented (default: include all major classes unless sound reasons exist for exclusion)
  2. Set long-term target percentages for each asset class, based on the investor’s objectives, time horizon, and volatility tolerance
  3. Specify tactical ranges (minimum/maximum) for each asset class to allow for adjustments

Security selection (step 4) can be done either passively via index funds or actively, predicated on the belief that exploitable inefficiencies exist at the individual security level.

The Brinson Study: Asset Allocation Explains 91.5% of Returns

Gary P. Brinson, L. Randolph Hood, and Gilbert L. Beebower (1986, updated 1991) studied 91 large pension plans over 1974–1983:

FactorContribution
Asset Allocation Policy91.5%
Security Selection4.6%
Market Timing1.8%
Other Factors2.1%

Despite this, the profession remains obsessively focused on security selection and market timing, because the hope of superior results has tremendous seductive appeal.

The Difficulty of Identifying Skill vs. Luck

If 1,000 managers make decisions based on rolling dice: after year one, 500 will be above average; after year two, 250; by year five, roughly 30 will have outperformed every year — purely by chance. Professor Barr Rosenberg showed that several decades of outperformance are typically required to confidently attribute results to skill rather than luck.

Modern Portfolio Theory as Evolution

Gibson draws an analogy to physics: Einstein did not invalidate Newton’s laws but defined the limited context in which they were true. Similarly, modern portfolio theory does not invalidate security analysis; it prescribes limits within which it can add value.

“The goal shifts from beating the market to devising long-term strategies that move investors to their goals with the least amount of risk.”

Chapter 2: Historical Review of Capital Market Investment Performance

“Hindsight is always 20-20.” — Billy Wilder

Managing Expectations Before Managing Money

Gibson’s cardinal rule: first manage the client’s expectations, then manage their money. The “ideal investment” is a myth — there are no liquid investment alternatives with stable guaranteed principal values which can provide real returns by consistently beating the combined impact of inflation and income taxes.

Inflation (CPI-U): 3.1% Compound Annual Rate

A $1 breakfast in 1925 cost $9.14 by 1998 — a ninefold increase. Key periods:

  • 1925–1932: Deflationary, averaging -4.7% per year
  • Post-WWII: Unlike prior wars, no postwar deflation occurred; inflation persisted at 2.8% compound annual rate
  • Mid-1960s through 1981: Inflation compounded at ~7% annually, doubling cost of living each decade
  • 1982–1998: Returned to a modest 3.3% compound annual rate

Persistent post-WWII inflation was a worldwide phenomenon; governments are primary beneficiaries because they tax nominal rather than real income, shifting wealth from the private to the public sector.

Treasury Bills: 3.8% Compound Annual Return

$1 invested at end of 1925 grew to $14.94 by end of 1998. The real (inflation-adjusted) return: approximately 0.7% per year. After income taxes, T-bill returns lagged considerably behind inflation.

The “Safe Haven” Illusion. The 50-year-old widow with $1 million in CDs at 5% with 4% inflation: even without touching a penny of principal, after 10 years her $50,000 income buys only $33,778 of goods. After 30 years, her purchasing power drops to $306,319 and income buys only $15,416 of goods.

“Many investors who fear equity risk seek what they perceive to be a safe haven in short-term interest-generating investments. Investors are often not sufficiently sensitive to inflation because of the time-lag way it takes its toll.”

Nominal vs. Real Returns. During 1979–1981, T-bills returned 12.1%, but inflation was 11.5%. A 30% tax bracket investor earned 8.5% after tax — producing a real after-tax loss. During 1982–1984, T-bills fell to 9.7% with inflation at only 3.9%, producing a positive real after-tax return. The lower nominal returns were actually far better in real terms.

Bonds: Key Concepts

Key terms: par value (face amount), coupon rate (stated interest rate), yield to maturity (internal rate of return), current yield (annual interest / market price). The term “fixed income” is misleading — only the maximum payment is fixed.

The Inverse Relationship. Bond prices move inversely to interest rates. If you buy a 7% coupon bond and rates rise to 8%, your bond’s price must fall until its total return matches new market rates.

Three Dimensions of Bond Risk:

  1. Interest-rate-risk sensitivity: longer maturity = more price fluctuation; gives rise to the normal upward-sloping yield curve
  2. Creditworthiness: from default-free government bonds to varying degrees of corporate credit risk
  3. Tax status: municipal bonds may be free from federal income taxes

Long-Term Government Bonds: 5.3% Compound Annual Return

$1 invested grew to $44.18 by 1998. Three distinct periods:

  1. 1926–1945 (Deflation/Low Inflation): Bonds excelled. Real returns were nearly identical to nominal.
  2. 1946–1981 (Prolonged Bear Market for Bonds): Bond compound annual return was only 2% — less than half of T-bills’ 4.1%. Capital appreciation index fell from $1.42 to a 73-year low of $0.48 — a cumulative capital loss of more than two-thirds.
  3. 1982–1998 (The Great Bond Rally): In 1982 alone, government bonds returned 40%.

Intermediate-Term Government Bonds: 5.3% Compound Annual Return

Surprisingly, intermediate-term bonds matched long-term bonds’ compound return ($43.93 vs. $44.18) with far less volatility — negative returns in only 7 of 73 years vs. 20 for long-term bonds. The lesson: longer maturity does not always mean higher total return.

Large Company Stocks (S&P 500): 11.2% Compound Annual Return

$1 invested at end of 1925 grew to $2,350.89 by end of 1998. Large stocks returned only 7.4% more than T-bills, yet produced over 157 times the accumulated wealth over 73 years — the miracle of compound interest.

Dividend Income Stability. The standard deviation of stock income returns is only 1.4%, vs. 19.6% for capital appreciation — virtually all of stock volatility comes from price movements, not dividend changes.

“Had the 50-year-old widow allocated a portion of her money to common stocks, her portfolio would not have been so susceptible to the devastating impact of inflation.”

Stocks vs. Bonds: The Forward-Looking Case. With common stocks, anticipated inflation is priced into expected returns, and the portfolio can maintain its purchasing power while generating a growing dividend stream. By contrast, bonds promise only the return of principal in nominal terms — you also have the assurance that your principal will lose to inflation.

Small Company Stocks: 12.4% Compound Annual Return

$1 grew to $5,116.65. The small stock premium of 1.2% over large stocks reflects higher volatility (33.8% vs. 20.3%), higher betas, and growth potential. The premium has a serial correlation of 0.39, indicating prolonged periods when one category outperforms the other.

Bond Duration

Maturity is an inadequate measure of interest-rate sensitivity. Duration — the present-value-weighted average time until all cash flows are received — is superior. A zero-coupon bond’s duration equals its maturity. Positive convexity means bonds gain more from yield decreases than they lose from equal yield increases.

The Miracle of Compound Interest

Small incremental differences produce enormous differences in wealth: T-bills (3.8%) turned $1 into $14.94; large stocks (11.2%, just 7.4% more) turned $1 into $2,350.89; small stocks (12.4%, just 1.2% more than large) turned $1 into $5,116.65.

Geometric Mean vs. Arithmetic Mean

A stock rises 25% in year one ($100 to $125), then falls 20% in year two ($125 back to $100). The arithmetic mean is +2.5%, but the geometric mean is 0%. The arithmetic mean is always greater than or equal to the geometric mean; the gap widens with greater variability. Use geometric means for multi-period wealth accumulation; use arithmetic means for single-period expected return estimates.

Standard Deviation, Normal Distribution, and Serial Correlation

68% of outcomes fall within one standard deviation of the mean; 95% within two standard deviations. Serial correlation near +1.0 indicates a trend; near 0 indicates a random walk. Key serial correlations: T-bill returns (0.92 — strong trend), stock total returns (0.01 — random walk), long-term bond total returns (-0.01 — random walk).

Chapter 4: Market Timing

“The evidence on investment managers’ success with market timing is impressive — and overwhelmingly negative.” — Charles D. Ellis
“He that cannot abide a bad market, deserves not a good one.” — John Ray (1678)

The Impossible Dream

A perfect market timer switching annually among T-bills, corporate bonds, large stocks, and small stocks from 1926 through 1998 would have turned $1 into over $20 million. Had $1 million been invested, the result would exceed $20 trillion — more than the $13 trillion market value of all publicly traded U.S. stocks. Our 1925 millionaire would now own all of corporate America. Such timing ability does not exist.

Why Random Markets Look Predictable

A random series of numbers does not always look random. Flip a coin 100 times and you will notice occasional runs of heads or tails, but each flip is independent. Prolonged bull and bear markets are not predictable trends — they are random sequences that look patterned in hindsight.

Efficient markets incorporate into current prices all relevant known information plus consensus expectations. What moves the market is information that was not previously known — surprises, which are random events by definition.

The Statistical Case Against Timing

Trinity Investment Management studied ten post-WWII cycles: bull markets last nearly three times as long as bear markets; within bear markets, 3–4 months out of 10 are still up months. In bull markets, just 8 months (out of 41) accounted for more than 60% of the total gain.

William Sharpe found that a market timer must be right roughly three out of four times merely to match a buy-and-hold competitor.

Robert Jeffrey showed that if a stock investor missed just the seven best years out of 73 (1926–1998), a $1 investment declined from $2,351 to just $202:

“The superior returns available from common stocks do not accrue in a uniform manner. Rather, they can be traced to a few periods of sudden bursts of strength… these positive surges often occur when pessimism is running high.”

Charles Ellis cited a study of 100 large pension funds: while all had attempted market timing, not one improved its return. 89 out of 100 lost, averaging a 4.5% loss over five years.

The 1990–1991 Example

At the end of 1990, stocks had posted a loss, recession was upon us, and the Persian Gulf War loomed. Many argued it was a good time to be out of stocks. Gibson urged clients to remain invested — not because he predicted a rally, but because it is impossible to know when the particularly good periods will occur. 1991 posted a 30.55% advance. Those who fled to cash missed it entirely.

The Paradox of Timing

If market timing actually worked, investors buying before rises and selling before declines would smooth out volatility. But when common stock volatility disappears, so does the reward for bearing it. The market timer’s worldview implies money is made in stocks despite volatility. Gibson’s worldview: money is made because of volatility.

“A plausible impossibility is always preferable to an unconvincing possibility.” — Aristotle
“In investment management, the real opportunity to achieve superior results is not in scrambling to outperform the market, but in establishing and adhering to appropriate investment policies over the long term — policies that position the portfolio to benefit from riding with the main long-term forces in the market.” — Charles D. Chapter 5: Time Horizon

“Time is Archimedes’ lever in investing.” — Charles D. Ellis
“He who wishes to be rich in a day will be hanged in a year.” — Leonardo da Vinci

Two Categories of Investments

All investments divide naturally into two groups:

Interest-Generating (Loaner)Equity (Owner)
ExamplesT-bills, intermediate bonds, long-term bondsLarge stocks, small stocks
Primary advantagePredictable cash flows; specified paymentsLong-term real capital growth; no upper limit
Major disadvantageSusceptible to inflation; no growthHigh short-run volatility; no promised return
Appropriate forShort time horizonsLong time horizons

A low return is the price paid by the loaner who wants a more predictable outcome. Short-run volatility is the price paid by the owner who wants long-term capital growth.

Why Investors Over-Fear Volatility

  1. Immediacy: The stock market can drop 20% in a single day; the worst annual inflation was 13%. Volatility inflicts visible, dramatic damage; inflation is insidious.
  2. Nominal thinking: Investors fondly remember “the good old days” of 10–11% T-bill returns, not recognizing that 12–13% inflation produced negative real returns.
  3. Permanence illusion: Investors who sold near the 1973–74 bottom and said “common stocks? — never again” missed one of the century’s best bull markets.

The Economics of Volatility Aversion

Declining marginal utility of wealth: the additional dollar gained from a good outcome is not as valuable as the dollar lost from a bad outcome. Rational investors demand higher expected returns from more volatile assets. The buying and selling activities of investors cause stocks to be priced to provide higher expected returns as compensation for bearing equity volatility.

Time Transforms Volatility from Enemy to Friend

Large company stocks outperformed T-bills:

  • 64% of individual years (47 of 73)
  • 84% of rolling 5-year periods
  • 94% of rolling 10-year periods
  • 100% of rolling 20-year periods
“The volatility of common stocks is undoubtedly an enemy in the short run, but it is the basis for their higher expected returns. Time transforms this short-run enemy into a friend for the long-term investor.”

The Daily Perspective

The 7.4% annual equity risk premium allocated across 365 days is negligible. On any given day, stocks have essentially a 50/50 chance of beating T-bills. The standard deviation of 20.3% completely swamps the equity risk premium in the short run. Short-term stock performance is meaningless noise for a long-term investor.

Time horizon is the key variable in determining the appropriate balance of interest-generating versus equity investments.

Chapter 6: A Model for Determining Broad Portfolio Balance

“Everything should be made as simple as possible, but not simpler.” — Albert Einstein

Five Key Areas of Client Assessment

  1. Specific Goals. “Early retirement” or “college for the kids” must be quantified: at what age, what lifestyle, what income level?
  2. Investment Objectives. Goals are often more ambitious than realistically achievable. Human desires tend to exceed available resources.
  3. Investment Knowledge. A client who says she “knows about” stocks may not understand them sufficiently for informed decisions.
  4. Risks. Clients are more accurately described as loss-averse rather than risk-averse — year-to-year variability may not trouble them as long as there are no negative returns.
  5. Volatility Tolerance. This is not a fixed characteristic like eye color. It can be modified through education. The informed modification of volatility tolerance is one of the advisor’s major responsibilities.
“A physician will not recommend a course of treatment based on the patient’s familiarity with various prescription drugs.”

For every 1% increase in modeled return, volatility increases by approximately +/-2.9%. The incremental volatility rises much faster than the incremental return.

Key Insights from the Model

  • Short-term performance is noise. The 40.6 percentage points of relative volatility dwarf the 7.4% reward. A quarterly report does not measure return so much as it measures volatility.
  • Portfolio-balance decisions are nondelegatable (Ellis). The client must make this choice; the advisor provides the framework.
  • Volatility tolerance evolves. This exercise should be repeated periodically as life events alter tolerance.
  • Focus on volatility tolerance, not return requirement. If the return at maximum tolerable volatility is insufficient, modify goals — do not distort the portfolio.

The balance chosen between interest-generating and equity investments is the most important investment decision the client will make.

Chapter 7: Diversification: The Third Dimension

“Better a steady dime than a rare dollar.”

Beyond Naive Diversification

Three scenarios illustrate the diversification effect:

  • Perfect positive correlation (+1.0): Combining merely averages volatilities. No diversification effect exists.
  • Perfect negative correlation (-1.0): Can completely eliminate portfolio volatility while retaining a weighted-average return. Does not exist in reality.
  • Middle-ground correlation: The real world. Portfolio volatility falls below the weighted average of its components’ volatilities.

The key insight: portfolio return is always a weighted average of component returns, regardless of correlation. But portfolio volatility is less than the weighted average in all cases except perfect positive correlation.

The Minimum Volatility Portfolio

Using 1926–1998 data (stocks: 13.2% return, 20.3% std dev; corporate bonds: 6.1%, 8.6%; correlation +0.26): the minimum volatility portfolio is 93% bonds and 7% stocks, with lower volatility (8.5%) and higher return (6.2%) than 100% bonds. Ultraconservative investors holding only bonds are missing a free lunch.

Variability in Historical Relationships

Rolling 60-month analyses reveal sobering instability: stock/bond correlation varied from +0.62 to -0.28 over 1926–1998 despite the overall figure of +0.26. This variability is a critical warning about the uncertainty inherent in optimization inputs.

The Efficient Frontier

Efficient portfolios maximize return for a given volatility or minimize volatility for a given return. Inefficient portfolios carry unnecessary volatility without compensation. More investment alternatives expand the efficient frontier upward.

Diversification as Return Enhancement

Although usually framed as volatility reduction, diversification equally serves as return enhancement. The smoothing effect of partially offsetting returns allows more aggressive investors to commit a greater percentage to equity investments than would otherwise be possible.

Diversifiable vs. Nondiversifiable Volatility

Gibson’s firefighter analogy: a firefighter deserves compensation for the unavoidable risks of the job (nondiversifiable volatility), but not for removing his protective suit before entering a burning building (diversifiable volatility).

“One big lesson of modern portfolio theory is that in an efficient market, there is no compensation for bearing volatility that can be easily avoided.”

The Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM)

Developed by William F. Sharpe in the mid-1960s: R_i = R_f + (R_m - R_f) x Beta_i. Beta measures nondiversifiable volatility relative to the market portfolio. The Security Market Line plots the upward-sloping relationship between nondiversifiable risk and expected return.

Chapter 8: The Rewards of Multiple-Asset-Class Investing

“My ventures are not in one bottom trusted, / Nor to one place; nor is my whole estate / Upon the fortune of this present year.” — Shakespeare, Merchant of Venice

The Case for Global Diversification

As of 1998, non-U.S. capital markets were as large as U.S. markets. Gibson’s thought experiment: two identical investment firms, one restricted to U.S. stocks and bonds, one investing globally. The global firm’s worst case is matching the domestic firm; its best case is finding attractive opportunities unavailable domestically.

International Bonds and Stocks

Rolling 20-year analysis shows adding 10–30% international bonds improved returns and reduced volatility in almost every period. Using the EAFE Index, in 8 of 10 rolling 20-year periods, international stock diversification improved portfolio returns.

“In essence, every international investment is two investments: one in the security itself and the other in the native foreign currency.”

The Blindfolded Portfolio Exercise

Gibson’s most compelling demonstration uses four equity asset classes (1972–1997): S&P 500, EAFE Index, NAREIT Equity Index, and Goldman Sachs Commodity Index. Fifteen portfolios (all combinations, equally weighted) are plotted. When audiences choose blindly, they consistently prefer more diversified portfolios.

Portfolio TypeAvg Compound ReturnAvg Std DevAvg Sharpe Ratio
Four-asset-class14.38%10.30%0.71
Three-asset-class14.23%11.55%0.66

The four-asset-class portfolio had 1.2 percentage points higher return with 47% less volatility than the average single-asset-class portfolio. Its Sharpe ratio was nearly twice the average of its components.

The GSCI Paradox

The Goldman Sachs Commodity Index had the lowest return and highest volatility of any single asset class, yet every one of the 5 highest-returning portfolios and 7 least-volatile portfolios included GSCI as a component — because its dissimilar pattern of returns produced the strongest diversification effect.

The REITs Paradox

Equity REITs had the highest return with least volatility of any single asset class (1972–1997). Yet a portfolio of the other three classes generated a higher return with approximately 50% less volatility than REITs alone.

Downside Risk Reduction

Every single-asset class had at least one year with losses worse than -20%. The four-asset-class portfolio’s worst year was a modest loss of -7.63% (1974), with fewer years of negative returns than any single-asset class.

Why Isn’t Everyone Doing It?

  1. Lack of awareness. Investors believe diversification impairs returns.
  2. Market timing illusion. A perfect timer (1984–1998) would have earned 32.19% annually. Among nearly 600 funds, not one came remotely close.
  3. Frame-of-reference bias. U.S. investors compare results to the S&P 500.
“I would rather follow an inferior strategy that wins when my friends are winning than a superior strategy that at times results in my losing when my friends are winning.”

The multiple-asset-class strategy is a tortoise-and-hare story. The GSCI rabbit led in the 1970s. The EAFE rabbit ran fastest in the 1980s. The S&P 500 rabbit outpaced all others in the 1990s. Yet the tortoise, in the long run, leaves the pack behind.

Chapter 9: Portfolio Optimization

“All business proceeds on beliefs, on judgments of probabilities and not on certainties.” — Charles W. Eliot

How Optimization Works

An efficient portfolio is one where each asset’s volatility-adjusted incremental contribution to portfolio expected return is equal. The iterative process eventually reaches the efficient frontier — analogous to standing atop a mountain where any direction you walk causes you to lose altitude.

Required Inputs and Their Dangers

Optimization programs require estimates of expected returns, standard deviations, and cross-correlations. Gibson’s stern warning:

“Garbage in, garbage out!”

Historical returns are particularly unreliable as inputs. For rolling 20-year periods ending 1979–1988, large company stocks barely outperformed T-bills by 2 percentage points.

Sensitivity Analysis: Small Changes, Radical Shifts

Five optimizations for the same client demonstrate alarming sensitivity:

  • Optimization 1 (unconstrained, 9 asset classes): Over half goes to real estate — driven by suspiciously low appraisal-based volatility statistics
  • Optimization 3 (REIT return reduced by just 1%): REIT allocation plummets from 21.0% to 5.9%
  • Optimization 5 (perfect REIT/small-cap correlation assumed): REIT jumps to 37.6% with complete elimination of small company stocks

Gibson’s conclusion: attempting to specify inputs with greater precision is futile. Instead, constrain the optimizer.

The Constrained Optimization Solution

By requiring minimum (5%) and maximum (25%) allocations for each asset class and a core 36% in corporate bonds/large company stocks, the constrained portfolio sacrificed only 0.4% in expected return with 0.6% more volatility — but produced a much more broadly diversified and sensible allocation.

Hindsight Test: 1995–1998

Large company stocks — completely excluded by the optimizer — earned a 30.5% compound annual return. Precious metals — allocated 12% by the optimizer — compounded losses at -17.40%. Computer programs cannot eliminate market uncertainty; they are not reliable fortune tellers.

The Nonlinear Nature of Portfolio Improvement

The relationship between asset allocation and portfolio return is nonlinear — steeply positive at first, then flat near the optimum. Moving from 0% to 15% in real estate captures most of the improvement; moving from 15% to 30% adds much less. This removes anxiety about finding the exact optimal allocation — being close is nearly as good.

“Psychologically, it is easy to believe that a more precise answer is more certain. An advisor who recommends ‘exactly 19.3% in international stocks’ may inspire more confidence than one who says ‘about 20%’ — even though there is no real difference.”

Chapter 10: Know Your Client

“The beginning is the most important part of the work.” — Plato

The 10-Step Money Management Process

  • Steps 1–2: Know your client (gather data, identify needs/constraints)
  • Steps 3–6: Manage client expectations (communicate philosophy, develop frame of reference, set objectives, determine diversification)
  • Steps 7–10: Manage the money (allocate assets, write IPS, implement, report/revise)

The critical point: money is not actually invested until Step 9. Rushing through the first six steps is the most common and costly mistake.

Step 1: Gather Client Data

The advisor should personally handle data gathering — psychological bonding begins here. Organize assets into four classifications: interest-generating investments, equity investments, lifestyle assets, and liabilities. Current portfolio composition reveals present volatility tolerance.

Step 2: Identify Needs, Constraints, and Circumstances

  • Liquidity: Non-liquid investments must be sized relative to possible future withdrawals
  • Portfolio cash withdrawal rate: Portfolios should not be designed to intentionally produce a specific yield. The broad portfolio balance should reflect the time horizon, not income needs.
  • Tax situation: Determines municipal bond advisability, timing of asset sales, IRA positioning
  • Time horizon: The crucial variable. Clients systematically underestimate their horizons by assuming retirement defines the endpoint. Inflation remains a threat throughout retirement.
  • Psychological factors: Preferences based on misconceptions require education, not accommodation

Chapter 11: Managing Client Expectations

“There is no free lunch.”

The Four Quadrants of Investment Worldview

Gibson’s 2x2 matrix based on two questions — “Is successful market timing possible?” and “Is superior security selection possible?”:

  • Quadrant 1 (Yes/Yes): Concentrate in the best asset class, pick the best securities. “Easy to sell, hard to deliver.”
  • Quadrant 2 (No/Yes): Diversify across asset classes but pick winning securities within each. The majority of professionals hold this view.
  • Quadrant 3 (Yes/No): Time asset class moves but use index funds. Fewest proponents.
  • Quadrant 4 (No/No): Markets are efficient. Diversify broadly using index funds. “Hard to sell, easy to deliver.”

The Trend Toward Quadrant 4

The more vigorously money managers compete to beat the market, the more quickly quadrant 4 becomes reality. In an efficient market without transaction costs, trading is a zero-sum game. Add real-world costs and fees, and it becomes a negative-sum game.

“The way to win a negative-sum game is not to play it.”
“If we think of the trend line of an asset class’s growth path as the signal and the random fluctuations of security prices around that growth path as the noise, quadrant 1, 2, and 3 investors believe the key to investment success lies in the noise, whereas quadrant 4 investors believe it lies in the signal.”

Gibson identifies a conspiracy against the quadrant 4 worldview: investors don’t want to accept that no one can protect them from market realities; the money management profession sees its existence threatened; and the financial press would have much less to write about.

“A cord of three strands is not easily broken.” — Ecclesiastes

Step 4: Develop a Frame of Reference

An advisor who educates clients about capital market behavior empowers them to make wise decisions with greater equanimity. The constant they can rely on: people will always prefer stability over unpredictability, ensuring that volatile assets will always be priced to deliver higher expected returns.

Step 5: Develop an Investment Objective

Gibson rejects labels like “aggressive growth.” He words objectives to acknowledge the return/volatility trade-off: (1) low volatility with low return, (2) medium return with medium volatility, (3) high return with high volatility. Clients should choose near the upper end of their volatility-tolerance range while ensuring they will remain committed through extremes.

Step 6: Determine Breadth and Degree of Diversification

As diversification increases, frame-of-reference risk also increases — the pattern of returns diverges further from the S&P 500. During 1973–74, the diversified portfolio lost only 7.63% in its worst year versus the S&P 500’s -26.47%. During 1995–98, the S&P 500’s dominance made diversification feel painful.

A Japanese investor would have been delighted with the exact same diversified results that frustrated American investors. Same results, same time period — different frame of reference.

Chapter 12: Money Management

“Don’t fight forces; use them.” — R. Buckminster Fuller

The Central Thesis

Much of the improvement from diversification flows from the simple policy decision to diversify rather than from superior skill. Strategy should be grounded in realistic capital market expectations, not reliance on skill that is inherently difficult to identify.

Step 7: Asset Allocation and Portfolio Design

Seven asset classes: short-term debt, domestic bonds, international bonds, domestic stocks, international stocks, real estate investments, and investment hedges (commodity-linked securities, precious metals).

Index Funds vs. Active Management

“Indexers always win. In any financial market, indexers owning all securities at low cost must provide better returns than active investors in the aggregate.” — John C. Bogle

Step 8: The Investment Policy Statement

The IPS is the most important document in the fiduciary process. Its advantages: supports disciplined execution, provides continuity, defends against Monday morning quarterbacking, and evidences proper fiduciary stewardship. Litmus test: An unfamiliar advisor should be able to implement the strategy from the IPS alone.

Step 9: Implementation

Dollar-cost averaging is recommended for transitioning to a new portfolio — 12–18 months for stocks, 6–12 for bonds.

IRA positioning strategy: Design the target portfolio first, ignoring the IRA. Then place investments generating the highest taxable income per dollar invested inside the IRA. Counterintuitively, this often means actively traded stock funds (with high capital gain distributions) go into the IRA.

Holistic portfolio management: Always consider all assets including non-discretionary employer plans. A client with 40% of wealth in an employer plan invested entirely in short-term debt will need a much more aggressive allocation of discretionary funds.

Step 10: Performance Reporting and Portfolio Revision

The S&P 500 is not a suitable benchmark for a diversified portfolio — use a weighted average of asset class index returns instead.

Passive rebalancing is fundamentally different from buy-and-hold. Buy-and-hold has serious flaws: equity commitment reaches maximum just as bear markets begin, and minimum as bull markets get underway. Passive rebalancing:

  1. Easy to understand and implement
  2. Maintains constant portfolio volatility
  3. Preserves balanced diversification
  4. Inherently contrarian — sells recent outperformers, buys relative underperformers
  5. Enforces discipline: sells stocks during bull markets, buys during bear markets

Dynamic asset allocation sells as prices decline for downside protection, buys as prices advance — characterized as “sell low, buy high.” Tactical asset allocation attempts to “buy low, sell high” through forecasting. Gibson cautions that without superior predictive ability, active approaches easily underperform passive strategies.

Chapter 13: Resolving Problems Encountered During Implementation

Client Inertia and Concentrated Positions

A revealing advisor-client dialogue:

Advisor: “You have a very large percentage of your portfolio in XYZ stock.”
Client: “Yes, I bought it at $10 per share and now it’s worth $85. Why should I get off a winning horse?”
Advisor: “Assume you don’t own the stock but have cash equal to its current value. Would you buy that much XYZ at $85 per share?”
Client: “No. I would never purchase a position that big at today’s price!”

The long pause reveals the client’s anchor to historical cost. Historical cost has nothing to do with whether a stock will continue to perform well from its current price.

The Paper Loss Syndrome

Client: “ABC stock has been rotten — I bought it at $50 and it’s now $8. But if I sell, I’ll lose money.”
Advisor: “You have already lost the money.”

What the client is really resisting is the admission of having made an investment that did not work out. Once sold at a loss, the verdict is unavoidable. But holding on preserves the hope of breaking even.

“Hold in your portfolio only those investments that you would actively repurchase if you did not already own them. Sell everything else.”

Tax Complications with Appreciated Assets

  • Carry appreciated investments on the balance sheet at after-tax value
  • Consider income yield impact: a stock bought at $10 now at $85 with a $3.60 dividend yields 4.2% on market value, but after a 30% capital gains tax on sale, reinvested proceeds would need an 8% yield to match
  • Stepped-up tax basis at death eliminates the capital gains tax entirely — terminally ill investors should generally retain appreciated positions

Total Return vs. Income Yield

A properly balanced 50/50 bond/stock portfolio with 9% total return (4% yield + 5% appreciation) far outperforms a 100% bond portfolio with 6% total return — even though the stock portfolio requires small principal invasions to meet current expenses.

Resistance to Specific Recommendations

When a client rejects a specific investment, dig deeper. Often the real objection is not to the specific fund but to the asset class itself. The portfolio design process should proceed hierarchically: broad balance first, then asset allocation, then specific positions. If an objection surfaces at the wrong level, withdraw the recommendation and return to the appropriate decision point.

Big Dollar Decisions

Expressing recommendations as percentages of total portfolio helps: “3% of your $1 million portfolio in commodity-linked securities” sounds far less dramatic than “$30,000 in commodities.”

Conclusion

“Money management is simple but not easy.”

Simple Because…

The principles are few and easy to understand. Two risks dominate: inflation and volatility. Avoiding one exposes you to the other. Time horizon determines which is more dangerous. Broad diversification benefits all clients regardless of volatility tolerance.

Because money management is simple, every client can be meaningfully involved in the decision-making process.

Not Easy Because…

Uncertainty cannot be eliminated. Gibson tells the Mount Fuji story: visitors traveled four hours to see the mountain but found it shrouded in fog. No matter how carefully they focused their cameras, they could only get a sharper picture of the fog.

“So it is with money management. All we can hope to accomplish is to get a better picture of the uncertainties — we cannot eliminate them.”
“The window to the future is opaque.”

For the client with a long time horizon, volatility is rewarded by higher expected equity returns. There is a difference between intellectually understanding something and living with it on a day-to-day basis. Successful investing will always be as much a psychological process as a money management endeavor.

The Las Vegas Casino Analogy

Imagine standing amid slot machines in Las Vegas. Jackpots are dropping coins noisily into metal trays. Although some players are ahead, on average the flow of money runs from the players’ pockets to the casino.

The day-to-day investment environment sends the same misleading signals: a new market-timing guru, an investment idea that seems superior to disciplined diversification. Without a very firm commitment to long-term investment policies, clients end up collecting portfolio positions the way they collect shells at the beach.

The Advisor’s Role

In a world trending toward efficient markets, the advisor’s role shifts from security selector to educator, architect, and general contractor:

  • Educator: Teaching clients the principles of capital markets
  • Architect: Designing broadly diversified strategies in collaboration with clients
  • General contractor: Implementing, monitoring, and providing perspective through extreme conditions
“Clients do not need sporadic investment suggestions. They need and will value the comprehensive management of their portfolios by knowledgeable investment professionals committed to their financial well-being.”
