Key Takeaways

  1. Global Liquidity is the dominant force in modern finance. The pool of Global Liquidity (~US$135 trillion, roughly 2× World GDP) drives asset prices, exchange rates, and economic activity—far more than interest rates, inflation, or traditional money supply measures.
  2. We live in a refinancing economy, not a new financing economy. Modern financial markets serve primarily to roll over existing debts rather than fund new investment. This makes balance sheet capacity (liquidity) more critical than the cost of capital (interest rates).
  3. China has surpassed America in liquidity creation. China's share of Global Liquidity jumped from 5.9% in 2000 to 27.5% by 2019, overtaking America's declining 22.5%. This shift represents the most profound change in global finance in decades.
  4. The quality of liquidity matters as much as quantity. Private sector liquidity (generated by productive cash flows) strengthens currencies and supports sustainable growth. Central Bank liquidity (printed money) often signals distress and weakens currencies. The mix predicts exchange rate movements 6-12 months ahead.
  5. Safe assets are chronically scarce. The post-2008 regulatory regime combined with fiscal austerity created an acute shortage of high-quality collateral. This scarcity amplifies liquidity cycles, increases volatility, and explains why quantitative easing often raises (not lowers) bond yields.
  6. Central Banks have three distinct transmission channels. (1) Liquidity provision (enables trading), (2) Maturity transformation (duration risk), and (3) Credit enhancement (credit risk). Each US$1 trillion in Fed maturity transformation reduces Treasury term premia by ~142 basis points, while liquidity provision paradoxically increases premia by ~56 basis points.
  7. The 1989 Berlin Wall collapse reversed global financial polarity. Pre-1989, liquidity flowed from emerging economies to developed markets. Post-1989, the Financial Silk Road reversed—developed market capital now chases emerging market growth, creating procyclical boom-bust dynamics.
  8. The US dollar plays a paradoxical dual role. As both the primary reserve currency and the currency of a current account deficit nation, the dollar creates systemic fragility. Weaker dollars fuel global liquidity booms; stronger dollars trigger emerging market crises.
  9. Exchange rate regimes determine where liquidity shocks appear. Policy-makers can choose between asset price inflation or nominal exchange rate adjustment—but not both. Export-oriented economies (China, Japan, Germany) stabilize currencies and accept asset bubbles; financialized economies (US, UK) stabilize asset prices and accept currency weakness.
  10. Shadow banking and wholesale finance have replaced traditional banking. The explosion of repos, derivatives, CICPs (complex, information-constrained, path-dependent securities), and offshore dollar markets means traditional monetary aggregates (M2, M3) have become obsolete. Global Liquidity must be tracked through comprehensive flow-of-funds analysis.
  11. Digital yuan threatens dollar hegemony. China's strategy to internationalize the yuan through digital currency, Belt and Road lending, and trade invoicing represents an existentialist threat to the US dollar-based system. The coming Capital War will be fought over safe asset status.
  12. Financial crises are liquidity events, not solvency events. Every major crisis since 1980—including the 2008 Global Financial Crisis—was fundamentally a dollar liquidity scramble, not an insolvency problem. The Federal Reserve's emergency swap lines with other central banks were the critical stabilization mechanism.
  13. The Global Liquidity Indexes (GLI) have remarkable predictive power. CrossBorder Capital's GLI measures lead bond markets by 3-6 months, equity markets by 6-12 months, and real economies by 15-20 months. They are Granger-causal for asset prices and economic data worldwide.
  14. Policy-makers systematically misunderstand the financial system. Central bankers remain trapped in outdated frameworks focused on traditional bank deposits and interest rates, blind to the dominance of shadow banking, cross-border flows, and collateral chains that actually drive liquidity conditions.
  15. The next regime will likely feature regionalism over globalization. As the US-China rivalry intensifies and capital controls proliferate, expect fragmentation into currency blocs, reduced cross-border flows, and the rise of digital state-backed currencies enabling unprecedented financial surveillance.

Contrarian Ideas

  1. Quantitative easing often raises bond yields, not lowers them. Empirical evidence shows US QE periods associated with rising term premia (average +134bp per phase) because safe asset scarcity intensifies hoarding demand. Academic consensus (Gagnon 2016: −67bp per 10% of GDP) is empirically wrong.
  2. The yield curve is a terrible recession predictor. The standard 10-year minus 2-year slope is "at best a flaky predictor." What matters is the combination of slope AND curvature, and term premia (a liquidity phenomenon) are a key component explaining curvature.
  3. Interest rates don't matter much anymore. In a refinancing-dominated system, balance sheet capacity (can lenders roll over existing debts?) trumps the cost of capital. Zero interest rates are irrelevant if no one has balance sheet space to lend.
  4. Money supply measures (M2, M3) are obsolete. Traditional aggregates focused on deposit-taking institutions miss the dominant sources of liquidity—shadow banks, cross-border flows, collateral chains, and CICPs. Flow-of-funds analysis is the only valid approach.
  5. Central Banks cause instability when they "normalize." Attempts to shrink balance sheets or raise rates after crises trigger liquidity shortages and market dysfunction—because the system has become structurally dependent on Central Bank liquidity provision.
  6. Strong dollars trigger global crises. Conventional view sees dollar strength as safe-haven demand during crises. Reality: dollar strength causes crises by draining liquidity from dollar-indebted emerging markets and commodity producers.
  7. China's financial immaturity is a strategic weakness. China's inability to provide safe assets and export yuan liquidity forces continued dollar dependence, constraining geopolitical options and making China vulnerable to US financial sanctions.
  8. Globalization increases volatility, not stability. As the world becomes more interconnected through cross-border capital flows, liquidity shocks amplify faster and spread wider. The procyclical monetary system based on USD and private credit guarantees bigger booms and busts.
  9. Austerity made the debt problem worse. Fiscal contraction reduced the supply of safe government bonds, increasing the scarcity premium and forcing private sector to manufacture flimsy substitutes (subprime securitizations), which eventually collapsed.
  10. Academic economists systematically lag financial reality. Universities claim credit for innovations developed by practitioners through trial-and-error. Policy-makers educated in outdated frameworks perpetuate destabilizing policies.

Author's Key Proposals

  1. Track Global Liquidity, not interest rates or M2. Use comprehensive flow-of-funds data covering traditional banks, central banks, shadow banks, and cross-border flows. Focus on the quality mix (private vs. Central Bank) as much as quantity.
  2. Build Global Liquidity Indexes for predictive asset allocation. CrossBorder Capital's GLI framework leads markets by months and economies by quarters—essential for macro investing and risk management.
  3. Recognize the refinancing vs. new financing distinction. Modern policy must focus on maintaining balance sheet capacity to roll over existing debts, not just lowering interest rates to encourage new borrowing.
  4. Expand the supply of genuine safe assets. Fiscal austerity is counterproductive when safe assets are scarce. Sovereigns with fiscal capacity should issue more high-quality debt to satisfy global demand.
  5. Reform Central Bank frameworks to acknowledge shadow banking. Regulation and monetary policy must adapt to the reality that wholesale funding markets and cross-border flows dominate liquidity creation.
  6. Prepare for yuan internationalization and digital currencies. The coming decades will see the rise of yuan-denominated finance and state-backed digital currencies. Investors and policymakers must position for a multi-polar currency system.
  7. Understand the US-China liquidity interdependence. "Understanding the Global Liquidity cycle may simply come down to interpreting the motives and actions of the two key Central Banks—the US Federal Reserve and the People's Bank of China."
  8. Use the forex risk index for currency forecasting. Private sector liquidity minus Central Bank liquidity, advanced 12 months, provides robust currency predictions for both developed and emerging markets.
  9. Monitor term premia and credit spreads as liquidity indicators. Term premia reflect safe asset scarcity; credit spreads reflect funding conditions. Together they reveal the state of Global Liquidity more reliably than policy rates.
  10. Accept that big Central Bank balance sheets are permanent. "Big balance sheets here to stay" (Andrew Hauser, Bank of England). The system has structurally adapted; attempts to shrink balance sheets cause dysfunction.

Relevance to Trading & Investing

  1. Asset allocation framework: Global Liquidity drives macro-valuation shifts across asset classes. The GLI leads equities by 6-12 months and bonds by 3-6 months—essential for tactical allocation.
  2. Currency forecasting: The forex risk index (private minus Central Bank liquidity, advanced 12 months) provides Granger-causal predictions for USD, EM currencies, and other major pairs.
  3. Credit market timing: The credit enhancement channel (Fed holdings of risky assets) predicts corporate spread movements. Each US$1 trillion in Fed credit holdings tightens B-Aaa spreads by ~415bp.
  4. Term structure trading: Understanding the three Central Bank channels (liquidity provision raises premia +56bp per US$1T, maturity transformation lowers premia −142bp per US$1T, credit enhancement affects corporates) enables relative value trades in the yield curve.
  5. EM exposure decisions: Track the gap between EM liquidity indexes and US liquidity index—widening gaps (advanced 12 months) predict EM currency weakness and signal time to reduce exposure (R² > 0.36).
  6. Regime identification: Distinguish between new financing regimes (liquidity fuels growth and new investment) vs. refinancing regimes (liquidity merely prevents collapse). Asset classes perform very differently across regimes.
  7. Dollar positioning: Strong dollar periods drain global liquidity and trigger EM crises—time to reduce risk. Weak dollar periods fuel global liquidity booms—time to increase EM and commodity exposure.
  8. Safe asset vs. risk asset allocation: When safe asset scarcity is acute (narrow credit spreads despite high leverage), expect sudden liquidity events. Maintain larger cash buffers and reduce leverage.
  9. Central Bank policy anticipation: "Fed watching" and increasingly "PBoC watching" are critical. But focus on balance sheet composition (the three channels) not just interest rate guidance.
  10. QE impact assessment: Contrary to academic consensus, QE often raises yields by intensifying safe asset scarcity. Track term premia decomposition: liquidity provision (positive for yields) vs. maturity transformation (negative for yields).
  11. Volatility trading: Liquidity multiplier collapse (from 14× in 2000 to 6.2× in 2019) signals chronic shortage—expect higher volatility and more frequent liquidity events. Structural long volatility positioning warranted.
  12. Long-term structural positioning: The coming multi-polar currency world and rise of digital yuan will reshape global finance. Position for regionalization, capital controls, and dollar hegemony challenges. Consider exposure to alternative reserve assets and decentralized systems.

Core Concepts: Understanding Global Liquidity

What Is Global Liquidity?

Definition: The underlying flow of funds (cash, credit, cross-border capital) circulating through the global financial system—distinct from traditional money supply measures like M2 or M3.

Size and scope: Approximately US$135 trillion (roughly 2× World GDP), tracking ~80 economies worldwide, covering:

  • Traditional commercial banks
  • Central Banks (Fed, PBoC, ECB, BoJ, BoE, etc.)
  • Shadow banks (money market funds, hedge funds, investment banks)
  • Corporations (both financial and non-financial)
  • Households
  • Cross-border flows and foreign investors

Two critical dimensions:

  1. Quantity: How much liquidity exists in total
  2. Quality: Who creates it—private sector liquidity ("good," generated by productive cash flows) vs. Central Bank liquidity ("bad," printed money often signaling distress)

Why Traditional Measures Are Obsolete

M2 and M3 focus exclusively on deposit-taking institutions and miss:

  • Repo markets: By 2007, US repo market reached US$10 trillion (equal to total commercial bank assets); intraday credit to single dealers approached US$100 billion
  • Shadow banking: Money market funds, securitization vehicles, hedge funds, structured investment vehicles (SIVs)
  • Cross-border dollar flows: Offshore Eurodollar markets operate outside Fed direct control but critically affect global liquidity
  • Collateral chains: Rehypothecation and collateral velocity create money-like instruments not captured by traditional measures
  • CICPs: Complex, Information-constrained, Path-dependent securities with opaque credit risk

Evidence of obsolescence: The liquidity multiplier collapsed from >14× in 2000 to ~6.2× in 2019—each dollar of Central Bank money creates far less liquidity because the financial plumbing has fundamentally changed.

Flow-of-Funds Analysis: The Only Valid Approach

Howell advocates comprehensive balance sheet analysis examining sources and uses of funds:

  • Core belief: Changes in sources of funds (financial flows) matter more than uses of funds (economic spending categories)
  • Double-entry discipline: "Like double-entry bookkeeping, contains built-in features that detect errors in logic... the amount of funds supplied must equal the amount demanded because it is impossible to lend money unless someone borrows it"
  • German Bundesbank endorsement: "Financial accounts add to the picture provided by the national accounts focusing on the real economy... by including transactions in the financial sphere that run in parallel with real transactions... provides information about the monetary policy transmission process"

The Three Historical Eras of Global Finance

Era 1: Pre-1989 (Bretton Woods / Gold Exchange)

  • Fixed exchange rates or managed floats
  • Capital controls widespread
  • Liquidity flowed FROM poor countries TO rich countries (capital scarcity in developed markets)
  • US dollar backed by gold (until 1971), then oil/commodity linkages
  • US current account surpluses

Era 2: 1989-2007 (Emerging Market Era / Dollar Hegemony)

  • 1989 Berlin Wall collapse: Pivotal turning point reversing financial polarity
  • Financial Silk Road: Capital now flows FROM developed markets TO emerging markets (China, Asia, commodity producers)
  • China joins WTO (2001): Massive acceleration of trade and capital flows
  • US current account deficits mirror global savings gluts
  • Massive reserve accumulation by EM central banks (especially China: US$4 trillion peak 2014)
  • Global imbalances build: China industrial exporter, America financial exporter
  • Explosion of cross-border dollar lending and Eurodollar markets

Era 3: Post-2008 (Refinancing Era / Safe Asset Scarcity)

  • 2008 Global Financial Crisis: Exposed acute dollar liquidity shortage
  • Fed emergency swap lines provide >US$580 billion to foreign central banks
  • Unprecedented Central Bank balance sheet expansion (QE programs)
  • Shift from new financing to refinancing: Financial markets serve primarily to roll over existing debts, not fund new investment
  • Safe asset scarcity: Basel III regulations + fiscal austerity removed ~US$9 trillion from safe asset supply
  • Chronic liquidity shortage despite zero/negative interest rates
  • Rise of shadow monetary base and CICPs as flimsy safe asset substitutes
  • China's liquidity share surpasses America's (27.5% vs. 22.5% by 2019)

Safe Assets: Definition and Scarcity

IMF (2012) formal definition of 'safe' asset:

  1. Low market and credit risks
  2. High market liquidity
  3. Limited inflation risks
  4. Low exchange rate risks
  5. Limited idiosyncratic risks

In practice, safe assets share several properties:

  • Negative beta: Tend to rise in value during bad economic times (flight-to-safety)
  • Deep and liquid markets: Ability to trade large size around prevailing prices without capital controls
  • Consistently lower yields: Krishnamurthy and Vissing-Jorgensen (2012) estimate US Treasury yields ~70bp lower on average due to better safety/liquidity features
  • Greater fiscal capacity: Low sensitivity of yields to increases in debt-to-GDP ratio; backing of powerful (possibly military) State

Canonical safe asset: 10-year US Treasury note

The post-GFC supply shock:

  • Sovereign downgrades removed ~US$9 trillion from safe asset pool
  • Fiscal austerity restricted new government bond issuance
  • Basel III regulations forced banks to hoard high-quality liquid assets
  • Collateral requirements increased for repos and derivatives
  • Result: Acute scarcity amplifies liquidity cycles and increases volatility

Paradoxical QE effect: When Central Banks buy safe assets (removing them from private markets), they intensify scarcity. This can RAISE term premia rather than lower them, as investors scramble to hoard remaining safe assets. Empirical evidence: US QE periods associated with average +134bp increase in term premia, contradicting academic predictions of −67bp.

The Shadow Monetary Base

The capacity of private balance sheets to create "money-like" instruments outside traditional banking:

  • Repo chains: Collateral rehypothecation creates multiple claims on same underlying asset
  • Securitization: Bundling loans into tradable securities (MBS, CDOs, CLOs)
  • Money market funds: Near-cash substitutes not counted in traditional M2
  • Commercial paper and trade credit: Short-term corporate IOUs function as money substitutes
  • Offshore dollar deposits: Eurodollar system operates parallel to Fed-regulated markets

Critical feature: The shadow monetary base depends on collateral quality and confidence. When trust evaporates (2008, March 2020), the shadow base collapses faster than Central Banks can expand the official base—causing acute liquidity crises despite "ample" bank reserves.

Refinancing vs. New Financing Economies

New financing economy:

  • Financial markets primarily fund new investment projects
  • Interest rates (cost of capital) critically affect investment decisions
  • Credit growth drives economic expansion
  • Liquidity goes toward productive capacity building

Refinancing economy (post-2008 reality):

  • Financial markets primarily roll over existing debts
  • Balance sheet capacity (ability to extend maturity) more critical than interest rates
  • Zero rates ineffective if lenders lack balance sheet space
  • Liquidity prevents collapse rather than fueling growth
  • Policy implication: Focus must shift from lowering rates to maintaining rollover capacity

Howell: "This makes the capacity of capital (balance sheet size) more important than the cost of capital (level of interest rates)."

Central Banks & Transmission Mechanisms

The Three Distinct Channels

Howell decomposes Central Bank balance sheets by functional impact, revealing three transmission channels with different (sometimes opposing) effects:

1. Liquidity Provision Channel

  • Function: Enables trade—notes, coins, check clearing, payment systems
  • Assets: Currency in circulation, repos, bank reserves
  • Effect on markets: Tightens credit spreads and bid-ask spreads; makes refinancing easier
  • Paradoxical term premium effect: INCREASES term premia by reducing Treasury liquidity premium (Treasuries lose relative scarcity value)
  • Empirical estimate: Each US$1 trillion increase in Fed liquidity provision raises average Treasury term premium by +56 basis points

2. Maturity Transformation Channel

  • Function: Long-term lending—Central Bank borrows short, lends long
  • Assets: Long-duration Treasuries and mortgage-backed securities
  • Mechanism: Removes duration risk from private markets; creates "preferred habitat" scarcity at specific maturities
  • Effect: Fed receives long-maturity returns, pays short-term bill rates → narrows yield/term spread
  • Empirical estimate: Each US$1 trillion in maturity transformation reduces average Treasury term premium by −142 basis points
  • Most powerful channel for affecting term structure

3. Credit Enhancement Channel

  • Function: Boost risk-taking—remove high credit risk securities from private markets
  • Assets: Corporate bonds, mortgage-backed securities with credit risk, asset-backed securities
  • Effect: Fed takes in credit returns, pays risk-free yields → tightens credit spreads
  • Empirical estimate: Each US$1 trillion in Fed credit holdings reduces B-Aaa corporate spread by −415 basis points
  • Target: Affects risk premia on corporate bonds, not Treasury term premia

Bernanke's "Credit Easing" vs. Pure QE

"Our approach—which could be described as 'credit easing'—resembles quantitative easing in one respect: It involves an expansion of the central bank's balance sheet. However, in a pure QE regime, the focus of policy is the quantity of bank reserves... In contrast, the Federal Reserve's credit easing approach focuses on the mix of loans and securities on the asset side of the central bank's balance sheet and on how this composition of assets affects credit conditions."
— Ben Bernanke, January 2009

Key insight: Not all QE is the same. The COMPOSITION of Central Bank asset purchases matters as much as the SIZE. Buying long-duration Treasuries (maturity transformation) has opposite effect on term premia compared to providing liquidity (reserve expansion).

Historical QE Impact Estimates

QE1 (2008-2010):

  • Added US$1.1 trillion liquidity (+40bp to premia)
  • Added US$1.5 trillion credit channel (tightened corporate spreads)
  • Net estimated impact: Reduced term premia by ~85bp

QE2 (2010-2011):

  • Added US$0.7 trillion liquidity (+40bp)
  • Added US$0.9 trillion maturity transformation (−125bp)
  • Reduced credit channel by US$0.5 trillion (much later)
  • Net estimated impact: More complex, timing-dependent

QE3 (2012-2014):

  • Added US$1.5 trillion liquidity
  • Added US$1 trillion maturity transformation
  • Added US$0.7 trillion credit enhancement
  • Net estimated impact: ~60bp reduction in term premia

The Federal Reserve

Operational focus: Treasury repo markets and long-duration securities

Balance sheet decomposition (end-2014):

  • Liquidity provision: 50.1% (US$4,263bn)
  • Maturity transformation: 28.9% (US$2,461bn)
  • Credit enhancement: 20.9% (US$1,777bn)

Critical role in 2008 GFC: Fed provided >US$580 billion in emergency dollar swap lines to foreign central banks (ECB, BoJ, BoE, SNB, etc.)—ultimate proof that crisis was a dollar liquidity event, not solvency crisis.

Offshore Eurodollar markets: Fed is only a small player in General Collateral market but wields outsize influence. NOT directly involved in offshore Eurodollar markets (world's largest liquid short-term dollar market), which operate outside Fed control—source of both elasticity and fragility.

The People's Bank of China (PBoC)

Evolution: From historic monopoly lender (pre-Deng era) to modern Central Bank hybrid

Four main monetary tools:

  1. Open market operations (repos with primary dealers)
  2. Reserve requirements (still actively used, unlike Fed/ECB)
  3. Medium-Term Lending Facility (MTLF) — 3 months to 3 years, typically at premium to repo rates
  4. Directed lending by policy banks (China Development Bank, Agricultural Development Bank, Export-Import Bank)

Massive forex reserve accumulation:

  • Post-2000 WTO membership: reserves jumped 20-fold
  • Peaked ~US$4 trillion in 2014
  • Since fallen and stabilized ~US$3 trillion
  • Reflects slower net inflows + policy shift away from accumulating more US Treasuries

Balance sheet transformation (2013 to mid-2019):

  • Foreign reserves fell from 86% to 60% of assets
  • Direct loans to Chinese banks rose from 4% to substantial 28% of assets
  • Reserve money remained stable ~85% of balance sheet

Policy implications:

  • PBoC can now conduct monetary policy more independently of forex flows
  • MTLF provides longer-duration liquidity (currently RMB3.5-4 trillion / ~US$500bn)
  • Policy banks engage in Belt and Road Initiative financing
  • Kiel Institute estimates PBoC responsible for large part of ~US$1.5 trillion Chinese foreign lending (much undisclosed)

Strategic significance: "PBoC-watching will become at least as important for investors over coming years as the science of Fed-watching today."

The European Central Bank (ECB)

Historical context: Began operations June 1, 1998 (Euro launch January 1, 1999) as culmination of Economic and Monetary Union (EMU)

Operational structure:

  • System of collateralized borrowing through repos (short-term focus, reflecting Europe's trade bill heritage)
  • ~1,500 eligible banks can bid for "automatically bid" term liquidity in ECB auctions
  • Banks bid repeatedly as repos come due—quantity changes affect Eurozone liquidity

Crisis focus: Credit enhancement channel during sovereign debt crisis (2010-2012)—buying peripheral sovereign bonds (Greece, Italy, Spain, Portugal) to prevent spreads from spiraling

The Bank of Japan (BoJ)

Distinctive features:

  • Window guidance and directed lending (1970s/1980s favored certain industries)
  • Massive balance sheet relative to GDP (>100% of GDP)
  • Extreme maturity transformation—buys long JGBs and equity ETFs
  • Yield curve control targeting specific maturities

The Bank of England (BoE)

Historical focus: Short-term 3-month trade bills (reflecting Britain's historic foreign trading roots)

Traditional role: Provided Lender of Last Resort facilities to discount houses to maintain "marketability" of bills

Crisis transformation: Replaced maturity transformation with liquidity provision during 2008 crisis; expanded balance sheet dramatically

"We are judged by historical standards, big balance sheets here to stay... We have a bigger responsibility than we did to provide liquidity to the system..."
— Andrew Hauser, Bank of England

The Impossible Trinity and Policy Trade-offs

Classic impossible trinity: Cannot simultaneously have (1) fixed exchange rate, (2) free capital flows, and (3) independent monetary policy

Howell's liquidity extension:

"Under the prevailing globalisation regime, policy-makers can therefore effectively choose between the level of asset prices and the nominal exchange rate."

Two policy approaches:

  • Export-oriented economies (China, Japan, Germany): Stabilize nominal exchange rate against USD → accept domestic asset price bubbles as adjustment valve
  • Financialized economies (US, UK): Stabilize domestic asset prices (protect bank collateral) → accept nominal exchange rate weakness

Why this matters: Explains late-1980s Japan bubble (yen didn't rise sufficiently to eliminate productivity advantage → liquidity inflated real estate and equities until collapse December 1989). Also explains US willingness to sacrifice dollar during crises to protect domestic asset values.

The US-China Liquidity Dynamic

China's Astonishing Rise in Global Liquidity

2000: China accounted for 5.9% of Global Liquidity (less than one-fifth of America's share)

2007 (GFC): China's share reached 10.1%

Mid-2019: China has swelled to 27.5%, significantly out-pacing America's slipping 22.5%

Implications: "China matters hugely to both the World economy and World finance." This represents the most profound shift in global financial power in modern history.

The Paradox: Industrial Giant, Financial Infant

China dominates global manufacturing and exports but has an immature, state-directed financial system:

  • Consequences: Forced reliance on US dollars for international trade and reserves
  • Recycling problem: Chinese authorities (rather than private firms) must recycle dollar export earnings
  • Strategic vulnerability: Cannot export yuan liquidity; must import dollar liquidity
  • Geopolitical constraint: Vulnerable to US financial sanctions and payment system exclusion

Howell: "Being major dollar user, China historically helped stabilise US dollar region by delivering timely fiscal boosts whenever offshore export economy requires. Chinese industry continues expanding; financial system remains immature (paradoxically) in many ways. Explains both why China had to become heavy dollar-user and why Chinese Authorities (rather than private firms) forced to re-cycle dollars."

The Two-Central-Bank Framework

"Understanding the Global Liquidity cycle may simply come down to interpreting the motives and actions of the two key Central Banks—the US Federal Reserve and the People's Bank of China."

Mechanism of joint control:

  1. Fed eases policy (lowers rates, expands balance sheet)
  2. US dollar weakens (private liquidity falls relative to Central Bank liquidity)
  3. Capital flows toward higher-yielding EM assets (especially China)
  4. PBoC faces choice: (a) Let yuan appreciate (hurt exports), or (b) Monetize inflows (boost domestic liquidity)
  5. PBoC typically chooses (b): Intervenes in forex market, accumulates dollar reserves, injects yuan into domestic economy
  6. Result: US monetary easing gets AMPLIFIED into larger Global Liquidity expansion

Reverse mechanism (tightening):

  1. Fed tightens → dollar strengthens
  2. Capital flows reverse (flee EM, return to US)
  3. PBoC faces reserve outflows → must tighten domestic liquidity to defend currency
  4. Result: US tightening gets AMPLIFIED into Global Liquidity contraction

Evidence: Whenever PBoC matches Fed actions, Fed enjoys huge leverage over Global Liquidity conditions. When they diverge (2015-2016 China tightening despite Fed accommodation), Global Liquidity stalls and markets suffer.

China's Currency Ambitions

"We should promote the Renminbi to be the primary currency of Asia, just as the US dollar first became the currency of North America and then the currency of the World... Every globalisation was initiated by a rising empire... As a rising super power, the 'One Belt, One Road' strategy is the beginning of China's own globalisation... it is a counter-measure to the US strategy of dollar hegemony."
— Major-General Qiao Liang, Chinese PLA, April 2015

China's Multi-Pronged Strategy

1. Digital Yuan (Cryptocurrency Leadership)

  • Status: "Allegedly, China already well-advanced"
  • Mechanism: Encrypted digital monies as cash substitutes that eliminate intermediaries, allow person-to-person transfers
  • Advantage: Digital monies disintermediate banks (internalize clearing and settlement) AND potentially disintermediate US dollar
  • Infrastructure: Dominant Chinese payment platforms (Alipay, WeChat) could accommodate State-issued digital currency via e-wallet
  • Historical symmetry: China (originator of paper money >1,000 years ago) may be first major nation to supersede it with electronic/digital money

2. Yuan Trade Invoicing and Credit

  • Develop yuan trade credit markets allowing China to lend more to foreigners
  • Open domestic Chinese yuan-denominated bond market to international capital
  • Provide development aid and government loans denominated in yuan (Belt and Road Initiative)
  • Goal: Create captive market for yuan in neighboring Central Asian economies

3. Supply Chain Repositioning

  • Attempt to reposition more supply chains onshore
  • Reduce imports of manufactured goods
  • Aligns with controversial Made in China 2025 policy (target 40% Chinese-domestic content by 2020, 70% by 2025 in high-tech sectors: pharma, auto, aerospace, semiconductors, IT, robotics)

4. Broadening Gross International Balance Sheet

  • Increase cross-border yuan assets and liabilities
  • Policy bank lending (China Development Bank, Export-Import Bank) for Belt and Road
  • Kiel Institute estimates ~US$1.5 trillion Chinese foreign lending (much undisclosed)

Yuan unlikely to see straight-line path to dominance:

  • Existentialist threat to US dollar hegemony: Will "stir-up countervailing geopolitical forces and likely spur attempts to halt free flows of capital"
  • US dollar payments system: Anchor for American foreign policy + vital component of US budgetary policy (lower Treasury funding costs from reserve status)
  • Historical precedent: US dollar held back Euro challenge (succumbed after 2010-2012 European banking crisis); British pound-gold standard defeated French-led silver-backed Latin Monetary Union (1865-1873) when Vatican cheated by issuing debased silver coins
  • Financial skirmishes: "Ultimate battle in Capital War must surely involve fight for supremacy between US dollar, Chinese Yuan and digital/cryptocurrencies"

Trust deficit: "Off-set counterargument: Trust (vital dimension of currency adoption) demands truly democratic State. Claim would not have impressed Romans and plainly did not derail exorbitant circulation of denarii. Nor will hold sway with regional neighbours eager for Chinese investment."

The Ironclad Question

"Could digital Yuan ever become twenty-first century's 'safe asset'?"

This question frames the coming Capital War. If China can establish digital yuan as safe, liquid, and trusted, it would fundamentally reshape global finance—but requires:

  • Deep, liquid yuan bond markets
  • Confidence in Chinese rule of law and property rights
  • Removal of capital controls (conflicts with political control)
  • Willingness of global investors to trust authoritarian state with reserve holdings

Chapter Notes

Chapter 1: Introduction — Capital Wars

Central thesis: The struggle for global financial dominance between American finance and Chinese industry defines twenty-first century capitalism. The battleground is liquidity, technology, and geopolitics.

The 2008 crisis was a dollar scramble, not a solvency crisis: Every major financial crisis since 1980 has been fundamentally a liquidity event—scramble for dollars, not insolvency. Fed emergency swap lines (>US$580 billion to foreign central banks) were the critical backstop. "007 agent James Bond quote: 'Once is happenstance. Twice is coincidence. Three times is enemy action.'"

Two puzzling features in policy debates:

  1. QE and bond yields: Academic consensus (Gagnon 2016) claims QE lowers yields by 67bp per 10% of GDP. Data refutes this—US QE periods UNEQUIVOCALLY associated with HIGHER yields. Term premia rose on average 134bp through each QE phase.
  2. Yield curve as recession predictor: Standard 10-2 year slope is "at best a flaky predictor." Different maturity spreads work at different times. What matters is CURVATURE (not just slope), and term premia (liquidity phenomena) are key component explaining curvature.

The 1989 turning point: Berlin Wall collapse ricocheted across the world, forced interest rates down, reversed polarity of global financial system. Capital faced Eastward along the Financial Silk Road. Politics and people tempted too many countries (notably China) to lean too heavily on US dollar and US Treasury market for safety.

Refinancing vs. new financing: "Today, financial markets increasingly serve as refinancing mechanisms rather than new financing mechanisms. This makes the capacity of capital (balance sheet size) more important than the cost of capital (level of interest rates)."

The ironies of reduced liquidity supply: Reduced supply of liquidity and safe assets INCREASES demand to hoard them. Together these features amplify swings in Global Liquidity. Explain why, as the World has got bigger, it has also become more volatile. Underlying scarcity of high-quality assets leads to Capital Wars.

Chapter 2: Global Money

How big is the pool? Global Liquidity roughly US$135 trillion (approximately 2× World GDP). The 10-fold expansion since 1980s driven by:

  • Financial deregulation and capital flow liberalization
  • China's economic opening (WTO 2001)
  • Explosion of shadow banking and wholesale finance
  • Cross-border dollar lending via Eurodollar markets

Distribution (mid-2019):

  • China: 27.5% (up from 5.9% in 2000)
  • United States: 22.5% (down from dominant position)
  • Eurozone: ~18%
  • Japan: ~8%
  • United Kingdom: ~6%
  • Rest of World: ~18%

What is Global Liquidity? Lance Taylor: "Liquidity is often interpreted as a measure of the financial flexibility of an individual actor, group of actors, or the financial system as a whole." Howell expands: comprehensive flow-of-funds analysis covering traditional banks, central banks, shadow banks, cross-border flows, and CICPs.

Types of liquidity:

  • Funding liquidity: Ability to raise cash when needed
  • Market liquidity: Ability to trade assets without moving prices
  • Monetary liquidity: Central Bank money and reserves
  • Financial liquidity: Broad credit and shadow banking aggregates

Public vs. Private liquidity: This distinction is CRITICAL for forecasting. Private sector liquidity (generated by productive cash flows) strengthens currencies. Central Bank liquidity (printed money) often signals distress and weakens currencies. The MIX predicts exchange rates 6-12 months ahead.

Policy-makers behind the curve: "Policy interest rates vs. broader monetary tools" — Central banks remain fixated on overnight rates while shadow banking dominates. In 2000, each US$1 of Central Bank money created >US$14 of Global Liquidity; by 2019 multiplier collapsed to barely US$6.2—dramatic evidence of chronic liquidity shortage.

The shift from retail to wholesale finance: Traditional high street deposit-taking represents shrinking share of liquidity creation. Wholesale funding markets (repos, commercial paper, Eurodollars) and shadow banks (money market funds, hedge funds, SIVs) now dominate. Traditional M2/M3 measures obsolete.

Two main independent drivers:

  1. Central Bank balance sheet changes (monetary policy)
  2. Cross-border capital flows (affected by US dollar movements, productivity differentials, risk appetite)

Chapter 5: Real Exchange Adjustment

The industrial and financial circuits of money: Keynes' alternative view distinguishes industrial circuit (production, wages, consumption) from financial circuit (savings, credit, asset prices). Investment depends on liquidity, not just savings. This explains why liquidity shocks affect exchange rates and asset prices.

The premium on money: Liquidity commands a scarcity premium. When safe assets are scarce, the premium rises—explains negative yields on government bonds and explains why QE can paradoxically raise term premia.

Four adjustment outcomes from liquidity shock:

  1. Nominal exchange rate appreciation
  2. Relative price level increases (especially non-tradables like real estate)
  3. Asset price inflation (stocks, bonds, property)
  4. Some combination of above

Qualitative vs. quantitative dimensions: QUALITY of liquidity (private vs. Central Bank) matters as much as QUANTITY. Forex risk index = private sector liquidity minus Central Bank liquidity. Advanced 12 months, this index Granger-causes currency movements with high R-squared.

Emerging market experience: Central Bank interventions to suppress nominal exchange rate appreciation often lead to domestic asset booms. Example: late-1980s Japan—yen rose significantly but not sufficiently to eliminate productivity advantage → export success liquidity build-up inflated huge bubble → burst December 1989 → Japanese markets never regained former heights.

"There is one and only one way to raise our commodity price level: that is by reducing the amount of gold in the dollar. A rise in prices this week of commodities was directly in proportion to the decline in the value of the dollar in foreign exchange."
— George F. Warren, letter to President Roosevelt, April 24, 1933

Testing the model with data:

  • Forex risk index (private minus Central Bank liquidity, advanced 12 months) is ONE-WAY GRANGER CAUSAL for US dollar movements
  • Same methodology works for EM currencies: EM forex risk minus US forex risk predicts JPMorgan EM forex basket (R² = 0.37)
  • Conclusion: “These analyses add weight to our belief that forex market movements depend crucially on the quality mix of liquidity and capital flows”

Chapter 6: Private Sector (Funding) Liquidity

Funding vs. market liquidity: Funding liquidity = cash on-hand plus ability to borrow (sources of funds). Market liquidity = ease of trading in size without moving prices (uses of funds). Unlike M2, true liquidity is global, includes wholesale markets, the entire private sector, and measures gross flows not just net.

Modern money beyond M2: M2 ignores secured repos, commercial paper, large corporate deposits, offshore Eurodollars. Combined broad US funding ~US$26 trillion vs. M2 of only US$15 trillion. “Modern money starts where conventional definitions end”—M0/M1/M2 = only tip of a growing iceberg.

Shadow banking explosion: Total broad shadow banking grew from $91.4T (2006) to $210.7T (2019)—130.6% increase. High-risk narrow shadow banking ~$60T, grown 120% since 2006. Assets exposed to bank run risk tripled since GFC (288.4% increase). Shadow banks don’t create much new lending—they repackage and recycle existing savings, increasing elasticity by relaxing capital constraints.

The polarity switch: CICPs worldwide exceed $30 trillion—representing a structural reversal where former lenders (banks) became borrowers from wholesale markets, and former borrowers (corporations) became lenders. Largest sovereign wealth funds: Norwegian Government Pension Fund ($1.1T), CIC ($941B), ADIA ($697B).

The repo market as engine room: Wholesale money markets = “engine room” behind Global Liquidity. By 2007, US repo market reached $10 trillion—same order of magnitude as total US commercial bank assets. Intraday credit to single dealers approached $100 billion.

Collateral and rehypothecation: Collateral = high-powered money component; haircut = reserve ratio equivalent; chain length = money multiplier equivalent. Collateral multiplier stood at 3× (2007), declined to ~2× (2017). With 2% haircuts, leverage up to 50× possible. Crisis example: US Treasury haircuts jumped from 0.25% to 3%; ABS haircuts soared from ~4% to nearly 60%; leverage collapsed from 25:1 to 1.7:1.

The liquidity multiplier collapse: Traditional money multiplier peaked near 15× (late 1990s), collapsed post-GFC. Each US$1 of Central Bank money created >US$14 of Global Liquidity in 2000; by 2019 barely US$6.2—dramatic evidence of chronic liquidity shortage.

A world of refinancing: Dominated by funding rollover of huge outstanding debts, not new capital projects. Balance sheet capacity more important than interest rates. September 2019: repo rates surged to 10% (vs. Fed Funds 2.14%) when money market flows and excess reserves dipped below critical thresholds ($1T and $1.5T).

"There exist still other forms of media of exchange... any increase or decrease of these money substitutes will have exactly the same effects... they spring up without being subject to any central control, but once they have come into existence their convertibility must be avoided if a collapse of credit is to be avoided."
— F.A. Hayek (1933)

Chapter 7: The Central Banks

"Don't Fight the Fed, Don't Upset the PBoC"

Treasury repo operations: Fed focuses on Treasury repo market to control Fed Funds (even though separate markets with different participants). Repo = collateralized loan; reverse repo = liquidity drain. Central Bank operations differ by maturity spectrum and size/scope through cycle.

Fed balance sheet decomposition by function: Table 7.4 (end-2014):

  • Liquidity provision: 50.1% (US$4,263bn)
  • Maturity transformation: 28.9% (US$2,461bn)
  • Credit enhancement: 20.9% (US$1,777bn)

How the three channels affect risk premia:

  • Liquidity provision: Each US$1T raises term premia by +56bp (paradoxical—Treasuries lose liquidity premium)
  • Maturity transformation: Each US$1T reduces term premia by −142bp (most powerful channel)
  • Credit enhancement: Each US$1T reduces B-Aaa corporate spread by −415bp (affects credit, not Treasuries)

Empirical QE impact:

  • QE1: Added US$1.1T liquidity + US$1.5T credit → net ~85bp reduction in term premia
  • QE3: Added US$1.5T liquidity + US$1T maturity + US$0.7T credit → net ~60bp reduction
  • BUT: Overall observed effect was term premia ROSE during QE periods because safe asset scarcity intensified hoarding demand

The People's Bank of China: Evolution from monopoly lender to modern Central Bank. Post-2000 WTO membership: forex reserves jumped 20-fold. Peaked US$4 trillion 2014, now stabilized ~US$3 trillion. Balance sheet transformation: foreign reserves fell from 86% to 60% of assets; direct loans to banks rose from 4% to 28%. Medium-Term Lending Facility (MTLF) now RMB3.5-4 trillion. Policy banks (China Development Bank, etc.) engage in Belt and Road lending.

The European Central Bank: Began operations June 1998. System of collateralized repos with ~1,500 eligible banks. Short-term repo dominance reflects Europe’s trade bill heritage.

Eurozone crisis (2010-2012): Triggered by Greece’s admission of full indebtedness. Key structural flaw: the ‘safe’ asset in the Eurozone is not the Euro but the German Bund. Doom loop between banks and sovereigns. ECB’s Article 123 initially disallowed sovereign bond purchases; credit quality threshold set at BBB-minus meant downgrades locked out banks from ECB refinancing.

ECB crisis programs: SMP (May 2010) → LTRO/LTRO2 under Draghi (Dec 2011–Feb 2012, ~EUR800 billion) → OMT (Aug 2012, “no prior limits”) → CBPP3/ABSPP (Sept 2014) → full QE (Jan 2015, EUR60 billion/month) → TLTROs (I, II, III from 2014–2019). ECB balance sheet leaped from EUR806 billion (1999) to EUR4.7 trillion (end-2018). Outright security purchases jumped from 2-3% to near 62% by end-2018.

“...is ready to do whatever it takes to preserve the Euro. And believe me, it will be enough.”
— Mario Draghi, July 2012

Chapter 8: Cross-Border Capital Flows

Dollar supremacy and three phases: (1) Gold Exchange Standard (1945-1971, Bretton Woods), (2) Oil Exchange Standard (1974-1989, secret Simon-Saudi agreement for dollar-priced oil), (3) Emerging Market Exchange Standard (1990-present, Berlin Wall fall + Deng’s reforms). China adopted dollar as benchmark in 1994; WTO entry (2001) cemented dollar usage despite Chinese-led supply chains.

“By a stroke of the pen, America had cleverly created a new demand for US dollars.”
— on Saudi Arabia’s undisclosed Treasury debt purchases

Fed as international lender of last resort: Swap lines provided $10.1 trillion (standardized) December 2007–August 2010. $8.0 trillion taken by the ECB alone. Adam Tooze (2018): swap lines reinforced dollar dominance but are now politicized—who sits in the White House matters.

Gross capital flows warned of GFC: Surged to 22.3% of World GDP in 2007 before crisis. For six major economies, peaked nearly 40% of GDP in 2007, skidding to barely 10% in 2018. Banking flows suffer >2× the volatility of other flow types; in crises 4-5 times larger. Net flows failed to flash warnings—research focused on current account imbalances, not gross flow sizes.

The “Global Savings Glut” critique: Bernanke (2005) coined the term, but Howell challenges it—GFC more likely caused by excessive elasticity of the international monetary system and depth of Western asset markets, not high Asian savings rates. Gross flows can drive the current account rather than passively accommodating it.

Eurodollar markets: Daily Eurodollar funding 3-4× larger than Fed Funds (~$150 billion). Non-bank US dollar-denominated debt outside US at ~$17 trillion—exceeding domestic US bank lending. Fed imposed zero-reserve requirement on Eurodollar deposits since 1990.

Carry trades and CIP breakdown: Cross-border borrowing creates currency mismatch. 2002-2008: US-to-Europe flows jumped from $2 billion to $1.54 trillion. Since 2008, persistent CIP basis violations challenge assumptions of riskless arbitrage—spread reflects global scarcity of dollar safe assets.

Global financial centre rankings (2019): New York (1), London (2), Hong Kong (3), Singapore (4), Shanghai (5). Huge jumps in Chinese centres: Shanghai +30 since 2009, Beijing +42. Pacific Asian centres rising at expense of American and European centres.

Capital flow structure: US dominated by debt/banking flows; China by FDI (44.3%); UK by banking flows (58.7%, London’s dominance). Chinese foreign assets: official gold/forex reserves 58.3%—emphasizes government’s active dollar recycling role; private sector plays small part. Zucman (2013): 8% of global financial wealth held offshore, at least 6% officially unrecorded.

Chapter 9: China and the Emerging Markets

Chinese monetary system: Total Chinese liquidity near RMB200 trillion (~US$28 trillion). State-owned banks dominate; shadow banks account for ~1/3 of system liquidity but drive the credit cycle fluctuations. Shadow banks funded through wholesale markets, dependent on buoyant collateral values, many tainted by association with local authorities and dubious real estate deals.

China’s financial immaturity: Gross foreign asset base ~US$7.5 trillion (vs $10T Germany, >$25T UK)—not only smaller but heavily skewed toward safe asset forex reserves (average 58%). Foreigners’ holdings of Chinese bonds barely 1/10 of comparable German figures. China is re-exporting US dollars when she should be exporting Yuan.

“Chinese gross banking activity is dominated by Chinese residents borrowing US dollars: lending in Yuan to foreigners and foreign purchases of Chinese domestic bonds is to date minimal. China is re-exporting US dollars, when she should be exporting Yuan. This has to change.”

RMB internationalization strategy: Develop transparent government bond market; digital Yuan via Alipay/WeChat platforms; price exports in Yuan; develop Euro-yuan offshore markets. De facto regional currency bloc already emerging—correlation between CNY and other Asian units rose from 0.27 (2015) to 0.52 (2019). China’s total liquidity ~US$65.6 trillion vs US$29.2 trillion.

Capital flows as double-edged sword for EMs: Three impact mechanisms—(1) Volume effects: foreign institutions large relative to small EM markets; (2) Infrastructure effects: underdeveloped markets can’t sterilize credit booms; (3) Policy effects: Central Banks targeting exchange rates monetize flows. One-fifth of all capital flow surges end in national financial crises; EM economies 3× more likely to experience crisis after surges.

The Balassa-Samuelson effect: Rising real exchange rates follow positive capital flow shocks. In supply-constrained EMs, increase in real exchange rate expressed through rising asset prices rather than nominal appreciation. High-productivity Asian economies (Singapore, Hong Kong, Korea) enjoy large speculative real estate developments.

Dollar strength and EM performance: Dollar weakness associated with positive EM performance. Strong dollar adversely affects EMs through tighter US policy AND direct negative effects on foreign borrowers repaying in more-expensive dollars. EM economies collectively hold ~US$7 trillion in forex reserves as self-insurance after 1997/1998 Asian Crisis.

Chinese liquidity spillovers: Correlation 0.505 between capital flows to EMs and Chinese domestic liquidity. Each percentage point rise in Chinese GDP growth leads to 1.7% increase in Chinese capital outflows. US push factors tend to be statistically weaker than Chinese pull factors.

“...every Emerging Market crisis is first-and-foremost a currency crisis.”
— Barings research (1994)

Chapter 10: The Liquidity Transmission Mechanism

Financial cycle vs. economic cycle: Financial cycles (Borio 2012) involve febrile interactions between risk appetites, collateral values, and funding availability. Can differ in length and amplitude from economic cycles. Upswing presages future economic trends; downswing frequently coincides with banking crises and recessions. Traditional view treats financial flows as passive accounting counterparts—in practice, liquidity inflows/outflows and risk-taking behavior drive markets.

Financial assets as luxury goods: Cross-country data shows every 10% growth in per capita incomes associated with near-15% increase in financial asset value per head (power function loading = 1.48). Goldsmith’s financial interrelations ratio (intangible/tangible assets) rises as economies develop.

P/E vs. P/M ratios: P/M (price-to-money) strongly favored over P/E for three reasons: (1) more intuitive with direct link to asset allocation, (2) liquidity easily incorporated (P/M correlation with excess liquidity: 0.57 vs P/E-P/M correlation: 0.30), (3) more stable and mean-reverting—P/E distorts when earnings collapse to zero.

Two key transmission channels:

  1. Quantity Channel: Absolute volume of liquidity affects risk appetite. Plentiful liquidity = widespread risk-taking; scarce = panic and flight to safety.
  2. Quality Channel: Hierarchical nature of money affects exchange rates. Vibrant private sector strengthens currency; aggressive Central Bank supply weakens it.

QE and the term premia paradox: Conventional narrative claims QE drives down term premia. Empirical evidence contradicts: term premia rose by average 135bp under QE, then fell as QE expired. Explanation: QE announcement reduces perceived systemic risk, encouraging investors to cut demand for safe assets—demand curve shifts leftward, driving up term premia. More liquidity via QE steepens yield curves; less liquidity flattens them.

Risk assets response to liquidity: Real estate lead-times ~3 years from liquidity impulse. Each 10-point GLI increase leads to ~2% annual rise in real estate values. Equities may be the marginal swing asset class used for quick portfolio mix adjustments.

Cross-country asset allocation: US investors highest equity exposure (36%); Japanese lowest equity (20%), highest bonds (45%); EM investors highest liquid assets (45%). World aggregate: ~31% equities (close to long-run 28% trend). Historical extremes: peaked 38.1% August 2000 (Y2K bubble); post-GFC trough under 19%.

Chapter 11: Financial Crises and Safe Assets

The financial cycle framework: 2007-2008 GFC stemmed from unsustainable bank balance sheets, not current account problems—resulted in Global Liquidity collapse. Standard economics ignores money and liquidity; markets assumed frictionless. Reality: illiquidity is the ultimate market friction.

Is stability destabilizing? Post-GFC improvements (more capital, Basel III, LCRs). But regulatory arbitrage persists. Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act (1999) removed Glass-Steagall separation; by 2010s, >3/4 of American financial assets controlled by ten largest conglomerates (vs barely 10% in 1990). Real problem is “too interconnected to fail” not “too big to fail.”

The refinancing problem: Shadow banks typically involved in 2/3 of funding for repackaging existing loans; only 1/3 for new credit. When intermediation chain A → B → C → ... → Z breaks, any link can notionally bankrupt “twenty-six” firms. Minsky’s Financial Instability Hypothesis: three financing regimes—hedged (stable), speculative (needs rollovers), Ponzi (needs liquidity AND rising asset prices). Stability begets instability.

A worldwide shortage of safe assets: IMF (2012) estimates ~US$9 trillion removed from safe asset supply (~16%) post-crisis through sovereign downgrades. Krishnamurthy and Vissing-Jorgensen (2012): US Treasury yields ~70bp lower on average due to safety/liquidity features. Paradox: investors accept losses on risky investments but not on supposedly safe ones; policy-makers trying to make systems safer create moral hazard.

“The Global Financial Cycle can be associated with surges and dry outs in capital flows, booms and busts in asset prices and crises...”
— Hélène Rey (2015)
“Making sure ‘It’ doesn’t happen here...”
— Ben Bernanke, famous anti-deflation speech (November 2002)

Chapter 13: Measuring Liquidity — The Global Liquidity Indexes (GLI)

CrossBorder Capital's GLI™: Family of composite indexes designed to unambiguously assess financial liquidity conditions across ~80 economies worldwide. Published regularly since early 1990s.

Predictive power:

  • Lead bond markets and forex by ~3-6 months
  • Lead equity markets by ~6-12 months
  • Lead real economies by ~15-20 months
  • Granger causal for many key economic and financial data series

What GLI measures (six types of variables potentially available):

  1. Asset prices and credit costs
  2. Credit spreads/risk premia
  3. Leverage and credit growth
  4. Off-balance sheet lending (shadow banks, securitization)
  5. Lending surveys
  6. Security new issuance data and ETF/mutual fund flows

CrossBorder Capital focuses on #3, #4, and part of #2:

  • Growth rates in volume of Broad Credit/Financial Savings
  • Growth rates in size of Central Bank balance sheets
  • Net inflows of cross-border financial capital relative to domestic liquidity pool size
  • Leverage ratios of private credit providers
  • Short-term credit spreads in money markets (LIBOR-OIS, T-ED)

What GLI excludes and why:

  • Lending surveys: not predictive, follow other credit measures with long lag
  • Issuance data and ETF/fund flows: useful but are a 'use' not a 'source' of funds, frequently non-predictive
  • Credit costs and asset prices: excluded except for very short-term spreads used to cross-check flow data

Index construction methodology: Three-stage process—(1) Raw data, (2) Transformation and Normalization, (3) Index Construction. Data frequency: monthly (compromise between daily noise and quarterly perspective). Number of variables: ~30 per economy (statistically significant, avoids over-signaling).

Key construction choices: Baskets of z-scores (normalized statistical series). Sample ~30 data series per economy covering traditional banks, Central Banks, shadow banks, corporations, households, foreign investors. Weights derived from principal component analysis to maximize signal-to-noise.

Chapter 14: Conclusion — A High-Water Mark?

Peak liquidity metaphor: Confederate positions at Gettysburg symbolize potential peak of Global Liquidity as US-China rivalry intensifies. US no longer fully embracing China; explicitly containing China’s economic/geopolitical challenge through controlling trade, technology transfer, and capital flows. China’s counter-strategy: thwart US dollar hegemony; retreat from globalisation toward new regionalism.

Dollar dependency paradox: World became more dollar-centric as fixed exchange rate system collapsed. US trade deficit largely explained by competitiveness of America’s financial markets, not industrial uncompetitiveness. 2/3 to 3/4 of real and financial market activity transacted in US dollars.

The Financial Silk Road redrawn: Fall of Berlin Wall (1989) enfranchised 2-3 billion new producers. Spheres now being redrawn: China consolidating via Belt and Road into Central Asia, Europe, Africa; Germany redeploying East; US expanding South into Latin America and picking up influence in Asia.

Social costs in the West: Middle-class employment shaken; cognitive/semiskilled jobs lost since dot-com bubble. Low wage growth; households encouraged to borrow. Gaping wealth disparities destroying political middle ground, leading to populist extremes. Mass financialisation left investors richer on paper than at 2000 dot-com top. Since Greenspan era: nominal GDP averaged 4-5%, but returns on equities, real estate, Treasuries, corporate bonds, junk bonds all outpaced the underlying economy.

“By sticking to the new orthodoxy of monetary policy and pretending that we have made the banking system safe we are sleepwalking towards that crisis.”
— Mervyn King, former Bank of England head (2019)

Policy-makers’ constraints: Natural rate of interest ~2-2.5% in US. Low ceiling means limited scope for rate cuts. Negative rates risk destroying the repo mechanism and commercial bank profitability—meaning destroying liquidity supply itself. Interest rates are not the price of money but an exchange rate. Central Banks likely forced to resort to QE4, QE5, QE6.

Policy prescriptions:

  • Move toward asset-based framework focused on credit quality
  • Steady supply of safe assets critical
  • Target currency blocs with greater exchange rate adjustments between blocs than within
  • Issue longer duration instruments (30, 50, 100-year government bonds)
  • Limit negative effects of excessive capital flows
  • Above all: ensure adequate levels of Global Liquidity

Four conclusions:

  1. As investment World gets bigger and more interconnected by rapid flows of international capital, it becomes more volatile. Procyclical monetary system amplifies booms and busts.
  2. Procyclical monetary system dominated by private sector credit and debt, featuring global supply chains, based around USD, driven by fast-moving flows of Global Liquidity. Refinancing economy makes balance sheet capacity critical.
  3. Policy-makers fail to understand changing financial structure. Rise of CICPs and focus of institutions on refinancing rather than new money-raising results in inappropriate and ultimately destabilising monetary policies; reversal of financial polarity.
  4. Regionalism (possibly based on digital monies) will consequently replace globalisation as capital rivalry limits cross-border trade, technology transfer and free-flow of risk capital.

The internationalisation of the yuan: Three most important sources of Global Liquidity: US Federal Reserve, People's Bank of China, cross-border flows of capital (dependent on dollar exchange rate). Greatest change in last two decades: Rise of China (5.9% → 27.5% of Global Liquidity). China's submissive role in currency choice cannot continue.

Major-General Qiao Liang, Chinese PLA (April 2015): "We should promote the Renminbi to be the primary currency of Asia, just as the US dollar first became the currency of North America and then the currency of the World... Every globalisation was initiated by a rising empire... As a rising super power, the 'One Belt, One Road' strategy is the beginning of China's own globalisation... it is a counter-measure to the US strategy of dollar hegemony."

China's strategy to rival and destroy US dollar: Broaden Chinese gross international balance sheet; invoke more trade in yuan; develop yuan trade credit markets; open domestic yuan bond market to international capital. Major part likely involves establishment and use of digital Chinese yuan. Encrypted digital monies disintermediate banks (internalize clearing and settlement) AND disintermediate US dollar, weakening US financial power. Dominant Chinese payment platforms (Alipay, WeChat) could accommodate State-issued digital currency via e-wallet.

Historical symmetry: China (originator of paper money >1,000 years ago) may be first major nation to supersede it with electronic and digital monies. Developing captive market for yuan in neighbouring Central Asian economies; providing development aid and government loans denominated in yuan. China will attempt to reposition more supply chains onshore (Made in China 2025 policy).

Geopolitical resistance: Yuan unlikely straight-line path to dominance. Existentialist threat to US dollar hegemony will stir countervailing geopolitical forces and likely spur attempts to halt free flows of capital. US dollar international payments system = anchor for American foreign policy + vital component of US budgetary policy (lower Treasury funding costs). Historical precedent: US dollar held back Euro challenge; British pound-gold standard defeated Latin Monetary Union when Vatican cheated by issuing debased silver coins.

Franco-Prussian War indemnity historical precedent: France paid massive debt (25% of GDP) by issuing new government bonds heavily over-subscribed across Europe—at stroke enlarged pool of international safe assets, mobilized inert savings, credit boomed and spread cross-border. Scale proved too great → Panic of 1873 (first truly World crisis). Emphasizes importance of safe asset creation and adolescent power of Global Liquidity.

The ironclad question for policy-makers, business leaders, investors: Could digital Yuan ever become twenty-first century's 'safe asset'? Must be prepared for next potential stage of Chinese development and power.

Appendix: Inflation, Deflation and Valuations

P/E multiples and inflation: Equity valuations include risk premium based on odds of default and inventory valuation losses during deflation. Higher bond yields drag on equity valuations during inflation. Humped pattern: equities enjoy highest real valuations when price inflation is low (~-2%).

Correlation regime dependence: At inflation threshold near equity valuation peak: bonds and equities negatively correlated. At higher inflation rates: positively correlated. Bonds provide good hedge to equities during periods of low inflation and price deflation.

Japanese market example: Late 1980s stock/bond correlation ~0.6 (inflation ~2.5%); late 1990s inflation turned negative, correlation collapsed to -0.4; post-GFC correlation tumbled to -0.6.

US historical CAPE data (1881-2019, Shiller): Five-year average CAPE plotted against five-year average inflation shows fitted cubic relationship with peak CAPE at close to zero inflation. Data support proposition that equity valuations are highest at low positive inflation rates.
