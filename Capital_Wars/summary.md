# Capital Wars

**Author:** Michael J. Howell
**Published:** 2020
**Pages:** 316

## One-Line Summary
Global Liquidity—not interest rates or traditional monetary aggregates—drives asset prices, currencies, and financial crises, and the battle for liquidity dominance between US dollar finance and Chinese industrial power will define twenty-first century capitalism.

## Key Takeaways
1. **Global Liquidity is the dominant force in modern finance:** The pool of Global Liquidity (now ~US$135 trillion, roughly double World GDP) is the primary driver of asset prices, exchange rates, and economic activity—far more important than interest rates, inflation, or traditional money supply measures.

2. **We live in a refinancing economy, not a new financing economy:** Modern financial markets serve primarily to roll over existing debts rather than fund new investment. This makes balance sheet capacity (liquidity) more critical than the cost of capital (interest rates).

3. **China has surpassed America in liquidity creation:** China's share of Global Liquidity jumped from 5.9% in 2000 to 27.5% by 2019, overtaking America's declining 22.5%. This shift represents the most profound change in global finance in decades.

4. **The quality of liquidity matters as much as quantity:** Private sector liquidity (generated by productive cash flows) strengthens currencies and supports sustainable growth. Central Bank liquidity (printed money) often signals distress and weakens currencies. The mix between the two predicts exchange rate movements 6-12 months ahead.

5. **Safe assets are chronically scarce:** The post-2008 regulatory regime combined with fiscal austerity has created an acute shortage of high-quality collateral. This scarcity amplifies liquidity cycles, increases volatility, and explains why quantitative easing often raises (not lowers) bond yields.

6. **Central Banks have three distinct transmission channels:** (1) Liquidity provision (enables trading), (2) Maturity transformation (duration risk), and (3) Credit enhancement (credit risk). Each US$1 trillion in Fed maturity transformation reduces Treasury term premia by ~142 basis points, while liquidity provision paradoxically increases premia by ~56 basis points.

7. **The 1989 Berlin Wall collapse reversed global financial polarity:** Pre-1989, liquidity flowed from emerging economies to developed markets. Post-1989, the Financial Silk Road reversed the flow—developed market capital now chases emerging market growth, creating procyclical boom-bust dynamics.

8. **The US dollar plays a paradoxical dual role:** As both the primary reserve currency and the currency of a current account deficit nation, the dollar creates systemic fragility. Weaker dollars fuel global liquidity booms; stronger dollars trigger emerging market crises.

9. **Exchange rate regimes determine where liquidity shocks appear:** Policy-makers can choose between asset price inflation or nominal exchange rate adjustment—but not both. Export-oriented economies (China, Japan, Germany) stabilize currencies and accept asset bubbles; financialized economies (US, UK) stabilize asset prices and accept currency weakness.

10. **Shadow banking and wholesale finance have replaced traditional banking:** The explosion of repos, derivatives, CICPs (complex, information-constrained, path-dependent securities), and offshore dollar markets means traditional monetary aggregates (M2, M3) have become obsolete. Global Liquidity must be tracked through comprehensive flow-of-funds analysis.

11. **Digital yuan threatens dollar hegemony:** China's strategy to internationalize the yuan through digital currency, Belt and Road lending, and trade invoicing represents an existentialist threat to the US dollar-based system. The coming Capital War will be fought over safe asset status.

12. **Financial crises are liquidity events, not solvency events:** Every major crisis since 1980—including the 2008 Global Financial Crisis—was fundamentally a dollar liquidity scramble, not an insolvency problem. The Federal Reserve's emergency swap lines with other central banks were the critical stabilization mechanism.

13. **The Global Liquidity Indexes (GLI) have remarkable predictive power:** CrossBorder Capital's GLI measures lead bond markets by 3-6 months, equity markets by 6-12 months, and real economies by 15-20 months. They are Granger-causal for asset prices and economic data worldwide.

14. **Policy-makers systematically misunderstand the financial system they regulate:** Central bankers remain trapped in outdated frameworks focused on traditional bank deposits and interest rates, blind to the dominance of shadow banking, cross-border flows, and collateral chains that actually drive liquidity conditions.

15. **The next regime will likely feature regionalism over globalization:** As the US-China rivalry intensifies and capital controls proliferate, expect fragmentation into currency blocs, reduced cross-border flows, and the rise of digital state-backed currencies that enable unprecedented financial surveillance and control.

## Thematic Breakdowns

### The Nature of Global Liquidity
- **Definition:** The underlying flow of funds (cash, credit, cross-border capital) circulating through the global financial system—distinct from traditional money supply measures.
- **Size and scope:** ~US$135 trillion (roughly 2× World GDP), tracking ~80 economies, covering banks, central banks, shadow banks, corporations, households, and foreign investors.
- **Two dimensions:** Quantity (how much) and Quality (who creates it—private sector "good" vs. Central Bank "bad").
- **Flow-of-funds foundation:** Based on comprehensive balance sheet analysis of sources and uses of funds, not just deposit-taking institutions.

### The Three Historical Eras of Global Finance
- **Pre-1989 (Bretton Woods/Gold Exchange Era):** Fixed exchange rates, capital controls, liquidity flows from poor to rich countries.
- **1989-2007 (Emerging Market/Dollar Hegemony Era):** Berlin Wall falls, financial polarity reverses, capital flows from rich to poor, China joins WTO (2001), global imbalances build.
- **Post-2008 (Refinancing/Safe Asset Scarcity Era):** GFC exposes dollar shortage, unprecedented Central Bank balance sheet expansion, chronic safe asset scarcity, shift from new financing to refinancing systems.

### Central Bank Operations and Transmission Mechanisms
- **Federal Reserve:** Focuses on Treasury repo markets and long-duration securities; three channels (liquidity, maturity, credit) have different and sometimes opposing effects on term premia.
- **People's Bank of China:** Evolved from monopoly lender to modern central bank; massive forex reserve accumulation (peaked ~US$4 trillion 2014); now shifting to domestic lending via Medium-Term Lending Facility and policy banks.
- **European Central Bank:** Short-term repo dominance reflecting Europe's trade bill heritage; 1,500+ eligible banks; credit enhancement focus during sovereign debt crisis.
- **Bank of Japan:** Window guidance and directed lending; massive balance sheet relative to GDP; extreme maturity transformation.

### The Dollar System and Cross-Border Flows
- **Exorbitant privilege and impossible trinity:** US benefits from seigniorage and lower funding costs but creates global instability through procyclical capital flows.
- **Offshore Eurodollar markets:** Vast unregulated dollar funding pools outside Fed control; critical source of elasticity and fragility.
- **Fed swap lines as crisis backstop:** During 2008 GFC, Fed provided >US$580 billion in emergency dollar liquidity to foreign central banks—the ultimate proof that crises are liquidity events.
- **Transfer problem revisited:** Keynes' WWI reparations debate applies to modern global imbalances—real resource transfers require relative price adjustments, not just financial flows.

### Safe Assets and Collateral Chains
- **Canonical definition:** Low market/credit risk, high liquidity, limited inflation/FX risk, negative beta to markets, deep tradable pools, fiscal capacity backing.
- **Supply constraints:** Post-GFC bank regulations (Basel III), fiscal austerity, sovereign downgrades removed ~US$9 trillion from safe asset pool.
- **Shadow monetary base:** The capacity of private balance sheets to create "money-like" instruments through repo chains, securitization, and rehypothecation.
- **Term premia dynamics:** Safe asset scarcity can cause QE to raise (not lower) yields as demand to hoard liquidity intensifies.

### China's Rise and Currency Ambitions
- **Industrial giant, financial infant:** China dominates manufacturing and exports but has an immature, state-directed financial system—forcing reliance on US dollars.
- **Strategic imperative:** Must internationalize yuan to match economic power; digital yuan, Belt and Road lending in yuan, expanding offshore yuan bond markets.
- **Geopolitical resistance:** US will not cede dollar hegemony easily—expect capital controls, payment system sanctions, financial "skirmishes" analogous to Latin Monetary Union vs. gold standard.
- **Historical parallel:** Just as Britain's industrial decline preceded sterling's currency decline by decades, expect yuan's rise to lag China's economic ascent.

### Exchange Rates and Financial Adjustment
- **Real vs. nominal exchange rates:** Real rate reflects productivity differentials; nominal rate determined by Central Bank intervention and liquidity mix.
- **Policy trade-off:** Governments choose between stable currencies (accept asset bubbles) or stable asset prices (accept currency volatility)—cannot have both under globalization.
- **Forex risk index predictive power:** Private sector liquidity minus Central Bank liquidity, advanced 12 months, Granger-causes future currency movements with high statistical significance.
- **EM currency dynamics:** Difference between EM forex risk and US forex risk predicts EM currency baskets 12 months ahead (R² > 0.36).

### Shadow Banking and Wholesale Finance Revolution
- **Traditional banking obsolete:** High street deposit-taking represents shrinking share of liquidity creation; wholesale funding markets and shadow banks dominate.
- **CICPs (Complex, Information-constrained, Path-dependent securities):** Modern securities with opaque credit risk, illiquid markets, and path-dependent valuations—not captured by traditional monetary data.
- **Repo explosion:** By 2007, US repo market reached US$10 trillion (equal to total commercial bank assets); intraday credit to single dealers approached US$100 billion.
- **Multiplier collapse:** In 2000, each US$1 of Central Bank money created >US$14 of Global Liquidity; by 2019 multiplier had collapsed to ~US$6.2—evidence of chronic liquidity shortage.

## Contrarian Ideas
- **Quantitative easing often raises bond yields, not lowers them:** Empirical evidence shows US QE periods associated with rising term premia (average +134bp per phase) because safe asset scarcity intensifies hoarding demand. Academic consensus (Gagnon 2016: −67bp per 10% of GDP) is empirically wrong.

- **The yield curve is a terrible recession predictor:** The standard 10-year minus 2-year slope is "at best a flaky predictor." What matters is the combination of slope AND curvature, and term premia (a liquidity phenomenon) are a key component explaining curvature.

- **Interest rates don't matter much anymore:** In a refinancing-dominated system, balance sheet capacity (can lenders roll over existing debts?) trumps the cost of capital. Zero interest rates are irrelevant if no one has balance sheet space to lend.

- **Money supply measures (M2, M3) are obsolete:** Traditional aggregates focused on deposit-taking institutions miss the dominant sources of liquidity—shadow banks, cross-border flows, collateral chains, and CICPs. Flow-of-funds analysis is the only valid approach.

- **Central Banks cause instability when they "normalize":** Attempts to shrink balance sheets or raise rates after crises trigger liquidity shortages and market dysfunction—because the system has become structurally dependent on Central Bank liquidity provision.

- **Strong dollars trigger global crises:** Conventional view sees dollar strength as safe-haven demand during crises. Reality: dollar strength causes crises by draining liquidity from dollar-indebted emerging markets and commodity producers.

- **China's financial immaturity is a strategic weakness, not just a development stage:** China's inability to provide safe assets and export yuan liquidity forces continued dollar dependence, constraining geopolitical options and making China vulnerable to US financial sanctions.

- **Globalization increases volatility, not stability:** As the world becomes more interconnected through cross-border capital flows, liquidity shocks amplify faster and spread wider. The procyclical monetary system based on USD and private credit guarantees bigger booms and busts.

- **Austerity made the debt problem worse:** Fiscal contraction reduced the supply of safe government bonds, increasing the scarcity premium and forcing private sector to manufacture flimsy substitutes (subprime securitizations), which eventually collapsed.

- **Academic economists systematically lag financial reality by decades:** Universities claim credit for innovations developed by practitioners through trial-and-error (the "Soviet-Harvard Department of Ornithology lectures birds on flight"). Policy-makers educated in outdated frameworks perpetuate destabilizing policies.

## Author's Key Proposals
1. **Track Global Liquidity, not interest rates or M2:** Use comprehensive flow-of-funds data covering traditional banks, central banks, shadow banks, and cross-border flows. Focus on the quality mix (private vs. Central Bank) as much as quantity.

2. **Build Global Liquidity Indexes for predictive asset allocation:** CrossBorder Capital's GLI framework leads markets by months and economies by quarters—essential for macro investing and risk management.

3. **Recognize the refinancing vs. new financing distinction:** Modern policy must focus on maintaining balance sheet capacity to roll over existing debts, not just lowering interest rates to encourage new borrowing.

4. **Expand the supply of genuine safe assets:** Fiscal austerity is counterproductive when safe assets are scarce. Sovereigns with fiscal capacity should issue more high-quality debt to satisfy global demand.

5. **Reform Central Bank frameworks to acknowledge shadow banking:** Regulation and monetary policy must adapt to the reality that wholesale funding markets and cross-border flows dominate liquidity creation.

6. **Prepare for yuan internationalization and digital currencies:** The coming decades will see the rise of yuan-denominated finance and state-backed digital currencies. Investors and policymakers must position for a multi-polar currency system.

7. **Understand the US-China liquidity interdependence:** "Understanding the Global Liquidity cycle may simply come down to interpreting the motives and actions of the two key Central Banks—the US Federal Reserve and the People's Bank of China."

8. **Use the forex risk index for currency forecasting:** Private sector liquidity minus Central Bank liquidity, advanced 12 months, provides robust currency predictions for both developed and emerging markets.

9. **Monitor term premia and credit spreads as liquidity indicators:** Term premia reflect safe asset scarcity; credit spreads reflect funding conditions. Together they reveal the state of Global Liquidity more reliably than policy rates.

10. **Accept that big Central Bank balance sheets are permanent:** "We are judged by historical standards, big balance sheets here to stay" (Andrew Hauser, Bank of England). The system has structurally adapted; attempts to shrink balance sheets cause dysfunction.

## Relevance to Trading/Investing
- **Asset allocation framework:** Global Liquidity drives macro-valuation shifts across asset classes. The GLI leads equities by 6-12 months and bonds by 3-6 months—essential for tactical allocation.

- **Currency forecasting:** The forex risk index (private minus Central Bank liquidity, advanced 12 months) provides Granger-causal predictions for USD, EM currencies, and other major pairs.

- **Credit market timing:** The credit enhancement channel (Fed holdings of risky assets) predicts corporate spread movements. Each US$1 trillion in Fed credit holdings tightens B-Aaa spreads by ~415bp.

- **Term structure trading:** Understanding the three Central Bank channels (liquidity provision raises premia, maturity transformation lowers premia, credit enhancement affects corporates) enables relative value trades in the yield curve.

- **EM exposure decisions:** Track the gap between EM liquidity indexes and US liquidity index—widening gaps (advanced 12 months) predict EM currency weakness and signal time to reduce exposure.

- **Regime identification:** Distinguish between new financing regimes (liquidity fuels growth and new investment) vs. refinancing regimes (liquidity merely prevents collapse). Asset classes perform very differently across regimes.

- **Dollar positioning:** Strong dollar periods drain global liquidity and trigger EM crises—time to reduce risk. Weak dollar periods fuel global liquidity booms—time to increase EM and commodity exposure.

- **Safe asset vs. risk asset allocation:** When safe asset scarcity is acute (narrow credit spreads despite high leverage), expect sudden liquidity events. Maintain larger cash buffers and reduce leverage.

- **Central Bank policy anticipation:** "Fed watching" and increasingly "PBoC watching" are critical. But focus on balance sheet composition (the three channels) not just interest rate guidance.

- **Long-term structural positioning:** The coming multi-polar currency world and rise of digital yuan will reshape global finance. Position for regionalization, capital controls, and dollar hegemony challenges.
