## Chapter 7: Central Banks (continued) — Eurozone Crisis and ECB Response

### 2010-2012 Eurozone Banking Crisis

**Triggered by Greece's 2010 admission** of full indebtedness and impending sovereign default. Fears widened to Mediterranean and Irish economies with sovereign bond yields rising sharply.

**Key structural flaw:** The 'safe' asset in the Eurozone system is not the Euro, but the **German Bund**. As peripheral sovereign bond yields rose against Bunds, the likelihood of default increased, which drove more sovereign yields up — creating a **doom loop** between banks and sovereign issuers.

**Two factors exacerbated the 2010 panic:**

1. **ECB's legal framework (Article 123)** disallowed purchase of sovereign bonds, preventing the Central Bank from immediately copying QE policies of the Federal Reserve

2. **Credit quality threshold** for Eurozone sovereign bond eligibility set at an optimistically high **BBB-minus** in 2005. When rating agencies downgraded sovereign bonds below this threshold, investing banks suddenly became illiquid — losing vital ECB refinancing access and hastening the rush into safe German Bunds

### ECB Crisis Response Programs

**May 2010: Securities Market Programme (SMP)** — discretionary purchases of Eurozone sovereign securities in secondary markets, alongside the **European Financial Stabilisation Mechanism (EFSM)**. SMP injected liquidity but markets remained skeptical.

**Ireland crisis example (November 2010):** Anglo-Irish Banking Group alone needed upwards of **EUR30 billion** — a sum the Irish Government could not easily borrow. Irish bond yields traded near distressed Greek bonds. Solution: Irish Government issued a **EUR31 billion promissory note** (IOU) to the bank, which used it as collateral for Central Bank of Ireland emergency liquidity assistance (ELA).

**December 2011: Longer term refinancing operations (LTRO)** under new ECB President **Mario Draghi** — 1% interest rate, 3-year term, with Eurozone government securities, mortgage-backed securities, and commercial paper all accepted as collateral. Extensively used by hard-pressed banks in Greece, Ireland, Italy, Spain.

**February 2012: LTRO2** provided around **EUR800 billion** to Eurozone banks with over **half a trillion** in low-interest loans.

**Critical shift July 2012** — renewed fears over Eurozone sovereign default prompted Draghi's famous commitment:

> "...is ready to do whatever it takes to preserve the Euro. And believe me, it will be enough"

**August 2012: Outright Monetary Transactions (OMT)** — replaced SMP with **no prior limits on duration or size**, conditional on beneficiary country adherence to adjustment programme.

### September 2014 Deflationary Response and QE

Persistent Eurozone inflation slide prompted two bond purchase programmes: **Covered Bond Purchasing Programme (CBPP3)** and **Asset-Backed Securities Programme (ABSPP)**. Extended into conventional QE in **January 2015** with sovereign bond purchases up to **EUR60 billion per month**.

**Targeted Longer-Term Financing Operations (TLTROs):** June 2014 (TLTRO I), March 2016 (TLTRO II), March 2019 (TLTRO III) — offered banks long-term funding at attractive conditions linked to loans to non-financial corporations and households, designed to encourage banks to lend into the real economy.

### ECB Balance Sheet Expansion

ECB consolidated balance sheet leaped from **EUR806 billion (1999)** to **EUR4.7 trillion (end-2018)**. Biggest driver: outright security purchases jumped from 2-3% to **near 62% by end-2018**.

**End-2018 breakdown:**
- Outright security purchases: **61.7%**
- LTRO: **15.4%**
- Gold & Forex Reserves: **15.4%**
- Other monetary operations: **7.3%**

**Late 2019:** ECB balance sheet expanding again thanks to Draghi's commitment to restart QE as 'final' act as ECB President.

### Concerns About ECB's Efficacy

**Debate within ECB** about negative interest rate policy efficacy. Higher commercial bank profits unlikely to achieve central policy goal given still parlous state of private bank balance sheets and ECB's likely inability to facilitate another bailout.

**European banks are 'over-banked'** by too fragmented a banking industry. Persistent fragmentation woes point to deep-seated structural problems about Euro-system operation. Impact of negative rates on credit supply suggests **still more quantitative easing is coming to Europe**.

## Chapter 8: Cross-Border Capital Flows

### Dollar Supremacy and Global Liquidity

> "follow the money" — **Watergate informant Deep Throat**

**International investment is big, procyclical and volatile.** Finance has frequently paralleled globalization of trade because open economies need to diversify asset bases to hedge against external shocks. Large volumes of trade invoiced and transacted in US dollars explain why trading nations hold dollar-denominated assets. Firms with large dollar-denominated liabilities invoice sales in US dollars to reduce currency mismatch.

**Global Liquidity rose paced by faster growth in cross-border capital flows**, expanding breathlessly from early 1990s.

**Post-GFC shifts:**
- Anti-globalization backlash with tighter regulations; gross flows slumped
- **China muscled forward in importance**
- Digital cross-border payments established a foothold
- Offshore low-tax centres forced to limit activities
- More stable **FDI** (spreading latest technologies) now comprises larger portion of flows

### America's International Balance Sheet

US international assets settled close to **125% of GDP**; liabilities to foreigners peaked over **180% of GDP**. Some analysts compare America's international activity to a giant hedge fund: 'short' cash and 'long' foreign risk assets.

**Unique feature:** Dollar's dominance allows US to issue predominantly 'safe' assets (currency and Treasury securities). Foreign sectors demand these, purchasing them with inflows. Americans then use inflows to purchase risky international assets (stocks, foreign bonds, FDI).

**Dollar dominance origins:** Many Emerging Markets invoice **70-80% of trade in US dollars** while only exporting around **10-15% directly to US**. Similarly, around half of Japanese and nearly one-quarter of European trade.

### Three Phases of US Dollar Supremacy

1. **Gold Exchange Standard (1945-1971):** Bretton Woods implementation post-WW2
2. **Oil Exchange Standard (1974-1989):** Secret 1974 agreement between Treasury Secretary **William Simon** and Saudi Arabia for oil pricing in dollars, creating massive demand via petrodollar recycling
3. **Emerging Market Exchange Standard (1990-present):** Berlin Wall fall (1989) and Soviet collapse, plus **Deng Xiaoping's reforms** fostered competition for international capital

> "By a stroke of the pen, America had cleverly created a new demand for US dollars" — on Saudi Arabia's undisclosed Treasury debt purchases

### China's Role in Dollar Dominance

- China adopted dollar as benchmark in **1994**, making Yuan convertible in current account
- China's WTO entry (2001) boosted dollar usage despite Chinese-led supply chains being dollar-denominated
- **China is now world's biggest US dollar creditor** and major buyer of EM assets
- Sustains dollar system through: (1) invoicing trade in dollars, (2) large government debt holdings, (3) fiscal/monetary policy supporting dollar-oriented trading

### Post-GFC Dollar Reinforcement via Swap Lines

**Federal Reserve swap lines** made the Fed de facto **"international lender of last resort"** — provided **$10.1 trillion** (standardized) December 2007–August 2010. **$8.0 trillion of the $10.1 trillion** was taken up by the **ECB alone**.

**Adam Tooze (2018):** Swap lines reinforced dollar dominance, questioned Euro stability. The decision now rests with the White House per Tooze — politicized arrangements, not just "friendly nations."

China trying to develop a **Yuan swap network** (RMB 350 billion agreement with ECB) but faces obstacles: US regulations confine dollar use, making China's dollar funding volatile with refinancing risk.

### Shadow Monetary System and Dollar Financing

**Shadow monetary base:** Collateral and cross-border pools of offshore Eurodollars as increasingly important source of global liquidity. Comprises "safe" assets (low haircuts, high re-hypothecation rates) — high-quality public/private debts, offshore cash pools.

- **35% of world trade financed by banks**; ~80% of bank trade financing is dollar-denominated
- GVCs are highly finance-intensive: firms need working capital for inventories, suppliers need "accounts receivable" financing
- **Dollar exchange rate as barometer:** Lending in dollars grows faster when dollar is weak, slower when strong
- Strong dollar = subdued GVC activity and lower trade-to-GDP ratio; weak dollar = high trade-to-GDP ratio

### The Dollar Cycle — Three Transmission Channels

1. **EM Policy Response:** EM economies targeting dollar exchange rates respond to shocks via monetary expansion (easing when dollar weak, tightening when strong)
2. **Offshore Redisposition:** Extra dollar supply in offshore Eurodollar wholesale markets improves funding via loans/swaps
3. **Collateral Effect:** Weaker dollar improves local currency collateral value, increasing funding opportunities

**International credit channel** operates through global bank balance sheets (Rey 2013; Bruno and Shin 2015). Weak dollar improves collateral for borrowers; strong dollar reduces collateral value, causing adverse balance sheet effects and potential recession.

### Large-Scale Capital Shifts: Core vs Periphery

Periphery composition changed dramatically: 1970s-1980s dominated by G7 (UK, Germany, Japan); post-1990s increasingly **Emerging Markets, especially China**. Capital shifts rapidly and sometimes violently between core and periphery, then back again.

**US current account debate:** Authorities long blamed imbalances on "foreigners' inappropriate exchange rate policies." Counterargument: US deficit reflects domestic savings shortage and capital account surplus, not lack of competitiveness. **US is not required to run permanent deficit** to supply world with dollars — only needs to accumulate long-term assets against short-term dollar claims.

Historical examples of pressure: **Super 301** trade initiative vs Japan (late 1980s); "Japan-bashing" and "China-bashing" to force currency appreciation; **1985 Plaza Accord** successfully forced Yen revaluation.

### Gross Capital Flows Surge and GFC Warning

**Gross flows surged to 19.1% of World GDP in 2006, rising to 22.3% in 2007** before GFC — a stark warning missed by policymakers. By 2018: flows slid back to **6% of GDP** (roughly 1990s levels).

For six major economies (US, China, Japan, UK, Germany, France): gross flows peaked **nearly 40% of GDP in 2007**, skidding to **barely 10% in 2018**. Gross capital inflows into Advanced Economies reached **26% of GDP (2007)**, gross outflows **25%** (combined **51%**).

**Banking flows suffer more than 2x the volatility** of other broad flow types; in crises **4-5 times larger** (ECB 2016). Elasticity between World GDP growth and gross flows as high as **2.5 times**. Net flows failed to flash warnings — research focused on current account imbalances, not gross flow sizes.

### Foreign Remittances

Over **three-quarters of cross-border remittances** flow from rich economies to Emerging Markets. More stable than conventional capital flows (cannot be suddenly withdrawn). World Bank: annual remittance flows to low-/middle-income countries rose to **record $689 billion (2018)**, up ~10% from $633 billion (2017) — larger than inward FDI.

**Top recipients (2018):** India ($79bn), China ($67bn), Mexico ($36bn), Philippines ($34bn), Egypt ($29bn).

### The "Global Savings Glut" Critique

**Bernanke (2005)** coined the term, but challenged by evidence:
1. Cross-border financing cannot be inferred from net (as opposed to gross) capital flows
2. Defining the 'border' separating investor residence from beneficial asset ownership is critical
3. Market interest rates are determined in broader credit markets, not simply by net savings

**GFC and Asian Crisis** more likely caused by **excessive elasticity of the international monetary/financial system** and **depth of Western asset markets** (New York, London), not high Asian savings rates.

**Key distinction: gross vs net flows.** Asset and liability flows show high correlations, grow faster in gross than net terms. High correlation more likely explained by **common risk factors** (e.g., Global Liquidity shock) than by differential returns.

**Gross flows can drive the current account** rather than passively accommodating it — e.g., large bank lending expansion to foreigners leads to faster export growth; increase in inward FDI leads to step-up in export activity. **Financial sector can initiate monetary shocks**: mid-2000s US current account deficit widened to historical highs while dollar soared in value, contrary to textbook predictions.

### Offshore Swap and Eurodollar Markets

**Eurodollars:** Unsecured foreign currency deposits held at banks outside currencies' legal jurisdictions. Daily Eurodollar funding **around 3-4x larger than Fed Funds** (~$150 billion). Started 1960s from Soviet Bloc concerns about US dollar safety; competition attracted Middle Eastern oil money to London; more recently, US multinationals hold large offshore dollar deposits.

**Fed chose since 1990** to impose **zero-reserve requirement** on Eurodollar deposits (treating as close substitutes for Fed Funds).

**Non-bank offshore dollar activity:** BIS data: non-bank US dollar-denominated debt outside US at **~$17 trillion** — easily exceeding domestic US bank lending and similar to interbank liabilities (~$16.9 trillion). Pool has flatlined since GFC, though bonds rose from ~45% (2000) to over 56% (2018).

Stock of cross-border/foreign currency borrowing by non-banks **exceeded 2008 peak of $9 trillion**, now touching nearly **70% of total** dollar-denominated debts. Can also be created through **derivative transactions** (currency swaps), growing in popularity among Central Banks.

### Carry Trades and Currency Volatility

**Carry trades:** Borrowing in low-cost jurisdictions, simultaneously investing in higher-return markets (e.g., borrow in Yen, invest in US dollars; borrow in dollars, invest in higher-yielding EM bonds). Cross-border borrowing creates **currency mismatch**, making flows highly sensitive to exchange rate movements.

**2002-2008 pre-GFC period:** Dollar fell ~one-third; cross-border banking flows soared. US-to-Europe flows jumped from $2 billion (2002) to **$1.54 trillion (2007)**; return leg from $56 billion to **$59 trillion (2007)**. Heightened currency volatility causes sharp contractions in cross-border activity.

### Covered Interest Rate Parity and Basis

**Basis swap spread** measures how much more a bank pays to borrow dollars via roundabout route (borrowing in foreign currency then swapping) versus a straightforward dollar loan priced at LIBOR. **Spread reflects global scarcity of dollar safe assets.**

**CIP theory** says positions should be entirely fungible (no basis). **Reality since 2008:** Strong evidence of **persistent positive basis**, challenging assumptions of riskless arbitrage and market efficiency. Textbook tendency to focus only on traditional banks needs correction.

### Global Financial Centre Rankings (2019)

**Top 5:** New York (1), London (2), Hong Kong (3), Singapore (4), Shanghai (5)

**Major climbers from 2009:** Shanghai (+30, from 35 to 5), Beijing (+42, from 51 to 9), Dubai (+11, from 23 to 12), Shenzhen (NA to 14). **Decliners:** Boston (-4), Frankfurt (-2).

Standout: **Huge jumps in Chinese money centre rankings** highlight growing sway of Chinese money. Pacific Asian centres rising at expense of East Coast American and European centres.

### Pull vs Push Factors in Cross-Border Flows

Constant debate: are flows result of **'pull'** (attractive foreign investments) or **'push'** (surplus capital from major centres)? Experience shows **both factors dominate**. Tax avoidance and lower reporting requirements help explain rise of offshore centres.

**Zucman (2013):** **8% of global financial wealth** held offshore, of which **at least 6%** (~$4-5 trillion) officially unrecorded.

### Capital Flow Structure by Major Economies (2005-2018)

- **US:** FDI 28.2%, Equity 11.3%, Debt 31.6%, Banking 28.8%
- **China:** FDI 44.3%, Equity 8.5%, Debt 41.2%
- **UK:** Banking flows **58.7%** of average capital movements (London's dominance)
- Most volatile categories: banking flows (std dev 56.1% of mean), debt flows (46.2%)
- Decline in volatile banking/debt flow share may improve international stability
- Chinese foreign assets: official gold/forex reserves **58.3%** — emphasizes government's active dollar recycling role; private sector plays small part

## Chapter 9: China and the Emerging Markets

### The Chinese Monetary and Financial System

**Total Chinese liquidity:** Near RMB200 trillion (~US$28 trillion), with **State-owned banks (SOBs)** as dominant lenders.

**Shadow banks** account for ~1/3 of Chinese system liquidity (smaller share than US equivalents). Shadow banking averages ~11% of money raised, included in official **total social financing** estimates. Consists of trust banks and non-bank financial institutions, often against financial collateral.

**Critical dynamics:** Commercial bank lending via SOBs shows stability; shadow banks experience pronounced credit cycles — fluctuations in overall Chinese liquidity are driven by larger swings in shadow banking. Shadow banks may indicate attempts to evade mainstream monetary controls. Funded through wholesale money/capital markets, dependent on buoyant collateral values. Many are subsidiaries of major banks; some tainted by association with local authorities and dubious real estate deals.

### China's Financial Immaturity

**Current account and capital flows:** China historically ran large domestic savings surplus. Recent slide in current account amid capital outflow restrictions has drained forex reserves. **China's fundamental balance** (current account + net FDI) averaged ~US$400 billion (2007-2015), more recently roughly halved — partly from slowing inward FDI, more importantly from strong outward FDI (Belt and Road Initiative).

**Structural imbalances in foreign balance sheet:**
- Gross foreign asset base: ~US$7.5 trillion (vs $10 trillion Germany, >$25 trillion UK)
- Not only far smaller relatively, but heavily skewed towards safe asset forex reserves (average **58%**, 2005-2018)
- Gross liabilities dominated by inward FDI (average **39%** of gross assets)
- Chinese foreign banking assets: 21% of total (Germany 44%)
- Outward FDI stock: ~15% (Germany 21%)
- Gross portfolio investment into China: 12% (Germany 33-35%)
- Gross flows roughly **three times as volatile** as Germany's

**The recycling problem:** Dollar proceeds from net capital stock to foreigners are re-exported as dollars and recycled back into US Treasuries.

> "Chinese gross banking activity is dominated by Chinese residents borrowing US dollars: lending in Yuan to foreigners and foreign purchases of Chinese domestic bonds is to date minimal. China is re-exporting US dollars, when she should be exporting Yuan. This has to change."

**Bond market immaturity:** Foreigners' holdings of Chinese bonds barely 1/10 of comparable German figures. Chinese international bank lending could easily double (or quadruple if FDI stripped out). Gaps suggest absence of: (1) domestic sovereign bond market with reserve currency status, (2) international market in RMB-denominated trade credit.

### China's Currency Strategy and RMB Internationalization

**Unlikely major dollar reserve accumulation.** Proposal for **de facto regional currency bloc** — Euro-like arrangement with exchange rate stability within bloc, greater flexibility between blocs. De facto already happening: correlation between CNY and other Asian units has risen from 0.27 (2015) to 0.52 (2019).

**Gurley-Shaw-Goldsmith view:** Financial systems naturally increase liquidity as economies develop. China's development lags institutionally, forcing international investors to rely on US capital markets and creating **structural shortage of safe asset collateral**.

**China's monetary system vs US:** China has larger Central Bank, bigger traditional bank assets, slightly smaller shadow banking; total liquidity ~US$65.6 trillion vs US$29.2 trillion.

**Pathways to greater Yuan usage:**
- **Transparent government bond market** attracting foreign investors (large, high-yielding Chinese bonds should attract investment)
- **Digital Yuan** via platforms like Alipay, WeChat — containing both electronic and "cyber-backed" digital monies
- Pricing exports in Yuan to develop Yuan-based trade credit
- **Euro-yuan offshore markets** likely to develop, parallel to existing Eurodollar markets
- Result: **gross balance sheet size increases** and diversifies toward world average

**Recent internationalization moves:** Hong Kong Stock Exchange's 2019 bid for London Stock Exchange; termination of quotas on foreign investment; further access through US ADR program. **US political resistance** likely — some experts suggest imposing hard limits on US portfolio flows into China.

### Capital Inflows as Double-Edged Sword for Emerging Markets

> "...every Emerging Market crisis is first-and-foremost a currency crisis" — Barings research (1994)

**Three mechanisms of capital flow impact on EM asset markets:**

1. **Volume Effects:** Foreign institutional investors large-sized relative to small EM asset markets; international/indigenous funds held offshore can move quickly as conditions change

2. **Infrastructure Effects:** Underdeveloped EM financial markets mean credit booms from capital inflows cannot be fully sterilized; net inflows frequently lead to domestic credit booms through second-round effects

3. **Policy Effects:** Central Banks targeting exchange rates allow capital flows to be monetized through large forex reserve pools; often-underdeveloped markets mean money/credit aggregates cannot be fully sterilized. Effect not always symmetric: capital outflows may initially tighten credit, but threat of negative second-round effects can lead to devaluation rather than economic slump

### Monetary Transmission and the Balassa-Samuelson Effect

Rising real exchange rates follow positive capital flow shocks (often induced by underlying productivity). In supply-constrained, informationally-determined EM economies, most goods prices are determined internationally while wages tend to be "sticky." Therefore, increase in real exchange rate is expressed through **rising asset prices**. High productivity growth Asian economies (Singapore, Hong Kong, Korea) enjoy large asset price gains and speculative real estate developments.

### Liquidity Shocks and Asset Price Booms

Initial liquidity shock appears through faster export growth and/or stronger capital inflows. Forex reserve changes transmitted through monetary base to broader credit. **Correlation coefficient 0.807** between EM forex reserves and EM high-powered money growth.

When capital inflows are translated into local currencies by Central Banks and accumulated as forex reserves, increase in base money provides new funding for bank credit expansion. Mechanisms can work in reverse unless local policy-makers neutralize effects through offsetting asset purchases/sales.

Even with fully flexible exchange rates, EMs remain vulnerable to global factors (Rey 2015). International capital movements have **exaggerated impact** on EM economies, making asset price bubbles more likely due to **procyclical second-round effects**.

### US Dollar Strength and EM Performance

**Dollar weakness associated with positive EM financial market performance.** Strong dollar adversely affects EMs through broader tighter US policy AND direct negative effects on foreign borrowers repaying in more-expensive dollars.

**Complex feedbacks:** US economy compels authorities to maintain domestic collateral values (asset prices) by pumping liquidity. Nominal dollar has historically taken the brunt by depreciating long-term. EM policy-makers follow US easing, weaken their dollar rate, which reduces debt burden but encourages more cross-border borrowing — raising dollar debt levels and increasing cross-currency mismatch.

US ease of international borrowing allows low savings rate and large fiscal deficit. **US trade deficit driven by manufacturing** — de-industrialization undermines long-term real exchange rate. Cross-border capital inflows back into US may reinforce initial Fed easing, promote domestic asset bubbles, compel retightening, and trigger debt crises back in EMs.

### EM Crisis Patterns and Self-Insurance

Direct US dollar effects may have lessened as China and EMs borrow more in local currencies. Nonetheless, **one-fifth of all surges in cross-border capital flows end in national financial crises** (IMF). **EM economies 3x more likely to experience crisis** after surges.

Though capital inflows initially appear to boost GDP, EMs suffering above-average capital flow volatility grow substantially slower. Many EMs self-insured after 1997/1998 Asian Crisis — accumulated huge reserves through SWFs and forex reserve cushions. EM economies collectively hold ~**US$7 trillion in forex reserves**.

**Recent unusual pattern:** Net private capital outflows have exceeded inflows. Less true when China excluded — large-scale "capital flight" from China 2014-2017 (coincident with Xi Jinping's anti-corruption drive).

### Chinese Liquidity and EM Capital Flows

**Strong co-movement** (correlation 0.505) between capital flows to EMs and Chinese domestic liquidity. Kiel Institute research: each percentage point rise in Chinese GDP growth leads to **1.7% increase in Chinese capital outflows** as % of GDP. Similar positive "spill" from looser Chinese monetary policy. US push factors tend to be statistically weaker.

### Conclusion on EM Flows

Act of benchmarking national currencies against US dollar leads to **procyclical and volatile EM investment cycle**. Not helped by EMs being caught between capital "push" from US-led Global Liquidity cycle and "pull" from fast-growing Chinese economy. **Archetypal EM Crisis:** follows Global Liquidity boom, involves heavy foreign borrowing by local banks. Rich detail hidden behind net capital flow numbers, which obscures China's financial immaturity and **underuse of Yuan instruments**.

## Chapter 10: The Liquidity Transmission Mechanism

### The Financial Economy vs. Real Economy

**Financial cycle** (Borio 2012): Febrile interactions between investors'/credit providers' risk appetites, collateral values, and funding availability. Upswing in credit/liquidity drives up stocks, real estate, and risk assets. By increasing collateral value, enables more borrowing until cycle peaks and reverses.

**Financial cycles differ from economic cycles:** Can be different lengths and amplitudes. Tend to amplify prevailing macroeconomic dislocations. Upswing presages future economic trends; downswing frequently coincides with major banking crises and recessions.

**Traditional view:** Financial flows are passive accounting counterparts of savings/investment. **In practice:** Financial markets moved by liquidity inflows/outflows and risk-taking behavior changes. Thinking of intermediaries as undertaking "risk-sharing": greater balance sheet size = more risk capacity. More liquidity = bigger balance sheets, more risk-sharing, less system-wide risk.

**ECB recognition:** Obtaining higher asset valuation is the implicit rationale for QE/LSAP by major central banks. The role of the central bank as issuer of ultimate safe and liquid asset and its capacity as intermediary and risk absorber of last resort come to the fore.

### Global Liquidity and Asset Returns

CrossBorder Capital data shows **strong correlation** between World financial asset returns and Global Liquidity (1981-2019). Close co-movement hides complex feedbacks between liquidity flows and investors' desired asset allocation.

**Flow of funds model:** Liquidity drives investors' risk appetite and asset allocation. Rising collateral values positively feed back to underpin new liquidity creation. Bull markets typically need a fundamental theme to stimulate and sustain investor interest alongside liquidity.

### Financial Assets as Luxury Goods

Cross-country relationship: every 10% growth in per capita incomes associated with **near-15% increase** in financial asset value per head (power function loading = **1.48**). Economists describe financial assets as **luxury goods** due to high income elasticity of demand.

**Goldsmith's financial interrelations ratio** (intangible assets / tangible assets): Each country's financial development goes through well-defined stages characterized by rising ratio, typically reaching limit slightly above one with progressively smaller increments.

**Pension fund example:** Workers subscribing at twenty, retiring at sixty-five need assets with **duration of 45 years** to match liabilities. As pension entitlement expands, demand for longer-dated assets increases. More roundabout production techniques (tying up capital longer) tend to be most profitable.

### P/E vs P/M Ratios: Choosing the Right Valuation Benchmark

**P/M ratio strongly favored** for three reasons:
1. More intuitive framework with direct link to asset allocation
2. Liquidity easily incorporated (P/M correlation with excess liquidity: **0.57** vs P/E-P/M correlation: **0.30**)
3. More stable and mean-reverting — during profit recessions, 'E' collapses causing P/E to surge and distort valuations

**P/E limitations:** Compromised when earnings are volatile or zero; less clear at aggregate market level what it means. **P/M advantage:** Relates to investors (people) rather than investments (securities).

### Two Key Liquidity Transmission Channels

**1. Quantity Channel:** Absolute volume of liquidity affects risk appetite. Plentiful liquidity = widespread risk-taking; scarce liquidity = market panics and flight to safety. Observable through interest rate spreads, risk premia, maturity spread, yield curve slope.

**2. Quality Channel:** Hierarchical nature of money affects price/exchange rate. Vibrant private sector attracts investment, strengthens exchange rate. Central Bank aggressive supply weakens exchange rate (quality mix deteriorates). Better quality mix favors corporate bonds over government securities.

### Safe Assets and the Demand-Supply Framework

**Safe asset definition:** Assets with ability to cover expected liabilities; derives from **liquidity**. **10-year US Treasury** is the canonical safe asset.

**Why deemed safe:** (1) Corporate bonds riskier due to higher default risk, (2) High liquidity reduces systemic risk by reducing frictions and default odds.

**Liquidity impact:** Greater liquidity availability reduces systemic risk, causing investors to cut safe asset holdings and shift to risk assets (equities). **Reverse:** Tight liquidity raises systemic risk, causing rush to safe assets (cash deposits).

### QE and Term Premia — The Paradox

**Conventional narrative:** Central Banks control policy rates through forward guidance, buying government bonds via LSAP/QE to drive down term premia. Event studies claim 10% GDP in asset purchases reduces 10-year yields by **50-100 bp**.

**Empirical evidence contradicts this:** Term premia **rose** by average **135 bp** under QE programmes, then **fell** as QE expired (quantitative tightening).

> "The error is that the standard narrative ignores the fact that the demand for safe assets can itself change as Central Banks signal their actions."

**How QE actually affects bonds:**
- LSAP reduces supply of bonds to private sector: (1) decreases duration risk, (2) creates scarcity effects
- BUT policy announcement + liquidity injection simultaneously: reduces perceived systemic risks, increases confidence, encourages private sector to cut demand for safe assets
- **Demand curve shifts leftward**, driving up term premia

**Result:** More liquidity via QE **steepens yield curves** and widens term premia. Less liquidity flattens term structure. Bank profitability rests on yield curve slope — critical for real economy transmission.

**Empirical confirmation:** CrossBorder Capital GLI demonstrates high correlation between US liquidity (advanced 9 months) and 10-2 year Treasury yield spread. **Liquidity is strongly one-way Granger causal** (p=0.0335) to yield curve slope.

**Gagnon's QE studies:** Standardized results show 10% GDP benchmark leads to average **67 bp** reduction in bond yields. **Author profoundly disagrees:** "What works in practice seemingly does not work in theory!" More liquidity drives down price of safe assets and up price of risky assets. During abundant liquidity: yield curves steepen, credit spreads narrow, equities outperform bonds, value stocks outperform growth stocks.

### Five Broader QE Channels (Bank of England Framework)

1. **Policy signalling:** Commitment to inflation targets, anchors low rate expectations
2. **Portfolio rebalancing:** Asset purchases raise prices, investors shift to higher-yielding assets, lowering borrowing costs
3. **Liquidity provision:** Improved market trading (effects persist only while purchases ongoing)
4. **Confidence effects:** Boosts investment and spending
5. **Bank lending effects:** Higher reserves encourage lending

**Most important:** Portfolio Balance Channel — purchases targeted toward **long-term assets held by non-bank financial institutions** (insurers, pension funds), encouraging shift toward riskier investments.

### Exchange Rates and the Quality Theory of Money

Exchange rates depend on **quality mix** between private sector ('good') and Central Bank ('bad') liquidity:
- **Central Bank liquidity** = additional currency supply — **weakens exchange rate**
- **Private sector liquidity** = economy's cash flow generation — moves procyclically, **strengthens exchange rate**

**Key distinction:** The *difference* between private and CB liquidity predicts future exchange rate movements. The *sum* (not difference) determines bond term premia. This contrasts with standard monetarist view.

### Risk Assets: Real Estate and Equities

**More liquidity:** Reduces average duration (D) available to private sector, cuts default rates, increases duration targets (D*), raises term premia on safe assets, lowers risk premia on risk assets, **drives hunt for more duration** — pushing risk asset prices higher.

**Real estate:** Lead-times between liquidity impulse and reaction ~**3 years** (positive and statistically significant). Each 10-point GLI increase leads to ~**2% annual rise in real estate values** (slightly more commercial, slightly less housing).

**Equities — three key relationships:**
1. Quantitative flow of liquidity drives bonds and real estate
2. Quality mix determines exchange rates
3. **Positioning within portfolios drives equity returns** — equities may be the marginal/swing asset class used for quick portfolio mix adjustments

**Contrarian opportunity:** Being contrarian in equities means doing opposite of the crowd **when it is at its most greedy and fearful extremes**.

### Cross-Country Asset Allocation Patterns

**Regional patterns:**
- **US investors:** Highest equity exposure (36%)
- **Japanese investors:** Lowest equity (20%), highest bonds (45%)
- **British (35%) and European (34%):** Near American levels
- **EM investors (24%):** Near Japanese equity; highest liquid assets (45%)

**World aggregate:** ~31% equities (close to long-run trend of 28% since 1990). Historical trend: 28% equities, 40% bonds, 29% liquid.

**Historical extremes:** Peaked 38.1% August 2000 (Y2K bubble); 34% October 2007 (>2 sd above average); post-GFC trough under 19% (~2.5 sd below average). Mean-reversion is clear.

**Factors driving variation:** Cultural, institutional, demographic reasons alongside economic factors. Low per capita income economies: more liquid assets. Developed with young workforces: favor equities. As workforces age: favor bonds. Most factors slow-moving — exception being **changes in risk appetite**.

## Chapter 11: Financial Crises and Safe Assets

### The Financial Cycle

Modern finance operates as a **global banking system** where private firms act largely independently of domestic economies. The 2007-2008 GFC stemmed from **unsustainable bank balance sheets**, not current account problems — resulted in **Global Liquidity collapse**.

**Standard economics ignores money and liquidity.** Markets assumed frictionless. Reality: **illiquidity is the ultimate market friction** — when credit supply breaks down or uncertainty spikes, investors hoard safe assets. **Efficient market hypothesis does not apply** to financial crises.

**Global Liquidity framework built on two interacting concepts:**
1. **Funding Liquidity:** Ability to convert monetary assets into goods/instruments domestically and internationally
2. **Market Liquidity:** Financial market depth — ease of trading, cost of converting financial assets into money

**Dominant players:** US Federal Reserve, China's PBoC, cross-border Eurodollar markets. US liquidity depends on wholesale markets receiving Chinese/corporate cash pools (CICPs). These are leveraged and collateral-based, suffering structural shortage of high-quality safe instruments — forcing demand into riskier, longer maturities and flakier substitutes.

**Feedback loops:** Liquidity cycle swings drive risk appetite, which positively feeds back to funding liquidity via market liquidity, amplifying the cycle. Market liquidity + funding liquidity + risk appetite = **financial cycle**.

> **Rey (2015):** "The Global Financial Cycle can be associated with surges and dry outs in capital flows, booms and busts in asset prices and crises..."

### Is Stability Destabilising?

**Post-GFC improvements:** More capital, less leverage, better regulation (Basel III). **Liquidity Coverage Ratios (LCRs)** require 100% liquidity matched for 30 days per foreign subsidiary. But **regulatory arbitrage persists** — pre-GFC, tight US regulation forced loan sales to unconstrained European banks, creating amplifying "liquidity pump."

**Critical failures in understanding financial structure:**
- Rapid subprime accumulation; dramatic loss of AAA-grade borrowing status
- Widespread uncontrolled derivatives, maturity/forex mismatches
- Growing securitization and off-balance sheet financing
- **Lehman bankruptcy sanctioned** (2008) despite its crucial role — short-sighted error
- **Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act (1999)** removed Glass-Steagall separation (in place since 1933), **accelerating concentration**: by 2010s, **>3/4 of American financial assets** controlled by ten largest conglomerates (vs barely 10% in 1990)
- Raises specter of **'too big to fail'** and forces more interventionist US authorities

**"Too interconnected to fail"** is the real problem:

> **Bernanke's forceful determination:** "Making sure 'It' doesn't happen here..." (famous anti-deflation speech, November 2002)

**Current debt concerns:** Global debt growing faster among non-financial companies. US dollar debt of non-banks outside US at record levels (Robert Triffin International Institute 2019). Substantial currency mismatches and high private sector forex exposure with rising reliance on international bond markets.

### The Refinancing Problem

Modern finance has created greater bias toward **refinancing existing debts** rather than providing new credit. Shadow banks typically involved in two-thirds of funding for "re-packaging" existing loans; only one-third for new credit (IMF data).

**Intermediation chain fragility:** When A lends to B, B to C, ... to Z, any break can notionally bankrupt **"twenty-six" firms**, not just one. Widespread use of **market-based collateral** introduces hierarchy, making the system both more procyclical and more dependent on Central Bank interventions.

**Electronic/digital money implications:** This dependence can only deepen. Ongoing dominance of **refinance over new financing** makes access to credit less important than interest rates. Emphasizes that **QE and QT are critical policy levers**.

**Minsky's Financial Instability Hypothesis:**
- Three financing regimes: (1) **hedged** (stable), (2) **speculative** (needs liquidity for rollovers), (3) **Ponzi** (needs liquidity AND rising asset prices)
- "Stability begets instability" — hedged regimes regress toward speculative and Ponzi over time

**International integration has jumped** — velocity of cross-border flows and scale of spillovers questions continuing validity of **Mundell's trilemma** (open economies operating independent monetary policies). Domestic economies can never be fully insulated from international monetary shocks.

### Safe Assets and Modern Capitalism

With vast stock-piles of past capital accumulation, modern capitalism must operate a **huge refinancing system** demanding stable credit instruments for debt rollovers. Gold is outstandingly elastic; State money often compromised by low inflation mandates and laissez-faire ideology.

> "Money does not manage itself" — Bagehot

**The paradox of safe assets:** Story of financial crises = story of failure of safe assets. Investors accept losses on risky investments but not on supposedly safe ones. Policy-makers try to make the system safer by encouraging safe asset investment, but by **not supplying enough** and hastily bailing out, they create **moral hazard** — making risky investments safe. Meanwhile, some most dangerous investments = those once believed completely safe.

### A Worldwide Shortage of 'Safe' Assets

**IMF (2012) formally defines 'safe' asset as:** providing (1) low market/credit risks, (2) high market liquidity, (3) limited inflation risks, (4) low exchange rate risks, (5) limited idiosyncratic risks. In practice, definition often fuzzy — involves geopolitical and psychological factors (trust, belief). A safe asset is 'safe' only if others agree.

**Properties of safe assets:**
1. **Rise in value during bad times** (negative beta to market)
2. **Deep and liquid markets** — ability to trade freely in large size
3. **Consistently lower yields** — Krishnamurthy and Vissing-Jorgensen (2012) estimate US Treasury yields ~**70 bp lower** on average due to safety/liquidity features
4. **Greater fiscal capacity** — low sensitivity of yields to debt-to-GDP increases, backing of powerful State (e.g., USA)

**Safe asset supply shortfall explained by:**
- Austerity policies restricting government securities issuance
- Tighter bank regulations limiting liability growth
- New liquidity rules increasing banks' need to hold high-quality liquid assets (reducing availability for repos)
- Even prudential rules have increased opportunity cost of insufficient liquid assets
- **Lack of collateral/liquidity amplified by weakened supply creates still greater demand** — exaggerating the shortfall

**IMF (2012) estimate:** Number of sovereigns with 'safe' debt has fallen, potentially removing ~**$9 trillion from supply** (~16%). Private sector production also declined (tainted by poor securitization).

**Current risk assessment:** Ratio of risk to safe asset holdings somewhat extended for World (~45%) and US (~55%) investors, but not to degree of 2000 or pre-GFC. Most important price in global system = always price of dominant economy's sovereign debt (US Treasury yield + dollar value).

## Chapter 13: Measuring Liquidity — The Global Liquidity Indexes (GLI)

### CrossBorder Capital (CBC) GLI Methodology

**Core belief:** Changes in **sources of funds** (financial flows) matter more than uses of funds (economic spending categories). Major inflections in funding cycle measured through **Global Liquidity Indexes (GLI)**.

**GLI characteristics:**
- Composite indexes assessing financial liquidity across ~**80 economies** worldwide
- Published regularly since early 1990s
- Data from supranational organizations (IMF, BIS), national Central Banks, trade organizations, money markets, shadow banks
- Consist of baskets of z-scores (~30 data series per economy)
- Cover traditional banks, Central Banks, shadow banks, corporations, households, foreign investors

### Predictive Power

**GLI indexes are Granger causal** for key series:
- Lead bond/forex markets by **3-6 months**
- Lead equity markets by **6-12 months**
- Lead economies by **15-20 months**

### What GLI Measures

**Six variable types potentially available:** (1) asset prices/credit costs, (2) credit spreads/risk premia, (3) leverage/credit growth, (4) off-balance sheet lending, (5) lending surveys, (6) security issuance/ETF/mutual fund flows.

**CBC focuses on #3, #4, and part of #2:**
- Growth rates in Broad Credit/Financial Savings
- Growth rates in Central Bank balance sheets
- Net cross-border capital inflows relative to domestic pool
- Leverage ratios of private credit providers
- Short-term credit spreads (LIBOR-OIS, T-ED) — used to cross-check flow data

**Why others excluded:** Lending surveys not predictive (follow with long lag). Issuance/fund flows are a 'use' not 'source' of funds. Asset prices excluded except very short-term spreads.

### Index Construction Methodology

**Three-stage process:** Raw data -> Transformation/Normalization -> Index Construction

**Key choices:** Monthly frequency (compromise between daily noise and quarterly lag). ~30 variables per economy (avoids over-signaling). Variables grouped by type into sub-indexes (Central Bank, CBC flows, private sector) to avoid bias.

**Z-score methodology:** Individual z-scores combined using likelihood-based methodology. Produces strongest signals when all measures aligned. **Nonlinear:** all sub-components with high z-scores = aggregate much higher (works from joint probabilities). Not biased by large extremes from one or two sub-components.

**Index scale:** 0-100 range. Mean at 50; 1 sd at 60/40; 2 sd at 80/20. Over 50 = liquidity increase vs 41-month trend; under 50 = decrease. Increase vs previous month = acceleration.

### US Federal Reserve Liquidity Sub-Index

**Five key sub-components:** Overall balance sheet growth, banks' excess reserves, holdings of government debt, foreign asset growth, monetary authorities' government debt holdings. Combined into **US Federal Reserve Liquidity Index (0-100)** read as cumulative probability score.

### Weighting Methodology

**CBC hybrid approach:** Optimized combination of principal component weights and outstanding stock weights. Central Bank liquidity gets higher loading than size-weighting alone would merit.

**Average weightings:**
- **32% Central Bank liquidity**
- **20% private sector liquidity**
- **20% cross-border flows**
- **16% short-term liquidity spreads**

**Global aggregation:** National economies weighted by relative sizes based on outstanding stock of liquidity in US dollar terms in base year.

## Chapter 14: Conclusion — A High-Water Mark?

### Peak Liquidity and the Capital Wars

**High-Water Mark metaphor:** Confederate positions at Gettysburg symbolize potential peak of Global Liquidity as US-China rivalry intensifies.

**US policy shift:** No longer fully embracing China; explicitly containing China's economic/geopolitical challenge through controlling trade, technology transfer, and capital flows. **China's counter-strategy:** Thwart US dollar hegemony; retreat from globalisation toward new regionalism based on ideology and geography.

**Technology competition as central axis:** AI, 5G networks, digital money, quantum computing reshaping geopolitical balance. Capital protectionism (limits on technology transfer) poses greater economic threat to China than traditional trade protection.

> "China needs to import 'intelligent' and risk-seeking capital to maintain her growth and stability"

### Dollar Dependency Paradox

Central Banks have power but not always control over Global Liquidity provision. Liquidity rests on potentially unstable base under America's and China's direction.

**Bretton Woods paradox:** World became more dollar-centric as fixed exchange rate system collapsed. US trade deficit largely explained by **competitiveness of America's financial markets**, not industrial uncompetitiveness.

**Multipolar exchange rate myth:** World not inevitably moving toward multipolar system — 2/3 to 3/4 of real and financial market activity transacted in US dollars. Financial cycle will remain "highly procyclical, sometimes fragile and always unpredictable."

### Why the Investment World Gets Bigger and More Volatile

**Core proposition:** As investment World gets bigger, it becomes more volatile.

**Stark division:** Main liquidity supplier = large, low productivity growth economy with developed financial markets and capital surplus (**US** via GVCs). Major user = large, high productivity growth economy with underdeveloped financial markets needing risk capital imports (**China**). China has industrial power; America retains financial powerhouse status. China forced to lean heavily on US dollar — additional risk for global markets.

### The Financial Silk Road

**Fall of Berlin Wall (1989):** Key event — Western capital surged Eastward, eventually enfranchising 2-3 billion new producers. Forces hastened China's reforms (Deng Xiaoping) and Latin American reform (Mexico's privatisation, NAFTA under President Carlos Salinas).

**Spheres of influence being redrawn:**
- China consolidating in Asia via **Belt and Road Initiative** into Central Asia, European, African markets
- Germany redeploying capital East (Ukraine, Russia, possibly Middle East including Turkey, Iran)
- US expanding Southward into Latin America, picking up influence in Europe and Asia (Japan, Taiwan, Korea, Southeast Asia, possibly India)
- **Post-WW2 settlement boundaries finally being redrawn**

### Risk Capital, Economic Catch-Up, and Social Costs

**Risk capital** forever pushed from large money centres toward faster growth economies, facilitating economic 'catch-up' — only guaranteed source of growth in modern industrial era.

China and EM high productivity growth forced US to run near-permanently loose monetary policy via convoluted real-exchange adjustment. Loose US money spilled into nascent EM credit systems, fueling multiple credit booms. New competition spurred cost-cutting in the West — firms trimmed capital spending, focused on raising leverage return on capital by slashing operating costs on existing capital.

**Industrial cash flows boosted**, channelled through wholesale money markets into high street banks or large mergers concentrating global industries and share-buybacks raising financial leverage. **Balance sheet capacity** (volume of liquidity) became more important than **cost of capital** (interest rates). Encouraging build-up of CICPs reversed polarity of international financial system.

**Social costs in the West:** Middle-class employment shaken; cognitive/semiskilled jobs lost since dot-com bubble. Low wage growth; households encouraged to borrow. Industries adopted 'asset-lite' models. Debt concentrated in hi-tech (healthcare, technology), offsetting slumps in traditional industries. **Gaping wealth disparities destroying political middle ground**, leading to populist extremes.

### The Rise of the Repo

China's WTO entry (2001) accelerated supply chain growth and intensive dollar use — period dubbed **'Bretton Woods II'**. Corporate cash pools (CICPs) outgrew banks' safe asset provision capacity.

Demands for evermore dollars forced private sector substitution. Offshore Eurodollar pools became ready sources of unregulated wholesale funding — no national flags, few boundaries, shifting rapidly at communication technology speeds — **amplifying the Global Liquidity cycle**.

> "The financial market volatility that once characterised the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries is back"

### Capitalism's Warning

**Long arc of history:** Capitalism excels at aggregate wealth creation but risks collapsing industrial cost structure and erecting towering financial superstructure. Free markets create high street price deflation alongside asset price inflation and volatility.

**Vicious spiral:** Western private sectors need more debt to grow, but greater debt flow reduces quality and future balance sheet capacity, ultimately requiring refinancing. More poor-quality debts funded by still more poor-quality private sector debts.

**Payment systems requirement:** Level of liquidity backstopping that no private entity can provide. State's efficacy in controlling money waxes and wanes. Private sector innovates outside State control — highly procyclical, and **in downturns when liquidity most needed, it simply is not there**.

### Mass Financialisation

Mass financialisation left many investors richer on paper than at dot-com top (2000). Central Banks created new money while policy-makers retired instruments from market — reducing available assets (especially higher quality) for private sector. Amount of money chasing those assets grew (**money power** underpins higher valuations).

Since late-1990s Greenspan-led Fed era: nominal US GDP averaged 4-5%, but returns on equities, real estate, agricultural land, Treasuries, corporate bonds, junk bonds, and leveraged loans **all outpaced the underlying economy**.

### Policy-Makers' Constraints

> "By sticking to the new orthodoxy of monetary policy and pretending that we have made the banking system safe we are sleepwalking towards that crisis" — **Mervyn King**, former Bank of England head (2019)

**Natural rate of interest:** ~2-2.5% in US. Low ceiling means limited scope for rate cuts in next crisis. Negative rates risk **destroying the repo mechanism and commercial bank profitability** — meaning destroying liquidity supply itself.

**Major tenet of liquidity theory:** Interest rates are not the price of money but an exchange rate. Low rates reflect poor return on industrial capital and/or large excess demand for safe assets. Maintaining austerity, cutting rates, and not expanding liquidity risks destroying the credit mechanism (as in early 1930s).

### Deflationary Forces

**Active capitalism always creates cost deflation:** Declining baby boomers, greater female labor participation, China, new technologies, OPEC quiescence. 'Money' is nowhere mentioned.

If high street inflation is no longer the issue — using Friedman's "inflation is always and everywhere a monetary phenomenon" — and if the Phillips Curve is dead, Central Banks targeting 2% inflation have been **wasting time** while ignoring financial stability. Targeting low inflation during deflationary supply shocks caused by globalisation and China's WTO entry resulted in asset bubbles.

Two big debates now: future roles of **digital money** and **MMT** — both reflecting doubts over Central Banks' ability to master the global financial system.

### Refinancing vs. New Financing

Policy-makers must re-focus on financial stability and sufficient safe asset/liquidity provision. Central Banks likely forced to resort to **QE4, QE5, QE6** to replace funding capacity permanently lost in GFC.

**Financial sector balance sheet size outweighs cost of capital** (interest rates). Debates around People's QE and MMT are misdirected — low investment has little to do with lack of money. The **unambiguous appetite for government debt** shown by hugely negative term premia may total equivalent of **cumulative 80% of World GDP since GFC**.

**Understanding changing financial structure** always critical. Real issue not 'too big to fail' but 'too interconnected to fail.' Modern financial system = vast self-referencing system more than new financing system. Central Banks are monopoly currency suppliers but only one among many credit providers.

**Policy prescriptions:**
- Move toward asset-based framework focused on credit quality (not liability-based systems)
- Steady supply of safe assets critical
- Target currency blocs with greater exchange rate adjustments between blocs than within
- Establish tools to quell excessive credit growth within each bloc
- Issue longer duration instruments (30, 50, 100-year government bonds)
- Limit negative effects of excessive capital flows
- **Above all: ensure adequate levels of Global Liquidity**

### The Internationalisation of the Yuan

**Three most important Global Liquidity sources:** US Federal Reserve, PBoC, cross-border capital flows. China accounted for 5.9% of Global Liquidity in 2000; today commands **27.5% share** — beating even America.

**China's submissive role in currency choice cannot continue.** Likely strategy: broaden gross international balance sheet, invoice more trade in Yuan, develop Yuan trade credit markets, open domestic bond market.

**Digital Chinese Yuan** may be central — encrypted digital monies that eliminate intermediaries, allow person-to-person transfers, and potentially **disintermediate the US dollar**. Alipay and WeChat could accommodate State-issued digital currency. Near historical symmetry: China (originator of paper money >1,000 years ago) may be first major nation to supersede it.

> **Major-General Qiao Liang (2015):** "...we should promote the Renminbi to be the primary currency of Asia, just as the US dollar first became the currency of North America and then the currency of the World..."

**Geopolitical resistance:** Existentialist threat to US dollar hegemony will stir countervailing forces, likely spurring attempts to halt free capital flows. US dollar recently held back Euro challenge (largely succumbed after 2010-2012 banking crisis). British pound-gold standard once challenged by French-led **Latin Monetary Union** (1865-1873) — challenge failed partly because Vatican State cheated by issuing silver coins of lower fineness.

**The ultimate battle:** Fight for supremacy between **US dollar, Chinese Yuan, and digital/cryptocurrencies** — possibly the latter two united as single foe. **Ironclad question:** Could digital Yuan ever become the twenty-first century's 'safe asset'?

### Historical Precedent: Franco-Prussian War Indemnity (1871)

Germany threatened to occupy Northern France until indemnity payments (totalling one quarter of French GDP) settled. France paid within years — handed over gold reserves and raised billions through massively over-subscribed Thiers government bonds. At a stroke, the pool of international safe assets was sizably enlarged. Previously inert savings mobilised; credit boomed and spread cross-border.

The resulting boom proved too great — the **Panic of 1873** became arguably the first truly World crisis, embracing Britain and Europe, touching America. Better known through the speculative railroad bubble, failure of Jay Cooke & Co., US Coinage Act demonetising silver, and sound money policies of General Grant's Second Administration.

**Emphasises the importance of 'safe' asset creation and the adolescent power of Global Liquidity** — flows of savings and credit, stocks, bonds and asset markets... NOT the economy.

### Four Conclusions

1. **As investment World gets bigger and more interconnected** by rapid international capital flows, **it becomes more volatile**

2. **Procyclical monetary system** dominated by private sector credit and debt, featuring global supply chains based around USD, driven by fast-moving flows of **Global Liquidity**

3. **Policy-makers fail to understand changing financial structure:** Rise of CICPs and institutional focus on refinancing rather than new money-raising results in inappropriate and ultimately destabilising monetary policies; reversal of financial polarity

4. **Regionalism (possibly based on digital monies)** will consequently replace globalisation as capital rivalry limits cross-border trade, technology transfer, and free-flow of risk capital

## Appendix: Inflation, Deflation and Valuations

### P/E Multiples and Inflation Relationship

**Theoretical relationship:** P/E multiple and (inverted) bond yield both drawn against inflation. Equity valuations include additional risk premium based on odds of default and inventory valuation losses during deflation. Higher bond yields drag on equity valuations during inflation.

**Humped pattern implies two features:**
1. Equities enjoy highest real valuations when price inflation low (~-2%)
2. Correlation between equity and bond movements **crucially depends on underlying inflation regime**

At inflation threshold near equity valuation peak: bonds and equities **negatively correlated**. At higher inflation rates: valuations **positively correlated**.

### Japanese Market Example: Changing Correlation Structure

- **Late 1980s:** Stock/bond correlation ~0.6 (inflation ~2.5%)
- **Late 1990s:** Inflation turned negative (~-0.5%), correlation collapsed to -0.4
- **Post-GFC:** Inflation ~-1%, correlation tumbled to -0.6
- **Bonds provide good hedge to equities during periods of low inflation and price deflation**

### US Historical Data: CAPE and Inflation

**US CAPE and inflation (1881-2019, Shiller database):** Five-year average CAPE plotted against five-year average inflation. Fitted cubic relationship shows peak CAPE at close to zero inflation. Data support proposition that equity valuations highest at low positive inflation rates.

**Message: Inflation matters hugely to asset allocation decisions.** Asset valuation is conditional on inflation — a common factor linking equity and bond valuations that facilitates arbitrage between markets. May explain why alternative relative valuation tools (earnings/dividend discount model) often prove better than separately comparing bonds by yields and equities by P/E.
