## Chapter 1: Introduction: Capital Wars

### Capital Wars: The New Trade Wars

- The 2007-2008 **Global Financial Crisis (GFC)** was a devastating **global liquidity shock**
- By the early 1980s, the doctrine of **earnings power** as the main driver of stock prices was being displaced by a specific type of money power: **Global Liquidity** — a US$130 trillion pool of footloose capital, two-thirds bigger than World GDP
- The GFC occurred when dramatic escalation in monetary tensions triggered a frantic scramble for US dollars — Europe's demands alone exceeded **US$8 trillion**
- No automatic international lender of last resort existed; the IMF's firepower was still counted in billions
- Only the US Federal Reserve and its system of swap lines were large enough to stabilize the crisis — but only just barely
- US dollar swap lines expanded post-GFC but remain **politicized** — excludes Emerging Market economies, notably China
- America's decisions on Fed swap lines allocation have become "Nero-like choices" — who sits in the White House matters
- With **US$17 trillion** of debt owed by non-US corporations (two-thirds dollar-denominated), these decisions are critical

> "The money power preys upon the nation in times of peace and conspires against it in times of adversity. It is more despotic than monarchy, more insolent than autocracy, more selfish than bureaucracy." — Abraham Lincoln (attributed)

### Why Global Liquidity Matters

- Financial flows, not high street inflation or the "real economy," drive economic cycles
- The distinction between **asset economy** and **real economy** is critical — **Keynes** distinguished the economy's financial and industrial spheres similarly
- Trying to stimulate the real economy with liquidity always risks creating **asset price bubbles** instead
- In 1987, after a near-identical situation to post-GFC, policymakers unleashed analogous stimulus: result was near-flat high street prices but soaring asset prices
- A fractured, uncertain World encouraged investors to hold excessive "safe" assets (government bonds, US dollar assets) rather than putting money to work productively
- Global Liquidity continues to climb — exceeded pre-GFC peak ratio to GDP in both 2009 and 2017
- US Federal Reserve actions now dictate whether global investors move risk-on or risk-off
- "Fed-watching" has become a prized skill, sometimes serving as dark art worthy of Hogwarts

### The Problem of "Safe Assets"

- When the State fails to produce sufficient safe assets, the private sector steps in with **pseudo safe-substitutes** whose values move **procyclically**
- Governments' austerity policies and quantitative tightening programs might not be as sound as they appear
- The **precautionary demand for money** (hurriedly skipped over in traditional textbooks) better describes growing systemic risks than the better-known speculative motive
- Global Liquidity is **never trapped** — it waves no flag, knows no boundaries, shifts between markets and asset classes

### Two Puzzling Features in Policy Debates

**First: QE and bond yields**
- Academic argument (Gagnon 2016): QE lowers term premia by 67bp per 10% of GDP injected
- Data refutes this: QE periods in the US were **unequivocally associated with higher yields** — term premia rising on average **134bp** through each phase

**Second: Yield curve as recession predictor**
- Many believe the slope of the yield curve is an unambiguous predictor of the business cycle — an inverted yield curve should warn recession is approaching
- Neither statement is true — the standard 10-2 year yield curve slope is, at best, a flaky predictor
- Different maturity spreads work at different times; what matters is **slope and curvature together**
- A key component explaining curvature is term premia — **term premia are liquidity phenomena**, reflecting excess demand for safe assets

### The 1989 Turning Point and Financial Silk Road

- Liquidity shocks that ricocheted across the World in 1989 as the Berlin Wall fell forced interest rates down and reversed the polarity of the global financial system
- Capital faced Eastwards along the **Financial Silk Road**
- Too many countries (notably China) leaned heavily on the US dollar and US Treasury market for safety

### Refinancing vs. New Financing

- Financial markets increasingly serve as **refinancing mechanisms** rather than **new financing mechanisms**
- This makes the **capacity of capital** (balance sheet size) more important than the **cost of capital** (interest rates)
- The heightened supply of poor quality "safe" assets = **shadow monetary base**, comprising private balance sheets' ability to roll over huge GFC-era debts
- Reduced supply of liquidity and safe assets increases demand to hoard them — together these features amplify swings in Global Liquidity
- Explains why, as the World has got bigger, it has also become more volatile
- The underlying scarcity of high-quality assets leads to **Capital Wars** — the battleground embraces money, technology, and geopolitics between **Chinese industry** and **American finance**

### China vs. America in Global Liquidity

- Year 2000: China accounted for 5.9% of Global Liquidity (less than one-fifth of America's share)
- At GFC: China reached 10.1%
- Mid-2019: China swelled to **27.5%**, significantly out-pacing America's slipping **22.5%** slice
- **China matters hugely to both the World economy and World finance**
- America needs to reinvigorate her industry; China has the more pressing need to rapidly develop her financial sector
- Like history, these are **processes, not events** — will the final victor be the US dollar or a digitally based Chinese Yuan?

### Major Financial Crises Since 1980

- Six asset boom/banking crises since 1980, each with at least six episodes of major price bubbles:
  1. 1980s Japan
  2. Early 1990s Sweden and Scandinavia
  3. Thailand and South-East Asian economies (mid-1990s)
  4. US (late 1990s and 2000s — twice)
- Social and economic costs were high — national banking systems collapsed, loan losses exceeding one-quarter of GDPs
- **007 quote from Goldfinger**: "Once is happenstance. Twice is coincidence. Three times is enemy action"

### The GFC's Chinese Fingerprints

- GFC dated from Bear Stearns run (March 2008), precipitated by the Lehman Brothers failure (September 15th)
- The single event froze interbank credit markets worldwide — banks with excess reserves became risk-averse
- Alternative explanation: roots trace to 2002-2006 US and China real estate boom with Chinese fingerprints
- PBoC tightened Chinese credit conditions in early 2008 (probably to improve air quality for Beijing Olympics)
- Cumulative drawdown exceeded **RMB650 billion (~US$95 billion)** or 6.3% of balance sheet
- Forced credit-starved Chinese borrowers into offshore Eurodollar markets — their demands may have clashed with rising Western needs from leveraged MBS after Bear Stearns failure

### Cross-Border Capital Flows and the Transfer Problem

- Every national crisis preceded by an economic boom (though not every boom followed by crisis)
- Cause: rapidly shifting cross-border capital flows — economies with severe crises had experienced above-average capital inflow increases
- Capital inflow ratios to GDP were multiple times bigger for smaller economies (Ireland, Greece, Spain, Iceland)
- Large-scale debt issuance by banks/corporations in offshore funding centres (New York, Frankfurt, London)
- Parallel increase from fast-growing EMs (China, South-East Asia) and oil exporters buying US dollar "safe" assets
- China's export windfall after joining WTO (2001) was unusually large; oil exporters benefited from tripling crude prices
- Architects of **Bretton Woods** deliberately restricted private capital movements, blaming the 1930s Depression on violent capital swings
- Experts missed the size and velocity of capital flows; failed to recognize **current accounts adjust to capital flows** (not vice versa)
- Echoes 1920s debate about the "**transfer problem**" over German WWI reparation payments
- Trade deficits mean deficits for domestic manufacturing — a key source of productivity growth and urban employment
- Some estimates: switching from American deficits to Chinese surpluses could dampen World GDP growth by **2% per annum**
- America has lost willingness and ability to absorb excess savings of others
- The **true price of money** is the exchange rate, not the interest rate

### Currency Arrangements and Three Eras

- For most of last two centuries, currencies pegged: under **gold standard (1717-1934)** then **Bretton Woods (1944-1971)**
- Bretton Woods era provided comparative economic nirvana — GDP growth rates roughly twice those of preceding/following decades, modest inflation, absence of major bank failures, more homogeneous income/wealth distributions
- Three eras of floating exchange rates:
  1. **1974 to early 1990s**: World operated on **oil-based standard** underwroting US dollar supremacy
  2. **Early 1990s onwards**: New demand from Emerging Markets replaced oil producers to underwrite US dollar
  3. Floating rates promised gradualism and independence but delivered the most tumultuous monetary history — larger movements, greater overshooting, failing ability to hold back destructive Global Liquidity waves

### Deregulation and Capital Flow Liberalization

- Almost first act of Britain's incoming **Thatcher Government (1979)** was abolishing UK capital controls
- Other economies followed, implementing **Washington Consensus** policies through 1990s: tax reform, fiscal discipline, trade liberalization, opening capital accounts
- Symbolic Fall of Berlin Wall (1989) and Deng Xiaoping's 1985 enactments economically enfranchised 2-3 billion workers
- China joined **WTO in 2001**, nailing down rapid growth path
- Several fast-growing countries fixed currencies to US dollar or closely shadowed it

### Global Liquidity Shocks as New Regime

- In 1960s: labor shocks; in 1970s-1980s: oil/commodity shocks; now: **Global Liquidity shocks**
- These are bigger, longer-lasting, and more pervasive than calibre of shocks predicted by Central Banks using DSGE models
- Since 1980, well over sixty countries experienced asset booms followed by banking crises
- GFC was as much a crisis about "bad liabilities" (unreliable funding structures) as about "bad assets" (poor investments)

### Book Structure and Key Influences

- A **hybrid of economic and finance theory and real-World experience**
- Concentrates on **asset allocation** and potential for macro-valuation shifts based on interaction of investment crowds and monetary institutions
- Influenced by: Ron Smith, Richard Portes, Helene Rey, Pavol Povala
- In business, worked with: Henry Kaufman, Marty Leibowitz, Chris Mitchinson, Ewen Cameron-Watt, Michael Baring
- Data collected by **CrossBorder Capital** (investment advisory firm, est. 1996)

## Chapter 2: Global Money

### How Big Is the Pool of Global Liquidity?

- Global financial markets total ~**US$250 trillion** of listed assets (~3.5x World GDP), ignoring OTC instruments
- Equivalent to over US$40,000 per person; US$42 of paper wealth per tree on the planet
- Ratio of households' net financial wealth to GDP has risen parabolically to test **4x income** — never has the World enjoyed such rapid per capita wealth increase
- More than **10-fold leap** in World financial markets since early 1980s, paced by similar explosion in Global Liquidity
- Pool of liquidity (retail + wholesale) totals close to **US$130 trillion**, two-thirds bigger than World GDP
- World credit markets more international and interconnected, spanned by complex intermediation chains involving **shadow banks**, financed by increasing use of market-based collateral

### Cross-Border Dimension

- Links Emerging Markets to Western wholesale money markets via network of foreign currency loans
- **Large global banks** funded through repos and commercial paper (usually US dollar-denominated), on-lent against local currency collateral
- US dollar devaluation (commonly associated with American monetary expansions) encourages still greater leverage
- EM policy-makers typically try to monetize capital inflows, further fueling Global Liquidity cycle

### China's Dominance

- EM Liquidity near **US$50 trillion** (~38% of total); **China** makes up ~**US$36 trillion** (70% of EM liquidity)
- China provided most of recent impetus, expanding by near-**15-fold** in less than 20 years
- China's share leapt from barely **6% (1990)** to **28%**; US fell from **39% (1985)** to under **23%**
- Since 2000: Global Liquidity increased **240%**, led by China's **1366% jump** and other EM liquidity of **374%**
- Japan skidded from peak **21% (1989)** to just **7%**
- China's huge financial footprint is entirely dollar-based — her challenge is encouraging international Yuan use
- Some economies (like Japan) have come and largely gone as major financial players; China continues to muscle forward
- Surge in Eurozone Liquidity after Euro introduction (1999) until GFC and 2010-2012 banking crisis — expanded through rapid cross-border loan growth between core (Germany) and periphery (Spain, Ireland, Greece)
- China's exponential rise following WTO entry (2001), particularly in immediate post-GFC period

### Defining Global Liquidity

**Definition**: Gross flows of credit, savings, and international capital feeding through banking systems and wholesale money markets to facilitate debt, investment, and cross-border capital flows. Determined by balance sheet capacity of all credit providers and private sector ability to access cash.

**Three sources**: (1) Domestic private sector funding; (2) Official monetary institutions (Central Banks); (3) Foreign investors and lenders (cross-border flows)

**Key distinction**: Liquidity as *sources of funds*, not traditional money supply (bank deposits as "use of funds"). Credit and international IOUs dominate; monetary savings sit alongside. Money as higher form of credit ultimately underwritten by State.

- **Lance Taylor**: "Liquidity is often interpreted as a measure of the financial flexibility of an individual actor, group of actors, or the financial system as a whole"
- **1959 Radcliffe Committee**: "whole liquidity position relevant to spending decisions" — spending limited not by money in existence but by amount people think they can access through disposal of capital assets or borrowing

### Funding vs. Market Liquidity

- **Funding liquidity**: Balance sheet capacity; private sector access to finance through savings and credit
- **Market liquidity**: Ease of trading; ability to execute large transactions with limited price impact, low transaction costs, immediacy
  - Low liquidity leads to frictions, costs, reduced efficiency, disrupted growth
  - Financial assets with lower liquidity have higher liquidity risk premia
  - Market makers (banks, trading firms) provide vital liquidity service
- Funding and market liquidity **closely connected**, particularly in market-based credit systems

### BIS and Alternative Measures

- **BIS estimates**: US$32.5 trillion cross-border component; falls to US$16T excluding interbank claims
  - Excludes domestic credit in local currencies (US$68.5T) and foreign lending by domestic banks (US$5T)
- Author's Global Liquidity figure: ~US$128.2 trillion
- Alternative measures:
  1. US monetary base + foreign official holdings at Fed = US$7.4T
  2. US + Eurozone + Japanese + UK + Swiss bases + Chinese FX reserves + US Treasuries at Fed = ~US$20T

### Public vs. Private Liquidity

- **Private sector liquidity dominates** in size; Finance Ministries and Central Banks matter more during stress
- State backstops bank balance sheets through deposit guarantees, emergency funding, official lender of last resort
- Without backstops, bank credits might trade at discounts related to perceived credit quality — JPMorgan credit is not the same as Citibank credit
- Credit risks escalate when State backstop boundaries exceeded (e.g., deposits >US$250,000, unregulated shadow banks)

### Commodity Money vs. Credit Money

**Commodity money** (gold, silver): Supply is countercyclical — gold hoarding at lower prices, production profitable at lower prices; self-balancing system

**Credit money** (modern debt-based system):
- Supply requires issuance and take-up of new credit
- Periods of deflation → borrowers reluctant, lenders unwilling (default risks rise when debt value rises)
- **Procyclical**: cheap in inflation, expensive in deflation
- Stronger US dollar = monetary tightening on cross-border lending; weaker dollar = easing
- Global Liquidity often proves fragile; adverse dynamics are self-sustaining around crises

### Policy Interest Rates and Beyond

- **Policy interest rate** = most visible monetary instrument (overnight rate, e.g., US Fed Funds)
- 2007-2008 GFC lesson: short-term interest rates alone are inadequate
- Forward guidance, QE/QT policies, regulatory capital/asset ratios matter much more
- Central Banks affect economy by slowing/stimulating banks' assets and liabilities, disturbing balance sheet quantities, affecting risk-taking, wealth, collateral values, GDP

### BIS/ECB/Fed Warnings on Global Liquidity

- **BIS (2011)**: "Global Liquidity has become a key focus of international policy debates... both official and private component"
- **ECB (2011)**: "Surges in global liquidity may be associated with strong asset price increases, rapidly rising credit growth and excessive risk-taking"
- **Federal Reserve Board (2012)**: "Financial credit crises are perpetuated by illiquidity... evolution away from traditional banking toward collateralised lending serves only to increase the primacy of liquidity"
- **Banque de France (2018)**: "Most of the channels which QE might work are entirely independent of the accompanying level of nominal interest rates"

### Industrial Economies and the Refinancing System

- Economic growth depends on **capital accumulation**, financed by liquidity (not just savings)
- Capital raised over several years needs **refinancing** multiple times over project lifetime
- **Hyman Minsky insight**: Mismatches between assets and liabilities create predictable periods of stable and unstable financing regimes
- Modern business cycle dominated by changes in broad capital structure, not just underlying pace of economic growth
- Modern capitalism = **refinancing system**, not new financing system

### Shift to Wholesale Finance

- Past two decades: global financial system moved from retail bank-based credit to wholesale market-based provision
- Source of liquidity: **repo** (not bank deposit); gross funding dominates net credit provision
- Repos require stable collateral base — traditionally safe-asset government bonds (Treasuries)
- Widespread austerity policies limited new safe asset supply; rising debt levels require ever-larger balance sheet capacity to roll over positions
- Lower quality private sector securities used as alternative collateral → liquidity procyclical and fragile
- **Solution requires**: major injection of safe assets (more government bond issuance) and/or greater Central Bank liquidity
- **Irony**: credit mechanism broken; policy-makers don't know how to fix it despite homage to Global Liquidity importance

### Two Main Independent Drivers of Global Liquidity

1. **US Federal Reserve**
2. **People's Bank of China (PBoC)** — already one-fifth bigger than US Federal Reserve
   - PBoC's large size helps stabilize US dollar-based international system (China is a major dollar user)

- Private liquidity increasingly **collateral-based** (not bank-based), depends significantly on risk-taking attitudes
- Rise of **shadow banks** as funding providers compromises existing methods of monetary control
- Fed by rapidly growing **CICPs** (corporate and institutional cash pools)
- Owe existence to geopolitical developments, demographics, financial deregulation

### Four Key Features of Modern Economies

1. High productivity of industrial capital
2. Ever-greater elasticity of finance
3. Persistent instability of investment cycle
4. Economic catch-up of China and Emerging Markets

- Instability becoming more regular, inclusive, deeper — crises systematic, not idiosyncratic
- Roots in progressive maturity of Western capitalism relative to financial underdevelopment of China/EMs
- Shift from capital-raising to capital distribution-focused system featuring inventive bankers and rapacious speculators (not Schumpeter's innovative industrial entrepreneurs)

### Financial System Changes (Past Three Decades)

- M2 money supply no longer sufficient — ignores market-based liabilities: secured repos, commercial paper, large corporate deposits
- Key shifts underway:
  - From unsecured to **secured** funding
  - Denomination toward **US dollar** (much transacted outside mainland USA)
  - Benchmark rate from bank-based **LIBOR** to collateral-based **US repo rate**
  - Credit provision from bank balance sheets to **asset managers and broker-dealers**
  - Toward **alternative monies** (Bitcoin, Ethereum, cryptocurrencies)
  - Proposals for "People's QE" signal growing distrust in global banks and Central Banks

### Academic Blindspot on Liquidity

- Standard neo-Keynesian textbooks (Woodford 2003, Gali 2008): no role for financial theory, spreads, or risk premia
- Identical individuals act selfishly, independently, with perfect foresight — removes quantity rationing, deep uncertainty, involuntary unemployment, balance sheets
- **"Drunkard searching for lost keys under streetlamp"** fallacy: economics prefers factors easily measured
- Economists worship foreign trade balances when welfare depends on *sum* of exports + imports (not differences)
- Current account sizes often forced to passively adjust to capital movements; net flows hide richer network of gross capital flows
- **Bulk of cross-border capital movements are speculative portfolio flows and bank financing** (not FDI)
- Balance sheet analysis reveals: risk-seeking capital enters EMs; risk-averse capital leaves for "safe" assets in New York/London/Frankfurt
- Former tends long-term, latter more short-term
- Modern economics misses importance of gross funding dimension — takes every credit as debt, every debt as credit, never acknowledges character or magnitude of gross flows

### Academic Usage of "Liquidity" — Three Senses

1. **Market Depth**: Ease of selling/buying security in size at short notice without affecting price
2. **Money-Supply**: Broadly defined money stock or characteristic of money (e.g., broad credit, high-powered money)
3. **Risk**: Robustness of financial sector balance sheets; ability to settle obligations with immediacy (ECB 2009); ability to unwind or settle positions as they come due (Basel Committee)

### Hybrid Liquidity Measures

- **Brunnermeier & Pedersen (2009)**: Funding liquidity risk arises when net capital of dealer bank decreases, short-term borrowing reduced, margin requirements increase
  - Heightened market risk → higher margins → tighter funding liquidity → reduced market liquidity (feedback loop)
- **Bank of England financial market liquidity index**: combines bid/ask spreads, LIBOR/OIS spread, VIX
- **Post-GFC academic consensus**: Financial sector shocks for real economy gained significant attention
  - Financial cycles and credit/asset price components = persistent leading indicators of crises (Borio & Drehmann 2009; Schularick & Taylor 2012)
  - Financial crises lead to deep/lengthy recessions (Jorda et al. 2018; Adrian et al. 2014)
  - Credit booms weaken medium-term industrial output (Mian et al. 2017; Lombardi et al. 2017)
  - **Borio et al. (2019)**: Greater predictive power of financial flows vs. yield curve for US + 16 advanced + 9 EM economies (1985-2017)

### Key Empirical Findings: Liquidity as Leading Indicator

- **Gerdesmeier et al. (2010)**: "Measures of excessive credit creation are very good leading indicators of building up financial imbalances"
- **Alessi & Detken (2011)**: **Global private credit gap** or **global M1 gap** (detrended ratios to GDP) = best early warning indicators (18 OECD countries)
- **Borio & Lowe (2002)**: **Domestic credit gap** = better early warning than asset price gap or investment gap (34 countries)
- **Drehmann et al. (2011)**: Domestic credit gap achieves lowest noise-to-signal ratio for predicting banking crises vs. 14 other indicators (36 countries)
- **Bierut (2013)**: Global liquidity measures outperform domestic measures; Basel III rules likely reduce traditional banking in favor of non-banks
- **Adrian & Shin (2007)**: Among first to detail procyclical amplification mechanisms in modern financial structure
- **Miranda-Agrippino & Rey (2019)**: Single global factor explains up to one-quarter of World risk asset price variation
- **Jorda et al. (2018)**: Co-movements of credit, housing prices, stock markets hit historical highs across 17 advanced economies over past 150 years
- **Baks & Kramer (1999)**: Global liquidity negatively correlated with interest rates, positively related to equity returns
- **IMF (2010)**: Rising global liquidity associated with rising equity returns and declining real interest rates in 34 economies
- **Bruno & Shin (2015)**: Strong dollar = tighter credit worldwide; US monetary policy drives global risk premia
- **Rey (2013)**: US monetary policy influences financing conditions even in economies with fully flexible exchange rates — seriously questions whether floating rates insulate against global capital
- **Schularick & Taylor (2012)**: "Past growth of credit emerges as the single best predictor of future financial instability"

### Transmission Channels

- **Credit channel** (Bernanke & Gertler 1995): Expansionary monetary policy increases net worth of borrowers → subsequent lending expansion
- **Risk-taking channel** (Borio & Zhou 2008; Bruno & Shin 2015; Coimbra & Rey 2019): System-wide monetary expansions relax leverage limits, encourage additional credit risks
- Two channels often complement and reinforce each other
- Market liquidity is derivative; **funding liquidity more fundamental**
- **Caballero et al. (2017)**: ~1/3 of total assets are "safe"; ~1/3 of these are government securities
- Bank deposits now down to **30% from 80% in 1950s** — composition of private "safe" assets fundamentally changed

### Central Bank QE and Liquidity Channels

- QE reduces transactional frictions through liquidity channel — increases opportunities for sellers of targeted securities
- **Market liquidity is derivative; funding liquidity more fundamental** — onshore and offshore wholesale money markets central to funding liquidity supply
- **Holmstrom & Tirole (2001)**: Risk-neutral firms willingly pay premium on "safe" assets providing benefits when liquidity is scarce
- **He & Krishnamurthy (2012), Adrian et al. (2014), Brunnermeier & Sannikov (2014), Moreira & Savov (2017)**: Emphasize role of private supply-side channel
- In previous retail-based market: dominant form of safe debt (demand deposits) was insured by State
- Quality mix of collateral in economy becomes **critically important** to liquidity supply
- Too low stock of outstanding Treasuries → increase in privately produced collateral and credit boom → likely increases financial fragility

### Flow of Funds Analysis

- **Morris Copeland (1952)** pioneered methodology; US Fed publishes since 1951; **Henry Kaufman** popularized in 1970s-80s
- **Raymond Goldsmith's** *Comparative National Balance Sheets* (1985): Flow of funds accounts are vital tools
- National Income accounting ignores financial assets and debts — may explain why finance not integrated into traditional analysis

> **Kaufman**: Flow of funds "provides perspective and, like double-entry bookkeeping, contains built-in features that detect errors in logic... the amount of funds supplied must equal the amount demanded"

- **Deutsche Bundesbank (2013)**: "Financial accounts are an integral part of the national accounts... [showing] who in an economy is providing or drawing what amount of funds in what form, and the financial intermediaries involved... used to analyse investment and financing behaviour of enterprises and households"
- **Gurley & Shaw's** *Money in a Theory of Finance* (1960): Continuum of financial assets based on "liquidity" — saw little difference between non-bank and bank financial institution assets
- Financial crises often result from abrupt drying-up of funding liquidity preventing refinancing
- **Kaufman (1986)** used flow of funds to foresee turmoil: "We need to implement the best aspects of deregulation and the best applicable safeguards... this will require injecting some friction into the debt creation process—not more lubricants"

### Missing Concepts in Academic Finance

Four concepts missing from Bernstein's *Capital Ideas* (1992):
1. Institutional theories of capital markets and savings (including seigniorage and Central Bank policy)
2. Inflation/deflation threats in asset allocation
3. Long-term interest rate term structure on pricing
4. Risk wrongly equated with volatility alone

Common themes across all four: **liability management, duration, Global Liquidity**

- **Nassim Taleb's** *Black Swan* and **Mandelbrot's** *The (Mis)Behaviour of Markets* exposed absurdity of Gaussian probability for modeling risk
- MPT and EMH focus on individual securities, not investment crowd behavior or monetary institutions
- **Nietzsche** taught: people go mad in crowds; crowds form because deep uncertainty is the dominant feature of financial markets
- Crowds with money are particularly unstable — explain roller-coaster swings between greed and fear
- Investment fundamentally about **risk, return AND liquidity** — relative importance shifts over time: pre-WW2 (return), 1980s (risk), post-GFC (**liquidity**)

## Chapter 5: Real Exchange Adjustment

### The Industrial and Financial Circuits of Money

- Traditional economics separates "real economy" from "financial economy" but they enjoy complex interrelationship
- **Henry Thornton (1802)**: "The subjects of coin, of paper credit, of the balance of commerce, and of exchanges [...are] intimately connected"
- Standard textbook divides interest rates into real and inflation components:
  - Real interest rate determined by savings-investment gap
  - Excess money creation when investment spending exceeds savings (I > S)
- **Keynes** argued equilibrium restored by changing **incomes**, not interest rates — excess savings reduce incomes until matching investment
- Nominal interest rates strongly affected by **risk and term premia**, governed by future expectations and access to liquidity

### Investment Depends on Liquidity, Not Just Savings

- In modern capitalism, investment depends on total pool of **liquidity**, not just savings
- Net savings (S) = capital expenditure minus net acquisition of financial assets — only one small component of overall funding
- Many ways to arrive at any given net change through gross balance sheet changes
- **Raymond Goldsmith (1985)**: "financial interrelations ratio" rises over time
- Narrow focus on net imbalances → "savings glut" story; broader gross flows show major force was huge increase in foreign liabilities of safe assets issued by developed economies
- Example: Global money centre banks increased lending ahead of GFC while EM investors piled into US Treasuries
- Cheap Chinese imports reinforce that inflation is largely cost-driven; real interest rates must also be affected by monetary shifts and risk/term premia fluctuations
- Financial history shows short-term policy rates **follow rather than lead** long-term rates

### Flow of Funds vs. National Income Accounts

- **National Income Accounts**: Track spending but don't explain financing or sustainability
- **Flow of funds**: Measure net acquisition of financial assets by sector — flows accumulate in rising stocks of assets/liabilities
- High debt and leverage ratios curtail further new flows; sustainability depends on future access to liquidity
- Interest rate better thought of as **premium** paid on money when borrowed — varies by time horizon and riskiness

### Four Adjustment Outcomes from a Positive Liquidity Shock

1. **Greater real investment** (productive and unproductive) — most feasible for EMs with abundant opportunities but savings shortage
2. **Rising financial asset values** — more likely for mature Western economies
3. **Falling financial liabilities**
4. **Lower national savings**
- When liquidity exceeds capital spending (L > I), private sector accumulates financial assets
- Change in financial asset values may induce further credit expansion — financial imbalances amplify initial shocks, may take years to restore

### Qualitative and Quantitative Dimensions

- As cycle extends, effective supply naturally expands as more assets used as money — amplifying shocks through:
  - Improving risk appetite (permits greater leverage)
  - Enhanced collateral values (increases stock of high-powered money)
- Effective high-powered money = traditional Central Bank reserves + **shadow monetary base** (collateral + offshore wholesale markets)
- Supply of genuine "safe assets" limited by fiscal austerity → Global Liquidity increasingly **procyclical and fragile**
- As additional liquidity channeled into financial circuit, default risks decline, risk premia narrow, term premia on "safe assets" increase as demand grows

### Liquidity and Exchange Rate Dynamics

- Money borrowed in upswing (expands money circuits) differs from money borrowed in downturn (closes them)
- Central Bank money functions as both means of purchase and settlement; private sector liquidity changes in its "moneyness"
- Strong private sector liquidity + weak Central Bank liquidity → narrower risk premia, **stronger** currency
- Strong Central Bank liquidity + weak private liquidity → exchange rate **fluctuation**
- This asymmetry explains why different liquidity mixes have opposite currency effects

### The Exchange Rate Channel

- Exchange rates rarely follow textbook adjustment — periods of appreciation often coincide with strong capital inflows and buoyant activity
- Exchange rates influence economies through both **real and financial channels**
- **Valuation channel**: changes in composition/value of external balance sheet
- **Gourinchas et al. (2019)**: One-third of external adjustment from valuation effects vs. 41% from trade

### The Real Exchange Rate

- Determined by relative productivity performance — faster-growing economies should have stronger real exchange rates
- US productivity: 1.5% p.a. (1981-2019); Japan: 1.4%; Korea: 4.3%; **China: 7.3%**
- Since 2010: US 1.0%, Japan 0.7%, Korea 2.0%, China 6.1%
- BIS data: US dollar lost ~10 index points per decade (~8%) from 1964-2016 — long downward trend may have ended

### Dollar Invoicing and Global Value Chains

- **Gopinath et al. (2018)**: 1% US dollar appreciation → **0.6-0.8% decline in World trade volume** over one year
- Wider dollar invoicing role → less price flexibility → adjustment through profit margins/volumes
- Deeper dollarization forces Central Banks to build precautionary dollar reserves

### Emerging Market Currency Intervention Fuels Bubbles

- Central Bank interventions to suppress nominal exchange rate appreciation often lead to domestic asset booms
- **Late-1980s Japan**: Yen didn't rise sufficiently to eliminate Japan's huge productivity advantage; unregulated *zaitech* products inflated a bubble that burst December 1989 — Japanese markets never regained former heights

> "Under the prevailing globalisation regime, policy-makers can effectively choose between the level of asset prices and the nominal exchange rate."

- Export-focused economies (Japan, Germany, China) favor exchange rate stability against US dollar
- Financial economies (US, UK) aim at preserving collateral values, more willing to accept currency weakness
- Explains why UK and US sacrificed exchange rates in times of trouble; may hint at why Phillips Curve trade-off seems not to work

### US Historical Context and Manufacturing

- US nominal devaluation has long history — successfully used to exit 1930s Depression
- **George F. Warren** (letter to Roosevelt, April 1933): "There is one and only one way to raise our commodity price level: reducing the amount of gold in the dollar"
- **US share of global manufacturing fell from 28% to 18%** (2002-2017); China became World's largest supplier in 2010
- Smaller industrial base may feed back to further weaken US real exchange rate
- US authorities' asymmetric loose monetary policy to underpin collateral → exports monetary largesse through cross-border flows → amplified into bigger Global Liquidity moves

### US-China Dynamic

- US negative productivity shock → Fed eases → weaker dollar → surplus liquidity spills into offshore markets → more cross-border lending, higher global asset prices
- Chinese authorities resist RMB depreciation by monetizing inflows → liquidity injections underpin rising domestic asset prices → spill to other EMs → those economies follow suit and monetize

> "Understanding the Global Liquidity cycle may simply come down to interpreting the motives and actions of the two key Central Banks—the US Federal Reserve and the People's Bank of China."

- Two separate US dollar effects on cross-border capital flows:
  1. Fed and PBoC indirectly exercise joint policy; nominal dollar movements depend on their joint actions
  2. Direction of capital flows often dictated by Chinese economy's tempo (determined by PBoC monetary policy)

### Predictive Power of Liquidity Mix (Empirical)

- **Forex risk index** (private sector liquidity minus Central Bank liquidity, advanced 12 months) predicts future currency movements
- More private sector liquidity = "good" and value-enhancing for exchange rate
- More Central Bank liquidity = "bad" and likely to weaken currency
- **Granger causality tests**: US forex risk Granger-causes future dollar movements (probability **0.0029**); reverse does not hold
- EM-US forex risk gap Granger-causes JPMorgan EM forex basket (probability **0.0091**)
- Excessive Fed liquidity → subsequent dollar weakness; buoyant private sector cash (2010s tech giants) → rising dollar

## Chapter 6: Private Sector (Funding) Liquidity

### Funding Liquidity Definition

- **Funding liquidity**: Cash on-hand plus ability to borrow from banking/credit markets — measure of cash flow (sources of funds)
- **Market liquidity**: Ease of trading, price/size in bid-ask spreads (uses of funds)
- Unlike M2, liquidity is global, includes wholesale markets, entire private sector, access to credit, measures gross flows (not just net)
- Best measured by gross flows (changes in balance sheet capacity) rather than net flows

### Modern Money Beyond M2

- M2 ignores increasingly important market-based liabilities: secured repos, commercial paper, large corporate deposits, offshore Eurodollars
- **Adrian & Shin (2009)**: "The money stock will be of less use in a market-based financial system... More useful may be measures of collateralized borrowing"
- **"Modern money" starts where conventional definitions end** — M0/M1/M2 = only tip of growing iceberg
- Combined broad US funding ~**US$26 trillion** vs. M2 of US$15 trillion

### Shadow Banking Data and Growth

- Total broad shadow banking grew from **$91.4T (2006)** to **$210.7T (2019)** — **130.6% increase**
- Components: ICs (insurance) grew 81.6%; PFs (pension) moderate; OFIs grew **160.5%** (largest)
- FSB estimates: investment funds $46T, broker-dealers $86T, money market funds $6T, hedge funds $5T, structured finance $5T, trust companies $5T, finance companies $5T, REITs $2.5T, CCPs $1T
- **High-risk (narrow) shadow banking**: ~$60T, grown **120%** since 2006 — double pre-GFC size
- Assets exposed to bank run risk **tripled** since GFC (288.4% increase)
- Shadow banks transform traditional bank liabilities into longer/complex intermediation chains
  - Don't create much new lending — repackage/recycle existing savings
  - Increase elasticity of banking system by relaxing capital constraints
  - Sell loans externally to GSEs (Fannie Mae, Freddie Mac) or internally to off-balance sheet vehicles
- **"Unborrowed fringe"** (Keynes): speculative appetite exists independent of interest rates
- System heightened because: collateral-based, subject to market pricing, highly **procyclical**

### Post-GFC Regulation

- **Liquidity Coverage Ratio (LCR)**: Standardized minimum daily HQLA requirement over 30-day stress period
- **Resolution Plans** ("living wills"): Rapid/orderly resolution during financial distress
- Shadow banking: ~2/3 of gross funding but <15% of new credit provision

### Quality of Liquidity

- **Gurley & Shaw (1960)**: Correctly foresaw non-bank institution proliferation undermining conventional monetary policies and threatening instability — later echoed by **Goldsmith (1985)**
- Quality of liquidity = vital barometer of financial stability, predictor of future inflation

> **Hayek (1933)**: "There exist still other forms of media of exchange... any increase or decrease of these money substitutes will have exactly the same effects... they spring up without being subject to any central control, but once they have come into existence their convertibility must be avoided if a collapse of credit is to be avoided"

### Wholesale Money and the Polarity Switch

- CICPs worldwide exceed **$30 trillion** — represents structural "polarity" switch in Western financial systems
- Former lenders (banks) became borrowers from wholesale markets; former borrowers (corporations) became lenders
- CICPs comprise: corporate treasury funds, forex reserve managers, **SWFs** ($8.1T direct, $20T+ total), institutional money managers, derivatives cash collateral
- Largest SWFs: Norwegian Government Pension Fund ($1.1T), CIC ($941B), ADIA ($697B), KIA ($592B), HKMA ($509B)

### The Engine Room: Wholesale Money Markets

- Wholesale money markets = **"engine room"** behind Global Liquidity
- US dollar portion expanded to nearly **$10 trillion**
- US broker/dealers' financial liabilities more than doubled to **$5T** between 2004-2008
- New York Federal Reserve (2014): "By 2007, the repo market had grown to $10 trillion—same order of magnitude as total US commercial banking assets—and intraday credit to any particular broker/dealer might approach $100 billion"

### The Repo Market

- **Repo**: One party sells assets with promise to repurchase; bankruptcy law favors repo investors (can sell collateral vs. automatic stay)
- Eclipsed pre-2008 uncollateralized interbank market; not restricted to traditional banks
- **Primary monetary policy conduit for Central Banks**; highly leveraged
- Transacts between all types: banks, broker-dealers, insurance companies, pension funds, hedge funds, mutual funds, corporations, government agencies

### Collateral and Rehypothecation

- US Treasuries and German bunds dominate pristine collateral; loans: overnight to 90+ days
- Collateral often re-lent (**rehypothecated**), generating credit outside traditional banking
  - Collateral = high-powered money component; haircut = reserve ratio equivalent; chain length = money multiplier equivalent
  - Counterparty risk and lender risk appetite play bigger roles than textbook reserve requirements
- International repo market: ~**$8-10T** of collateral
- Pre-GFC: gross transactions peaked over **$7T**, net flows testing $1.6T (2008)
- **Collateral multiplier** (IMF estimates, Singh 2019): Stood at **3x (2007)**, declined to **~2x (2017)**
  - Hedge funds $2.2T, securities lending $1.5T, total $3.7T, pledged collateral $7.5T
- Rehypothecation: 2% haircut allows leverage up to **50x**; continual re-pledging reduces credit-risk potential
- Several agents counting on same collateral → heightened systemic risk → may encourage precautionary hoarding
- **Crisis example**: US Treasury haircuts jumped from **0.25% to 3%**; ABS haircuts soared from **~4% to nearly 60%**; leverage collapsed from **25:1 to 1.7:1**

### The Liquidity Multiplier and Twin Multipliers

- Traditional money multiplier peaked near **15x** (late 1990s), collapsed post-GFC
- Collateral multipliers show far greater stability, generally rising since 2000 toward 1x and 2x
- **Private sector liquidity rises pari passu with collateral value increases**
- If more collateral needed for future liquidity growth, fiscal austerity depletes financial markets of precious safe asset collateral
- Two overlapping relationships (traditional monetary base + available collateral) impose regulatory and risk limits on balance sheet expansion
- Traditional money multiplier no longer valid: (1) only covers traditional banks; (2) ignores collateral role; (3) bank lending neither constrained by deposits nor Central Bank reserves

### A World of Refinancing

- Dominated by funding rollover of huge outstanding debts, not new capital projects
- **Balance sheet capacity (liquidity) more important than interest rates (cost of capital)**
- Crises occur when funding stops/slows — from lack of quality collateral and/or withdrawal of Central Bank support
- When both combine (2007-2008): significant crisis unfolds
- QT + public sector austerity = **dangerous mix** threatening severe financial instability
- September 2019: repo rates surged to **10%** (vs. Fed Funds 2.14%) when money market flows and excess reserves dipped below critical thresholds ($1T and $1.5T)
- **Critical question**: May these thresholds prove the liquidity danger lines US monetary authorities dare not cross?

## Chapter 7: The Central Banks: Don't Fight the Fed, Don't Upset the ECB and Don't Mess with the PBoC

### What Do Central Banks Do?

- **Walter Bagehot** outlined Central Banking principles in *Lombard Street* (1873)
- **Francis Baring** on Bank of England: "the centre or pivot for enabling every part of the monetary and credit machine to move"
- **Ben Bernanke**: "Monetary policy is 98% talk and only 2% action"
- Central Banks retain considerable power but increasingly lack control
- Two main liquidity channels: (1) interest rate and forward guidance; (2) changes to balance sheet size/composition

> "Money does not manage itself, and Lombard Street has a great deal of money to manage" — Walter Bagehot

### Limited Control

- **Reversal rate** (Brunnermeier & Koby 2019): Effective lower bound where further cuts become contractionary
  - Two-way pull: lower interest margins vs. larger capital gains on bonds; QE may reduce capital gain potential
- Central Banks relatively powerless over: volume of liquidity (vs. volatile international capital flows), lending decisions when bankers refuse or debtors reluctant, term structure in practice

### The Credit-Money System

- Current system: Central Bank guarantees discount window for regulated banks; FDIC underwrites first $250,000 per depositor
- Banks leverage balance sheets subject to funding access and reserve/capital requirements
- Safety guarantee makes deposits transferable — dollar credit from Citibank = dollar credit from Wells Fargo (notional equivalence)
- **Hypothetical e-money system**: Central Bank operates integrated national ledger — would break traditional credit creation mechanism
  - Central Bank balance sheet would multiply immediately with deposits
  - High street banks would attract low/zero/negative rates; no conventional money/credit multiplier
  - Collateral would replace traditional credit risk mitigation
  - Banks become specialist lenders funded by bidding for deposits

### Central Bank Balance Sheet Composition

**Assets (three active components):**
1. Gold and foreign exchange reserves
2. Directed lending programmes
3. Securities holdings

**Liabilities (four key categories):**
1. Cash-in-circulation (responds passively to retail demand)
2. Banks' reserves held at Central Bank
3. Reverse repos
4. Treasury balances

- Movements in the six non-passive categories define change in national monetary conditions
- **Sterilization**: Changes in foreign reserves offset by equivalent changes to other balance sheet categories
- **Open market operations** now called **QE/QT**; post-GFC rebranded as "large-scale asset purchases" (LSAP)

### Shadow Monetary Base

- Safe assets (collateral that can be rehypothecated) + high-quality debts + offshore cash pools
- Conventional US monetary base enlarged in early 2000s by new high-powered money sources
- Table 7.3: Each US$1 of World Central Bank money created >US$14 of Global Liquidity (2000); by 2019 barely **US$6.2** — dramatic collapse in credit system efficiency
- Collapse general (not just US); China uniquely enjoyed flat-to-moderately-rising multiplier
- Multiplier data: World 14.04→6.42; Developed 17.60→6.09; Emerging 6.38→7.00; ECB 26.95→8.05; PBoC 5.50→7.32; BoJ 8.48→2.03; Fed 20.78→8.04

### The Wholesale Funding Revolution

- Modern capitalism = **capital refinancing and distribution mechanism** for rolling over existing positions
- **Capacity of capital** matters more than **cost of capital**
- Three cyclicality factors: (1) inherent cyclicality of private money market flows; (2) uneven liquidity distribution; (3) liquidity never fungible in crises

> "We lent if by every possible means... we were not upon some occasions over nice; seeing the dreadful state in which the public were, we rendered every assistance in our power." — Jeremiah Harman, Bank of England (1819)

### Post-GFC Funding System Changes

Five key developments:
1. Banks have more capital
2. Regulators more vigilant with macro-prudential analyses
3. Swap lines bigger; more IMF funding
4. Shorter, higher quality, better understood intermediation chains (extreme shadow banks gone)
5. Larger, more active public sector role through Central Bank balance sheets and safe asset provision

- Major remaining inequalities: heavy US dollar reliance; politicization of foreigners' access to Fed (2010 Dodd-Frank Act)
- High street banks effectively regulated out of credit provision; now quasi-savings organizations (like building societies/mortgage banks), not money/capital market conduits
- Industrial corporations became wholesale funds providers to banks (not net borrowers) — market rates differ due to large risk premia fluctuations

> "We are judged by historical standards, big balance sheets here to stay... we have a bigger responsibility than we did to provide liquidity to the system" — **Andrew Hauser, Bank of England**

### Global Central Bank Expansion

- Since 2000: >10-fold increase in Central Bank money to >US$20T; ~US$14T from QE since GFC
- Standout increases: Swiss National Bank (**3002%**); Bank of England (**1460%**); PBoC (**932%**)
- Four Central Banks dominate: Fed, PBoC, ECB, BoJ
- PBoC held title of World's biggest Central Bank 2010-2018; recently pipped by BoJ (largely due to adverse currency movements)

### QE Programs (US Federal Reserve)

**QE1 (Nov 2008 – Mar 2010)**: Bought US$1.25T MBS + US$175B agency debt + US$300B Treasuries
**QE2 (Aug 2010 – Jun 2011)**: US$600B long-term Treasuries
**QE3 (Sep 2012 – Oct 2014)**: US$40B/month agency MBS + US$45B/month Treasuries (from Jan 2013)

### QE Transmission — Two Broader Channels

1. **Credit channel**: Actions altering volume of system-wide liquidity (QE, cross-border inflows, exchange rate changes, collateral effects)
2. **Risk-taking channel**: Actions altering ratio between risk and safe assets; includes portfolio effects, perceptions about business climate, geopolitical shocks

### Three Central Bank Balance Sheet Functions

**(1) Liquidity provision** (50.1% of Fed balance sheet end-2014, $4,263B):
- Tightens credit and bid-ask spreads; more liquidity → easier refinancing → stimulates activity
- Illiquid/risky instruments benefit more than safe/liquid ones

**(2) Maturity transformation** (28.9%, $2,461B):
- Removing long-duration securities from market; creates preferred habitat scarcity; narrows term spread
- Each US$1T **reduces** average real term premium by **142bp**

**(3) Credit enhancement** (20.9%, $1,777B):
- Removes high credit risk securities; tightens credit spreads
- Each US$1T reduces B-Aaa corporate spread by **415bp** (at peak 2008: spread hit **1670bp**)

> "Our approach—which could be described as 'credit easing'... the Federal Reserve's credit easing approach focuses on the mix of loans and securities on the asset side." — **Ben Bernanke, January 2009**

### QE Impact Empirical Results

- Regression: weekly data 2007-August 2015 (438 observations), R-squared 0.347
- **Liquidity provision**: Each US$1T increases average term premium by **56bp** (significant at 1%)
- **Maturity transformation**: Each US$1T reduces real term premium by **142bp** (significant at 1%)
- **Credit enhancement**: Insignificant at 5% level for Treasuries
- QE1 estimated impact: reduced term premia by ~85bp; QE3: ~60bp reduction
- Central Bank consensus (QE reduced yields ~50-100bp) **contradicts data** showing QE raised term premia average **134bp** across three phases
- Same experts failed to predict QT adoption (2015, 2017) would cause market interest rates/term premia to collapse
- Early trouble forecasts: May 2013 "taper tantrum" when Fed QE reduction caused investor panic
- Renewed tensions forced Federal Reserve to reconfirm QE's stabilization role

> "If any aspect of our normalization plans was somehow interfering with our achievement of our statutory goals, we wouldn't hesitate to make a change" — **Jerome Powell, January 2019**

### Federal Reserve Balance Sheet (August 21, 2019)

**Assets (US$ millions)**: Total **3,813,146** — including US Treasury securities 2,088,920; MBS 1,500,670; gold/forex 37,070
**Liabilities**: Currency in circulation 1,751,265; Reserve balances 1,517,820; Reverse repos 301,218; Treasury General Account 131,447
**Custody**: US Treasury securities 3,030,813; Federal agency/MBS 358,293 — **Total custody 3,469,762**

- Foreign assets barely 1% of balance sheet — yet dollar is international settlement currency
- Fed holds **US$3.5T in official reserve assets** of other governments
- US monetary base + custody assets = ~US$7.5T "US dollar monetary base" proxy

### Fed as International Lender of Last Resort

- Global Liquidity denominated in dollars → Fed serves as de facto international LoLR (though legal authority is national)
- During GFC: Fed provided ~**US$10.1T** (US$4.45T standardized) from Dec 2007 to Aug 2010, largely to European banks (Adam Tooze 2018)
- Cross-currency basis swaps not obeying covered interest parity; deviations correlated with dollar strength
- Regression: World forex reserves vs. Central Bank money = R-squared **39.9%**; Global Liquidity onto reserves = R-squared **49.3%**
- China alone: correlations plunged post-2017 (R-squared fell to 2.2%) — signals growing need to watch independent PBoC moves

### Forward Guidance and the Dot Plot

- Janet Yellen popularized forward guidance — FOMC members depict expected future rates in "dot plot"
- Many insist interest rates are sole focus; quantities don't matter
- This misses: (1) in crises, liquidity rarely fungible (quickly hoarded); (2) balance sheet size determines liquidity volumes and signals policy intention

### Interest Rate Corridor and Repo Operations

- **Floor**: IOER (interest on excess reserves); **Ceiling**: repo/official discount rate
- Federal Funds rate and Treasury rates generally trade within corridor; other market rates trade outside
- Fed's ceiling not automatic (unlike ECB) — notional/loose constraint dependent on speedy reaction
- Banks often unwilling to borrow from discount window in extremis (signals greater distress)
- Recent demands: Fed establish a **standing repo facility**
- When banks close to effective minimum reserve levels: demand curves become highly inelastic

**Repo Transmission Mechanism:**
- Central Bank injects funds through repos → increases cash resources of money market dealer
- Dealer pays short-term financing but keeps coupon payment (less margin)
- Enables dealer to purchase more bonds in open market → potentially more risk-taking elsewhere
- Loan supply stimulated by second-round effects as collateral refinancing value rises
- Some worry Central Bank repo activity removes precious collateral from private sector — but negative drag seems offset (and more than offset) by subsequent collateral value increases

**Different Central Bank operational focuses:**
- **Fed**: Treasury repo operations to maintain "marketability" of long-term securities
- **Bank of England**: Short-term 3-month trade bill (reflects Britain's historic foreign trading roots)
- Fed not directly involved in offshore Eurodollar markets (ready alternative source for Fed Funds)
- In crises: however hard Central Banks push, funding liquidity not easily transformed into required market liquidity

- **Marcia Stigum** (*Money Market*, 1987): Traditional economic theory cannot convincingly explain how Fed gets traction

> "From long experience, Fed technicians knew that the Fed could not control money supply with the precision envisioned by the textbooks." — **Henry Kaufman (1986)**

- **Stigum**: "Much of the macro-theory that links money supply to price level depends on concepts that have little relevance to the workings of a modern financial and industrial society"

### The People's Bank of China

**Four monetary tools**: Open market operations; reserve requirements; medium-term lending facility (MTLF); directed lending by policy banks

- Post-WTO: forex reserves jumped **20-fold**; multiplier stable at ~5-6x; entire primary expansion fed through to boost Chinese credit by ~**12-fold**
- Reserves peaked ~**US$4T (2014)**, stabilized near **US$3T** — shift away from accumulating Treasuries
- **MTLF** introduced 2014: provides funds at 3 months to 3 years; currently RMB3.5-4T (~US$500B)
- **Policy banks** (China Development Bank, Agricultural Development Bank, Export-Import Bank) engage in direct project finance for **Belt and Road Initiative** schemes
- External bond claims (Germany's Kiel Institute estimate): PBoC responsible for large part of ~US$1.5T of Chinese foreign lending (much undisclosed in official data)
- Fall in forex reserves tightened domestic monetary conditions, prompting open market operations (repos with primary dealers) to improve market liquidity

**PBoC Balance Sheet (mid-2019, RMB billions):**
- Total assets: **36,360** — Foreign assets 21,852 (60%); Claims on depository corporations 10,186 (28%); Claims on government 1,525
- Total liabilities: **36,360** — Reserve money 31,309 (85%); Currency issue 7,824; Deposits of financial corporations 22,182

**Key shift (2013-2019):** Foreign reserves fell from **86% to 60%** of assets; direct loans to Chinese banks rose from **4% to 28%**
- PBoC tightened sharply from late 2015 to temper capital outflows; again in late 2018 to protect Yuan amid escalating US trade tensions

### The European Central Bank

- ECB began operations June 1, 1998 (few months before Euro launch January 1, 1999), as third stage of Economic and Monetary Union (EMU) agreed in 1992
- System of collateralized borrowing through repos to **1,500+ eligible banks** who bid for "automatically bid" term liquidity
- Must post satisfactory collateral (high-quality public/private sector debt instruments + some member state public debt)
- Increase/decrease in quantity of notes changes liquidity in Eurozone economy
- Historical division: US Treasury competed against industrial capital needs (hence long-dated focus); European governments competed against short-term trade/financing bills (hence **3-month trade bill on London** as linchpin of 19th century finance)
- **Structural flaw of fixed exchange rate systems**: Rich regions get richer, poor get poorer — without fiscal transfers, assets shift from low to high productivity economies, undermining precious collateral
- Currency unions that work (US-Canada, Australia-NZ, Norway-Sweden) float currencies against each other
- Pre-GFC: speculative capital from Germany/Northern Europe flooded Southern European **consumers** (not productivity-boosting FDI)
- Intra-European FDI instead headed to Eastern Europe (Poland, Czech Republic, Hungary) — economies that **floated** currencies against Euro
- Eurozone banks took the strain of these consumer debts
