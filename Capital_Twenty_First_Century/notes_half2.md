## Chapter 10: Inequality of Capital Ownership (continued)

### Wealth Concentration in Belle Époque Europe

- France: top decile owned 80–85% of all wealth (1800–1810); nearly
  **90% by 1900–1910**. Top centile: 45–50% (1800–1810) → 60% by 1910.
- **Paris** (1/20th of population, 1/4 of national wealth): top centile ~55%
  early 19th c → 60% (1880–90) → **70% on eve of WWI** (Figure 10.2).
- French Revolution's effect on wealth distribution was minimal: slight
  decrease 1780–1810, then gradual increase 1810–1910. The **"Émigré
  billion"** (compensation to returning nobility post-1815) helped
  reconstitute aristocratic fortunes. Aristocratic names in top Parisian
  centile: ~15% (1800s) → ~30% (1840s) → <10% (1890s) — bourgeois
  capital replaced aristocratic capital.
- Before and after the Revolution, France was a **patrimonial society**
  where inheritance and marriage were more decisive than work.
- Bottom 50%'s share remained <5% throughout 19th–20th centuries.
  Two-thirds of Parisians died with zero or negative net wealth;
  half of all French people nationwide.
- *Père Goriot* (Balzac): Goriot died in abject poverty, burial costs
  exceeded his personal effects.

**Britain** (data: Atkinson, Harrison, Lindert):
- Top decile: ~85% (1810–1870) → surpassed **90% in 1900–1910**.
- Top centile: 55–60% (1810–1870) → nearly **70% in 1910–1920**.
- Slightly more concentrated than France throughout.
- France and Britain had nearly identical wealth concentration despite
  Third Republic elites portraying France as egalitarian vs. monarchical
  Britain — "the formal nature of the political regime clearly had very
  little influence on the distribution of wealth."

**Sweden** (data: Ohlsonn, Roine, Waldenstrom; 1810–1870 by Soltow):
- Top decile: 80–90% (1900–1910) → barely 50% by 1970–1980 (historical
  low), now ~60%. Sweden was NOT historically egalitarian — it was as
  concentrated as France/Britain in 1900–1910.

The same 80–90% top-decile / 50–60% top-centile concentration appears
in most pre-19th-century societies (Middle Ages, antiquity, traditional
agrarian societies).

### 20th-Century Redistribution

- Three questions: (1) Why so extreme and increasing before WWI? (2) Why
  still below historical peak today despite rising capital/income ratios?
  (3) Is this irreversible?
- 1914–1945 shocks caused a break that has never fully recovered:
  - France: top decile 90% (1910–20) → 60–70% (1950–70); top centile 60%
    → 20–30%.
  - Britain: top decile >90% (pre-WWI) → 60–65% (1970s), now ~70%; top
    centile ~70% (1910–20) → barely 20% (1970–80), now 25–30%.
- Structural change: emergence of a **patrimonial middle class** owning
  ~one-third of national wealth — first time any large group other than
  the elite owned meaningful capital.

### Inequality of Wealth in America

- Data: Lampman, Kopczuk, Saez; Federal Reserve surveys; Alice Hanson
  Jones, Lee Soltow.
- ~1800: US wealth inequality lower than Sweden in 1970–80 — new country,
  little accumulated wealth. Northern states more equal; southern states
  closer to European levels (slavery effects).
- By 1910: top decile ~80%, top centile ~45% — high but **markedly lower**
  than Europe (Figure 10.5/10.6).
- **Gilded Age**: converging toward European levels — Rockefeller, Carnegie,
  J.P. Morgan alarmed observers. **Willford King**'s 1915 book captured
  this anxiety.
- Fear of resembling Europe partly motivated US pioneering a **progressive
  estate tax** (1910–1920) and progressive income tax — deemed
  incompatible with US values.
- Today: US is *more* inegalitarian than Europe; a century ago, the
  perception and reality were the opposite.

### US vs. Europe: Divergent Trajectories

- US wealth inequality decreased 1910–1950 but **much less than Europe**:
  top decile dropped ~80% → ~70%; in Europe from 90% → 60%.
- By 2010: top US decile >70%, top centile ~35%.
- In Europe, the 20th century was a total transformation — wealth
  inequality fell to historically unprecedented lows, enabling nearly
  half the population to own some capital for the first time.
- This explains post-1945 *Trente Glorieuses* euphoria — Europeans felt
  capitalism had been overcome.
- In the US, a (white) patrimonial middle class already existed in the
  19th century; the 20th century is not synonymous with social progress
  — wealth inequality today exceeds the early 19th century.

### The Mechanism: r vs. g in History

- **Primary explanation** for pre-WWI hyperconcentration: **low-growth
  societies where r >> g**.
- Historical growth rates before 18th–19th centuries: 0.5–1%/year.
- Historical return on capital: consistently **4–5%/year**.
- **Mechanism**: if g = 1% and r = 5%, wealthy individuals need only
  reinvest one-fifth of capital income to grow faster than average income.
- **France 1820–1913 (Figure 10.7)**: r ~5% vs. g ~1%; capital income
  ~40% of national income; saving one-quarter of capital income (~10%
  savings rate) drove upward wealth concentration.
- Most wealth in this period came from inheritance, not entrepreneurial
  accumulation — confirmed by French probate data.

### Why r > g Historically

- **r > g is a historical fact, not a logical necessity**.
- Pre-17th century: annual growth never exceeded 0.1–0.2% for long;
  return always 4–5%; r was 10–20× greater than g.
- **Figure 10.9 (Antiquity to 2100)**: pretax r has always exceeded g;
  gap narrowed sharply in 20th century (especially 1950–2012, when global
  growth hit 3.5–4%); gap expected to widen again as demographic growth
  slows.

### Taxes and the 20th-Century Exception

- Pre-WWI: taxes on capital negligible (no income tax, no corporate tax,
  estate taxes only a few percent) → after-tax r ≈ pretax r.
- After WWI: high marginal tax rates temporarily reversed this.
- Post-1980: ideological shift + financial globalization → tax competition
  driving capital taxes steadily downward.
- **Figure 10.10 scenario assumptions**:
  - Average tax rate on capital: 30% (1913–2012), 10% (2012–2050), 0%
    (2050–2100).
- **Result**: net-of-tax r fell to 1–1.5% during 1913–1950 (below g);
  remained below g through 1950–2012 due to exceptionally high growth.
- **The 20th century was historically unprecedented**: for the first time,
  net r < g — product of wartime destruction + progressive taxation +
  exceptional postwar growth. Lasted ~100 years but is ending.

> "The inequality r > g has clearly been true throughout most of human
> history, right up to the eve of World War I, and it will probably be
> true again in the twenty-first century."

### Time Preference and Theoretical Critiques

- **Time preference (θ)**: standard explanation for r > g; actors are
  impatient (e.g., θ = 5% means sacrificing 105 euros tomorrow for 100
  today). Piketty: theory is **tautological** — can always explain by
  assuming utility functions that produce that behavior.
- Historical floor: r has never persistently fallen below 2–3%; mean
  return averages 4–5% (pretax).
- **"Infinite horizon" model problems**:
  (1) Implies infinitely powerful saving/dissaving reactions to any tax
      change — empirically unrealistic.
  (2) Implies r must rise faster than g in a rapidly growing economy —
      contradicts historical evidence.
- **Conclusion**: r > g is a **historical reality** driven by multiple
  independent forces — g is structurally low once demographic transition
  completes; r shaped by technology, psychology, social norms converging
  around 4–5%.

### Equilibrium Distribution

- Divergence can be limited by:
  1. Rising capital/income ratio eventually depresses r (takes decades;
     open economies delay by accumulating foreign assets).
  2. Demographic/economic shocks to individual fortunes (inheritance
     dispersal, bad investments, early death).
- **Primogeniture** amplified inequality by preserving large estates;
  magnified Pareto coefficient vs. equipartition systems.
- **Belle Époque dynamic**: Britain and France invested heavily abroad,
  delaying compression of r — top centile's share kept rising toward WWI.

### The Civil Code and the Illusion of the French Revolution

- French **Civil Code (1804)** abolished primogeniture and substitutions
  héréditaires; established equipartition among siblings.
- *Quotité disponible*: only 1/4 of estate freely disposable with 3+
  children; strict equal division is default.
- **Jane Austen connection**: the British "entail" system is why Elinor
  and Marianne (*Sense and Sensibility*) inherit nothing. Sir Walter's
  estate (*Persuasion*) bypasses his daughters entirely. Austen herself
  was disfavored by inheritance laws.
- US post-Revolution: entails forbidden even in the South. **Thomas
  Jefferson**: "the Earth belongs to the living." Britain retained
  primogeniture until **1925**; Germany abolished entails only in
  **1919** (Weimar).
- **The illusion exposed**: wealth concentration in France *increased*
  throughout the 19th century and peaked comparable to monarchical
  Britain — despite the Civil Code.
- **Why the Civil Code failed**: when r significantly and durably exceeds
  g, accumulation dynamics automatically produce extreme concentration.
  French estate tax was only 1–2% throughout the 19th century.
- **Mathematical model result**: equilibrium wealth distribution is a
  **Pareto distribution** whose coefficient is a steeply increasing
  function of r − g.
- At r = 5%, g = 1%: model predicts top decile owns ~90%, top centile
  >50% — exactly matching data.
- Paris 1913 under the Napoleonic Code: top centile owned >70% — more
  than in Britain.
- **The Aristocats** (Disney, set Paris 1910): Piketty uses it to
  illustrate the Belle Époque — Adélaïde de Bonnefamille's fortune was
  so large that even lavish spending on piano lessons for kittens
  couldn't outpace capital accumulation.
- To significantly reduce equilibrium concentration requires either g
  >1.5–2%, OR taxes reducing net r below 3–3.5%.

### Pareto and the Illusion of Stable Inequality

- **Vilfredo Pareto** (1890–1910): discovered "Pareto law" / power laws
  for upper-tail wealth distributions; promoted by Italian Fascists as
  theory of elite stability; Pareto himself hailed Mussolini in 1923.
- Pareto's data: tax tables from Prussia, Saxony, Swiss and Italian
  cities, 1880–1890 — only ~10 years of data, actually showed a slight
  trend toward higher inequality which Pareto "intentionally sought to
  hide."
- **Corrado Gini**: Gini coefficient gives a simplistic, overly
  optimistic picture of inequality.
- Same mathematical form (Pareto distribution) can describe:
  - Top decile receiving 20% (Scandinavia 1970–80)
  - Top decile receiving 50% (US 2000–10)
  - Top decile receiving 90%+ (France/Britain 1900–10)
- Calling something a "Pareto distribution" tells you almost nothing
  — everything depends on the coefficient; **the distribution is not
  stable**.

### Why Inequality Hasn't Returned to Historical Peak

- Core question: Why hasn't wealth inequality returned to Belle Époque
  levels? Piketty offers no single definitive answer — multiple factors.

**Mechanism of wealth destruction at the top:**
- Interwar rentiers did not cut expenditures fast enough; ate into
  capital to fund consumption, leaving heirs smaller fortunes.
- Top 1% Parisians in Belle Époque: capital income ~80–100× average
  wage; could live lavishly and still reinvest — self-reinforcing
  1872–1912. Interwar: left heirs only 30–40× by late 1930s, down to
  20× — "beginning of the end" for the rentier class.
- Foreign assets ~25% of largest fortunes on eve of WWI; nearly half
  was sovereign debt (especially Russian, on verge of default).

**Portfolio composition (Table 10.1 — Parisian portfolios 1872–1912):**
- Top 1%: ~42% real estate, ~56% financial assets (equities, private
  bonds, public bonds). Belle Époque portfolios were modern and
  diversified — not static landed capital — yet prodigiously inegalitarian.

**Post-Liberation redistribution (France):**
- Nationalization as sanctions — **Renault** is the emblematic example.
- **National solidarity tax (1945)**: one-time progressive levy on
  capital and wartime acquisitions; extremely high rates.

### Taxation as the Primary Structural Explanation

- Pre-WWI: essentially **zero tax** on capital income or corporate
  profits (or <5%); ideal conditions for accumulation.
- Post-WWII: average capital income tax ~30% in rich countries 1950–80.
- Mechanical effect: 30% tax reduces pretax return of 5% to net 3.5%;
  given compounding logic, this alone accounts for most deconcentration.
- Critical nuance: the tax **does not reduce total wealth** — it
  redistributes from top centile to middle class. Total capital stock
  can remain the same even as distribution changes radically.

**Progressive estate/inheritance taxes:**
- 19th-century estate taxes: 1–2% — effectively a registration fee.
- France made estate tax progressive in 1901 but top rate only 5% —
  negligible.
- Post-1914: estate tax rates rose to 20–30%+.
- Each generation had to save more to maintain family rank; conversely,
  easier for those starting lower to buy up estates at probate.
- Germany's top estate tax is 15–20% vs. France's 30–40%; likely
  explains why German capital incomes have remained more concentrated.

> Both theoretical simulations and historical data confirm that taxes
> alone suffice to explain most observed changes.

### The Twenty-First Century: Even More Inegalitarian Than the Nineteenth?

- Two additional factors: (1) slight decrease in capital's share and in
  r over the long run; (2) g, though slowing, will be higher than
  near-zero pre-18th-century rates.
- **Future scenario (Figure 10.11)**: r perhaps 4–4.5% (vs. 4.5–5%),
  g perhaps 1–1.5% (vs. 0.1–0.2%) — r − g gap smaller but still positive.
- Even if capital taxes eliminated entirely, concentration would probably
  not exactly return to 1900–1910 levels.
- **Grounds for concern**:
  - **Negative demographic growth** could push g below 19th-century
    levels, giving inherited wealth unprecedented importance.
  - **Financial globalization** increasing correlation between return and
    initial portfolio size — "more perfect" capital markets amplify r > g.
  - If fiscal institutions destroyed by tax competition, inequality could
    return to or exceed Belle Époque levels.

> "It is an illusion to think that something about the nature of modern
> growth or the laws of the market economy ensures that inequality of
> wealth will decrease and harmonious stability will be achieved."

## Chapter 11: Merit and Inheritance in the Long Run

- Capital's overall importance today ≈ 18th century (only form changed:
  land → industrial, financial, real estate).
- **Core question**: What are the relative roles of inheritance vs. saving?
  Same aggregate capital can arise from very different structural realities.

> "Whenever the rate of return on capital is significantly and durably
> higher than the growth rate of the economy, it is all but inevitable
> that inheritance predominates over saving."

- **r > g** implies "the past tends to devour the future": wealth from
  the past grows faster than wealth from work, amplifying past inequalities.
- 21st-century forecast: if low growth + high r persist (especially in
  Europe), inheritance will again be as important as in the 19th century.
- US currently less affected due to higher demographic growth — but UN
  median forecasts suggest global convergence to low growth.
- 21st-century inequality won't be identical to 19th century: more "medium
  rentiers" and fewer ultra-wealthy; supermanager salaries add a new layer;
  wealth and earned income more correlated.

### Inheritance Flows over the Long Run

- **Annual inheritance flow** = total bequests + inter vivos gifts as %
  of national income.
- **France 1820–2010 (Figure 11.1)**:
  - 19th century through 1914: steady **20–25% of national income**.
  - 1900–1910 flow *higher* (~25%) than 1820s (~20%) — inheritance growing.
  - Post-1914: **spectacular collapse** — fell by factor of 5–6; barely
    4–5% of national income in 1950s.
  - Post-1950: steady rebound, accelerating 1980s; by 2010: **~15%** of
    national income — roughly midway between 1950s nadir and 1910 peak.
- Inheritance flow collapse was **nearly twice as large** as the decline
  in private wealth itself.
- **Generational perception gap**: baby boomers grew up when inheritance
  was negligible → assumed "the new normal." Cohorts born 1970s–80s
  already experiencing inheritance's return.

### The Three Forces: b_y = μ × m × β

> **b_y = μ × m × β**

- **β** = capital/income ratio
- **m** = adult mortality rate
- **μ** = ratio of average wealth at death to average wealth of the living

Pure accounting identity — always true, all times and places.

**Force 1 — β**:
- Higher total private wealth → larger potential inheritance flow.
- Optimistic belief: modernization reduces inherited (nonhuman) capital
  relative to human capital → β should decline. Reality: β followed a
  U-shape in the 20th century and is now nearly back to Belle Époque
  levels. **Gary Becker**'s human capital theory implicitly assumes
  inherited wealth will decline; data contradicts this at aggregate level.

**Force 2 — m**:
- French adult mortality: ~2.2% in 19th century → 1.1–1.2% by 2000–2010.
- Optimistic belief: longer lives → smaller flows → end of inheritance
  society.
- BUT: mortality rate *rising again* from 2000–2010 and projected to
  ~1.4–1.5% by 2040–2050 — because baby boomers (unusually large cohort)
  are dying. Baby boom temporarily made population younger, suppressing
  mortality; as boomers die, mortality rises.

**Force 3 — μ**:
- **Franco Modigliani's life-cycle theory** (1950s): people save for
  retirement and dissave in old age → μ → 0 → inheritance vanishes.
  - Extreme case: all capital in pension funds/annuities ("annuitized
    wealth") that die with the owner.
  - Reinforced by Talcott Parsons-style functionalist sociology: a
    managerial society where inherited wealth is irrelevant.
  - Very influential for baby-boom generation, still popular today.
- BUT: the U-shape in France's inheritance flow is composed of three
  simultaneous U-shapes in β, m, and μ — all three collapsed together
  post-1914 and are all rebounding together.
- The "end of inheritance" conclusion was an illusion produced by all
  three forces temporarily pointing the same direction simultaneously.

### Cohort Size, Mortality, and Inheritance Flows

- Countries with *shrinking* cohorts (Germany, Italy, Japan) will see
  much larger rises in mortality rate → larger inheritance flows.
- Extreme case: if each couple has one child, inheritance flow could
  reach unprecedented levels. Rapidly growing cohorts (Africa) keep
  inherited wealth negligible.
- Only sustained cohort growth permanently reduces inheritance flows.

### Wealth Ages with Population: The μ × m Effect

- Even as mortality rate falls with longer lives, μ rises because wealth
  *ages* with the population — the two effects largely cancel out.
- **Figure 11.3**: Average age of adult decedents rose from ~60 to ~80
  during 20th century; average age of inheritors rose from ~30 to ~50
  — the gap always ~30 years (generational duration stays stable).
- **Rate of estate devolution** (μ × m): stable at ~3.3–3.5% (1820s to
  1910s); collapsed to ~2% in 1950s; risen steadily to >2.5% by 2000–2010.

### Wealth of the Dead, Wealth of the Living

- In France, μ has been >100% throughout 1820–present — the dead are
  always wealthier than the living — except briefly in 1940–1950.
- Modigliani's theory predicts μ ≈ 0 — **cannot explain the observed
  facts**. Annuitized wealth is <5% of private wealth in France, 15–20%
  in English-speaking countries — not enough to change inheritance
  fundamentals.
- **Gifts inter vivos** have exploded: 30–40% of inheritance value
  (1820–1870) → 20–30% (1870–1970) → 40% (1980s) → 60% (1990s) →
  **>80% (2000–2010)**. Today gifts are nearly as important as bequests.
- If gifts made before death included in μ, corrected value >220%: the
  dead are nearly twice as wealthy as the living (corrected).
- Most gifts go to children ~10 years before donor's death, often for
  real estate; recipient avg age ~35–40 vs. heir avg age ~45–50.
- Rise of gift-giving started in the 1970s — before tax incentives of
  1990–2000 — driven by parents' awareness that children needed wealth
  earlier given longer parental lifespans.

### Age-Wealth Profile in France (Table 11.1)

| Year | Octogenarians vs. Fifty-year-olds |
|------|-----------------------------------|
| 1820 | 53% wealthier |
| 1912 | **257%** wealthier — wealth aged dramatically |
| 1947 | 62% — wartime shock reversed everything |
| 2010 | 134% — re-aging underway |

- By 1900–1910 in Paris, septuagenarians and octogenarians on average
  **3–4× wealthier** than fifty-year-olds — impossible to explain by
  labor income alone.
- **Mechanism**: if r = 5% and elderly consume only 2/5 of capital income
  (reinvesting 3/5), wealth grows ~3%/yr → more than doubled by age 85
  vs. age 60.
- Belle Époque had modern capital markets, diversified portfolios
  (domestic/foreign, public/private). Entrepreneurs of 19th-century
  France were creative, but r > g inevitably turned them into rentiers:
  a good idea at 35 doesn't keep producing ideas at 75, but the fortune
  keeps growing.

### The Rejuvenation of Wealth Owing to War

- 1940–1950: for the first and only time in French history, average
  wealth at death *fell below* average wealth of the living (μ < 100%).
- By 1947, fifty-somethings were 40% *wealthier* than octogenarians
  (complete reversal from Belle Époque).
- Explanation: younger generations (age 30 in 1940) had little to lose
  and time to rebuild; older generations (age 60 in 1940) who lost
  everything had no time to recover and died with little to leave.
- Wars "wiped the slate clean" — creating the *illusion* that capitalism
  had been structurally transformed.
- Annual inheritance flow fell from ~25% of national income pre-WWI to
  just **4–5%** in the 1950s — nearly five-sixths drop.
- "**Reconstruction capitalism**" was transitional, not structural.

### How Will Inheritance Flows Evolve in the 21st Century?

**Figure 11.6 — two scenarios (France, 2010–2100)**:
- Central: g = 1.7%, r = 3% → flows stabilize at **16–17%** of national
  income by 2030–2040 (partial comeback).
- Alternative: g = 1%, r = 5% (capital taxes eliminated) → flows reach
  **24–25%** of national income by 2060–2070 (full Belle Époque return).

- Key theoretical result: when r > g and growth is low (~1%), increase
  in μ nearly exactly offsets decline in mortality rate m — so **μ × m
  is virtually independent of life expectancy** and determined mainly by
  generational duration (~30 years). This refutes the intuition that
  aging populations will spend down savings and end inherited wealth.

> "In an aging society, heirs come into their inheritances later in life
> but inherit larger amounts … so the overall importance of inherited
> wealth remains unchanged."

- Massive dissaving by the elderly (as Modigliani predicts) does *not*
  occur even as life expectancy rises; the upper decile always owns
  50–60% of wealth within every age group.

### From Annual Inheritance Flow to Stock of Inherited Wealth

**Figure 11.7 results**:
- 19th century: inherited wealth = **80–90%** of all private wealth.
- Low point (1970s): fell to ~**40–50%** — first time saved wealth
  exceeded inherited wealth; majority of capital was self-made.
- 1980s: inherited wealth majority again; by 2010 ≈ **two-thirds** of
  French private capital.
- If trends continue: >70% by 2020, ~80% by 2030s; under 1%g/5%r
  scenario could reach ~90% by 2050s (Belle Époque levels).
- Annual inheritance flow crossed the savings rate (~10%) again in the
  1980s and now stands at ~**15% of national income** (inheritances +
  gifts).
- As fraction of *disposable household income* (~70–75% of national
  income): inheritance already ~**20% of disposable income** in early
  2010s — back to 19th-century levels.

### Back to Vautrin's Lecture: Cohort Inheritance Shares (Figure 11.9)

- Share of inheritance in *total lifetime resources* by birth cohort:
  - 1790s cohort: inheritance ~**24%** of lifetime resources.
  - All 19th-century cohorts through WWI: consistently ~25%.
  - Cohorts born 1910–1920 (who inherited in 1950–1960): fell to **<10%**
    — the exceptional mid-century minimum.
  - Cohorts born 1990–2010: rising back toward ~24–25%.
- Lowest point: cohorts born **1910–1920**, who inherited 1945–1960 —
  inheritance only **8–10%** of total resources.
- Cohorts born 1970–80 (inheriting 2000–2010): inheritance back to
  **22–24%** of total resources — not seen since 19th century.

### Rastignac's Dilemma

- Resources expressed as multiples of average income of the bottom 50%:
- **19th century**: top 1% of heirs had resources **25–30×** lower class
  standard; top 1% earners (judge, prosecutor) had only ~**10×** —
  inheritance dominated labor.
- **Cohorts born 1910–1920**: top 1% inheritances fell to barely **5×**
  lower class; top 1% earners still **10–12×** — for the first time in
  history, work beat inheritance at the top.
- **Baby boom (1940–1950)**: top earners ~10–12×, top inheritors ~6–7×
  → work still clearly superior; Vautrin's lesson obsolete.
- **Cohorts born 1970s**: top inherited wealth of top centile ~12–13×,
  earned income ~10–11× → today's cohorts face genuinely complex choices.

> "For the first time in history, one could live better by obtaining a
> job in the top centile rather than an inheritance in the top centile."
> — Piketty on cohorts born 1910–1920

### The Basic Arithmetic of Rentiers and Managers

Two conditions required for a **patrimonial society** where capital income
dominates at the top:

1. **Large capital stock**: capital/income ratio ~6–7, mostly inherited;
   true until 1914 and approaching again today.
2. **Extreme concentration of inherited wealth**: must overcome the volume
   effect (labor income is distributed among far more people).

- 19th century: top centile owned **50–60%** of total wealth (70% in
  Britain/Belle Époque Paris) vs. top centile earners claiming only 6–7%
  of wages → **10:1 concentration ratio** overcomes 3:1 volume ratio →
  inheritance wins by ~3×.
- **France today**: top centile owns ~20% of wealth vs. 6–7% of wages
  → only ~3:1 concentration ratio, roughly balancing the volume effect.
- During *Trente Glorieuses*: concentration effect (3:1) too small to
  overcome volume effect → managers dominated completely.

### The Classic Patrimonial Society: The World of Balzac and Austen

Comfortable life threshold: **~30× average income** of the day — true
for both French and English novelists.

**Balzac's monetary world (France, ~1800–1840)**:
- Average per capita income: ~500 fr/year.
- *Père Goriot*: Goriot reduced to 500 fr/yr = abject poverty. Daughters'
  dowries → 25,000 fr/yr rent = 50× avg — true wealth.
- Rastignac family estate: 3,000 fr/yr (6×) = moneyless lesser nobility.
- Rastignac's potential salary as royal prosecutor: 5,000 fr/yr (10×) =
  "the very symbol of mediocrity."
- Vautrin's target: 25,000 fr/yr (50×) minimum; better yet 50,000+ fr.

**Austen's monetary world (England, ~1800–1810)**:
- Average income: barely 30 pounds/year.
- Norland estate (*Sense and Sensibility*): 4,000 lb/yr = **100×** average.
- Colonel Brandon's Delaford: 2,000 lb/yr (60×).
- Willoughby: 600 lb/yr (20×) = barely comfortable.
- Dashwood half-sisters: 500 lb total (125 lb each, ~4×) = "woefully
  inadequate."
- Edward Ferrars as pastor: 200 lb/yr (6–7×) = "nearly taken for a saint."

> "They were neither of them quite enough in love to think that three
> hundred and fifty pounds a year would supply them with the comforts of
> life." — Austen, *Sense and Sensibility*

**American parallels**:
- **Henry James**, *Washington Square*: Catherine Sloper's fiancé flees
  when dowry yields $10,000/yr not $30,000 (20× vs. 60× average).
- **Orson Welles**, *The Magnificent Ambersons*: George's fall from
  $60,000/yr (120×) to $350/yr = industrial-era disruption.

### Extreme Inequality of Wealth: A Condition of Civilization?

- 19th-century novelists implicitly justified extreme inequality: without
  a wealthy minority, no one could concern themselves with anything beyond
  subsistence. Crucially, this was **NOT presented as meritocratic**.
- Average per capita purchasing power in Britain/France in 1800: **~one-
  tenth** of 2010. So 20–30× average income in 1800 ≈ only 2–3× today.
- **Contrarian point**: modern meritocratic society is "harder on the
  losers" — it justifies domination on grounds of virtue and merit,
  whereas the patrimonial world at least didn't pretend the rich
  deserved it.

### Meritocratic Extremism

- **Adolphe Thiers** (1831): "prefects should be able to occupy a rank
  equal to the notable citizens in the *départements*."
- **Paul Leroy-Beaulieu** (1881): even 15,000–20,000 fr/yr "makes it
  impossible to live with elegance."
- **US supermanager pay**: same logic — without stratospheric pay, only
  heirs could achieve true wealth; therefore millions/year to supermanagers
  = social justice.
- **Piketty's warning**: this argument could produce worst of both worlds
  — very large inherited wealth inequality PLUS very high wage inequality
  justified by merit claims (with little factual basis).
- **Michèle Lamont** research: hundreds of interviews with upper middle
  class in US and France → in both countries, "educated elite" emphasized
  personal merit and moral qualities to justify position.

### The Society of Petits Rentiers

- For 1970s-born cohorts: inheritance ~25% of total lifetime resources
  (central scenario); could reach 30–40% under low-growth, high-return
  scenario.
- Volume returned to 19th-century levels but **distribution is different**:
  top centile's share fell from 60% (1910–20) to ~20% today → far fewer
  massive estates.
- A Balzacian dowry of 500,000 fr (50× avg income) = **30 million euros
  today** — still exists but far rarer.
- **What has grown**: medium-sized inheritances — 200,000 to 2 million
  euros; not enough to live on interest alone but substantial vs. lifetime
  labor income.
- Transition: from **few very large rentiers** to **many petits rentiers**.

**Figure 11.11 metric** — % of each cohort inheriting more than the
lifetime labor income of the bottom 50% (~750,000 euros over a 50-year
career at 15,000 avg/yr):
- 19th century: ~10% of cohort received such inheritances.
- Cohorts born 1910–20: fell to ~2%.
- Cohorts born 1930–50: ~4–5%.
- Cohorts born 1970–80: ~12%; may reach **15%+ for 2010–2020**.
- One-sixth of each cohort will receive inheritance > lifetime labor
  earnings of the bottom half — this half largely coincides with those
  who inherit almost nothing.

### The Rentier, Enemy of Democracy

- "Rent" and "rentier" became **pejorative** in 20th century. ECB
  president declared "We must fight against rents" — meaning monopoly
  abuse (taxis, hairdressers).
- **Piketty's clarification**: income from capital has nothing to do with
  monopoly imperfection — it is the consequence of a **pure, perfect
  capital market** where all owners earn the highest diversified return.
- Growing sophistication of capital markets **sharpens** the distinction
  between owners and managers — the opposite of blurring labor/capital
  lines.
- **r > g is not a market imperfection**: freeing markets won't eliminate
  rentiers; universal suffrage ended legal domination of politics by the
  wealthy but did not abolish the economic forces producing a rentier
  society.
- Economic/technological rationality ≠ democratic rationality; democracy
  requires its own specific institutions beyond just markets.

### The Return of Inherited Wealth: European Pattern

- French U-shaped inheritance curve appears across Europe — Germany and
  Britain data confirm the same pattern (Figure 11.12).

**Germany**: inheritance flows ~16% of national income (1910) → ~2% (1960)
  — even steeper than France due to twin war shocks; rebounded sharply to
  10–11% by 2000–2010. Rebound driven heavily by gifts: from 10–20% of
  total inheritances (before 1970) to ~60% by 2000–2010. With today's
  stagnant German demographics, flows may eventually exceed France's.

**Britain**: ~20–25% of national income at 1900; fell less sharply (wealth
  stock less violently destroyed); fell to ~6% (1970–80), rebounded to
  ~8% by 2000–10. Britain's weaker rebound partly explained by lower
  gift-giving: gifts stayed ~10% of inheritances since 1970 vs. 60–80%
  in France and Germany. If Britain's gift/inheritance ratio matched
  France's, flow would also reach ~15% of national income.

### US Inheritance Data Problems

- **US federal estate tax** (created 1916) applies to <2% of estates;
  gift reporting requirements limited.
- Survey data severely underestimate bequests — French surveys show
  declared gifts/bequests = barely half the fiscal data.
- People systematically forget or underreport inherited wealth.
- Famous 1980s controversy: **Modigliani** (inherited wealth = 20–30%
  of US capital) vs. **Kotlikoff-Summers** (70–80%). Both used poor
  data. Piketty's verdict: truth closer to Kotlikoff-Summers; inherited
  wealth probably ≥50–60% of total US private capital in 1970–80.
- US shows a **less pronounced U-shape**: smaller at both ends of the
  20th century, larger in 1950–70. Main reason: higher US demographic
  growth → smaller β and less pronounced aging effects. This is NOT
  cultural — it's demographics.

**Emerging Countries**:
- China: with income growth of 5–10%/year, wealth is built primarily
  from savings, not grandparents. Inheritance currently of limited
  importance.

## Chapter 12: Global Inequality of Wealth in the Twenty-First Century

### The Inequality of Returns on Capital

- Most economic models assume **equal returns regardless of portfolio
  size** — Piketty argues this is wrong and consequential.
- Two reasons wealthier owners earn **higher average returns**:
  1. **Economies of scale** in portfolio management — large fortunes can
     hire better advisors and access superior investments.
  2. **Risk tolerance** — wealthy owners can be more patient and take
     more risk, boosting long-run returns.
- Plausible range: if average return = 4%, wealthy may earn 6–7% while
  small savers earn only 2–3%.
- Evidence: largest global fortunes have grown at **6–7%/year** above
  inflation in recent decades — far above average wealth growth.
- **Key insight**: unequal returns amplify r > g; r − g can be large for
  top fortunes even if modest for the economy as a whole.
- Only "natural" countervailing force: high economic growth, which
  moderates the relative advantage of large fortunes. At 3.5% global
  growth (1990–2030), large fortunes still grow faster but less
  spectacularly than at 1–2% growth.

### The Evolution of Global Wealth Rankings

- Piketty defends using **Forbes billionaire rankings** despite flaws —
  they respond to a legitimate social demand governments fail to supply.
- **Forbes global billionaire list** (since 1987): ~140 billionaires
  (1987) → ~1,400 (2013); total wealth $300B → $5,400B.
  - Per 100 million adults: 5 billionaires (1987) → 30 (2013).
  - Share of global private wealth: 0.4% (1987) → 1.5% (2013).
- Fixing the *percentage* of world population and tracking wealth:
  - Top 1/20 million (~150–225 adults globally): average wealth grew
    from $1.5B to $15B (1987–2013) = **6.4%/year real**.
  - Top 1/100 million (~30–45 adults): $3B → $35B = **6.8%/year real**.
  - Compare: average global wealth per capita grew 2.1%/year; average
    income 1.4%/year (Table 12.1).
- Shares of top wealth fractiles in global wealth tripled in under 30
  years (Figure 12.3).

### Global Wealth Reports and Current Distribution

- **Crédit Suisse** (since 2010) publishes annual global wealth report;
  **Merrill Lynch**, **Allianz** track HNWI.
- Best current estimate of global wealth inequality in early 2010s —
  comparable to **Europe in 1900–1910** (the Belle Époque peak):
  - Top thousandth (~4.5M people): ~20% of global wealth (~€10M avg).
  - Top centile (~45M people): ~50% of global wealth (~€3M avg).
  - Top decile: 80–90% of global wealth.
  - Bottom half: <5%.
- Caveat: global inequality may NOT be clearly increasing at present —
  poorer countries catching up may temporarily offset divergence at top.

### Heirs and Entrepreneurs in the Wealth Rankings

- **Key finding**: past a certain threshold, all large fortunes — inherited
  or entrepreneurial — grow at similarly extreme rates.
- **Bill Gates** (entrepreneur, Microsoft): fortune grew $4B → $50B
  (1990–2010).
- **Liliane Bettencourt** (L'Oréal heiress, never worked): fortune grew
  $2B → $25B over same period.
- Both grew at **>13%/year nominal** (10–11% real) — **identical rates**.
- Once a fortune exceeds a threshold, nearly all income can be reinvested;
  compound growth takes over entirely.
- **Steve Jobs** at peak (2011): worth ~$8B — 1/6 of Gates, 1/3 of
  Bettencourt. Forbes lists dozens of heirs wealthier than Jobs.
- Forbes journalists systematically **undercount inherited fortunes**
  (held in diversified portfolios, harder to detect).
- Forbes owner **Steve Forbes** is himself an heir (grandfather founded
  the magazine in 1917) — ideological bias built in.
- Rankings break fortunes: ~1/3 pure entrepreneurs, ~1/3 pure heirs,
  ~1/3 heirs who "grow their wealth." Accounting for biases: **inherited
  wealth likely represents 60–70%** of the largest global fortunes (vs.
  80–90% in Belle Époque France — lower today partly because high growth
  admits new emerging-economy fortunes).
- **The entrepreneurial argument cannot justify unlimited wealth
  concentration**: r > g + unequal returns by portfolio size means
  fortunes grow and perpetuate themselves "beyond all reasonable limits
  and beyond any possible rational justification in terms of social
  utility."
- **Entrepreneurs inevitably become rentiers** — not just across
  generations but within a single lifetime as life expectancy extends.

### The Moral Hierarchy of Wealth — and Why It Fails

- **Progressive annual tax on capital** sidesteps futile moral debates:
  every fortune is "partially justified yet potentially excessive."
- **Carlos Slim** (Mexico, telecom/real estate, monopoly rents via
  corrupt government) vs. **Bill Gates** (hailed as meritorious):
  contrast is "largely arbitrary" — Gates also profited from a virtual
  monopoly on operating systems; his innovations depended on thousands
  of engineers.
- Japanese billionaires **Yoshiaka Tsutsumi** and **Taikichiro Mori**
  topped Forbes 1987–1994, largely forgotten in West — yet Japanese
  growth 1950–1990 was the greatest in history.
- **Teodorin Obiang** (son of Equatorial Guinea's dictator): French court
  seized 200 cubic meters of luxury property (2012). Finding similar
  cases among Russian oligarchs or Gulf billionaires in Paris/London
  would not be hard.
- A fortune deemed suspect often correlates with owner's race/nationality,
  not actual facts; **a capital tax is a less blunt, more systematic
  instrument**.

> "Every fortune is partially justified yet potentially excessive.
> Outright theft is rare, as is absolute merit."

### The Pure Return on University Endowments

- ~850 US universities manage endowments (data from **National Association
  of College and University Business Officers** since 1979).
- Endowment range: $11.5M (North Iowa Community College) to ~$30B
  (Harvard); Yale ~$20B; Princeton and Stanford >$15B; MIT and Columbia
  ~$10B.
- Total: ~$400B across 850 universities.

**Table 12.2 — Real Annual Returns on US University Endowments, 1980–2010**:

| Category | Avg. Real Annual Return |
|---|---|
| All universities (850) | 8.2% |
| Harvard, Yale, Princeton | 10.2% |
| Endowments >$1B (60) | 8.8% |
| Endowments $500M–$1B (66) | 7.8% |
| Endowments $100M–$500M (226) | 7.1% |
| Endowments <$100M (498) | 6.2% |

- Returns are **net real** — after inflation (avg. 2.4%/yr 1980–2010),
  management fees, and administrative costs.
- **Key finding 1**: average return of 8.2% (7.2% for 1990–2010) —
  extremely high, consistent with Forbes billionaire returns.
- **Key finding 2**: return rises systematically with endowment size —
  Harvard/Yale/Princeton earn **twice** what the smallest endowments earn;
  gap is persistent, not driven by greater risk-taking.

**Why larger endowments earn more — Alternative investments:**
- <$50M endowments: ~10% in alternatives.
- $50–100M: ~25%; $100–500M: ~35%; $500M–$1B: ~45%; >$1B: >60%.
- "Alternatives" = private equity, unlisted foreign stocks, hedge funds,
  derivatives, real estate, raw materials, energy.
- Harvard spends ~$100M/yr to manage its $30B endowment (0.3%/yr).
- A $1B endowment can afford only ~$5–10M in management.
- North Iowa Community College: $11.5M → can afford a quarter-time
  financial advisor.
- A median-wealth US citizen has ~$100,000 — must rely on their
  "brother-in-law."
- Returns at Harvard/Yale are no more volatile than smaller endowments
  — higher returns reflect superior strategy, **not greater risk**.
- Alumni gifts are only 1/10 to 1/5 of annual endowment returns — the
  investment return, not philanthropy, funds university prosperity.

**Caveats**:
- 1980–2010 returns partly reflect a **long-term rebound in global asset
  prices** that may not continue.
- **Family vs. institution**: institutions are immortal; families have
  prodigal children — random shock prevents indefinite individual
  concentration; but a feeble check on divergence.
- Wealthy families increasingly use **trust funds and foundations** to
  approximate institutional immortality.

### Inflation Does Not Reduce Inequality of Returns to Capital

- Common belief: inflation reduces average return on capital → **FALSE**.
- Asset prices (real estate, financial securities) tend to rise at same
  pace as consumer prices → no reduction in average real return.
- What inflation *does* do: **redistributes** average return among
  citizens in complex, largely unpredictable ways.
- True in one sense: inflation penalizes holders of idle cash — "inflation
  is a tax on the idle rich."
- **But**: investing in real assets (real estate, stocks) fully escapes
  the inflation tax. University endowment data confirms large fortunes
  easily earn 10% real returns even at 2% inflation.
- Inflation may actually **worsen** inequality of returns:
  - Large fortunes: financial managers navigate inflation easily.
  - Mid-size fortunes (€10–50M): stockbrokers and advisors.
  - Small savers (€10–50K): checking/savings accounts paying little more
    than inflation. In the 19th century (zero inflation), small savers
    could earn 3–4% real by simply buying government bonds — now closed.
- **Real estate size effect**: those with wealth inherit or buy homes
  (equivalent 3–4% real return); those without pay rent while bank
  savings barely keep pace with inflation.
- Piketty's conclusion: inflation does not eliminate rent; it probably
  **makes the capital distribution more unequal**. Inflation is "an
  extremely blunt instrument, and often a counterproductive one."
- A **progressive tax on capital** is far more appropriate.

### Sovereign Wealth Funds: Capital and Politics

- **Norwegian sovereign wealth fund**: >€700B in 2013 (twice all US
  university endowments combined); most transparent; more conservative
  strategy; returns apparently lower; management costs <0.1% of assets.
- **Abu Dhabi Investment Authority** (world's largest SWF, ~same size
  as Norway): claims real return >7% (1990–2010), >8% (1980–2010).
- **Saudi Arabia's SWF** (3rd largest): reports only 2–3% average return,
  mainly from US Treasury bonds. Why low-yield Treasuries?
  - Political/military: Saudi Arabia effectively lends at low rates to
    the country that protects it militarily.
  - "The dynamics of the global distribution of capital are at once
    economic, political, and military" — same as the colonial era.

### Will Sovereign Wealth Funds Own the World?

- Total SWF assets in 2013: ~$5.3 trillion.
  - Petroleum-exporting states: ~$3.2 trillion (Norway, Abu Dhabi, Saudi
    Arabia, Kuwait, Qatar, Russia, Dubai, Libya, Kazakhstan, others).
  - Non-petroleum states: ~$2.1 trillion (China, Hong Kong, Singapore).
- This equals roughly the **total wealth of Forbes billionaires** (~$5.4T
  in 2013) — both represent ~1.5% of total world private wealth.
- Key difference from billionaires: petroleum SWFs grow from **both**
  reinvesting returns AND from investing new oil sale proceeds.
- Natural resource rents: ~5% of global GDP since mid-2000s (half
  petroleum, half gas/coal/minerals/wood) — up from ~2% in 1990s,
  <1% in early 1970s.
- Plausible scenario: SWFs own 10–20%+ of global capital by 2030–2040.
- r > g with continuous petroleum income creates **permanent divergence**
  in global capital distribution.

### Will China Own the World?

- Piketty's central scenario: as emerging countries converge on
  productivity, their growth rates will slow; diffusion of knowledge is
  fundamentally an **equalizing process**.
- Central scenario: savings rates stabilize at ~10%; capital/income
  ratios converge; no major global imbalance.
- **Figure 12.4**: world capital/income ratio projected near **700%** of
  world income by end of 21st century.

**International Divergence vs. Oligarchic Divergence**:
- Key distinction: international divergence (rich countries acquired by
  China/petroleum exporters) vs. **oligarchic divergence** (all countries
  owned by their own or global billionaires).
- Oligarchic divergence already well underway; as global growth slows and
  capital competition heats up, r >> g in decades ahead.
- Fear of Chinese ownership is "pure fantasy":
  - European household net real estate + financial assets: ~70 trillion
    euros.
  - Chinese sovereign wealth funds + Bank of China reserves: ~3 trillion
    euros (less than 1/20th of European figure).
  - 97% of Paris real estate price increase caused by wealthy French
    buyers — foreign buyers explain barely 3%.
- **Real source of dispossession feeling**: political secession of largest
  fortunes — wealth is private, cannot be mobilized by governments.
- **Oligarchic divergence harder to combat**: requires high international
  coordination among states that ordinarily compete.

> "Oligarchic divergence is not only more probable than international
> divergence, it is also much more difficult to combat." — Piketty

### Are the Rich Countries Really Poor?

- Official statistics show wealthy countries' net asset position vs. rest
  of world is slightly **negative**: ~−4% of global GDP in 2010.
- Global balance of payments is **regularly negative** — "Earth must be
  owned by Mars." Attributed to statistical bias and measurement error.
- **Gabriel Zucman** (Swiss bank data): most plausible explanation is
  large amounts of **unreported financial assets in tax havens**.
- Zucman's estimate: ~10% of global GDP held in tax havens (cautious
  lower bound; NGOs estimate 2–3x larger).
- At least three-quarters belong to residents of **rich countries**.
- Conclusion: rich countries' net position is actually **positive** —
  masked by wealthy residents hiding assets offshore.

## Chapter 13: A Social State for the Twenty-First Century

### Opening Argument

- Core question: Can we imagine political institutions to regulate
  **patrimonial capitalism** justly and efficiently, or must we await
  the next crisis or global war?
- Ideal policy: **progressive global tax on capital** — would also expose
  wealth to democratic scrutiny, enabling effective regulation of banking
  and international capital flows.

### The Crisis of 2008 and the Return of the State

- 2008 crisis: most serious since 1929; production fell no more than 5
  percentage points in wealthy countries (vs. −25% in 1929–1935);
  unemployment rose but no Great Depression.
- Why less severe: governments and central banks created liquidity to
  avoid bank failures — unlike 1929 "liquidationist" orthodoxy under
  **Herbert Hoover**. **Franklin Roosevelt** replaced Hoover 1933.
- Pragmatic 2008 policies avoided the worst but did NOT address structural
  problems: lack of financial transparency, rising inequality.

> "The crisis of 2008 was the first crisis of the globalized patrimonial
> capitalism of the twenty-first century. It is unlikely to be the last."

- No real "return of the state": post-2008 Washington debating whether
  Obama could raise top rate from ~35% to ~40% (Clinton-era level), vs.
  Roosevelt raising it to 80%+ within years of inauguration (from
  Hoover's 25%).

### The Growth of the Social State in the Twentieth Century

- Best measure of state's role: total taxes as % of national income
  (Figure 13.1: US, Britain, France, Sweden, 1870–2010).
- Striking similarity: taxes **< 10% of national income** in all four
  countries through 19th century and up to WWI.
- State at that time confined to "regalian" functions: police, courts,
  army, foreign affairs (7–8% sufficient; military often >half).
- Between 1920 and 1980: tax share multiplied by **3–4x** (Nordic
  countries: 5x) — driven by social spending.
- Post-1980: tax share **stabilized everywhere**:
  - US: just over 30%; Britain: ~40%; Germany: ~45%; France: ~50%;
    Sweden: ~55%.
- "The state's great leap forward has already taken place: there will
  be no second leap."

**Composition of social spending (~25–35% of national income)**:
- **Education + health**: 10–15% (public spending ~75% of costs in Europe,
  ~50% in US).
- **Replacement incomes + transfer payments**: 10–20% (pensions,
  unemployment insurance, welfare).
- Pensions dominate: 2/3–3/4 of replacement income.
  - Continental Europe: pensions alone = 12–13% of national income
    (Italy and France at top).
  - US and Britain: only 6–7% of national income.
- Unemployment insurance: typically 1–2% of national income.
- Income support/welfare: <1% of national income — smallest item but
  most politically contested.

### Modern Redistribution: A Logic of Rights

- Modern redistribution ≠ simple transfers from rich to poor; rather:
  financing **public services and replacement incomes roughly equal for
  everyone**.
- Built around a **logic of rights** and equal access to fundamental goods.
- **John Rawls**, *Theory of Justice*: "difference principle" —
  inequalities acceptable only if they benefit the least advantaged.
- **Amartya Sen**: "capabilities" approach — similar logic.
- In practice, 19th-century political systems emerging from both
  revolutions focused mainly on **protection of property rights**,
  not social rights.

### Modernizing Rather Than Dismantling the Social State

- Broad consensus in Europe around the "European social model" — no
  serious movement to return to 10–20% tax/regalian state.
- Two reasons further major expansion is neither realistic nor desirable:
  1. Postwar expansion facilitated by 5%/year growth; with 1%/year per
     capita growth since 1980s, steady tax increases mean slower/negative
     real income growth.
  2. Beyond a certain size, public sector faces serious organizational
     problems.
- Education + health already = **20% of employment and GDP** in developed
  economies — more than all industry sectors combined.

### Do Educational Institutions Foster Social Mobility?

- Despite massive rise in average education levels, **earned income
  inequality did not decrease** — qualification levels simply shifted
  upward (diplomas inflated).
- **Intergenerational correlation of education and earned incomes shows
  no trend toward greater mobility** over the long run; mobility may have
  decreased recently.
- Most robust finding: intergenerational reproduction is **lowest in
  Nordic countries**, **highest in the United States** (correlation
  coefficient two-thirds higher than Sweden).

### American Exceptionalism Myth

- The belief that the US had exceptionally high mobility compared to
  class-bound Europe was true in the early 19th century.
- Today, available data suggest **social mobility is lower in the US
  than in Europe**.
- Data: proportion of college degrees earned by children from the **bottom
  two income quartiles stagnated at 10–20% in 1970–2010**, while it rose
  from 40–80% for children with parents in the top quartile.
- "**Parents' income has become an almost perfect predictor of university
  access**."
- Average income of **Harvard** students' parents: ~$450,000 — the top
  2% of US income hierarchy.
- **Sciences Po** (Paris): average parental income ~90,000 euros (top
  10% of France).

**France's grandes écoles**: more public money spent on students from
  advantaged backgrounds; less on university students from modest
  backgrounds — the state amplifies inequalities of origin.

**Emile Boutmy** founded Sciences Po in 1872 explicitly to preserve
upper-class political hegemony through a meritocratic veneer:

> "Obliged to submit to the rule of the majority, the classes that call
> themselves the upper classes can preserve their political hegemony only
> by invoking the rights of the most capable." — Emile Boutmy, 1872

### The Future of Retirement: PAYGO and Low Growth

**PAYGO mechanics**:
- Rate of return equals the growth rate of the economy.
- When introduced mid-20th century, growth rates were ~5% in continental
  Europe — ideal conditions.
- Today: growth ~1.5% in rich countries; **r > g** means return on capital
  (4–5%) significantly exceeds PAYGO return.

**Why not switch to capitalized pensions?**
- Switching raises a **fundamental transition problem**: an entire
  generation of retirees would be left with nothing.
- Return on capital is **extremely volatile** — PAYGO returns are 5–10×
  less volatile than capital returns.
- Conclusion: PAYGO will remain, but reforms are needed.

**Pension reform challenges**:
- Raising retirement age has merit but must differentiate: intellectual
  workers vs. manual workers with arduous jobs and lower life expectancy.
- Key reform proposal: **unified retirement scheme based on individual
  accounts with equal rights for everyone**.

> "A public pension is the patrimony of those without patrimony."

### The Social State in Poor and Emerging Countries

- Poorest countries (Sub-Saharan Africa, South Asia): governments took
  10–15% of national income in 1970–80. Fell further to ~10% by 1990s.
- At only 10–15%, a state can barely fulfill regalian functions — nothing
  left for education and health.
- Post-1980 ultraliberal wave forced poor countries to cut public sectors;
  trade liberalization eliminated customs duties (~5% of national income
  in 1970s) without ensuring replacement revenue.
- Rich countries used the developing world as a "field of experimentation,
  without really seeking to capitalize on the lessons of their own
  historical experience."
- **China**: advancing in tax modernization — income tax applicable to
  large population, possibly developing a European-style social state.
  **India**: greater difficulty moving beyond low-taxation equilibrium.

## Chapter 14: Rethinking the Progressive Income Tax

### The Question of Progressive Taxation

- **Taxation is not a technical matter** — it is preeminently political
  and philosophical; without taxes, society has no common destiny.
- Three main tax categories: **taxes on income**, **taxes on capital**,
  **taxes on consumption** — plus 20th-century addition: **social
  insurance contributions**.
- Consumption taxes (VAT, excise duties) are often the most hated and
  heaviest burden on lower classes — the **salt tax** under the Ancien
  Régime is the archetype.
- **Proportional tax** ("flat tax"): same rate for everyone.
- **Progressive tax**: higher rates for higher earners/owners.
- **Regressive tax**: rate falls for richer individuals — e.g., Thatcher's
  **poll tax**, which ended her tenure in 1990.

### Modern Tax Systems Are Roughly Proportional — With a Regressive Top

- **France 2010 case study**: overall tax rate averages 47% of national
  income, but:
  - Bottom 50%: pay 40–45%.
  - Middle 40%: pay 45–50%.
  - Top 5% and especially top 1%: pay lower rates.
  - **Top 0.1%: pay only 35%**.
  - Reason: capital income (largely exempt from progressive tax) dominates
    at top; consumption taxes and social contributions dominate at bottom.
- If the wealthy effectively secede fiscally, middle-class consent for
  the social state collapses.

### The Progressive Tax: A Product of War, Not Democracy

- Pre-WWI: progressive income tax laws existed in many countries but
  **top rates stagnated at insignificant levels** — generally below
  5–10%, never raised significantly.
  - Prussia: top rate stable at 3% (1891–1914), rose to 4% during WWI,
    then jumped to **40%** in 1919–20.
  - US: top rate jumped to 67% then 77% in 1918–19.
  - Britain: 8% in 1909, suddenly >40% after the war.
  - France: top rate **2% in 1914**; raised to 50% in 1920, 60% in 1924,
    72% in 1925.
- June 25, 1920 French law (top rate to 50%) was passed by the right-
  wing **"blue-sky Chamber"** — same politicians who had vehemently
  opposed 2% before the war; wartime debt, strikes of May–June 1919,
  and the **Bolshevik Revolution of 1917** changed the political calculus.
- Without the shock of 1914–1918, top rates might never have risen as high.

### Pre-WWI Tax Systems

- **Ancien Régime France**: direct taxes included the *taille*, *dixième*,
  *vingtième* — flat 5–10% rates with many exemptions.
- **1707**: **Vauban** proposed *dixième royal* — 10% on ALL income
  including aristocratic rents — never fully implemented.
- **Revolutionary France** adopted an "indicial" tax system (taxes based
  on proxies, not declared income). The **"door and window tax"** —
  number of openings used as a proxy for wealth.
- Before WWI, in all developed countries, income tax rates above 10%
  were not considered "reasonable" regardless of income level.

### Germany's Estate Tax Debate

- **August Bebel** and **Eduard Bernstein** (Social Democratic Party)
  argued estate taxes could replace burdensome indirect taxes on workers.
- 1906 and 1909 reforms created a tiny estate tax, but **bequests to
  spouses or children were entirely exempt** regardless of size.
- Not until **1919** did Germany extend the estate tax to family bequests;
  top rate jumped from 0 to **35 percent**.

### Figure 14.2: Top Inheritance Tax Rates, 1900–2013

- US top estate tax rate: dropped from 70% (1980) to 35% (2013).
- Britain reached **98%** on highest incomes/estates in the 1940s and
  again in the 1970s — **absolute historical record**.
- France and Germany: top rates never exceeded 30–40% except Germany
  1946–1949 (90%, set by occupying US authorities; fell to ~50% once
  Germany regained fiscal sovereignty in 1950).

### Confiscatory Taxation of Excessive Incomes: An American Invention

- The **confiscatory progressive tax** on "excessive" incomes was an
  **Anglo-Saxon invention**, especially American.
- US was first to try rates above 70%: on income in **1919–1922**, and
  on estates in **1937–1939**.
- Primary purpose of 70–80%+ rates: **not revenue** but to put an end
  to incomes and fortunes deemed socially unacceptable and economically
  unproductive.

### Why Anglo-Saxon Countries Led

- US and Britain value individual liberty highly → attracted to
  progressive tax as a tool vs. continental Europe's preference for
  public ownership and direct salary controls.
- **Irving Fisher** (1919), as American Economic Association president:
  "2 percent of the population owns more than 50 percent of the wealth"
  — an "**undemocratic distribution of wealth**." Proposed estate tax
  rising to two-thirds of the estate, reaching 100% if more than three
  generations old.
- **Great Depression**: Roosevelt raised top income tax rate:
  - 63% (1933) → 79% (1937) → 88% (1942) → **94% (1944)**.
  - Stabilized ~90% until mid-1960s; fell to 70% in early 1980s.
  - **Average top federal income tax rate 1932–1980: 81%** — nearly
    half a century.
- No continental European country has ever imposed such high rates for
  as long as half a century.

### The Explosion of Executive Salaries: The Role of Taxation

- After 1980, US and Britain reversed course — top rates fell from
  80–90% to 30–40%.
- US low point: **28 percent** after Reagan's 1986 tax reform.
- Continental Europe and Japan: top rates **held steady** at 50–60%.

### Key Empirical Finding: Tax Cuts and Inequality Are Perfectly Correlated

- Across all developed countries: **the size of the decrease in top
  marginal income tax rate since 1980 is perfectly correlated with the
  increase in the top centile's share of national income**.
- Countries that cut top rates most saw the biggest explosions in
  executive pay; countries that didn't saw moderate increases.
- **Supply-side explanation is implausible**: per capita GDP growth has
  been almost exactly the same in all rich countries since 1980 — Britain
  and the US have NOT grown faster than Germany, France, Japan, Denmark,
  or Sweden.
- **Bargaining power model** (Piketty, Saez, Stantcheva): lower top tax
  rates transform how executive pay is set — when 80–90% of a raise goes
  to government, executives have little reason to fight for it and boards
  have little reason to grant it; when rates fall to 30%, both sides
  change behavior.
- Executive pay shows higher elasticity with respect to **"luck"** (sector-
  wide gains unrelated to individual talent) than to actual performance —
  inconsistent with productivity-based pay theory.
- Elasticity with respect to luck is higher in countries with lower top
  marginal tax rates.
- **US productivity counter-evidence**: US productivity growth was
  **nearly twice as high** in 1950–1970 as in 1990–2010 — when the US
  was at the world technology frontier in both periods.

### Rethinking the Question of the Top Marginal Rate

- **Optimal top marginal tax rate in developed countries is probably
  above 80%**.
  - Applies to extremely high incomes: top 1% or top 0.5%, i.e., above
    $500,000–$1 million/year.
  - An 80% rate on incomes above $500K–$1M would **not reduce US economic
    growth** but would distribute fruits of growth more widely and
    discourage economically useless behavior.
  - Such a rate would raise little revenue (quickly eliminates the incomes
    it targets) — to fund the social state, rates of 50–60% on incomes
    above $200,000 would also be needed.
- Political obstacle: US political process may be **captured by the 1
  percent**; US politicians of both parties are far wealthier than
  European counterparts.
- **Josiah Wedgwood** (1938): "political democracies that do not
  democratize their economic systems are inherently unstable."

> "The egalitarian pioneer ideal has faded into oblivion, and the New
> World may be on the verge of becoming the Old Europe of the twenty-
> first century's globalized economy."

## Chapter 15: A Global Tax on Capital

### A Global Tax on Capital: A Useful Utopia

- The **progressive income tax and social state**, though essential, are
  not sufficient to regulate globalized patrimonial capitalism — new tools
  are required.
- The **ideal tool**: a **progressive global tax on capital**, coupled
  with very high international financial transparency.
  - Would prevent endless inegalitarian spiral and control dynamics of
    global capital concentration.
- Acknowledged as **utopian** in the short term; useful as a reference
  point.
- Pathway: countries can move incrementally, starting at regional level
  (e.g., Europe).
- Without such progress, a **nationalist defensive reaction** — protectionism,
  capital controls — is likely, causing frustration and international
  tensions.

### The Tax Structure Proposed

- **Base**: progressive annual tax on **individual net wealth** — all
  assets (real estate, financial, business) minus debt. No exceptions;
  covers listed and unlisted firms.
- **Illustrative rate schedule**:
  - 0% below €1 million.
  - 1% on €1–5 million.
  - 2% above €5 million.
  - Possibly 5–10% above €1 billion.
  - Optional: 0.1% below €200,000; 0.5% on €200,000–€1 million.
- **Key distinction from existing wealth taxes**: current property taxes
  and European *impôts sur la fortune* are riddled with exemptions,
  arbitrary valuations, and flat rates.

### Primary Purpose: Regulation, Not Revenue

- Capital tax would raise only **a few points of national income** at most.
- Primary goals:
  1. Stop the indefinite increase of wealth inequality.
  2. Impose effective regulation on financial/banking systems.
- Essential prerequisite: **democratic and financial transparency** —
  clarity about who owns what assets around the world.

### Financial Transparency as the Primary Goal

- A flat 0.1% tax on all assets would generate ~0.5% of global income
  (global capital stock ≈ 5× global income).
- Revenue is secondary; **financial transparency** is the real payoff.
- Without a global capital tax, citizens must rely on *Forbes*, glossy
  wealth-manager reports, and other unofficial sources.
- A 0.1% tax functions more as **compulsory reporting law** than a true
  tax — ownership must be declared to be legally recognized.
- Analogy: French Revolution's cadastral surveys and compulsory property
  registration.

### Automatic Transmission of Banking Information

- No technical obstacle to sharing banking data internationally.
- Tax haven justification ("governments will misuse data") is self-
  refuting — why would domestic account holders be treated differently?

> "The most plausible reason why tax havens defend bank secrecy is that
> it allows their clients to evade their fiscal obligations... No one
> has the right to set his own tax rates. That is outright theft." — Piketty

- **FATCA** (Foreign Account Tax Compliance Act, US, 2010, phased in
  2014–2015): most ambitious attempt — requires ALL foreign banks to
  report to Treasury on US taxpayer accounts.
- Far stronger than 2003 EU directive (covered only interest-bearing
  deposits, not stocks; applied only to European banks).
- EU directive is "timid and almost meaningless": **Luxembourg** and
  **Austria** won exemption from automatic reporting.
- FATCA weakness: 30% surtax on noncompliant banks' US income is
  insufficient — smaller banks without US operations can manage foreign
  assets with complete impunity.
- Effective enforcement requires **sanctions on countries**, not just
  banks — e.g., 30%+ tariff on exports of noncompliant states.
- Europe's largest countries (France, Germany) bear primary
  responsibility for failure to achieve tax coordination.

### What Is the Purpose of a Tax on Capital?

**Contributive justification (primary)**:
- Income is not a well-defined concept for the ultra-wealthy.
- Example: **Liliane Bettencourt** (30B+ euro fortune) declared income
  never exceeded 5M euros/year — less than 1/100th of her true economic
  income (dividends + capital gains + retained appreciation).
- This is not tax evasion — it is perfectly legal to let returns
  accumulate in family trusts and holding companies.
- Solution options:
  1. Tax all income including amounts accumulating in trusts/partnerships.
  2. Impute a flat yield (e.g., 5%) on capital stock (tried by
     **Netherlands**, ran into difficulties).
  3. Apply progressive tax directly to total wealth (cleanest approach).

**Incentive justification (secondary)**:
- Capital tax forces inefficient owners (earning 2–3%) to sell to more
  dynamic investors (earning 10%).
- Ideal system = compromise between incentive logic (tax capital stock)
  and insurance logic (tax revenue stream).
- All three taxes — inheritance, income, capital — play useful,
  complementary roles.

### A Blueprint for a European Wealth Tax

- **Proposed rate schedule** (moderate, permanent annual basis):
  - 0% on net wealth below €1M.
  - 1% on €1–5M.
  - 2% above €5M.
  - Affects ~2.5% of EU population; would raise **~2% of EU GDP**.
- Higher rates justified for very large fortunes: real returns on top
  fortunes are 6–7%+, so 5% or even 10% on billionaires would not be
  confiscatory.

**Italian case study (failure, 2012)**:
- Set real estate rate at 0.8% and financial assets at 0.1% (stocks
  totally exempt) — effectively **regressive** since large fortunes are
  mostly financial assets. Candidate who proposed it was "roundly defeated
  at the polls."
- Root problem: without automatic EU-wide bank data sharing, a solo
  country must heavily exempt financial assets or face capital flight to
  Switzerland, Austria, France.

- France's **ISF**: more modern but riddled with exemptions — especially
  "business assets."
- Germany and Sweden abolished their capital taxes (1990–2010) due to
  archaic design (fixed assessments losing touch with market values),
  not tax competition per se.

**Ideal rate-setting principle**: set rates based on **observed returns
in each wealth bracket** over several prior years; adjust progressivity
to control desired degree of wealth concentration.

### Capital Taxation in Historical Perspective

- Most common historical response to r > g: **prohibition of usury** in
  Christianity and Islam.
- **Aristotle**: money should not give birth to more money; Greek word
  for interest (*tocos*) means "child."
- Prohibition of usury was illogical in practice: it restricted commercial
  lending but never questioned land rents — from which the Church itself
  benefited. It was social control, not principled anti-capital policy.
- **Karl Marx**: proposed abolishing private ownership of means of
  production entirely. Soviet experiment: eliminated all private capital
  returns; **r > g** became "a bad memory." Fatal flaw: private property
  and markets also coordinate millions of individual decisions — Soviet
  centralized planning caused human disasters.
- Progressive capital taxation is the **"less violent and more efficient"**
  response.

### Alternative Regulation: Protectionism and Capital Controls

- **Protectionism**: useful for sheltering infant industries; does nothing
  to counter r > g.
- **Capital controls**: since 1980s, OECD/World Bank/IMF advocated
  complete capital liberalization; since 2008 crisis, serious doubts
  about this consensus.
- **China**: never had convertible currency; enforces strict controls on
  both incoming and outgoing capital. Chinese notion of property rights
  fundamentally different from European/American notions.
- A Chinese billionaire with 20% of Telecom China could not easily
  emigrate to Switzerland and collect dividends, unlike a Russian
  oligarch.
- Piketty does not endorse China's system (opaque and probably unstable)
  but notes capital controls do contain wealth inequality dynamics.
- China has more progressive income tax than Russia (which adopted a flat
  tax in the 1990s); invests revenues in education, health, and
  infrastructure at far larger scale than India.
- **"The capital tax is the liberal form of capital control and is better
  suited to Europe's comparative advantage."**

### The Redistribution of Petroleum Rents

- **Egypt's entire education budget** (85M people): under $5 billion
  (2012). **Saudi Arabia** (20M citizens): $300 billion in oil revenues.
  **Qatar** (300,000 Qataris): $100+ billion annually.
- Without global governance, redistribution happens violently — Iraq
  invaded Kuwait in 1990–91 partly because Kuwait held nearly equal
  petroleum reserves. Western powers sent 900,000 troops to restore
  Kuwait's ownership — proving governments can mobilize massive resources
  when they choose.

### Redistribution through Immigration

- The US grew from 3M to 300M largely through successive immigration
  waves — this kept it from becoming the "new Old Europe."
- **Immigration as stabilizer**: prevents accumulated capital from
  acquiring the dominance it has in Europe; makes large labor income
  inequalities politically bearable.
- Limits: even after per-capita convergence, the **dynamics of global
  wealth concentration** remain — immigration postpones but does not
  resolve the need for progressive capital taxation.

## Chapter 16: The Question of the Public Debt

### Overview

- Rich-country public debt now averages ~1 year of national income (~90%
  of GDP) — level not seen since 1945.
- **Europe's paradox**: highest private wealth in the world, yet greatest
  difficulty resolving its public debt crisis — the rich world is rich
  but its governments are poor.
- Emerging economies: much lower debt (~30% of GDP) — public debt is a
  question of **distribution** between public and private actors.

### Three Methods to Reduce Public Debt

**In order of Piketty's preference**:
1. **Exceptional tax on private capital** — most just and efficient.
2. **Inflation** — historically how most large debts were reduced; second-best.
3. **Austerity** — worst solution in terms of both justice and efficiency;
   yet Europe's current path.

**European wealth context**:
- National wealth ≈ 6× national income; nearly all privately owned.
- Net public wealth ≈ zero (public assets ≈ public debt).
- Wealthiest 10% own 60% of all private wealth, including (indirectly)
  all of Europe's sovereign debt.

### The Exceptional Capital Tax Solution

- A flat **15% tax on private wealth** would yield ~1 year of national
  income — enough for immediate full debt repayment.
- Better than **debt repudiation ("haircut")** for two reasons:
  1. Haircuts have unpredictable incidence — large portfolio holders can
     restructure to avoid losses.
  2. Capital tax adjusts contribution to size of fortune.
- A **progressive capital tax** is superior to a flat levy:
  - Spare modest fortunes; require more from the largest.
- **Practical example**:
  - To reduce debt by 20% of GDP (90% → 70%): apply one-time levy of 0%
    up to €1M, 10% on €1–5M, 20% above €5M.
  - France used progressive rates of 0–25% in its 1945 exceptional capital
    tax to substantially reduce postwar debt.
- **"Redemption fund"** idea (proposed 2011 by German economists): mutualize
  all Eurozone debt above 60% of GDP, reduce gradually — lacks democratic
  governance; could be paired with a ten-year progressive capital tax.

### Does Inflation Redistribute Wealth?

**How inflation works on debt**:
- Government bonds are nominal assets; inflation erodes their real value.
- Raising inflation from 2% to 5% would reduce real public debt by more
  than 15% of GDP — considerable but imprecise.

**Historical use of inflation**:
- France and Germany averaged 13% and 17% annual inflation respectively,
  1913–1950 — this is how they entered the 1950s with minimal debt burden.
- Germany used inflation most aggressively (along with outright repudiation)
  throughout its history — explains deep inflation aversion and ECB's
  extreme hawkishness.

**Austerity timelines**:
- At zero inflation + 2% GDP growth + 1% deficit limit: **20 years** to
  reduce debt by 20 points.
- Britain's 19th-century example: a **full century** of primary surpluses
  (2–3% of GDP, 1815–1914) to clear Napoleonic war debt; British taxpayers
  spent more on debt interest than on education.

**Problems with inflation as a tool**:
1. Hard to control: France exceeded 50% inflation for four consecutive
   years (1945–48) — wiped out small savers.
2. Germany 1923: prices multiplied by 100 million; society permanently
   scarred.
3. Expectation effects: once embedded, lenders demand higher rates.
4. Distributive imprecision: inflation spares real-asset holders (real
   estate, stocks) and debtors; harms cash-holders and savers — the
   wealthy mostly hold real assets so largely escape.

> "When it comes to decreasing inequalities of wealth for good or reducing
> unusually high levels of public debt, a progressive tax on capital is
> generally a better tool than inflation."

### What Do Central Banks Do?

**Post-gold standard**:
- Great Depression lesson: central banks refused to create liquidity →
  wave of bankruptcies → deflationary spiral.
- Universal consensus since 1930s: central banks must be **"lenders of
  last resort."**
- **Milton Friedman** and **Anna Schwartz**, *Monetary History of the
  United States* (1963): Fed's restrictive policy in early 1930s
  transformed the stock market crash into a credit/deflationary crisis.
  Political conclusion: only a well-run Fed is needed; the New Deal was
  a "costly and useless sham." This message created intellectual climate
  for the **conservative revolution of 1979–1980**.

**Post-2008 "unconventional" monetary policies**:
- Fed, Bank of England, Bank of Japan bought sovereign debt from crisis
  start; ECB eventually followed.
- Central bank balance sheets roughly **doubled** post-2008:
  - Fed: 10% → 20%+ of GDP.
  - ECB: 15% → 30% of GDP.
  - Still modest vs. total net private wealth (500–600% of GDP).

**What central banks actually do**:
- They do **not create wealth** — they redistribute it. A central bank
  loan creates matching assets and liabilities; net national capital does
  not change by a single dollar.
- **Key strength**: speed — can create unlimited liquidity in seconds.
- **Key weakness**: cannot decide with democratic legitimacy who should
  receive loans, in what amount, for what purpose.

### The Cyprus Crisis (2013)

- Cyprus: 1 million inhabitants; hypertrophied banking sector fed by
  large Russian deposits attracted by low taxes.
- **Troika's proposed levy** (March 2013): 6.75% on deposits ≤€100,000;
  9.9% above. Approved unanimously by 17 Eurozone finance ministers.
- Two fatal flaws: (1) nearly flat rate — small Cypriot savers taxed
  almost identically to Russian oligarchs with €10M accounts; (2) tax
  base limited to bank deposits, easily avoided by shifting to brokerage
  accounts, bonds, or real estate — effectively **regressive**.
- Final solution exempted deposits under €100,000.
- **Key lesson**: central banks can act quickly but cannot correctly target
  redistribution without data infrastructure; obstacle is **ideological,
  not technical**.

### The Euro: A Stateless Currency

- Europe created — for the first time in history at such scale — **a
  currency without a state**; Europe's GDP ≈ 25% of global GDP in 2013.
- **1992 Maastricht Treaty**: euro agreed in wake of Berlin Wall fall;
  became physical reality January 1, 2002.
- Conceived in the late 1980s–early 1990s when dominant ideology held
  central banks should only target **low inflation** — a product of 1970s
  stagflation. **2008 crisis shattered** this static vision.
- **ECB's unique constraints**:
  - Inflation-control objective has absolute statutory priority over
    growth and employment.
  - Cannot purchase newly issued government debt directly.
  - Faces 17 separate national debts and 17 governments.
- **Post-2008**: rates diverged rapidly; southern European countries lost
  the devaluation option; speculation on national interest rates proved
  **more destabilizing** than old exchange-rate speculation.

### The Question of European Unification

- Only solution: **pooling of public debts** among Eurozone members.
- Piketty's proposal: a **European "budgetary parliament"** drawn from
  members of national parliaments (not the existing European Parliament).
  - Decides by majority vote after open public debate.
  - European councils of heads of state meet "in secret, produce triumphal
    midnight communiqués — worthy of the Congress of Vienna (1815)."
- Tools that no single country can deploy alone:
  1. **Progressive tax on capital** — requires cross-border data sharing.
  2. **Tax on corporate profits** — since early 1990s, Ireland led a
     "race to the bottom"; proper fix: require single EU-level profit
     declaration, apportion revenues by sales/wages within each country.
  3. **Capital asset residence principle**: tax where the company operates,
     not where the owner lives.
- Without European political union, **tax competition** will continue to
  push toward consumption-based taxes (no progressivity possible) — the
  19th-century tax system.

### The Golden Rule of Capital Accumulation

- **Edmund Phelps (1961)**: **"golden rule of capital accumulation"** —
  maximum capital accumulation occurs when **r = g**.
  - At r = g: α = s (capital's income share equals savings rate) — all
    returns must be reinvested just to maintain capital/income ratio.
  - If r > g: capital yields something net; rentiers can consume (r − g).
  - Golden rule implies **capital saturation**: rentiers must reinvest
    everything — the reign of the rentier ends.
- Historical reality: r has **always** exceeded g significantly.
- To satisfy golden rule would require capital stock of **10–30 years**
  of national income (vs. current 6–7 years) — implausible.
- **Practical verdict**: simpler and more effective to tax rentiers than
  to accumulate capital until r falls to g.
- **Bottom line**: golden rule is of **limited practical utility**.

### Law and Politics

- **Maastricht Treaty (1992)**: stipulated deficits <3% of GDP and total
  debt <60% of GDP — economic logic for these specific numbers was never
  explained.
- These constraints are **historically unprecedented** — US, Britain,
  Japan have never imposed such rules on themselves.
- Piketty explicitly: "I have no particular liking for public debt":
  - Debt is often a **backdoor redistribution from poor to rich**: people
    with modest savings pay taxes to fund interest payments to those
    wealthy enough to lend to governments.
- **Striking fact**: European national wealth has never been higher — net
  public wealth ≈ zero, but **net private wealth** is so high that total
  national wealth is as great as it has been in a century.
  - "Private wealth rests on public poverty" — governments spend far more
    on debt interest than on higher education.

### Climate Change and Public Capital

- **The Stern Report (2006)**: potential environmental damage could reach
  dozens of points of global GDP per year.
- Core debate: **discount rate**.
  - **Nicholas Stern** (British): low discount rate ≈ growth rate
    (1–1.5%/yr) → justifies spending **≥5% of global GDP now** to
    mitigate climate change.
  - **William Nordhaus** (American): discount rate ≈ average return on
    capital (4–4.5%/yr); future generations will be richer and can cope
    → large present expenditure unreasonable.
  - Same cost estimates, opposite conclusions — debate comes down to
    **pure value judgments** about intergenerational weighting.
- Piketty sides with Stern.
- Unlike debt (reducible by capital tax), climate change **cannot be
  eliminated at the stroke of a pen** — requires knowing what to invest
  in, how to organize at 5% of global GDP scale.

### Economic Transparency and Democratic Control

- The public/private capital divide is not as clear as post-1989 ideology
  assumed; education, health, culture, media already involve hybrid forms.
- Democratic control of capital depends on **economic and financial
  transparency** — not for surveillance but for democratic governance.
- Example: **Lonmin, Inc.** (owner of Marikana platinum mine, South
  Africa, where 34 strikers were shot dead in August 2012) — published
  accounts do not reveal how wealth produced is divided between profits
  and wages; data "grouped in very broad statistical categories that
  reveal as little as possible about what is actually at stake."
- Without genuine accounting transparency AND real worker rights (including
  **board seats**), economic democracy is impossible.

> "If democracy is someday to regain control of capitalism, it must start
> by recognizing that the concrete institutions in which democracy and
> capitalism are embodied need to be reinvented again and again."

## Conclusion

### The Central Contradiction of Capitalism: r > g

- Market economy left to itself contains powerful **convergence forces**
  (diffusion of knowledge and skills) BUT also powerful **divergence
  forces** threatening democratic societies.
- **Principal destabilizing force**: private rate of return on capital r
  can remain significantly above growth rate g for long periods.

> "The entrepreneur inevitably tends to become a rentier, more and more
> dominant over those who own nothing but their labor. Once constituted,
> capital reproduces itself faster than output increases. The past
> devours the future."

- Consequences for wealth distribution are "potentially terrifying" —
  especially since return on capital **varies directly with size of
  initial stake**.
- Growth cannot solve this: education/knowledge/green investment cannot
  raise long-run growth above **1–1.5%** for countries at the
  technological frontier.
- 20th century appeared to refute r > g — but it took **two world wars**
  to reduce the return on capital and create that illusion.
- **The right solution**: **progressive annual tax on capital**.
  - Proposed schedule: 0.1–0.5% on fortunes under €1M; 1% on €1–5M;
    2% on €5–10M; 5–10% on fortunes of hundreds of millions or billions.
  - Would contain unlimited wealth growth without eliminating
    entrepreneurial incentives.

### Progressive Tax Requires International Cooperation

- The progressive capital tax solution is **not within reach of nation-
  states alone** — requires high-level international cooperation and
  regional political integration.
- Pure and perfect competition cannot alter r > g, which is **not a
  consequence of market "imperfection"** — it's structural.
- The nation-state remains the right level for social/fiscal modernization.
- Only **regional political integration** can effectively regulate
  globalized patrimonial capitalism of the 21st century.

> "If we are to regain control of capitalism, we must bet everything on
> democracy — and in Europe, democracy on a European scale."

## For a Political and Historical Economics

### Piketty's Methodological Manifesto

- Economics should be understood as a **subdiscipline of the social
  sciences**, alongside history, sociology, anthropology, and political
  science.
- Rejects the term **"economic science"** as "terribly arrogant."
- Prefers **"political economy"** — conveys the political, normative, and
  moral purpose that distinguishes economics from other social sciences.
- Political economy's original goal: study the ideal role of the state
  in economic/social organization.

**Critique of modern economics**:
- Economists have defined themselves by supposedly scientific methods
  relying on **immoderate use of mathematical models** — "often no more
  than an excuse for masking vacuity of content."
- Too much energy on **pure theoretical speculation** without specifying
  what economic facts one is actually trying to explain.
- Controlled experiments (new enthusiasm): useful in moderation, but
  succumb to a "**scientistic illusion**" — can prove a pure causal
  relation while forgetting the question is of limited interest.
- These new methods lead to **neglect of history** — historical experience
  remains "our principal source of knowledge."

> "The imperfect lessons that we can draw from history … are of
> inestimable, irreplaceable value, and no controlled experiment will
> ever be able to equal them."

- Economists must be **more pragmatic in methodological choices**, use
  whatever tools are available, work more closely with other social
  sciences.

### The Role of Social Scientists in Public Life

- Social scientists, like all citizens, **ought to participate in public
  debate** — cannot content themselves with invoking abstract principles
  of justice, democracy, and world peace.
- The world is NOT divided between a political elite and an army of
  commentators who only vote every four or five years.

### The Furet Case Study

- **François Furet** began as a promising historian studying class incomes
  — rigorous, data-driven, empirical. Later became famous for cultural/
  political history of the French Revolution, waged battle against dominant
  Marxist historians. In that battle, Furet **turned against economic and
  social history of any kind** — a pity, Piketty argues.

### The Stakes: Numbers and Power

- **Bipolar confrontations of 1917–1989** sterilized research on capital
  and inequality — Cold War ideological battles left their stamp on
  historical methods.
- All social scientists, journalists, activists, and citizens should take
  a serious interest in **money, its measurement, and its history**.
- "Those who have a lot of it never fail to defend their interests."

> "Refusing to deal with numbers rarely serves the interests of the
> least well-off."

## Selected Notes and Endnotes: Key Substantive Clarifications

### Chapter 10 Notes

- **"Mortality multiplier" method**: reweights each observation by inverse
  of mortality rate in age cohort — developed by Mallet, Séaillès, Strutt,
  Stamp (1900–1910); validated against Nordic wealth tax data.
- **US top centile ownership ~1800**: some estimates below 15% of total
  national wealth — but these focus only on free individuals (controversial).
- **Pareto "inverted coefficients"**: range 1.5–3.5; 1.5 = low inequality;
  3.5 = very high (top 0.1% earns 5× the top 1%).
- **National solidarity tax (France, August 15, 1945)**: exceptional levy
  on all wealth as of June 4, 1945; rates up to 20% for largest fortunes;
  plus exceptional levy on all nominal wealth increases 1940–1945 at up
  to 100%.
- **Infinite horizon model**: equilibrium r = θ + γ×g (θ = time
  preference, γ = concavity of utility function; typically γ ∈ [1.5, 2.5]).

### Chapter 11 Notes

- **Inheritance flow → wage income ratio**: ~3× larger in 18th–19th
  centuries and 21st century (inheritance ≈ ¼ of resources); ~10× larger
  in the 20th century (inheritance ≈ 1/10).
- **Albert de Foville, Clément Colson**: showed national wealth ≈ 30×
  annual inheritance flow ("estate multiplier").
- **Napoleon's *majorat* (1807)**: allowed certain landed estates linked
  to titles of nobility to pass to eldest males; definitively abolished
  1848.
- **Women and Civil Code**: formally equal rights in dividing estates,
  but wife could not freely manage bank accounts or sell property — until
  1970s.
- **Arthur Young** (October 1788, *Travels in France*): living standards
  comparable across Europe at 15,000–20,000 livres/year (30–50× average
  income) — 6 men-servants, 5 maids, 8 horses.
- **Revolutionary pay scale attempt (1792)**: tried to limit ratio to
  8:1; finally adopted in 1948 but quickly circumvented by opaque bonuses
  for highest civil servants.
- **Tax asymmetry**: labor income taxed ~30% on average; average effective
  tax rate on inheritances <5% — even though inheritance gives rise to
  same rights as labor income regarding publicly financed transfers.
- **Durkheim's theory** (1893): predicted end of inheritance as a
  political theory; prediction no more accurate than others; wars of
  20th century may have merely postponed the problem to the 21st century.
- **British suffrage history**: <2% of British population could vote
  until 1831; under Restoration, <1% of adult males had right to vote
  in France (90,000 of 10 million).

### Chapter 12 Notes

- **Global GDP** (2012–2013): ~$85 trillion (PPP); total private wealth
  ~$340 trillion (~4× GDP).
- **Top twenty-millionth fractile** (1,400 billionaires): if divergence
  rate observed 1987–2013 continues, their share rises from 1.5% of
  global wealth (2013) → 7.2% (2050) → **59.6% (2100)** — a staggering
  extrapolation.
- **Billionaire consumption is negligible**: with 10B euros capital at
  5% return, only 0.1% needed for 10M/year consumption; at 10% return,
  99% can be reinvested — wealth grows regardless of lifestyle.
- **University endowments**: total US university capital = ~3% of GDP;
  annual income = ~0.2% of GDP (>10% of total higher education
  expenditure). For most-endowed schools, endowment income = 30–40%
  of resources.
- **Harvard endowment detail**: averaged ~10% real return 1990–2010;
  new gifts added ~2%/year; total real income = 12% of endowment. Of
  that, 5% used for expenses, 7% reinvested → grew from $5B (1990) to
  nearly $30B (2010), while spending 2.5× what it received in gifts.
- **Gabriel Zucman** (Swiss bank data): Europe and US are net debtors
  only because offshore assets are unrecorded; if counted, Europe's net
  position turns strongly positive.

### Chapter 13 Notes

- **US carceral state substitutes for welfare state** for childless
  individuals: ~1% of adult US population imprisoned (2013), highest
  in world. For adult black males, rate >5%.
- **US health inefficiency**: adding private insurance, US spends nearly
  20% of NI on healthcare vs. 10–12% in Europe — more expensive, with
  worse coverage and health outcomes.
- **Intergenerational mobility**: correlation coefficients of earned income
  range 0.2–0.3 (Sweden/Finland) to 0.5–0.6 (US); Britain 0.4–0.5;
  Germany/France ~0.4.
- **Harvard undergraduate cost (2012–13)**: $54,000/year including room
  and board; tuition alone $38,000.
- **British university tuition trajectory**: £1,000 (1998) → £3,000
  (2004) → £9,000 (2012).
- **Sciences Po (Paris) tuition**: ranges from zero (lowest income) to
  10,000 euros/year (income >200,000 euros).

### Chapter 14 Notes

- **Progressive income tax originally targeted capital income**: everyone
  knew capital dominated the income hierarchy; exemptions for capital
  income would have seemed absurd to early 20th-century reformers.
- **Joseph Caillaux** (French finance minister, upon seeing estate tax
  data): "I have been led to believe and to say that France was a country
  of small fortunes.... The statistics … force us to retreat from this
  position." (*L'impôt sur le revenu*, 1910)
- **Record tax rates**: Britain's 98% top rate in force 1941–52 and
  again 1974–78; capital income also taxed at 98% (labor income 83%).
- **George McGovern** (1972 US presidential campaign): proposed 100% top
  inheritance tax rate. His crushing defeat by Nixon "marked the beginning
  of the end of the United States' enthusiasm for redistribution."
- **No evidence** that executives 1950–1980 were compensated in kind to
  make up for high top rates — all evidence suggests such benefits have
  *increased* since 1980 (undermining the argument that high taxes led
  to hidden compensation).
- **Optimal top income tax rate** (Piketty, Saez, Stantcheva): no
  statistically significant relationship between decreasing top marginal
  rate and GDP growth rate across 18 OECD countries. Elasticity of labor
  supply can be ruled out at >0.1–0.2, justifying high optimal marginal
  rates (~82%).
- **Xavier Gabaix and Augustin Landier** argued skyrocketing CEO pay is
  mechanical consequence of increased firm size; Piketty's rebuttal:
  company size increased similarly everywhere but pay only spiked in
  Anglo-Saxon countries.
- **Progressive tax serves two distinct functions**:
  (1) Confiscatory rates (80–90%) on top 0.5–1% to end "indecent and
      useless compensation."
  (2) High but non-confiscatory rates (50–60%) on top 5–10% to raise
      revenue for social state.

### Chapter 15 Notes

- **Bettencourt political scandal**: family trust managed by wife of budget
  minister, whose party received large donations from Bettencourt and had
  reduced the wealth tax by two-thirds while in power.
- **Optimal capital tax formula**: t = 1 − G/R, where G = generational
  growth rate, R = generational return on capital. Approaches 100% when
  growth → 0; approaches 0% when growth → return on capital.
- **Thomas Paine**, *Agrarian Justice* (1795): proposed 10% inheritance
  tax on "unaccumulated" estates.
- **Swedish capital tax history**: created 1947, suspended 1997, abolished
  2005 — "a good example of the growing inability of smaller countries
  to maintain an independent fiscal policy."
- **European proposed capital tax revenue**: ~2.5% of European adults
  own >€1M; 0.2% own >€5M. Annual revenue at proposed rates ~€300B on
  €15T GDP. Top centile owns ~25% of total wealth = ~125% of European GDP.

### Chapter 16 Notes

- **Germany's WWII debt cancellation**: Allies cancelled much of Germany's
  debt after WWII. German historian **Albrecht Ritschl** calculates these
  amounts would be substantial if recapitalized — includes occupation fees
  levied on Greece. Complicates Germany's current insistence on pure
  austerity logic for others.
- **1920s savings wiped out**: stock market crash destroyed interwar
  savings; inflation 1945–48 delivered additional shock → "old-age
  minimum" created 1956, PAYGO pension system created 1945.
- **Italian debt burden (1970–2010)**: interest payments on Italian debt
  averaged **7% of GDP** — "astronomical."
- **China's People's Assembly wealth**: average net worth of 70 richest
  members >$1B (Hurun Report 2012); US House of Representatives members
  average only $15M.
- **One of first acts of French Revolutionary assemblies (1789–90)**:
  compiled a "compendium of pensions" listing by name 23,000 individuals
  receiving royal payments — a **1,600-page book** published April 1790.
  Demonstrates financial transparency is politically achievable.

### Conclusion Notes

- **Note 1**: An increase in growth rate g would also likely raise r, so
  the gap r − g would not necessarily shrink — growth alone cannot solve
  the inequality problem mechanically.
- **Note 2**: Jean-Paul Sartre, Louis Althusser, Alain Badiou — for these
  philosophers, capital and class inequality seem of "only moderate
  interest" and serve "mainly as a pretext for jousts of a different
  nature entirely."
