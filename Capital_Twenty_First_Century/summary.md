# Capital in the Twenty-First Century
**Thomas Piketty** | Harvard University Press | 2014 | 723 pages

## Overview

A landmark work of economic history that assembles wealth and income data
across 20+ countries and three centuries to argue that capitalism, left
to itself, generates structural inequality that democracies may be unable
to manage. The central thesis: when the rate of return on capital (r)
consistently exceeds the rate of economic growth (g), inherited wealth
accumulates faster than output, pushing society toward the concentrated
patrimonial hierarchies of the 19th century.

## Core Argument

Three fundamental laws organize the book:

1. **α = r × β** — Capital's share of national income equals the rate of
   return multiplied by the capital/income ratio. An accounting identity.

2. **β = s / g** — In the long run, the capital/income ratio equals the
   savings rate divided by the growth rate. A country that saves a lot
   and grows slowly accumulates an enormous stock of capital.

3. **r > g** — The rate of return on capital has historically and
   persistently exceeded economic growth. This is not a market failure
   or an accident: it is the structural condition of capitalism. The
   20th-century exception (when wars and policy temporarily reversed this)
   is ending.

## The Historical Argument

The book traces wealth and income from the 18th century to today across
Britain, France, Germany, the US, Sweden, and other countries. Before
WWI, top deciles in Europe owned 85–90% of all wealth; top 1% owned
50–60%. Wars, inflation, nationalizations, and progressive taxation
compressed inequality dramatically between 1914 and 1970. Since 1980,
concentration has been rising again — in the US especially, driven by
"supermanager" salaries; globally, driven by the return of capital.

## The Solution

Piketty proposes a progressive annual global tax on capital — 0% below
€1M, 1% on €1–5M, 2% above €5M, potentially 5–10% on billionaires.
He acknowledges this is utopian in the near term but argues it is the
only alternative to a world of rentiers more extreme than the Belle
Époque, or a reversion to violent disruption and nationalist protectionism.

## Why It Matters

Published in 2013 (English translation 2014), the book sparked a global
conversation about inequality not seen since Marx. Its distinctive
contribution is empirical, not ideological: 15 years of archival research
producing the World Top Incomes Database. Piketty does not predict
revolution; he describes a mechanism, shows the historical evidence, and
proposes institutional responses. Whether those responses are politically
achievable is a separate question — and one the book ultimately cannot
answer.
