## Introduction

### Central Questions

**Thomas Piketty** frames the core inquiry: Does private capital accumulation
inevitably concentrate wealth, as **Karl Marx** believed, or does growth lead
to reduced inequality, as **Simon Kuznets** theorized?

- Examines wealth and income over three centuries across 20+ countries
- Modern growth avoided the Marxist apocalypse but has NOT resolved deep
  structural inequalities of capitalism
- When **r (return on capital) > g (growth rate)**, capitalism automatically
  generates arbitrary, unsustainable inequality

> "Social distinctions can be based only on common utility."
> —Declaration of the Rights of Man and the Citizen, Article 1, 1789

### The Problem: Debate Without Data

Historical wealth distribution debates long lacked rigorous data.
- **Literature captured reality better than statistics**: **Jane Austen** and
  **Honoré de Balzac** (1790–1830) depicted wealth hierarchy with vivid
  accuracy — marital strategies, power relations, personal hopes
- Role of research: define sources precisely, unmask fraudulent notions,
  focus attention on the right questions, inform democratic debate

### Three Historical Approaches

#### Malthus
**Thomas Malthus** (*Essay on the Principle of Population*, 1798): feared
**overpopulation** as primary threat. France was Europe's most populous country:
20 million (1700) → 30 million (1780). **Arthur Young** traveled France
(1787–1788) and documented rural poverty but was tainted by nationalist
prejudice. Malthus concluded welfare assistance must be halted to prevent
overpopulation chaos.

#### Ricardo
**David Ricardo** (*Principles of Political Economy*, 1817): focused on
**long-term land prices and rents**. As population grows, land becomes
**increasingly scarce** → landlords claim growing share → social equilibrium
destabilizes. Solution: **steadily increasing tax on land rents**.
- The **scarcity principle**: certain relative prices (urban real estate, oil)
  could rise so steeply over decades that they destabilize societies

#### Marx
By 1867 (*Capital*), industrial capitalism had transformed the question.
Industrial workers lived in urban misery; wages stagnated while profits soared.
Marx's **principle of infinite accumulation**: industrial capital has no natural
limit; can accumulate indefinitely in fewer hands. Two apocalyptic outcomes:
1. Rate of return falls so low accumulation ceases → violent capitalist conflict
2. Capital's income share rises indefinitely → proletarian revolt

Marx published *Communist Manifesto* (1848) before researching the data.
He neglected technological progress and wage increases. Communist revolution
occurred in backward Russia (not advanced countries), undermining his theory.
Yet his analysis of capital's internal contradictions was profound.

#### Kuznets and the Optimist Turn
**Simon Kuznets** (1950s): **income inequality automatically decreases in
advanced capitalism** until reaching acceptable levels. "Inverted U" curve:
rises in early industrialization, then naturally falls. Relied on the first
historical income distribution statistics (1953). "Growth is a rising tide
that lifts all boats" captured postwar optimism (**Trente Glorieuses**).

### Piketty's Core Argument

Modern growth and knowledge diffusion **avoided the Marxist apocalypse** but
have **NOT modified deep structures of capital and inequality**.

- When **r > g** persistently, inherited wealth grows faster than output and
  income; people with inherited wealth need save only a fraction of capital
  income to see wealth grow faster than the economy
- In the 19th century and likely the 21st, r > g; the 20th century (1900–1980)
  saw exceptional conditions where wars and policy forced r ≈ g
- Solutions exist: **progressive taxation, global capital taxation, economic
  openness** — but require democratic will

### Kuznets's Foundational Work (and Its Limits)

- **Kuznets**, *Shares of Upper Income Groups in Income and Savings* (1953):
  first systematic inequality measurement; US only, 1913–1948
- Data sources: **US federal income tax returns** (post-1913) and Kuznets's
  national income estimates
- Key contributors to early national accounts: **John W. Kendrick** (US),
  **Arthur Bowley** and **Colin Clark** (Britain), **L. Dugé de Bernonville** (France)
- Progressive income tax adoption: 1913 (US), 1914 (France), 1909 (Britain),
  1922 (India), 1932 (Argentina)
- What Kuznets found: top decile held **45–50% of US income** in early
  1910s–1920s; fell to **30–35%** by late 1940s — ~10 pp drop equal to half
  the income of the entire bottom 50%
- Kuznets privately knew the compression was largely **accidental** — Great
  Depression and WWII shocks; his 1953 book warned against hasty generalizations
- But at the **December 1954 AEA presidential address**, he offered an
  optimistic interpretation (published 1955 as "Economic Growth and Income
  Inequality") — the **Kuznets curve** — partly for Cold War ideological reasons
- Piketty's critique: the inequality compression 1914–1945 was due to wars
  and their violent shocks, not any "tranquil process of intersectoral mobility"
- Since the 1970s, income inequality has risen sharply in rich countries,
  especially the US — by 2010, concentration regained/exceeded 1920s levels

### Sources

**Income inequality data** — tax records extended globally beyond Kuznets's US series.
Building the database: Piketty extended to France (2001); joined with
**Anthony Atkinson** and **Emmanuel Saez** to expand globally; **Facundo Alvaredo**
(Argentina, Spain, Portugal), **Fabien Dell** (Germany, Switzerland),
**Abhijit Banerjee** + Piketty (India), **Nancy Qian** (China).
Result: **World Top Incomes Database (WTID)** — ~30 researchers, primary source
for this book; series generally begin when income tax was established (1910–1920
in most countries; 1880s in Japan and Germany).

**Wealth data — three complementary approaches:**
1. **Estate tax returns** — French data with **Gilles Postel-Vinay** and
   **Jean-Laurent Rosenthal**: continuous series since the Revolution;
   **Jesper Roine** and **Daniel Waldenström** (Sweden);
   **Robert Lampman** (1962, US); **Atkinson** and **Alan Harrison** (1978, Britain)
2. **Inheritance and savings data** — relative importance of inherited wealth
   vs. lifetime savings; most complete for France
3. **National wealth / capital-income ratio** — work with **Gabriel Zucman**;
   extends **Raymond Goldsmith**'s 1970s national balance sheets;
   France and Britain data push back to early 18th century

### Key Forces

**Forces of convergence:** diffusion of knowledge and investment in skills —
the primary mechanism. China's growth is the current exemplar: catching up via
technology and skill diffusion.

**"Rising human capital hypothesis"**: optimistic view that technology
increasingly rewards skill over capital → Piketty calls this largely illusory.
**Life-cycle savings hypothesis**: wealth accumulation is about retirement, not
dynasty → also largely illusory; inequalities within age cohorts remain primary.

**Two distinct divergence processes (both show U-shaped curves):**

**Process 1 — Income inequality (Figure I.1, US 1910–2010):**
- Top decile: 45–50% in 1910s–1920s → ~30–35% by 1950 → back to 45–50%
  by 2000–2010
- Driven by explosion of very high labor incomes ("supermanager" pay)
- Most pronounced in US and Britain; less so in Japan, Germany, France

**Process 2 — Capital/income ratio (Figure I.2, Europe 1870–2010):**
- Private wealth in Europe 1870–1910: **6–7 years of national income**
- Fell sharply 1914–1945: dropped to **2–3 years**
- Rising steadily from 1950; approaching **5–6 years** again by early 21st century

### The Fundamental Inequality: r > g

- **r** = average annual return on capital (profits, dividends, interest, rents,
  capital gains, as % of total capital value)
- **g** = rate of growth of the economy (annual increase in income/output)
- Additional amplifiers: savings rates rise with wealth; larger endowments get
  higher effective returns; Ricardian scarcity reinforces divergence
- Counterintuitively: the more perfect the capital market, the more likely r > g
- Proposed remedy: **progressive global tax on capital**

### Geographical Scope

Primary focus: **US, Japan, Germany, France, Great Britain**.
France receives special attention: richest estate records in the world;
first to experience demographic transition; the French Revolution was the
most ambitious equality experiment (abolished all legal privileges).

**Why France is structurally different from the US:**
- France's population 1789–today: ~30M → ~60M (2×); US: 3M → 300M (100×)
- In high-population-growth countries, inherited wealth matters less;
  US exceptional demographics are not generalizable

### Book Structure

- **Part One**: Income and Capital — definitions and growth
- **Part Two**: Dynamics of Capital/Income Ratio — historical trends
- **Part Three**: Structure of Inequality — labor vs. capital, wealth concentration
- **Part Four**: Regulating Capital in the 21st Century — policy solutions

### Key Formulas (Introduced Here, Used Throughout)

- **α = r × β** (capital's share = rate of return × capital/income ratio)
- **β = s / g** (capital/income ratio = savings rate ÷ growth rate, long run)
- **r > g** (fundamental inequality driving wealth concentration)

## PART ONE: Income and Capital

## Chapter 1: Income and Output

### The Marikana Massacre as Opening Frame

**August 16, 2012**: South African police fired on striking workers at the
**Marikana platinum mine** (owned by **Lonmin, Inc.**, London stockholders)
near Johannesburg — 34 miners killed. Miners sought to double wages from
500 to 1,000 euros/month; company offered a monthly raise of 75 euros.
Historical parallels: **Haymarket Square, Chicago (May 1, 1886)** and
**Fourmies, northern France (May 1, 1891)** — police fired on workers
striking for higher wages.

Capital's share in capital-intensive sectors like mining can be as high as
one-half of total output. The intensity of conflict stems from the **extreme
concentration of capital ownership** — capital inequality is always much
greater than labor income inequality.

### The Capital-Labor Split in the Long Run

- **Conventional textbook view**: labor's share ≈ two-thirds, capital's share
  ≈ one-third of national income — treated as stable
- **Piketty's revision**: stability is a myth; shocks of 1914–1945 (WWI,
  **Bolshevik Revolution of 1917**, Great Depression, WWII, rent control,
  nationalizations, capital controls) pushed capital's share to historically
  low levels in the 1950s
- Capital reconstituted after **Margaret Thatcher** (1979) and **Ronald Reagan**
  (1980); financial globalization and deregulation in the 1990s further shifted
  the balance
- By 2010, capital was prospering as it had not done since 1913

Capital's form has changed radically: from land and real estate (18th century)
→ industrial and financial capital (21st century). Current importance of capital
in wealthy countries is primarily due to: (1) slowing demographic and
productivity growth, (2) political regimes that favor private capital.

### National Income

**National income** = sum of all income available to residents in a given year,
regardless of legal classification.

Two differences from GDP:
1. Subtract **capital depreciation** (~10% of GDP in most countries) → "net
   domestic product" ≈ 90% of GDP
2. Add (or subtract) **net income from abroad** (profits, dividends, interest,
   rents flowing in vs. out)

Formula: **National income = domestic output + net income from abroad**

In most major economies today (France, US, Germany, UK, China, Brazil, Japan,
Italy), national income is within **1–2% of domestic product**.

**Key point**: capital inequality is primarily a *domestic* issue, not an
international one. Common fears about France being "owned by California pension
funds or the Bank of China" are fantasy.

Historical exception: colonial era. On eve of WWI, **Britain's national income
was ~10% above its domestic product; France's gap exceeded 5%**.
In **1913**, European powers owned an estimated **1/3 to 1/2 of the domestic
capital of Asia and Africa** and **>3/4 of their industrial capital**.

### What Is Capital?

**Piketty's definition**: sum total of **nonhuman assets** that can be owned
and exchanged on some market. Excludes "human capital" (skills, training,
abilities) because it cannot be permanently owned by another person in modern
legal systems.

Exception: **slave societies** where slaveholders fully owned another person's
human capital, including offspring.

Capital includes:
- All forms of **real property** (including residential real estate)
- **Financial and professional capital**: plants, infrastructure, machinery, patents
- **"Immaterial" capital** such as patents (captured via stock market valuations)
- Owned by private individuals (**private capital**) or government (**public capital**)

Piketty uses **"capital" and "wealth" interchangeably**.
Includes **residential real estate** as capital (yields "housing services"
measured by rental equivalent); residential and productive capital each account
for roughly **half the capital stock** in developed countries.

> "Capital is not an immutable concept: it reflects the state of development
> and prevailing social relations of each society." — Piketty

### National Wealth

**National wealth** = total market value of everything owned by residents and
government, tradeable on some market.
- Nonfinancial assets: land, dwellings, buildings, machinery, infrastructure, patents
- Financial assets: bank accounts, bonds, stocks, pension funds
- Minus financial liabilities

**National wealth = private wealth + public wealth**
Public wealth in most developed countries is currently **insignificant or even
negative** (public debt exceeds public assets).

**National wealth = domestic capital + net foreign capital**

### The Capital/Income Ratio (β)

- **Income** = a *flow* — quantity of goods produced in a given period
- **Capital** = a *stock* — total wealth owned at a given point in time
- **β** = total capital stock divided by annual national income

In developed countries today, β generally varies between **5 and 6**:
- France, Britain, Germany, Italy, US, Japan: national income ~€30,000–35,000
  per capita; private wealth ~€150,000–200,000 per capita (5–6× income)
- β > 6 in Japan and Italy; β < 5 in US and Germany

Capital stock splits into **two roughly equal halves**: residential capital and
professional capital.

Typical citizen (2010): earned ~€30,000/year, owned ~€180,000 of capital —
~€90,000 in a dwelling, ~€90,000 in stocks/bonds/savings.

### The First Fundamental Law of Capitalism: α = r × β

- **α** = capital's share of national income
- **r** = rate of return on capital
- **β** = capital/income ratio
- Formula: **α = r × β** (pure accounting identity, all societies, all periods)

Example: β = 600%, r = 5% → α = 30%
- €30,000/yr per capita income → €21,000 labor income (70%) + €9,000 capital income (30%)
- €9,000 / €180,000 capital = 5% average return

**Rates of return by asset type:**
- Stocks: 7–8% average long-run return
- Real estate and bonds: 3–4%
- Stock-market cap of listed companies: 12–15× annual profits → 6–8% annual return

In 19th-century novels of Austen and Balzac, land yielded ~5% (capital worth ~20
years' rent). Paris apartment worth €1M rents for ~€2,500/month = 3% return;
small apartment (€100K) → ~€400/month = 5%.

α = r × β is an **accounting identity** — does not explain how β is determined;
that requires savings rates, investment rates, and growth rates
(→ **Second Fundamental Law**, Chapter 5).

### National Accounting

National accounts are a **social construct** that reflects the preoccupations
of the era when they were built — not mathematical certainties.

**History:**
- Late 17th–18th century: first estimates. Britain: **William Petty** (1664),
  **Gregory King** (1696). France: **Pierre le Pesant de Boisguillebert** (1695),
  **Sébastien de Vauban** (1707) — *Projet de dîme royale* (Royal Tithe plan).
  **Antoine Lavoisier**: *La Richesse territoriale du Royaume de France* (1791)
- 19th century: **Robert Giffen** (1870–1900) regularly updated British national
  capital estimates; **Alfred de Foville** and **Clément Colson** (France)
- Interwar: first annual income series: Kuznets and **Kendrick** (US),
  **Bowley** and **Clark** (Britain), **Dugé de Bernonville** (France)
- Post-WWII shift: focus moved from wealth to income/output — Great Depression
  trauma, governments needed annual output data
- 1990s–2000s revival: financial capitalism of 21st century couldn't be analyzed
  with 1950s tools; central banks compiled annual balance sheet data

### The Global Distribution of Production

- 1900–1980: **70–80% of global output** concentrated in Europe and America
- By 2010: Europe–America share fell to ~50% — back to 1860 level
- Europe peaked at ~47% of world GDP in **1913**; down to 25% by 2012
- America peaked in the **1950s** at ~40% of world output
- **Convergence** underway: Industrial Revolution allowed Europe/America to claim
  output share 2–3× their population share; that divergence phase is now over

**Table 1.1 (2012 data, PPP):**
- World: 7B people, ~€70 trillion output, ~€10,000 per capita GDP (~€760/month)
- EU (540M): >€27,000/year per capita (~€2,040/month); Western Europe core: ~€31,000/yr
- US-Canada (350M): ~€40,000/year (~€3,050/month)
- Latin America (600M): ~€10,000/year (~€780/month) — exactly world average
- China (1,260M): ~€8,000/year (~€580/month)
- India (1,350M): ~€3,200/year (~€240/month)
- Sub-Saharan Africa (900M): ~€2,000/year (~€150/month); total output less than France

**PPP vs. exchange rates:** China correction coefficient: 1.6×; India: 2.5×
Rich countries' share of global income: **46%** using PPP vs. **57%** using
market exchange rates (2012).

### Foreign Ownership

Africa's **national income** is ~5% less than its domestic output (up to 10%
lower in individual countries). With capital's share ~30%, this implies ~20%
of African capital is foreign-owned; manufacturing capital may exceed 40–50%.

Classical theory of convergence via capital flows has **two major defects:**
1. Equalization produces convergence of per capita *output*, but NOT necessarily
   per capita *income* — rich countries may continue to own the capital
   indefinitely (the Africa trap)
2. Japan, South Korea, Taiwan, and China all financed development through
   **domestic savings** — not foreign investment

True driver of convergence: **diffusion of knowledge**. Open trade accelerates
knowledge transfer; gains come mainly from productivity gains via competition,
not static specialization gains.

## Chapter 2: Growth: Illusions and Realities

**Core argument**: Growth has always been relatively slow except in exceptional
periods; the 21st century may return to a low-growth regime. This matters
enormously for inequality because slow growth amplifies the power of inherited
wealth (r > g).

Total growth = **population growth** + **per capita output growth** — only the
latter improves living standards.

### Growth over the Very Long Run

**Table 2.1 — World growth since the Industrial Revolution:**
| Period | World output | Population | Per capita output |
|--------|-------------|------------|-------------------|
| 0–1700 | 0.1% | 0.1% | 0.0% |
| 1700–2012 | 1.6% | 0.8% | 0.8% |
| 1700–1820 | 0.5% | 0.4% | 0.1% |
| 1820–1913 | 1.5% | 0.6% | 0.9% |
| 1913–2012 | 3.0% | 1.4% | 1.6% |

Pre-industrial near-zero rate is mathematically constrained: any higher rate
would imply impossibly tiny population at year 0 (fewer than 10,000 people at 0.8%/yr).

### The Law of Cumulative Growth

0.8%/yr population growth from 1700–2012 = **tenfold increase** (600M → 7B).
**Table 2.2 (key examples):**
- 1%/yr → ×1.35 after 30 yrs, ×3 after 100 yrs, ×20 after 300 yrs
- 2%/yr → ×1.81 after 30 yrs, ×7.2 after 100 yrs
- Growth rates above 1–1.5%/yr **cannot be sustained indefinitely** without
  generating vertiginous population increases

The **law of cumulative returns** is the mirror image: a few percent annual
return on capital, compounded over decades, produces very large capital
accumulation. An apparently small gap between r and g has powerful destabilizing
effects on inequality over the long run.

**Table 2.3 — Demographic growth:**
| Period | World | Europe | America | Africa | Asia |
|--------|-------|--------|---------|--------|------|
| 0–1700 | 0.1% | 0.1% | 0.0% | 0.1% | 0.1% |
| 1700–2012 | 0.8% | 0.6% | 1.4% | 0.9% | 0.8% |
| 1913–2012 | 1.4% | 0.4% | 1.7% | 2.2% | 1.5% |
| Proj. 2012–2050 | 0.7% | −0.1% | 0.6% | 1.9% | 0.5% |
| Proj. 2050–2100 | 0.2% | −0.1% | 0.0% | 1.0% | −0.2% |

Global demographic peak: 1.9% in 1950–1970; still 1.8% in 1970–1990; 1.3%
in 1990–2012. All future growth (2050–2100) driven entirely by **Africa**.
Germany, Italy, Spain, Poland: fertility below 1.5 children/woman in the 2000s.
**China's one-child policy** (1970s): India set to surpass China as most
populous by ~2020.

### Growth as a Factor for Equalization

**Mechanism 1 — Dilution of inherited wealth:**
Strong demographic growth dilutes inherited wealth because family wealth gets
divided among more heirs each generation. Stagnant/declining population
increases the weight of inherited wealth.

**Mechanism 2 — Social mobility:**
Even low growth (0.5–1.5%/yr) creates new professions each generation,
allowing people outside the elite to rise. **Charles Dunoyer** (1845,
*De la liberté du travail*): "one consequence of the industrial regime is
to destroy artificial inequalities, but this only highlights natural
inequalities all the more clearly" — used to oppose all labor law.

**Key implication**: with low growth, r > g almost certainly holds, making
capital-dominated, inheritance-driven societies highly probable.

### The Scale of Long-Run Growth

- Western Europe, North America, Japan: per capita income rose from ~€100/month
  in 1700 to ~€2,500/month in 2012 — a **twentyfold increase**
- Per capita income in Europe ~€400/month around 1900; ~€2,500 by 2010

**What does a tenfold increase in purchasing power mean?**
Three broad sectors:
- **Industrial goods**: productivity grew faster → relative prices fell
- **Foodstuffs**: productivity grew more slowly → tracked average inflation
- **Services**: productivity growth low or zero → service prices rose faster
  (barbers as canonical example)

**Bicycle example (France)**: cheapest model cost **6 months' wages in the 1880s**.
By 1910: one month's wages. By 1960s: less than one week's wages. Overall
purchasing power in bicycles rose **40x between 1890 and 1970**.

**Table 2.4 — Employment by sector, France and US, 1800–2012:**
- France 1800: Agriculture 64%, Manufacturing 22%, Services 14%
- France 2012: Agriculture 3%, Manufacturing 21%, Services 76%
- US 1800: Agriculture 68%, Manufacturing 18%, Services 13%
- US 2012: Agriculture 2%, Manufacturing 18%, Services 80%

Health and education represent the most tangible improvement in living
standards: life expectancy from ~40 to ~80; near-universal literacy vs.
near-universal illiteracy.

### The End of Growth?

All past spectacular growth occurred at no more than **1–1.5% per year**.
Historical examples of 3–4%+ growth occurred only during **catch-up phases**,
which by definition are temporary and cannot happen globally.

- Recent advanced-country growth (1990–2012): Western Europe 1.6%, North
  America 1.4%, Japan 0.7%
- **Robert Gordon** (Northwestern): predicts US per capita growth below 0.5%
  between 2050 and 2100; recent innovation waves (IT) have lower productivity
  impact than steam or electricity
- Piketty's median scenario: **1.2% long-term per capita output growth** in
  wealthy countries

> "No historical example of a country at the world technological frontier whose
> growth in per capita output exceeded 1.5 percent over a lengthy period."

**1% per year → 35% cumulative growth per generation** (30 years); enough so
that a quarter to a third of all occupations did not exist 30 years earlier.

### The Postwar "Trente Glorieuses"

**Figure 2.3**: Western Europe grew >4% per year 1950–1970; Continental Europe
only (excluding Britain): average per capita growth ~**5%** 1950–1970 — "well
beyond that achieved in other advanced countries over the past two centuries."
North America maintained steady 1.5–2% throughout 1820–2012 (no equivalent
"golden age").

**Piketty's verdict**: France/Germany/Japan would have caught up regardless of
policy after their 1914–1945 collapse. US/UK economic liberalization "neither
increased growth nor decreased it."

### The Double Bell Curve of Global Growth

Global per capita output growth exceeded **2%** between 1950 and 1990:
European catch-up (1950–1990), then Asian/Chinese catch-up (1990–2012) with
China exceeding **9% per year** — "a level never before observed."

**Median scenario (Figure 2.4)**: rich countries grow 1.2%/year to 2100;
emerging economies attain 5%/year 2012–2030 and 4% 2030–2050; convergence
complete ~2050; global growth then falls to ~1.5%.

### Inflation: A 20th-Century Phenomenon

Before World War I, inflation was effectively **zero** in all countries with
long-run price series — at most **0.2–0.3%/year** 1700–1820 and 1820–1913
in France, Britain, US, Germany.

**French Revolution exception**: revolutionary paper currency (the **assignats**)
caused high inflation through 1794–95. Resolved by the **franc germinal**
(April 7, 1795) restoring metal coinage — 1-franc coin = exactly 4.5g fine
silver. Prices in francs 1800–1810 were essentially identical to prices in
livres tournois 1770–1780. **Balzac** moved freely between francs and livres
tournois; gold value of the franc set in 1803 remained officially unchanged
until **June 25, 1928**.

**WWI shattered this world permanently**: governments abandoned **gold
convertibility** in August 1914. Collapse timeline:
- Britain abandoned gold: **1931**
- United States: **1933**
- France: **1936**
- Post-WWII Bretton Woods gold standard: established 1946, ended **1971**

**Inflation data (1913–1950):**
- France: >13%/year (prices ×100 over period)
- Germany: 17%/year (prices ×300+)
- Britain and US: ~3%/year (prices ×3)

**Jane Austen** (early 1800s): average British income ~30 pounds/year —
same as in 1720 or 1770; "comfortable" life required 500–1,000 pounds/year
(20–30× average). Money was stable enough to function as a **social coordinate
system**. After 1914–1945, specific monetary references virtually disappeared
from novels. **Naguib Mahfouz** (Cairo, interwar) still used them; **Orhan Pamuk**
(Istanbul, 1970s) could not.

## PART TWO: The Dynamics of the Capital/Income Ratio

## Chapter 3: The Metamorphoses of Capital

### The Nature of Wealth: From Literature to Reality

In 19th-century novels, wealth had two primary forms: **agricultural land** and
**government bonds** (perpetual *rentes*).

- *Père Goriot*: Goriot made fortune in pasta manufacturing → converted to
  government bonds; Rastignac family owned farmland
- *Sense and Sensibility*: Norland estate = agricultural land; Elinor and
  Marianne left only interest from government bond portfolio
- *Mansfield Park*: **Sir Thomas** travels to West Indies to manage plantations
  — managing colonial assets thousands of miles away was genuinely difficult
- **César Birotteau** (Balzac): perfume entrepreneur who ruined himself
  speculating on Paris real estate (La Madeleine district, 1820s)

**Key contrarian point:** 19th-century "rentier" capital was not purely passive
— Père Goriot was first an entrepreneurial pasta maker who converted gains to
safer assets, exactly like a startup founder reinvesting in bonds.

> "Capital is never quiet: it is always risk-oriented and entrepreneurial, at
> least at its inception, yet it always tends to transform itself into rents as
> it accumulates in large enough amounts — that is its vocation, its logical
> destination." — Piketty

Government bonds are a claim of one group (interest recipients) on another
(taxpayers) — should be excluded from **national wealth**, included only in
**private wealth**.

### The U-Shaped Curve: Britain and France (Figures 3.1–3.2)

**Two major conclusions from the data:**

**1. U-shaped capital/income ratio:**
- 18th–19th centuries (through 1914): national capital stable at **6–7 years
  of national income** in both Britain and France
- WWI → Depression → WWII: ratio collapsed to **2–3 years** by 1950
- 1950–2010: steady climb back to **5–6 years**

> "Broadly speaking, it was the wars of the twentieth century that wiped away
> the past to create the illusion that capitalism had been structurally
> transformed." — Piketty

**2. Radical shift in capital composition:**
- 1700: **farmland** = 4–5 years of national income (~two-thirds of all capital)
- Today: farmland = <10% of national income, <2% of total wealth
- Rise of **housing**: from ~1 year of national income (18th c.) to >3 years today
- Rise of **other domestic capital** (business capital, infrastructure, machinery,
  financial assets): from ~1.5 years to ~3 years today
- Bottom line: **nature of capital transformed, total value unchanged**

Formula:
> National capital = farmland + housing + other domestic capital + net foreign capital

### The Rise and Fall of Foreign Capital

**Britain at peak (eve of WWI):**
- Foreign assets = nearly **2 years of national income** (6× total value of
  British farmland); yielded ~5%/yr → British national income was ~**10% higher**
  than domestic product

**France at peak:**
- Foreign assets = >1 year of national income → national income **5–6% higher**
  than domestic product — equal to total industrial output of northern and eastern
  *départements*

**Colonial trade dynamics (1880–1914):**
Both countries ran **structural trade deficits** (1–2% of national income) with
no problem because foreign asset income >5% of national income. The logic:
accumulating foreign assets so one could later *run* trade deficits — "the
advantage of owning things is that one can continue to consume without working."

**Collapse (1914–1950s):**
Two wars, Great Depression, and decolonization wiped out these holdings.
By 1950s: net foreign assets of France and Britain near **zero**.

**Specifics:**
- Foreign capital lost via: **Bolshevik repudiation** of Russian loans (1917);
  **Suez Canal nationalization** by Nasser (1956) — British/French shareholders
  had collected dividends since 1869

### Public Wealth, Private Wealth

**Current situation (Britain and France):**
- Total public assets ≈ 1 year of NI (Britain), ~1.5 years (France)
- Public debt ≈ 1 year of NI in both
- **Net public capital ≈ zero** in Britain; ~30% of NI in France

**Table 3.1** (France, 2012): National capital = 605% of national income
- Public capital (net): 31% — just 5% of national capital
- Private capital (net): 574% — **95% of national capital**

Over 300 years, net public wealth in Britain and France oscillated between
roughly +100% and −100% of national income — large swings but small compared
to private wealth of 700–800%.

### Great Britain: Public Debt

Britain hit ~**200% of GDP in public debt twice**: after the Napoleonic Wars
(~1815) and after World War II (~1950). Britain **never defaulted**.

**France's path**: monarchy couldn't modernize taxation → Revolution → new
tax system (1790–91) → **"banqueroute des deux tiers"** (1797): two-thirds
default on outstanding debt, compounded by assignats hyperinflation → debt
fell to <20% of NI by 1815.

**Britain's path**: chose to borrow without limit → debt rose to ~200% of NI
by 1810s — 10× France's debt at the same moment. Required a **century of
budget surpluses** (1815–1914) to reduce debt to <30% of NI.

British public debt in 1770–1810 was financed by a corresponding increase in
**private saving** — national capital stayed stable at ~7 years, but private
wealth rose to >8 years as net public capital went deeply negative.

> "It is no surprise that wealth is ubiquitous in Jane Austen's novels:
> traditional landlords were joined by unprecedented numbers of government
> bondholders." — Piketty

19th-century bonds were lucrative: inflation virtually zero (1815–1914),
yields 4–5% — **significantly higher than growth rate**.

### Why 19th-Century Socialists Distrusted Public Debt

**Marx** saw public debt as a tool of private capital — "not without perspicacity."
The **"émigrés' billion"** (1825): France borrowed to compensate aristocrats
who fled during the Revolution. **Marx's** *The Class Struggle in France* (1849–50):
attacked finance minister **Achille Fould** for raising drink taxes to pay rentiers.

Belle Époque (1880–1914): French public debt 70–80% of NI; government paid
2–3% of NI in interest annually (more than the education budget).

### The 20th Century: Debt as Redistribution Tool

New view emerged: debt as instrument to redistribute **to** the least well-off
via **inflation** — lenders bear the cost instead of taxpayers.

- France 1913–1950: inflation averaged **>13%/year** → prices ×100 → rentiers
  saw bond purchasing power fall to 1/100th by 1950
- **Proust** published *Swann's Way* in 1913 when bonds seemed indestructible;
  by 1950 those bondholders had "virtually nothing left"
- Britain (1913–1950): inflation ~3%/year (prices ×3); debt reached >200% of
  GDP by 1950; required 1950s–1970s inflation (4–15%/year) to bring to ~50% of GDP

**Two problems with inflation as redistribution:**
1. **Blunt targeting**: bank deposit holders (not only the rich) also lose
2. **Self-defeating**: once permanent, lenders demand higher nominal rates →
  redistributive effect disappears → stagflation

**David Ricardo** (1817): formulated **"Ricardian equivalence"** — under certain
conditions, public debt has no effect on national capital accumulation. **Robert
Barro** later proposed radical reinterpretation; Piketty's critique: Barro's
**representative agent models** assume everyone owns the same wealth, ignoring
that public debt is owned by a *minority* → debt repayment is always an internal
redistribution from taxpayers to bondholders.

### France: "Capitalism without Capitalists" (Postwar Period)

**Key nationalizations (1944–45)**: banking sector, coal mines, automobile industry.
**Louis Renault** arrested as collaborator (September 1944) → Renault factories
seized punitively → nationalized January 1945.

By 1950: French public assets >1 year of NI; net public wealth ~1 year of NI,
while total private wealth was barely 2 years.
- Government owned **25–30% of France's national capital** in 1950
- In industrial and financial sectors: state share exceeded **50%** from 1950 to 1980
- Piketty calls this **"capitalism without capitalists"** — this period coincides
  with the **Trente Glorieuses** — France's fastest growth ever

### The Privatization Wave (post-1980)

After 1980: privatization reversed nationalization globally.
- **France's paradox**: 1981 Socialist-Communist coalition won on platform of
  extending nationalizations; by 1986 a liberal majority launched privatizations;
  continued by socialist majority 1988–1993
  - Renault became joint-stock company (1990); France Telecom privatized (1997–98)
- Net public wealth fell to near zero; private wealth returned to pre-WWII levels

"France totally transformed its national capital structure at two different points
in time without really understanding why."

## Chapter 4: From Old Europe to the New World

### Germany

Germany's capital trajectory similar to Britain and France overall, with key differences:

**Far fewer foreign assets**: on eve of WWI, Germany had ~50% of NI in foreign
assets vs. ~100% for France and ~200% for Britain. Germany had **no colonial empire** —
a source of "powerful political and military tensions" (Moroccan crises of 1905, 1911;
contributing to 1914). Lenin's *Imperialism, the Highest Stage of Capitalism* (1916):
colonial competition → war.

Germany 2010: net foreign asset position ~50% of NI (half accumulated since 2000) —
almost same as 1913, but now via trade surpluses not colonialism.

**Germany's inflation and its legacy:**
Average inflation ~17%/year between 1930 and 1950 → prices ×300.
**Paradox**: Germany used inflation most violently yet emerged with the strongest
**antiinflationist** attitude — hyperinflation of the 1920s destabilized society so
severely that Germany refuses any price rise >2%/year. Britain, which always paid its
debts honestly, has a flexible attitude toward inflation.

**"Rhenish Capitalism":**
State of Lower Saxony owns >15% of shares (and 20% of voting rights, guaranteed by
law despite EU objections) of **Volkswagen**.

**Germany's private wealth paradox:**
German private wealth was exceptionally low in 1950 (~1.5 years of NI) but by 2010
rose to 4+ years — still below France/Britain (5–6 years) and Italy/Spain (6+ years).

Two explanations for Germany's lower wealth:
1. Low real estate prices — reunification flooded market with low-cost eastern housing;
   stricter rent control
2. **Low stock market valuation of German firms** (bigger factor) — if book value used
   instead of market value, German private wealth rises to French/British levels

The stock market gap reflects **"Rhenish capitalism" / "stakeholder model"**:
firms co-owned not just by shareholders but by workers (who sit on boards),
regional governments, consumers' associations, environmental groups. Implies
**lower market valuation but not lower social valuation**.

### Shocks to Capital in the Twentieth Century

Capital/income ratio collapsed **from 6.5–7 years of NI in 1913 to ~2.5 years in 1950**
— a drop of more than 4 years.

**Physical destruction explains only a fraction:**
- France: ~1 year of NI destroyed (1/5 to 1/4 of total decline)
- Germany: ~1.5 years (~1/3 of total decline)
- Britain: physical destruction <10% of NI, yet national capital fell by **4 years** —
  more than 40× the physical losses

**True causes (two-thirds to three-quarters of the drop):**
1. **Collapse of foreign portfolios**: British net foreign capital dropped from
   2 years of NI to slightly negative by the 1950s
2. **Very low savings rate** 1914–1945: wealthy Europeans sold capital to maintain
   living standards; Depression bankrupted stock/bondholders
3. Private savings absorbed by **enormous public deficits** during wars — savers
   lent to governments, then expropriated by inflation

**Low asset prices** (one-quarter to one-third of the drop): postwar rent control →
rents rose less than prices → real estate values fell; stock markets depressed by
nationalizations, new dividend/profit taxes.

> "Ultimately, the decline in the capital/income ratio between 1913 and 1950
> is the history of Europe's suicide, and in particular of the euthanasia
> of European capitalists." — Piketty

### Capital in America: More Stable Than in Europe

At US independence (1770–1810), national capital was **barely 3 years of national
income** — vs. ~7 years in Britain and France.
- Farmland = 1–1.5 years of NI; land was so abundant its market price was very low
- **Tocqueville (1840)**: "In America, land costs little, and anyone can easily
  become a landowner"

US capital/income ratio oscillated between **4 and 5 years from 1910 to 2010**.
Europe dropped from 7+ years to under 3 then rebounded to 5–6 — dramatic U-shape.
US "U-shaped curve" exists but is much smaller in amplitude.

FDR adopted rent control and progressive taxation further than Europe — but no
sweeping nationalizations (goal was reducing inequality, not eliminating private property).

American private wealth fell from ~5 years in 1930 to <3.5 years in 1970. Americans'
more stable capital experience may explain why they take a **more benign view of
capitalism than Europeans**.

### New World and Foreign Capital

US was never a colonial power; net foreign capital position was always of limited
importance. On eve of WWI: US domestic capital = 500% of NI; foreign-owned share =
~10% → net national wealth ~490% → US was **98% American-owned**.
"The United States owned itself."

US net foreign debt by early 2010s: domestic capital ~450%, net foreign debt ~20% →
net national wealth ~430% → US still **95%+ American-owned**.

**Canada**: in 1910, foreign-owned share = **120%** (mainly British, in natural resources).
Why different from US? **Political factors**: Canada remained a British colony —
no violent rupture, no expropriation. By 1950–1990, Canada's net foreign debt fell to
~10% of domestic capital; today ~98% Canadian-owned.

### The Importance of Slavery

**Thomas Jefferson** owned 600+ slaves; signed law ending slave imports after 1808 but
slave population grew by natural increase: ~400,000 in the 1770s → 1 million by 1800
→ 4 million by 1860 (tenfold in under a century).

In 1800: slaves = **~20% of total US population** (1M of 5M); in the South, 40%.
By 1860: 15% of total US population; in South still 40% (4M slaves, 6M whites).

**Slave capital as a fraction of wealth:**
- Market value of slaves = **~1.5 years of US national income** throughout late 18th
  and first half of 19th century — roughly equal to total value of farmland
- Sources: probate records (**Alice Hanson Jones**), tax/census data
  (**Raymond Goldsmith**), slave market transactions (**Robert Fogel**)
- In the American **South**: value of slaves = 2.5–3 years of NI; combined farmland
  + slaves exceeded **4 years** → southern slave owners controlled **more wealth than
  the landlords of old Europe**
- Southern capital with slaves = **6 years of southern income** ≈ total capital in
  Britain or France; Northern states (virtually no slaves): total wealth barely 3 years

The New World combined two diametrically opposed realities: the relatively
egalitarian North and the hyper-concentrated South.

In a slave society where slaves yield 60% of national income at 5%/yr capitalization,
**slave capital** = 12 years of NI. Market price of a prime male slave in 1860 = **$2,000**
vs. average free farm laborer wage ≈ $200/yr.

British Empire abolished slavery 1833–38; French Empire definitively 1848.

**World Bank fallacy:** Some economists capitalize labor income flows at 4–5% to
conclude "human capital is the leading form of wealth in the 21st century."
Piketty's critique: **trivially obvious** — whenever labor >50% of NI, capitalizing
labor and capital flows at the same rate makes human capital larger by construction.

## Chapter 5: The Capital/Income Ratio over the Long Run

### The Second Fundamental Law of Capitalism: β = s / g

- **β = s / g**: in the long run, capital/income ratio = savings rate ÷ growth rate
- Example: s = 12%, g = 2% → β = 600% (capital worth 6 years of income)
- **A country that saves a lot and grows slowly accumulates an enormous stock of
  capital relative to income**

> "In a quasi-stagnant society, wealth accumulated in the past will inevitably
> acquire disproportionate importance." — Piketty

**Small variations in growth rate have very large long-run effects:**
- g = 2%, s = 12% → β = 600%
- g = 1.5% → β = 800%
- g = 1% → β = 1,200%
- g = 3% → β = 400%

The g in this law = **total national income growth** = per capita growth +
population growth rate.

**β = s / g is an asymptotic law** — valid only in the long run; takes several
decades to manifest. This explains why the shocks of 1914–1945 took so long to fade.

**Contrast with First Fundamental Law:**
- First law: α = r × β — pure **accounting identity**, valid at all times
- Second law: β = s / g — describes a **dynamic equilibrium** economy tends toward;
  never perfectly realized

**Three caveats:**
1. Significant natural resources can mean high β without savings
2. Asset price effects can elevate β without new savings (law assumes prices balance
   in the long run)
3. The law is independent of *why* people save — β = s / g holds regardless

### Europe vs. US: Why β Is Structurally Higher in Europe

Per capita income growth similar across rich countries (1.6–2.0%/yr, 1970–2010).
Key divergence is **demographic**: Europe/Japan population growth <0.5%/yr;
US/Canada/Australia = 1.0–1.5%/yr. Total growth rate: Europe/Japan ≈ 2%, US ≈ 3%.
With similar savings rates, lower total growth mechanically produces higher β.

### Three Factors Behind Capital's Structural Return

1. **Slower growth (especially demographic)** — primary long-run force via β = s / g
2. **Privatization**: gradual transfer of public wealth to private hands in 1970s–80s
3. **Asset price catch-up**: long-term rise in real estate and stock prices,
   accelerated in politically favorable 1980s–90s environment

**Japanese bubble of 1990**: private wealth jumped from ~4× to ~7× NI in the 1980s,
then collapsed sharply in early 1990s, stabilizing at ~6× from mid-1990s.
**Spain bubble**: net private capital hit **8 years of NI** just before 2007–2008
crisis — beat Japan's 1990 record — then collapsed rapidly 2010–2011.

Corporate retained earnings can account for up to **half** of total private savings
in some countries. Tax incentive: dividends taxed more heavily than retained earnings.

**Net vs. Gross Savings:**
Capital depreciation in developed economies ≈ 10–15% of NI, absorbing nearly half of
gross savings (gross savings ≈ 25–30%).

### Privatization Examples

**Italy** (most extreme): Public wealth declined by ~1 year of NI (1970–2010);
private wealth rose from ~2.5 years to ~7 years. ~¼ of private wealth gain reflected
growing public debt: Italians who had the means bought government bonds.

Across all rich countries: **public dissaving accounted for 1/10 to 1/4 of the increase
in private wealth** (Table 5.4):
- US: national saving 5.2% (private 7.6%, public −2.4%)
- Japan: 14.6% (14.5%, +0.1%) — only country with near-zero public dissaving
- Italy: 8.5% (15.0%, −6.5%) — most extreme deficit

**Former Soviet Bloc** — largest privatization in capital history:
- Pre-1989: private wealth <1 year NI; public capital = 3–4 years NI
- Post-collapse: private wealth ~4 years NI, public wealth near zero — complete reversal
- Rapid enrichment of **Russian oligarchs** had nothing to do with β = s/g — purely
  ownership transfer from state to individuals

### Tobin's Q and Asset Price Rebound

**Tobin's Q** (named for **James Tobin**): ratio of a firm's market value to its book value.
- **Immaterial investments** (brand value, R&D) not on balance sheet → market > book;
  explains US/Britain ratios of 100–140% in late 1990s–2000s
- **"Rhenish capitalism" / stakeholder constraints** — shareholders must share control
  → market < book; explains France (~80%), Germany and Japan (~50–70%)
- **Control premium**: buyers seeking to acquire a firm pay ~20% above minority-share price

Asset price rebound accounts for **1/4 to 1/3 of the increase in the capital/income
ratio 1970–2010** (with large country variation).

**Financialization:** total financial assets/liabilities grew much faster than net wealth.
- Early 1970s: total financial assets/liabilities ≤ 4–5 years NI
- By 2010: **10–15 years in US/Japan/Germany/France; 20 years in Britain**
  (absolute historical record)

### 21st-Century Projection

Using β = s/g with projected slowing growth (~3% → ~1.5% by late 21st c.) and assumed
savings rate stabilizing at ~10%:
- **Global capital/income ratio could approach 700% by end of 21st century**
  — roughly the level of Europe from the 18th century through the Belle Époque

> "By 2100, the entire planet could look like Europe at the turn of the
> twentieth century, at least in terms of capital intensity." — Piketty

## Chapter 6: The Capital-Labor Split in the Twenty-First Century

### Historical Pattern: The U-Shaped Capital Share

Using the first fundamental law α = r × β:

**Capital's share over time (Britain and France):**
- 18th–19th centuries: ~35–40% capital / 60–65% labor; r = 5–6%
- Mid-20th century: fell to ~20–25% capital; r = 7–8% (capital scarcer)
- Late 20th–early 21st century: rebounded to ~25–30% capital; r = 4–5%

r partially offsets movements in β: r is higher when β is lower, and vice versa.

**Return by asset type:**
- Riskiest assets (industrial equity, stocks): often >7–8%
- Farmland (18th–19th centuries): ~4–5%
- Real estate (early 21st century): ~3–4%
- Checking/savings accounts: 1–2% or even **negative** in real terms

**The "pure rate of return"** = observed return minus estimated informal management
costs; typically 1–2 percentage points lower. Pure rate estimates should be treated
as **minimum values** — largest fortunes likely earn significantly higher pure returns.

**Core finding**: pure return on capital has oscillated around **4–5% per year**
(range 3–6%) from 18th century to present — **no pronounced long-term trend**.

Traditional conversion rate: capital valued at **20 years of annual income = 5% return**.

Average **tax rate on capital income today ≈ 30%** in most rich countries — primary
reason after-tax returns feel much lower than economic returns.

### Elasticity of Substitution

**Marginal productivity of capital**: value of additional output from one additional
unit of capital. Under perfect competition, r = marginal productivity.

**Elasticity of substitution**: how easily capital can be substituted for labor.
- Elasticity = 0 (fixed coefficients): no substitution; surplus capital has zero
  marginal productivity
- Elasticity = ∞: marginal productivity of capital completely independent of
  quantity of capital; always profitable to accumulate more

**Cobb-Douglas** (Charles Cobb and Paul Douglas, 1928): elasticity = 1 → capital
share α always fixed regardless of β. Example: if α = 30%, capital always earns
30% whether β = 6 or β = 3.
- Critical flaw: stability of capital's income share does NOT imply stability of
  the capital/income ratio β — entirely different things

**Piketty's central empirical claim**: over the **very long run**, elasticity of
substitution between capital and labor has been **greater than one**.
- As β rises, α also rises — volume effect outweighs price effect
- Historical estimate: **1.3 to 1.6** (uncertain and imprecise)
- No self-corrective mechanism prevents simultaneous rise in both β and α
- Figure 6.5: capital income as % of NI in rich countries rose from 15–25%
  in 1970 to **25–30%** in 2000–2010

Two reinforcing explanations: (1) elasticity > 1 technologically; (2) rising
bargaining power of capital due to increased capital mobility and tax competition.

### Historical Context

**Arthur Bowley** (1920): found relatively stable capital-labor split in Britain
1880–1913 — original source of the "stability" idea. **Keynes** (1939): called it
"one of the best-established regularities in all of economic science" — based only
on British manufacturing in the 1920s; a rash claim later shown false.

**Engels' pause** (term coined by **Robert Allen**): during early Industrial
Revolution in Britain (1800–1860), capital's share rose ~10 percentage points
from 35–40% to 45–50% — the period when Marx wrote *The Communist Manifesto*.

**France post-WWII (Figure 6.6):** three phases:
1. 1945–1968: sharp profit rise
2. 1968–1983: pronounced profit decline (May 1968 and aftermath)
3. 1983–early 1990s: rapid profit recovery, then stabilization

France profit share in corporate value added: gross profits rose from **25% (1982)
to 33% (2010)**; net profits rose from 12% to 20%.
France housing rent (Figure 6.7): rose from **2% of NI (1948) to 10% (2010)**.
France total capital share (Figure 6.8): rose from **15% of NI (1982) to 27% (2010)**.

### Back to Marx

Marx's "principle of infinite accumulation" is equivalent to β = s/g in the special
case where **g = 0** (no structural growth). With g = 0 and s > 0: β → ∞; r → 0.

Marx assumed g ≈ 0 because before **Robert Solow's** work in the 1950s, economists
did not clearly understand that long-run growth requires **productivity growth**,
not just capital accumulation.

**Cambridge capital controversy** (1950s–1960s):
- **Roy Harrod** and **Evsey Domar** (late 1930s): first introduced β = s/g explicitly
  but inverted it; Harrod argued β was technologically fixed → growth is "on a razor's
  edge"
- **Robert Solow** (1956): introduced production function with substitutable factors
  → β = s/g as the long-run equilibrium
- Cambridge, England side: **Joan Robinson**, **Nicholas Kaldor**, **Luigi Pasinetti**
- Cambridge, Massachusetts side: **Solow**, **Samuelson**, **Franco Modigliani**
- Piketty's verdict: the controversy had a postcolonial dimension and more obscured
  than enlightened; both sides lacked the historical data to resolve it

### Summary: Capital's Comeback

**Principal lesson of Part Two**: no natural force inevitably reduces capital's
importance over the course of history. The post-WWII era of compressed capital
was driven by political forces (wars, policy), not technology.

> "Modern growth, which is based on the growth of productivity and the diffusion
> of knowledge, has made it possible to avoid the apocalypse predicted by Marx
> and to balance the process of capital accumulation. But it has not altered
> the deep structures of capital." — Piketty

Low/zero demographic growth → return of capital via β = s/g. With β at 7–8 years
and r at 4–5%, **α could reach 30–40% globally** — back to 18th–19th century levels.

## PART THREE: The Structure of Inequality

## Chapter 7: Inequality and Concentration — Preliminary Bearings

### Vautrin's Lesson

**Balzac's *Père Goriot*** (1835) as literary introduction to inequality mechanics.

Père Goriot, former pasta maker, sacrifices his fortune to marry off daughters
**Delphine** and **Anastasie** to aristocratic Parisian society; dies in poverty.
Young boardinghouse companion **Eugène de Rastignac**, a penniless provincial noble
studying law in Paris, falls in with the *grande bourgeoisie*.

**Vautrin** — a cynical ex-convict — offers Rastignac a cold-eyed portrait:

> "By the age of thirty, you will be a judge making 1,200 francs a year…
> can you name five lawyers in Paris who earn more than 50,000 francs a year
> at the age of fifty?" — Vautrin to Rastignac

Alternative: marry **Mademoiselle Victorine** (loves Rastignac), gain **1 million
francs**, drawing **50,000 francs/year** at 5% — ten times what a royal prosecutor
could earn after decades of work. To inherit, Victorine's brother must be killed;
Vautrin offers to handle it for a commission.

**Core lesson:** inheritance > study, in 19th-century France. The figures are
structurally accurate. WWI marked "the suicide of the patrimonial societies of the past."
In *Gone with the Wind*, Scarlett O'Hara's suitors can no more rely on talent than
Rastignac — the size of one's father-in-law's plantation matters most.

Post-WWII: **Vautrin's lesson ceased to be valid** in Europe — for the first time
in history, work and study became the surest routes to the top.

### Capital: Always More Unequally Distributed Than Labor

**Universal regularity**: in every country for every period, inequality of capital
ownership is always greater than inequality of labor income.

- **Top 10% of labor income**: receives **25–30%** of total labor income
- **Top 10% of capital**: always owns **more than 50%** of all wealth (sometimes up to **90%**)
- **Bottom 50% of wages**: receives a significant share (~25–35%) of total labor income
- **Bottom 50% of wealth**: owns almost nothing — **always less than 10%**, generally less than 5%

> "No society has ever existed in which ownership of capital can reasonably be described
> as 'mildly' inegalitarian." — Piketty

| Middle 40% | 18% | 25% | 30% | 35% |
| Bottom 50% | 45% | 40% | 20% | 15% |
| Gini | 0.26 | 0.36 | 0.49 | 0.58 |

**Concrete interpretation (average wage = €2,000/month, US 2010):**
- Top 10%: ~€7,000; middle 40%: ~€2,000; bottom 50%: ~€1,000
- If US trend continues to 2030: top 10% → €9,000; bottom 50% → €800 — top 10%
  could literally hire bottom 50% as domestic servants

**Wealth hierarchy today (Europe, avg net wealth ≈ €200,000/adult):**
- Poorest 50% (own ~5%, avg €20,000): mostly renters with a few thousand euros in savings;
  a quarter have zero or negative net wealth
- Top 1% (avg ~€5M): wealth is primarily **stocks and business assets**
- Next 9% (avg ~€800K): real estate accounts for half or more of wealth
- Above €2–5M: real estate < one-third; above €10M: real estate < 10%, wealth is
  almost entirely stock
- **Housing is the favorite investment of the middle class**; at the very top,
  financial and business assets dominate completely

### Two Paths to High Inequality

**Path 1: "Hyperpatrimonial Society" (Society of Rentiers)**
- Inherited wealth dominates; top decile owns ~90% of all wealth (top centile owns 50%)
- Historical examples: Ancien Régime France, Belle Époque Europe

**Path 2: "Hypermeritocratic Society" (Society of Supermanagers)**
- High inequality driven by extreme **labor income** concentration, not inherited wealth
- Peak of income hierarchy = very high salaries ("supermanagers"), not rentiers
- Largely invented by the **United States** in recent decades

Warning: if both logics reinforce each other, "the future could hold in store a new
world of inequality more extreme than any that preceded it."

> "What primarily characterizes the United States at the moment is a record level of
> inequality of income from labor (probably higher than in any other society at any
> time in the past, anywhere in the world) together with a level of inequality of
> wealth less extreme than the levels observed in traditional societies." — Piketty

### The Patrimonial Middle Class

**1900–1910 baseline** (France, Britain, Sweden): top 10% owned virtually ALL national
wealth — upper decile's share reached **90%**; top 1% alone owned >50%;
top centile exceeded 60% in Britain. Middle 40% owned just 5–10% — barely more
than the bottom 50%. There was effectively **no middle class** in terms of wealth.

Such concentration was a **necessary condition** for the rentier societies of Austen
and Balzac to exist.

**What changed**: wealth share of top centile fell from >50% in Europe in 1900 to
~20–25% by end of 20th century. Today's reality: top 10% still own 60% in Europe
and >70% in US; bottom 50% still own ~5% (same as 1910). Middle 40% gained roughly
one-quarter to one-third of national wealth.

Piketty's verdict: the middle class gain was **real but fragile** — a major historical
transformation, but less than commonly believed, and potentially reversible.

### Problems of Synthetic Indices

**Gini coefficient** (named for Italian statistician **Corrado Gini**, 1884–1965):
- Labor income distributions: Gini ~0.2–0.4
- Capital ownership distributions: Gini ~0.6–0.9
- Scandinavia 1970s–80s labor: Gini = 0.19; Belle Époque European wealth: Gini = 0.85

**Piketty's critique**: Gini collapses multidimensional reality into one number and
confuses labor inequality with capital inequality. Most official OECD/government
reports use **P90/P10 interdecile ratios** — totally ignore everything above the
90th percentile: "it is as if an official government report on inequalities in France
in 1789 deliberately ignored everything above the ninetieth percentile."

**Piketty's preferred method**: distribution tables showing shares of bottom 50%,
middle 40%, and top 10% — more stable, more concrete, more politically transparent.

Conceptually similar to **"social tables"** popular in 18th–early 19th century
political economy: first developed by **Gregory King** (1688); later **Diderot's
*Encyclopedia*** under "political arithmetic."

## Chapter 8: Two Worlds

### France in the Twentieth Century (Figure 8.1)

Top decile's share of national income fell from **45–50% (Belle Époque)** to
**30–35% today** — a drop of 15 percentage points.

**Key finding**: the 20th-century compression was **entirely due to the fall of top
capital incomes** — NOT a compression of wage inequality.
- Wage inequality (top decile share of wages) remained **remarkably stable** throughout
  the entire century — always around 25%
- "If top incomes from capital had not decreased, income inequality would not have
  diminished in the twentieth century"

**Figure 8.2 (Fall of rentiers in France):**
- Top centile's share fell from ~20% (Belle Époque) to ~8–9% today
- The fall was entirely in capital income, not wages
- This is "the fall of rentiers" — destruction of inherited wealth by the shocks of 1914–1945

Inequality history is concentrated in one period: **1914–1945**. Before WWI, nothing
presaged spontaneous reduction in capital concentration — France first imposed income
tax in **1914** (Senate had blocked it since the 1890s).

> "It was war, and not harmonious democratic or economic rationality, that erased
> the past and enabled society to begin anew with a clean slate." — Piketty

### From "Society of Rentiers" to "Society of Managers"

- In **1932**: capital income still the main source for the top **0.5%** of the
  distribution despite the Depression
- **Today**: capital income only exceeds labor income at the top **0.1%** of distribution

Shift: from **society of superrentiers** (top centile dominated by people living off
capital) to **society of managers** (top centile dominated by highly paid labor).

**The top decile: two distinct worlds:**
- **"The 9 percent"** (P90–99): income from labor clearly predominates (80–90% of total)
  — private managers, engineers, senior civil servants; pay 2–3× average
- **"The 1 percent"** (P99–100): capital income progressively dominates

**The Chaos of the Interwar Years:**
Over full 1914–1945 period: top decile fell ~18 points, top centile ~14 points —
"the 1 percent" alone accounts for **three-quarters** of the decrease in inequality.

**The paradox of 1929–1935** (top decile up while top centile down):
- "The 1 percent" lived primarily on dividends and interest — as the Depression crushed
  profits, the top centile's income evaporated
- "The 9 percent" — managers and civil servants — were **beneficiaries** of deflation:
  civil servants received raises 1927–1931 and were immune from unemployment risk;
  private sector wages fell >50% between 1929 and 1935; deflation (prices fell 25%
  in France 1929–1935) → civil servants' real wages **rose**
- Result: 9 percent's income share rose >5 points, more than offsetting the 1 percent's
  decline → top decile share rose

**Popular Front (1936)**: reversed this — **Matignon Accords** raised workers' wages
sharply; franc devalued September 1936.

### Three Phases of French Inequality Since 1945

1. **1945–1967**: Inequality rose sharply — top decile share from <30% to 36–37%;
   driven by restoration of capital's share + widening wage hierarchy; minimum wage
   created 1950 but rarely raised
2. **1968–1983**: Inequality decreased significantly — top decile share fell to ~30%;
   - **May 1968** events → **Grenelle Accords** → 20% minimum wage increase
   - From 1970, minimum wage indexed to mean wage; minimum wage purchasing power
     rose **>130%** between 1968 and 1983; mean wage rose only ~50%
3. **1983–2010**: Inequality increased steadily — top decile share climbed to ~33%
   - **1982–1983**: Socialist government reversed course → austerity, wages frozen,
     annual minimum wage boosts abandoned; capital's profit share skyrocketed

### The Explosion of US Inequality (Figure 8.5)

- **US was more egalitarian than Europe** at turn of 20th century (top decile ~40%
  of US NI vs. 45–50% in France); now **more inegalitarian**
- 1920s: US inequality surpassed Europe — top decile >50% of NI by 1929,
  driven heavily by **capital gains** from stock market boom
- 1950–1980: US top decile held 30–35% — **Paul Krugman** calls this
  "the America we love"
- Post-1980: surged from 30–35% to **45–50%** — a 15-point increase
- At current pace, the upper decile would claim **60% of national income by 2030**
- Best estimate: upper decile exceeded **50% of national income** on eve of 2008 crisis

**Excluding capital gains entirely**: top decile's share still rose from **32% to 46%**
(1970s to 2010) — the increase was **steady and structural**.

**Breaking down the top decile (1970s to 2000–2010):**
- The 1% ($500K+/yr): 9% → ~20% of NI — 11 points
- The next 4% ($150K–$352K): 13% → 16% — 3 points
- The bottom 5% of top decile ($108K–$150K): 11% → 12% — 1 point
- Three-quarters of the total 15-point gain went to **the 1%**; half of that to **the 0.1%**

**Key inequality-crisis link:**
1977–2007: richest 10% appropriated **three-quarters** of all US economic growth;
richest 1% absorbed nearly **60%** of the total increase. For the bottom 90%: income
grew at less than **0.5%/year** over 30 years. Internal transfer (15 points of NI)
is **four times larger** than the US trade deficit with China/Japan/Germany (~4 points).

### The Rise of Supersalaries

Post-1980 US inequality is **primarily a wage story**: unprecedented increase in wage
inequality, especially at the summit. **National War Labor Board** (1941–1945) froze
managers' salaries in nominal terms; only lowest-paid workers received raises.

1950s wage equilibrium: upper decile ~25% of wages, upper centile 5–6%.
From mid-1970s: top 10% and especially **top 1%** began explosive wage growth.
Upper decile's wage share rose from 25% → 35% — about two-thirds of the total
increase in the top decile's share of NI.

**Mobility does not explain away the wage gap**: US government data allows lifetime
wage averaging over 10, 20, or 30-year periods. Result: **the increase in wage
inequality is identical** no matter what reference period is chosen.

**The supermanager phenomenon:**
- Research matching tax returns with corporate compensation data: **60–70%** of the
  top 0.1% of income earners in 2000–2010 were top managers (not athletes or artists)
- Athletes, actors, and artists: **less than 5%** of this group
- Finance professionals: about **twice as common** in the top 0.1% as in the economy
  overall (~20% vs. <10% of GDP) — but **80%** of top earners are NOT in finance

> "In this sense, the new US inequality has much more to do with the advent of
> 'supermanagers' than with that of 'superstars.'" — Piketty

## Chapter 9: Inequality of Labor Income

### Race between Education and Technology

Dominant mainstream theory: wages equal **marginal productivity** → skill biased
technological change raises demand for educated workers; if education supply doesn't
keep pace, inequality rises.

**US case — Claudia Goldin and Lawrence Katz**: compared wage gap (college vs. high
school) against growth rate of college degrees, 1890–2005. The curves move in
**opposite directions**. Wage gap narrowed until the 1970s, then suddenly widened
in the 1980s — precisely when college graduate growth stalled.
Conclusion: rising US wage inequality due to **failure to invest in higher education**.

**France case**: wage hierarchy remained stable over the 20th century despite massive
educational democratization — all skill levels advanced together.

### The Limits of the Theoretical Model: The Role of Institutions

The **marginal productivity model** fails to explain the diversity of wage distributions
across countries at similar technological levels.

**France's minimum wage history — three distinct subperiods:**
1. 1950–1968: minimum wage rarely adjusted → wage hierarchy expanded
2. 1968–1983: minimum wage rose rapidly → wage inequalities fell sharply
3. 1983–2012: minimum wage rose slowly → wage hierarchy expanded again

**US federal minimum wage comparisons:**
- Introduced **1933** (nearly 20 years before France)
- US minimum wage peak in **real terms**: 1969, at $1.60/hour (~$10.10 in 2013 dollars)
- 1980–1990 (Reagan and George H.W. Bush): frozen at $3.35 — significant real decline
- Rose to $5.25 under **Clinton** in 1990s; frozen under **George W. Bush**;
  stood at $7.25/hour in 2013
- US minimum wage in 2013 was **one-third below** the French minimum wage — the reverse
  of the early 1980s situation

**Britain**: introduced minimum wage in 1999 at £6.19/hour (~€8 in 2013).
**Germany and Sweden**: no national minimum wage — rely on **trade union negotiations**
by industry branch; effective minimums ~€10/hour in many sectors in 2013.

**David Card and Alan Krueger** studies: US minimum wage had fallen so low it could
be raised without employment loss — sometimes with gains.

### The Supermanager Phenomenon: An Anglo-Saxon Phenomenon

**Anglo-Saxon countries (Figure 9.2):**
- Top centile's share in 1970s: 6–8% across all four (US, UK, Canada, Australia)
- By early 2010s: ~20% in US, 14–15% in Britain and Canada, barely 9–10% in Australia
- US increase was roughly **twice** that of Britain/Canada and **three times** that
  of Australia and New Zealand

**Continental Europe and Japan (Figures 9.3–9.4):**
- Japan/France: top centile share ~7% in 1980s → ~9% today
- Sweden: from ~4% (lowest ever in WTID) → ~7%
- Germany: ~9% → ~11%
- Income transfer to "the 1 percent" = 2–3 points of NI in Europe/Japan vs.
  **10–15 points in the US** — 5 to 7 times greater

> "The divergence between the various regions of the wealthy world is all the more
> striking because technological change has been the same more or less everywhere:
> the revolution in information technology has affected Japan, Germany, France,
> Sweden, and Denmark as much as the United States, Britain, and Canada."

**Top 0.1% (Figures 9.5–9.6):**
- US top thousandth share: rose from 2% → nearly **10%** — "an unprecedented rise"
- France/Japan: top thousandth rose from ~1.5% → ~2.5%
- A 10% share for 0.1% means each individual earns **100× the national average**
  (e.g., €3M/year if average is €30,000)

**Historical baseline (1900–1910):** Europe was MORE unequal than the US.
Top centile's share exceeded **20% in all European countries** in 1900–1910, including
Nordic countries. New World: US/Canada top centile ~16–18%; Australia/New Zealand
~11–12%. Japan had ~20% top centile share — "part of the same 'old world' as Europe."

By end of WWII, US slightly more unequal than Europe (capital shocks were larger
in Europe). Post-1980 divergence: US top decile reaches 45–50% by 2000–2010,
matching Europe's 1900–1910 levels — "a full circle."

**Germany's peculiarities:** top centile share rose unusually fast 1933–1938
(Nazi rearmament boosted industrial profits). German tax records missing 1938–1950 entirely.

### Emerging Economies

Historical tax data available for: **South Africa** (from 1913), **India** (1922),
**Indonesia** (1920), **Argentina** (1932), **China** and **Colombia** (from mid-1980s).

**Key finding**: emerging economy inequality is roughly similar to rich countries.
- Peak (1910–1950): top centile took ~20% of NI in all four core countries
- More egalitarian phase (1950–1980): top centile fell to 6–12%
- Since 1980: top centile rebounded to ~15% (12–13% India/Indonesia;
  16–18% South Africa/Argentina)

**China**: started from near-Scandinavian equality in mid-1980s (<5% top centile
— not surprising for Communist economy); rapid rise to 10–11% by 2000–2010.

**Colombia**: top centile ~20% throughout 1990–2010 — one of most unequal in WTID.

**Household surveys (primary tool of World Bank)** are seriously misleading — in
Colombia, surveys show highest incomes only 4–5× average (implying top centile <5%),
vs. 20%+ from tax records.

**"Black hole" of growth in India and China**: official GDP growth based on production
statistics; household surveys cannot account for it — because disproportionate growth
went to top earners not captured in surveys.

India stopped publishing detailed income tax data in early 2000s — "a lack of
democratic transparency."

### The Marginal Productivity Illusion

For **senior managers of large firms**, "individual marginal productivity" is essentially
unmeasurable — "close to a pure ideological construct." No observable discontinuity in
years of education, institution selectivity, or professional experience between "the 9
percent" and "the 1 percent" — yet remuneration "literally exploded" at the very top.

In practice, top executive salaries are set by **compensation committees** whose members
are themselves senior executives of other large corporations — circular self-validation.

**Social norms as the true driver**: Why did supermanager pay explode in the US and UK
after 1970 but not in Germany, France, Sweden, Japan, Italy? Social norms evolved
differently; multimillion packages remain more shocking in continental Europe and Japan.

**Key empirical test (Bertrand and Mullainathan)**: executive pay at publicly listed firms
correlates more with *external* variance (macroeconomic conditions, sector-wide trends)
than with internal nonexternal variance — i.e., **"pay for luck"** rather than pay
for performance. Strongest evidence against the marginal productivity theory.

**Tax rate feedback loop**: large decrease in **top marginal income tax rates** in
English-speaking countries after 1980 transformed incentives → executives had much
stronger reasons to negotiate large raises → political feedback: high incomes →
increased political influence → pressure to keep/lower top tax rates → further increases.

## Chapter 10: Inequality of Capital Ownership (Opening)

### Overview

The reduction of **inequality of capital ownership** — not labor income inequality —
was the *sole* reason total income inequality fell during the first half of the 20th century.
Labor income inequality did NOT decrease structurally between 1900–1910 and 1950–1960
in France or the US.

Capital inequality is now reconcentrating as capital/income ratios rise and growth
slows — potentially more worrisome than the supermanager phenomenon (geographically
limited to Anglo-Saxon countries).

Universal law across all societies:
- Bottom 50% own virtually nothing (~5% of total wealth)
- Top 10% own a clear majority (usually >60%, sometimes ~90%)
- Middle 40% own 5–35% — the **"patrimonial middle class"** is the most important
  structural transformation of the 20th century

Detailed data available for four countries: **France, Britain, the United States, Sweden**.

### France: An Observatory of Private Wealth

In **1791**, following abolition of noble fiscal privileges, France established a
universal estate and gift tax covering all property types, all owners, all fortune sizes
— a remarkable innovation. A **cadastre** (property registry) established in the late
1790s–early 1800s still exists today.

By contrast: Britain's comparable data only starts from 1919–1920; US federal estate
tax not created until 1916 and covered only a tiny minority.

Piketty collaborated with **Postel-Vinay and Rosenthal** to collect tens of thousands
of individual declarations from national and departmental archives, covering each
decade 1800–2010. French estate tax was strictly proportional 1791–1901, made slightly
progressive in 1901 after a lengthy parliamentary battle.

**Key finding (Figure 10.1)**: before the shocks of 1914–1945, there was NO visible
trend toward reduced inequality of capital ownership in France — on the contrary.
