Glossary

Core Securities

Warrant: An option to buy common stock at a fixed exercise price before a set expiration date. Think of it as a coupon that says "pay $25 and receive one share of XYZ Corp" — the coupon itself has value because the stock might be worth $40.

Exercise price (striking price): The fixed price a warrant holder must pay to convert the warrant into one share of common stock. A Sperry Rand warrant with exercise price $25 means paying $25 per share upon conversion.

Adjusted warrant: When a warrant converts into more or less than one share (due to stock splits or dividends), the "adjusted warrant" normalizes it to exactly one share. If 1 warrant buys 1.08 shares, the adjusted warrant price is the market price divided by 1.08.

Perpetual warrant: A warrant with no expiration date, lasting the life of the corporation. Examples include Alleghany Corporation and Tri-Continental.

Convertible bond: A corporate bond that can be exchanged for a fixed number of common shares. Equivalent to an ordinary bond plus embedded "latent warrants."

Latent warrant: The hidden warrant embedded inside a convertible bond or convertible preferred stock. Calculated by subtracting the bond's investment worth from its market price, then dividing by the number of shares it converts into.

Call option: A short-term warrant issued by an individual (the "writer"), not by the corporation. Typically lasts less than one year and cannot trade on exchanges.

Put: The right to sell stock at a fixed price before expiration — the mirror image of a call. Profits when the stock falls.

Key Strategies

Basic system: Short overpriced warrants while simultaneously buying the underlying common stock. The hedge cancels directional risk while capturing profit from warrant overpricing and time decay.

Reverse hedge: The mirror image — buy underpriced warrants long, short the common. Profits from large moves in either direction.

Mix: The ratio of warrants shorted per share of common purchased. A "3-to-1 mix" means shorting 300 warrants for every 100 shares held long.

Avalanche effect: As shorted warrants decline, margin is released, enabling additional short positions without new capital. The compounding creates snowballing returns.

Straddle: Writing both a put and a call at the same striking price — functionally equivalent to the basic system.

Market Mechanics

Short selling: Selling a borrowed security, then buying it back later at (hopefully) a lower price. The profit is the difference between the selling price and the repurchase price.

Initial margin: The minimum deposit required to open a position, set by the Federal Reserve. In 1966, it was 70% for most listed securities.

Maintenance margin: The minimum equity required to hold a position, set by the NYSE. Falling below triggers a margin call.

Buying power: The amount of new securities that can be purchased with excess equity in a margin account. If equity exceeds the initial margin requirement, the surplus can fund new trades.

Buy-in: When the lender of borrowed shares demands their return, forcing the short seller to purchase shares in the open market — potentially at an unfavorable price.

Up-tick rule: Short sales can only execute when the last different-price transaction was lower, preventing shorts from driving prices down during panic selling.

Short squeeze: When someone corners a heavily-shorted security, gaining control of nearly all available shares and forcing short sellers to cover at any price.

Valuation Tools

Normal price curve: The empirically derived relationship between warrant price and stock price at a given time to expiration. Warrants above the curve are overpriced; below it, underpriced.

Zero profit line: A line on the warrant-stock diagram showing all stock/warrant price combinations where the hedge breaks even. Points below the line represent profit.

Standardized price (S/E, W/E): Stock price or warrant price divided by the adjusted exercise price. Allows comparison of warrants with different exercise prices on the same chart.

Premium: The amount a warrant trades above its intrinsic conversion value. Speculators pay premiums for leverage; premiums erode as expiration approaches.

Investment worth: The estimated price a convertible bond would fetch without its conversion feature — the "pure bond" value.

Chapter 1: A System Is Born

In October 1961, Sheen Kassouf made his first investment using a method that would eventually average 25% annually over five years. But his path to that moment was paved with failure. He had started four years earlier doing everything the conventional way — subscribing to advisory services, buying stocks like Emerson Radio on expert recommendations, and watching the fundamentals. The stock declined sharply. He lost $1,500 before giving up. The advisory service had offered "facts" that proved worthless in the face of actual market behavior.

Kassouf spent time in Wall Street's boardrooms, observing two species of investor. In the elite upstairs offices, dignified men discussed fundamental analysis — the attempt to calculate a stock's "intrinsic value" from its earnings, dividends, and growth prospects. Downstairs in the ground-floor "circus" offices, chartists stared at price patterns, convinced that heads and shoulders, triangles, and wedges could predict the future. Thorp later tested the chartists by covering the name and half of a stock chart, then asking technical analysts what the pattern predicted. Their forecasts were no better than random guesses.

"Chart reading seems scientific but it isn't."— Thorp & Kassouf

The fundamental approach had its own fatal flaw. Even if you correctly identified an undervalued stock, you needed the rest of the market to eventually agree with you — and it might not, for years. Kassouf learned this the hard way when he purchased Columbia Broadcasting at 40 and General Dynamics at 38 using careful fundamental analysis, while the market averages were near all-time highs. Both stocks declined steadily. Meanwhile, friends following hot tips on speculative "cats and dogs" made easy profits. The lesson was painful but clarifying: being right about value does not guarantee returns.

The Dow Theory case study exposed just how thin the chartist advantage really was. A hypothetical $100 invested in the Dow Jones industrials in 1897 would have grown to $11,237 by 1956 if the investor timed every trade perfectly. But University of Chicago research showed that random buying and selling between 1926 and 1960 produced an average annual return of 9%. Dow Theory, the most sophisticated version of chart reading, barely beat randomness. The ritual language of boardrooms — "this market already discounted the slowdown, but are they going to discount it twice?" — helped investors cope with uncertainty. It did not help them predict prices.

The 1961 market frenzy added another lesson. Companies with exotic or scientific names flooded the market with new issues daily. A friend urged Kassouf to buy Adler Electronics at $11; it quickly hit $20. Another tip on Hydrocarbon rose 11 points. Kassouf sat both out, and his restraint felt foolish at the time. But the mania was teaching him something valuable: hot tips and momentum could generate short-term profits, but they offered no systematic, repeatable edge. He was looking for something that worked on logic, not luck.

The breakthrough came while studying Textron, Inc. and its warrants on the American Stock Exchange. A warrant is simply an option to buy common stock at a fixed price — and Kassouf noticed that warrant prices move in a predictable relationship with the underlying stock price. He charted the behavior and began hunting for mispriced warrants. Molybdenum Corporation warrants appeared the most severely mispriced of all. He considered shorting them but hesitated — Wall Street mythology held that short selling was dangerous and almost subversive.

Then one evening, studying the charts of warrant-to-stock price relationships, the insight hit. A strategy existed that could profit whether the stock rose dramatically, fell dramatically, or went nowhere. By combining a short position in overpriced warrants with a long position in the underlying common, the risks canceled while the profits remained. It looked too good to be true. He called his brother late that night to walk through the logic. His brother agreed it was promising but cautioned that they might be overlooking something. To raise capital for the pilot investment, Kassouf sold his careful fundamental picks — Columbia Broadcasting and General Dynamics — at a combined loss exceeding $1,500.

The Molybdenum trade was virtually assured of success. For the first time, investment returns did not depend on chart formations or on the market eventually agreeing about value. Success continued through the 1962 stock market crash and the Cuban missile crisis of October 1962, when prices swung violently in both directions. The system worked in falling markets and rising ones alike. Kassouf enrolled at Columbia University as a graduate student, testing the system's logic in seminars with Professor Arthur F. Burns, who had served as President Eisenhower's chief economic advisor. Burns scrutinized the approach and found it sound.

Over five years of live trading from 1961 to 1966, the method yielded 25% per year with virtually no risk. Kassouf would eventually collaborate with Edward O. Thorp, the mathematician who had already proven that blackjack could be beaten with card counting. Together they would refine the approach into what they called the "basic system" — the first scientifically validated method for consistent stock market profits.

Chapter 2: Warrants — Options on the Future

A warrant is a piece of paper that says: pay the exercise price and receive one share of common stock. Sperry Rand issued 2.2 million warrants in 1957, each allowing the holder to buy one share at $25 until September 1963, then at $28 through September 1967. The company attached these warrants to a $110 million bond offering as sweeteners — by making the bonds more attractive, Sperry could borrow at a lower interest rate. The warrants were detachable, meaning they could be separated from the bond and traded independently on the American Stock Exchange.

The leverage that warrants provide is extraordinary. Tri-Continental Corporation perpetual warrants cost three cents each in 1942. Four years later, they sold for $5.625 — a return of 18,750%. A $1,000 investment became $187,500. By 1965, those same warrants reached $47.375, turning $1,000 into $1,575,000. The common stock of Tri-Continental returned a more modest 73-fold over the same period. Warrant holders earned twenty times more than stockholders on identical capital because warrants amplify every move in the underlying stock.

"This leverage is the fundamental appeal to investors seeking amplified returns."— Thorp & Kassouf

Thorp's key analytical contribution was the warrant-stock diagram. Instead of the conventional bar chart showing price over time, he plotted warrant price on the vertical axis against stock price on the horizontal axis. Each dot represented a month-end closing price pair. Using Sperry Rand's 1960 data, twelve monthly dots clustered in a curved pattern running from lower-left to upper-right. The curve revealed something the bar chart could not: the precise mathematical relationship between warrant and stock prices.

Two iron rules govern this relationship. First, the warrant price must always be less than the stock price, because the warrant holder still has to pay the exercise price to get the share, and the common pays dividends while the warrant never does. Second, the warrant price plus the exercise price must be at least as much as the stock price. If it were less, an arbitrageur could buy the warrant, exercise it immediately, sell the resulting share, and pocket a risk-free profit. In the 1930s, when capital was scarce and warrants poorly understood, this second rule was sometimes violated. By the 1960s, arbitrageurs killed any such discrepancy almost instantly.

Between these two boundaries — the maximum value line and the minimum value line — Thorp conjectured that for each warrant at each point in its life, there exists a normal price curve. The warrant's actual price should lie near this curve. As expiration approaches, the curve drops toward the minimum value line because time itself has value: a warrant with two years left is worth more than an identical one with six months left, all else being equal. The family of curves for different maturities forms the foundation of the entire system. Warrants trading above their curve are overpriced; warrants below it are underpriced.

Thorp verified the two rules against real market data. On July 21, 1966, he checked 22 warrants across NYSE and AMEX listings. Rule 1 held in every single case. Rule 2 was violated in only one — Textron, where the stock at 51.875 exceeded the warrant-plus-exercise total of 51.75 by just one-eighth of a point. After commissions of roughly three-quarters of a point, the "arbitrage" produced a net loss. Both rules were essentially enforced at all times by market participants hunting for risk-free profits.

Many warrants convert into more or less than one share due to stock splits and dividends. Sperry Rand's warrant originally bought one share for $25, but after three stock dividends, it bought 1.08 shares for $28. The concept of the adjusted warrant normalizes this: divide the market price by the conversion ratio to get the price of one "adjusted warrant" converting into exactly one share. The adjusted exercise price is the exercise price divided by the same ratio. All analysis uses these adjusted figures, making the framework universal across any warrant regardless of its specific conversion terms.

Reading warrant prices from the financial pages required care. On July 22, 1966, Sperry Rand was the most active stock on the NYSE, trading 346,300 shares at a close of $29. Its warrant was the second most active security on the AMEX, trading 127,400 at a close of 10.375. The adjusted warrant price came to $9.61 (10.375 divided by 1.08) and the adjusted exercise price to $25.93 (28 divided by 1.08). The $6.54 difference between the warrant-plus-exercise total ($35.54) and the stock price ($29) was the premium — the extra cost a buyer accepted in exchange for the warrant's leverage. This premium would erode as expiration approached, and that erosion was the basic system's source of profit.

Chapter 3: Short Selling — Profits in Bad Times

Stocks have returned roughly 9-11% per year on average, but that average conceals devastating crashes. From 1929 to 1932, stocks fell to just 13% of their starting value. U.S. Steel dropped from 262 to 22. In 1962, the Dow fell 26% in three and a half months. Short selling is the tool that lets an investor profit from these declines, and it is essential to the hedging system. You sell a stock you don't own by borrowing it, then buy it back later at a lower price. The difference is your profit.

The mechanics are precise. You instruct your broker to sell short. He searches his cage room for certificates held in street name. If found, the sale proceeds are deposited as collateral with the lender — the short seller cannot touch this cash. If the stock rises, the lender demands more collateral; if it falls, excess collateral is returned. Short sales can only execute on an up-tick — the last different-price transaction must have been lower — which means you cannot always short at the exact moment you want to.

Near-expiration warrants are almost always overpriced. Buyers consistently overpay for the lottery-ticket appeal of leverage. The evidence is stark: of 11 listed warrants purchased 18 months before expiration and held until two months before expiration, 8 produced large losses when held long. The worst performers included Radio-Keith-Orpheum at -99.2% and Pan American at -98.4%. Only three produced gains. The average 16-month return from buying warrants was -46%. Flip that around: the average return from shorting them was +46%. With 70% margin, the annualized return from systematic warrant shorting approached 50%.

"Short selling is something every serious investor in the market should understand but that few do. It is one of the crucial tools that allows our system to make money whether stocks go up or down."— Thorp & Kassouf

The distinction between margin on long positions and margin on short sales matters. Initial margin in 1963 was 70% for most listed stocks, meaning a buyer needed to put up 70% of the purchase price and could borrow the rest from the broker. Maintenance margin of 30% was the minimum equity required to hold an open position. For short sales, the sale proceeds served as collateral deposited with the lender of the borrowed certificates. The short seller could not use this cash — it was a bookkeeping entry until the position was closed. But the short seller also paid no interest on this deposit, unlike margin buyers who paid at least 1.5 points above prime on their borrowed funds.

Low-priced securities had special margin rules that significantly affected the strategy. Stocks under $2.50 required the full $2.50 per share as margin on short sales. Between $2.50 and $5, the margin was the share price itself. Above $5, the greater of $5 per share or the standard percentage applied. This meant that shorting a warrant trading at $1 required $2.50 per share in margin — an effective margin rate of 250%. At that level, even a warrant heading to zero could not produce an attractive return on capital. The practical implication was clear: avoid shorting warrants trading below $1.

What made short selling so powerful for warrants specifically was the statistical evidence of systematic overpricing. Buyers of near-expiration warrants were paying for the dream of leverage — the slim chance that the stock would surge and multiply their money. That dream was almost always overpriced. The 11-warrant study showed that buying warrants 18 months before expiration was a losing proposition in 8 out of 11 cases, and the average loss was catastrophic. The short seller harvested the spread between the speculator's hope and mathematical reality.

The most spectacular illustration of short selling's power is the avalanche effect. In October 1962, Thorp sold 1,000 Molybdenum warrants short at 13 with 50% margin, depositing $6,500. As the warrant price fell, initial margin requirements dropped, releasing capital for additional shorts. He reinvested at every point decline — 12, 11, 10, 9, all the way down to 1. By the time warrants reached 1/2, he had pyramided to 2,068 warrants short. His $6,500 investment produced $84,292 in profit — a fourteen-fold multiplier. The mathematics are clean: with 50% margin, if a security falls from $13 to $0.50, continuously reinvested short proceeds compound to extraordinary multiples.

Thorp worked out the general formula for the avalanche effect: P equals V-zero times the quantity (W-zero over W-final) raised to the power (one over margin fraction), minus one. The formula predicts that even a modest decline in a shorted security, when combined with disciplined reinvestment of released margin, produces returns far beyond what the raw percentage decline would suggest. With 25% margin on a stock falling from 500 to 5, a $250 initial investment could theoretically yield nearly $2 billion. The numbers are absurd at the extremes, but they illustrate the exponential nature of the compounding.

Chapter 4: The Basic System

The fundamental insight is that two risky investments — shorting overpriced warrants and buying common stock — can be combined to cancel out risk while retaining profit. This is the basic system. Warrant hedging had been known since the 1930s, but prior writers failed to identify which warrants were overpriced or to realize that the hedge should only apply to those specific situations. Thorp and Kassouf provided the missing precision.

Consider a concrete example. Company XYZ has a warrant with an exercise price of $10, expiring in 18 months, currently trading at 3. The common stock is at 6. The strategy: sell 100 warrants short at 3 and simultaneously buy 100 shares of common at 6. If the common is above $10 at expiration, the warrant will be worth about $3 less than the stock price, and the common will have risen at least $4 — yielding a net profit between $300 and $700 regardless of how high the stock goes. If the common falls, losses on the stock are offset by gains on the short warrant position.

"No matter how much the common rises in the next 18 months, our combination of investments guarantees us a profit of from $300 to $700."— Thorp & Kassouf

Why does the hedge work? Because the warrant and the common stock are tied together by the conversion relationship but move at different speeds. When the stock rises, the warrant rises too, but less than one-for-one if the stock is near or below the exercise price. The common side gains more than the warrant side loses. When the stock falls, the warrant falls with it, but the short warrant position profits while the long common position loses — and the warrant's decline is typically comparable to or greater than the common's decline in percentage terms. The asymmetry between the two legs of the trade creates a positive expected value across a wide range of outcomes.

The mix — the ratio of warrants shorted per share of common held — determines the risk-reward profile. A 1-to-1 mix guarantees $300 to $700 if the common stays above the exercise price. A 1.5-to-1 mix profits if the common trades anywhere between approximately 1.75 and 27 just before expiration. A 2-to-1 mix costs $1,740 in initial investment and produces an average return around 52% in 18 months, or roughly 34% annualized. Higher mixes amplify returns but narrow the range of stock prices that produce a profit. Lower mixes are safer but earn less.

A properly hedged portfolio using the basic system yielded roughly 25% annual returns without substantial loss risk. Violent short-term stock price swings had "comparatively little effect" on the hedged portfolio because the two legs of the position pulled in opposite directions. The system worked in rising markets (common appreciates, warrant held steady or rises less), falling markets (common declines, warrant declines more), and stationary markets (warrant decays toward zero while common stays flat). The only scenario requiring caution was an extreme gap move with no intermediate prices at which to adjust.

The key conceptual advance was understanding that the risk did not lie in either leg of the trade individually — both legs were risky on their own. Shorting warrants naked could produce catastrophic losses if the stock surged. Buying common stock could lose everything in a crash. But the combination cancelled out the directional risk while preserving the profit from warrant overpricing. Prior writers who had discussed warrant hedging since the 1930s never identified which warrants were overpriced or computed the optimal mix. Without that precision, their hedges were hit-or-miss. Thorp and Kassouf's contribution was rigorous identification of the mispricing plus the right ratio to exploit it.

The zero profit line on the warrant-stock diagram visualizes these tradeoffs. It passes through the starting position with a slope equal to one divided by the mix ratio. Every point below this line represents a profit; every point above it, a loss. For a 3-to-1 mix, the slope is one-third — the stock needs to rise three points for every one-point rise in the warrant just to break even. As time passes, the warrant decays toward the minimum value line, pulling the actual price further below the zero profit line and locking in gains. This is why the basic system works: time is on the hedger's side.

Thorp visualized the position's evolution over time using a series of normal price curves for 18, 12, 6, and 3 months before expiration. At each interval, the curve drops lower on the diagram, and the actual warrant-stock pair tends to track downward with it. Constant profit lines drawn for different mixes showed that the 2-to-1 mix offered the highest peak return but required the stock to stay within a narrower range, while the 1-to-1 mix offered the widest safety margin with a lower ceiling. The visual made the tradeoff tangible: every investor could see exactly how much protection they were buying with a more conservative mix and how much upside they were sacrificing.

Chapter 5: The System in Action

Before presenting his real trading results, Thorp warns about spurious systems. He cites the Gridiron Method: an arbitrage rule based on Harvard-Yale and California-Stanford football scores that was profitable from 1920 to 1940. The correlation was entirely coincidental. Thousands of such "systems" exist if you mine enough data. What distinguishes the basic system is that its rules derive from commonsense logic about warrant valuation, not from pattern-matching historical coincidences.

The Molybdenum Corporation trade began in October 1961. Kassouf bought 150 shares of common at an average price of 33 and shorted 400 warrants at an average of 18, committing about $8,500 in total capital. The warrants were exercisable at $28.83. Even if the common stock became completely worthless, the investment would return 26%. The worst realistic case projected a loss of just $625; the best case projected a profit of $6,574 — a 77% return. The math was overwhelmingly favorable.

The 1962 crash tested the position immediately. On Blue Monday, May 28, 1962, President Kennedy accused steel executives of "utter contempt for the interest of 185 million Americans," sending stocks into a panic. Moly fell four and five-eighths points; the NYSE ticker did not print the final price until 5:59 PM, two and a half hours after the close. The next day saw 14.75 million shares trade — the second most active day in NYSE history at that time. Over a thousand issues hit new lows; just two hit new highs. But the hedged Molybdenum position was barely affected. As the common fell, the warrant fell with it, and the short position absorbed the blow.

Thorp learned a costly lesson about brokerage mechanics when his broker forced a buy-in on 200 Moly warrants. The broker claimed the warrants were "scarce" and covered the position at 15 — warrants that Thorp had shorted at 20. He made $1,000 profit, but one week later the warrants fell to 10.50. The premature buy-in cost him thousands in potential gains. After that experience, Thorp made it a rule to verify the broker's inventory before shorting any security and to maintain accounts at multiple firms.

A dramatic short squeeze attempt developed in mid-1962. Lewis Harder, president of International Mining, began aggressively buying Moly warrants, eventually accumulating 36,300 — about 20% of the 186,000 outstanding. Kennecott Copper Corporation held another 14,285. The potential for a corner was real: if one party controls nearly all the certificates, short sellers can be forced to buy back at any price. Eddie Gilbert had previously cornered E.L. Bruce stock, driving it from 17 to 195 before fleeing to Brazil when his other ventures collapsed. But Harder's squeeze on Moly warrants collapsed when the common advanced to 34 while warrants fell to 19 — the mathematics were simply too unfavorable for warrant holders to sustain inflated prices.

The Bunker-Ramo (originally Teleregister) trade illustrated the avalanche effect in practice. Starting in April 1963, Kassouf went long 1,000 shares of common at 4.50 and short 1,000 warrants at 2.625, with total investment of $5,041. By June 1964, he shorted an additional 400 warrants at 5.125. When the warrants fell to 3.50, enough purchasing power was released to short 200 more warrants without depositing additional margin. An erroneous margin call from the clerk taught another lesson: always verify your own account calculations, because mixed accounts (simultaneously long and short) frequently trigger margin errors. Over 26 months, the average monthly investment was $3,500 and total profit reached $8,964 — more than 250%, or roughly 120% annualized.

The Sperry Rand trade was the longest and largest. Over 47 months beginning in September 1962, Thorp accumulated roughly 5,000 shares of common stock mostly below 15 and shorted about 7,500 warrants mostly above 8. The average monthly investment was $40,000. When Univac finally reported a profitable quarter in July 1966, mutual funds piled into Sperry and the common surged to 28 — but the warrants lagged at 10, well below normal price. Thorp closed the position for a total profit of $50,150 after all costs and commissions, equivalent to 23% per year compounded.

Across all three situations plus several smaller trades in National Tea, Universal American, Pacific Petroleums, and Realty Equities, total profits from 1961 through October 1966 reached approximately $85,000 — consistently exceeding 25% per year. More than a hundred investors who sought Thorp's advice during this period achieved similar returns.

Chapter 6: How to Use the Basic System

This chapter is the operating manual. Thorp distills the method into a six-step checklist that any investor can follow. Step one: identify all warrants listed on the American Stock Exchange. The AMEX was the primary venue, listing 15 to 20 warrants at any time, with better transparency and liquidity than the over-the-counter market. Sources included the AMEX handbook from Francis Emory Fitch, Inc., the Wall Street Journal's AMEX listings (look for the "wt" suffix), and Moody's Manuals, though Thorp warned that even Moody's contained errors and critical details should be verified directly with the transfer agent.

Step two: limit candidates to warrants expiring in less than four years. Short-term warrants decay faster, delivering more profit per unit of time invested. Step three: eliminate any warrant where the stock trades at more than 1.2 times the adjusted exercise price. Historical study of all warrants from 1946 to 1966 showed that shorting above this threshold was usually unprofitable.

Step four introduces the standardized warrant-stock diagram. The problem with comparing warrants directly is that they have different exercise prices. The solution is to divide both the stock price and the warrant price by the exercise price, creating dimensionless ratios (S/E and W/E) that can be plotted on a single chart. On this chart, the normal price curves show where the average warrant of a given maturity should trade. Curves for 24, 18, 12, 6, and 1.5 months to expiration were derived from all AMEX warrants listed after 1946. Warrants trading at or above their maturity curve are overpriced — the basic system's target.

Step five: choose the mix. The working rule derived from normal price curve slope analysis is elegant in its simplicity. If S/E is below 0.3, short warrants only without buying common — the curve is nearly horizontal and the implied hedge ratio is enormous. If S/E is between 0.3 and 1.2, use a 3-to-1 mix: short 300 adjusted warrants for every 100 shares of common purchased. Above 1.2, do not use the basic system at all. The 3-to-1 standard matches the average slope of the normal price curves in the critical range, minimizing short-term losses from price movements immediately after entry.

Step six: open the position. Venturesome investors or those expecting the stock to decline should choose higher mixes. Cautious or bullish investors should use lower mixes. No single ratio is optimal — it depends on risk tolerance and market outlook. But for nearly all combinations, a hedged position is superior to either a straight long position or a naked short. With under $10,000, Thorp recommends concentrating in one situation, preferring the warrant closest to expiration for fastest capital recycling. Above $10,000, split between two or more attractive situations for diversification.

"The basic system need not be applied mechanically. To improve performance, consider the factors that make each situation unique."— Thorp & Kassouf

Comparing candidates visually on the standardized diagram revealed clear winners. On July 21, 1966, Pacific Petroleums and Universal American both traded considerably above their normal price curves — heavily overpriced and ideal for shorting. Sperry Rand sat close to the curve, acceptable but not spectacular. General Acceptance and Rio Algom fell below the curve and were eliminated. Standardized monthly profit profiles confirmed that Pacific Petroleums and Universal American were superior at every price point. Pacific Petroleums was safer: the stock had to fall more than 96% or rise more than 190% before the hedge lost money. Sperry required only a 73% fall or 40% rise to produce a loss in 13.8 months.

Capital allocation depended on account size. With under $10,000, Thorp recommended concentrating in the single best situation, preferring the nearest expiration for fastest recycling of capital. Above $10,000, splitting between two or more situations reduced risk. A sample allocation from July 1966: 40% Universal American, 40% Pacific Petroleums, 20% Sperry Rand. Sperry received the smallest weight because it was slightly below the normal price curve and its S/E ratio was approaching the 1.2 cutoff. The logic was simple: put the most money where the edge was largest.

Practical notes complete the chapter. You need a margin account at a broker with seats on both the AMEX and NYSE. Verify before trading that the broker can actually borrow the warrants you want to short — some firms refuse to short certain issues. Avoid shorting warrants trading below $1, because the $2.50 maintenance margin requirement creates an effective margin of 250%, crushing returns. And watch for short squeeze risk: large short interest can force early covering as lenders recall warrants before expiration.

Chapter 7: Further Proof

The personal track record proved the system worked from 1961 to 1966, but that raised an obvious question: were those years uniquely favorable? The answer came from a simplified mechanical strategy applied to every AMEX-listed warrant from 1946 to 1966 — twenty years of data covering every market condition. Five rules defined the strategy: restrict to warrants expiring in under four years; eliminate those trading below 6% of exercise price or where the stock exceeds 1.2 times exercise price; eliminate warrants under $1; from the survivors, select the nearest to expiration and short three warrants per share of common; when the warrants expire, roll into the next qualifying situation or hold Treasury bills.

The results were striking. Over 17 active years (four years had no qualifying warrants), the system produced an arithmetic average of roughly 30% per year and an equivalent compound annual rate of 26% before taxes. After a flat 25% tax rate, the compound return was still 22%. An initial $1,000 grew to $22,289 after taxes and approximately $50,000 before taxes — a fifty-fold multiplication. These figures deliberately omitted the avalanche effect; with reinvestment of released margin, profits would have been "often substantially" higher.

"The equivalent annual compounding rate, which is 26% before taxes, is a more important figure than the 30% arithmetic average."— Thorp

The tax treatment amplified the advantage. Under 1966 law, profits on common stock held longer than six months qualified for long-term capital gains, taxed at a maximum of 25% or half the ordinary income rate, whichever was less. Short-sale profits, however, were always taxed as ordinary income. Despite this disadvantage on the short leg, the after-tax compound return of 22% per year still vastly outperformed any alternative. An investor paying a flat 25% tax on all profits would have turned $1,000 into $22,289 over 17 years. The tax code penalized short selling, but the system's edge was large enough to absorb the penalty.

Critically, the back-test results omitted the avalanche effect entirely. The simplified mechanical strategy assumed no reinvestment of released margin — profits from declining warrant positions simply sat idle in the account. If the investor had reinvested as Thorp did in his personal trading, the returns would have been "often substantially" higher. The 26% compound annual rate was therefore a conservative estimate of the system's actual potential, not an optimistic one.

Corporate incentives favored more warrant issuance in the future. When a company sold a $1,000 bond with $300 of warrant value attached, it effectively received only $700 for the bond portion. At redemption it paid $1,000, creating a $300 amortizable loss over the bond's life — a significant tax savings. Convertible bonds did not receive this amortization treatment. As more corporate treasurers learned about this advantage, Thorp expected warrant issuance to increase, expanding the universe of basic-system opportunities.

The comparison with alternatives was devastating. An investor who only bought the common stocks (no warrant short) turned $1,000 into about $2,200 over 17 years. The basic system did 22 times better. An investor who only shorted warrants (no common hedge) turned $1,000 into about $22,000 but experienced erratic, gut-wrenching swings — including the Mack Trucks disaster, where warrants shorted at 17.50 surged to 35.50, triggering margin calls that would have forced covering at a $20,000 loss before the warrants eventually settled back. The basic system matched the naked short's returns at a fraction of the risk.

The ultimate stress test was the 1929 crash. Applied to the warrant market of June 28, 1929, the system would have doubled the investor's money while the S&P 500 fell 35%. American Commonwealth Power, the best candidate that day, saw its common plunge 90% from 23.125 to 2.25 — yet the hedged position turned a profit because the warrants fell even more precipitously. General Cable and Curtiss-Wright positions would have produced 67-73% profits even if their common stocks fell to zero. The basic system was not merely crash-resistant; it was crash-profitable.

Looking ahead, Thorp argued that warrant issuance would likely increase because corporations enjoy a significant tax advantage from warrants versus convertible bonds. When a company issues a bond with a $300 warrant attached for $1,000, it effectively receives $700 for the bond and can amortize a $300 loss over the bond's life — a substantial tax savings that convertible bonds do not provide. More warrants meant more basic-system opportunities.

Chapter 8: More on Warrants and Hedging

Beyond the AMEX, warrants traded in the over-the-counter market, on regional exchanges, and on the Toronto and Montreal exchanges. About 60 OTC warrants, 25 Toronto-listed, and 1 Montreal-listed were known as of September 1966. But OTC trading introduced friction that listed warrants did not have. The spread between bid and ask prices was wide enough to eat into profits. Some firms refused to short OTC securities entirely. Others would not short anything trading below $3 or any warrant at all. The Lynch Corporation case study illustrated the frustration: Thorp tried to short the warrants at various prices but every offer was refused, with the bid-ask spread widening to 67% of the bid price. He switched to the listed Universal American warrant instead.

Sheen Kassouf's doctoral dissertation at Columbia provided the first mathematical analysis of warrant prices. He found that the normal price curves varied considerably from the average, depending on five factors: time until expiration, potential dilution (the percentage of new shares created if all warrants were exercised), dividend rate, exercise price, and recent stock price history. Higher dilution and higher dividends pushed warrant prices down. The trend effect was counterintuitive: when a stock was above its 11-month average, warrant buyers grew skeptical and the warrant traded below the normal curve; when a stock was below its average, buyers expected a bounce and paid a premium.

Volatility emerged as a critical selection factor. Measured as the yearly high-minus-low range as a percentage of the midpoint price, volatility determined which side of the hedge was more attractive. Consolidated Oil and Gas at 82% volatility was a worse basic-system candidate than Gyrodyne at 51%, despite similar standardized prices, because high volatility hurts the short-warrant side. Conversely, high volatility is an advantage in reverse hedging.

The reverse hedge is the mirror image of the basic system: buy underpriced warrants long and short the common. The payoff is asymmetric — the warrant near the minimum value line rises faster in percentage terms than the common on up-moves, but falls no faster (or slower) on down-moves. Realty Equities Corporation provided the dramatic proof. In July 1965, with the warrant at 1.125 and common at 7.375, Thorp bought 4,000 warrants and shorted 1,000 shares of common for a total cash investment of about $4,500. A one-point rise in the common (13% gain) translated to a four-fifths-point rise in the warrant (83% gain) — the warrant moved six times faster. No margin was required on the short sale under Regulation T Section 220.3(d)(3) because the warrants were convertible into the shorted stock.

"The hedged investor does not fear a market collapse."— Thorp & Kassouf

Realty Equities acquired the Schine empire of real estate holdings. By April 1966, the stock peaked at 12.625 and the warrant at 8.375. Thorp's profit: $26,300 on the warrants minus $6,300 on the common short, for a net gain of $20,000 — a return of 445% in nine months. The downside scenario, had the stock fallen to its 1965 lows, would have produced a loss of just $30 on the $4,500 investment — less than 1%. Comparing all alternatives (buying warrants on cash, on margin, buying common on cash, on margin, various hedge ratios), the 4-to-1 reverse hedge showed -0.7% at worst and +445% at best. No other approach came close to this risk-reward profile.

The mathematics of warrant valuation depended on two properties of the underlying stock: trend and volatility. Trend was the expected price change between now and expiration. If the stock was expected to rise, the warrant was worth more; if expected to fall, worth less. Volatility measured the spread of possible prices at expiration. Greater volatility meant more possible outcomes above the exercise price, which increased the warrant's value. A warrant on a stock with 80% annual volatility was worth substantially more than an identical warrant on a stock with 30% volatility. The normal price curves in Chapter 6 represented averages across all volatilities — individual warrants on highly volatile stocks would trade above the curve, making them look overpriced when they might simply reflect fair compensation for the risk.

The mathematical expectation framework made the pricing concrete. If a stock had a 50% chance of reaching $5 at expiration and a 50% chance of reaching $15, and the exercise price was $10, then the warrant's expected payoff was 50% times zero (stock below exercise) plus 50% times $5 (stock above exercise), equaling $2.50. The actual warrant price might differ from this depending on each investor's utility function — their personal risk preferences. But the basic system did not require predicting prices or accounting for utilities. It simply required identifying warrants trading above the normal curve and letting time decay erode the overpricing.

Four criteria identified reverse-hedge candidates: the stock should be within 20% of the adjusted exercise price; the warrant should not expire in less than four years; it should be underpriced relative to the average curve; and the more volatile the common, the better. At least 26 OTC warrants whose common traded on the NYSE or AMEX met these criteria in September 1966.

Chapter 9: Can Anything Go Wrong?

Thorp believed 25% per year was safely achievable and dedicated this chapter to surveying every known risk. The short squeeze was the most feared. If someone corners the warrant market, short sellers can be forced to cover at ruinous prices. But Thorp dismissed this as unlikely: the AMEX and NYSE had regulations outlawing corners, and SEC scrutiny added another layer of deterrence. The nearest historical approach was the International Mining accumulation of Molybdenum warrants, which ultimately collapsed because the warrant mathematics worked against the buyers.

The widely believed myth that high short interest is bullish for a security was demonstrably false. The logic — "shorts must eventually cover, creating demand" — ignores that each short sale also creates supply. Sperry Rand warrants had the highest short interest ever recorded in a warrant (346,608 shares in December 1965), yet the warrant price fell from 8.50-9.375 to 6.50-7.875 over the next ten months, even as short interest rose further to 373,100. Three other warrants (Universal American, Bunker-Ramo, and Molybdenum) all fell steadily to zero despite large and growing short positions. When the New York Times reported that Mack Trucks warrant speculators had been "squeezed," the paper retracted the story the next day — they had miscalculated the conversion ratio.

Could the system survive another 1929? It already had, in the back-test. The hedged investor doubled his money during the crash. More importantly, as prices decline, more attractive short candidates appear and buying power is generated without additional capital. The position becomes more flexible, not less. A properly managed hedge can extend its downside safety limit all the way to zero by shorting additional warrants with the released margin.

Volatile price movements presented a theoretical risk if no intermediate adjustments were made. A gap opening from 10 to 0 (fraud) or from 10 to 45 (oil discovery) with no tradeable prices in between would prevent protective adjustment. Thorp acknowledged this but considered it extremely rare. For normal volatility, adjustment was straightforward: if the common falls, short additional warrants using generated buying power; if it rises, close the position and bank the profit.

"The things that can go wrong with a hedged position are few and unlikely."— Thorp & Kassouf

Thorp introduced the concept of desert isle strategies — positions so robust they would profit even if the investor set them and walked away until expiration with no monitoring. The simplified mechanical strategy from Chapter 7 was essentially a desert isle strategy: buy common, short warrants, hold to expiration, repeat. The profit range for most positions was wide enough that only extraordinary price movements (stock declining 90%+ or tripling) would produce a loss. For investors who wanted even more safety, adjustment was available: short additional warrants on declines using released buying power, or close the position and bank profits on rallies. The desert isle approach sacrificed some upside protection but eliminated the need for constant monitoring.

The more sophisticated investor could manage the position dynamically. If the common fell from 10 to 5, the warrant would drop to about 2, and the investor could short an additional 100 warrants at $2 using generated buying power without depositing new cash. The new position had 300 warrants short at an average of $3.33 against 100 shares long at $10. The downside safety now extended to zero — the stock could become worthless and the position would still profit. The upside limit dropped from 38 to 30, but with the stock at 5, a rise to 30 represented a six-fold increase and was an acceptable threshold. Dynamic management was optional but powerful.

Extensions of warrant privileges had occurred in only 2 of 44 AMEX warrants between 1946 and 1966 — Eureka Corporation and McCrory Corporation. Eureka's extension still resulted in a basic-system profit. McCrory's occurred ten years before expiration, outside the system's four-year window. The banning of short sales by the exchange was a more real concern: the AMEX frequently banned shorts in a specific warrant about six months before expiration when short interest was large. Investors already short were protected, but new entries were blocked. The lesson: don't wait too long to establish positions.

What about widespread adoption? If everyone used the system, warrant premiums would compress as shorts flooded the market. But this was self-correcting: if warrants became too cheap, the trade would reverse and reverse hedging would become profitable. The system contained its own equilibrium mechanism.

Chapter 10: Convertible Bonds and Latent Warrants

Everything in the first nine chapters applies to a tiny corner of the market — the 15 to 20 warrants listed on the AMEX. But the same principles extend to a vastly larger universe. As of October 1966, Standard & Poor's Bond Guide listed 336 actively traded convertible bonds with a total face value of $5 billion. A convertible bond is simply an ordinary bond with latent warrants embedded inside it. Strip out the bond's "investment worth" (what it would sell for without the conversion feature), and whatever remains is the value of the latent warrants. The same pricing framework, the same normal price curves, and the same hedging strategies all apply.

Collins Radio illustrated the analysis. The company had two convertible bonds outstanding: the 4.75% of 1980 and the 4.75% of 1983. The first, trading at $882.50 and convertible into 16.25 shares, had an investment worth of $880, leaving just $2.50 of conversion value — each of 16.25 latent warrants priced at about fifteen cents. The exercise price was $54.15, while the common stock sat at 25.625. These were deeply out-of-the-money options, essentially free insurance. The second bond, trading at $1,125 and convertible into 36.36 shares, had investment worth of $870, leaving $255 of warrant value — each of 36.36 latent warrants priced at $7.01. This one was much closer to the money.

Thorp executed a reverse hedge on the second Collins Radio bond. He bought six bonds (three through his broker at 70% margin, three via bank loan at 70% of price) and shorted 100 shares of common. Under Regulation T, no margin was required on the short because the bonds were convertible into more shares than he had shorted. The total cash investment was $3,390. Carry costs were essentially zero: the bonds' $262.50 annual coupon nearly offset the $204 annual interest on borrowed capital, and the dividend payment to the stock lender absorbed the remainder. When Collins Radio common surged to 65.50 by March 1966, the bonds rose to $2,450 each. Profit: $8,000 on bonds minus $4,100 on the short, netting $3,900 — a 115% return in nine months.

He then opened a second reverse hedge using the 4.75% of 1980 bond (the one with near-worthless latent warrants, now trading at 115 as the stock had risen). Six bonds purchased, 100 shares shorted at 65.50, total investment $6,930. This time the stock fell to 44. The bonds lost $1,400 but the short position gained $2,000, netting $600 — a 9% return in eight months. The two Collins Radio trades together demonstrated the essential asymmetry: the first profited when the stock advanced, the second profited when it declined.

"A profit results whether the common rises or falls."— Thorp & Kassouf

The Xerox 4% of 1984 bond demonstrated the basic system applied to latent warrants. The bond at $1,310 with investment worth of $910 contained ten latent warrants at $40 each, with an exercise price of $91. The common traded at 94 — just 3% above exercise price — yet the warrant was at 44% of exercise price, an unusually high premium for a latent warrant. Thorp bought 100 shares of Xerox common and shorted ten bonds. Six months later, with the stock at 143, the gain on common was $4,900 and the loss on bonds was $1,400, yielding a net $3,500 — 22% in half a year. Dresser Industries yielded 14% in one year using the same approach.

Finding the best candidates required the same standardized diagram used for pure warrants. Plotting S/E against W/E for all 178 listed convertible bonds on January 9, 1967, Thorp identified nine reverse-hedge candidates in the ideal zone: S/E between 0.8 and 1.2, with very low W/E near the "corner" of the minimum value line. Holly Sugar stood out as the best — so far below the average curve that profit was virtually guaranteed in either direction. The evaluation checklist was straightforward: conversion privilege extending past three years (Holly's ran to 1983), bond yield roughly matching the common dividend yield (so carry costs netted to zero), and the bond callable above current price (103.625 versus the purchase price of 87). Four months later the common rose from 18 to 34.50, the bond from 87 to 172, and the net profit was $1,725 — roughly 50% on $3,480 invested.

The data source for convertible bond analysis was the weekly Convertible Fact Finder published by Kalb, Voorhis & Co., which listed all 178 listed convertible bonds and over 147 OTC convertibles with full conversion terms. More than half of the OTC convertibles were convertible into listed common stocks, making them attractive reverse-hedge candidates since no short-selling difficulty existed on the warrant side — you were buying the convertible bond, not shorting it. The universe of opportunities was enormous compared to the small AMEX warrant market.

Latent warrants carry smaller premiums than pure warrants for three reasons: the inconvenience of buying an entire bond to access the option; the uncertainty of expiration, since corporations can call bonds at any time; and the fluctuating exercise price (tied to interest rates), whereas pure warrants have fixed exercise prices. These features make latent warrants less attractive for the basic system (short side) but more attractive for reverse hedging (long side), since the warrants are more likely to be underpriced. Holly Sugar was identified as the best reverse-hedge candidate in January 1967, with a position so favorable that profit was likely regardless of stock direction. Four months later, the common rose from 18 to 34.50 and the bond from 87 to 172 — a net profit of $1,725, roughly 50% on $3,480 invested.

Chapter 11: Deciphering Your Monthly Statement

Understanding brokerage account mechanics is not academic — it directly affects the returns achievable with the basic system. A brokerage account comprises three subaccounts: the cash account, the margin account, and the short account. The cash account handles fully paid transactions. The margin account tracks leveraged purchases, where the broker lends you part of the purchase price at a rate at least 1.5 percentage points above prime. The short account records all short sales and short covering, with the proceeds held as collateral by the lender of the borrowed securities.

The critical concept is buying power. When your total equity exceeds the initial margin requirement (70% of the market value of all securities held long and short), the difference is excess equity. This excess can be withdrawn as cash or invested in new securities. With $5,000 of equity and $6,000 of market value, the excess is $5,000 minus $4,200 (70% of $6,000) equals $800, which translates into $1,143 of buying power toward 70%-marginable securities. The basic system generates buying power automatically: when both the common and the warrant fall in price, the hedged portfolio loses little equity but the market value drops substantially, releasing margin for new positions.

The same-day substitution rule under Regulation T Section 220.3(g) allows a restricted account to sell one security and buy another of equal value on the same day without posting additional margin. This is essential for rolling from one basic-system position into another. Dividends and interest received in a margin account are deposited into the cash account and can be withdrawn freely, even if the margin account is restricted. This creates a "haven" for funds that would otherwise be absorbed into margin requirements.

The interplay between long and short positions created subtle opportunities. When both the common and the warrant fell in price, the hedged portfolio lost little equity because the gains on the short side offset losses on the long side. But the total market value of securities in the account dropped substantially, which reduced the initial margin requirement. The difference between unchanged equity and reduced margin requirement was excess equity — free buying power that could fund additional positions. This was the account-level mechanism behind the avalanche effect: the hedge preserved capital while declining prices unlocked leverage.

When prices rose, the opposite occurred. The market value increased faster than equity, making the account restricted. No excess equity existed and no new positions could be opened. But this situation often meant the hedge was profitable and should be closed. Upon closing, the proceeds could be reinvested the same day under the same-day substitution rule, either into a new basic-system position or into short-term bonds to preserve the buying power until a new opportunity appeared.

Restricted accounts had a valuable loophole: dividends and interest received on securities in the margin account were deposited into the cash account and could be withdrawn freely, regardless of the margin account's condition. The cash account functioned as a "haven" for income that would otherwise be absorbed into margin requirements. Smart investors used this to extract income from restricted positions without disturbing the hedge.

Equity in the short account should always be zero by definition. The credit balance in the short account should always equal the market value of the securities sold short. If the account was not regularly marked to the market, these balances could drift apart, leading to incorrect margin calculations and missed opportunities to offset interest charges. Thorp recommended requesting regular marking from the broker, since the adjustment could transfer funds from the short account to the margin account, reducing the debit balance and cutting interest costs.

Marking to the market keeps the short account's credit balance equal to the current market value of the securities sold short. If the shorted security falls in price, the lender returns excess collateral to the margin account, potentially offsetting interest charges on debit balances. Smart investors request regular marking to reduce borrowing costs. Equity in the short account should always be zero by definition — all equity belongs to the margin account.

Chapter 12: Practical Considerations

Thorp demonstrates how to squeeze every dollar from the basic system through portfolio management techniques. When closing a Sperry Rand position — selling 200 common at 27.125 and covering warrants at 10 and 10.50 — he had $9,569.27 in buying power. Rather than let it sit idle, he immediately opened a new Pacific Petroleums position (buying 200 common at 11.50, shorting 200 warrants at 5.375) and parked the remaining $5,988 in six Pennsylvania Railroad bonds at 99.125, earning 5% interest. The bonds served purely as a buying-power preservation vehicle — their commission was just $1.25 per $1,000 bond versus $2.50 for normal trades. Total capital wasted: only $193.73.

Counterintuitively, a decline in the common stock can be advantageous. When the common falls from 16 to 10 over six months, the warrant with 14 months remaining drops to about 2.50. The direct profit is only $100, barely covering commissions. But the decline releases equity: with 70% margin, a $2,120 position now requires only $1,200 in margin, freeing $1,020 for reinvestment. Even better, the warrant-common pair has moved to a more favorable position on the normal price curve, so the expected return going forward is now much higher. The avalanche effect works in reverse: declining prices continuously release margin, enabling pyramiding downward into increasingly attractive opportunities.

Diversification with the basic system is straightforward. With two equally attractive investments, splitting capital equally preserves the same average payoff while cutting the probability of total loss. A $1,000 bet on a single 50/50 double-or-nothing proposition averages $1,500. The same $1,000 split between two independent such bets has the same average but only a 25% chance of losing both, versus 50% chance of losing the single bet. Hedging itself is an unusually efficient form of diversification — two correlated instruments, long one and short the other.

Thorp recommends opening a separate brokerage account for each basic-system position. When two positions share one account, they offset each other: margin released by a falling position automatically covers the rising one, negating both the avalanche effect and the margin release benefit. Separate accounts preserve these advantages independently. The tradeoff is more paperwork and difficulty tracking the overall portfolio, but for a system generating 25% annually, the administrative burden is well worth it.

The advantage of operating on small margin was counterintuitive. Normally, small margin is extremely risky — a 30% decline wipes out the equity. But the basic system's hedge meant that moves in either direction rarely produced losses. When the stock declined, the warrant short gained; when the stock rose, the common long gained. The combination was stable even at high leverage. Small margin became a virtue rather than a danger, amplifying the 25% annual return into something considerably higher for investors willing to use their full buying power.

Short-term listed bonds served an unexpected role as buying-power preservation vehicles. When Thorp closed the Sperry Rand position and opened the Pacific Petroleums position on the same day, he had $5,988 of buying power left over. Rather than let it sit idle, he parked it in six Pennsylvania Railroad bonds at 99.125 — bonds due December 1, 1968, paying 5% interest. The commission was only $1.25 per $1,000 bond, versus $2.50 for normal stock trades. A round trip cost roughly 0.25%. The bonds held the buying power in place, earning interest, until the next opportunity appeared. Only $193.73 of the original capital was wasted in the transition.

The logic of maintaining separate accounts deserves emphasis. In a single account with two basic-system positions, one position might be declining (releasing margin) while the other rises (consuming margin). The released margin from the declining position would be automatically applied to cover the rising position's margin needs — destroying both the avalanche effect on the declining side and the buying-power release. With separate accounts, each position operated independently. The declining one could pyramid additional shorts, while the rising one could be closed at a profit and rolled into something new.

Tax optimization matters. Short-sale profits are always taxed as ordinary income, but common stock held longer than six months qualifies for long-term capital gains at a maximum rate of 25%. When a basic-system position shows profit on the common component and the warrant has been covered, holding the common for the full six months before selling can save meaningful tax dollars. Positions approaching the six-month mark deserve special attention: slightly longer holding periods may generate disproportionate after-tax returns.

Chapter 13: Why We Are Sharing the Secret

The practical question is obvious: if the system works, why publish it instead of simply getting rich? Thorp and Kassouf explored every alternative. They could manage others' money for a fee, but SEC regulations required anyone compensated by 15 or more clients to register as an investment advisor — and registered advisors were prohibited from profit-sharing arrangements. The prohibition existed because unscrupulous advisors could put each client in a different volatile stock, collect a share of the winners, and bear no cost for the losers. Fee-only advisors charged about 0.5% of principal, or $5,000 per year on a million-dollar account — hardly compelling.

Recruiting wealthy clients proved impossible. There were 35,000 Americans with annual income exceeding $1 million in 1964, but the SEC prohibited soliciting them without registration. Those who heard the pitch informally were deeply skeptical. An oil baron earning over $1 million per year was unimpressed by 25% — he expected 50% from his own business and was perpetually cash-hungry. A millionaire friend who had watched his portfolio shrink from $1 million to $400,000 in the 1966 crash invested $20,000 with Thorp and acknowledged it was a "sure thing" — but considered four to five years too slow to recover his losses. He preferred the same hot tips that had cost him $600,000, believing the market would "give back" his losses just as fast.

"You can figure blackjack odds but the stock market is psychology — you can't figure that."— a skeptical potential client

Thorp's rebuttal was characteristically precise: even poker, a game of pure psychology and bluffing, has been solved mathematically. Optimal bluffing strategies provably outperform any opponent over the long run, as demonstrated by von Neumann and Morgenstern in Theory of Games and Economic Behavior. The objection that "the market is psychology, not math" confused the nature of the edge. The basic system did not require predicting psychology. It required identifying a mathematical relationship between two prices and exploiting the gap when that relationship deviated from its theoretical value. Whether the deviation was caused by psychology, ignorance, or institutional constraints was irrelevant to the profit.

Arthur Koestler's The Sleepwalkers provided the analogy Thorp found most apt. Economics and investment were at a pre-Newtonian stage — superstition was being swept away by scientific data analysis, but the process was far from complete. Most market participants still relied on folklore, ritual language, and authority figures rather than quantitative evidence. The basic system was an early example of what the scientific revolution would eventually bring to finance: testable hypotheses, empirical validation, and reproducible results.

The real reason to publish was strategic: the system would inevitably be rediscovered. Kassouf's dissertation had circulated thousands of copies. Sidney Fried's warrant service had begun including basic-system hedges. Paul Samuelson at MIT had studied warrants for eleven years. At an Air Force scientific conference, Thorp merely described "25% returns with high safety" and Colonel Beckham in the audience immediately guessed "warrant hedging." The secret was leaking. Better to be the ones who told it properly.

The anecdotes of rejection revealed as much about investor psychology as about the system. The oil baron who dismissed 25% returns was anchored to his own business returns of 50%+, failing to recognize that his oil income came with enormous concentration risk while the basic system's 25% came with near-zero probability of loss. The millionaire who invested only $20,000 despite acknowledging the system was a "sure thing" was exhibiting the same behavior that had cost him $600,000 — preferring the excitement of hot tips to the tedium of systematic profit.

The profit-sharing prohibition revealed a deeper irony in the regulatory framework. The SEC banned profit-sharing to prevent abusive practices, but the ban also prevented competent managers from aligning their incentives with their clients'. An advisor earning a flat fee had no financial stake in the client's returns. An advisor sharing in profits would work hardest for the clients who needed the most help. Thorp would eventually solve this problem by founding a limited partnership — the hedge fund structure that permitted performance fees outside the investment advisor framework. But in 1967, that solution was still two years away.

Those who had independently discovered warrant hedging typically used imperfect versions and got mediocre results. The precise mix ratio — how many warrants to short per share of common — was the key they missed. Without it, hedges were either too aggressive (too many warrants short, vulnerable to stock surges) or too conservative (too few warrants, leaving profit on the table). The six-step checklist and the normal price curve framework were the missing pieces that transformed a known concept into a reliable system.

Chapter 14: What the Future Holds

Widespread adoption would compress warrant premiums and eventually destroy the basic system's edge. But how much capital would it take? As of October 1966, the three best situations — Pacific Petroleums, Sperry Rand, and Universal American — could absorb about $4.6 million of basic-system investment before the AMEX would likely intervene. Even at 50% of warrants outstanding sold short, the upper limit was roughly $15.3 million. These are small numbers, but Thorp drew a direct parallel to his blackjack experience: Beat the Dealer had been published for years and the card-counting system still worked, because only a handful of people actually had the discipline and capital to execute it.

The self-defeating mechanism had an important corollary: the system was self-correcting. If basic-system users compressed warrant premiums below fair value, the trade would reverse. Underpriced warrants would create reverse hedge opportunities — buying the cheap warrants and shorting the common. The mathematical framework identified both overpricing and underpricing with equal precision. An investor who understood the normal price curves could profit on whichever side of the mispricing the market offered. The system would not be "ruined" so much as transformed.

Beyond the basic system, the entire system encompassed a vastly larger opportunity set. Convertible bonds and convertible preferreds represented $5 billion in face value. Puts and calls, OTC and Canadian warrants, and foreign options added further depth. These markets were large enough that widespread exploitation was many years away. Even if every AMEX-listed warrant were efficiently priced, the convertible bond market alone provided hundreds of latent warrant situations with the same mathematical structure.

The analogy to blackjack was instructive. Beat the Dealer had been published in 1962, revealing the card-counting system in complete detail. Four years later, the system still worked because only a handful of people had the discipline, bankroll, and willingness to execute it under casino conditions. The stock market presented even higher barriers: most institutional funds were not permitted to sell short, individual investors lacked the mathematical training to construct proper hedges, and the cultural stigma against short selling deterred all but the most determined. Thorp estimated the basic system would remain profitable for several more years at minimum.

The grand ambition was even larger. Thorp believed that computers would eventually enable methods for predicting the prices of major common stocks — the 30 Dow Jones industrials, for example — with enough accuracy to produce a 20% or greater annual edge. Groups were already attempting this in the late 1960s. The warrant hedging system was, in Thorp's view, merely the first successful application of scientific method to the stock market. The future held much more.

The appendices provided the mathematical foundations for readers who wanted the proofs. Appendix C offered a formal demonstration that hedging produces high expected returns under very weak assumptions — the only requirement was that stock prices not systematically drift downward. Under 70% margin on both legs, the expected return was at least 40%. Appendix D presented Kassouf's warrant pricing formula: y equals (z-squared plus 1) raised to (1/z) minus 1, where z incorporated time, dividends, dilution, trend, and stock price. For Sperry Rand in November 1966, the model predicted $9.98 versus the actual $8.125, confirming the warrant was underpriced relative to fair value. The mathematical rigor behind these appendices separated Beat the Market from every prior book on warrant investing.

The capacity question had precise numbers. On October 14, 1966, total existing short interest in the three best situations — Pacific Petroleums, Sperry Rand, and Universal American — was far below what the market could absorb. If all existing shorts were basic-system positions at a 3-to-1 mix, the total investment was only $4.6 million. Even at the upper bound — short interest at 50% of warrants outstanding, the level at which the AMEX would likely ban further short sales — the market could hold $15.3 million. These were modest sums that a few dozen sophisticated investors could deploy without distorting prices. The basic system's capacity was limited, but within those limits, it was robust.

The historical data in Appendix E tracked every listed warrant from 1946 to 1966, plotting average monthly returns against time to expiration for naked shorts and various hedge ratios. The pattern was consistent: warrants declined faster as expiration approached, hedges reduced variance substantially while preserving positive expected returns, and higher mix ratios increased both expected return and risk. The data provided empirical validation that the theoretical framework was not merely elegant but accurate. The basic system worked not because of luck or favorable market conditions, but because warrants were systematically overpriced and time decay was a mathematical certainty.

Trading & Investing Lessons

Hedging Is the Original Market-Neutral Strategy

The basic system is the ancestor of every long-short equity fund and market-neutral strategy that followed. Thorp's insight — don't predict direction, exploit relative mispricing — anticipated by decades the approach that would make Renaissance Technologies and Citadel billions. For any trader, the lesson is powerful: the best edge often comes not from predicting where a stock will go, but from identifying where the relationship between two correlated instruments has deviated from fair value.

Time Decay Is the Short Seller's Best Friend

Warrants lose value as expiration approaches, just like modern options. Thorp was selling theta decades before the Greeks had names. Options sellers today face the same dynamics: near-expiration options with high implied volatility are the most attractive candidates for selling, and the time decay accelerates nonlinearly as expiration approaches. The basic system is essentially a covered warrant write — the intellectual ancestor of the covered call strategy.

The Avalanche Effect Is Compounding on Steroids

Reinvesting released margin into additional short positions as the trade works in your favor is a form of geometric compounding that most investors never access. The principle applies broadly: any declining asset that releases collateral can fund additional positions. In modern markets, traders who scale into winning positions using freed-up margin are applying Thorp's avalanche in spirit if not in name.

Liquidity Risk Is Real

Thorp's buy-in on Molybdenum warrants and the Lynch Corporation fiasco both demonstrate that theoretical profits mean nothing if you cannot execute. Before entering any position — long or short — verify that the market is liquid enough to let you in and, crucially, to let you out. In modern terms: check the bid-ask spread, the average daily volume, and the short interest as a percentage of float before shorting anything.

Separate Accounts for Separate Strategies

Thorp's recommendation to maintain separate brokerage accounts for each position has a modern analogue: mental accounting is sometimes real accounting. When positions share an account, gains and losses offset each other in ways that distort margin calculations and obscure true performance. Traders running multiple strategies should consider isolating them — whether through separate subaccounts, separate brokers, or at least separate tracking.

Publication Doesn't Destroy an Edge Immediately

Both Beat the Dealer and Beat the Market published their methods in full and the strategies continued working for years afterward. Only a small fraction of people who read about an edge actually execute it with discipline. This is equally true in modern markets: well-known anomalies like the January effect, momentum, and value persist for years after academic publication because implementation requires conviction, capital, and patience that most investors lack.

Key Takeaways

Predict Relationships, Not Prices

The foundational insight: stop trying to forecast where individual stocks will go. Instead, exploit the predictable mathematical relationship between a derivative (warrant) and its underlying (common stock). This is the philosophical core of all derivatives-based hedging.

Systematic Overpricing Creates Systematic Opportunity

Near-expiration warrants are almost always overpriced because speculators pay premiums for leverage and lottery-ticket appeal. The average 16-month return from buying warrants was -46%. This is not a random anomaly — it is a structural feature of how humans price optionality, and it persists in modern options markets.

The Hedge Eliminates Directional Risk

By combining a short position in overpriced warrants with a long position in the underlying stock, the investor profits whether the stock rises, falls, or stays flat. The only losing scenario requires an extreme, rare price movement that prevents intermediate adjustment. In 20 years of back-tested data, no such scenario occurred.

The Avalanche Effect Multiplies Returns

Reinvesting released margin as shorted securities decline produces geometric compounding. Thorp turned $6,500 into $84,292 on a single trade using this technique. The mathematics are exact: P = V0 times (W0/Wf) raised to (1/m) minus 1.

Convertible Securities Massively Expand the Opportunity Set

The same analysis applies to convertible bonds, convertible preferreds, calls, puts, and straddles. The $5 billion convertible bond market dwarfed the tiny warrant market and provided hundreds of additional candidates for both basic-system and reverse-hedge positions.

Account Mechanics Are Not Trivial

Understanding margin calculations, buying power, same-day substitution rules, and the short account's relationship to the margin account is essential for maximizing returns. Margin errors on mixed accounts are common; always verify your own calculations. Separate accounts for separate positions preserve the avalanche effect and margin release benefits.

Self-Correcting Equilibrium

If the basic system is widely adopted and warrant premiums compress, the trade reverses: reverse hedging (buying underpriced warrants) becomes profitable. The system contains its own equilibrium mechanism. Markets correct for both overpricing and underpricing; the scientific investor adapts to whichever mispricing currently exists.

Contrarian Ideas

High Short Interest Is Not Bullish

Wall Street folklore insists that high short interest signals pent-up buying demand. Thorp demolished this with data: Sperry Rand warrants had record-high short interest and the price fell steadily. Three other warrants fell to zero despite large short positions. Each short sale creates supply as well as future demand; the effects cancel.

Fundamental Analysis Is Insufficient

Being right about a stock's intrinsic value does not guarantee profits. Kassouf's carefully selected fundamental picks (Columbia Broadcasting, General Dynamics) lost money while speculative "cats and dogs" soared. The market must agree with your valuation, and it may refuse to for years. The basic system sidesteps this problem entirely by avoiding directional bets.

Safe Investments Don't Require Low Returns

The standard risk-return tradeoff says higher returns require higher risk. Thorp's system earned 25% annually with near-zero probability of loss — not by taking hidden risks, but by exploiting a structural mispricing that the market had not yet learned to correct. Risk and return are linked for passive investors; active investors with genuine insight can break the tradeoff.

Chart Reading Is No Better Than Random

Thorp tested technical analysts by showing them half-charts and asking for predictions. Results were no better than guessing. Dow Theory followers from 1926-1960 averaged 9% annually, while random buy-and-sell timing produced the same result. Chartism feels scientific but lacks empirical support.

Short Selling Is Conservative, Not Reckless

Wall Street mythology portrays short selling as dangerous and almost unpatriotic. In the context of a hedge, short selling is the opposite: it is the conservative leg that protects against downside. The basic system's safety comes precisely from the short position.

Proposals & Frameworks

The Basic System: Six-Step Checklist

  1. Identify all listed warrants (AMEX focus, scan for "wt" suffix in newspaper listings)
  2. Limit to warrants expiring in less than four years
  3. Eliminate if stock price exceeds 1.2 times adjusted exercise price
  4. Consider only warrants at or above the normal price curve for their maturity
  5. Set mix at 3-to-1 (short 300 warrants per 100 shares long) when S/E is between 0.3 and 1.2; adjust for risk tolerance
  6. Open one to several positions depending on available capital; prefer nearest-expiration warrant for fastest recycling

Reverse Hedge: Four Criteria

  1. Stock within 20% of adjusted exercise price (warrant near the "corner")
  2. Warrant has at least four years to expiration
  3. Warrant is underpriced relative to the average curve
  4. The more volatile the common stock, the better

Kassouf's Warrant Pricing Formula

The empirical formula from Kassouf's doctoral dissertation: y = (z-squared plus 1) raised to (1/z) minus 1, where z incorporates time to expiration, dividend yield, dilution, trend relative to 11-month average, and current stock price. This predicted warrant prices with enough accuracy to identify mispricings for both basic-system and reverse-hedge opportunities. For Sperry Rand in November 1966, the model predicted a warrant price of $9.98 versus the actual $8.125 — confirming the warrant was cheap and a basic-system opportunity existed.

Avalanche Effect Formula

P = V-zero times [(W-zero / W-final) raised to (1 / margin-fraction) minus 1]. This quantifies the compound return from continuously reinvesting released margin as a shorted security declines. The formula reveals exponential sensitivity to the margin fraction: lower margin requirements produce dramatically higher returns for the same price decline.

The Mix-Selection Framework

S/E below 0.3: short warrants only, no common purchase needed. S/E between 0.3 and 1.2: use a 3-to-1 mix, matching the average slope of the normal price curves. S/E above 1.2: do not use the basic system. This framework reduces the continuous judgment of mix selection to three simple zones.

What Didn't Fit

The Loan Clerk Problem

The difficulty of shorting warrants often had nothing to do with genuine scarcity. Thorp discovered that whether a security appeared "scarce" or "available" depended heavily on the competence and motivation of the broker's loan clerk. Many loan clerks were simply too lazy to call enough other firms to locate borrowable certificates. A clerk who made four phone calls and heard "no" would declare the security unborrowable, while an aggressive clerk at another firm could locate the same certificates in fifteen minutes. This is why Thorp maintained accounts at multiple brokerages — the bottleneck was not supply but the human intermediary who connected borrowers to lenders. The NYSE was developing a computerized "control-certificate" system that would eventually eliminate this problem by matching borrowers and lenders automatically.

The IBM Avalanche Thought Experiment

To illustrate the theoretical extremes of the avalanche effect, Thorp calculated what would happen if IBM stock fell from $500 to $5 while an investor continuously reinvested released margin from short sales. With 50% margin, a $250 initial investment would yield nearly $1.7 million. With 25% margin, the same $250 could theoretically produce $2 billion. The numbers are absurd — they assume continuous reinvestment with no friction, no margin changes, and a stock declining 99% without bankruptcy. But they perfectly illustrate how exponential compounding from margin reinvestment differs qualitatively from simple percentage returns. The formula is exact; only the assumptions are extreme.

The Straddle Writer's Edge

Writing straddles (selling both a put and a call at the same striking price) produces a payoff profile nearly identical to the basic system. Thorp demonstrated this with a United Artists straddle in July 1966: an option broker bid $400 for a 65-day straddle at the market price. The writer posted $909 in margin. Over the next 65 days, United Artists fluctuated narrowly and closed at 43 versus the 43.625 striking price — both options expired worthless and the writer kept the entire $400 premium, a 44% return in 65 days. The risk was that unlike a basic-system position, the straddle seller cannot control when the investment terminates — the option holder can exercise at any time.

Perpetual Warrants and Tri-Continental

The most dramatic returns in warrant history belong to perpetual warrants — those with no expiration date. Tri-Continental Corporation's perpetual warrants cost three cents in 1942 and were worth $47.375 by 1965, a return of 157,900%. A $1,000 investment became $1,575,000. The common stock returned a mere 7,300% over the same period. Perpetual warrants magnify returns so dramatically because they never suffer time decay — the property that makes expiring warrants such good short candidates. The irony: the same feature that makes perpetual warrants spectacular investments makes them terrible basic-system candidates, since there is no expiration date to force the overpricing to resolve.

The Margin Error Epidemic

Thorp reported that margin calculation errors on mixed accounts (simultaneously long and short) were common among brokerage clerks. During the Bunker-Ramo/Teleregister trade, he received an erroneous margin call that the head margin clerk later reversed. His observation: margin errors on mixed accounts tend to be unbiased — about as often in the investor's favor as against it. But the investor who cannot verify the math will occasionally sell a position prematurely to meet a phantom call, or miss an opportunity to reinvest excess buying power because the broker failed to flag it. The lesson is that brokerage accounting is not infallible, and serious hedgers must be able to calculate their own margin requirements from first principles.

About the Author

Edward O. Thorp is a mathematician, author, and hedge fund manager who spent his career proving that rigorous quantitative analysis could beat systems everyone assumed were random. After earning his Ph.D. in mathematics from UCLA, he became a professor at MIT and then UC Irvine. His first book, Beat the Dealer (1962), demonstrated that blackjack could be beaten with card counting — the first mathematically proven gambling system. Casinos changed their rules in response. He then turned to the stock market, co-authoring Beat the Market with economist Sheen T. Kassouf in 1967.

Sheen T. Kassouf was an economist who earned his doctorate from Columbia University with a dissertation on warrant pricing — the first mathematical and computer analysis of listed warrant prices. His empirical formula for predicting warrant values, derived from data spanning 1945 to 1965, provided the theoretical foundation for the basic system. His earlier book, Evaluation of Convertible Securities (1962), had begun spreading awareness of hedging strategies before his collaboration with Thorp refined them into a complete system.

In 1969, two years after Beat the Market was published, Thorp founded Princeton Newport Partners, one of the first quantitative hedge funds. It earned 19.8% annually over 19 years with only three losing months. Thorp later managed Ridgeline Partners, which earned 18% annually from 1994 to 2002. He is widely regarded as the father of quantitative finance — a title earned by actually doing it, not merely theorizing about it.

Why This Book Matters

Beat the Market is one of the most important books in the history of quantitative finance. Published in 1967, it laid the intellectual groundwork for the Black-Scholes options pricing model (1973) by demonstrating empirically that options were systematically mispriced and that mathematical models could quantify the mispricing. Thorp and Kassouf were pricing warrants using computers and probability theory six years before Black, Scholes, and Merton published their famous formula. In many ways, Black-Scholes formalized what Thorp had already been doing profitably.

The book's deeper significance is philosophical. It proved that markets are not perfectly efficient — that structural mispricings exist, can be identified with rigorous analysis, and can be exploited with disciplined execution. This was heresy in 1967, when the efficient market hypothesis was ascending in academia. Thorp did not merely argue against efficiency; he produced audited returns that refuted it. His subsequent hedge fund career, spanning decades and multiple vehicles with consistent high returns, vindicated the approach conclusively.

For modern traders and investors, the specific warrant-hedging techniques are historical curiosities. But the principles are timeless: exploit relative mispricing rather than directional prediction; use hedging to isolate the edge from market noise; let time decay work for you, not against you; and understand the plumbing of your brokerage account well enough to maximize every dollar of buying power. These ideas, first articulated in this slim 239-page volume, went on to power an entire industry.
