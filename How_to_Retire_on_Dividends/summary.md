# How to Retire on Dividends

**Authors:** Brett Owens & Tom Jacobs
**Year:** 2019
**Pages:** 171

## Summary

Brett Owens and Tom Jacobs make a deceptively simple argument:
the conventional retirement framework is broken, and the fix
is already sitting in plain sight. The 4% safe withdrawal rate
forces retirees to sell shares — more shares when prices are low,
fewer when high — a compounding death spiral called reverse
dollar-cost averaging. The standard dividend aristocrat approach
yields 2–3%, leaving a millionaire earning poverty wages.

Their alternative is the **8% No Withdrawal Portfolio**: a
deliberately constructed income machine yielding 8% or more,
funded entirely by dividends, so the principal never gets
touched. The vehicle is largely the closed-end fund (CEF) —
a structure most retail investors ignore, which allows them
to buy $1.00 of assets for $0.85–$0.92 by exploiting
persistent discount-to-NAV windows created by the CEF's
fixed share pool structure.

The intellectual framework is Howard Marks' second-level
thinking: going where the crowd doesn't, buying what's
unloved, and understanding what's actually priced in.
Applied to dividends: when a REIT's yield spikes to its
historical ceiling because of scary headlines, that's
the entry signal — not the exit.

The book covers six asset classes in detail: bond CEFs,
preferred shares, covered call funds, equity CEFs,
REITs (healthcare, industrial, commercial), and dividend
growth stocks. It closes with a nine-chapter playbook:
15–20 positions, 50% stocks and 50% bond CEFs, no
rebalancing, sell only when fundamentals change, use
covered calls as an optional income accelerator.

The advantage belongs exclusively to small investors.
Warren Buffett has $111 billion in Berkshire cash because
he can't deploy it at scale. Jeffrey Gundlach manages $120B.
CalPERS has $200B to move. None of them can buy a $3B REIT
or a $1.5B CEF without moving the price. You can.
