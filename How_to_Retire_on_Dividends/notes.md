### Dividend "Aristocrats" Aren't (What They Seem)

**Definition** (Investopedia): A dividend aristocrat has continuously

  - Even in the 2008–09 crash JNJ *still didn't pay 4%*

### The "4% Withdrawal" Fallacy

**Origin**: **William Bengen**, aeronautical engineer (MIT grad),
became a financial planner. His clients kept asking what they could
safely withdraw in retirement.

- Bengen tested a 50/50 stocks-and-bonds mix from **1926 to 1976**:
  a 4% annual withdrawal never exhausted a portfolio in under 33 years
- Became the **"Bengen Rule"** / **Safe Withdrawal Rate (SWR)**;
  validated by the **Trinity Study**
- Bengen later updated to 4.5% adjusted annually for inflation, but
  most advisors still stuck on the original 4% maxim
- Why advisors don't customize: Wells Fargo, Merrill Lynch need rules
  that apply across thousands of clients and trillions in AUM — they
  pick the most conservative approach to avoid liability

**Why the 4% rule is dangerous:**
- It *requires* selling principal, not just living on income
- In bear markets, you must sell *more* shares to make up the income
  shortfall — at exactly the wrong time
- **Microsoft 2008–09 example**: Dividend rose 18%, but share price
  fell 56% peak-to-trough. A retiree following 4% rule was forced to
  sell at the bottom.
- Dollar-cost averaging (that *built* your wealth by buying low and
  high) becomes **reverse dollar-cost averaging** in withdrawal: you
  sell more when prices are low, decimating upside when markets recover
- As you deplete principal, there's less capital to generate dividends
  — a compounding death spiral

**The alternative**: The **"8% No Withdrawal Portfolio"** — earn 8%
yield so you withdraw only income, never principal. Account grows
instead of shrinks.

> "Rather than dust off Bengen's formula and model a custom
> spreadsheet for every client, the advisor will simply 'toggle' the
> risk meter up or down by ticking the yearly withdrawal rate up or
> down." — *Owens & Jacobs on advisor laziness*

## Chapter 3: Our Edge: Second-Level Thinking

### Howard Marks and Second-Level Thinking

- **Howard Marks**, chief of **Oaktree Capital Group** ($120B AUM), 40+ years investing; *Forbes* Billionaires list regular
- His *Oaktree Memos* described by *Business Insider* as "Michael Lewis ghostwriting for Warren Buffett"
- Key book: **_The Most Important Thing: Uncommon Sense for the Thoughtful Investor_** — endorsed by **Joel Greenblatt, Jeremy Grantham, Seth Klarman**, and **Warren Buffett**
- **First-level thinking**: "It's a good company; buy the stock" — what everyone does
- **Second-level thinking**: goes deeper — what does everyone else already think? What's priced in? What's the surprise?

> "First-level thinking says, 'The outlook stinks, let's dump our stocks.' Second-level thinking says, 'The outlook stinks, but everyone else is selling in panic. Buy!'" — Marks, *The Most Important Thing*, p. 4

- Oaktree's second-level moves: raised **$10.9B distressed debt fund in 2008** (largest to date); earned clients **19% annualized after fees** on distressed debt funds
- Marks made himself a billionaire twice over with this approach; Oaktree serves institutional/ultra-HNW only

### Applying Second-Level Thinking to Dividend Investing

- First-level income seekers find a 4% yield and buy; second-level thinkers ask: is this sustainable? What's the payout ratio? Is there a cheaper way in?
- "Buy and hope" investors pick stocks and pray for price appreciation — no plan for generating cash flow
- The **age-based bond allocation rule** (hold bonds = your age in %) is classic first-level thinking — ignores individual circumstances, inflation, longevity
- Second-level version: buy dividend-growing stocks where rising dividends drive price appreciation — counters inflation, avoids longevity risk
- **Dividends = ~50% of stock market's long-term returns** (Hartford Funds data) → higher-yielding portfolio should structurally outperform S&P
- Key principle: **stock prices are irrelevant** to an income investor focused on cash flow — price volatility only matters if you're forced to sell
- Avoid "stalwart" dividend payers with commodity exposure: **Exxon, Chevron, BP** stocks were crushed when oil fell below $30/barrel — dividends held but capital didn't
- Target: unloved payers whose underlying business is fine and dividends continue like clockwork
- **Net buyers of stocks want lower prices** (accumulation phase); only net sellers need high prices

## Chapter 4: A Better Way to Buy Bonds: Closed-End Funds

### Why Standard Bond Vehicles Fail

- US 10-year Treasury bond yields just **2.8%** = $28,000/year on $1M — not enough for retirement
- Popular bond mutual funds and ETFs (**Vanguard, Fidelity, iShares**) fail on two counts:
  1. Don't pay enough (need 6–8%+ for No Withdrawal portfolio)
  2. Trade at fair value — no discount, no upside for contrarians

### What Are Closed-End Funds (CEFs)?

- **CEF**: pooled fund with active manager, shares trade on open market like stocks
- Critical structural difference from mutual funds/ETFs: **fixed share count** — managers cannot issue new shares to meet demand
- Investors must sell shares to other buyers (like stocks) — managers never forced to sell positions to meet redemptions
- This means CEF managers can **ride out panics** without dumping good assets at distressed prices
- CEFs can trade at **premiums or discounts to NAV** (net asset value = liquidation value of assets minus debt)

### The Discount-to-NAV Advantage

- When a CEF falls out of favor, its fixed share count means it can trade at **10–20% discounts to NAV**
- Buying at a 10% discount = buying $1 of assets for $0.90 — structural "free money" (assets marked to market)
- Two benefits of buying CEFs at discounts:
  1. **Higher effective yield**: a fund earning 5% on NAV yields 5.5–5.6% to buyers at discount
  2. **Price upside**: discount-to-NAV window frequently closes, generating capital gains on top of income
- CEF managers also have **wider mandates** than mutual funds — more flexibility to generate alpha
- CEFs can **borrow cheaply at LIBOR** (~2%) and invest proceeds for higher returns — the spread between borrowing cost and yield turns good returns into great ones

### Three Fatal Flaws of Bond ETFs

**Flaw #1: Underperformance**
- **iShares iBoxx High Yield Corporate Bond ETF (HYG)**: $15B in assets, pays 5.3%, but **lost 2% total return in 2018** (dividends included)
- ETFs are marketing products designed to attract assets for managers, not maximize investor returns
- **New America High Income Fund (HYB)** — the CEF alternative — outperformed HYG by **34 percentage points since 2007**: HYB +115% vs HYG +81%
- HYB 30-year track record: **9.5% annualized**, most returns paid as cash
- HYB current yield: **8.3%**, trades at **13.8% discount to NAV** (buy $1 of bonds for ~$0.86)
- HYG: 5.3% yield, trades at par (100 cents on dollar)

**Flaw #2: Ranking the Worst First (Bond Indexing Problem)**
- Bond indexes weighted by amount of debt outstanding — the more a company borrows, the bigger its index weight
- This is *the opposite* of sound credit analysis: maximizes exposure to the most indebted issuers
- Example: HYG's 6th largest holding = **Sprint (S)** bonds maturing 2023 — a highly indebted telecom
- Active CEF managers make credit quality judgments; passive ETFs just follow the debt issuance
- Active management in fixed income (unlike equities) *can* and does add real value

**Flaw #3: False Sense of Liquidity**
- Bond ETFs offer instant liquidity (sell anytime) but underlying bonds are often **illiquid**
- During panic selling: ETF must sell its bond holdings at market prices to fund redemptions
- If underlying bonds are illiquid (like Sprint paper), ETF may have to sell at **70–80 cents on dollar** — crushing NAV
- CEFs: **fixed asset pools** — no forced selling during panics; managers can hold and wait
- CEFs' three structural advantages over ETFs:
  1. Actively managed by pros with genuine edge
  2. Fixed pools allow discount-to-NAV purchases (safety + upside)
  3. Can use leverage cheaply via LIBOR borrowing to amplify returns

### The Upside of Stocks from Bonds (cont.)

- CEF price upside comes two ways: (1) **discount window** narrows/closes as price
  catches up to NAV; (2) **NAV itself rises** (liquidation value of underlying assets)
- Buy at $0.90 for $1.00 of actual investment value — margin of safety built in
- Target funds that are "unfairly out of favor" with big discounts

### PIMCO Dynamic Credit and Mortgage Fund (PCI) — Case Study

- Dividend ranged 8–9%+; earned **68% total returns** in ~2 years (Jul'16–Jul'18)
- Three-part win: distributions collected + NAV gains + discount window closing
  (eventually flipping to a **premium**)
- Strategy: hold and collect monthly dividend when at fair value;
  buy more aggressively when discount re-opens
- When a fund rallies to too rich a premium (e.g. paying $1.10 for $1.00 of assets)
  → **sell**
- Example exit: **Nuveen NASDAQ 100 Dynamic Overwrite Fund (QQQX)** — sold
  at 41% total return when discount flipped to aggressive premium

### Why CEFs Create Bargains (Fixed Share Pool)

- Mutual funds and ETFs issue new shares on demand → price tracks NAV closely
- CEFs have **fixed share pool** → prices trade like stocks, can diverge from NAV
- This structural quirk creates persistent mispricings most investors miss
- Contrarian edge: buy diversified bond portfolios at steep discounts to fair value

#### Rule 4: Only Buy CEFs That Trade at a Discount

- Always demand a discount; few funds deserve a premium; those that do can
  usually be bought at a discount eventually
- **Price always matters** — even for dividend investors

- Two Gundlach funds, identical mandate, same manager

**Why discounts boost yields:**
- CEF sets distribution as % of its portfolio value
- Buying at a discount = same payout for less money = higher effective yield
- DSL at 7% discount + 1.44% fee = fee effectively "comped" (net ~3% free assets)

  - **Gabelli Utility Trust (GUT)**: 31% premium → $1.31
- Paying a premium is the **wrong approach to income investing** — you're already

#### Rule 5: Watch Out for Discount Traps

- A discount alone is insufficient — need a *catalyst* to close the window
- **BlackRock Resources & Commodity Fund (BCX)**: persistent 10%+ discount
  but down -25% since inception — "worse than a piggy bank"
- "Value investing" in CEFs fails just like cheap stocks — some are cheap
  for good reason (**value trap**)
- Check: has the discount window *ever* closed historically? If not, it may
  be stuck permanently

#### Rule 6: Where Is the Dividend Being Paid From?

- CEFs can pay distributions from three sources:
  1. **Investment income** (preferred — most reliable)
  2. **Capital gains** (risky — disappear in down markets)
  3. **Return of capital** (red flag if persistent — depletes NAV)
- Funds paying big yields from capital gains can fund payouts by eroding NAV
  at the same pace — sustainable-looking yield, declining asset base
- Examples of "big yields, lackluster returns" (2014–2019):
  - **Nuveen Energy**: -25% total return while S&P +58%
  - **Sprott Physical Silver Trust**: -30% total return
- "Dividends can't fix losers" — if the underlying portfolio isn't generating
  income, a high distribution rate signals trouble

### Closing the Discount Window — Catalysts

- Three tools fund managers use to close the discount:
  1. **Good performance** — attracts new investors who bid price toward NAV
  2. **Share buybacks** (repurchasing own stock) — closes window AND boosts
     NAV per share as shares are removed
  3. **Paying dividends from capital** — partially "takes fund private,"
     returning capital to investors forces discount window to narrow
- Buybacks are especially powerful: reduce share count → remaining shares
  each represent more underlying NAV → double benefit
- Paying dividends from capital is misunderstood — widely seen as bad,
  but in a deeply discounted fund it can be a valid mechanism to force
  window closure
- Example: fund at 17% discount → buying it out at $0.83, selling assets
  at $1.00 = instant arbitrage; same logic applies when management buys
  back shares or returns capital

## Chapter 5: Safe High-Yield CEFs in Any Rate Environment

### The Rate Fear Myth

- Quality CEFs yield ~8% even in low-rate environments — $1M invested = $80K/year
  without selling principal; compares to ~2.8% on "safe" Treasuries
- Main reason investors avoid CEFs: **Federal Reserve** fear — specifically two
  arguments the authors call "wrongly stated" and rarely challenged by media
- Buying CEFs when out of favor (contrarian) locks in outsized yields AND provides
  upside as discount windows close — total returns can exceed 8%

### CEF Rate Fear #1: Leverage (Borrowing) Will Become Costly

- CEF managers borrow at **LIBOR** (tied to fed funds rate) to amplify returns;
  20–33% leverage is typical — earns ~6%, leverages to 8%+
- Fear: rising rates make borrowing expensive, forcing distribution cuts
- **Counterargument — historical evidence:** During the 2004–2006 rate hike
  cycle (fed funds: 1% → 5.25%), prominent CEFs did not suffer
- **Nuveen AMT-Free Muni Credit Fund (NVG):** rose in tandem with
  Greenspan's rate hikes in 2004–2006; same pattern repeated in 2016–2019
- **AllianceBernstein Global High-Income Fund (AWF)** and
  **Aberdeen Asia-Pacific Income Fund (FAX):** delivered 30% and 23% total
  returns (including dividends) vs. 19% for S&P 500 over that two-year span
- Conclusion: rates would need to hit ~5%+ again to stress CEF leverage —
  authors estimate 5–10 years of runway before that's a concern

### CEF Rate Fear #2: More High-Paying Competition

- Second fear: rising 10-year Treasury yields make CEFs less attractive as
  "bond proxies"
- **Counterargument:** 10-year Treasury rose from post-WWII low of 1.4% to ~2.8% —
  still near historical nadir (peaked at ~16% in 1980s)
- An 8% CEF yield vs. a 2.8%–3% Treasury is not close competition; the spread
  remains enormous
- Both fears are repeated so often they're accepted as common knowledge —
  but neither has been rigorously challenged in mainstream financial media

### Floating on Air with Floating-Rate Bonds

- **Fixed-rate bonds** problem in rising-rate environment: principal loses value
  as newer, higher-coupon bonds become available
- **Floating-rate bonds** solution: variable coupons reset quarterly or monthly
  against a reference rate (e.g., fed funds rate) — as rates rise, payouts rise
- Two floating-rate Treasury ETFs exist: **iShares Treasury Floating Rate Bond
  ETF (TFLO)** and **WisdomTree Bloomberg Floating Rate Treasury ETF (USFSR)**
  — but since Feb 2014 inception: only 3.4% and 2.8% total return, respectively
- These are "not losing" instruments, not income generators — authors reject them
- Preferred solution: **floating-rate corporate bond CEFs** — higher yields,
  active management, ability to cycle into higher-paying issues as they mature
- **VanEck Vectors Investment Grade Floating Rate ETF (FLTR):** pays only 2.6%,
  delivered just 9.6% total return since inception (~7 years) — "barely 1% per year"

### Bonds That Rise With Rates — Floating-Rate Corporate Bond Funds

- Favorite manager: **Scott Page** of **Eaton Vance** (firm founded 1989,
  one of oldest US investment managers)
- Manages **Eaton Vance Floating-Rate Income Trust (EFT)** — note: ticker EFT,
  not to be confused with ETF
- Key structural advantage: loans reset on average every **48 days**; portfolio
  is **90% floating-rate** — not stranded when rates spike
- When bonds mature, Scott rolls capital into new corporate bonds from both
  public and private markets (larger universe than stocks)
- **EFT + BlackRock Floating Rate Strategies Fund:** averaged **20% returns**
  Dec 2015–Dec 2018 (Fed hiking cycle), mostly in monthly cash distributions;
  yielding 5–6% annually throughout
- Historical parallel: after Greenspan capped hikes in summer 2006, both funds
  continued rallying through summer 2007 even with flat rates — 20% gains
  in 12 months while 10-year traded sideways between 4.4%–5.3%
- Funds peaked when 10-year hit its 2007 high; authors' rule of thumb:
  stay long floating-rate until 10-year breaks above 3.2% or below 1.5%
- **BlackRock Floating Rate Strategies (FRA):** yields 6.2%, trades at
  **13% discount to NAV** — cheapest in 10 years
- **EFT:** pays 6.3%, trades at **12% discount to NAV** — not this cheap

## Chapter 6: Stock "Hybrids"

### The Retirement Dilemma

- Retirees gravitate to bonds for income and low drama — but long life expectancy
  means more time for **inflation to erode purchasing power**; being too
  conservative too early is itself a risk (even age 65–70 may be too early)
- Solution: **hybrid vehicles** that extract high payouts from equities while
  providing downside buffers — preferred shares, convertible bonds, covered calls

### Preferred Shares: They Pay Double "Common" Dividends

- Most investors overlook **preferred shares** — they only consider common stock
- S&P 500 common stock yields ~2.0% on average; preferreds can double, triple,
  or quadruple that yield while *reducing* risk
- How they work: company issues preferred shares like bonds; pays regular
  dividends; preferred shareholders receive payouts *before* common holders;
  priority claim on earnings/assets in bankruptcy (after secured debt)
- Tradeoff: less price upside than common stock

### Covered Calls

- Most investors buy stocks and *hope* for price appreciation; they generate
  no cash flow while waiting
- **Covered call writing** strategy: sell call options on stocks you own to
  collect premium income — targeting **10%+ cash yields**
- A **call option** is a contract giving the buyer the right to purchase
  a stock at a set price within a set time; buyer pays an upfront **premium**
- Key dynamic: **time decay** — options lose value with every passing day;
  this hurts buyers but benefits sellers (writers) who keep the premium regardless
- Time decay accelerates sharply in final 30 days before expiration

**ETF example — Horizons NASDAQ 100 Covered Call ETF (QYLD):**
- Buys NASDAQ 100 index, writes calls just above current price expiring ~1 month out
- 0.60% management fee; yielded 12.9% over past 12 months
- Two structural flaws limiting performance:
  1. Sells index calls instead of individual stock calls (individual calls
     generate larger premiums)
  2. Uses expirations too long — shorter expirations maximize time-decay capture
- Result: less volatile than NASDAQ but investors miss most of the index's upside

## Chapter 8: Stocks

### The 8% Yield Problem

- 123 stocks with market caps above $500M trade on US exchanges
  and yield 8%+; only a basket of them will serve a retirement portfolio
- Key warning: when a stock pays a dividend, its share price drops
  by that amount — **"tapping the stock price"** means you're not
  really getting the full yield when you sell
- Of those 123 candidates, **99 have not delivered 40% total returns
  over the past five years** — the minimum bar for an 8% payer
- Of those 99 underperformers, **57 are "biggest losers"** — stocks
  that actually lost investors money while the S&P 500 gained 61%
- Core principle: smartly select stocks that pay dividends *without
  tapping their own share prices to fund the payout*

### Case Study: New Residential Investment (NRZ) — A Rare Winner

- **Mortgage REITs (mREITs)** typically buy mortgage loans from
  lenders and collect interest; print money when long-term rates are
  steady or falling; suffer when rates rise (portfolios decline,
  dividend cuts follow)
- **NRZ's pivot:** instead of buying mortgages, it shifted to
  **mortgage service rights (MSRs)** — the right to collect payments
  from borrowers without owning the loans
- MSRs rise in value when mortgage refinancing slows (i.e., when
  rates rise) — the opposite of a traditional mREIT
- Result: NRZ doubled investors' money and its own book value in
  under three years; 151% total return; paid double-digit dividends
  throughout
- Lesson: past performance charts can mislead — understand *why* a
  business changed before dismissing it

### Funds from Operations (FFO) — The REIT Metric

- **FFO** = net income + depreciation + amortization − gains on
  property sales; represents the *actual rent checks* a REIT collects

  for dividends
- Want **FFO per share > dividend per share**; if not, management is
  funding the payout with borrowed money
- Target: **dividend-to-FFO payout ratios below 80%**
- Declining payout ratios = rising FFO = precursor to dividend hikes
- Rising FFO = higher occupancies = higher rents = bigger dividends

### Warning: Be Careful with Mortgage REITs (mREITs)

**How mREITs work:**
- Own paper (mortgage loans), not buildings
- Business model: **borrow short, lend long** — profit from spread
- Profit when long-term rates are steady or *declining*
  (existing mortgages become more valuable as new loans pay less)
- Attractive fat yields: AGNC 12%, NLY 11.6%, NRZ 12.2%, MFA 11.1%,
  CIM 10.6%, TWO 10.0%

**The fatal flaw — rising rates crush mREITs:**
- Rising rates → mortgage portfolio *declines* in value
- Historically deadly for dividends; stocks can drop 50%+ on cuts
- **Annaly Capital (NLY)** example: Alan Greenspan hiked June
  2004–June 2006; investors held on until NLY chopped its payout
  in mid-2005 — dividend dropped from ~$3.04 to $0.13/share
- By then, investors had already lost years of dividends overnight
- Give NLY credit: they adapted this cycle — hedged portfolio,
  diversified into commercial loans → **47% total return** since
  Fed began hiking Dec 2015

**New Residential Investment (NRZ) — the smarter mREIT:**
- Doubled investors' money *and* its own book value in <3 years
- Secret: investment in **MSRs (mortgage servicing rights)**
- MSRs *rise in value* when mortgage refinancing slows down
- Refi activity has slowed significantly as rates rose
- At time of writing: only 5% of outstanding US mortgages were
  refinanceable → NRZ in ideal position
- NRZ total return: **110%** vs. NLY's 47% in same period
  (NRZ book value: +92% vs. NLY's +10%)

### The BDC Yield Trap

### Three Parting Words for REIT-Like Investing

#### Watch Out for Debt

- REITs must grow assets to grow income; they do this via borrowing
  or issuing equity (unlike regular stocks where buybacks are positive)
- **Ladder Capital** example: grew balance-sheet loans from $538M (2013)
  to $3.8B (2018) — 51% CAGR — entirely through borrowing
- Ladder's **weighted average LTV of 68%** = 32% equity cushion
  (vs. typical homebuyer's 20%) — called "*A-1 credit quality*"
- Losses only occur when collateral value drops more than 32%

#### Look for Internal Management

- **Charlie Munger** (Buffett's partner): "back the jockey, not the horse"
  — great manager with decent business beats mediocre manager with
  great business
- **Externally managed REITs**: assets housed in company, but buy/sell
  decisions outsourced to third-party team paid on AUM — incentive
  to grow assets via debt, not to maximize returns
- **Internal management** preferred almost exclusively; exceptions exist

> "Externally managed REITs typically underperform their counterparts
> that have the 'luxury' of in-house management teams."

- **Exception: Blackstone Mortgage Trust (BXMT)** — externally managed
  by **Blackstone (BX)** but benefits from parent's global reach:
  - Over $100 billion in investor capital, offices worldwide
  - Borrows at just **LIBOR +2%** (dirt-cheap vs. peers) due to
    Blackstone's balance sheet
  - Conservative **LTV of 62%** = 38% equity cushion, bigger than Ladder
  - At IPO, stock appeared risky (new spinoff + mREIT classification
    with Annaly) creating an artificially high yield of ~8% — a
    mispricing the second-level thinker could exploit
  - Why it was cheap: spinoff newness + grouped with toxic mREITs
    (Annaly, etc.) despite having a completely different, simpler model
  - Still yields ~7.4% with dividend growth likely

#### You Want Inside Ownership

- **Ladder Capital insider ownership**: senior managers average 28 years
  experience; in 2018 bought ~5x as many shares as they sold
- Insiders own **$239 million = 12% of market cap** — exceptional for
  a REIT
- Proof of aligned interests: in 2018 Ladder received a **$15/share
  buyout offer** (lowball); management rejected it; shares up **30%+**
  after rejection
- Inside ownership is rare in REITs — treat it as a quality signal

## Chapter 9: Building Your Portfolio

### Portfolio Construction

- Recommended structure: **15–20 positions**, split **50% stocks / 50% bond CEFs**
- Rationale: concentrated enough for good ideas to matter; diversified
  enough that one blowup doesn't destroy retirement income
- Goal is NOT to be a mutual fund (dilutes the strategy) and NOT so
  concentrated that one bad pick ruins everything

### No Rebalancing

- Explicitly against traditional **rebalancing** (selling winners, buying
  losers on a fixed schedule)
- Preferred approach: buy and hold; only sell when **business fundamentals
  deteriorate** or a better security goes on sale
- The portfolio metric that matters: **income growth**, not price level

> "Remember, the 8% No Withdrawal Portfolio is not about whether your
> portfolio is up or down: it's about whether your income keeps growing."

### The Income Compounding Math

- Starting at 7% yield with 2% annual dividend growth:
  - Year 10: ~8.5% on original investment
  - Year 20: **10.2% on original investment** (no price appreciation needed)
- This is the built-in **COLA (cost-of-living adjustment)** — replaces
  what Social Security-style COLAs no longer reliably provide
- REIT contracts typically include **inflation escalators** on rents —
  added protection against real purchasing power erosion

### The Glide Path Problem

- Traditional advisor advice: **"glide path"** — put age% in bonds (e.g.,
  60 years old = 60% bonds, 40% stocks); by 90 = 90% bonds
- Also used by **target-date funds**
- Two risks of going bond-heavy in retirement:
  1. Inflation eats savings
  2. Miss the long-term stock market gains needed to survive inflation
- **Society of Actuaries** data: 65-year-old man has 41% chance of
  reaching 85, 20% chance of reaching 90; woman 53% and 32% respectively
- Married couple: 72% chance *one* of them reaches 85 — portfolios
  must be built for 25–30 year horizons

### Sequence-of-Returns Risk

- Biggest retirement risk: large market crash **in the 5–10 years BEFORE**
  retirement (readjusts your entire capital base)
- New academic thinking: load up to 80% bonds *before* retirement,
  then *increase* equity exposure *after* retirement (hold 90% stocks
  by age 90) — counterintuitive but addresses longevity + inflation risk
- Authors' practical alternative: **start the 8% No Withdrawal strategy
  5–10 years before retirement**, reinvesting all dividends; switch to
  collecting income at retirement
- This builds a compounded income base, avoids the reverse DCA trap,
  and doesn't require market timing

### When to Sell

- Sell when **story, facts, or fundamentals change** from your original
  purchase thesis — ignore the original price paid
- **Brookfield Real Assets Income Fund (RA)** sell example: yielded 10%
  but was tapping its own NAV to pay the dividend — interest income
  insufficient even with cheap leverage; NAV declined
- **Aberdeen Asia-Pacific Income Fund** sell example: fixed-rate bond
  holdings in a rising-rate environment; 9% yield couldn't be covered
  from income alone; sold before price dropped further

### Trailing Stops — Why to Avoid Them

- **Stop-loss**: sell if stock drops X% below buy price
- **Trailing stop**: sell if stock drops X% off recent high
- Problem: for **dividend investors**, stops trigger at price lows —
  exactly when yields are highest and stocks are most attractive
- **Omega Healthcare (OHI)** example: stop-losses would have fired at
  exactly the wrong moments (temporary tenant/operator drama) when
  dividend was safe and stock was cheapest
- Stop-loss orders also often **fail to execute** in fast-moving markets
- Better framework: ask "Is the dividend safe?" If yes, buy more on
  pullbacks rather than selling

### The Two Core Principles (Reiterated)

1. Buy stocks and funds **out of favor** (prices low, yields high)
2. Rely on **dividends alone** for income — price volatility is irrelevant

### Covered Calls as Income Accelerator

- Covered calls work best when **volatility is high** (option premiums rise)
- How it works: own 100+ shares, sell (write) call options at or above
  current price in exchange for cash premium; agree to sell at that
  "strike" price if exercised
- Limitation: works better on high-volume **stocks** than on illiquid CEFs
- **DoubleLine Income Solutions Fund (DSL)** — "bond god" **Jeffrey Gundlach**'s
  fund, 9.7% yield; but covered call options are thin (only 210-day
  calls at $0.20/share = 1.1% bonus, ~2% annualized) — not worth it
- **Omega Healthcare (OHI)** example at $38.06:
  - March $39 calls (56 days): $0.53/share = 1.4% instant payout
  - If Omega closes above $39: keep $0.53 premium + sell shares at
    $39 for $0.94 capital gain
  - If below $39: keep $0.53 and write another round of calls
  - Worst case: accelerates dividend from 6.8% → **15.9% annually**
  - Best case (premium + cap gains + dividend): **~32% annualized**
- Covered calls are an *acceleration* tool, not a core strategy —
  start with solid dividend-paying holdings first

## Appendix A: Frequently Asked Questions

### Dollar-Cost Averaging (DCA)

- **DCA**: invest fixed dollar amount at regular intervals regardless of
  price — automatically buys more shares when prices are low, fewer
  when high, smoothing average cost
- Benefit: eliminates need to predict market tops/bottoms; prevents
  emotional selling and panic-buying
- Warning: even investors who correctly exit at market tops (e.g.,
  fall 2007) typically fail to re-enter at the right time — miss the
  recovery; "the rule, not the exception"
- **Reverse DCA**: the trap of retirement withdrawals — forced selling
  at low prices locks in losses; the No Withdrawal approach avoids this
  by living off dividends instead of selling shares
- DCA requires emotional discipline — must keep investing through
  downturns to capture the benefit

## FAQ Continued & Appendices

### "Isn't this portfolio designed to never go down?"

- The strategy is a **No Withdrawal Portfolio**, NOT a "Never Go Down" portfolio
- Key distinction: dividends are far less volatile than share prices — you
  never have to sell into a downturn
- A "Never Go Down" portfolio (cash + Treasuries) wouldn't generate enough
  income to retire on — you'd be back to withdrawing principal and risking
  running out of money
- **Reverse dollar-cost averaging** (selling shares in a downturn) is the
  danger the strategy sidesteps; dividend income eliminates that need

### How to Stay Ahead of Inflation

- Target portfolio dividend growth rate > current inflation rate (e.g., 2%+)
- Individual payments may be static or growing at 5%+; what matters is the
  **blended total** across the whole portfolio growing faster than inflation
- Variation across positions is fine — aggregate growth is the metric

### RMDs and the No Withdrawal Portfolio in an IRA

- Traditional and rollover IRAs require annual **Required Minimum
  Distributions (RMDs)** per IRS tables — unlike taxable accounts
- RMD deadline: first RMD by April 1 after turning 70½; all subsequent by
  Dec 31 of that year
- **IRS Uniform Lifetime Table (Table III)** — distribution period at 70 is
  27.4 years, declining each year (e.g., 80 = 18.7, 90 = 11.4, 100 = 6.3)
- Formula: IRA balance ÷ distribution period = RMD for that year
- As distribution period shrinks, RMD grows — eventually exceeds annual
  dividend income generated by the account (the **"crossover" point**)
- For traditional IRAs: crossover typically occurs in the **mid-to-late 80s**
  in most scenarios; account still retains significant value into the 90s
  and beyond
- For **Inherited IRAs**: crossover tends to occur in the account owner's
  late 70s (earlier than traditional IRA) — but account retains value at
  roughly the same rate, and the heir is typically accumulating other
  assets simultaneously

## Appendix B: Tax Strategies for Popular Dividend Investments

### Municipal Bonds: Often Federal Tax-Exempt

- **Municipal ("muni") bonds** issued by states, cities, and counties for
  capital projects (transit, infrastructure)
- Interest is often **exempt from federal and/or state income tax**
- High-bracket investors benefit most — the after-tax effective yield is
  significantly higher than the nominal yield
- Example: **Nuveen Enhanced AMT-Free Municipal Credit Opportunities Fund**
  distributions are exempt from federal taxes
- Nuveen provides a **taxable equivalent yield calculator** on its website
- Illustrated example: a 5.5% tax-exempt yield for a joint filer in the
  40.8% bracket equals a **9.3% taxable equivalent yield**

> "What looks like an okay-but-not-good-enough-for-us 5.5% becomes a juicy
> 9.3%."

### Qualified Dividends: Maximum 20% Tax Rate

- **Qualified dividends** come from common shares of regular US equities
- Taxed below ordinary income rate: **0% to 20%** depending on bracket
  (vs. up to 37%+ for ordinary income)

### Nonqualified Dividends: Taxed as Ordinary Income

- **REIT distributions** (e.g., Blackstone Mortgage Trust) are generally
  classified as **nonqualified** — taxed at ordinary income rates
- However, REITs now benefit from the **"pass-through" deduction**: investors
  can deduct **20% of REIT dividend income**
- Net effect: REIT income is taxed at a lower effective rate than direct
  rental income from physical properties — which also lacks the deduction
- Owning REITs vs. being a landlord: lower taxes, no hassle

### Closed-End Funds: A Mix of Tax Categories

- Each **CEF** can distribute income from four sources, each taxed differently:
  - Interest payments (ordinary income rate)
  - Dividends (qualified rate if eligible)
  - Capital gains (short- or long-term rates)
  - **Return of capital** (tax-deferred; reduces cost basis)
- For historical breakdown of how a CEF classified its distributions, check
  the fund's website or **Morningstar.com**
- Morningstar shows breakdown by income, S/T cap gain, L/T cap gain, and
  return of capital
- Example: **BlackRock Floating Rate Income Strategies Fund (FRA)** — most
  distributions classified as income; small return-of-capital component in
  2014–2015
- CEF management rarely forecasts how upcoming distributions will be funded
  (to preserve flexibility), so use historical data as a proxy

### Avoiding the MLP "K-1 Headache"

- **Master Limited Partnerships (MLPs)** issue a **K-1** at year-end —
  complex tax document that creates work for accountants
- Brett's own accountant asked him to stop buying MLPs in his personal
  portfolio — he obliged
- Alternative: **MLP-focused CEFs** — buy at a discount to NAV at times,
  avoid the K-1 entirely (CEFs issue **1099s** instead, which are simpler)
- Authors do not own MLPs in their income-generating portfolios
