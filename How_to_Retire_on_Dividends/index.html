Glossary

The Core Strategy

8% No Withdrawal Portfolio: A retirement income strategy targeting 8%+ annual dividend yield so the investor lives entirely on income — the principal is never sold. Example: $1M at 8% yields $80,000/year while the account balance stays intact.

Reverse Dollar-Cost Averaging (Reverse DCA): The trap of retirees following the 4% withdrawal rule — forced to sell more shares when prices are low and fewer when prices are high, compounding losses and depleting capital at the worst possible time.

Safe Withdrawal Rate (SWR): The 4% annual withdrawal rule developed by William Bengen in the 1990s based on historical stock/bond data. Widely used by advisors, but requires selling principal rather than living on income.

Dividend Magnet: The mechanism by which rising dividends pull stock prices higher over time. A 10% dividend increase on a 3% yielder creates a 3.3% yield; buyers flood in until the price rises to restore the ~3% yield — capital appreciation that follows the dividend with a lag.

Yield Ceiling: The historically highest yield a stock or REIT has traded at — a level that reliably marks maximum pessimism and the best entry point. When yield hits the ceiling, price is at a floor.

Closed-End Funds (CEFs)

Closed-End Fund (CEF): A pooled investment fund with a fixed number of shares that trade on a stock exchange like individual stocks. Unlike mutual funds, new shares are not issued to meet demand, so the price can diverge from the underlying asset value. Managed actively by professional portfolio managers.

Net Asset Value (NAV): The per-share liquidation value of a fund's assets minus liabilities. If a CEF holds $100M in bonds and has 10M shares, its NAV is $10/share. The market price may be higher (premium) or lower (discount).

Discount to NAV: When a CEF's market price is below its NAV. A 10% discount means buying $1.00 of assets for $0.90 — instantly building a margin of safety into the position. Discounts can persist for extended periods and generate capital gains when they eventually close.

Premium to NAV: When a CEF's market price exceeds its NAV. Paying $1.10 for $1.00 of assets — a structural disadvantage that leaves you immediately underwater before the first dividend arrives.

Discount Window: The period during which a CEF trades at a discount to NAV. When the window "closes" — price catching up to NAV — shareholders earn a capital gain on top of their dividend income.

Return of Capital (ROC): A distribution source that reduces the shareholder's cost basis rather than paying out actual income or gains. Persistent ROC in a CEF erodes NAV over time and is a warning sign that the yield is not sustainable.

REITs & Real Estate

REIT (Real Estate Investment Trust): A company that owns income-producing real estate and must distribute at least 90% of taxable income to shareholders as dividends. Passes through rents, not corporate earnings, to investors — avoiding double taxation.

mREIT (Mortgage REIT): A REIT that owns mortgage loans or mortgage-backed securities rather than physical properties. Borrows short-term and lends long — profitable in stable rate environments, vulnerable in rising rate cycles.

MSR (Mortgage Servicing Right): The right to collect mortgage payments from borrowers in exchange for a fee. Unlike mortgages themselves, MSRs rise in value when refinancing activity slows — making them a hedge against rising rates in a mortgage portfolio.

FFO (Funds from Operations): The REIT equivalent of earnings per share. Calculated as net income plus depreciation and amortization, minus gains from property sales. Represents actual rent cash flow and is the correct metric for assessing dividend sustainability.

CMBS (Commercial Mortgage-Backed Securities): Bonds secured by a pool of commercial real estate loans. Rated by credit agencies; highly-rated tranches are considered secure. Owned by commercial mREITs like Ladder Capital.

Hybrid Securities

Preferred Shares: A class of stock that pays a fixed dividend before common shareholders receive anything — similar to a bond coupon but issued on the balance sheet as equity. Typically yields 5–8% and has a senior claim in bankruptcy versus common stock.

Convertible Bond: A corporate bond that pays interest but gives the holder the option to convert into common stock at a set price. Participates in equity upside while offering bond-like downside protection.

Covered Call: An options strategy where the owner of 100+ shares sells (writes) a call option, collecting an upfront premium. The seller keeps the premium regardless of outcome — an income accelerator on existing positions.

Time Decay (Theta): The daily erosion of an option's value as expiration approaches. Accelerates sharply in the final 30 days. Sellers of covered calls are paid by time decay — the faster the better.

Fixed Income & Other Vehicles

Floating-Rate Bond: A bond whose interest payment resets periodically (often quarterly) against a benchmark rate like LIBOR. When rates rise, coupon payments rise too — providing natural protection against rising-rate environments.

Municipal Bond (Muni): A bond issued by a state, city, or county to fund public projects. Interest is typically exempt from federal income tax — making munis especially attractive to high-bracket investors. A 5.5% muni yield equals a 9.3% taxable equivalent at a 40.8% combined tax rate.

BDC (Business Development Company): A publicly traded company that lends to small, private businesses, structured like a REIT (must pay 90%+ of income). High headline yields, but most BDCs fund payouts partly by eroding their own asset base — making them frequent yield traps.

MLP (Master Limited Partnership): A tax-advantaged investment vehicle common in pipelines and energy infrastructure. Yields can be attractive but generate a K-1 tax form, complicating returns. CEF wrappers holding MLPs avoid the K-1 entirely.

RMD (Required Minimum Distribution): Mandatory annual withdrawals from traditional IRAs mandated by the IRS beginning at age 70½. Calculated using IRS Uniform Lifetime Table — distribution period at age 70 is 27.4 years, shrinking each year until withdrawals may eventually exceed dividend income.

Payout Ratio: The percentage of earnings paid out as dividends. For regular stocks, a ratio above 50% raises sustainability concerns; anything near or above 100% signals a potential cut. For REITs, use the dividend-to-FFO payout ratio (target: below 80%).

Introduction: The $1M Problem

Imagine you've done everything right. You've saved diligently, reached the finish line with a million dollars, and you're ready to retire. Then you run the numbers. A 10-year Treasury bond yielding 2.8% hands you $28,000 a year — roughly $540 a week, or barely above the poverty line for a family of four. The Vanguard Dividend Appreciation ETF (VIG), one of the most popular dividend funds in America, pays just 2.0%, producing $20,000 annually. Even the more generous iShares Select Dividend ETF (DVY) comes in at 3.4% — $34,000 a year on a million-dollar portfolio.

The standard financial advice is to live off these yields and supplement by selling shares when needed. This is where the arithmetic turns sinister. When your dividends don't cover your bills, you start selling stock. In a falling market you must sell more shares to generate the same dollars, which means fewer shares remain to produce income next year — a compounding death spiral called reverse dollar-cost averaging. The very mechanism that built your wealth on the way up (buying more shares cheaply during downturns) works in reverse against you in retirement.

Brett Owens and Tom Jacobs spent years assembling the alternative: a portfolio yielding 8%, 9%, or more from investments that most Wall Street firms are structurally prohibited from recommending. The math is simple — $1 million at 8% yields $80,000 a year while the principal stays completely intact. The insight is where the strategy gets interesting. The high-yield investments required are precisely the ones that giant institutions like CalPERS ($200B to deploy), Jeffrey Gundlach's DoubleLine ($120B), and Warren Buffett's Berkshire ($111B in cash he can't invest at scale) are locked out of by their own size. Your relatively modest portfolio is not a weakness — it is the decisive advantage.

The book promises three things simultaneously: great yields, automatic annual raises, and downside protection. This is not a pitch for junk bonds or lottery stocks. It is a systematic case for an overlooked corner of the market — closed-end funds, high-quality REITs, preferred shares, and dividend-growing equities — assembled into a retirement machine that never forces a retiree to sell into a downturn again.

Chapter 1: Not Enough Low-Risk Income

The retirement income crisis has a specific shape. The S&P 500 pays roughly 2% in dividends today — $20,000 a year on a million dollars. Most people hold large-cap index funds or household-name stocks that track the index. Many of those names are so popular they rarely go on sale, and even when they do, the yield only ticks up modestly. Owens and Jacobs call these the dividend aristocrats — companies that have raised their dividends for 25+ consecutive years — and they spend the opening chapter methodically dismantling the conventional respect for them.

General Mills (GIS) is the first example. Its dividend staircase chart looks compelling — a steady upward march of payments, year after year, through market turmoil. But GIS stock fell 49% over roughly two years as demand for Cheerios and Green Giant flagged. The dividend stayed intact, but it was being paid from a business in visible decline. The core lesson: you are never buying just the dividend. You are buying a business. When the underlying business is deteriorating, the dividend chart is a lagging indicator of a problem that the price chart already knows about.

Johnson & Johnson (JNJ) makes the same point. Its dividend nearly doubled over ten years — a 96% increase — and it was widely regarded as the gold standard of safety. Then, in 2018, a jury awarded $4.7 billion to plaintiffs who linked JNJ baby powder to cancer. The stock lost 7.6% in a year. More importantly, JNJ's yield hovered between 2.25% and 3% throughout — even at $3 million in JNJ, a retiree would only collect $67,500 to $90,000 annually. For most people, that still isn't enough.

Four more blue-chip aristocrats compound the problem:

The average yield of that basket is 2.8% — well short of a livable income. And several are paying out more in dividends than they earn. Coca-Cola's payout ratio stood at 230% — it was paying out more than twice its earnings as dividends, only possible by borrowing or selling assets. The dividend staircase continues because management is willing to raise payouts even as underlying earnings fail to support them. Eventually, either earnings recover or the dividend gets cut. Either way, the aristocrat status is a trap for income investors expecting safety above all.

The deeper structural problem is that aristocrats are permanently popular. Because they are famous, they are expensive almost all the time — rarely going on sale, rarely offering an entry yield above 4%. Meanwhile, every year spent waiting for a better entry point is a year of lost income that can never be recovered. The opportunity cost of holding overvalued, low-yield stalwarts is the entire premise of the alternative strategy that follows.

The 4% safe withdrawal rate — often cited as the solution to funding retirement from a stock and bond portfolio — has its own problems. William Bengen, an aeronautical engineer turned financial planner, developed it by backtesting a 50/50 portfolio from 1926 to 1976. His finding: a 4% annual withdrawal never exhausted a portfolio in under 33 years. This became the Bengen Rule, later validated by the Trinity Study, and subsequently adopted by virtually every financial advisor as gospel.

But the 4% rule requires selling shares. In the 2008–09 crash, Microsoft's dividend rose 18% while the stock price fell 56%. A retiree following the 4% rule was forced to sell shares at the exact bottom to generate living expenses — locking in permanent losses. Large wirehouses like Wells Fargo, Merrill Lynch, Morgan Stanley, Edward Jones, Raymond James, and robo-advisors like Betterment and Wealthfront use the 4% rule because they need a single rule that works for millions of clients. The rule wasn't designed to maximize income — it was designed to minimize advisor liability.

"Rather than dust off Bengen's formula and model a custom spreadsheet for every client, the advisor will simply 'toggle' the risk meter up or down by ticking the yearly withdrawal rate up or down." — Owens & Jacobs on advisor laziness

The fundamental fix is a change in philosophy: instead of planning to sell principal, plan to live entirely off income. The 4% withdrawal rate can be replaced by an 8% dividend yield — one that pays twice as much, keeps the account intact, and eliminates the devastating math of reverse dollar-cost averaging during bear markets.

Chapter 2: The 8% No Withdrawal Portfolio

The 8% No Withdrawal Portfolio rests on a single structural insight about who gets to invest in what. The financial firms most people rely on — Fidelity, Schwab, Vanguard, Betterment, every major wirehouse — are forced to recommend the largest, most liquid names on the market. They manage hundreds of billions of dollars. If they started buying a $2 billion CEF or a $3 billion healthcare REIT, they would move the price before their own clients got filled, use up the entire float, and create a liability problem. By necessity, they concentrate in mega-caps: Apple, Amazon, Facebook, Berkshire, Microsoft. Those names dominate the S&P 500, and recommending them is essentially recommending an index fund — which yields about 2%.

Individual investors with a few hundred thousand to a few million dollars face none of these constraints. The sweet spot — investments with $1B to $10B in market cap — is too small for pension funds and institutional managers to touch without unacceptable market impact. Those names are followed by fewer analysts, held by fewer institutions, and as a result are often mispriced relative to their income-generating power. That mispricing shows up as higher yields, bigger discounts, and fatter total returns.

The case study that anchors the concept is Medical Properties Trust (MPW) versus Apple. MPW is a hospital landlord REIT yielding 5.9% at time of writing — three times Apple's 1.9%. Hospitals are, arguably, more indispensable than iPhones. MPW raises its dividend annually. Its market cap of roughly $6 billion makes it invisible to pension funds and too small for CalPERS to bother with, but perfectly sized for individual investors. Readers of the Contrarian Income Report who bought in November 2015 were earning a 9.0% yield on their original cost basis a few years later — and had collected 93% total returns versus the S&P 500's 35% over the same period.

The math of the No Withdrawal approach is compelling at every level. At 8% on $1 million, the portfolio generates $80,000 a year — enough for a comfortable middle-class retirement in most of the country — while the original $1 million stays completely intact. That principal is also available for legacy or estate planning, something that a portfolio being drawn down over 30 years cannot offer. Unlike the 4% withdrawal model, the income doesn't shrink as the market falls. Dividends from quality payers are far less volatile than share prices — a stock that drops 30% rarely cuts its dividend by 30%.

Not every high-yield investment qualifies. The strategy explicitly excludes what Owens and Jacobs call paper payout tigers — stocks and funds that display impressive yields but are quietly eroding their own asset base to fund them. These get their own chapter (Chapter 6), with detailed criteria for telling the real thing from the trap. The target range is 5% to 10% yield with price stability and the potential for 10–15% price upside — income plus capital appreciation, not a choice between the two.

The strategy also converts a psychological challenge into an advantage. In a traditional 4% withdrawal model, a bear market is an emergency — you must sell more shares at lower prices to generate the same income. In the No Withdrawal model, a bear market is an opportunity: prices fall, yields rise, and a patient income investor can add to positions at better yields without ever touching principal. The retirement portfolio behaves like a business generating consistent cash flow, not like a bank account being drawn down.

4% withdrawal depletes principal over time; 8% No Withdrawal keeps principal 100% intact while generating the same or more income.

The target audience is notably specific. The strategy is built for individual investors with $300,000 to $3M who want their retirement funded by real income without ever needing to sell. It also works perfectly for pre-retirees who start 5–10 years early: reinvesting all dividends through the accumulation phase builds a compounded income base, then switching to collecting the income at retirement provides a seamless transition with no forced selling — ever.

The prescription is a direct repudiation of the blue-chip default: sell the household-name aristocrats, collect a 2–3% yield, watch the payout ratio balloon, and hope the dividend holds through the next downturn. The No Withdrawal approach offers what Owens and Jacobs call a "4x pay raise" — swapping a 2% yield for an 8% yield on a different but equally sound set of businesses. The mainstream portfolio doesn't fail because its stocks are bad; it fails because its stocks are overvalued, over-owned, and yield-constrained by their own popularity. The contrarian's edge is precisely the unpopularity of the alternatives.

Chapter 3: Second-Level Thinking

The intellectual foundation for everything that follows comes from a Los Angeles money manager who has spent 40 years investing in the world's most unwanted assets. Howard Marks, co-founder and chairman of Oaktree Capital Group ($120B in AUM) and a regular on the Forbes Billionaires list, has written a series of famous investor memos that Business Insider once described as "Michael Lewis ghostwriting for Warren Buffett." His book The Most Important Thing was endorsed by Joel Greenblatt, Jeremy Grantham, Seth Klarman, and Buffett himself. The core concept is second-level thinking.

First-level thinking is what almost everyone does. "It's a good company; buy the stock." "The economy is slowing; sell everything." "This dividend looks safe; I'll collect it." First-level thinking is fast, intuitive, and popular — which is exactly why it fails. If everyone is thinking the same thing, the conclusion is already priced in. Second-level thinking goes deeper: not just what is true but what is already believed to be true, and what the surprise from reality might be.

"First-level thinking says, 'The outlook stinks, let's dump our stocks.' Second-level thinking says, 'The outlook stinks, but everyone else is selling in panic. Buy!'" — Howard Marks, The Most Important Thing, p. 4

Marks built himself a billionaire twice over by applying this to credit markets. When the 2008 financial crisis struck and every first-level thinker was selling distressed debt, Oaktree was raising capital. They closed a $10.9 billion distressed debt fund in 2008 — the largest ever assembled to that point — and earned clients 19% annualized returns after fees over the life of that fund. The window was exactly the moment when everyone else was running the opposite direction.

Applied to dividend investing, second-level thinking completely changes how you read headlines. When a REIT announces a dividend "freeze," first-level reaction is to sell — cutting and running before things get worse. Second-level question: is the freeze caused by a structural problem with the business, or just a temporary tenant issue the market is catastrophizing? When the answer is the latter, the elevated yield created by the fearful selloff is a gift. When it's the former, the elevated yield is a trap. The entire framework of the book is about telling those two apart.

Second-level thinking also explains why conventional advice is as bad as it is. The age-based bond allocation rule — hold the same percentage in bonds as your age (60 years old = 60% bonds) — is pure first-level thinking. It ignores individual circumstances, actual longevity, inflation exposure, and the fact that being 100% bonds at age 90 during a decade of rising rates is catastrophic. The second-level response is to ask: what does this person actually need? What risks are specific to their situation? What does the market believe about bonds that might be wrong?

The data reinforces the dividend focus beyond just the income argument. Hartford Funds calculated that dividends have accounted for roughly 50% of the stock market's long-term total returns over most historical periods. A portfolio targeting significantly higher yields should structurally outperform a low-yield index approach over time — not by luck but by mathematics. The compounding of reinvested dividends at 7–9% annually generates returns that pure price appreciation, at the S&P 500's historical 7% long-term average, merely matches.

There is also a psychological advantage that goes largely unmentioned in standard investing literature. When you buy a stock for its dividend and the price drops, you have no need to sell. A falling price means a higher yield — which is attractive, not threatening. The investor living on dividends is effectively immune to day-to-day market noise, because that noise doesn't change whether the rent checks keep arriving. Price volatility only matters if you are forced to sell, and the entire strategy is designed to ensure you never are.

Second-level thinking also cuts against a subtle trap embedded in energy and commodity REITs. Companies like Exxon, Chevron, and BP look like dividend stalwarts — decades of increases, steady payouts, solid brands. But when oil crashed below $30 a barrel, the stocks were crushed even as the dividends technically held. The first-level income investor looked at the yield and was satisfied. The second-level investor asked: what is the underlying business generating, and is the commodity cycle working for or against us? The right answer was neither oil nor gas stocks — it was the dividend-growing businesses whose cash flows were genuinely indifferent to energy prices.

Chapter 4: Closed-End Funds — The Key Vehicle

Most investors have never heard of closed-end funds. They know stocks and mutual funds. Some know ETFs. CEFs exist in the overlap between stocks and mutual funds — they are professionally managed portfolios of securities, like mutual funds, but they trade on stock exchanges with real-time prices, like stocks. The critical structural difference is the one that creates the opportunity: a CEF has a fixed number of shares. When a mutual fund gets popular, the manager issues new shares to meet demand. A CEF cannot. Its share count is set at launch and stays fixed.

This fixed pool of shares means the market price of a CEF can diverge dramatically from the underlying value of its assets. When investor sentiment turns against a fund — rate fears, sector headlines, management changes — the share price can fall well below what the portfolio is actually worth. A fund holding $100 million in investment-grade bonds might trade at a market cap of $85 million. Buy $850,000 of those shares and you own $1,000,000 of bonds. That 15% structural discount is the margin of safety baked into the position before the first dividend arrives.

The fixed pool also protects the manager. When a mutual fund or ETF faces heavy redemptions during a market panic, the manager must sell portfolio assets to raise cash for departing investors — often at distressed prices, at exactly the wrong moment. CEF managers never face forced selling. Their assets are locked in a fixed pool. During the 2008 crisis, while bond ETF managers were dumping illiquid corporate bonds at 70–80 cents on the dollar to meet redemptions, CEF managers simply held, collected coupons, and waited. The fixed pool isn't a quirk — it is a structural advantage.

The comparison between iShares iBoxx High Yield Corporate Bond ETF (HYG) and New America High Income Fund (HYB) makes the point concretely. HYG is a $15 billion passive ETF — one of the most widely held bond funds in the world. It yields 5.3% and uses an index weighting methodology that Owens and Jacobs describe as fundamentally broken: bond indexes are weighted by debt outstanding, which means the most indebted issuers get the biggest weights. The borrower most likely to default gets the largest position. HYB, the CEF alternative, is actively managed, trades at a 13.8% discount to NAV, and has a 30-year track record of 9.5% annualized returns — most of them paid as cash distributions. Since 2007, HYB has outperformed HYG by 34 percentage points: +115% versus +81%.

The authors lay out six rules for selecting CEFs:

Rule 1 — Demand alpha above passive alternatives. Management must earn its fees through genuinely superior returns. Paul DeNoon of AllianceBernstein exemplifies this standard. Running the Global High Income Fund (AWF) since 1992, he has generated 754% total return versus the S&P 500's 156% since fund inception — bonds beating stocks by nearly five times. Over 25 years of history, annualized returns exceed 10%. His mandate: roam globally, find inefficiencies, buy bonds the big money can't touch.

Rule 2 — Check the portfolio composition. The best corporate bond deals sit just below the investment-grade cut-off — in the BB, B, and BBB range. Pension funds, insurance companies, and banks are prohibited by regulation from owning sub-investment-grade paper. That prohibition creates a structural discount on some of the best bonds in the market. The right CEF manager lives in that space. BlackRock Floating Rate Income Strategies Fund (FRA) holds 94% of its portfolio in BBB-, BB-, or B-rated debt — 59% in B-rated alone. Over ten years, FRA returned 151% versus 134% for a comparable ETF.

Rule 3 — Require insider ownership. Fewer than half of all CEFs have any insider ownership. DeNoon personally owns 65,000 shares of AWF. Douglas Feebles, CIO of AllianceBernstein's fixed-income division, owns 40,000 shares. Skin in the game isn't a guarantee of returns, but its absence is a yellow flag.

Rule 4 — Only buy at a discount. Price always matters, even for income investors. The Gundlach twin-fund case study is the starkest possible demonstration. Jeffrey Gundlach manages both the DoubleLine Opportunistic Credit Fund (DBL) and the DoubleLine Income Solutions Fund (DSL) — same manager, same mandate, essentially identical portfolios. In 2016, DBL was trading at a 17% premium — investors were paying $1.17 for $1.00 of assets. DSL was trading at a 7% discount — the same assets available for $0.93. Over the 13 months that followed, DSL investors earned 33% total returns. DBL investors saw their money go essentially nowhere. Same manager. Same bonds. Different entry price. Different outcome.

"Seeking at least a 5% discount before buying is the minimum. Don't just buy any big discount — require a catalyst for the window to close." — Owens & Jacobs

Rule 5 — Watch for discount traps. A perpetually discounted fund is often discounted for good reason. BlackRock Resources & Commodity Fund (BCX) had a persistent 10%+ discount that attracted value-seekers for years — and was down 25% since inception. The discount window never closed because the underlying business wasn't generating returns worth owning. A cheap price is not the same as a good value.

Rule 6 — Identify the income source. CEFs can distribute from investment income, capital gains, or return of capital. Investment income is sustainable. Capital gains disappear in bear markets. Return of capital that persists is quietly eroding the fund's NAV — paying you back your own money and calling it income. Two funds to illustrate the trap: Nuveen Energy returned −25% total between 2014 and 2019 while the S&P 500 gained 58%. Sprott Physical Silver Trust returned −30% over the same period. High distributions can't fix a losing portfolio.

Discount windows close through three mechanisms: strong performance attracting new buyers, share buybacks that reduce the float and lift NAV per remaining share, or distributions from capital that force a partial unwinding of the discount. The last one is commonly misunderstood as negative. In a deeply discounted fund, it is actually a shareholder-friendly mechanism — the manager is buying $1.00 of assets for $0.83 on behalf of investors every time they distribute capital.

Chapter 5: Safe High-Yield CEFs in Any Rate Environment

The most common objection to CEF investing — especially bond CEFs — is the Federal Reserve. Rising interest rates are supposed to destroy bond fund performance: existing bonds lose value as new bonds offer higher coupons, and the leverage many CEFs use becomes more expensive to carry. Owens and Jacobs spend an entire chapter challenging both claims with historical data.

During the 2004–2006 rate hike cycle — when Alan Greenspan took the fed funds rate from 1% to 5.25%, one of the most aggressive hiking campaigns in modern history — prominent bond CEFs not only held their ground but delivered compelling returns. AllianceBernstein Global High-Income Fund (AWF) and Aberdeen Asia-Pacific Income Fund (FAX) delivered 30% and 23% total returns respectively over that two-year period, versus 19% for the S&P 500. The rate fear failed to materialize for the simple reason that the yields being earned on the portfolios — 7–10% on assets — comfortably exceeded the incremental increase in borrowing costs.

The leverage fear is overblown for a more nuanced reason. CEFs typically borrow 20–33% of portfolio value at short-term rates tied to LIBOR, then invest the proceeds at longer-term yields. The spread between borrowing cost (~2%) and earning yield (~6–8%) is the profit margin. Rates would need to reach approximately 5%+ again before that spread compresses enough to threaten distributions — and even then the managers would cycle out of fixed-rate bonds into floating-rate assets to compensate.

The second rate concern — that rising Treasury yields make bond CEFs less attractive as "bond proxies" — ignores just how wide the spread remains. A 10-year Treasury at 2.8% is not serious competition for a CEF yielding 8–9%. Even after the post-2016 rate increases that took the 10-year from post-WWII lows near 1.4% to ~2.8%, the gap remained enormous. CEF yields haven't been tested at 10-year rates above 5%, which hasn't happened since the early 2000s.

The most rate-intelligent choice for a rising-rate period is the floating-rate corporate bond CEF. These funds own loans whose interest payments reset every 30–90 days against a benchmark rate. When rates rise, the coupons on their holdings rise automatically — no portfolio restructuring needed. Scott Page, manager of the Eaton Vance Floating-Rate Income Trust (EFT), runs a portfolio that is 90% floating rate with bonds resetting on average every 48 days. When rates rise, his fund effectively gets a raise.

During the post-2015 Fed hiking cycle, EFT and the BlackRock Floating Rate Strategies Fund (FRA) averaged 20% total returns over three years — mostly delivered as monthly cash distributions. Historical precedent suggests that floating-rate bond funds continue performing even after rate hikes stop: after Greenspan capped hikes in 2006, both funds rallied through mid-2007 on flat rates, capturing another 20% in twelve months. The authors' simple rule: stay long floating-rate until the 10-year breaks above 3.2% or below 1.5%.

At time of writing, both funds were trading at their cheapest levels in a decade or more — FRA at its cheapest since the post-financial-crisis years of 2009–2011. That combination of high current yield plus an unusually wide discount created a setup the book describes as rare: the potential for income above 6% plus a capital gain as the discount window closes, even in a flat-to-rising rate environment.

"During rate hike periods, you want to own the best floating-rate funds because they steadily grind higher." — Owens & Jacobs

The core reframe throughout the rate chapter is about the direction of causality. Conventional wisdom says rising rates cause bond CEF prices to fall. The actual relationship is more nuanced: rates affect the spread, the leverage cost, and the relative attractiveness to competing instruments. For floating-rate funds, rising rates improve economics. For discounted funds, the price recovery from window closure often more than offsets modest yield compression. The first-level fear of "rising rates hurt bonds" breaks down when you examine the actual mechanics of how these specific vehicles generate returns.

Chapter 6: Stock Hybrids — Preferred Shares, Convertibles & Covered Calls

Conventional retirement portfolios force a choice: bonds for income, or stocks for growth. Owens and Jacobs spend Chapter 6 dismantling that binary. There is an entire middle tier of instruments — preferred shares, convertible bonds, and covered call funds — that deliver equity-level or near-equity-level cash flows while providing bond-like downside buffers. Most retail investors have never been told these exist.

Preferred shares are the most underappreciated of the three. When a company issues common stock, it creates one class of equity. When it issues preferred shares, it creates a second class that pays a fixed dividend ahead of common holders and has a senior claim in liquidation. If the company goes bankrupt, preferred shareholders get paid before common shareholders (after secured debt). The tradeoff is limited price upside. The practical result: preferred shares of major banks and financial companies yield nearly double their common stock counterparts. JPMorgan Chase (JPM) common stock yields 3.1%; its Series DD preferred pays 5.75%. Bank of America (BAC) common yields 2.1%; its preferred pays 5.88% — a 180% income raise from the same underlying institution.

Convertible bonds sit at the hybrid intersection of bonds and stocks: they pay interest like bonds but can be converted into shares of common stock at the holder's discretion. Covered call writing is the income accelerator that most stock owners don't use. When you own 100 shares of a stock, you can sell someone else the right to buy those shares from you at a fixed price before a set date. The buyer pays you an upfront cash premium. If the stock never reaches that price, you keep both the premium and the shares. If it does reach the price, you keep the premium and sell the shares at the agreed price — typically above where they're trading.

Time decay is what makes this work systematically. Options lose value every day simply from the passage of time, independent of whether the stock moves. That daily decay runs to the benefit of the option seller. The best CEF covered call manager exploits this by selling calls on individual stocks (generating larger premiums than index calls) with shorter expirations (capturing maximum time decay). Keith Hembre runs the Nuveen NASDAQ 100 Dynamic Overwrite Fund (QQQX) this way — adjusting the percentage of the portfolio covered by calls from 35% to 75% based on market outlook, managing the upside/income tradeoff dynamically rather than mechanically. QQQX generated 220% returns from 2012 to 2018 while paying an 8% annual cash yield, essentially keeping pace with the NASDAQ at 259% while distributing substantially more income.

Chapter 8: Stocks and REITs

The stock portion of the No Withdrawal portfolio targets a different return profile than the bond CEFs. Where bond CEFs deliver 7–9% income with modest price appreciation, dividend-growing stocks are the engine for 12%+ total annual returns — because they carry the dividend magnet. A stock paying $1.00 on a $20 share has a 5% yield. When the company raises that dividend 10% to $1.10, buyers who want a 5% yield must pay $22 per share. The price rises to accommodate the higher payout. If dividends grow 5% per year, stock prices rise approximately 5% per year on top of the income — the compounding is built into the structure.

Of the 123 US stocks with market caps above $500M that yield 8% or more, only 24 have actually delivered 40%+ total returns over five years — the minimum bar for an 8% payer to justify the yield. The other 99 are traps of varying severity, including 57 that lost investors money while the S&P 500 gained 61%. The difference is almost always the same thing: whether the dividend is being funded by genuine business income, or by quietly selling the assets that generate it.

Three red-flag case studies illustrate the trap: Buckle Inc (BKE), a fashion retailer yielding 5.6% with revenues, earnings, and free cash flow all in multi-year decline. Its payout ratio stood at 139% of profits and 135% of free cash flow — borrowing to pay the dividend, robbing Peter to pay Paul. CenturyLink (CTL) yielded 14.4% and had piled up $47 billion in long-term debt trying to compete with AT&T. The CEO assured investors the payout was safe; weeks later it was cut in half. L Brands (LB), owner of Victoria's Secret, had a net income payout ratio of 87% — uncomfortable but not alarming. What the notes revealed was that its cash payout ratio was 110% — paying out more in dividends than actual free cash flow generated, a subtler and more dangerous signal that most investors sleepwalk past.

REITs are the core stock vehicle. Congress created the REIT structure in 1960 requiring companies to distribute 90% or more of taxable income as dividends — in exchange for essentially no corporate tax. The result is a class of investments that structurally cannot retain earnings, producing among the highest sustainable yields of any publicly traded security. A 55-year study published as Historical Returns of the Market Portfolio found REITs delivered 6.4% real annual returns (after inflation) from 1960 to 2015 — outperforming broad equities (5.5%), non-government bonds (3.5%), and government bonds (3.1%).

The three best REIT sectors at the time of writing are healthcare, industrial warehouses, and commercial lending. Healthcare benefits from pure demographic tailwinds: 10,000 baby boomers turn 65 every single day. Total Medicare skilled nursing facility volume is projected to grow from roughly 79 million days in 2012 to 120 million days by 2031. The landlords of those facilities don't face competition from Amazon or China — they collect rent from operators who service an aging population that only grows.

Industrial warehouses are the less obvious Amazon play. Rather than buying Amazon stock at 200x earnings and hoping it stays dominant, an investor can buy the landlords Amazon rents from. The Economist projects online retailers will need 2.3 billion square feet of new warehouse space over the next 20 years — with most needing to sit within same-day delivery distance of major cities. The demand is structural, the supply constrained, and the rental growth inevitable. Commercial lending, meanwhile, surged as Basel III doubled bank capital requirements in 2010, causing banks to retreat from commercial real estate loans. Non-bank lenders filled the void — a niche with growing demand and shrinking traditional competition.

The buy-signal mechanic for REITs is the yield trendline — comparing the current yield to its historical range. Every REIT has an implicit price band. When something scares investors — a bad quarterly report, an operator problem, a rate headline — the stock sells off and the yield spikes above its normal range. That spike is not a warning sign. It is a valuation signal.

"Secure REIT yields like Omega's are truly the 'rubber duckies' of the investing world. Mr. Market can push them underwater for a period of time, but eventually, they rocket up to the surface." — Owens & Jacobs

Omega Healthcare Investors (OHI) is the canonical example. In early 2018, the company announced a dividend "freeze" — no cut, just no increase — after some of its skilled nursing facility operators fell behind on rent. The stock dropped to a 10.2% yield — historically the extreme top end of its range, a point that had marked a major bottom every prior time it was reached. Investors who understood the business knew that the operator problems were tenant problems, not OHI balance sheet problems. CEO Taylor Pickett, who had been running the company for 18 years with 3,000%+ cumulative returns, replaced the deadbeat operators. Cash flow recovered. OHI rallied 64% over the following period (including dividends) while the broad market traded sideways.

Medical Properties Trust (MPW) tells a similar story with a deeper texture. Ed Aldag founded MPW in 2003 with zero assets and a business plan — specifically a plan to solve a problem no one else had noticed. Hospital operators couldn't get conventional mortgage financing without pledging all their assets as collateral. Ed offered a lease structure instead — hospital operators got access to capital without encumbering their balance sheets, and MPW collected rent on the only publicly traded company investing solely in hospitals. From zero assets, MPW grew to a $6.4 billion market cap.

In November 2016, one of MPW's tenants — Adeptus Health (ADPT), operator of freestanding emergency rooms — reported a disastrous quarter and revealed it had just $6.1 million in cash. The stock crashed. MPW stock was dragged down with it, even though MPW was the landlord, not the operator. The market threw out the cash cow with the bath water. Ed's team got Adeptus stabilized, and in the worst case would simply have replaced them with another operator — a one-time cash flow disruption, not a structural break. Add to that the rate fears (MPW IPO'd during Greenspan's hike cycle and did fine), and you had two unfair discounts stacked simultaneously — exactly the contrarian entry that the No Withdrawal strategy looks for.

On the mREIT side, the strategy draws a sharp line between businesses that benefit from rising rates and those that are destroyed by them. Traditional residential mREITs — borrowing at short-term rates to hold long-term fixed mortgages — are fundamentally threatened by rate increases. Annaly Capital (NLY) had its dividend cut from $3.04 to $0.13 per share during the 2004–2006 Greenspan cycle. Investors who held on waiting for recovery lost years of income in one announcement.

New Residential Investment (NRZ) solved this by shifting focus from mortgage ownership to mortgage servicing rights (MSRs). MSRs are the right to collect payments from borrowers — and they rise in value when refinancing slows down, which happens precisely when rates rise. NRZ effectively built a business that gets better as rates go up. The result: NRZ doubled investors' money and doubled its own book value in under three years, delivering 151% total returns while paying double-digit dividends throughout.

Business Development Companies (BDCs) warrant similar caution. Created under the 1940 Investment Company Act with the same pass-through tax structure as REITs, BDCs lend to small private businesses. The headline yields are attractive — ARCC (Ares Capital) at 9.6%, PSEC (Prospect Capital) at 10.6%, Apollo Investment at 12%. The reality is that most BDCs are competing in an oversaturated lending niche that drives them to either cut rates or cut credit quality. Over five years, only Main Street Capital (MAIN) delivered total returns that actually exceeded its promised yield. The other four major BDCs paid lower dividends than they did five years earlier while underperforming their own yields on a total return basis.

MAIN is the exception because it does two things its peers don't: it grows investment income annually rather than just recycling capital, and it pays a conservative regular dividend with surplus distributed as a year-end special. Since IPO in 2007, MAIN has raised its monthly dividend 77% and has never cut it. The caution: MAIN consistently trades at a premium to its book value. Worth owning, but only when the price-to-book dips below approximately 1.3 — the entry discipline that separates a great business at a fair price from an overpayment.

Three final rules for REIT-like investments: Watch debt levels (LTV below 70% is the safety standard; Ladder Capital maintained 68%, Blackstone Mortgage Trust 62%). Prefer internal management — externally managed REITs pay their managers on assets under management, creating an incentive to grow the balance sheet via debt rather than maximize returns per share. Require insider ownership — Ladder Capital's senior management owned $239 million in company stock, or 12% of market cap, and received a $15/share buyout offer in 2018 that they rejected as a lowball. Shares rose 30%+ after the rejection. Insiders with that much skin in the game don't take the first low offer.

Chapter 9: Building Your Portfolio

The recommended portfolio structure is disarmingly simple: 15–20 positions, half stocks and REITs, half bond CEFs. Concentrated enough that good ideas move the needle; diversified enough that one blowup doesn't destroy retirement income. No rebalancing on a fixed schedule. The only reason to sell is when the original thesis changes — when fundamentals deteriorate, not when prices fluctuate. The performance metric isn't portfolio value; it's income growth.

"Remember, the 8% No Withdrawal Portfolio is not about whether your portfolio is up or down: it's about whether your income keeps growing." — Owens & Jacobs

The mathematics of income compounding over time are remarkable and frequently overlooked. Starting at a 7% yield with 2% annual dividend growth — a very conservative assumption for a quality dividend portfolio — the yield on the original investment reaches 8.5% by year ten and 10.2% by year twenty, purely from compounding. No price appreciation required. This built-in growth rate is the portfolio's cost-of-living adjustment (COLA) — the equivalent of what Social Security's inflation escalator provides, but under investor control and generally tracking faster than official inflation. Many REIT leases include explicit inflation escalators in the rent contracts, adding another layer of automatic purchasing power protection.

The traditional glide path approach to retirement investing — shifting progressively from stocks to bonds as you age, based on the rule that you should hold bonds equal to your age in percentage — is challenged on both fronts. First, longevity: the Society of Actuaries found that a 65-year-old man has a 41% chance of reaching 85 and a 20% chance of reaching 90. A married couple has a 72% chance that at least one of them reaches 85. A portfolio built for a ten-year horizon when you have a 72% chance of needing it for twenty years is systematically under-designed.

Second, and more counterintuitively, newer academic research suggests the right answer for many retirees is actually the reverse of the glide path: hold more bonds before retirement (to protect against a crash that would reset your capital base at the wrong moment), then gradually increase equity exposure after retirement, holding perhaps 90% in stocks by age 90. The logic is that a retiree in their 80s has more years of compounding ahead of their portfolio than the conventional model assumes. The No Withdrawal approach handles this naturally: because you're never forced to sell, you can afford to wait out any market decline regardless of age.

Sequence-of-returns risk is the specific enemy of people approaching retirement. A large crash in the five to ten years before you retire resets your capital base at the exact wrong time. A person with $1 million who suffers a 50% crash and then a 100% recovery ends up back at $1 million — but they needed to withdraw income throughout. The account they're withdrawing from never actually recovers to its pre-crash level on a per-withdrawal basis. The practical hedge is to start the 8% No Withdrawal strategy five to ten years before the target retirement date, reinvesting all dividends to build a compounded income base. When you retire, you simply stop reinvesting and start spending the distributions.

The sell discipline is clearly defined and deliberately narrow. Do not use trailing stops. Stop-loss and trailing stop orders are instruments designed for growth investors who care about price. For dividend investors, price drops signal higher yields and better values — the exact moment to buy more, not less. OHI's trailing stop would have fired at precisely the moment its yield hit 10% and the stock was at its most attractive. More practically, stop-loss orders frequently fail to execute in fast-moving markets, triggering at much worse prices than the stop was set.

Sell when the story changes. Two examples: the Brookfield Real Assets Income Fund (RA) was paying 10% — a compelling headline. The fine print: it was tapping its own NAV to fund the distribution, as interest income didn't cover the payout even with cheap leverage. NAV was steadily declining. Sold. The Aberdeen Asia-Pacific Income Fund held fixed-rate bonds in a rising-rate environment; the 9% yield couldn't be covered from income alone; exit before prices fell further. The original thesis on both had been invalidated. Not by price movement — by fundamental change.

Covered calls as an income accelerator deserve a separate mention. On a quality dividend stock, selling short-dated calls against your position generates additional premium on top of the dividend. On Omega Healthcare (OHI) at $38.06, March $39 calls expiring in 56 days were selling for $0.53 per share — an immediate 1.4% return for 8 weeks. If OHI closes above $39, the caller keeps the $0.53 premium plus sells shares at $39 for a $0.94 capital gain. If OHI stays below $39, the caller keeps the $0.53 and writes the next round of calls. In the worst case, the strategy accelerates OHI's effective yield from 6.8% to approximately 15.9% annually. In the best case, premium plus capital gain plus dividend approaches 32% annualized. Covered calls are an acceleration tool, not a core strategy — they require owning quality dividend positions first and only add meaningful premium on liquid individual stocks.

Trading & Investing Lessons

Discount-to-NAV as Margin of Safety

The CEF discount-to-NAV is the clearest expression in finance of the principle that price is what you pay and value is what you get. Buying at a 10–15% discount converts the same portfolio of bonds into a position with a built-in margin of safety — before any analysis of the underlying holdings. Traders in equities spend enormous effort calculating intrinsic value. CEF investors can read it directly from the NAV figure and compare it to the market price in real time.

The Yield Trendline as a Valuation Shortcut

For dividend-growing REITs, historical yield range is a faster and more intuitive valuation tool than P/E, price-to-book, or DCF analysis. When a stock's yield spikes 30–50% above its normal range, it is by definition trading well below historical fair value — assuming the dividend is safe. The skill is not spotting that the yield is high (that's first-level), but determining whether the elevated yield reflects a fixable problem or a structural deterioration. Second-level investors buy at yield spikes; first-level investors sell.

Nimble Size as Edge

Most edge frameworks in investing focus on information or analytical superiority. Owens and Jacobs identify a structural edge that requires neither: sheer smallness. The investments that generate 7–10% yields are invisible to institutions managing more than $10–20 billion. That invisibility creates persistent mispricings that a patient individual investor can exploit indefinitely. The edge doesn't erode over time — the more money flows into large-cap passive funds, the more neglected the mid-cap income universe becomes.

Never Fight the Thesis You Actually Hold

Traditional investors use stop-losses, portfolio alerts, and price-anchored risk management. All of those tools are calibrated to price movement — which is exactly the wrong metric for an income investor. A dividend stock dropping 20% is not a signal to exit; it is a signal to analyze the dividend. If the dividend is safe, the lower price means a better yield. The investor who understands what they own can add on weakness while the price-anchored trader is selling. The advantage is psychological and structural simultaneously.

Dividend Growth Beats Rate Increases

The standard trade against high-yield stocks and bond CEFs is rising interest rates — conventional wisdom says yield-sensitive instruments fall when rates rise. The historical data from multiple rate cycles says otherwise for dividend growers: REITs with growing dividends outperformed virtually everything else in the 36 months following Fed rate increases (Nuveen/ Ned Davis Research data). The mechanism is the dividend magnet: annual increases pull stock prices higher faster than modest rate movements can push them lower.

The Quality of Income Matters as Much as the Quantity

An 8% yield funded by investment income is fundamentally different from an 8% yield funded by return of capital. The former compounds; the latter depletes. An 8% yield from a growing business is different from an 8% yield from a declining one — the former carries embedded capital appreciation while the latter conceals capital destruction. The due diligence task for income investors is not finding the highest yield; it is finding the highest sustainable yield with the highest growth potential. Everything else is a trap dressed in attractive clothing.

Key Takeaways

The 4% Rule Is Broken

Bengen's 4% safe withdrawal rate requires selling shares — more in down markets, fewer in up markets. Reverse dollar-cost averaging is a compounding death spiral that the 8% No Withdrawal approach eliminates entirely by substituting income for capital liquidation.

Your Size Is the Advantage

Individual investors with $100K–$3M can access investments that Buffett, Gundlach, and CalPERS are locked out of by their own scale. The $1–10B investment universe yields 6–10% and is structurally underpriced because institutional capital can't reach it without moving prices.

CEFs Are the Core Vehicle

Closed-end funds with fixed share pools can trade at 10–20% discounts to NAV — building in an automatic margin of safety and capital gains potential that open-end mutual funds and ETFs can never offer.

Buy Discounts, Never Premiums

Paying $1.17 for $1.00 of assets (as in the DBL case) versus $0.93 (DSL) produced +33% versus 0% over 13 months from the same manager and same portfolio. The discount is the entire edge.

Dividend Growth Doubles Your Returns

7% yield with 2% annual dividend growth = 10.2% yield on original investment in twenty years. The dividend magnet pulls the stock price up as dividends rise — combining income and capital appreciation without requiring market timing.

Three REIT Sectors Dominate

Healthcare (10,000 baby boomers per day), industrial warehousing (2.3B sq ft needed for e-commerce), and commercial lending (banks retreating from Basel III) are the structural growth areas where yield plus growth combine most powerfully.

Never Use Stop-Losses on Dividend Stocks

Price drops signal higher yields — the best entry point, not an exit trigger. Trailing stops fire at exactly the moment a quality REIT is cheapest and most attractive. Sell when the thesis changes; never sell because the price changed.

Sell When the Story Changes

The original purchase thesis is the test. When fundamentals change — dividend source eroding, business model failing, debt spiraling — sell. Not before. Price fluctuation is noise. Fundamental deterioration is signal.

Most BDCs and High-Yield mREITs Are Traps

14%+ yield is almost always a warning sign rather than an opportunity. CenturyLink, Annaly's history, and 4 of 5 major BDCs all confirm: extremely high yields usually signal either fundamental deterioration or dividend funding from return of capital. Main Street Capital is the rare exception worth owning.

Covered Calls Accelerate Income

Writing short-dated calls against quality dividend positions can double the effective annual yield on an individual position — turning a 6.8% REIT yield into 15%+ with premium income on top. An accelerator tool, not a standalone strategy.

Contrarian Ideas

The Safest Portfolio Never Owns Dividend Aristocrats

The conventional definition of dividend safety is "25+ years of consecutive increases." The reality: aristocrat payout ratios expand to unsustainable levels as earnings plateau, concentrating risk in expensive stocks whose best days are behind them. GIS, KO, MCD, and WMT all showed declining sales growth alongside rising payout ratios — the very combination that precedes a cut. The safest income comes from growing businesses with moderate payout ratios, not famous names with payout ratios above 100%.

Rising Rates Are Good for the Right Bond Portfolio

Everything about conventional wisdom in bonds says rates up = prices down = sell bonds. For floating-rate bond CEFs, the opposite is true: rising rates directly increase portfolio income, as loan coupons reset higher every 30–90 days. The 2004–2006 and 2015–2019 hiking cycles both produced positive returns for the floating-rate CEF universe.

Paying Premium Is More Dangerous Than Buying Junk

The PIMCO premium funds — PGP at 48% premium, RCS at 42%, PHK at 39% — illustrate the irony: investors chasing the most prestigious managers paid so much above NAV that they were guaranteed to underperform the very portfolios they were buying. Brand recognition is the enemy of value in CEF investing.

The Smaller the Company, the Bigger the Income

Financial media equates size with safety and prestige with value. The actual relationship runs the other way for income investors: the most consistently undervalued income securities are in the $1–10B market cap range, ignored by institutions, neglected by media, and trading at discounts that simply wouldn't survive if anyone powerful enough to arbitrage them were watching.

Don't Own the Tenant — Own the Landlord

Amazon is a $1 trillion company trading at 100x earnings. The warehouses Amazon fills are owned by REITs trading at 7–9% yields with contractual rental increases. The growth of e-commerce is a tailwind you can capture from the asset side without paying for Amazon's multiple. Same structural insight applies to hospitals (own MPW, not the hospital operators) and data centers (own the real estate, not the tenants).

Proposals & Frameworks

The 8% No Withdrawal Portfolio System

The complete framework has five operational rules: (1) Target 8%+ yield so dividends cover all living expenses. (2) Never sell principal — only income is spent. (3) Diversify across 15–20 positions, 50% stocks/REITs and 50% bond CEFs. (4) Sell only when the original investment thesis changes, never in response to price movements. (5) Start the strategy 5–10 years before retirement, reinvesting all dividends to compound the income base.

CEF Selection Trifecta

Three conditions that must be simultaneously present: (1) Yield significantly above passive alternatives (typically 7%+) — management must earn its fee. (2) Discount to NAV — never pay par or premium. (3) Return on NAV since inception of 7.5%+ — the lifetime achievement score that distinguishes a skilled manager from a lucky one. Any two of three is insufficient; all three together represents a genuinely differentiated position.

The Dividend Yield Buy Signal

Compare a REIT's or dividend stock's current yield to its historical range using a long-term chart. When yield is meaningfully above the historical norm (20–50% above the midpoint), the stock is trading below fair value on a dividend-adjusted basis. Combine this with a fundamental check on dividend safety (payout ratio, FFO coverage, debt levels, management track record) to distinguish a genuine opportunity from a yield trap.

The Income Compounding Model

Target: 7% starting yield with 2% annual dividend growth. Year 1: $70,000 on $1M. Year 10: ~$85,000 (8.5% on original). Year 20: ~$102,000 (10.2% on original). No price appreciation required. The blended portfolio dividend growth rate must exceed the inflation rate to preserve real purchasing power — achieved through a mix of REIT escalators, growing common dividends, and periodic repositioning from stagnant to growing payers.

Covered Call Acceleration Formula

On any liquid dividend position: sell monthly calls 1–5% out of the money, targeting $0.50–$1.00 per share premium. If exercised: capture the premium plus the capital gain to the strike price. If not exercised: keep the premium and repeat. Minimum acceleration: doubles the effective annual yield on typical quality dividend positions. Maximum scenario with exercised calls: 30%+ annualized when premium + capital gain + dividend are all captured simultaneously.

What Didn't Fit

Cohen & Steers Infrastructure Fund (UTF)

UTF holds stocks and preferred shares of top global infrastructure and utility companies — electric utilities, airports, toll roads, railroads, and pipelines worldwide. The preferred share access is the key: these are institutional-grade securities not available to retail investors directly. Cohen & Steers (founded 1986) uses moderate leverage of roughly 24% of portfolio, generating a 9.2% annualized NAV return since inception and powering an 8.4% dividend yield. Management made its third major distribution increase since 2013 at the time of writing — raising the monthly payout nearly 16%. Buying at 94 cents on the dollar for a growing 8.4% yield is the combination the book prescribes. UTF outperformed the Vanguard Real Estate ETF 956% versus 371% since inception.

The IRS Uniform Lifetime Table and REIT IRAs

The No Withdrawal strategy hits an unavoidable complication inside a traditional IRA: Required Minimum Distributions. The IRS Uniform Lifetime Table specifies a distribution period starting at 27.4 years at age 70½ and declining annually. As the distribution period shrinks, the required annual withdrawal grows. In a traditional IRA invested for 8% dividends, the RMD eventually "crosses over" the dividend income — the required withdrawal exceeds what the portfolio generates. For most scenarios, this crossover occurs in the mid-to-late 80s for traditional IRAs and somewhat earlier for inherited IRAs. The account retains significant value well into the 90s regardless, because the underlying portfolio continues compounding. The key practical lesson: Roth IRA conversions in the years before RMDs begin can significantly delay or eliminate the crossover point — a coordination of the No Withdrawal strategy with standard IRA conversion planning.

Tekla Healthcare Investors (HQH)

The Equity CEF Hall of Fame entry that deserves more attention: Tekla Healthcare Investors (HQH) employs medical doctors and research scientists to pick healthcare and biotech stocks. This isn't a marketing gimmick — the edge is structural. A cardiologist evaluating a heart drug clinical trial can assess the probability of FDA approval more accurately than any generalist portfolio manager. HQH returned 11% annualized on NAV since inception, outperforming the iShares Nasdaq Biotech ETF (IBB) by 292% to 241%. It pays a 9.9% yield and trades at a 10% discount — the deepest discount among the five hall-of-fame equity CEFs. Domain expertise bought at a 10% discount is the single most compelling risk/reward in the equity CEF universe.

The DollarCost Averaging Timing Problem

The FAQ appendix contains a sobering data point buried inside the DCA discussion: even investors who correctly identify a market top and exit at the right time almost universally fail to re-enter at the right time. They wait for the all-clear signal that never comes clearly, watch prices recover without them, and end up buying back in at worse prices than the tops they successfully called. The book's response is to sidestep the problem entirely: if you never sell, you never need to time a re-entry. The dividend stream through a 50% market crash is far less volatile than the price chart suggests — quality REITs and bond CEFs continued paying distributions throughout 2008–09, making the portfolio's income narrative very different from its price narrative.

The Adeptus Health Scare in Detail

The November 2, 2016 MPW collapse deserves its full context. Adeptus Health (ADPT) operated freestanding emergency rooms in Texas and Colorado — a small but growing niche in healthcare real estate. MPW was Adeptus's landlord and Adeptus represented about 6% of MPW's projected 2017 revenues. When Adeptus reported a catastrophic quarter — missed estimates, slashed guidance, $6.1 million in cash remaining — its stock crashed. MPW was dragged down despite having no credit exposure to Adeptus's operational problems. MPW's risk was limited to finding a replacement tenant if Adeptus couldn't pay rent — a one-time disruption, not a structural impairment. Ed Aldag's team stabilized the situation, and the scare proved to be the perfect textbook entry point: two simultaneous unfair discounts (tenant drama plus rate fears) stacked against a business that was fundamentally unaffected by either.

Why This Book Matters

Most retirement literature tells the same story: save consistently, diversify broadly, follow the 4% rule, and hope the sequence of returns goes your way. How to Retire on Dividends breaks from that consensus completely and makes a compelling case that the entire framework is wrong — not slightly suboptimal, but structurally broken. The reverse dollar-cost averaging trap alone should permanently change how anyone thinks about withdrawal strategies. The evidence is clear: selling shares in declining markets to fund living expenses is the single most wealth-destructive act a retiree can take, yet it is what every standard retirement plan demands.

The book also performs a genuine service in surfacing a universe of investments — closed-end funds, preferred shares, floating-rate corporate bonds — that the mainstream financial press essentially ignores. These instruments are not obscure because they are dangerous; they are obscure because they are too small for the institutions that shape financial media coverage. The result is persistent structural mispricing that individual investors can exploit as long as they remain individual investors. The strategy's edge literally cannot be scaled away.

For anyone within ten years of retirement with meaningful savings, the practical framework here — build the dividend income base first, live on it completely, never sell principal — offers the most concrete and systematic alternative to the 4% withdrawal orthodoxy available in the popular investment literature. The concepts are accessible, the specific vehicles are named, the selection criteria are clear, and the historical evidence is compelling. Whether you follow the exact portfolio construction or take only the philosophy, the core insight — that a retirement funded entirely by income rather than asset liquidation is achievable, permanent, and far superior to the alternative — is worth the time of any serious investor.
