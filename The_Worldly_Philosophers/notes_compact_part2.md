## Chapter 6 (continued): The Inexorable System — Marx's Endgame

### The Crisis Cycle and Final Collapse

- Each crisis = capitalism's renewal mechanism, not its failure — but each
  renewal ends the same way: competition → higher wages → labor-displacing
  machinery → smaller surplus value base → worse crisis
- Each crisis, bigger firms absorb smaller; when industrial monsters fall,
  wreckage is far greater than when small firms buckle
- The **dialectic** ends in inevitable collapse: capitalism squeezes out its
  own energy source (surplus value); planless nature makes collapse certain

> Along with the constantly diminishing number of the magnates of capital...
> grows the mass of misery, oppression, slavery, degradation, exploitation;
> but with this too grows the revolt of the working-class... The expropriators
> are expropriated. — *Das Kapital*

### Marx vs. Prior Economists on Capitalism's Fate

- **Smith**: capitalist escalator climbs upward indefinitely
- **Ricardo**: progress stalled by population pressure; windfall to landlords
- **Mill**: distribution can be adjusted by society's will
- **Marx**: Mill's escape hatch is untenable — the state is merely the
  political organ of the economic rulers, incapable of acting as neutral referee

### Marx's Vision of the Successor System

- Post-capitalism: **"classless"** — property basis for economic division
  removed once society owns means of production
- Marx left details vague: who "owns" factories, how society manages
  antagonisms between managers and managed — all unaddressed
- Transitional period: "socialism" → **"dictatorship of the proletariat"**
  → eventually "pure" communism
- Marx was not the architect of actual socialism — that task fell to **Lenin**
- *Das Kapital* = "Doomsday Book of capitalism" — almost nothing looks
  beyond the Day of Judgment

### Critiques of the Marxist Framework

- System built on **labor value** and **surplus value**; transitioning from
  value-world to price-world, Marx makes a mathematical mistake
- Mistake not irreparable — equations can be rectified — but critics declared
  Marx "wrong" and ignored the fix when it came
- Deeper problems: Can surplus value apply in a world of monopolies or
  scientific technology? Is "labor" a valid measuring rod of value?

### Two Properties Marx's Critics Miss

**1. Critique of economics itself (socio-analysis)**
- Marx invented a new task: *the critique of economics itself*
- Smith and Ricardo: how do prices reflect labor-time embodied in goods?
- Marx's deeper question: how can "labor" be a common denominator when a
  fisherman's labor and a hunter's labor are utterly different?
- Answer: capitalism creates **abstract labor** — labor detached from personal
  attributes, buyable and sellable like wheat or coal
- Real insight of labor theory of value: not price determination but
  *identification of a social system in which labor-power becomes a commodity*
- Historical forces (e.g., the **enclosure movement**) created a propertyless
  class with no alternative but to sell their labor-power

**2. "Laws of motion" — predictive power**
- Profits *do* tend to fall in capitalist economies (competition + rising wages
  cut margins; no business can permanently maintain prices above costs)
- Only way to perpetuate profits: economy must grow → ceaseless quest for
  new techniques; technological progress is not an accompaniment of capitalism
  but a **vital ingredient**
- **Business cycles**: no other economist of Marx's era recognized boom-and-bust
  as an *inherent* feature of capitalism
- **Concentration**: in 1867 bigness was the *exception*; predicting giant firms
  would dominate was as bold as predicting today that small proprietorships
  will displace corporations
- **Proletarianization**: early 19th-century America ~75% of workers were
  self-employed; by Heilbroner's time only ~10% self-employed
- Marx *was* wrong that profits would display a long downward secular trend
  (they fall within cycles but not irreversibly over time)

### Why Capitalism Didn't Collapse — The Social Variable Marx Missed

- In Russia/Eastern Europe capitalism *was* displaced; Germany/Italy drifted
  into fascism — not entirely wrong, but...
- European capitalism failed largely for *social* reasons, not purely economic
- Marx's prediction founded on capitalism being *politically* impossible to
  reform — the state would always serve ruling-class interests
- **America's exception**: no aristocratic lineage, no fixed class attitudes;
  pragmatism toward power and democratic ideals steered it past the rocks
- Marx's fatal blind spot: **sociopolitical culture** — a factor he barely mentions
  - European welfare states provide free college, universal healthcare, pensions
    at scales making US poverty rates 3-4× higher

### Heilbroner's Final Assessment of Marx

- Marx remains "the gravest, most penetrating examination the capitalist system
  has ever undergone" — dispassionate analysis, not moral lecturing
- **Engels's graveside oration**: "Just as Darwin discovered the law of evolution
  in organic nature, so Marx discovered the law of evolution in human history"
  — too much to claim, but Marx's vision of history as class struggle is genuine
- Marx taught us not just to look *at* history but *through* it — as Freud
  taught us to look through personality to psychic processes
- Better thought of as **"unavoidable"** — not infallible, but a great explorer
  whose footprints are indelibly imprinted on the continent of social thought

## Chapter 7: The Victorian World and the Underworld of Economics

### The Dog That Didn't Bark — Capitalism's Unexpected Resilience

- Marx's *Manifesto* (1848) pronounced capitalism terminally ill; many Marxist
  laws of motion *were* verified: big business grew, recurrent depressions plagued society
- But one symptom was absent: the **"increasing misery of the proletariat"**
  failed to increase (at least physically)
- **Sir Robert Giffen** (1880s): fifty years ago, average worker's wages implied
  periodic starvation; by 1880s wages had risen faster than prices — *for the
  first time* workers could keep body and soul together
- Hours also fell: at Jarrow Shipyards work week dropped from 61 to 54 hours;
  mill owners complained wage costs rose 20%+
- **Engels** mourned: "The English proletariat is actually becoming more and
  more bourgeois... the most bourgeois of all nations"

### Economics Becomes an Academic Profession

- Victorian boom made capitalism look stable → less urgency to ask disturbing
  questions; economics shifted to **professordom**: pinpoint beams, not wide-searching beacons
- New academics (**Alfred Marshall**, **Stanley Jevons**, **John Bates Clark**)
  examined the system in detail but expressed no doubt about its basic merits
- World was "peopled entirely with agreeable, if imaginary, sheep"
- Marx's *Capital* met not with abuse but the "more crushing ignominy of indifference"

### Francis Edgeworth and Mathematical Psychics

- **Francis Ysidro Edgeworth** (nephew of Maria Edgeworth): *Mathematical
  Psychics* (1881), published two years before Marx died
- Core assumption: ***every man is a pleasure machine***
  — from **Jeremy Bentham**'s **Felicific Calculus** with mathematical precision
- Mathematical proof: in a world of **perfect competition**, each pleasure machine
  achieves the highest amount of pleasure society can provide
- **Trade unions** = transient imperfections (mathematically proven to lose in
  the long run); inequality of birth = reconciled because some are *better*
  pleasure machines ("aristocracy of skill and talent" more responsive than
  "clodlike laboring classes")
- **Henry Sidgwick** protested: he ate dinner not because he toted up satisfactions
  but because he was hungry
- Mathematical psychics: "so neat, so beguiling, so bereft of troublesome
  human intransigences" that it succeeded immediately

### The Mathematical School in Europe

- **von Thünen** (Germany): formula for the precise just wage = √(a·p);
  liked it enough to have it engraved on his tombstone
- **Leon Walras** (France): proved mathematically the exact prices that would
  clear every market simultaneously — requiring equations for every single good,
  running to hundreds of thousands (theoretically possible; practically absurd)
- **W. Stanley Jevons**: reduced the struggle for existence to a "Calculus of
  Pleasure and Pain"; the shift from "political economy" to "economics" was
  significant
- **Equilibrium** concept: Walras's equations still used to depict a social
  system at rest
- Fatal limitation: equilibrium depicts a **static** system — the dynamic,
  expansive, tension-filled vision of Smith through Marx was simply dropped

### The Underworld of Economics — Cranks and Heretics

- Parallel to the sterile academic world: an **underworld of economics** flourished
- **Bernard Mandeville**: *Fable of the Bees* — profligate rich give work to poor;
  virtuous penny-pinchers do not; private immorality may serve public welfare;
  convicted as a public nuisance by a Middlesex grand jury in 1723
- **Marx**, **Malthus** (with "general gluts"), the Utopians — all banished to underworld
- Anyone whose diagnosis left room for moral doubt or radical reform was
  excluded from Victorian "correct" economics

### Frédéric Bastiat — The Wit of Economics

- **Frédéric Bastiat** (1801–1850): applied economics' most devastating weapon — **ridicule**
- Famous *Petition of the Candlemakers*: manufacturers petition to block windows
  and shutters to shut out the "intolerable competition" of the sun, which floods
  the market with light at zero price
- On import duties to protect French workers: pass a law requiring only blunt
  hatchets so that three hundred axe-blows replace one hundred
- Published *Economic Sophisms* — "as close to humor as economics has ever come"
- Began *Economic Harmonies*; died 1850, whispering "Truth, truth...."
- Assessment: enormously conservative but not influential; real function was to
  prick pomposity; deeper question: does the system always make sense?

### Henry George: The Single Tax

- **Henry George** (1879): left school at 13, sailed as foremast boy, gold
  prospector, journalist — never went to college; an unlikely figure for theory
- Arrived in San Francisco 1858; years of wretched poverty; married **Annie Fox**
  (17 years old); during her second pregnancy stopped a stranger on the street
  for $5: "If he had not, I think I was desperate enough to have killed him."
- Received a vision "in daylight and in a city street"; finished ***Progress and
  Poverty*** alone at night, "flung myself on my knees and wept like a child"
- Central observation: as a city grows, ALL productivity gains accrue to
  **landowners** — not workers, not capitalists

> "Will, in ten years, interest be any higher?" — "No."
> "Will the wages of common labor be any higher?" — "No."
> "What, then, will be higher?" — "Rent, the value of land."

- Land speculator can "sit down and smoke your pipe," do zero work, add zero
  wealth, yet become rich — while the city builds an almshouse next door
- Goes beyond **Ricardo**: rent also crushes workers AND causes industrial
  "paroxysms" — boom-bust cycles driven by land speculation
- Remedy: the **Single Tax** — absorbing all land rents, replacing all other
  taxes; "raise wages, increase earnings of capital, extirpate pauperism,
  abolish poverty, give remunerative employment to whoever wishes it"

### Critique of George's Thesis

- Heilbroner: George's mechanical diagnosis is **superficial** — blaming
  depressions on land speculation is to blow one small aspect out of proportion
- Severe depressions occurred in countries where land values were not inflated
- Rental income as share of US national income shrank from **6%** (1929) to
  under **2%** — the rent "problem" was far less drastic than George portrayed
- Moral critique has more force: Why benefit from mere ownership? But landlords
  aren't the only passive beneficiaries

### George's Impact and Legacy

- *Progress and Poverty* became a **massive bestseller** — sold more copies than
  all economic texts previously published in the US combined
- New York *Tribune*: "no equal since the *Wealth of Nations*"
- **General Francis A. Walker**: "I will not insult my readers by discussing a
  project so steeped in infamy"
- Beat **Theodore Roosevelt** in a three-cornered New York mayoral race; narrowly
  lost to the Tammany candidate
- Ideas filtered (in watered form) into **Woodrow Wilson**, **John Dewey**,
  **Louis Brandeis**
- Died 1897 on eve of a second mayoral campaign; reputation went "straight into
  the underworld of economics: almost-Messiah, semi-crackpot, and disturbing
  questioner of the morality of our economic institutions"

### The Age of Imperialism: Background

- Late 19th century: between **1870 and 1898**, Britain added 4 million sq. miles
  and 88 million people; France gained similar area with 40 million souls;
  Germany won 1 million miles and 16 million colonials
- Attitude had reversed since Smith, who despised colonies; even **Disraeli**
  called colonies "millstones around our necks" as late as 1852
- **Lord Rosebery**: British Empire was "the greatest secular agency for good
  the world has ever known"
- Working-class amenities in Britain partly funded by sweated colonial labor —
  "the colonies were now the proletariat's proletariat"
- Mainstream economists observed neutrally; the underworld critics saw imperialism
  as a structural tendency of capitalism that led inevitably to war

## J.A. Hobson and the Theory of Imperialism

### Hobson's Background

- **John A. Hobson** (b. 1858): frail, shy Oxford graduate destined for obscurity
- Formative influences: **John Ruskin** (economics as humanism) and
  **A.F. Mummery** (mountain climber/businessman, died 1895 on Nanga Parbat)
  who theorized depressions were caused by **excessive saving** — the chronic
  inability of the business system to distribute enough purchasing power
  to buy its own output
- Hobson and Mummery co-wrote ***The Physiology of Industry*** — the
  establishment was scandalized: every great economist from Smith onward had
  stressed saving as the source of capital accumulation
- Result: London University Extension Lectures dropped him; Hobson became
  an **outcast**

### From Oversaving to Imperialism

- Hobson traveled to Africa, met **Cecil Rhodes**, **Kruger**, **Smuts**;
  witnessed the Boer War firsthand
- Rhodes had articulated the imperial logic candidly:

> "I was in the East End of London yesterday and attended a meeting of the
> unemployed.... The Empire, as I have always said, is a bread and butter
> question." — Cecil Rhodes

- The colonies were the outlet for undigested domestic savings

### Hobson's *Imperialism* (1902)

- "The most important and searing criticism that had ever been levied against
  the profit system"
- Marx said capitalism would **destroy itself**; Hobson said it might **destroy
  the world**
- Core argument:
  1. Unequal income distribution → rich have incomes too large to consume
  2. Poor cannot buy enough; rich physically cannot consume enough
  3. Rich are **forced** to save — incomes simply too large to spend
  4. Classical answer (invest savings in more factories) fails: markets glutted
     because workers' incomes are too low to buy output
  5. Solution: invest savings **overseas** — imperialism is the structural
     escape valve
  6. All capitalist nations face the same problem simultaneously → **race to
     partition the world** → inevitable war

> "The endeavor of the great controllers of industry to broaden the channel
> for the flow of their surplus wealth by seeking foreign markets and foreign
> investments to take off the goods and capital they cannot use at home."
> — J.A. Hobson, *Imperialism*

### Lenin's Adoption

- Hobson's theory adopted by **Nikolai Bukharin** and **Lenin**, who made it
  the capstone of Marxist doctrine
- In Marxist garb: imperialism = the inevitable "highest phase of capitalist
  development" — "decaying, dying capitalism... the onset of the socialist
  world revolution"
- Hobson had hoped rival imperialisms might peacefully coexist; Lenin made
  the outcome inexorable catastrophe
- Precursors: German economists **Rodbertus** and **Rosa Luxemburg**

### Assessment of the Imperialism Theory

- Empirical support: between 1870–1914, **one-half** of English savings invested
  abroad; foreign investment dividends = **10%** of British national income
- Ceylon tea gardens returned **50% dividends** in banner years; British cotton
  industry depended almost entirely on the Indian market
- But not ironclad: conquest predates capitalism; Soviet invasions of Hungary
  and Czechoslovakia show imperialism continues without capitalist "oversaving"
- Modern view: driving force of capitalist expansion is the **extraordinary
  efficiency and dynamism** of capitalist production — not purely undigested savings
- Better understood as **"internationalization of capital"** — began before
  capitalism fully formed, still ongoing
- In **1897**: nearly half of US overseas capital in plantations, railways, mining;
  today most flows to **manufacturing in developed nations** — three-quarters of
  international investment now flows to Europe, Canada, other developed nations
- Ironic outcome: the movement begun as relief from pressure on capital has
  intensified global competition (low-cost manufacturing in Hong Kong, Taiwan,
  South Korea, Mexico undersells domestic producers in the very metropole)
- Hobson died 1940; London *Times* obituary noted "prescient ideas and his
  lack of general recognition"

### Marshall: Equilibrium and Time

- **Alfred Marshall**'s key word: **"equilibrium"** — the self-adjusting,
  self-correcting nature of the economic world
- **Keynes** on Marshall: he created "a whole Copernican system, in which all
  the elements of the economic universe are kept in their places by mutual
  counterpoise and interaction"
- ***Principles of Economics*** (1890): still standard; main contribution is
  the importance of **time** as the quintessential element in equilibrium

### Short Run vs. Long Run

- **Short run**: fixed quantity of goods; demand/utility exercises more
  immediate influence on price (buyers and sellers haggle over diamonds
  merchants already brought)
- **Long run**: supply can adjust; cost of production reasserts its upper hand
- Supply and demand are like **"the blades of a pair of scissors"** — fruitless
  to ask which blade does the cutting

### Marshall's Vision of Economic Man

- Shifted economics from social dynamics to the **individual** as the rock
  on which the economy rests; shedding the word **"Political"** from Political
  Economy removed the central question of earlier thinkers: how do relations
  of **power and obedience** structure society?

> "A rich man, in doubt whether to spend a shilling on a single cigar, is
> weighing against one another smaller pleasures than a poor man, who is
> doubting whether to spend a shilling on a supply of tobacco that will last
> him for a month." — Marshall, *Principles of Economics*

### Marshall's Fatal Limitation

- His "time" was **abstract time** — mathematical curves, NOT irreversible
  historical time
- Lived through Russian revolution, WWI, early anticolonialism — yet his
  motto remained *Natura non facit saltum* ("nature makes no sudden leaps")
  from 1890 to 1920
- Heilbroner's verdict: "an intellectual tragedy of the first order" — had
  Marshall possessed Hobson's vision or George's moral outrage, the catastrophes
  of the 20th century might have been anticipated

## Chapter 8: The Savage Society of Thorstein Veblen

### The Robber Baron Milieu

- In Europe: hereditary social status still mattered; in **America**: money was
  the only passport; individual achievement was the only measure
- **Cornelius Vanderbilt** to business associates threatening his interests:

> "Gentlemen: You have undertaken to ruin me. I will not sue you, for law
> takes too long. I will ruin you. Sincerely, Cornelius Van Derbilt"

- **J. Pierpont Morgan**: "I don't know as I want a lawyer to tell me what
  I cannot do. I hire him to tell me how to do what I want to do."
- **Jim Fisk** vs. Morgan for Albany-Susquehanna Railroad: each side mounted
  a locomotive and ran them into each other
- Stock market: "a kind of private casino for the rich in which the public
  laid the bets and the financial titans fixed the croupier's wheel"

### The Anaconda Copper Swindle

- **Henry Rogers** and **William Rockefeller** acquired **Anaconda Copper
  Company** without spending a dollar of their own — a five-step legerdemain:
  1. Gave Marcus Daly a $39M check conditioned on depositing it untouched at
     National City Bank
  2. Set up shell company **Amalgamated Copper**, bought Anaconda with $75M in
     freshly printed Amalgamated stock
  3. Borrowed $39M from National City Bank using the Amalgamated stock as
     collateral — to cover the original check
  4. Sold Amalgamated stock on market for $75M
  5. Retired the $39M loan, pocketed $36M profit

> "I owe the public nothing." — **J. Pierpont Morgan**

### Official Economics as Apologist

- American economists sanitized the Gilded Age:
  - Cutthroat competition = "thrift and accumulation"
  - Outright fraud = "enterprise"
  - Gilded extravagance = colorless "consumption"
- **John Bates Clark**, *Distribution of Wealth* — one could read it and
  never know America was a land of millionaires
- **Professor Laughlin** in *Atlantic Monthly*: great fortunes resulted from
  "sacrifice, exertion, and skill"; every man had a right "to enjoy the products
  of his exertion to the exclusion of everybody else"
- What was needed: the eye of a stranger — like **de Tocqueville** or **Bryce**
  — someone foreign to the scene

### Thorstein Veblen: Biography and Character

- **Thorstein Bunde Veblen** (1857–1929): "an American by birth but a citizen
  of nowhere by nature"
- Appearance: peasant looks, lank flat hair, gnomelike head, scraggly beard,
  thick unpressed suit, safety pins suspending his socks, noiseless hunter gait
- Keynote: **alienation from society** — nearly hermetic insulation; other
  economists (even Marx) were *of* their society; Veblen stood apart —
  "uninvolved, unentangled, remote, aloof, disinterested"
- Refused telephone; stacked books in packing cases; didn't make beds;
  washed dishes by hosing them down
- Gave all students the same grade regardless of work
- Writing style: "razorlike English" — involuted, precise
  - Philanthropy = "essays in pragmatic romance"
  - Religion = "the fabrication of vendible imponderables in the nth dimension"
  - Churches = "chain stores"; individual church = "retail outlet"

### Veblen's Origins and Education

- Born 1857, fourth son of Norwegian immigrant family on Minnesota frontier
- Grew up in tight Norwegian-American enclave; learned English as foreign tongue
- Sent to **Carleton College Academy**; threw faculty into uproar with "A Plea
  for Cannibalism" — explained as "scientific observations"
- Teacher: **John Bates Clark** (later eminent economist)
- After Carleton: Yale Ph.D. 1884 — "but with no future and no prospects"
- Seven years of isolation on the family farm; brother: "Thorstein was the
  only loafer in a highly respectable community... He read and loafed."
- 1891: walked into **J. Laurence Laughlin**'s office at Cornell wearing a
  coonskin cap and corduroy trousers: "I am Thorstein Veblen"
- When Laughlin moved to head the new **University of Chicago** economics
  department (1892), brought Veblen along at $520/year
- University of Chicago founded by **Rockefeller** — Veblen would later dissect
  the very type of institution that employed him
- His classes: mumbling, rambling, digressing; dwindled to one student;
  those who stayed found it worth it

### *The Theory of the Leisure Class* (1899)

- Published at age 42; **William Dean Howells** devoted two long reviews to it;
  became the *vade mecum* of the intelligentsia: "electric, grotesque, shocking,
  and amusing"
- True question: *What is the nature of economic man? How does society produce
  a leisure class at all?*

> "We all feel, sincerely and without misgiving, that we are the more lifted up
> in spirit for having, even in the privacy of our own household, eaten our
> daily meal by the help of handwrought silver utensils, from hand-painted china
> laid on high-priced table linen."

### Veblen's Anthropological Challenge to Classical Economics

- Classical economists: leisure class via rational self-interest — successful
  men naturally minimize labor once fortune allows
- Veblen's anthropology showed primitive societies (**American Indians, Ainus of
  Japan, Todas of the Nilgiri hills, Australian bushmen**) had *no* leisure class
  — everyone worked; **pride of workmanship** drove effort
- Contrast: **Polynesians, ancient Icelanders, feudal Japanese shogunates** had
  leisure classes — but these were not idlers; they were busy *predators* who
  seized riches by force or cunning

### The Predatory Origins of the Leisure Class

- Predatory leisure classes admired because their societies were rich enough to
  sustain them and aggressive enough to respect force
- Result: **attitudes toward work inverted** — predation became honorific, pure
  labor became tainted with indignity
- The "irksomeness of work" that classical economists took as inherent human
  nature was, Veblen argued, a cultural artifact

### Modern Man as Barbarian

- Modern man is "only a shade removed from his barbarian forebears"
- Leisure class accumulates *money* instead of scalps — lavish display is the
  modern tepee trophy

> "The discipline of savage life has been by far the most protracted and
> probably the most exacting of any phase of culture in all the life-history of
> the race; so that by heredity human nature still is... savage human nature."
> — Veblen

- **Conspicuous waste** signals predatory prowess; everyone from workman to
  capitalist participates — "keeping up with the Joneses" as atavistic status competition

### Social Cohesion via Emulation (Not Class War)

- Key insight against Marx: lower classes are *not* at swords' points with the
  upper; they are bound by **common attitudes of emulation**
- Workers don't seek to displace their managers — they seek to *emulate* them;
  they acquiesce that their own work is less "dignified"
- "In the theory of the leisure class lies the kernel of a theory of social
  stability" — explains why revolution doesn't happen
- **Robert and Helen Lynd** (*Middletown*) found during the Great Depression:
  working class retrenched on food and clothing before cutting "necessary"
  luxuries — Veblen's competitive emulation validated empirically

---

### *The Theory of Business Enterprise* (1904)

- The businessman as ***saboteur* of the system** — flew in the face of every
  economist since Adam Smith
- Began not with human interests but with **technology and the machine** —
  society as a gigantic mechanism requiring technicians and engineers, not profit-seekers
- The businessman's goal — *accumulation* — is alien to the machine's logic;
  he achieves profit by **conspiring against** the productive system, causing
  breakdowns so values fluctuate and he can capitalize on confusion
- On top of the productive machine, businessmen built a superstructure of
  **credit, loans, and make-believe capitalizations** — finance as parasite on production

### Robber Baron Evidence

- **Jay Gould** fighting Vanderbilt for Erie Railroad (1868): while rails were
  worn beyond safety, Gould was entirely focused on financial maneuvering
- **U.S. Steel (1901)**: real assets ~$682M, but sold $303M in bonds, $510M
  preferred stock, $508M common stock — financial company was *twice* the size
  of the real one; steel rails quoted at $28/ton for 13 years when production
  cost was under $14 — all efficiency gains subverted to maintain make-believe finance

### The Businessman as "Watchful Waiting" Toad

> "Doubtless this form of words, 'watchful waiting' will have been employed in
> the first place to describe the frame of mind of a toad who has reached years
> of discretion and has found his appointed place along some frequented run
> where many flies and spiders pass and repass..."
> — Veblen, "The Captain of Industry"

### Veblen's Theory of Social Change

- *The Engineers and the Price System*: eventual **technocratic revolution** —
  engineers hold real productive power; once aware of the incompatibility of
  business with industry, would dispense with "lieutenants of absentee ownership"
- If they don't? Business predatoriness grows until it becomes naked force —
  *"We would call such a system fascism"*
- Compared to **Count Henri de Saint-Simon** — both extolled producers, mocked
  ornamental functionaries

### Veblen's Later Life and Death

- Left Chicago (1906) over philandering scandals; went to **Stanford**, became
  known as "the last man who knew everything"
- At University of Missouri wrote *The Higher Learning in America* and
  *Imperial Germany* — the latter barred from mails for insulting both Germany
  *and* Britain/US
- 1918: New School for Social Research with **John Dewey, Charles Beard,
  Dean Roscoe Pound**
- **H.L. Mencken**: "Veblenism was shining in full brilliance. There were
  Veblenists, Veblen Clubs, Veblen remedies for all the sorrows of the world.
  There were, in Chicago, Veblen Girls..."
- Hoped Russian Revolution would usher in the age of engineers and technicians;
  was disappointed; "gave signs of a certain relaxation of will and interest,
  of a kind of turning toward death"
- Turned down presidency of the **American Economic Association**: "They
  didn't offer it to me when I needed it"
- Died 1929 — months before the Great Crash; left instructions for cremation
  with no ritual, no tombstone, no biography. His request was ignored.

### Assessment of Veblen

- Key limitation: like Marx, did not seriously inquire how institutions *adapt*;
  the machine itself would force businessmen into bureaucratic, managerial molds
- Lasting contribution: **technology and science as the primary force of modern
  social change** — a historic vision his contemporaries entirely missed

> "No other such emancipator of the mind from the subtle tyranny of circumstance
> has been known in social science, and no other such enlarger of the realm of
> inquiry." — **Wesley Clair Mitchell** (Veblen's student)

## Chapter 9: The Heresies of John Maynard Keynes

### The Great Depression as Context

- By 1933 US national income fallen from $87B (1930) to $39B — half the
  prosperity of four years prior
- Devastating statistics: residential construction -95%, 9M savings accounts
  lost, 85K businesses failed, salaries -40%, dividends -56%, wages -60%
- 14 million unemployed — "the proud spirit of hope had been permanently
  crushed out of America"
- **Unemployment** of this scale was simply not listed among the possible ills
  of the system by classical economists — it was deemed absurd, impossible

### Keynes the Man

- **John Maynard Keynes** — born 1883, the very year Karl Marx died
- Marx: bitter, draftsman of "Capitalism Doomed"; Keynes: buoyant, architect
  of "Capitalism Viable"
- Polymath: math treatise on probability that **Bertrand Russell** called
  "impossible to praise too highly"; accumulated £500,000 trading international
  currencies half an hour a day while still in bed; Cambridge don, chairman
  of a life insurance company, Director of the Bank of England; knew Roosevelt,
  Churchill, Shaw, Picasso
- As Bursar of King's College: turned £30,000 into £380,000
- One regret: wished he had drunk more champagne

### Early Life and Career

- Cambridge: **Alfred Marshall** begged him to become a fulltime economist;
  befriended **Lytton Strachey**; nucleus of the **Bloomsbury group** formed
- Civil service exam: scored second overall; lowest mark was in economics —
  "I evidently knew more about Economy than my examiners"
- 1907: posted to India Office; work consisted of shipping one pedigreed bull
  to Bombay; resigned after two years; wrote *Indian Currency and Finance*
  (1913) — "a small masterpiece"; appointed to Royal Commission on Indian
  currency at age 29
- Editor of the *Economic Journal* for 33 years

### WWI and *The Economic Consequences of the Peace* (1919)

- Called to the Treasury during WWI; men of ripe judgment said he contributed
  more to winning the war than any other person in civil life
- Acquired £100K worth of Impressionist art (Corot, Gauguin, Manet, Cézanne)
  for Britain during Paris mission while Big Bertha shelled the city; got a
  Cézanne for himself
- Met **Lydia Lopokova** (leading ballerina of Diaghilev's company) — later married her
- At Paris Peace Conference (1919): watched in agony as Wilson was outmaneuvered
  by Clemenceau; vindictive peace replaced humane one

> "The Peace is outrageous and impossible and can bring nothing but misfortune
> behind it." — Keynes, letter to his mother, 1919

- Resigned; wrote *The Economic Consequences of the Peace* in a fury (Dec 1919):
  predicted Versailles reparations would force Germany into vicious international
  practices and goad a formidable resurgence of German autarchy/militarism
- Portrait of Clemenceau: "He had only one illusion—France, and one
  disillusion—mankind, including his own colleagues not least"
- Portrait of Wilson: "like Odysseus, he looked wiser when seated"
- The **Dawes Plan** of 1924 began undoing the 1919 impasse, confirming
  Keynes's prophetic gift

### Key Works Leading to the *General Theory*

- *Tract on Monetary Reform* (1923): attacked gold standard fetishism and
  abdication of conscious currency control

> "In the long run we are all dead." — Keynes, *Tract on Monetary Reform*

- *Treatise on Money* (1930): central question — what makes the economy oscillate
  between prosperity and depression?

### The Business Cycle Problem

- Business cycles observed for over a century (England bad 1801, good 1802...)
- **W. Stanley Jevons** blamed **sunspots**: business cycles 1721–1878 averaged
  10.46 years, sunspot periodicity was 10.45 years — theory collapsed when more
  careful calculation lengthened sunspot periodicity to 11 years
- Search returned to **Malthus's** intuition: saving might cause a "general
  glut" — scorned by Ricardo and Mill as dangerous nonsense

### The Saving/Investment Split

- In the early 1800s, those who saved were the same people who invested —
  saving = "accumulation," a two-sided coin
- By mid-19th century this identity broke down: wealth spread, businesses grew
  large and sought capital from anonymous masses of savers
- **Saving and investing became divorced** — separate operations by separate
  groups of people; Malthus was right after all

### The Income Flow and the Savings Leak

- National prosperity = *flow* of incomes from hand to hand; every purchase
  transfers income into someone else's pocket
- Most income recirculates via consumption spending; dangerous break: money
  *saved* does not go directly back into circulation
- Normally, savings re-enter via investment; **but there is nothing automatic
  about this channel** — business only needs savings when *expanding*
- When the business outlook is poor, impetus to invest wanes — savings
  accumulate but don't get invested

> *If our savings do not become invested by expanding business firms, our
> incomes must decline.*

- A "tragedy without a villain": virtuous citizens saving prudently, virtuous
  businessmen declining to invest when prospects are poor — yet together their
  rational decisions can destroy the economy

### The *Treatise*'s Flaw and the Road to the *General Theory*

- *Treatise* identified savings-investment seesaw but couldn't explain why a
  depression would *persist*
- Classical safety switch: excess savings → lower interest rates → cheaper
  borrowing → business expansion → recovery
- This *actually failed* in the Great Depression: interest rates fell, nothing happened

### The *General Theory* (1936)

- Keynes wrote to **George Bernard Shaw** in 1935: "I believe myself to be
  writing a book on economic theory which will largely revolutionize... the way
  the world thinks about economic problems... I'm quite sure."
- *The General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money* — in the same league
  as *Wealth of Nations* and *Capital*

### Core Conclusion

- Classical view: economy is a seesaw that always rights itself
- Keynes's conclusion: **the economy is an elevator** — can go up, down, or
  stand still; can reach equilibrium *with* mass unemployment
- Flaw in the automatic-recovery argument: at the bottom of a slump there
  is **no flood of savings** — as income contracts, savings dry up
- Data: In 1929 Americans saved $3.7B; by 1932–33 saving was *nothing* —
  drawing down old savings; corporations that saved $2.6B at the boom's peak
  were *losing* nearly $6B three years later
- **Equilibrium with unemployment**: the unemployed are economic zeros, exerting
  no effective demand on the marketplace

### Three Gloomy Diagnoses of the *General Theory*

1. An economy in depression could *stay* there — nothing inherent pulls it out
2. Prosperity depends on investment; if business capital spending falls, a spiral
   of contraction begins; only rising investment reverses it
3. Investment is an undependable drive wheel — perpetually threatened by
   satiety (markets eventually get built out); uncertainty at the core of capitalism

- Keynes on investment satiety:

> "Ancient Egypt was doubly fortunate... in that it possessed two activities,
> namely pyramid-building and the search for the precious metals, the fruits
> of which, since they could not serve the needs of man by being consumed, did
> not stale with abundance... Two pyramids, two masses for the dead are twice
> as good as one; but not so two railways from London to York."

### The Cure — Government Spending

- The New Deal's **Hundred Days** had already begun applying the medicine
  before Keynes articulated it
- Deliberate government spending as the substitute for insufficient private
  investment: the state could fill the gap that savings left unfilled

> "If the Treasury were to fill old bottles with bank notes, bury them at
> suitable depths in disused coal mines... and leave it to private enterprise...
> to dig the notes up again... there need be no more unemployment."
> — Keynes (tongue in cheek)

### Why Pump-Priming Fell Short

- Business investment collapsed from $15 billion (wages, salaries, profits)
  in 1929 to $886 million in 1932 — a 94% drop
- Government spending rose from ~$10B (1929–33) to $15B by 1936; private
  investment recovered two-thirds; national income/consumption +50%
- Yet **9 million still unemployed** by 1936
- Two reasons:
  1. **Spending too small** — WWII showed the real scale needed: $103B brought
     full employment; peacetime politics made that level impossible
  2. **Business misread the medicine** — government spending as a helping hand
     was interpreted as a threatening gesture; New Deal antibusiness atmosphere
     chilled private investment exactly when it was needed

### Keynes as Conservative, Not Radical

- Saw government spending as **"abnormal"** — a temporary bridge, not permanent;
  asked *how long* intervention was advisable, not whether to make it permanent
- Explicitly anti-Communist:

> "How can I accept the [Communistic] doctrine which sets up as its bible...
> an obsolete textbook which I know not only to be scientifically erroneous
> but without interest or application to the modern world?" — Keynes, 1931

> "It is better that a man should tyrannize over his bank-balance than over
> his fellow citizens." — *The General Theory*

- On **Friedrich Hayek's** *The Road to Serfdom*: sympathized but disagreed
  on conclusions; wanted more planning, not less, provided planners were
  morally oriented
- Quoted Lenin approvingly on inflation:

> "By a continuing process of inflation, governments can confiscate, secretly
> and unobserved, an important part of the wealth of their citizens... There
> is no subtler, no surer means of overturning the existing basis of society
> than to debauch the currency." — *The Economic Consequences of the Peace*

### Bretton Woods and the End

- Six trips to the US (1941–) to negotiate Britain's war finance and postwar
  monetary order
- Helped design the **International Monetary Fund** and **World Bank** at
  **Bretton Woods** (New Hampshire) — replacing dog-eat-dog currency competition
  with cooperative monetary support
- Dominated the conference by force of personality; elevated to peerage as
  **Lord Keynes, Baron of Tilton**
- Made Director of the Bank of England
- Died April 1946 in Sussex; services at Westminster Abbey

### Keynes's Legacy and the Decline of Keynesianism

- **"Keynesian" economics** dominated US economics 1940s–1960s; by 1980
  **Alan Blinder** noted: "It was hard to find an American economist under
  forty who professed to be a Keynesian."
- Causes of decline:
  - Failure to reconcile Keynes's **"macro"** view (aggregate expenditure flows,
    "animal spirits") with **Marshallian "micro"** view (rational individual markets)
  - Resurgence of inflation as central concern
  - Growing disenchantment with activist government role
- European economists (Scandinavia, Germany, Netherlands, France) developed
  pragmatic micro-macro fusion: capitalism as the only workable system but
  requiring strong government presence, welfare, and education for
  globalization's casualties

### Keynes on the Ideal Economist

> "The master-economist must possess a rare combination of gifts. He must be
> mathematician, historian, statesman, philosopher... purposeful and disinterested
> in a simultaneous mood; as aloof and incorruptible as an artist, yet sometimes
> as near the earth as a politician." — Keynes on Alfred Marshall (and himself)

## Chapter 10: The Contradictions of Joseph Schumpeter

### *Economic Possibilities for Our Grandchildren* (Keynes, 1930) + Schumpeter

- Amid Depression, Keynes looked long-term: by **2030**, the economic problem
  (scarcity) might be solved; another century at the same pace would multiply
  England's productive wealth **seven and one-half times**; new problem would
  be **leisure**, not scarcity
- **Joseph Alois Schumpeter** (1883–1950): born in Austria same year as Keynes;
  schooled at the **Theresianum** (exclusive school for sons of the aristocracy)
  after his mother married a distinguished general
- Three wishes: to be a great lover, a great horseman, and a great economist
  — "life had granted him only two of the three"
- Star student at **University of Vienna** under **Eugen von Böhm-Bawerk**;
  first book written in Egypt while financial adviser to a princess (cut her
  rents in half, doubled her income); at 27 published ***The Theory of Economic
  Development*** (1912)
- Shared with Keynes: admiration for cultivated bourgeois life, belief in
  capitalism's general values; but reached **diametrically opposite conclusions**
  on capitalism's future
- Keynes: capitalism intrinsically threatened by stagnation, needs government support
- Schumpeter: capitalism intrinsically dynamic; no need for permanent government spending
- Yet Schumpeter's long-term verdict was darker: "Can capitalism survive? No.
  I do not think it can."

### *The Theory of Economic Development*: The Circular Flow

- Strategy: begin with a **static, no-growth capitalism** (the "circular flow")
  to isolate where profits actually come from
- **Circular flow**: production reproduces itself perfectly; wages/rents absorb
  all output; **capitalists receive nothing** (their capital goods' contribution
  absorbed by the labor that made them)
- Key insight: *in a static economy there is no place for profit*
- Standard explanations all fail: Smith wavered; Mill said reward for "abstinence"
  but didn't explain why capitalists deserved a reward for acting in their own interest

### The Entrepreneur as Source of Profit

- Profits arise when the circular flow is disrupted by **technological or
  organizational innovations**: new/cheaper ways of making things, or wholly new things
- Innovator produces at lower cost → earns a "rent" from the differential →
  but this rent is **transient**: imitators learn the trick, competition forces
  prices down, profits disappear as routine takes over
- The innovator: **entrepreneur** — not a normal businessman following routines,
  but someone who combines factors of production in new ways
- Entrepreneurship is not hereditary; it is a special kind of leadership;
  the entrepreneur is always somewhat an "upstart," his ways "readily laughed at"

### Business Cycle from Innovation

- Innovation is rare and pioneering; **followership is easy** — a "swarm" of
  imitators follows each innovator
- Swarm → bank borrowing surge → investment boom → generalization removes
  differential advantage → prices fall → profits evaporate → investment falls →
  possible contraction as ill-timed investments unwind
- This explains the **business cycle** as endogenous to capitalism's own growth
  process, not an external shock

### Schumpeter's Career and Personal Life

- 1919: Joined German socialist nationalization commission; when asked how he
  could serve it: "If somebody wants to commit suicide, it is a good thing if
  a doctor is present"
- Finance minister of Austria briefly — drafted stabilization plan, forced to
  resign before approval
- President of the **Biedermann bank** in Vienna — bank failed; paid creditors
  in full rather than use bankruptcy law; spent 10 years repaying from income
- Married young Annie (apartment superintendent's daughter); she died in
  childbirth within a year; later: Harvard; married **Elizabeth Boody**
- Declared the Great Depression "a good cold douche"

### *Business Cycles* (1939) and the Depression

- Three overlapping cycle types:
  1. Short-duration cycle
  2. Seven-to-eleven year rhythm
  3. **Fifty-year "Kondratieff" pulse** — tied to epochal inventions (steam
     locomotive, automobile)
  - Depression = all three cycles hitting bottom simultaneously
- *Business Cycles* ends on an ominous note: capitalism needs **faith in its
  civilizational values**, and that faith was losing its mobilizing force

> "But the sociological drift cannot be expected to change." — Schumpeter,
> *Business Cycles* (closing line)

### *Capitalism, Socialism and Democracy* (1942)

- "A book that changed the way we think about the system"
- Opens with Marx — Schumpeter's true intellectual antagonist
  (not Keynes, whom he dismissed uncharitably)
- Schumpeter had studied Marx with **Rudolph Hilferding** and **Otto Bauer**
  — was more open-minded about Marx than about Keynes

### Schumpeter vs. Marx

- Marx: capitalism's dynamism stems from class struggle — surplus value
  extraction drives labor-saving innovation
- Schumpeter: capitalism's dynamism stems from the **bourgeois side**,
  specifically from the **outsider entrepreneur** — "an interloper, an upstart"
- "The evolution of the bourgeois style of life could be most tellingly
  described in terms of the genesis of the lounge suit"

### The Central Argument of *CSD*

- **"Can capitalism survive? No. I do not think it can."** — but for
  *Schumpeter's* reasons, not Marx's
- Innovation as **"perennial gale of creative destruction"** — monopolies
  themselves are the agents of change
- The contradiction: capitalism is an **economic success but a sociological failure**

> "Capitalism creates a critical frame of mind which, after having destroyed
> the moral authority of so many other institutions, in the end turns against
> its own; the bourgeois finds to his amazement that the rationalist attitude
> does not stop at the credentials of kings and popes but goes on to attack
> private property and the whole scheme of bourgeois values."

- The entrepreneur becomes unnecessary: innovation gets **institutionalized
  and reduced to routine**; bureaucratic management replaces heroic leadership
- The bourgeois family — "the great transmission belt of capitalist values"
  — becomes infected with rationalism; the **bourgeois class loses faith in itself**

### On Socialism

- **"Can socialism work? Of course it can."** — a benign, bureaucratic
  planned economy run by the bourgeois elite who have the managerial talent

### Heilbroner's Assessment

- Schumpeter's prognosis is **uncannily prescient** for 1942: technological
  frontier, bureaucratization trend, waning bourgeois ethic
- But uneven:
  - Vast multinationals did *not* become passive giants — they compete
    aggressively in world markets
  - The 1990s saw a **revival of belief in capitalism** (collapse of Soviet
    Union), contradicting the drift toward socialism
- Crucial limitation: **Schumpeter's prognosis is not ultimately an economic
  one** — it's historical sociology; the disaffected intellectual who spoils
  capitalism's future doesn't obey the same economic logic as the accumulating
  capitalist

### The Theory of Elites

- Schumpeter's hidden framework: **capitalism is driven by elites**, not classes
- Entrepreneurs come from no particular class — "simply the possessors of a
  talent for innovation"

> "We can assume that every healthy man can sing if he will. Perhaps half...
> have the capacity... and finally, we come to the Carusos."

- Population distribution of leadership capacity:
  - Bottom quarter: consigned to routine clerical functions
  - Middle half: normal innovating capacity — "practically all business people"
  - True elite: "people who are a type characterised by supernormal qualities
    of intellect and will"
- **Counter to Marx**: The proletariat cannot be the force for change — by
  sheer numbers it must lie in the normal range; leadership belongs only to
  a tiny minority
- "The upper strata of society are like hotels which are indeed always full
  of people, but people who are forever changing"

### The Vision Behind the Analysis

- **"Vision"** is Schumpeter's own word — coined in his posthumous *History
  of Economic Analysis* (unfinished at his death in 1950)

> "Analytic work embodies in the picture of things as we see them, and wherever
> there is any possible motive for wishing to see them in a given rather than
> another light, the way in which we see things can hardly be distinguished
> from the way in which we wish to see them." — Schumpeter

- **Marshall** missed Keynes's consumption/investment distinction because his
  vision emphasized *pricing* — if he had been looking at total output, he
  would have seen what Keynes saw
- Schumpeter's own vision: the **aristocracy of talent** — elites chosen not
  by blood but by "intellect and will"; this justifies both capitalism and
  Schumpeter's own group

## Chapter 11: The End of the Worldly Philosophy?

### "End" Has Two Meanings

- **Termination** and **purpose** — both meanings apply here
- Economics is "an explanation system whose purpose is to enlighten us as to
  the workings, and therefore to the problems and prospects, of that complex
  social entity we call the economy"
- The worldly philosophers' visions vary enormously — but they share a
  **common structural core**

### What Made Economics Possible: The Three Innovations of Capitalism

1. **Legitimation of acquisitiveness** — never before in any society had
   wealth-pursuit been celebrated for everyone; kings and adventurers, yes;
   the lower classes, never
2. **The market as distributor** — guidance of production and distribution
   left to the "encouragements and discouragements" of buying and selling;
   no parallel in hunting/gathering or command societies
3. **Dual authority** — public (government: force and law) and private
   (profit-seekers: production and distribution); government cannot dragoon
   labor as pyramid builders did or punish workers as feudal lords could

- "The worldly philosophy is the child of capitalism and could not exist
  without it"

### The Disappearing Word: "Capitalism"

- Crucial change in modern economics: **Science** has replaced **Capitalism**
  as the organizing vision
- Evidence:
  - **N. Gregory Mankiw**, *Principles of Economics*: "Economists try to
    address their subject with a scientist's objectivity... in much the same
    way as a physicist approaches the study of matter"
  - **Joseph Stiglitz**, *Economics* (997 pages): the word "capitalism"
    **does not appear**
  - Pre-1950s *American Economic Review* vs. recent decades: sharp increase
    in scientific method references, precipitous decline in the word "capitalism"

### Why "Economics as Science" Is Problematic

**Reason 1: Human volition**
- Marshall: "economics cannot be compared with the exact physical sciences
  for it deals with the ever-changing and subtle forces of human nature"
- Electrons don't "decide" where to move; buyers and sellers do
- Economic behavior has **"lawlike" regularities** (price up → quantity bought
  down) but these are probabilistic, not deterministic — advertising campaigns
  can override price signals
- Human behavior requires **volition** — the unpredictable capacity to change
  our minds up to the last moment

**Reason 2: The political nature of social life**
- All societies above hunting/gathering create categories of privilege and
  disprivilege — aristocracy to slavery, property rights to penury
- **Distribution of wealth, inheritance laws, sweatshops** are not immutable
  laws of nature but "highly mutable determinations of the sociopolitical order"
- Claiming "scientific objectivity" conflates natural arrangements (value-neutral)
  with social ones (value-laden)
- A pseudoscientific view of economics "would indeed spell its termination
  as a worldly philosophy"

### The Purpose of Economics Going Forward

- Heilbroner's answer: economics' purpose is to help us **better understand
  the capitalist setting** in which we will shape our collective destiny
- Concedes capitalism will persist — 20th-century socialism's failures make
  a benign rebirth unlikely
- But capitalism faces severe stresses:
  - **Global warming** — containing damage in poor nations and reducing
    emissions in wealthy source nations
  - **Nuclear proliferation** and ethnic/racial/religious hatreds
  - **Globalized economy** that arises within individual capitalisms but
    escapes their control, threatening sovereignty even of the wealthiest nations

### The Spectrum of Capitalisms

- All capitalisms share three core properties: **drive for capital
  accumulation**, **market guidance and constraints**, and **bifurcation of
  power** between public and private sectors
- But vary enormously in *intensity* of capital drive, *degree* of freedom
  accorded markets, and *location* of the public/private boundary

### The US vs. European Contrast

- **Executive compensation** in top US corporations is *twice* that of France
  or Germany
- **Upward mobility** of American poor is *half* that of France/Germany and
  *one-third* that of Sweden
- First comparison signals a **culture of greed**; second signals **social
  indifference** — a combination ill-suited for world leadership
- Scandinavia and Europe: socially *and* economically successful capitalisms
- United States: economically successful but **socially disastrous** capitalism

### The Role of Worldly Philosophy Going Forward

- **Economic vision** — a reborn worldly philosophy — can broaden capitalism's
  motivations, increase flexibility, develop social responsibility
- Economics must be "deepened and enlarged" beyond its current "desiccated
  residue" — incorporating psychology, sociology, political science

> "Bearing in mind the two meanings of 'end' in our title, it is to this hopeful
> vision of tomorrow's worldly philosophy that this book is dedicated."
> — Heilbroner, closing line
