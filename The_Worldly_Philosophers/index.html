Chapter I: Introduction — The Power of Ideas

By the usual measures of history — armies commanded, empires built, national decisions made — the great economists were nobodies. Adam Smith was a Glasgow professor who once walked fifteen miles in his dressing gown before snapping out of a reverie. David Ricardo was a retired stockbroker who spoke in the Commons "as if he had dropped from another planet." Karl Marx spent his life in poverty in a London slum, sometimes lacking money for postage stamps. Yet Heilbroner opens with the claim that their work was "more decisive for history than many acts of statesmen who basked in brighter glory."

That claim needs defending, and Heilbroner defends it with a formidable piece of evidence: the world these men described — a world of markets, wages, profits, and accumulation — did not exist before they began writing about it. Their ideas were not commentaries on something already understood. They were the very instrument by which a bewildering new social arrangement became legible to itself. The factory owner who read The Wealth of Nations found not just a description of what he was doing but a justification — an entire moral and intellectual framework for believing his pursuit of profit served humanity.

Heilbroner calls these men the worldly philosophers because they took the most worldly of all human activities — the drive for wealth — and tried to build a philosophy out of it. The phrase captures something important about how unusual this was. For most of human history, the naked pursuit of gain was considered morally suspect at best. Medieval Christianity taught that no Christian ought to be a merchant; Shakespeare's contemporaries believed the goal of ordinary life was to maintain one's station, not to advance it. To treat the accumulation of money as a legitimate subject for philosophical inquiry was, in itself, a small revolution.

What made these philosophers "worldly" was not just their subject matter but their method. They did not test their ideas in a laboratory. Their experiments played out in society itself, in prices and wages and trade balances, in the prosperity of nations and the misery of the poor. When their theories were wrong — as they sometimes catastrophically were — the consequences fell on real people. Heilbroner: "Unlike the physical scientists, these men could not protect themselves and the world from the implications of their work by relegating it to the laboratory."

The act of faith that united all the worldly philosophers was a particular kind of courage. They believed that the milling, chaotic world of commerce — the pauper and the speculator, the landlord and the factory hand, the mob and the greengrocer — could be woven into a single coherent tapestry. That what looked like random individual decisions could, when viewed from high enough, reveal pattern and law. This was not obvious. For centuries before Smith, the idea that markets had natural laws was no more apparent than the idea that planets had natural laws before Copernicus.

The book is structured as a series of portraits. Each chapter follows one thinker — or a pair of thinkers — through the social crisis that animated their work, the ideas they developed in response, and the legacy they left behind. The portrait format matters because Heilbroner insists the ideas cannot be fully understood apart from the men who held them and the world that produced those men. Ricardo's abstract model of landlord rents makes a different kind of sense once you know he was arguing against the Corn Laws at a time when bread cost nearly twice a workman's weekly wage.

There is also a larger argument threading through all the portraits. Economics did not develop in a steady upward line of cumulative knowledge. It lurched — from optimism to pessimism, from social critique to mathematical formalism, from sweeping historical vision to pinpoint technical analysis — driven each time by the social pressures of its era. The book's final chapter asks whether modern economics, in its flight toward scientific respectability, has abandoned the broader mission that gave it its power in the first place.

The opening epigraph comes from Keynes himself, writing in 1936, and it sets the tone for everything that follows:

"The ideas of economists and political philosophers, both when they are right and when they are wrong, are more powerful than is commonly understood. Indeed the world is ruled by little else. Practical men, who believe themselves to be quite exempt from any intellectual influences, are usually the slaves of some defunct economist." — Lord Keynes

Chapter II: The Economic Revolution

Before the worldly philosophers could exist, something extraordinary had to happen — something so fundamental that Heilbroner calls it "more disturbing by far than the French, the American, or even the Russian Revolution." Human society had organized its survival for thousands of years through two mechanisms: tradition and command. Tradition passed tasks down through custom and caste. Command enforced production through the whip of authority. The pyramids were not built by enterprising contractors seeking profit; they were built because a pharaoh said build them. India's caste system did not assign workers to occupations through market signals; birth determined what you would do. Neither system required economists because neither system relied on anything as complicated or self-regulating as a market.

What the market revolution introduced — and what took centuries of violence and anguish to establish — was a third possibility: allow each person to follow their own monetary self-interest, and trust that the necessary tasks of society will somehow get done. This sounds simple. It was, in practice, a total reorganization of human society. The scale of pre-market commerce was almost comically small. When merchant Andreas Ryff traveled through France in 1550, he paid thirty-one separate customs levies between Basle and Cologne — roughly one toll every ten miles. All the goods crossing via the Saint Gothard pass in an entire year would not have filled a modern freight train.

The profit motive itself was not natural or universal. Sir William Petty observed in the seventeenth century that when wages rose, labor became scarce — workers took more time off rather than working more. The drive to better one's material lot was absent in ancient Egypt, in classical Greece, in medieval Europe. The medieval Church taught that no Christian ought to be a merchant; in Shakespeare's time, the ordinary goal of life was to maintain one's station, not to advance it. The Fugger family — great sixteenth-century German bankers who owned mines and trade concessions and the right to coin money — were regularly offered the chance to expand their empire, and as regularly refused. Old Anton Fugger's nephew George reportedly said he "would rather live in peace."

The categories that seem most natural to a modern market economy — land, labor, and capital — did not exist as abstract, tradeable commodities in the medieval world. Land was not property in the modern sense; it was the core of a social system of obligation, prestige, and military organization. A medieval nobleman selling his estate "would be as unthinkable as a Connecticut governor selling counties to Rhode Island." There was no labor market because serfs were bound to estates and apprentices were regulated by guilds in every detail of their working lives. Capital existed but there was no impetus to use it aggressively — the guild motto was "Safety first," and advertising was forbidden. When mass weaving production appeared in sixteenth-century England, guilds protested and the king outlawed the "wonderworkshop" as "a bad precedent."

The transition from traditional to market society took from roughly the thirteenth to the nineteenth century and was violent throughout. To commercialize land required uprooting the entire social structure of feudalism. To create a mobile labor force required dispossessing people who had lived on common lands for generations. The Enclosures — the conversion of common pasture to privately owned sheep runs — produced mass displacement. John Hales wrote in 1549: "where XL persons had their lyvings, now one man and his shepherd hath all." By 1820, the Duchess of Sutherland had dispossessed 15,000 tenants from 794,000 acres and replaced them with 131,000 sheep.

The resistance was fierce and sometimes murderous. In France, importing calico fabric was deemed so threatening to the domestic textile industry that enforcement of the ban cost 16,000 lives — 77 hanged, 58 broken on the wheel, 631 sent to the galleys in Valence alone — for the crime of wearing forbidden cloth. In 1779, a mob of 8,000 workers burned a mill. By 1811 the Luddite protests swept England, and Southey warned that "nothing but the Army preserves us from that most dreadful of all calamities, an insurrection of the poor against the rich." The market revolution was not welcomed by its victims.

What finally tipped the balance were four converging forces. The rise of national political units broke up feudal fragmentation and replaced 112 local measures of length and 92 square measures with national laws and currencies. The decay of religious spirit — accelerated by the Protestant Reformation — gave acquisitiveness a new moral status. Where Catholic doctrine had treated usury as sin, Protestant leaders preached that it was pious to make the most of God-given talents; riches eventually became identified with spiritual election. Technical changes — the printing press, double-entry bookkeeping, the mechanical clock — made large-scale commercial organization possible for the first time. And a rising tide of scientific curiosity turned human attention from the next world to this one.

By 1700, the new dicta were in place: "Every man is naturally covetous of lucre"; "Gaine is the Centre of the Circle of Commerce." The idea of "economic man" — a pale wraith who followed his adding-machine brain wherever it led — was being born. Early social philosophy took the form of Mercantilism: the accumulation of gold as the goal of economic policy, Thomas Mun's prescription that "wee must ever observe this rule; to sell more to strangers yearly than wee consume of theirs in Value." The time was ready for a thinker who could explain, justify, and give intellectual shape to the whole strange new world. Adam Smith appeared, right on schedule, in 1776.

Chapter III: The Wonderful World of Adam Smith

Adam Smith was born in 1723 in Kirkcaldy, Scotland, a town of about 1,500 people where nails were still used as local currency. At age four he was briefly kidnapped by gypsies. He grew up to be legendarily absent-minded — once walking fifteen miles in his dressing gown before snapping out of a reverie, frequently talking to himself in the street. At Oxford on a scholarship, he spent six years at a university where the professors had abandoned even the pretense of instruction; one account describes a 1788 faculty meeting where all four participants read novels in silence. Smith was nearly expelled for possessing a copy of David Hume's Treatise of Human Nature. He found Oxford's intellectual climate so deadening that he later called it the best argument for a reformed university system.

His real education came from the Scottish Enlightenment in Glasgow, where he held the Chair of Moral Philosophy at age twenty-eight and became beloved by students for lectures that ranged across jurisprudence, ethics, and rhetoric. His first book, The Theory of Moral Sentiments (1759), made him famous overnight. Its central puzzle — how can self-interested man form moral judgments that transcend self-interest? — was answered through the concept of the impartial spectator: we imaginatively put ourselves in a third-person position and judge our own conduct from the outside. The book caught the attention of Charles Townshend, the Chancellor of the Exchequer who would later, with characteristic ineptitude, help precipitate the American Revolution by imposing duty on American tea. Townshend hired Smith to tutor the young Duke of Buccleuch for a Grand Tour of Europe, offering £500 per year plus expenses — nearly three times Smith's professorial income.

In France, Smith met and worshipped Voltaire and in Paris encountered François Quesnay, physician to Louis XV and founder of Physiocracy. Quesnay had developed the tableau économique, a diagram showing how wealth circulated through society, and Mirabeau the Elder ranked it with writing and money as one of the three great inventions of history. Physiocracy's fatal flaw was its claim that only agricultural labor truly produced wealth — manufacturing merely "altered its form." Smith took the framework and threw out the flaw. Labor, not nature, is the source of value. He would have dedicated The Wealth of Nations to Quesnay had Quesnay not died first.

Back in Kirkcaldy, Smith spent ten years writing, dictating while standing at his fireplace and rubbing his head against the wall until a dark streak formed on the paneling. He discussed ideas with Dr. Samuel Johnson (their first meeting featured both men calling each other liars) and with Benjamin Franklin, who described America's future importance with enough clarity that Smith noted it "seemed very likely to become one of the greatest and most formidable nations that ever was in the world." The result was The Wealth of Nations, published in 1776 — the same year American political democracy was born — "the outpouring not only of a great mind, but of a whole epoch." The index alone runs sixty-three pages. Smith digresses for seventy-five pages on the history of silver.

The first great idea in The Wealth of Nations is the invisible hand. Self-interest, left to itself, produces not chaos but order — because competition constrains it. The butcher who overcharges loses customers to a rival; the employer who underpays loses workers to a competitor who pays more. The famous formulation: "It is not from the benevolence of the butcher, the brewer, or the baker that we expect our dinner, but from their regard to their self-interest." What is revolutionary here is not the observation that people act selfishly, but the claim that selfish action — disciplined by competition — produces social coordination without anyone planning it. No authority needed to tell the glovemaker to make more gloves when demand rose; rising prices signaled that opportunity, and capital and labor flowed accordingly.

"People of the same trade seldom meet together, even for merriment and diversion, but the conversation ends in a conspiracy against the public, or in some diversion to raise prices." — Adam Smith, The Wealth of Nations

The second great idea is the division of labor, illustrated by Smith's famous pin factory. One man working alone could not make twenty pins a day — possibly not one. Ten men, each specializing in a single step of the process, could produce forty-eight thousand pins a day. Specialization drives productivity, and productivity drives the "universal opulence which extends itself to the lowest ranks of the people." Even a day-laborer's coarse wool coat, Smith calculated, required the cooperation of thousands of workers: shepherds, wool-combers, dyers, weavers, fullers, and the sailors and merchants who moved raw material and finished goods. What looked like simple poverty was, by any historical standard, extraordinary material wealth.

The third idea is the law of accumulation. The capitalist's primary drive is to save and reinvest. Richard Arkwright died leaving £500,000. Josiah Wedgwood left £240,000. Samuel Walker built a forge into a steel foundry worth £200,000. This accumulation funded machinery, which enabled further division of labor, which multiplied output, which created demand for more workers, which temporarily raised wages. Smith also articulated the law of population: like any commodity, labor is produced to meet demand. Higher wages reduced infant mortality, raised population, eventually brought wages back down. His full growth model was an endless chain: accumulation → machinery → division of labor → productivity → wages → population → back to subsistence, and around again.

Smith's real enemy, throughout the book, was monopoly in any form. He was as suspicious of businessmen as he was of government regulation — he thought both would conspire against the public given the chance. The passage about men of the same trade never meeting without scheming to raise prices was not a throwaway line; it was a warning. Smith also argued that mass production stupefied workers and called for government to prevent it — a passage conveniently ignored by the industrialists who made him their patron saint. The Wealth of Nations was not quoted in Parliament for eight years, and then only by Charles James Fox, who admitted he had never read it. Full recognition came around 1800, by which time the book's comfortable message — let the market alone — had been extracted and the uncomfortable parts quietly set aside. When Smith entered a dinner hosted by William Pitt, the Prime Minister and all the guests rose; Pitt refused to sit until Smith was seated first: "We are all your scholars."

Chapter IV: The Gloomy Presentiments of Parson Malthus and David Ricardo

In the forty years after The Wealth of Nations, something went badly wrong with Smith's optimistic vision. England had split into two hostile camps: the rising industrial capitalists who wanted cheap grain (low food prices meant lower wages meant lower production costs), and the great landowners who controlled Parliament and wanted high grain prices. East Lothian farms were returning 56 percent on capital; rents had doubled across England in twenty-five years; wheat reached 117 shillings per quarter — nearly twice a workman's entire weekly wage. Parliament's response was the Corn Laws, a sliding tariff that effectively created a permanent price floor for domestic wheat. Into this charged atmosphere came two gloomy successors to Adam Smith who gave economics its reputation as the dismal science.

Reverend Thomas Robert Malthus published his Essay on the Principle of Population in 1798, anonymously, as a direct attack on the utopian hopefulness of William Godwin. Godwin had promised a future with "no war, no crime, no administration of justice, no disease, anguish, or resentment." Malthus's father was an admirer of Godwin; the young parson debated his father and became convinced of what he called an "insurmountable barrier" between existing society and any such paradise. The barrier was arithmetic: population tends by nature to double every twenty-five years (a modest estimate — America had actually managed it for 150 years), while the productive capacity of land can grow only arithmetically. The ratio: 1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32, 64, 128 against 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8. In 225 years, 512 to 10.

"Famine seems to be the last, the most dreadful resource of nature... gigantic inevitable famine stalks in the rear, and with one mighty blow, levels the population with the food of the world." — Thomas Robert Malthus

The gap between population and subsistence was kept sealed by what Malthus called the "positive checks" — war, disease, poverty, and infanticide. The only voluntary escape was moral restraint: delayed marriage and celibacy. Helping the poor, Malthus argued, only aggravated total misery — the infamous line about "no vacant cover at nature's mighty feast." He was "the best abused man of his age"; students at Haileybury College called him "Pop" for the cleft palate that made his speech distinctive, and debated his ideas endlessly. The population principle seemed to foreclose the possibility of permanent improvement, which was exactly Malthus's intention. No social engineering could outrun the multiplication of mouths.

David Ricardo was Malthus's temperamental opposite in almost every way. Malthus was an empiricist who distrusted abstraction; Ricardo was an abstract theorist who distrusted facts. Malthus came from the English upper-middle class; Ricardo was the son of a Jewish merchant-banker from Holland who left school at fourteen. Malthus spent his life in modest academic comfort; Ricardo retired at forty-two with somewhere between £500,000 and £1,600,000 (estimates vary) made as a stockbroker. At the battle of Waterloo, Ricardo held a maximum bull position in government securities; when Wellington won, he made what was described as an "immense killing" while Malthus was "discomfited." They met in 1809 and became fast friends despite disagreeing on almost everything for the rest of their lives.

Ricardo's great contribution was the theory of differential rent. Consider two neighboring farms: Farm A (fertile soil) produces 1,500 bushels with the same inputs that yield only 1,000 bushels on Farm B. Both farms sell grain at the same market price. Farm A's owner pockets the difference in production costs as rent — a pure windfall from nature's inequality, earned without additional labor or capital. If it's barely profitable to farm bad land at two dollars a bushel, the good-land owner producing at fifty cents pockets $1.50 as rent. As population grew and society was forced to farm progressively worse land, the margin between good and bad land widened — and rent soared while wages and profits were squeezed.

Ricardo's full system was a tragedy in three acts. Capitalists accumulate; more workers are hired; wages temporarily rise. Higher wages support larger families; the labor supply expands; wages fall back to subsistence. More mouths mean less fertile land must be farmed; grain costs rise; wages must rise again to keep workers alive. Capitalist profits are squeezed between rising wages and rising rents. The landlord, who does nothing, gains everything. "The interest of the landlords is always opposed to the interest of every other class in the community." Ricardo's own formulation, written in 1815, made the Corn Laws not just a policy error but a structural offense against the productive classes.

Malthus fought back with what he called the problem of the general glut — the possibility that commodities could flood the market with no buyers to absorb them. Ricardo dismissed this as logically impossible. He relied on Jean-Baptiste Say's law: every act of production creates exactly as much income as is needed to buy what was produced; demand must equal supply in aggregate; a general glut cannot occur. Malthus sensed a flaw — what if saving caused demand to fall short of production? — but couldn't articulate it precisely. Ricardo replied: "Mr. Malthus never appears to remember that to save is to spend." The argument was unresolved at Ricardo's death in 1823. He died suddenly at fifty-one, and Malthus wrote: "I never loved anybody out of my own family so much." The glut controversy would sleep for a century, then awaken as the central problem of the Great Depression.

Together, Malthus and Ricardo turned economics from optimism to pessimism. Smith's system was, in the long run, a happy one — accumulation drove growth, growth raised living standards, progress was the natural condition of a market economy. After Malthus and Ricardo, the natural condition looked more like a trap: workers condemned to subsistence by population mechanics, capitalists condemned to shrinking profits by rising rents, landlords rewarded for doing nothing. Both men shared Smith's blind spot: the working class appeared only as a passive force, driven by biological imperatives, incapable of reorganizing society on its own terms. That realization would fall to someone else.

Chapter V: The Dreams of the Utopian Socialists

The social cost of the Industrial Revolution was not abstract. The Lion, a radical newspaper, published the story of Robert Blincoe in 1828 — one of eighty pauper children shipped to a factory at Lowdham at age ten, where they were whipped day and night, where employer Ellice Needham pinched children's ears until his nails met through the flesh, where children worked nearly naked in winter. Sixteen-hour workdays were common. Factory clocks were accelerated during meal breaks to shorten the actual rest. Workers were forbidden to carry their own watches. The laws of Smith, Malthus, and Ricardo seemed to explain not just how wealth was distributed but how it had to be. Nature decreed it. The Utopians' response was to say: if the laws look inviolable and the system is intolerable, then the whole system must change.

Robert Owen was the most practical of the Utopians — a self-made man who started with £100 borrowed from his brother and ended up owning one of Scotland's largest textile mills. At age eighteen, he walked into a Manchester spinning firm, demanded £300 a year — more than any other applicant had asked — and got the job. Within years he had fallen in love with the daughter of Mr. Dale, a wealthy mill owner, and arranged to buy her father's mills at New Lanark by borrowing capital, winning the mills, and winning the daughter as a package deal. Within a year he had transformed New Lanark and made a fortune of at least £60,000.

What set Owen apart was his core belief: mankind is no better than its environment. Change the environment, achieve paradise. At New Lanark, he put this to the test. Workers were housed in homes with two rooms each. A colored-cube deportment system replaced punishment; black cubes on a worker's post indicated yesterday's behavior, white cubes indicated excellence. No children under ten or eleven were employed. The workday was ten and three-quarter hours — shorter than elsewhere. No corporal punishment. A schoolhouse where no child's question was ever to go unanswered. Between 1815 and 1825, twenty thousand visitors came to observe, including the Grand Duke Nicholas, the future Tsar of Russia. And New Lanark was marvelously profitable — proof that squalor was not economically necessary.

Emboldened, Owen moved from factory reform to the redesign of civilization. He proposed Villages of Cooperation: communities of eight hundred to twelve hundred people working farm and factory in self-sustaining units, their houses arranged in parallelograms, children over three boarded separately for character-forming education. A special committee including Ricardo was formed to raise £96,000 for a trial village; the funding never materialized. William Cobbett was appalled: "This gentleman is for establishing communities of paupers!... of such a thing as a community of paupers, I believe no human being has ever before heard." Owen sold his New Lanark interest and bought thirty thousand acres on the Wabash River in Indiana, from a German Rappite religious sect, to found New Harmony.

New Harmony failed immediately and spectacularly. Eight hundred settlers arrived helter-skelter without preparation or selection. Owen was bilked by an associate who set up a whiskey distillery. Rival communities sprang up on the same land. By 1828 Owen admitted the failure; he had lost four-fifths of his fortune. But the working classes had taken Owen's ideas seriously in a way the upper classes never had. Twenty-eight weavers known as the Rochdale Pioneers drew on Owen's principles to found the consumer cooperative movement, which became a major pillar of the British Labour Party. Owen lived until 1858, age eighty-seven, "still as buoyant and as hopeful as ever" — dying as he had lived, certain that paradise was just around the corner.

Count Henri de Rouvroy de Saint-Simon was the aristocratic eccentric of the Utopian movement. He had his valet wake him each morning with: "Arise, Monsieur le Comte, you have great things to do today." He had fought in the American Revolutionary War, proposed a canal to precede the Panama Canal, speculated in Church lands during the French Revolution, and turned himself in to prison when his hotel proprietor was accused of collaborating in his escape — a gesture of gentlemanly honor that cost him his fortune. In prison, Charlemagne appeared to him in a dream and told him his family was reserved to produce both a hero and a philosopher of the first rank. He spent the rest of his life trying to be the philosopher.

Saint-Simon's thought experiment remains devastating two centuries later. Imagine France suddenly lost its fifty leading physicists, chemists, mathematicians, engineers, artists, and poets. Catastrophe — France would be robbed of her soul. Now imagine France lost the king's brother, the duchesses, the ministers of state, the officers of the Crown, the ten thousand richest proprietors. "The loss would be purely a sentimental one; the state would hardly suffer." This was not socialism in the modern sense; it was a protest against idle wealth and a paean to productive work. Saint-Simon died after shooting himself in despair — only succeeding in losing an eye — with the words: "Remember that in order to do great things one must be impassioned!"

Charles Fourier was the most bizarre of the three — a small-town French tradesman who believed he alone had "confounded twenty centuries of political imbecility." He worked out a complete cosmology: the earth had an eighty-thousand-year lifespan; five of eight stages of advancement had passed; in full Harmony, the North Pole would sprout a Northern Crown, the sea would become lemonade, six new moons would orbit, and new species including the antilion and the antiwhale would appear. Humans would live 144 years, 120 of them in unrestricted sexual love. Despite the madness, his practical prescription was precise: phalansteries, grand-hotel communities of 1,600 residents where everyone worked only at what they liked. Dirty work would be assigned to children, who enjoy filth by nature (the Little Hordes to slaughterhouses and road repair; the Little Bands to tend flowers). Profits split: five-twelfths to labor, four-twelfths to capital, three-twelfths to ability. Fourier spent his last years sitting in his room waiting for the great capitalist who would fund his scheme. No one ever came.

John Stuart Mill, the greatest economist of his generation, arrived at Utopian conclusions by a very different route. His father James Mill gave him Greek at age three; by seven he had read most of Plato; by twelve he had completed a survey of political economy and a Roman history. He had no holidays and no boyhood friends. In his twenties he suffered a nervous breakdown — the dry intellectual world had become sterile — and was rescued by Goethe, Wordsworth, and Harriet Taylor, with whom he wrote and traveled for twenty years before Mr. Taylor's convenient death made marriage possible. Mill insisted: "it is the product not of one intellect and conscience, but of three." His great discovery, buried in Principles of Political Economy (1848), was the distinction between the laws of production and the laws of distribution. Nature governs production — scarcity, diminishing returns — but distribution depends on "the laws and customs of society." It could be arranged differently. This was a body blow to Ricardo's fatalistic system: no appeal to natural law could justify the existing distribution of wealth. The New Deal and the Scandinavian welfare states are, in a direct line, expressions of Mill's vision.

Chapter VI: The Inexorable System of Karl Marx

The Communist Manifesto opens: "A spectre is haunting Europe — the spectre of Communism." It was published in February 1848, and within weeks the spectre had become flesh: Louis Philippe abdicated and fled to a Surrey villa, Parisian workingmen ran up the Red Flag over the Hôtel de Ville, Berlin saw barricades, Prague and Vienna were seized by popular uprisings. The uprisings were spontaneous and aimless — they won initial victories and then stalled. Paris mobs were subdued at a cost of ten thousand casualties. Louis Napoleon converted the Second Republic into the Second Empire. The Communist League, whose manifesto had helped light the fuse, viewed the events as a dress rehearsal. Unlike the Utopians, who had appealed to sympathy and idealism, the Manifesto offered something colder: not a moral argument but a mechanical prediction. The proletariat's victory was not a matter of justice. It was a matter of historical inevitability.

Karl Marx and Friedrich Engels were as different as their work was unified. Marx: dark-skinned (they called him "The Moor"), stocky, formidable beard, his home a chaos of papers and tobacco smoke, ponderous, meticulous, morbidly perfectionist. Engels: tall, fair, elegant, athletic — he could stutter in twenty languages, rode to hounds, and loved wine; facile, quick, broad. "Where Engels supplied breadth and dash, Marx provided the depth." Their first real conversation, in Paris in 1844, lasted ten days. Engels had been sent to Manchester to manage his pietist father's textile mill and found himself unable to look away from what he saw. Manchester had two faces: pleasant suburbs for mill owners, and a hidden population "living in a state of filth and despair, turning to gin and evangelism and doping itself and its children with laudanum." He told a gentleman friend about the misery; the friend listened quietly and replied: "And yet there is a great deal of money made here; good day, sir." The exchange radicalized Engels permanently.

The intellectual foundation Marx and Engels built was dialectical materialism. It was dialectical in Hegel's sense — change is the fundamental rule of life; every idea or social arrangement breeds its opposite. It was materialist in Marx's revision of Hegel — the driver of change is not ideas but social and physical conditions, specifically the organization of production. The famous formulation: "The hand-mill gives you society with the feudal lord; the steam-mill, society with the industrial capitalist." Change the technology, and you change the social relations built on it — and with them the law, government, religion, and philosophy that serve as the superstructure of the economic base. Every society, at any moment, contains within itself the seeds of its own supersession.

Marx's economic analysis centered on the concept of surplus value. He erected the most idealized capitalism imaginable — no monopolies, no unions, everything selling at exact value — and then showed that even this perfect capitalism was destined to destroy itself. The key insight: labor power is the one special commodity. Its value — what it costs to reproduce — is the subsistence wage. But the worker doesn't just produce his own subsistence; he works a full day. If subsistence takes six hours to earn, the remaining four or five hours produce surplus value — unpaid labor appropriated by the capitalist. This is not fraud; it is the perfectly legal, perfectly normal operation of a system in which workers have nothing to sell but their labor. Evidence from Manchester factories in 1862: the average work week over a month and a half was eighty-four hours.

"What the bourgeoisie therefore produces, above all, are its own gravediggers. Its fall and the victory of the proletariat are equally inevitable." — Karl Marx and Friedrich Engels, The Communist Manifesto

The mechanism of capitalism's self-destruction was the falling rate of profit. Capitalists compete; to compete they must accumulate; to accumulate they bid wages up; to defend profits they introduce labor-saving machinery; the machinery throws workers onto the street, creating an Industrial Reserve Army that pushes wages back down. But by substituting machines for men, the capitalist substitutes nonprofitable means of production for profitable ones. Only living labor creates surplus value; machines transfer their value to products but generate no new value. "He is killing the goose that lays the golden egg" — and he must do it, because competitors will if he doesn't. The ratio of living labor to total output shrinks; the rate of profit falls; production eventually becomes unprofitable; consumption collapses; depression follows. A Greek tragedy in which all the actors cooperate to bring about their own destruction.

Marx's personal life during the writing of Das Kapital was a sustained catastrophe. He and Jenny von Westphalen — the daughter of a Prussian aristocrat who had chosen the dark radical next door — shared two dismal rooms in a London slum. They once begged money to buy a coffin for one of their children. Engels in Manchester, living a double life as a respectable Stock Exchange figure, supplied never-ending checks. Marx worked daily at the British Museum from ten in the morning to seven at night; wrote articles for the New York Tribune for income; was once rejected for a railway clerical job because of his atrocious handwriting. After writing in agony all day, he came home and remarked: "I hope the bourgeoisie as long as they live will have cause to remember my carbuncles" — he had just finished the chapter on the Working Day. Jenny died in 1881; Marx lingered two more years, finally declared "I am not a Marxist," and died quietly.

Heilbroner's verdict on Marx is the most carefully balanced section of the book. The errors are real: the labor theory of value contains a mathematical inconsistency when translating value-world into price-world; the prediction that capitalism would develop a long secular downward trend in profits has not materialized; the revolution came to Russia and Eastern Europe, not to the industrial West that Marx was describing. The intolerance Marx injected into working-class politics — his ruthless excommunication of rivals, his inability to tolerate dissent — is communism's "single greatest inherited flaw." Yet the book remains, in Heilbroner's phrase, "the gravest, most penetrating examination the capitalist system has ever undergone." The predictions about concentration, proletarianization, business cycles, and the dynamism required to sustain profits were all broadly vindicated. What Marx missed was not economic but social: the possibility that democratic institutions, welfare states, and pragmatic reform could redirect capitalism's energies without overturning it.

The best formulation is Heilbroner's own: Marx is not right or wrong so much as unavoidable. He invented a new kind of inquiry — the critique of the economic system itself, not from outside but from within its own categories. As Freud taught us to look through personality to the psychic processes underneath, Marx taught us to look through history to the class conflicts that drove it. You can reject his conclusions while remaining permanently changed by his way of seeing. The footprints are indelibly imprinted on the continent of social thought.

Chapter VII: The Victorian World and the Underworld of Economics

Something strange happened after Marx. The Manifesto had pronounced capitalism terminally ill; many of its predictions about bigness and concentration were being confirmed. But the one symptom that would have sealed the diagnosis — the "increasing misery of the proletariat" — was not increasing. By the 1880s, Sir Robert Giffen could document that for the first time, English workers were earning real wages high enough to keep body and soul together. Hours were falling too: at the Jarrow Shipyards the work week dropped from sixty-one to fifty-four hours. Engels was dismayed: "The English proletariat is actually becoming more and more bourgeois — the most bourgeois of all nations." The revolution kept not arriving.

Economics responded to capitalism's unexpected stability by becoming comfortable. It retreated into the university, shed its earlier social urgency, and turned its attention to mathematical precision. Francis Ysidro Edgeworth's Mathematical Psychics (1881) assumed every man was a pleasure machine — a direct descendant of Jeremy Bentham's Felicific Calculus — and proceeded to prove, mathematically, that in a world of perfect competition each pleasure machine achieved the maximum pleasure the system could provide. Trade unions were mathematically proven to lose in the long run. Inequality of birth was reconciled because some people were better pleasure machines than others. Henry Sidgwick protested: "I eat dinner not because I toted up satisfactions but because I am hungry." The establishment ignored him.

Leon Walras in France proved mathematically the exact prices that would clear every market simultaneously — requiring, in principle, equations for every single good in the economy, running to hundreds of thousands. Theoretically possible; practically absurd; and fatally static. Alfred Marshall's Principles of Economics (1890), the most durable textbook of the era, introduced the concept of equilibrium — the self-adjusting, self-correcting tendency of economic life. Keynes later wrote that Marshall had created "a whole Copernican system, in which all the elements of the economic universe are kept in their places by mutual counterpoise." Marshall's great innovation was insisting on the importance of time — distinguishing the short run (when supply is fixed and utility drives price) from the long run (when supply adjusts and cost reasserts itself). Supply and demand were like the blades of scissors: fruitless to ask which one does the cutting.

Marshall's limitation was that his "time" was abstract and mathematical, not historical and irreversible. He lived through the Russian Revolution and the First World War while his motto remained Natura non facit saltum ("nature makes no sudden leaps") — from 1890 to 1920, unchanged. He stripped the word "Political" from Political Economy and with it removed the central question that had animated all the earlier thinkers: how do relations of power and obedience structure the economy? Heilbroner calls this "an intellectual tragedy of the first order": had Marshall possessed the moral outrage of the men in the underworld of economics, the catastrophes of the twentieth century might have been anticipated.

The underworld of economics was what Heilbroner calls the parallel tradition of cranks, heretics, and dangerous thinkers excluded from the academic mainstream. Bernard Mandeville's Fable of the Bees had suggested, in 1714, that private immorality might serve public welfare — profligate rich give work to the poor; virtuous penny-pinchers do not. He was convicted as a public nuisance by a Middlesex grand jury. Frédéric Bastiat attacked economic orthodoxy with ridicule: his famous Petition of the Candlemakers asked Parliament to block windows and shutters to shut out the sun, which flooded the market with light at zero price — the same logic used to justify tariffs against foreign competition. He died in 1850 whispering "Truth, truth...."

Henry George arrived at his theory with the force of religious revelation. He had left school at thirteen, sailed as a foremast boy, failed as a gold prospector, and scraped along as a journalist in San Francisco. During his wife's second pregnancy, with no money, he stopped a stranger on the street and asked for five dollars: "If he had not given it to me, I think I was desperate enough to have killed him." One day, riding in the hills outside Oakland, he received a vision of such clarity that he "dismounted and wept." As a city grows, every improvement — every new road, every factory, every school — drives up the value of land. The worker gets no higher wages; the capitalist gets no higher return; but the landlord, who contributed nothing, pockets all the gain. The remedy: a Single Tax absorbing all land rents, replacing every other tax, extirpating poverty at a stroke.

Progress and Poverty (1879) became one of the greatest bestsellers in American publishing history — selling more copies than all economic texts previously published in the United States combined. Henry George beat Theodore Roosevelt in a three-cornered New York mayoral race; only the Tammany machine kept him from winning. His ideas filtered, in watered form, into Woodrow Wilson, John Dewey, and Louis Brandeis. General Francis Walker called George's proposal "a project so steeped in infamy." Heilbroner's assessment: the diagnosis was superficial (rental income as share of US national income shrank from 6 percent in 1929 to under 2 percent — the rent "problem" was far smaller than George claimed) but the moral question was real — why should anyone benefit from the mere passage of time and the labor of others?

J.A. Hobson built the most consequential theory to come out of the Victorian underworld. A shy Oxford graduate, he had been radicalized by A.F. Mummery, a mountain climber and businessman who argued that depressions were caused by excessive saving — the chronic inability of the business system to distribute enough purchasing power to buy its own output. Hobson and Mummery co-wrote The Physiology of Industry; London University Extension Lectures dropped him as a result. He then traveled to Africa, met Cecil Rhodes and Smuts, witnessed the Boer War, and came back with Imperialism (1902): the colonies were the structural escape valve for the undigested domestic savings that the poor couldn't spend and the rich couldn't consume. All capitalist nations facing the same problem simultaneously would inevitably race to partition the world — and that race would end in war. Marx said capitalism would destroy itself. Hobson said it might destroy the world. Lenin adopted Hobson's framework wholesale and made it the capstone of Marxist doctrine.

Chapter VIII: The Savage Society of Thorstein Veblen

The Gilded Age in America produced a kind of economic life that the existing vocabulary of economics was completely inadequate to describe. Cornelius Vanderbilt wrote to business associates threatening his interests: "Gentlemen: You have undertaken to ruin me. I will not sue you, for law takes too long. I will ruin you." J. Pierpont Morgan: "I don't know as I want a lawyer to tell me what I cannot do. I hire him to tell me how to do what I want to do." Jim Fisk and Morgan fought for the Albany-Susquehanna Railroad by each side mounting a locomotive and running them into each other. American academic economists described this milieu in terms of "thrift and accumulation," "enterprise," and "consumption." John Bates Clark's Distribution of Wealth could be read cover to cover without suspecting that America was a land of millionaires. What was needed was someone genuinely foreign to the scene — someone who could see it with unclouded eyes.

Thorstein Bunde Veblen was "an American by birth but a citizen of nowhere by nature." Born in 1857, the fourth son of Norwegian immigrant farmers on the Minnesota frontier, he grew up in a tight Norwegian-American enclave and learned English as a foreign tongue. He was sent to Carleton College Academy, where he threw the faculty into uproar with a paper called "A Plea for Cannibalism," which he described as "scientific observations." His teacher was John Bates Clark, who would later become one of the eminent economists Veblen would demolish. After a Yale Ph.D. in 1884, Veblen spent seven years of isolation on the family farm — his brother noted that "Thorstein was the only loafer in a highly respectable community... He read and loafed."

In 1891, Veblen walked into J. Laurence Laughlin's office at Cornell wearing a coonskin cap and corduroy trousers and announced: "I am Thorstein Veblen." He wanted to study economics. Laughlin put him to work. When Laughlin moved to head the new University of Chicago economics department — a university founded by Rockefeller, Veblen would later note — he brought Veblen along at $520 a year. His classes started large and dwindled to one student; those who stayed found it worth it. He refused the telephone, stacked books in packing cases, didn't make beds, washed dishes by hosing them down, and gave all students the same grade regardless of their work. His writing style was "razorlike English" — involuted, precise, and devastatingly funny. Philanthropy was "essays in pragmatic romance." Religion was "the fabrication of vendible imponderables in the nth dimension." Individual churches were "retail outlets."

The Theory of the Leisure Class (1899), published when Veblen was forty-two, became the vade mecum of the American intelligentsia — "electric, grotesque, shocking, and amusing." Its central question: what is economic man actually like, and how does a leisure class form? Classical economists answered with rational self-interest: successful men naturally minimize labor once fortune allows. Veblen's anthropology showed that primitive societies — American Indians, the Ainus of Japan, the Todas of the Nilgiri hills, Australian bushmen — had no leisure class at all. Everyone worked; pride of workmanship drove effort. The leisure class appeared only in societies rich enough to sustain non-workers and aggressive enough to respect predation — the Polynesians, the ancient Icelanders, the feudal Japanese shogunate. These leisured figures were not idlers; they were predators who seized riches by force and cunning.

From this anthropological observation, Veblen built a theory of modern social behavior. Because predatory prowess was historically admired, attitudes toward work inverted: predation became honorific, pure labor became tainted. The "irksomeness of work" that classical economists treated as an inherent human trait was, Veblen argued, a cultural artifact — something imposed by a particular social history. Modern man was "only a shade removed from his barbarian forebears": instead of collecting scalps, he collected money; lavish display was the modern tepee trophy. Conspicuous consumption, conspicuous waste, and the relentless keeping-up with the Joneses were not the rational maximizing of a pleasure machine — they were atavistic status competition dressed in a morning coat.

The insight that most unsettled Marx's framework was Veblen's account of social cohesion. The lower classes were not at swords' points with the upper; they were bound by common attitudes of emulation. Workers did not seek to displace their managers; they sought to imitate them. They accepted that their own work was less "dignified," that the businessman's activity was more valuable, that the aspiration to climb rather than to overthrow was the natural response to inequality. This explained what Marx had never adequately explained: why the revolution kept not arriving. Robert and Helen Lynd's study of Muncie, Indiana (Middletown) found during the Great Depression that working-class families retrenched on food and clothing before cutting "necessary" luxuries — Veblen's competitive emulation running even in the worst of times.

The Theory of Business Enterprise (1904) turned the picture upside down. The businessman was not the productive hero of the system; he was its saboteur. Society was a gigantic productive mechanism requiring engineers and technicians; the businessman's goal — accumulation — was alien to the machine's logic. He achieved profit by causing breakdowns so that values fluctuated and he could capitalize on the confusion. On top of the productive machine, businessmen had built a superstructure of credit, loans, and make-believe capitalizations — finance as parasite on production. U.S. Steel, formed in 1901 with real assets of about $682 million, was sold for $303 million in bonds, $510 million in preferred stock, and $508 million in common stock — a financial company twice the size of the real one. Steel rails were quoted at $28 per ton for thirteen years when production cost was under $14 a ton. Every efficiency gain was captured not for lower prices or higher wages but for the maintenance of make-believe finance.

Veblen was dismissed from Chicago over philandering scandals, drifted to Stanford, the University of Missouri, and finally the New School for Social Research. For a few years after the First World War, "Veblenism was shining in full brilliance" — there were Veblenists, Veblen Clubs, Veblen Girls. He hoped the Russian Revolution would usher in the age of engineers and technicians; was disappointed; gave signs, according to his students, of "a certain turning toward death." He turned down the presidency of the American Economic Association: "They didn't offer it to me when I needed it." Died in 1929 — months before the Great Crash — leaving instructions for cremation with no ritual, no tombstone, no biography. His request was ignored.

Chapter IX: The Heresies of John Maynard Keynes

By 1933, the United States had lost half its prosperity in four years. National income had fallen from $87 billion to $39 billion. Residential construction had collapsed by 95 percent. Nine million savings accounts had been wiped out. Eighty-five thousand businesses had failed. Salaries had fallen 40 percent, wages 60 percent, dividends 56 percent. Fourteen million people were unemployed. The striking thing — what Heilbroner emphasizes — is not just the scale of the catastrophe but the intellectual bewilderment it produced. Unemployment of this scale had not been listed among the possible ills of a market economy by any classical economist. It was deemed, by the logic of Say's Law, simply impossible. The economy would self-correct. It was not correcting.

John Maynard Keynes was born in 1883, the very year Karl Marx died. Where Marx was bitter and visionary, Keynes was buoyant and practical. He was a polymath who wrote a treatise on probability that Bertrand Russell called "impossible to praise too highly," accumulated £500,000 trading currencies half an hour a day while still in bed, served as Cambridge don and chairman of a life insurance company and Director of the Bank of England simultaneously, and knew Roosevelt, Churchill, Shaw, and Picasso. As Bursar of King's College he turned £30,000 into £380,000. He later said his one regret in life was not having drunk more champagne.

His career had taken him from the Cambridge circle of Alfred Marshall (who begged him to become a full-time economist) through civil service work at the India Office (his main task was shipping one pedigreed bull to Bombay), to the Treasury during the First World War, where men of ripe judgment said he contributed more to winning the war than any other civilian. In Paris in 1917, while Big Bertha shelled the city, he acquired Corot, Gauguin, Manet, and Cézanne for Britain — and a Cézanne for himself — during a special purchasing mission. At the Paris Peace Conference in 1919, he watched in "sick and weary" agony as Clemenceau outmaneuvered Wilson to impose a vindictive peace on Germany. He resigned, went home in a fury, and wrote The Economic Consequences of the Peace in December 1919. His prediction: the Versailles reparations would goad Germany into a formidable and dangerous resurgence. He was right within twenty years.

The business cycle — periods of boom followed by depression — had been observed in England for over a century. Explanations had ranged from the cosmic (W. Stanley Jevons blamed sunspots, noting that the business cycle from 1721 to 1878 averaged 10.46 years while sunspot periodicity was 10.45 years — until more careful calculation lengthened sunspot periodicity to 11 years and the theory collapsed) to the intuitive (Malthus's "general glut"). Keynes's diagnosis began with a structural change that had occurred since Malthus's time. In the early nineteenth century, those who saved were the same people who invested — saving was accumulation, a single two-sided coin. By the twentieth century this identity had broken down. Wealth had spread; corporations had grown enormous; savings now flowed from millions of anonymous households to businesses through financial intermediaries. Saving and investing had become divorced — separate operations by separate groups of people. Malthus had been right after all.

"In the long run we are all dead." — John Maynard Keynes, Tract on Monetary Reform (1923)

The mechanism Keynes identified was the savings leak. National prosperity flows like a river of income from hand to hand; every purchase moves money into someone else's pocket. The dangerous break: money saved does not go directly back into circulation. Normally savings re-enter the economy through investment — businesses borrow to expand. But there is nothing automatic about this. Business invests when it sees opportunity to profit; when the outlook is poor, the impetus to invest wanes. Savings accumulate but don't get invested. Income contracts. And as income contracts, even the savings dry up: Americans saved $3.7 billion in 1929; by 1932-33 they were drawing down old savings, not adding to new ones. The classical safety switch — excess savings pushing interest rates down and making borrowing cheap enough to stimulate investment — failed completely during the Depression. Interest rates fell to historic lows. Nothing happened.

The General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money (1936) demolished the classical assumption that the economy was a seesaw that always returned to full employment. The economy was not a seesaw; it was an elevator — it could go up, down, or stand still. Most unsettling: it could reach equilibrium with mass unemployment. The unemployed were economic zeros, exerting no effective demand on the marketplace; from the market's point of view, they might as well not exist. The system had no internal mechanism that would automatically pull them back in. Keynes wrote to George Bernard Shaw in 1935: "I believe myself to be writing a book on economic theory which will largely revolutionize... the way the world thinks about economic problems... I'm quite sure." He was not wrong.

The prescription was government spending as deliberate substitution for insufficient private investment. The state could fill the gap that fearful businessmen left empty. Keynes illustrated the logic with mock seriousness: if the Treasury were to fill old bottles with bank notes, bury them in disused coal mines, and leave it to private enterprise to dig them up again, "there need be no more unemployment." The New Deal's Hundred Days had already begun applying this medicine before the General Theory was published — not because Roosevelt had read Keynes, but because the situation was desperate enough to try anything. It worked, partially. National income rose 50 percent, unemployment fell substantially, but nine million remained out of work by 1936. Peacetime politics made the scale of spending actually needed — the wartime level of $103 billion that finally produced full employment — politically unimaginable in 1936.

What is often missed is how conservative Keynes's intent was. He saw government spending as a temporary bridge, not a permanent feature; he was explicitly anti-Communist; he wanted to save capitalism, not replace it. On Hayek's Road to Serfdom he wrote sympathetically but disagreed on conclusions — he wanted more planning, not less, provided the planners were morally oriented. "It is better that a man should tyrannize over his bank-balance than over his fellow citizens." His last great project was designing, with the Americans, the postwar monetary order at Bretton Woods in New Hampshire — the International Monetary Fund and World Bank, replacing the pre-war dog-eat-dog currency competition with cooperative monetary support. He dominated the conference by sheer force of personality. Made a lord — Baron Keynes of Tilton — he died in Sussex in April 1946. Services were held at Westminster Abbey.

Chapter X: The Contradictions of Joseph Schumpeter

Joseph Alois Schumpeter was born in Austria in 1883 — the same year as Keynes, the same year Marx died. He was schooled at the Theresianum, the exclusive school for sons of the aristocracy, after his mother married a distinguished general. He declared three wishes for his life: to be a great lover, a great horseman, and a great economist. He later said that life had granted him only two of the three. He was a star student at the University of Vienna under Eugen von Böhm-Bawerk; wrote his first book in Egypt while serving as financial adviser to a princess (he cut her rents in half and doubled her income); published The Theory of Economic Development at age twenty-seven. He shared with Keynes a deep admiration for cultivated bourgeois life and a genuine belief in capitalism's general values — and reached diametrically opposite conclusions about capitalism's future.

Schumpeter's argument began with a thought experiment he called the circular flow. Imagine a capitalism that is perfectly static — no growth, no innovation, just the same production reproducing itself year after year. In this imaginary economy, wages and rents absorb all output; capitalists receive nothing. This is not a flaw in the model; it is its central insight. In a static economy there is no place for profit. The standard explanations all fail: Smith wavered; Mill said profit was a reward for "abstinence" but couldn't explain why capitalists deserved a reward for acting in their own interest. Schumpeter's answer: profit arises only when the circular flow is disrupted by innovation — new or cheaper ways of making things, or wholly new things. The innovator produces at lower cost, earns a rent from the differential, but this rent is transient: imitators learn the trick, competition forces prices down, and profit disappears as the innovation becomes routine.

The agent of disruption was the entrepreneur — not a normal businessman following established routines, but someone who combined factors of production in genuinely new ways. Entrepreneurship was not hereditary; it was a special kind of leadership; the entrepreneur was always "somewhat an upstart, his ways readily laughed at." And once an entrepreneur broke through, followers swarmed — each innovator attracting imitators who borrowed heavily and invested in the new technology. This swarm of followers drove a boom; when the innovation's differential advantage was exhausted, investment fell, loans went bad, and a contraction followed. The business cycle was not an external shock but a natural consequence of capitalism's own growth process — built into the system's DNA.

Schumpeter lived his ideas with characteristic intensity. In 1919 he joined the German socialist nationalization commission; when asked how he could serve a movement he despised, he replied: "If somebody wants to commit suicide, it is a good thing if a doctor is present." He served briefly as Finance Minister of Austria, drafted a stabilization plan, and was forced to resign before it was approved. He became president of the Biedermann bank in Vienna — the bank failed during the turbulence of the 1920s — and, rather than use the bankruptcy law, paid his creditors in full and spent ten years paying off the debt from his academic income. When the Great Depression hit, he called it "a good cold douche" — a healthy purging of accumulated financial excess. His second wife, Annie, died in childbirth within a year of their marriage. At Harvard, he married a third time and settled into the most productive phase of his career.

"Can capitalism survive? No. I do not think it can." — Joseph Schumpeter, Capitalism, Socialism and Democracy (1942)

Capitalism, Socialism and Democracy (1942) is the most paradoxical of the great economic texts. Its opening argument is that capitalism is the most dynamic productive system in human history — driven by the "perennial gale of creative destruction" that obsoletes every existing advantage and constantly regenerates the economy. Its conclusion is that capitalism cannot survive — not because of Marx's economic contradictions, but because of a sociological one. Capitalism's own rationalism — its insistence on subjecting every claim to empirical scrutiny — eventually turns on itself. The same rationalist acid that dissolved the divine right of kings dissolves the moral authority of private property. The bourgeois finds to his amazement that "the rationalist attitude does not stop at the credentials of kings and popes but goes on to attack private property and the whole scheme of bourgeois values."

The deeper mechanism was institutional. Entrepreneurship — heroic, individual, disruptive — was being replaced by organized, bureaucratic R&D. The corporation had reduced innovation to routine; corporate management had replaced visionary leadership. The bourgeois family, "the great transmission belt of capitalist values" across generations, was shrinking and losing its social authority. And a class of disaffected intellectuals — educated by capitalism, employed in analysis and journalism, structurally critical of the system that supported them — was growing in influence. They could not build anything themselves, but they could delegitimize everything. Schumpeter's prognosis was not that capitalism would be overthrown from below by an angry proletariat (Marx's view), but that it would be corroded from within by its own rationalism and the parasitic intellectuals that rationalism produced.

Heilbroner's assessment is admiring but pointed. Schumpeter's vision of a bureaucratized, routinized capitalism losing its élan seems prescient for 1942. But vast multinationals did not become passive giants — they compete aggressively in world markets. And the 1990s brought a revival of entrepreneurial energy and popular faith in capitalism (partly driven by the collapse of the Soviet Union) that flatly contradicted the sociological drift Schumpeter had predicted. More fundamentally, Schumpeter's framework was not ultimately an economic argument — it was historical sociology. The disaffected intellectual who spoils capitalism's future does not obey the same economic logic as the accumulating capitalist; he occupies a different theoretical universe. The prediction may still prove right, but not for the reasons Schumpeter gave.

The final section of Capitalism, Socialism and Democracy introduces a concept Schumpeter called vision — the pre-analytic cognitive act that precedes all economic analysis. Before you build a model, you decide what to look at. Marshall's model missed Keynes's central insight not because Marshall was less intelligent, but because Marshall's vision emphasized pricing; if you're looking at prices, you don't see total output. Schumpeter's own vision — he was honest enough to identify it — was the aristocracy of talent: elites chosen not by blood but by intellect and will. "We can assume that every healthy man can sing if he will. Perhaps half have the capacity... and finally, we come to the Carusos." The upper strata are "like hotels which are indeed always full of people, but people who are forever changing." This vision justified both capitalism and Schumpeter's own class. It was, he conceded, a vision. Whether it was also a truth was a different question.

Chapter XI: The End of the Worldly Philosophy?

The final chapter takes its title from an ambiguity. "End" means both termination and purpose. Has economics come to its terminus, losing its identity as a worldly philosophy? And if not, what is the purpose it should be serving? Heilbroner's answer requires going back to what made economics possible in the first place.

Three things distinguished capitalism from every prior form of social organization and called economics into being. First, capitalism legitimized acquisitiveness for everyone, not just kings and adventurers. Second, it installed the market as distributor — guidance of production and distribution left entirely to the encouragements and discouragements of buying and selling, with no parallel in any other social system. Third, it created a dual authority: public authority (government — force and law) and private authority (profit-seekers — production and distribution), in permanent creative tension. Without these three properties, the questions the worldly philosophers asked could not even be formulated. "The worldly philosophy is the child of capitalism and could not exist without it."

The danger Heilbroner identifies is the disappearance of the word "capitalism" from modern economics. N. Gregory Mankiw's leading introductory text describes economics as approaching its subject "with a scientist's objectivity, in much the same way as a physicist approaches the study of matter." Joseph Stiglitz's 997-page economics textbook does not contain the word "capitalism" — not once. Counts of the word's frequency in the American Economic Review show a precipitous decline in the decades after 1950, accompanied by a sharp increase in references to scientific method.

Two problems afflict this scientific self-image. The first is that human economic behavior, unlike the behavior of electrons, involves volition — the capacity to change one's mind up to the last moment. Economic behavior has lawlike regularities (price up, quantity bought down) but they are probabilistic, not deterministic; an advertising campaign can override price signals; a shift in sentiment can overturn a decades-long trend. Marshall himself admitted: "economics cannot be compared with the exact physical sciences for it deals with the ever-changing and subtle forces of human nature."

The second problem is that economic arrangements are not natural. The distribution of wealth, inheritance laws, property rights, and the structure of labor markets are "highly mutable determinations of the sociopolitical order" — not immutable laws of nature. Claiming scientific objectivity about social arrangements conflates natural facts (value-neutral) with social facts (always value-laden). The claim that economics is a science like physics is not modesty; it is a very specific political act — one that insulates existing arrangements from criticism by treating them as natural law. A pseudoscientific economics would indeed "spell its termination as a worldly philosophy."

Heilbroner's closing argument is neither pessimistic nor naively hopeful. Capitalism will persist — the twentieth century's experiments with its replacement make a benign successor unlikely. But capitalism faces severe stresses that economics, narrowly conceived, cannot address: global warming, nuclear proliferation, ethnic and religious hatreds, a globalized economy that arises within individual capitalisms but increasingly escapes their control. The American version — economically successful but, measured by upward mobility and poverty rates, socially disastrous — compares poorly with the European variants that have built both prosperity and social cohesion. US executive compensation in top corporations runs twice that of France or Germany; American upward mobility is half that of France or Germany and a third that of Sweden. "A combination ill-suited for world leadership" — written in 1999.

The worldly philosophy does not end. But it must be deepened and enlarged — beyond its current desiccated residue, into the territory of psychology, sociology, and political science. It must recover the courage of Smith and Marx and Veblen to name what it sees — to use the word "capitalism" and mean it. The great economists were called worldly philosophers because they embraced the most worldly of human activities and tried to understand it philosophically. That task is not finished.

Trading & Investing Lessons

Visions Drive Every Market Framework

Schumpeter's concept of the "vision" — the pre-analytic cognitive act that precedes all formal analysis — is one of the most useful ideas in the book for traders. Before you build a model, you decide what to look at. Marshall missed the savings-investment problem because his vision emphasized pricing; Ricardo missed the general glut because he was asking who gets what, not how much there is. Every trading framework contains an implicit vision of what matters: price action, fundamentals, macro flows, sentiment. The danger is mistaking your framework's focus for reality itself. Periodically asking "what am I not looking at?" is more valuable than refining the model you already have.

Creative Destruction as the Investor's Permanent Context

Schumpeter's "perennial gale of creative destruction" is the single most important structural fact for long-term investors. No competitive advantage is permanent; every moat eventually gets bridged by a new technology or business model. The companies Veblen described as "watchful waiting toads" — parasitic financiers exploiting static positions — were already being swept away in his lifetime. In markets, the rotation from declining industries to rising ones is not an interruption of the normal order; it is the normal order. Investing with the gale rather than against it — tilting toward industries at the beginning of their disruption cycle rather than the end — is the practical application of Schumpeter's framework.

Keynes's Elevator and Macro Regime Recognition

Keynes's central metaphor — the economy is an elevator, not a seesaw — maps directly onto the distinction between trending and range-bound macro environments. Classical economists insisted the economy would always revert to full employment; Keynes showed it could equilibrate at any level. For traders: the assumption that prices will revert to some equilibrium is not always wrong, but it is regime-dependent. In Keynesian demand shortfalls (Japan in the 1990s, the US after 2008), the elevator can sit on a low floor for years. Recognizing which regime you are in — self-correcting or self-reinforcing — is worth more than any short-term edge.

Ricardo's Rent and the Structural Winner in Any Market

Ricardo's theory of differential rent identifies a persistent pattern in any competitive market: the most productive asset captures all the surplus. The owner of the best land earns rent precisely because worse land exists. In financial markets, the "best land" is the structural position — the lowest-cost producer in a commodity, the dominant platform in a network-effect business, the most liquid exchange. These positions earn rent that competitors cannot replicate by working harder. When analyzing a potential investment, the Ricardian question is: what is the differential advantage here, and is it structural or temporary? Temporary advantages compress; structural ones compound.

Hobson's Savings Glut and Modern Capital Flows

Hobson's theory — excess savings that cannot find domestic investment outlet must flow abroad — has direct relevance to the persistent global imbalances of the twenty-first century. China's current account surplus, Germany's chronic trade surplus, the flood of dollars into US Treasuries from oil exporters — these are all Hobsonian phenomena. The "search for yield" that distorts asset prices during periods of low interest rates is excess saving looking for places to go. Recognizing a Hobsonian savings-glut environment — characterized by compressed risk premia, extended valuations, and flows into exotic or illiquid assets — is useful for both macro positioning and risk management.

Veblen's Conspicuous Consumption and Asset Bubbles

Veblen's observation that status competition drives behavior independently of rational economic calculation is the best single explanation for financial manias. Dot-com stocks in 1999, Miami condos in 2006, meme stocks in 2021: in each case, participation was driven not by expected return but by the social cost of being left out. Keeping up with the Joneses applies to portfolio returns as much as to kitchen renovations. When Veblen's conspicuous consumption logic infects an asset class, prices decouple from fundamentals — not irrationally, in a social sense, but unsustainably, in a financial sense. The signal is when returns become a form of status display.

Key Takeaways

The Market Is Not Natural — It Was Invented

The market system required five centuries of violence, dispossession, and legal transformation to create. Treating it as the natural form of human economic organization — rather than one historically specific solution among several — is the source of most economic ideology.

Every Great Economist Was Responding to a Social Crisis

Smith: the emergence of industrial capitalism needed intellectual justification. Malthus: population pressure seemed to foreclose improvement. Ricardo: landlords were capturing all the gains while capitalists and workers were squeezed. Marx: industrial capitalism was producing mass misery while generating extraordinary wealth. Keynes: 14 million unemployed and the classical remedy wasn't working. Ideas do not arise in a vacuum; they arise in response to intolerable situations.

The Labor Theory of Value's Deeper Insight

Marx's labor theory of value is usually dismissed as wrong — prices don't equal labor content. The deeper insight survives the technical critique: capitalism is the first social system in which labor power itself becomes a commodity, bought and sold in a market, divorced from the person who provides it. This is historically extraordinary and genuinely strange.

Say's Law Was the Economists' Great Blindspot

The belief that supply creates its own demand — that aggregate production always generates enough purchasing power to buy everything produced — was the central error that prevented economists from understanding the Great Depression. The savings-investment divorce, which became structurally significant in the twentieth century, made Say's Law increasingly inapplicable.

Capitalism Contains Genuine Tensions, Not Just Inefficiencies

The worldly philosophers — even those who defended capitalism — agreed that it contained real internal contradictions. Ricardo's tragic squeeze between wages and profits. Marx's falling rate of profit. Keynes's investment uncertainty. Schumpeter's sociological self-dissolution. These are not bugs that better management can fix; they are structural features of the system.

Visions Precede Analysis

Schumpeter's concept is the book's most important methodological contribution. Every economist, and every market participant, begins with a pre-analytic vision of what matters. That vision determines what their model can and cannot see. Identifying one's own vision — rather than treating it as transparent reality — is the beginning of honest analysis.

The Decline of the Worldly Philosopher Is Dangerous

When economics abandoned social critique for scientific formalism, it lost its ability to ask the questions that matter most: what kind of capitalism, for what purposes, at whose cost? The retreat into mathematical precision is not neutral; it implicitly endorses existing arrangements by refusing to name them.

Contrarian Ideas

The Profit Motive Is Modern, Not Universal

Modern economics treats the desire to maximize monetary gain as a fundamental human trait. Heilbroner documents that it is, in historical terms, almost entirely novel — "as ubiquitous as it now is, it is as modern as the printing press." Ancient, classical, and medieval societies organized production without it. This does not make capitalism bad; it makes it contingent — and therefore changeable.

Smith Was Not the Free-Market Hero He Is Made Out to Be

The Adam Smith invoked by free-market advocates is a selective reading. The actual Smith was deeply suspicious of businessmen ("people of the same trade... conspire against the public"), argued for government education to prevent the stupefaction of workers, and was primarily an opponent of monopoly rather than a champion of business. The ideology extracted from The Wealth of Nations omitted everything inconvenient.

Marx's Most Powerful Insights Were Not Economic

The labor theory of value has real technical problems. But Marx's most enduring contribution was not economic in the narrow sense: it was the idea of ideology — the observation that the economic base of society shapes the "superstructure" of law, religion, and philosophy that appears neutral but in fact serves the interests of the ruling class. This insight survived the fall of Soviet communism and is more applicable than ever in an era of concentrated media ownership and corporate political influence.

Keynes Was Conservative, Not Radical

Keynes is routinely cited by the left as an advocate for government expansion. He was, in his own understanding, a conservative trying to save capitalism from its own instabilities. He opposed communism explicitly; he saw government spending as a temporary bridge; he wanted the state to maintain the conditions for private enterprise, not to replace it. His radicalism was tactical: the system required occasional active management, and refusing to provide that management in the name of laissez-faire was itself a form of political choice.

Capitalism's Greatest Threat Is Internal, Not External

Schumpeter's argument — that capitalism erodes its own cultural and institutional foundations through the very rationalism it requires — is more unsettling than any external critique. The enemies of capitalism are not usually foreign ideologies; they are the disaffected beneficiaries of the system itself. The tenured professor, the think-tank intellectual, the investigative journalist — all products of capitalism's surplus, all structurally critical of the values that support them.

Proposals & Frameworks

Smith's Four Accomplishments of the Market

Smith identified four things the competitive market system achieves automatically: (1) prices kept near actual production costs; (2) producers induced to supply what society wants; (3) high prices self-cure by expanding production; (4) similar skill levels earn similar incomes across the economy. The entire edifice of classical economics is built on these four claims.

Mill's Production/Distribution Distinction

Mill's great discovery: the laws of production concern nature (scarcity, diminishing returns) and are as absolute as physics. The laws of distribution concern society and can be arranged however society chooses. This distinction opened the door to reformist economics and all the welfare state variants of capitalism that followed.

Hobson's Oversaving Theory of Imperialism

Structural argument: (1) unequal income distribution forces saving on those with more income than they can consume; (2) domestic investment markets cannot absorb the surplus because workers' wages are too low to buy the output; (3) capital must therefore flow abroad; (4) all capitalist nations face the same problem simultaneously; (5) competition for colonial outlets leads inevitably to war. Lenin made this the core of Marxist imperialism theory.

Schumpeter's Three Business Cycle Types

Schumpeter identified three overlapping cycle durations: short cycles of a few years driven by inventory adjustments; medium cycles of seven to eleven years driven by investment; and fifty-year Kondratieff waves tied to epochal general-purpose technologies (steam, electricity, automobiles). Major depressions occur when all three cycles hit bottom simultaneously — a rare alignment that produces severity out of proportion to any single cause.

Heilbroner's Three Preconditions for Economics

Economics — as a discipline — requires capitalism's three innovations: the legitimation of acquisitiveness, the market as distributor, and the dual authority of public and private sectors. Remove any one of these and the questions that define economics can no longer be formulated. This is why "economics" as practiced today is specifically capitalist economics, not a universal science of resource allocation.

What Didn't Fit

The Anaconda Copper Swindle

The robber baron era produced financial engineering so brazen it reads like farce. Henry Rogers and William Rockefeller acquired the Anaconda Copper Company without spending a dollar of their own in a five-step maneuver: they gave Marcus Daly a $39 million check conditional on its being deposited untouched at National City Bank; created a shell company called Amalgamated Copper, which bought Anaconda with $75 million in freshly printed Amalgamated stock; borrowed $39 million from National City Bank using the Amalgamated stock as collateral to cover the original check; sold the Amalgamated stock on the open market for $75 million; and retired the $39 million loan, pocketing $36 million in profit. Morgan's comment on public accountability — "I owe the public nothing" — captures the era's self-understanding. This was the world Veblen was dissecting: finance as pure predation on productive industry, the businessman as extractive parasite rather than productive agent.

The Saint-Simonian Church and Its Waistcoats

After Saint-Simon's death, his followers created an actual religious sect — the Saint-Simonian Church — with branches in Germany and England. They developed a distinctive theology around labor and productive contribution, held services, and published a journal. Most remarkably, they designed special waistcoats that could not be put on or taken off unassisted — specifically requiring help from another member — as a daily physical reminder of dependence on one's brothers. The waistcoat ceremony was practiced seriously. The Saint-Simonians attracted some significant followers — including early figures in the French positivist and technocratic movements — before the sect dissolved into splinter groups in the 1830s. As an expression of utopian earnestness, the buttoned-in-the-back waistcoat has a strange poetry to it: the ideology of interdependence made literal in clothing.

Keynes's Art Collection

During a wartime mission to Paris in 1917, while German artillery was shelling the city, Keynes was authorized to purchase Impressionist art for the British national collection. He bought Corot, Gauguin, Manet, and Cézanne for Britain — and then, with his own money, acquired a Cézanne for himself, which he hung at his country house in Sussex. The gesture encapsulates the man: in the middle of industrial slaughter, he was making room for beauty; in the service of national duty, he was also serving himself; doing both without apparent contradiction or embarrassment. The Cézanne sold after his death for many times what he paid. Keynes the currency trader and Keynes the aesthete were the same person.

Malthus at Haileybury and the Origins of Development Economics

Malthus spent thirty years as Professor of History and Political Economy at Haileybury College, the training school for administrators of the East India Company. This was the first professorship of economics in England — arguably in the world — and it was created specifically to train colonial administrators. The implications are considerable: the first systematic instruction in economics was embedded in the machinery of imperial governance, designed to produce men who could manage colonial economies. Malthus's students went on to administer India, and many of his ideas — particularly on population and subsistence — shaped colonial agricultural policy in ways that had catastrophic consequences for South Asian famines in the nineteenth century. The connection between early political economy and colonial administration is largely forgotten today.

The Fugger Refusal and the Cultural Roots of Capitalism

The Fugger family story — great sixteenth-century bankers who had the wealth and connections to build an empire but repeatedly refused — is one of Heilbroner's most telling details about the pre-capitalist mentality. When the old patriarch's nephews were offered something equivalent to a financial empire, brother George reportedly said he "would rather live in peace." The modern instinct is to find this inexplicable. But in a world where social status derived from land, lineage, and honor rather than accumulation, more money beyond a comfortable sufficiency offered no increase in social standing — and the stress of managing more would diminish the gentleman's real goods: leisure, dignity, and peace. This was not irrationality; it was a different rationality, embedded in a different value system. The profit motive did not simply exist and require only permission to flourish; it had to be culturally constructed.

About the Author

Robert L. Heilbroner (1919–2005) graduated summa cum laude from Harvard in 1940, studied economics in government and business, and completed his graduate work at the New School for Social Research in New York — the same institution where Adolph Lowe's seminar on Adam Smith inspired The Worldly Philosophers. He spent most of his academic career at the New School as the Norman Thomas Professor of Economics, a chair named for the six-time American Socialist presidential candidate. The combination of elite education, unconventional institution, and politically freighted professorship captures something essential about Heilbroner's position: insider enough to understand the mainstream, independent enough to criticize it.

The Worldly Philosophers was first published in 1953, when Heilbroner was thirty-four, and became an immediate success. It was translated into three dozen languages, went through seven editions, and became one of the best-selling economics books ever written — outsold among economics texts only by Paul Samuelson's famous introductory textbook. Heilbroner continued writing prolifically throughout his life, producing works including The Future as History (1960), An Inquiry into the Human Prospect (1974), and The Nature and Logic of Capitalism (1985). His approach throughout was consistent: economics as a social and historical discipline, not a natural science; capitalism as a specific historical form with specific contradictions, not the default condition of humanity.

By the time of the seventh edition in 1999, Heilbroner could look back on fifty years of a discipline that had, in his view, largely abandoned the worldly-philosophical ambition that had made it great. His concern in the final chapter — that economics was treating a social system as a natural science and thereby removing it from political and moral debate — proved prescient. The mathematization of economics has accelerated since his death; the word "capitalism" remains largely absent from leading textbooks. The Worldly Philosophers endures precisely because it refuses that retreat: it insists that economics is about real people making real choices in a real social system with a real history.

Why This Book Matters

The Worldly Philosophers has been in continuous print for more than seventy years, through seven editions, in dozens of languages. It introduced more people to serious economic thinking than any other book of its era — not by dumbing down the ideas but by setting them in the lives and times of the people who produced them. The format was not accidental. Heilbroner believed that economic ideas could not be understood apart from the social crises that generated them, and that understanding why ideas arose was as important as understanding what they said. This is a rarer and more difficult kind of economic education than most textbooks attempt.

The book's intellectual contribution is the cumulative portrait it builds of capitalism as a specific, historically contingent system — not the natural form of human economic life, but one among several that have existed and might exist. By showing each great economist responding to the crises of his time with the conceptual tools available to him, Heilbroner implicitly argues that new crises will require new tools — and that the courage to develop them is the defining characteristic of a worldly philosopher. The question the book leaves hanging is whether that tradition of critical engagement can survive the professionalization and mathematization of economics that Heilbroner mourns in the final chapter.

For anyone who wants to understand why markets work the way they do, why economic arguments take the forms they take, and why intelligent people have disagreed so fundamentally about capitalism for three centuries, The Worldly Philosophers remains the best single starting point. It is, as Heilbroner hoped, a book about the most worldly of human activities — the pursuit of wealth — treated with the seriousness and philosophical depth it deserves.
