## Preface to the Seventh Edition

- Written early 1950s at the **New School for Social Research**; inspired by a
  seminar on **Adam Smith** taught by **Adolph Lowe** (lived to 102)
- Title from **Frederick Lewis Allen** (*Harper's*): Heilbroner wanted *The Money
  Philosophers*; Allen said "you mean 'worldly'"
- 7th edition adds organizing thread: **"visions"** — pre-analytic worldviews
  underlying all social analysis; concept credited to **Josef Schumpeter**
- Final chapter "The End of the Worldly Philosophy?" — question mark implies
  change in economics' nature, not a eulogy

---

## Chapter I: Introduction

- The great economists were, by schoolbook standards, **nobodies**: no armies,
  no empires — yet "more decisive for history than many acts of statesmen who
  basked in brighter glory"; they "left in their train shattered empires and
  exploded continents; they buttressed and undermined political regimes"

> "The ideas of economists and political philosophers, both when they are right
> and when they are wrong, are more powerful than is commonly understood. Indeed
> the world is ruled by little else. Practical men, who believe themselves to be
> quite exempt from any intellectual influences, are usually the slaves of some
> defunct economist." — **Lord Keynes**

- Economics = "the science that has sent men to the barricades"; mistakes were
  calamitous, not academic — played out in society itself
- The economists' **common denominator**: fascination with how man creates
  material wealth, then treads on his neighbor to get a share of it
- Called **worldly philosophers** because they embraced "the most worldly of all
  man's activities — his drive for wealth" within a philosophical system
- Their act of faith: that seemingly unrelated threads (pauper, speculator,
  greengrocer, mob) could be woven into a **single tapestry** — the milling world
  resolves into an orderly progression
- **Adam Smith** lived at the time of the American Revolution — yet 6,000 years of
  recorded history had produced no economist; the pre-Smith world made an economist
  "not only unnecessary, but impossible"

## Chapter II: The Economic Revolution

### Three Solutions to the Survival Problem

Man has found only three ways to ensure social continuity:

1. **Tradition** — tasks handed generation to generation by custom; Ancient Egypt
   (per Adam Smith): "every man was bound by a principle of religion to follow the
   occupation of his father"; India assigned by caste
2. **Command (Authority)** — whip of authoritarian rule; the pyramids were NOT
   built by enterprising contractors; Soviet Five Year Plans did NOT follow custom;
   both are **Command societies** — survival enforced by edicts
3. **The Market System** — the revolutionary third solution: society assures its
   own continuance by allowing each individual to follow one rule: **do what is
   to his best monetary advantage**

- Tradition and command needed no economists; economists were called forth only
  by the **market system**, whose workings were not obvious

### The Market System as Revolutionary Puzzle

- Lure of **gain** — not tradition, not authority — steers society's labor;
  each person free to follow acquisitive nose, yet all necessary tasks get done
- The market system was "the most important revolution... fundamentally more
  disturbing by far than the French, the American, or even the Russian Revolution"
- Men were "not at all sure that the market system was to be viewed without
  suspicion, distaste, and distrust"

### Vignettes of the Pre-Market World

**Scale of pre-market commerce:** All goods crossing France via the Saint Gothard
pass in a year would not fill a modern freight train; between Basle and Cologne
**Andreas Ryff** (1550) paid **31 separate customs levies** every ~10 miles.

### The Profit Motive Is Modern

- **Sir William Petty** (17th c.) observed that when wages rose, labor became
  scarce — workers took more time off rather than work more; the drive to better
  one's material lot was absent in Egyptian, Greek, Roman, and medieval cultures;
  "as ubiquitous as it now is, it is as modern as the printing press"
- The **social sanction** of gain is even newer: Medieval Church taught no
  Christian *ought* to be a merchant; in Shakespeare's time, the goal of ordinary
  life was to *maintain* one's station, not advance it; to Pilgrim forefathers,
  gain as a life goal was "a doctrine of the devil"
- The **Fuggers** — great 16th-century German banking family owning mines, trade
  concessions, the right to coin money — yet old Anton Fugger's nephews refused
  the empire: brother George "would rather live in peace"
- The **idea of "making a living"** had not come into being — work was not a means
  to money; work was an end in itself, part of tradition and natural life

### Land, Labor, and Capital: Didn't Yet Exist

The medieval world couldn't conceive the market system because **Land, Labor, and
Capital** did not yet exist as abstract categories:

- **Land**: Estates were the core of social life (prestige, military, judicial
  organization). A medieval nobleman selling his land "would be as unthinkable as
  a Connecticut governor selling counties to Rhode Island"
- **Labor**: No labor market. Serfs tied to estates. Apprentices regulated by guilds
  in every detail. "No more of a labor market than is provided by interns in a
  hospital"
- **Capital**: Funds existed but no impetus for aggressive use. Guild motto:
  "Safety first." Not shortest and most efficient, but longest and most
  labor-consuming production preferred. Advertising forbidden. In 16th-c. England,
  when mass weaving production appeared, guilds protested; the king outlawed the
  "wonderworkshop" (reportedly 200 looms, butchers and bakers on staff) as a
  "bad precedent"

### The Revolution Was Violent, Unwanted, Agonized

- Transition from traditional to market society drawn out over centuries
  (13th–19th): "an agonized convulsion of society"
- To commercialize land required uprooting feudal life; to make "workers" out of
  serfs and apprentices required creating the **proletariat**; to make capitalists
  out of guild masters meant teaching "the laws of the jungle to denizens of
  the barnyard"

**Resistance examples:**
- France 1666: cloth weaver must get permission from four oldest merchants and
  four oldest weavers to try an innovation; button-makers' guild had wardens
  arrest people on the street for wearing cloth buttons
- **Calico imports**, France: the response costs **16,000 lives** — 77 hanged,
  58 broken on the wheel, 631 to the galleys in Valence alone, for dealing in
  forbidden calico

**The Enclosures:**
- **Wool** became profitable → common lands enclosed → **John Hales** (1549):
  "where XL persons had their lyvings, now one man and his shepherd hath all"
- By 1820: Duchess of **Sutherland** dispossessed 15,000 tenants from 794,000
  acres, replaced with 131,000 sheep
- Parliament's "localization" of pauperism (tying paupers to parishes) *prevented*
  the only solution: a fluid, mobile labor force

> "The great chariot of society, which for so long had run down the gentle slope
> of tradition, now found itself powered by an internal combustion machine."

### Forces That Created the Market Revolution

1. **Rise of National Political Units** — peasant wars and kingly conquest broke
   up feudalism; replaced 112 local measures of length, 92 square measures, 163
   dry measures with national laws and currency; royal exploration → national
   adventure (**Sir Francis Drake's** Golden Hynde: Queen Elizabeth's shareholder
   cut paid off all England's foreign debts and balanced the budget)
2. **Decay of Religious Spirit / Rise of Protestantism** — Italian Renaissance
   humanism shifted attention to Today; Church of Rome viewed merchants with
   suspicion, calling usury a sin; **Protestant leaders** preached it was pious
   to make the most of God-given talents; acquisitiveness became a recognized
   virtue — eventually, riches identified with spiritual excellence
3. **Material and Technical Changes** — slow monetization shifted power toward
   merchants; **double-entry bookkeeping** (Venetians, 12th c.) not standard
   until the 17th century — without rational accounting, large-scale operations
   couldn't run; in 500 years feudal age built 1,000 towns
4. **Rise of Scientific Curiosity** — printing press, paper mill, windmill,
   mechanical clock, the map — experimentation looked on "with a friendly eye"

### The Birth of "Economic Man" and Early Mercantilism

- By 1700: new dicta — "Every man is naturally covetous of lucre"; "No laws are
  prevalent against gaine"; "Gaine is the Centre of the Circle of Commerce"
- New idea: **"economic man"** — a pale wraith who followed his adding-machine
  brain wherever it led
- **John Law** (Scottish adventurer), France 1718: organized the **Mississippi
  Company**, sold shares to mine "mountains of gold in America"; one hotel waiter
  netted 30 million *livres*; when company collapsed, government marched 1,000
  beggars through Paris armed with picks and shovels as "miners bound for Eldorado"
- Early social philosophy = **Mercantilism**: accumulation of gold as the goal
  of economic endeavor; great armadas in pursuit of treasure
- **Thomas Mun**, *England's Treasure by Forraign Trade*: "wee must ever observe
  this rule; to sell more to strangers yearly than wee consume of theirs in Value"
- New social problem: **how to keep the poor poor** — unless poor, they wouldn't
  do honest toil without demanding exorbitant wages

> "To make the Society Happy... it is requisite that great numbers should be
> Ignorant as well as Poor." — **Bernard Mandeville**, early 18th century

- **Dr. Samuel Johnson**: "There is nothing which requires more to be illustrated
  by philosophy than trade does." The time for the economists had arrived.

- **Adam Smith** published *The Wealth of Nations* **1776** — same year American
  political democracy was born; Smith never saw himself as a revolutionary but
  gave the world the image of itself for which it had been searching

## Chapter 3: The Wonderful World of Adam Smith

### Biography

- Born 1723, **Kirkcaldy**, Scotland (pop. ~1,500; nails still used as local
  currency); kidnapped by gypsies at age four, rescued by uncle
- Legendary absent-mindedness: once walked 15 miles in a dressing gown before
  snapping out of a reverie
- Oxford at 17 on scholarship — 6 years; professors had abandoned even the
  pretense of instruction (1788 debate: all four participants read novels in
  silence); nearly expelled for possessing **David Hume**'s *Treatise of Human
  Nature*
- 1751: **Chair of Moral Philosophy** at University of Glasgow (not yet 28);
  Glasgow center of the **Scottish Enlightenment**; beloved by students;
  became dean 1758

### The Theory of Moral Sentiments (1759)

- Instant sensation; core question: how can self-interested man form moral
  judgments that transcend self-interest? Answer: we imagine ourselves as an
  **impartial spectator**, a third-person observer
- Sparked *das Adam Smith Problem* in Germany; caught the attention of
  **Charles Townshend**

### Grand Tour (1764–1766)

- **Charles Townshend** (Chancellor of the Exchequer, helped precipitate the
  **American Revolution** by imposing duty on American tea) hired Smith to tutor
  the young Duke of Buccleuch — offered **£500/year + expenses + £500 pension
  for life** (Smith's professorial income was at best £170/year)
- In France: met and "worshiped" **Voltaire**; in Paris met **François Quesnay**,
  physician to Louis XV, who developed **Physiocracy** and the *tableau économique*
  — **Mirabeau the Elder** called it equal in rank to writing and money
- Physiocracy's fatal flaw: only agricultural labor *produces* wealth; manufacturing
  merely "alters its form"; Smith's break: **labor — not nature — is the source
  of value**
- Smith would have dedicated *Wealth of Nations* to Quesnay had Quesnay not died first
- Tour ended 1766: the duke's younger brother died of fever; Smith returned to
  Kirkcaldy to write

### The Wealth of Nations (1776)

- ~10 years writing in Kirkcaldy; dictated standing at fireplace, rubbing his
  head against the wall until a dark streak formed on the paneling
- Discussed ideas with **Dr. Samuel Johnson** (first meeting: both called each
  other liars) and **Benjamin Franklin** (who described America's future
  importance; Smith wrote it seemed "very likely to become one of the greatest
  and most formidable [nations] that ever was in the world")
- "The outpouring not only of a great mind, but of a whole epoch" — 100+ authors
  cited; index alone runs 63 pages; digresses 75 pages on silver
- Memorable observations: England "a nation of shopkeepers"; "By nature a
  philosopher is not in genius and disposition half so different from a street
  porter, as a mastiff is from a greyhound"
- **Revolutionary** in democratic sense: wealth = the goods *all* people consume,
  not gold hoards or kingly treasuries; gone are the prerogatives of merchants,
  farmers, guilds; for the first time, **the consumer is king**

### The Laws of the Market

**Self-Interest as Driving Force:**
> "It is not from the benevolence of the butcher, the brewer, or the baker that
> we expect our dinner, but from their regard to their self-interest." — Adam Smith

**Competition as Regulator:**
- Self-interest alone = community of ruthless profiteers; the check is
  **competition** — rivals exploit any excess; overcharging loses buyers,
  underpaying loses workers; selfish motives transmuted by market → **social harmony**

**Prices / Quantities / Incomes:**
- 100 glove manufacturers: each wants to raise price, but cannot — competitors
  undersell; even collusion vulnerable (a shoemaker can shift capital and undercut)
- If consumers want more gloves / fewer shoes: glove prices rise → profits rise →
  capital and labor flow in → output increases; self-interest + competition
  reallocate production without central dictate
- Market also regulates **incomes**: excess profits in a field attract rivals until
  returns normalize; unusually high wages attract workers until premium disappears

**Smith's Four Accomplishments via Market Logic:**
1. Prices kept near actual production costs
2. Producers induced to supply what society wants
3. High prices self-cure by expanding production
4. Similar skill levels earn similar incomes across the economy

**The "Invisible Hand":**
- Private interests and passions led toward "the interest of the whole society"
- Paradox: the market, acme of individual freedom, is also "the strictest
  taskmaster of all" — no appeal from its anonymous pressure
- Smith's world = **atomistic competition**: no single agent powerful enough to
  resist market pressures; already straining in his day — manufacturers' cartels,
  journeymen's associations, and the Lombe brothers' factory (harbinger of giant
  industry, child labor) showed deviations from the blueprint

### The Laws of Motion: Growth, Accumulation, Population

**Division of Labor — The Pin Factory:**
> "One man draws out the wire, another straights it, a third cuts it... I have
> seen a small manufactory where ten men only were employed... they could make
> among them about twelve pounds of pins in a day [~48,000 pins]. But if they
> had all wrought separately, they certainly could not each make twenty, perhaps
> not one pin a day." — *The Wealth of Nations*

- Division of labor → "universal opulence which extends itself to the lowest
  ranks of the people"; even a day-laborer's coarse coat required the cooperation
  of "many thousands"

**Law of Accumulation:**
- The capitalist's primary drive is to *accumulate* savings and reinvest; examples:
  **Richard Arkwright** (died 1792 leaving £500,000); **Josiah Wedgwood**
  (£240,000 + landed property); **Samuel Walker** (forge → steel foundry worth
  £200,000)
- Accumulation funds machinery → enables division of labor → multiplies output

**Law of Population:**
- Labor, like any commodity, is produced to meet demand; high wages → workers
  survive and reproduce more → labor supply rises → wages pressed back down
- Not naive: "not uncommon in the Highlands for a mother who has borne twenty
  children not to have two alive"; half of English children died before age four

**The Endless Chain — Smith's Growth Model:**
1. Market equalizes returns; prices compete down to production costs
2. Accumulation → more machinery → greater division of labor → higher productivity
3. More machinery → more demand for workers → wages rise
4. Rising wages → lower infant mortality → population grows
5. Larger labor supply → wages pressed back toward subsistence
6. Profits restored → further accumulation → return to step 2

**Long-Run Limits:**
- In the very long run, population growth eventually pushes wages back to
  "natural" (subsistence) level; growth ends when the economy exhausts unused
  resources; Smith estimated perhaps **200 years** as the longest flourishing
  period
- At steady state: laborer at subsistence wages, capitalist at modest stable
  profits, only the landlord benefits

### Smith on Government

- Three legitimate government duties:
  1. National defense
  2. Justice administration
  3. Public works that "could never repay the expense to any individual" —
     roads and education cited
- His true enemy is **monopoly in any form**:

> "People of the same trade seldom meet together, but the conversation ends in
> a conspiracy against the public, or in some diversion to raise prices."
> — Adam Smith

- Smith also warned that mass production stupefies workers — called for
  government to prevent this; saw **corporations** (joint-stock companies) as
  unlikely to work (spectacularly wrong)
- *The Wealth of Nations* was quoted by industrialists to oppose the first
  humanitarian legislation — the very abuses Smith himself abhorred

### Smith's Legacy

- Book took hold slowly — not quoted in Parliament for 8 years; **Charles James
  Fox** the first to do so (admitted he'd never read it); full recognition ~1800
- The capitalists took one message: *let the market alone* — ignoring Smith's
  suspicion of their motives and concern for laborers
- At death (1790): **William Pitt** the PM rose when Smith entered and refused to
  sit: "We will stand until you are first seated, for we are all your scholars"
- Smith was the economist of **preindustrial capitalism** — never lived to see
  enormous enterprises, business cycles, or disruptions of the Industrial Revolution

> "Consumption is the sole end and purpose of all production." — Adam Smith

## Chapter 4: The Gloomy Presentiments of Parson Malthus and David Ricardo

### The Population Anxiety of 18th-Century England

- England worried about population size relative to Continental rivals; no
  reliable data — first census not until 1801 (feared as "totally subversive
  of the last remnants of English liberty")
- Amateur statisticians: **Dr. Price** (Nonconformist minister): proved via
  hearth-money taxes that England's population had *declined* 30%+ since the
  Restoration — almost certainly wrong but widely believed
- **William Pitt** introduced a poor-relief bill to boost birth rates — paying
  child allowances because children "enriched" the country even if they turned
  out to be paupers

### William Godwin's Utopia

- **William Godwin**, *Political Justice* (1793): promised future with "no war,
  no crime, no administration of justice... no disease, anguish, melancholy,
  or resentment"; called for complete equality and anarchic communism
- **Daniel Malthus** (father of Malthus), friend of Hume and admirer of Rousseau,
  discussed Godwin's ideas sympathetically; young **Reverend Thomas Robert
  Malthus** debated his father and grew convinced of an "insurmountable barrier"
  between existing society and Godwin's paradise

### The Malthusian Trap

- 1798: anonymous 50,000-word treatise: *An Essay on the Principle of Population*
- Core thesis: **population tends by nature to outstrip all possible means of
  subsistence**; humanity trapped at the brink of existence, not ascending toward
  utopia
- Malthus assumed humans double every **25 years** — modest; America had actually
  done so for 150 years, backwoods areas every 15
- Land cannot be doubled; it grows only *arithmetically*:
  - Population: 1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32, 64, 128, 256...
  - Subsistence: 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9...
  - In 225 years: ratio = 512:10; in 2,000 years: incalculable

> "Famine seems to be the last, the most dreadful resource of nature... gigantic
> inevitable famine stalks in the rear, and with one mighty blow, levels the
> population with the food of the world." — Malthus

- Gap sealed by: war, disease, poverty, infanticide — the "positive checks"
- Only escape: **"moral restraint"** — delayed marriage, celibacy; Malthus himself
  married late
- Helping the poor only aggravates total misery ("at Nature's mighty feast there
  is no vacant cover")

### Historical Context: The Corn Laws

- In 40 years since *Wealth of Nations*, England split into two hostile factions:
  1. **Rising industrialists** — wanted cheap grain (low grain = low wages = lower
     production costs)
  2. **Great landowners** — owned Parliament, wanted high grain prices
- East Lothian farms averaged 56% returns; typical 300-acre farm profits: £88
  (1790) → £121 (1803) → £160 (1813); rents doubled across England in 25 years
- **The Corn Laws**: sliding duties on imported grain — effectively a permanent
  floor for domestic wheat prices
- By 1813: wheat at 117 shillings/quarter — nearly **twice a workman's entire
  weekly wage**
- **Alexander Baring** (banker) told Parliament the laborer had no interest in
  grain prices — "he will get dry bread in the one case and dry bread in the other"

### Biographical Contrast: Malthus vs. Ricardo

| | **Malthus** | **Ricardo** |
|---|---|---|
| Origin | English upper-middle class | Son of Jewish merchant-banker from Holland |
| Education | University, philosophically tutored | Left school at 14, worked for father |
| Career | First professional economist; Haileybury College | Set up business at 22; retired at 42 |
| Wealth | Never well-to-do | Retired with £500,000–£1,600,000 |
| Temperament | Empiricist, fact-minded | Abstract theorist, "invisible laws" |
| Reception | "Best abused man of his age" | "Walked like a god"; educated Commons |

- Paradox: Malthus (modest income) defended wealthy landowners; Ricardo
  (wealthy, later a landlord) fought against them
- Ricardo in Parliament: spoke "as if he had dropped from another planet";
  ladies hired governesses based on whether they could teach Ricardo's principles
- Malthus physically: tall, handsome, gentle — students called him "Pop";
  had inherited cleft palate, making speech difficult

### The Malthus-Ricardo Friendship

- Met 1809; lifelong friendship despite disagreeing on almost everything;
  "hunted together in search of the Truth, and huzzaed when they found her,
  without caring who found her first" — **Maria Edgeworth**
- At Waterloo: Ricardo held a maximum bull position in government securities;
  Wellington won; Ricardo made an "immense killing"; Malthus was "discomfited"
- Ricardo's trading philosophy: people exaggerate importance of events; buy
  on small rises; sell into panic
- Last letter from Ricardo to Malthus (1823): "These discussions never influence
  our friendship; I should not like you more than I do if you agreed in opinion
  with me." Died suddenly at 51.
- Malthus on Ricardo: **"I never loved anybody out of my own family so much."**

### Enter David Ricardo

- **David Ricardo**, *Principles of Political Economy* (1817): dry, abstract,
  architectural — "as basic, bare, unadorned as Euclid"; described as a
  *tragic* system; a **puppet show** stripped to economic motivations

> "The interest of the landlords is always opposed to the interest of every
> other class in the community." — Ricardo, 1815

### Ricardo's Three Classes

- **Workers**: condemned long-run to subsistence; every wage rise triggers more
  children, flooding the labor market, erasing the gain
- **Capitalists**: gray, uniform accumulators; competition among themselves erases
  excess profits; profits depend on wages paid → leads to trouble as system expands
- **Landlords**: uniquely privileged; income (**rent**) held in check by neither
  competition nor population pressure; arises from **differential productivity of land**

### Theory of Differential Rent

- Two neighboring farms: Farm A (fertile) raises 1,500 bushels with 100 men;
  Farm B (less fertile) raises 1,000 bushels — both sell at same market price;
  Farm A owner pockets the **difference in production costs** as rent
- Rent = pure windfall from nature's inequality; not earned by labor or capital
- If it's barely profitable to farm bad land at $2.00/bushel, the good-land owner
  (producing at $0.50/bushel) collects ~$1.50/bushel as rent

### The Tragic Squeeze: Ricardo's System in Motion

1. Capitalists accumulate → hire more workers → wages temporarily rise
2. Higher wages → workers have more children → labor supply expands → wages
   fall back to subsistence
3. More mouths → must farm **marginal land** (less fertile) → grain costs rise
4. Rising grain prices → wages must rise (workers need bread)
5. Rising wages → capitalist profits shrink
6. Rents on **good land** soar as the margin between best and worst land widens
7. Result: **double squeeze on the capitalist** — higher wages AND rising rents;
   the landlord who does nothing gains everything

> "The worker just barely subsisted, the capitalist was cheated of his efforts,
> and the landlord gloated." — Heilbroner's summary

- Ricardo fought the **Corn Laws** because free trade in grain would undercut
  landlord power; industrial capitalists embraced Ricardo's theory — it told them
  low wages weren't their fault and that they were society's true engine

### The General Glut Debate (Malthus vs. Ricardo)

- **Malthus** worried about a "**general glut**" — commodities flooding the market
  with no buyers; Ricardo dismissed this as logically impossible
- Ricardo relied on **Jean-Baptiste Say** ("**Say's Law**"):
  1. Desire for goods is infinite
  2. Every cost of production becomes someone's income
  3. Therefore aggregate demand always equals aggregate supply — a glut *cannot* occur
- Malthus sensed a flaw: what if **saving** caused demand to fall short of supply?
  Ricardo: "Mr. Malthus never appears to remember that to save is to spend"
- Malthus could not articulate his objection precisely — but was stumbling toward
  the **problem of boom and depression** that would consume economists a century
  later (**Keynes**)
- Real reason they disagreed: Malthus was asking *How Much Is There?* (aggregate
  output); Ricardo was asking *Who Gets What?* (distribution) — talking past each other

### Why Malthus and Ricardo Differed from Smith: Technology

- Smith's technology = **division of labor** — a finite improvement; once pins
  are optimized, growth stalls
- Malthus/Ricardo's technology = **spinning jenny, steam engine, iron puddling** —
  endless new avenues; industrial growth potentially infinite → more threatening
  population pressure AND richer pickings for landlords → hence the darker outlook

### Legacy

- **Ricardo's gift**: the tool of **abstraction**; also introduced the
  **"Ricardian Vice"** — using abstraction to sidestep awkward facts and
  non-rational behavior
- **Malthus's gift**: named the population problem; intuited general depression
- Joint contribution: transformed **economics from optimism to pessimism** —
  natural forces no longer seemed benevolent; progress might end with workers at
  subsistence, capitalists cheated, landlords gloating
- Both (and Smith) shared one blind spot: the **working class as passive** —
  no hint workers might ever reorganize society on their own terms

## Chapter 5: The Dreams of the Utopian Socialists

### England in the 1820s: The Social Bill

- *The Lion* (1828): story of **Robert Blincoe**, one of 80 pauper children sent
  to a factory at Lowdham aged ~10 — whipped day and night; employer **Ellice
  Needham** pinched children's ears until his nails met through the flesh; children
  worked nearly naked in winter with teeth filed down
- 16-hour workdays common; factory clocks accelerated during meal breaks; workers
  forbidden to carry their own watches
- 1779: mob of 8,000 workers burned a mill; by 1811 **Luddite** protests swept
  England; **Southey**: "At this moment nothing but the Army preserves us from
  that most dreadful of all calamities, an insurrection of the poor against the rich"

### New Lanark

- Drew 20,000 visitors (1815–1825) including Grand Duke Nicholas (future Tsar
  Nicholas I); workers' homes with *two rooms* each; colored-cube deportment
  system; no children under 10–11; 10¾-hour workday; no corporal punishment;
  children in a schoolhouse — "no child's question was ever to go unanswered"
- And New Lanark was **marvelously profitable** — proof that squalor was not
  economically necessary

### Robert Owen

- Born Wales 1771, poor family; left school at 9; apprenticed to a linen draper
- Age 18, borrowed £100 from his brother → set up textile machinery business;
  walked into Manchester's Mr. **Drinkwater** (large spinning firm), demanded
  £300/year (more than all other applicants combined), and got the job
- Within years: bought the mills at **New Lanark** — co-owned by mill-owner
  **Mr. Dale**, whose daughter Owen had fallen in love with; borrowed capital,
  bought the mills, won the daughter
- Within 1 year: New Lanark transformed; made a fortune of at least **£60,000**
- Core belief: **mankind is no better than its environment** — change the
  environment, achieve paradise on earth

### Villages of Cooperation

- Post-Napoleonic depression (1816–1820): Owen presented not factory reform
  but **Villages of Cooperation** — 800–1,200 people working farm and factory
  in self-sustaining units; parallelogram-arranged houses, common rooms; children
  over 3 boarded separately for character-forming education
- Committee reacted with alarm; 1819 special committee (including Ricardo) formed
  to raise **£96,000** for a trial Village; funding never materialized
- **William Cobbett**: "This gentleman is for establishing *communities* of
  paupers!... of such a thing as a *community of paupers*, I believe no human
  being has ever before heard"

### New Harmony (1824–1828)

- Owen sold his New Lanark interest, bought **30,000 acres on the Wabash River**,
  Indiana (from German Rappite religious sect)
- July 4, 1826: dedicated **New Harmony** with a Declaration of Mental Independence
  — freedom from Private Property, Irrational Religion, and Marriage
- Failed immediately: 800 settlers arrived helter-skelter; Owen bilked by an
  associate who set up a whiskey distillery; rival communities sprang up
- By 1828: admitted a failure; Owen lost **four-fifths** of his fortune
- Working classes had taken Owen's ideas seriously; **28 Rochdale Pioneers** began
  the **consumer cooperative movement** — became a major pillar of the British
  **Labour party**

### Owen's Grand National (1833)

- **Grand National Consolidated Trades Union** — first nationwide English
  working-class union; 500,000 members
- Goals: not just wages/hours but abolition of money, Villages of Cooperation
- Collapsed within two years: local strikes weakened the body, government
  suppressed it under antiunion laws
- Owen continued as "grand old man of labor"; died 1858, age 87, still hopeful
- Legacy: **"Man is the creature of circumstances"** — more powerful than all
  his fanciful schemes; a philosophy of hope

### Saint-Simon

- **Count Henri de Rouvroy de Saint-Simon** — born 1760; awakened each morning
  by his valet: *"Arise, Monsieur le Comte, you have great things to do today."*
- Fought in the American Revolutionary War, five campaigns, won the **Order of
  Cincinnatus**; proposed a canal to precede the Panama
- During French Revolution: speculated in Church lands, made a fortune; turned
  himself in to prison when his hotel proprietor was accused of collaborating
  in his escape — a noble gesture
- **In prison, dream**: Charlemagne appeared, told him his family was reserved
  to produce both a hero and a philosopher of first rank
- After prison: spent his fortune trying to know *everything*; fortune exhausted,
  dependent on an old servant; wrote furiously, begged patrons

> "Be my saviour, I am dying of hunger.... For 15 days I have lived on bread and
> water..." — Saint-Simon, letter to patrons

- 1823: shot himself in despair; only succeeded in losing an eye; last words:
  **"Remember that in order to do great things one must be impassioned!"**

### Saint-Simon's Ideas

- Core gospel: **"man must work"** to share in society's fruits
- Famous thought experiment: if France lost its 50 leading physicists, chemists,
  mathematicians — a catastrophe, robbing France of her soul. But if it lost the
  king's brother, the duchesses, officers of the Crown, ministers of state, judges,
  and the 10,000 richest proprietors — *"the loss would be purely a sentimental one;
  the state would hardly suffer"*
- Proposed **society organized as a gigantic factory** — government should be
  economic, not political; rewards apportioned to social contribution;
  the "active members of the factory" should benefit, not "lazy onlookers"
- Not socialism in the modern sense — a paean to industrial productivity and
  a protest against idle wealth; no program for *how* to achieve it
- After his death: an actual **Saint-Simonian Church** with branches in Germany
  and England; disciples wore special waistcoats that could not be put on or taken
  off unassisted (symbolizing dependence on one's brothers)

### Charles Fourier

- Born 1772, son of a tradesman of Besançon; passions were flowers and cats;
  believed he alone had "confounded twenty centuries of political imbecility"
- Cosmology: earth had 80,000-year lifespan; five of eight stages of advancement
  passed; ahead lay **Guaranteeism** and **Harmony**; in full Harmony: Northern
  Crown at the Pole; sea would become lemonade; six new moons; new species
  (*antilion*, *antiwhale*); humans living 144 years, 120 spent in unrestricted
  sexual love
- Despite the madness, a precise prescription: **phalanxes** (*phalanstères*)
  — communities like Grand Hotels with surrounding fields and industrial
  establishments; residents live at first, second, or third class; everyone works
  a few hours daily but only at what they *like*
- Dirty work assigned to children (**Little Hordes** to slaughterhouses and road
  repair; **Little Bands** to tend flowers)
- Profit split: 5/12 to labor, 4/12 to capital, 3/12 to "ability"
- Spent his last years sitting in his room waiting for the great capitalist who
  would finance his schemes. No one ever came.
- At one time over **40 phalansteries** in America; at least **178 actual Utopian
  groups** with 15–900 members each; notable examples: **Oneida**, **Brook Farm**,
  **New Icaria**, North American Phalanx (NJ, 1843–1855)

### The Utopians in Context

- The Utopians faced a world that rationalized cruelty under "economic law":
  financier **Necker**: "Were it possible to discover a kind of food less agreeable
  than bread but having double its substance, people would be reduced to eating
  only once in two days." Not cruelty — just economics.
- Laws of **Smith, Malthus, Ricardo** seemed to explain not just how wealth *was*
  distributed but how it *should* be
- The Utopians' response: the laws look inviolable AND society is intolerable →
  **the whole system must change**
- Why **Utopian Socialists**: they hoped to *persuade* the upper classes, not
  overthrow them; contrasted with Communists who addressed the masses and urged
  violence; they were the first to react to *economic* as well as political injustice

### John Stuart Mill — The Economist Who Went Socialist

- **John Stuart Mill** — by common consent the greatest economist of the age;
  father **James Mill** — historian, philosopher, pamphleteer, intimate of Ricardo
  and **Jeremy Bentham**

### Mill's Extraordinary Education

- Born 1806; Greek at age 3; by 7 had read most of Plato's dialogues; by 8–12:
  Virgil, Horace, Livy, Sallust, Aristotle, Sophocles; mastered geometry, algebra,
  differential calculus; wrote a Roman History and a History of Holland
- Age 13: complete survey of political economy; no holidays, no boyhood friends
- In his twenties: **nervous breakdown** — dry intellectual world became sterile;
  discovered beauty through Goethe, Wordsworth, Saint-Simon; then met **Harriet
  Taylor** (for 20 years they wrote and traveled together in "perfect innocence";
  after Mr. Taylor's death they married)

> "Whoever, either now or hereafter, may think of me and of the work I have done,
> must never forget that it is the product not of one intellect and conscience,
> but of three." — J.S. Mill, *Autobiography*

### Mill's Key Discovery — Production vs. Distribution

- *Principles of Political Economy* (two volumes): surveyed rent, wages, prices,
  taxes; retreaded Smith, Malthus, Ricardo
- **Monumental discovery**: the true province of economic law is **production**,
  not **distribution**
- Laws of production concern nature — scarcity, diminishing returns — as absolute
  as physics. But **distribution is different**:

> "The things once there, mankind, individually or collectively, can do with them
> as they please.... The distribution of wealth, therefore, depends on the laws
> and customs of society. The rules by which it is determined are what the opinions
> and feelings of the ruling portion of the community make them, and are very
> different in different ages and countries, and might be still more different, if
> mankind so chose." — J.S. Mill

- A **body blow to Ricardo's disciples**: no appeal to "natural law" could justify
  how wealth was shared; society could tax, subsidize, expropriate, redistribute —
  however it chose
- Caveat: intervening in distribution also intervenes in production; **Marx**
  pointed out that different societies arrange payment as integral to production
  modes — production and distribution cannot be cleanly separated
- Yet: the **New Deal** and **Scandinavian welfare capitalisms** are direct
  expressions of Mill's vision

### Mill on Communism

> "If ... the choice were to be made between Communism with all its chances, and
> the present state of society with all its sufferings and injustices... all the
> difficulties, great or small, of Communism would be as dust in the balance."
> — J.S. Mill

- But stopped short: private property had not yet had a fair trial; the laws of
  Europe still reflected violent feudal history
- Two reservations against communism:
  1. Competition is a necessary vent for human energies
  2. Freedom of individuality: *"No society in which eccentricity is a matter of
     reproach can be in a wholesome state."*

### Mill's Economic Model — The Stationary State as Socialism

- Rejected Ricardo's population doom: if workers could be **educated** to
  understand the Malthusian peril, they would voluntarily regulate their numbers
- With population pressure removed, wages would rise, capital accumulation would
  end — approaches a high **stationary state** (same endpoint as Smith/Ricardo,
  but achieved through education, not misery)
- Mill reframed the stationary state as the **first stage of a benign socialism**:
  state would tax away unearned landlord gains and inheritance; **workers'
  cooperatives** would displace enterprises where men were subordinate to masters

### Mill's Legacy

- *Principles* went into 7 editions; Mill printed cheap editions for working-class
  readers; lived until 1873, "a venerated, almost worshiped man"
- His program (taxation of rents, inheritance taxes, workers' cooperatives):
  "a doctrine English to the core: gradualist, optimistic, realistic, and devoid
  of radical overtones"
- *Principles* published 1848 — same year as **The Communist Manifesto**, which
  *"undid, in bitter words, all the calm and buoyant reasonableness with which
  J. S. Mill had endowed the world"*

## Chapter 6: The Inexorable System of Karl Marx

### The World of 1848

- The *Manifesto* opened: *"A spectre is haunting Europe — the spectre of
  Communism. All the powers of old Europe have entered into a holy alliance
  to exorcise this spectre: Pope and Tsar, Metternich and Guizot..."*
- 1848: Louis Philippe abdicated, fled to a Surrey villa; Parisian workingmen
  ran up the Red Flag over the **Hotel de Ville**; Berlin saw barricades;
  Prague and Vienna seized by popular uprisings
- Uprisings were spontaneous, undisciplined, aimless — they won initial victories
  then stalled; Paris mobs subdued at cost of 10,000 casualties; Louis Napoleon
  converted Second Republic to Second Empire; Frederick William IV of Prussia
  refused a constitutional crown, declaring he would accept no crown from
  "the ignoble hands of commoners"

### The Communist League and the Manifesto

- The **Communist League** viewed 1848 as a dress rehearsal; unlike the Utopians
  (who appealed to sympathy and idealism), the Manifesto offered a cold analysis
  of which side *had* to win — proletariat's victory not a matter of morality
  but of **historical mechanics**
- Irony: the authors expected revolution within years, not 70; they looked to
  Western Europe, never toward Russia

### Marx and Engels: The Men

- **Karl Marx**: dark-skinned ("The Moor"), stocky, formidable beard, home a chaos
  of papers and tobacco smoke; ponderous, meticulous, morbidly perfectionist
- **Friedrich Engels**: tall, fair, elegant, athletic — could stutter in twenty
  languages, rode to hounds, loved wine; facile, quick, broad
- "Where Engels supplied breadth and dash, Marx provided the depth"; met for the
  second time in Paris 1844; their first real conversation lasted ten days

### Engels's Path to Radicalism

- Son of a pietist Calvinist manufacturer in the Rhineland; shipped to Bremen
  to learn export business; converted to communism by age 22; then sent to
  Manchester to manage his father's textile mill
- Manchester had two faces: pleasant suburbs for mill owners; hidden slum
  population "living in a state of filth and despair, turning to gin and
  evangelism and doping itself and its children with laudanum"
- Documented in **The Condition of the Working Class in England in 1844** —
  "the most terrible verdict ever passed on the world of industrial slums"
- Engels complained to a gentleman friend about Manchester's misery; the man
  listened quietly and replied, "And yet there is a great deal of money made
  here; good day, sir."

### Marx's Path to Radicalism

- Born 1818 in Trier, Germany; liberal Jewish family that converted to Christianity
  so his father **Heinrich Marx** could practice law; raised on Voltaire, Locke, Diderot
- At Bonn and Berlin, swept into controversy over **Hegel**: change as the rule
  of life, every idea breeding its opposite (**the dialectic**); Hegel's one
  exception: the Prussian state — exempt from dialectical change
- Joined the **Young Hegelians**; turned to journalism; suppressed after
  criticizing the Tsar; expelled from Paris; moved to Brussels, then London

### Dialectical Materialism

- Marx and Engels's philosophy: **dialectical materialism**
  - *Dialectical*: incorporates Hegel's idea of inherent, ceaseless change
  - *Materialist*: grounds change not in ideas but in social and physical environment
    — the economic base, not philosophy

> "The ultimate causes of all social changes and political revolutions are to be
> sought, not in the minds of men... but in changes in the mode of production and
> exchange." — Engels, *Anti-Dühring*

- Every society built on an **economic base** (how it organizes production); over
  it rises a **superstructure** of law, government, religion, philosophy — the
  superstructure must reflect and serve the base
- Ideas are the *product* of environment even though they aim to change it

> "Men make their own history, but they do not make it just as they please...
> but under circumstances directly found, given, and transmitted from the past."
> — Marx, 1852

### Technology Drives Class Conflict

- "The hand-mill gives you society with the feudal lord; the steam-mill, society
  with the industrial capitalist."
- New technologies create new social classes; as technical forces of production
  change, social relations change too → **class conflict** — feudal lords fight
  rising merchants; guild masters oppose young capitalists

### Capitalism's Internal Contradiction

- Capitalism's economic base = industrial production (organized, interdependent)
- Capitalism's superstructure = private property (the most *individualistic*
  of social systems)
- The contradiction: factories necessitate social planning; private property abhors
  it; "Capitalism had become so complex that it needed direction, but capitalists
  insisted on a ruinous freedom"
- Two predicted results:
  1. **Capitalism destroys itself** — planless production leads to constant crises
  2. **Capitalism breeds its own successor** — factories create the technical base
     for socialism AND the disciplined class (the proletariat) that will be its agent

> "What the bourgeoisie therefore produces, above all, are its own gravediggers.
> Its fall and the victory of the proletariat are equally inevitable."
> — The Communist Manifesto

### Marx's Life in London

- Married **Jenny von Westphalen** (daughter of a Prussian aristocrat; chose the
  dark radical young man next door); shared two dismal rooms in a London slum;
  once begged money to buy a coffin for one of their children
- Engels in Manchester (living a double life as a respected Stock Exchange figure)
  supplied never-ending checks and loans
- Marx worked daily at the British Museum, 10am–7pm; wrote articles for the
  *New York Tribune*; rejected for a railway clerical job because of his atrocious
  handwriting; sometimes lacked money for postage stamps

> "My wife is ill. Little Jenny is ill. Lenchen has a sort of nervous fever and I
> can't call in the doctor because I have no money to pay him. For about eight or
> ten days we have all been living on bread and potatoes..." — Marx to Engels

- After writing in agony all day, Marx came home and remarked: "I hope the
  bourgeoisie as long as they live will have cause to remember my carbuncles" —
  he had just written the chapter of *Das Kapital* on the Working Day
- Jenny died 1881; Marx lingered two more years, finally declared "I am not a
  Marxist," and died quietly

### Marx's Revolutionary Organizations

- **Communist League** (1848): paper organization; died with the 1848 revolution
- **International Workingmen's Association** (1864): boasted 7 million members;
  fell apart after 5 years — Owenites, Proudhonists, Fourierists, lukewarm
  Socialists, rabid nationalists; some followed **Bakunin** — oratory "so moving
  that his listeners would have cut their throats if he had asked them to"
- Marx excommunicated rivals ruthlessly: expelled **Wilhelm Weitling** for failing
  a "scientific" cross-examination; expelled **August Willich** for believing
  "pure will" could power revolution

### The Proudhon Feud

- **Pierre-Joseph Proudhon** (*What Is Property?* — answer: "Property is Theft"):

> "Let us together seek the laws of society... but... do not let us in our turn
> dream of indoctrinating the people.... let us never regard a question as
> exhausted.... Otherwise, no!" — Proudhon to Marx

- Marx's answer: Proudhon had written *The Philosophy of Poverty*; Marx annihilated
  it with *The Poverty of Philosophy*
- Heilbroner's verdict: the intolerance Marx injected into working-class politics —
  the inability to tolerate dissent — is communism's **"single greatest"** inherited
  flaw, more lasting than any organization he built

### Das Kapital: The Argument

- 18 years in process; Marx died 1883 with three volumes unfinished; Engels edited
  Vol. II (1885) and Vol. III (1894); 2,500 pages; capital has a "vampire thirst
  for the living blood of labour"
- **Marx's method**: erect the most rigorous, idealized capitalism possible (no
  monopolies, no unions, everything selling at exact value) — prove that *even
  perfect* capitalism is headed for disaster

### The Labor Theory of Value

- Value of a commodity = the amount of **labor** it contains (direct labor +
  overhead labor spread across many goods + past labor embodied in machines)
- Two protagonists: **worker** (free agent who sells one commodity — **labor
  power**) and **capitalist** (must accumulate or be accumulated; competition
  forces endless expansion)

### Surplus Value — The Engine of Profit

- Paradox: if everything sells for exactly its value, how can there be profit
  *in the whole system*?
- **Labor power** is the one special commodity:
  - Its value = the labor to *produce* labor power = the subsistence wage
  - But the worker agrees to work a full day (~10–11 hours in Marx's era) —
    far longer than the ~6 hours needed to earn his subsistence wage
  - The extra hours produce **surplus value** — unpaid labor appropriated by
    the capitalist
- Evidence: Manchester factory, 1862 — average work week over a month and a
  half was **84 hours**; for the prior 18 months, 78.5 hours

### The Falling Rate of Profit — Capitalism's Self-Destruction

- Capitalists compete → must accumulate → bid wages up → surplus value falls →
  profits squeezed
- Solution: introduce **labor-saving machinery** → throws workers onto the street
  → **Industrial Reserve Army** of unemployed → wages compete back down
- Fatal twist: by substituting machines for men, the capitalist substitutes
  *nonprofitable* means for profitable ones — machines transfer their value to
  products but create no new surplus value; only living labor creates surplus value
- "He is killing the goose that lays the golden egg" — yet he *must* do it;
  competitors will if he doesn't
- Result: ratio of living labor to total output shrinks → **falling rate of profit**
  → production becomes unprofitable → consumption dwindles → bankruptcies →
  depression
- "A kind of Greek drama where men go willy-nilly to their fate, and in which
  they all unwittingly cooperate to bring about their own destruction"
