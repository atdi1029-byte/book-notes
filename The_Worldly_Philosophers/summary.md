# The Worldly Philosophers
**Robert L. Heilbroner** | 1953 (7th ed. 1999) | 351 pages

## Summary

A survey of the great economic thinkers from Adam Smith to
Joseph Schumpeter, told as intellectual biography. Heilbroner's
thesis: the worldly philosophers didn't just describe markets —
they gave capitalism a coherent self-image and, in doing so,
changed the course of history.

## Chapters

1. **Introduction** — Why economics is the most consequential
   intellectual discipline: ideas no one noticed brought down
   empires and spawned revolutions.

2. **The Economic Revolution** — Before markets, society ran
   on tradition (caste, custom) or command (pharaohs, kings).
   The market was a violent, centuries-long revolution in how
   people organized survival.

3. **Adam Smith** — Scotland, 1776. The invisible hand, the
   pin factory, accumulation, and the idea that self-interest
   plus competition equals social harmony.

4. **Malthus and Ricardo** — The gloomy successors: population
   outstrips food, wages sink to subsistence, landlords
   pocket everything. Economics earns its "dismal science" tag.

5. **The Utopian Socialists** — Owen's New Lanark and New
   Harmony, Saint-Simon's technocratic vision, Fourier's
   phalansteries and lemonade seas. The first systematic
   critique of industrial capitalism's human cost.

6. **Karl Marx** — Das Kapital, surplus value, the falling
   rate of profit, the inexorable collapse. Heilbroner's
   measured verdict: not infallible, but unavoidable.

7. **Victorian World and the Underworld** — Comfortable
   academics (Marshall, Jevons) and dangerous heretics (Henry
   George, J.A. Hobson). Economics retreats to math while
   imperialism and land speculation build their cases.

8. **Thorstein Veblen** — The Norwegian immigrant who watched
   Gilded Age robber barons with alien eyes. Conspicuous
   consumption, predatory business, and the engineer as
   capitalism's true productive force.

9. **John Maynard Keynes** — The Great Depression, the
   savings-investment leak, and the counter-intuitive cure:
   government spending as substitute for insufficient private
   investment.

10. **Joseph Schumpeter** — Creative destruction, the
    entrepreneurial elite, and the paradox: capitalism is an
    economic success and a sociological failure. "Can
    capitalism survive? No. I do not think it can."

11. **The End of the Worldly Philosophy?** — Has economics
    abandoned its role as capitalism's conscience? The risk of
    treating a social system as a natural science.

## Key Ideas

- The **market system** is historically radical, not natural —
  a 500-year revolution that replaced tradition and command
- **Visions** (Schumpeter's term) precede analysis — what you
  see depends on what you're looking for
- Each great economist was shaped by the social crisis of his
  era; their "laws" were crisis responses
- The capitalist state is never neutral — it reflects the
  balance of social power, not eternal truth
- Economics' retreat into "science" strips it of moral and
  political content — a dangerous abdication

## Why It Matters

Heilbroner's masterwork has sold millions of copies and
introduced two generations of students to economic thought.
Written with a novelist's eye for character and a historian's
sensitivity to context, it remains the best single-volume
account of how economic ideas shaped the modern world.
