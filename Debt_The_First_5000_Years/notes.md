# Debt: The First 5,000 Years — Notes
**David Graeber** | 647 pages | Melville House, 2011/2014 (Updated & Expanded)

## Chapter 1: On the Experience of Moral Confusion (pp. 11–30)

- Opens at a garden party at Westminster Abbey — Graeber, an
  activist anthropologist, talks to a lawyer who asks: "Surely one
  has to pay one's debts?" She works for a poverty relief charity
  but finds debt cancellation morally troubling.
- Graeber had been part of the global justice movement and helped
  "almost completely destroy the IMF" — the movement forced IMF
  to stop imposing structural adjustment policies.
- The IMF origin story: 1970s oil crisis → OPEC petrodollars poured
  into Western banks → Citibank and Chase sent agents to convince
  Third World dictators to take out loans ("go-go banking") → interest
  rates skyrocketed to 20% in early 1980s due to U.S. tight money
  policy → Third World debt crisis → IMF stepped in demanding
  abandonment of food price supports, healthcare, education as
  conditions for refinancing.
- Madagascar: malaria had been eradicated from highlands; IMF
  austerity forced government to cut mosquito monitoring program →
  malaria returned → 10,000 people died. Graeber lived there for
  nearly 2 years, met grieving mothers.
- Haiti: first nation founded by former slaves who defeated Napoleon.
  France demanded 150 million francs ($18 billion equivalent) as
  "compensation" for expropriated plantations + costs of military
  expedition France sent to re-enslave them. All nations (including
  the US) imposed embargo until paid. Haiti still paying this in the
  20th century — permanent debt peonage as punishment for winning.
- The moral confusion at the core: we simultaneously believe
  (1) paying one's debts is basic morality, and (2) moneylenders
  are evil. No other profession so universally condemned in world
  literature.
- Medieval France: Catholic Church forbade interest-bearing loans;
  mendicant friars traveled town to town warning usurers of Hell;
  usurers excommunicated, bodies buried outside consecrated ground.
  Story of dead usurer — priest put corpse on donkey, which went
  straight to the gibbet where thieves were hanged.
- Eastern Himalayas (1970s example from anthropologist Jean-Claude
  Galey): low-caste "vanquished ones" had to borrow from landlords
  just to eat, repay in labor. For weddings/funerals they'd borrow money
  and lender would demand the bride as security — she'd spend her
  first months after wedding night in lender's household as concubine,
  then in a timber camp as a prostitute to pay off father's debt.
  Nobody expressed outrage — "that's just how things work."
- Medieval Hindu law: debtors who don't repay reborn as slaves or
  oxen in creditor's household. Japan (776 AD) — story of Hiromushime,
  greedy moneylender's wife, who after death transformed half into
  ox, ate grass, lay in excrement. Monks prayed over her for 7 days.
  Buddhist paradox: debtors reborn as oxen, but also unreasonable
  creditors — who ends up as the animal?
- The key insight: DEBT is a moral statement, not just an economic
  one. "Surely one has to pay one's debts" is how outrageous things
  are made to seem unremarkable. Violence + quantification work
  together: it's almost impossible to find one without the other.
- U.S. foreign debt = treasury bonds held by countries (Germany,
  Japan, South Korea, Taiwan, Gulf States) that are effectively U.S.
  military protectorates. Are these "loans" or "tribute"? If America
  lost military supremacy, these would suddenly be real liabilities.
- 18th-century debtors' prisons: aristocratic inmates dined with
  servants and prostitutes; poor debtors shackled in filthy cells,
  dying of hunger and fever — same building, two standards.
- The 2008 financial crisis: debt totaling more than combined GDP
  of all countries. Banks crafted mortgages designed to default,
  bet on those defaults, packaged the bets as investments, sold to
  pension funds, then got bailed out. Polls showed Americans wanted
  ordinary debtors bailed out, not banks — but opposite happened.
- U.S. historically least sympathetic to debtors — colonial days
  saw debtors' ears nailed to posts; U.S. last country to adopt
  bankruptcy law despite 1787 Constitution mandate, didn't pass
  until 1898 on "moral grounds." After 2008: use of arrest warrants
  against debtors jumped 60% in Minnesota in 4 years; Illinois and
  Indiana judges jailing debtors for missing payments; one man
  sentenced to "indefinite incarceration" until he paid $300 lumber debt.
- The central question of the book: What does it mean to reduce moral
  obligations to debts? What changes when obligation becomes debt?
  Debt is quantifiable, transferable, impersonal — unlike a favor
  or gratitude owed to a specific person. A debt of $40,000 at 12%
  allows the creditor to be indifferent to the human consequences.
- Language of morality emerged from language of debt: "reckoning,"
  "redemption," "guilt," "freedom," "forgiveness," "sin" — all
  rooted in ancient financial conflict.
- Historical pattern: for 5,000 years, popular insurrections begin
  the same way — ritual destruction of debt records. After that,
  rebels go after land records. Moses Finley: "In the ancient world,
  all revolutionary movements had a single program: cancel the debts
  and redistribute the land."
- Book's method: cast the history of debt across 5,000 years to show
  debt's relationship to money, war, slavery, religion, and morality
  is far more complex than anyone has admitted.
- Credit money is the ORIGINAL form of money — not barter evolving
  into cash. Virtual credit existed in ancient Mesopotamia. History
  cycles between credit (virtual) money eras and bullion eras.
- In past eras of virtual credit money, institutions arose to protect
  debtors from usurers. The current era of credit money is unique:
  it began backwards, with the IMF protecting creditors, not debtors.

## Chapter 2: The Myth of Barter (pp. 33–50)

- The standard economics textbook story: primitive barter →
  inconvenient double coincidence of wants problem → invention of
  money → then credit. This story traces to Adam Smith (1776).
- Economists present this as a "thought experiment," not actual
  history — "imagine a world without money." But it has become
  received truth anyway, even a Malagasy spirit medium Graeber
  interviewed told him ancient people "used to barter directly."
- Adam Smith's motivation: he wanted to argue that money, markets,
  and property exist PRIOR to and independent of government. This
  was his philosophical project — establishing economics as its own
  discipline with its own laws, separate from ethics and politics.
  He was heir to John Locke's liberal tradition.
- Smith's foundational claim: humans have a natural "propensity to
  truck, barter, and exchange." "Nobody ever saw a dog make a fair
  and deliberate exchange of one bone for another."
- Caroline Humphrey (Cambridge, definitive anthropological study):
  "No example of a barter economy, pure and simple, has ever been
  described, let alone the emergence from it of money; all available
  ethnography suggests that there never has been such a thing."
- Barter DOES exist, but almost exclusively between strangers or
  enemies — not between neighbors or community members.
- Nambikwara of Brazil: when two bands meet to trade, they first
  hide women and children, then trade with mock-hostile arguments
  about object quality. "If a man values an object and wants much
  in exchange, instead of saying it is valuable he says it is no good."
  Disputes can lead to actual fights, occasionally deaths.
- Gunwinggu of Australia (dzamalag ceremony): elaborate ritual
  barter between neighboring peoples where visiting women attack
  host musicians, drag them off for sex, exchange cloth for spears.
  A festive game version of the same latent hostility of trade between
  near-strangers. Visiting men "pretend to spear the women, but
  instead hit them with the flat of the blade — we have already
  speared you with our penises."
- What both cases show: barter is for one-time transactions with
  strangers you may never meet again. It requires laying a mantle
  of sociability (music, feasting) over latent hostility. "Truck and
  barter" in pre-Smith English literally meant "to trick or rip off."
- In real communities, people don't barter — they operate on credit
  and gift. Henry needs shoes from Joshua: he doesn't barter potatoes.
  He makes it a gift, and Joshua "owes him one." There is no double
  coincidence of wants problem because they are neighbors and will
  encounter each other's needs over time. Everyone keeps track of
  who owes what to whom.
- Barter is actually a MODERN phenomenon that appears when money
  collapses: Russia in the 1990s, Argentina in 2002. Prison cigarette
  currency: people who grew up with money creating substitutes.
  When Europe "reverted to barter" after Rome collapsed, people
  actually kept accounts in the old Roman monetary units.
- The Iroquois: main economic institution was longhouses with goods
  allocated by women's councils — no arrowheads traded for meat.
  Lewis Henry Morgan documented this clearly by mid-19th century;
  economists simply ignored it.
- Pukhtun of Northern Pakistan: famous for hospitality but also for
  adal-badal (barter), which is practiced specifically with NON-
  relatives to try to get advantage. Best partner is someone distant
  spatially who can't complain.
- The gift economy: Kalahari Bushmen story — Marshall gave a knife
  to a favorite informant; returned a year later to find essentially
  everyone in the band had possessed it at some point. Arab
  convention: if you admire something, you're expected to offer
  "take it" — but if you want to keep it, say "it was a gift."
- Key economic distinction: unit of account (allows comparison of
  value) vs. medium of exchange (the thing you use to pay). Smith's
  story assumes exchange comes first. Graeber argues unit of account
  and credit tracking — not physical exchange — came first.
- Smith's own commodity-money examples debunked: Newfoundland
  cod (credit accounts in pounds, shillings, pence settled with fish),
  Scottish workers with nails (employer owed wages, nails were de
  facto interest on that debt, workers ran pub tabs and settled with
  nails), Virginia tobacco (planters forcing merchants to accept
  tobacco as credit at harvest time). All credit arrangements.
- Ancient Mesopotamia (3500 BC): Temple/Palace bureaucracies ran
  vast operations — temple staff numbered in thousands. Silver
  shekel = 1 gur (bushel) of barley; 1 shekel = 60 minas (1 ration).
  Money created by bureaucrats to track resources, not by traders.
  Silver sat in treasuries for thousands of years, rarely circulated.
  Debts calculated in silver but paid in barley, goats, furniture,
  lapis lazuli — anything the temple could use. Tavern tabs settled
  at harvest time. Coinage not needed — nor invented — for millennia.
- The definitive conclusion (Mitchell-Innes, 1913-14, Banking Law
  Journal): "Our standard account of monetary history is precisely
  backwards. We did not begin with barter, discover money, and then
  develop credit. Virtual money came first. Coins came later. Barter
  is largely what people do when they have no money." Nobody
  refuted this. Economists simply ignored it.

**Credit Theory vs. State Theory (Chartalism)**
- Credit Theory (Mitchell-Innes, U.S. and German economists):
  Money is not a thing but an accounting tool — a unit of measurement.
  A coin is an IOU. A gold coin is merely a promise to pay something
  of equivalent value. Value of currency = measure of trust in other
  people, not intrinsic worth.
  - Keith Hart's brother story: British soldier in 1950s Hong Kong
    wrote checks on UK accounts; local merchants endorsed and
    passed them around; he once saw one of his own checks from 6
    months prior covered in ~40 tiny Chinese inscriptions.
- State Theory / Chartalism (G.F. Knapp, 1905): Money is whatever
  the state accepts in payment of taxes. Taxes CREATE markets —
  they don't just fund governments. Classic example: A king who
  wants to feed 50,000 soldiers simply coins money and demands
  taxes back in that coin. Instantly, the entire economy becomes
  organized around supplying those soldiers.
- Tally sticks (medieval England): hazelwood twigs notched to
  record debts, split in half. Creditor kept "the stock" (origin of
  "stockholder"), debtor kept "the stub" (origin of "ticket stub").
  Circulated as currency. The Bank of England operates on similar
  logic today.
- Bank of England (1694): consortium lent £1.2M to the king at 8%
  interest; received monopoly on issuing banknotes — the right to
  lend IOUs based on what the king owed them. The original loan
  has NEVER been repaid and CAN NEVER be repaid — if it were,
  the entire British monetary system would cease to exist.
- Madagascar colonial tax: General Gallieni's "impôt moralisateur"
  (educational tax), 1901 — payable only in newly issued Malagasy
  francs, due at harvest when rice prices were lowest. Farmers sold
  rice cheap, borrowed it back at high prices, fell into debt, forced
  to grow cash crops or send children to work in cities. Internal
  government documents explicitly stated goal was to create consumer
  habits and tastes that would persist after the colonizers left.
  Malagasy resistance: some villages paid the tax, then gave remaining
  money to lineage elders who bought cattle for ancestor sacrifice
  rather than spending at Chinese shops.
- The Wizard of Oz (1900): Populist parable. Wicked Witches = East/
  West Coast bankers. Scarecrow = farmers (no brains to escape
  debt). Tin Woodsman = industrial workers (no heart to unite with
  farmers). Cowardly Lion = politicians. Yellow brick road = gold
  standard. Silver slippers (NOT ruby). Oz = ounce. William Jennings
  Bryan ran for president three times on Free Silver platform; lost.
- Keynes: spent years in 1920s studying Mesopotamian cuneiform
  records ("Babylonian madness"). Concluded: "For the last four
  thousand years money has been effectively a creature of the state."
  "All civilized money is, beyond the possibility of dispute, chartalist."
  Also: banks create money with no intrinsic limit — whatever they
  lend gets deposited back in a bank, so total debits/credits always
  cancel.
- Chartalism seen as crankish by mainstream economists — yet those
  same economists, when advising colonial governments, routinely
  recommended tax-based market creation. Theory said markets were
  spontaneous; practice was to engineer them.
- Chapter bridge: Neither credit theory nor state theory fully answers
  "by what right does the state demand taxes?" — primordial debt
  theory, developed by French researchers (Aglietta, Orléans, Théret),
  attempts to answer this.

## Chapter 3: Primordial Debts (pp. 56–89)

- Primordial debt theory: emerged partly from debates about the euro.
  Core argument: monetary policy and social policy are inseparable.
  Governments use taxes to create money because they are guardians
  of the debt all citizens owe each other. This social debt is the essence
  of society, older than money or markets.
- Vedic texts (1500–1200 BC), earliest known philosophical reflections
  on debt: debt synonymous with guilt and sin in these hymns.
  Yama (god of death) figures prominently — to be in debt is to have
  a weight placed on you by Death. Gamblers "wander homeless, in
  constant fear, in debt, and seeking money."
- The Brahmanas' conclusion: "A man, being born, is a debt; by his
  own self he is born to Death, and only when he sacrifices does he
  redeem himself from Death." Sacrifice = "tribute paid to Death."
  But the paradox: if you pay the debt, you die. Sacrifice is merely
  interest — a postponement, with animal life substituting for your own.
- Extended primordial debts: debt to gods (repaid by sacrifice), to
  Sages who created Vedic knowledge (repaid by study), to ancestors
  "the Fathers" (repaid by having children), and to humanity as a whole
  (repaid by hospitality to strangers).
- Graeber's critique of the theory: the Vedic texts are an "empty
  canvas" since we know almost nothing about the society that
  produced them. In other ancient civilizations we DO know about,
  there's no such conception of sacrifice-as-debt-payment. Exchange
  implies equality; with gods, equality was considered impossible.
- Ancient Greeks and Romans didn't even pay direct taxes — only
  subject/conquered peoples paid tribute. Benjamin Franklin was wrong:
  "in this world nothing is certain except death and taxes." In fact,
  taxes have often not existed for free citizens. This undermines
  any theory linking our existential debt to government taxation.
- Barbarian Law Codes (600s-700s AD — Goths, Frisians, Franks):
  Philip Grierson (numismatist) suggested money emerged from legal
  practice — wergeld (compensation for injury/murder). The codes have
  incredibly detailed compensation schedules: specific prices for loss
  of arm, hand, each finger, fingernail, a blow to the head so that
  bone projects, etc. Fine in marten pelts but culprit has squirrels —
  how many squirrels? This forced elaborate price equivalences.
  Salic Law: separate titles for theft of pigs, cattle, sheep, goats,
  dogs, each with breakdown by age and sex.
  English "to pay" derives from word meaning "to pacify, appease."
- Sumerian debt cancellations: Interest-bearing loans originated in
  Mesopotamia before writing — probably Temple administrators
  financing caravan trade. The river valleys produced food but lacked
  stone, wood, metal, even silver. Merchants advanced goods, sold
  overseas; interest was the Temple's share of profits. Consumer
  loans (usury) followed quickly — peasants fell into debt peonage.
  Process: grain/goats/furniture first, then fields and houses, then
  servants, then children, wives, finally the debtor himself. Debt-peons
  not quite slaves but perpetual household servants.
  Crisis: after bad harvests, large proportions of the peasantry fell
  into debt peonage, farms abandoned, social breakdown threatened.
- Babylonian "clean slate" (amargi): Sumerian/Babylonian kings
  periodically announced general amnesties — all consumer debts null
  and void, lands returned to original owners, debt-peons freed. Kings
  typically declared this on assuming power, often had to repeat it.
  The Sumerian word amargi = the first recorded word for "freedom"
  in any known human language. Literally means "return to mother"
  — because that's what freed debt-peons could do.
  Note: Commercial debts between merchants NOT cancelled. Only
  consumer/personal debt between peasants and wealthy was cleared.
- Graeber's deeper critique: These Vedic debts (to nature, sages,
  ancestors, humanity) are nothing like commercial debts. You "repay"
  your debt to ancestors by having children — but that would mean
  lending cash to someone else repays your creditors. The Brahmanas
  may actually be showing that our relationship to the cosmos is
  NOT like a commercial transaction at all. To imagine yourself as
  an equivalent to Everything Else that Exists is the real crime.
- The concept of "society" as a debt-object is actually a modern
  invention, not an ancient one. It emerged around the French
  Revolution alongside the nation-state. Auguste Comte (coined
  "sociology") proposed a literal "Religion of Society" (Positivism),
  with vestments whose buttons were on the back so you needed
  others' help. He argued: "We are born under a load of obligations
  of every kind... On what human foundation could one seat the idea
  of rights?" Only duties, not rights — we owe everything to society.
- Émile Durkheim: "God" and "society" are ultimately the same thing.
  All gods are projections of society. All religions are ways of
  recognizing our mutual dependence on each other.
- The Soviet Union used primordial debt logic to justify forbidding
  emigration: "The USSR created these people, raised and educated
  them — what right do they have to take the product of our
  investment and transfer it elsewhere?"
- The great trap: Market logic says we start as free individuals who
  owe nothing to anyone. State logic says we begin with an infinite
  debt to the society that formed us. We're told these are the only
  options. But: states created markets; markets require states. They
  were born together and cannot exist without each other.

## Chapter 4: Cruelty and Redemption (pp. 90–)

- Keith Hart: "Two sides to any coin — heads = political authority
  that minted it; tails = precise specification of its exchange value.
  One side: money is a relation between persons (a token). Other
  side: money is a thing capable of entering into relations with things."
- Money is neither purely commodity nor purely IOU — it is both.
  Pure credit money has limits: trust becomes scarce among strangers.
  A Roman silver coin stamped with Tiberius's image circulated above
  its bullion value because the state accepted it at face value.
- Currency as community token: 19th-century Siam used porcelain
  casino chips as small change. English shops issued wood/lead/leather
  token IOUs for centuries, technically illegal but widespread.
  Medieval Pomeranian peasants forced rulers to accept taxes payable
  in wine, cheese, peppers, chickens, eggs, and herring. This was
  an area with free peasants in a strong political position.
- Money always hovers between commodity and debt-token; political
  contestation determines which wins out at any moment.
- Nietzsche (Genealogy of Morals, 1887): starts from Adam Smith's
  assumptions and drives them to their logical end. The feeling of
  personal obligation originated in the buyer-seller, creditor-debtor
  relationship — the "oldest and most primitive personal relationship."
  Calculating, measuring, assessing values IS what makes us human.
  German schuld = both "debt" and "guilt." Creditors punished debtors
  unable to repay by literally cutting flesh off the debtor's body. As
  communities grew, the ancestors became gods; with Christianity,
  Adam the creditor became Adam the debtor (Original Sin), and
  the debt became infinite. God's solution: sacrifice himself to pay
  himself. "The creditor sacrificing himself for the debtor, out of love."
- Graeber's response to Nietzsche: the premise is insane. The Inuit
  story: hunter drops off hundreds of pounds of walrus meat;
  Graeber thanks him; the man is indignant: "Up in our country we
  are human! Since we are human we help each other. By gifts one
  makes slaves and by whips one makes dogs." The hunter knew
  exactly how commercial calculation works — and deliberately
  refused to let it govern his relationships. Being truly human means
  REFUSING to calculate who owes what.
- Redemption (Hebrew: padah and goel): "to buy something back."
  In context of ancient Judaea — recovering ancestral land, recovering
  family members held as debt-pawns. The Jubilee law (Law of Moses,
  revived by Nehemiah 444 BC): all consumer debts canceled after 7
  years ("Sabbath year"), all those in debt-bondage released.
- Nehemiah: born in Babylon (familiar with Babylonian clean-slate
  tradition), appointed governor of Judaea by the Persian king,
  found social crisis on arrival — peasants unable to pay taxes,
  creditors carrying off children. "Some of our daughters are brought
  unto bondage already: neither is it in our power to redeem them."
  Nehemiah furious, convened a "great assembly," issued a classic
  debt jubilee. Set maximum interest rates.
- Parable of the Unforgiving Servant: King wants to "settle accounts"
  with servants (already absurd — kings can't enter exchange with
  subjects). Servant owes "10,000 talents" = joke, means "a sum
  no human being could ever conceivably repay." King forgives it.
  Servant then throws fellow servant in prison for debt of 100 denarii
  (~$1,000). King casts forgiven servant into torture until he repays
  everything — which equals eternity, since the amount was infinite.
  The parable's uncomfortable implication: forgiveness in this world
  is ultimately impossible. "Forgive us our debts as we forgive our
  debtors" — we all know we don't actually forgive our debtors.
- Why debt protest carried more moral weight than slavery/caste:
  debt is premised on equality — two parties begin as equals in a
  contract. Slaves and untouchables are assumed to be intrinsically
  inferior (pure hierarchy). But a peasant who borrowed from a
  rich cousin, on assumption that "humans help each other," watched
  his farm seized and his children led away — this was a betrayal of
  an assumed equality. Framing it as a breach of contract made it a
  moral issue AND made it the debtor's fault. The outrage of
  "Our flesh is as the flesh of our brethren" is the demand to
  be treated as an equal — and thus far more politically explosive.
- Throughout history: overt class conflict took the form of demands
  for debt cancellation + land redistribution. Ancient Athens, Rome,
  China — similar dynamics everywhere. Chinese legend: coinage
  invented by an ancient emperor to redeem children of families
  forced to sell them after devastating floods.

## Chapter 5: A Brief Treatise on the Moral Grounds of Economic Relations (pp. 107–)

- To tell the history of debt is to reconstruct how marketplace
  language came to pervade all of human life — including the moral
  and religious language used to oppose it. Both Vedic and Christian
  thought first describe all morality as debt, then demonstrate that
  morality CAN'T really be reduced to debt.
- Graeber argues for starting not with cosmological answers but
  with the tiny details of everyday social existence: passing the salt,
  bumming a cigarette. Anthropology reveals both enormous diversity
  AND remarkable moral commonalities.
- Contemporary social theory treats economics as "master discipline."
  Rational choice theory starts from assumptions experimental
  psychologists have repeatedly proven false — that humans are
  self-interested calculating machines.
- The "reciprocity" tradition: Marcel Mauss (1924 "The Gift"), Levi-
  Strauss (3 spheres: language = exchange of words, kinship = exchange
  of women, economics = exchange of things, all governed by same
  law of reciprocity). Levi-Strauss's star has fallen but the assumptions
  persist. Almost everyone still assumes social life is fundamentally
  reciprocal — and therefore debt is the root of all morality (debt =
  a balance not yet restored).
- Problem: not all reciprocity is just. "An eye for an eye" = vindictive
  brutality. "I'll scratch your back, you scratch mine" = corruption.
  The mother-child relation is deeply moral but not reciprocal.
  Ernest Thompson Seton (naturalist writer): on his 21st birthday,
  his father presented him with a bill for every expense of his
  childhood, including the doctor's delivery fee. Seton paid it, but
  never spoke to his father again. Paying the bill and terminating
  the relationship was the correct response — because squaring
  accounts means walking away from each other.

**The Three Moral Foundations: Communism, Hierarchy, Exchange**

**Communism** (as Graeber defines it — not Soviet ideology):
"From each according to their abilities, to each according to their needs."
Exists everywhere already, in all human societies. No society has been
organized exclusively on this principle, but no society functions without
it. "Baseline communism" = the assumption that if the need is great
enough or the cost low enough, help will be provided.
- Capitalist firms operate internally on communist principles: workers
  hand tools without demanding payment. The most "communist"
  organizations are efficient precisely because they allocate tasks
  by ability.
- In disasters (floods, blackouts, economic collapse) hierarchies and
  markets become luxuries; people revert to communism. "Strangers
  become sisters and brothers."
- Nuer (E.E. Evans-Pritchard): gave him wrong directions at first
  (he was a stranger, possibly an enemy — also a British government
  agent whose government had recently bombed the settlement).
  Later gave him correct directions and shared everything.
- Nuer: anyone known to have extra grain/tobacco/tools sees
  their stockpile disappear — yet cattle are sacred, never bought or sold.
- Iroquois Six Nations: each village divided into moieties, each side
  buries the other's dead. It would be absurd to say "We buried 5 of
  yours last year, you only buried 2 of ours."
- Arab burglar story: tasted salt from a jar, realized he'd "eaten at
  the owner's table" — dutifully put everything back.
- English words "host," "hostile," "hostage," "hospitality" all from
  same Latin root — all-or-nothing logic of dealing with strangers.
- Shopkeepers in poor neighborhoods are almost never of the same
  ethnic group as customers — if they were, communistic obligations
  would make running a business impossible.
- Nasruddin and the king: charged 100 gold pieces for quail eggs.
  "It's not quail eggs that are rare around here — it's visits from kings."

**Exchange**:
- About equivalence. Both parties are keeping accounts. The entire
  relationship can be canceled out at any time.
- Madagascar cloth market: Graeber's friend spent 4 hours bargaining
  for a sweater with elaborate mock-indignation. A second friend
  walked up, asked the price, asked "what's your real final price?",
  paid it. "Some people enjoy that sort of thing."
- Tiv women (Nigeria, Laura Bohannan): neighbors brought little
  gifts — two ears of corn, one vegetable marrow, one chicken,
  five tomatoes, handful peanuts. All had to be returned — but
  NOT the exact value. Exact value = you don't want to see them
  again. Slightly more or less = you want to continue the relationship.
  Women walked miles to return a handful of okra "in an endless
  circle of gifts to which no one ever handed over the precise value
  of the object last received."
- Pierre Bourdieu on Kabyle Berber men (Algeria) — challenge and
  riposte: exchange of insults, blows, gifts all follow identical logic.
  Counter-gift must be slightly different and slightly grander.
  Must "pick on someone one's own size." Overwhelming a poor man
  with a gift he can't repay is cruel. Indonesian story: rich man
  sacrificed a magnificent ox to shame a rival; rival sacrificed a
  chicken and won.
- Giving gifts to kings is impossible: Nasruddin and the turnips.
  Roman inventor of unbreakable glass — Tiberius had him killed
  to protect the value of his treasury. King of Sind (Ibn Battuta):
  always gave many times the value of any gift received — local
  bankers financed visitors' gifts knowing they'd profit from the
  king's one-upmanship.
- Exchange implies equality — which is why kings don't like it.
  In 18th-century New Zealand, English settlers learned not to admire
  a Maori warrior's jade pendant — he'd insist on giving it and then
  return to claim your coat or gun.

**Hierarchy**:
- Does NOT operate by reciprocity. Operates by precedent.
- "The peasants provide food, the lords provide protection" sounds
  reciprocal but is not. Actual principle: once a transfer is made,
  it establishes a precedent — it becomes what is owed thereafter.
- Continuum from plunder to charity: only at the extremes can
  material exchange occur between people with no social relation.
  Only lunatics mug their neighbors. True charity is anonymous.
- Santa Claus: patron saint of both children AND thieves. The
  archetypal anonymous benefactor — no social relations possible,
  no debt created. Also does not actually exist.
- Gang tokens in post-Soviet states: gangs prey so systematically on
  travelers that they issue tokens proving you've already been robbed.
  "One step toward the creation of a state." Ibn Khaldun's theory:
  states originate in nomadic raiders systematizing their predation —
  pillage becomes tribute, rape becomes "right of first night."
- Hierarchy operates by precedent: Persian provinces vied to send
  gifts to the Great King → became obligatory tribute. Medieval
  Ardres: lord had a bear; inhabitants fed it; bear died; lord continued
  to exact the loaves of bread. Gifts to superiors required "letters of
  non-prejudice" so they wouldn't become permanent obligations.
- Identity and hierarchy are always entangled: ranking creates
  essentially different kinds of human being. From noble castes to
  race ideologies. Even in intimate relations: if a friend is unusually
  generous once, you reciprocate. If she's generous repeatedly, you
  conclude she's "a generous person" and stop reciprocating.
- The logic of hierarchy makes abuse self-legitimating. Sarah Stillman:
  if a middle-class 13-year-old is kidnapped and killed = national
  crisis. If a 13-year-old child prostitute is killed = "what can you
  expect from someone like that."
- Medieval feudalism: priests pray, nobles fight, peasants feed —
  nobody calculated how many prayers or how much military
  protection equals a ton of wheat.
- Big men (Papua New Guinea): charismatic individuals who spend
  their time acquiring wealth specifically to give it away at great
  feasts. North American Indian chiefs: formally had no power to
  compel anyone, famous for oratory; often the poorest man in the
  village because of constant pressure to give. You can judge how
  egalitarian a society is by whether those in authority are mere
  conduits for redistribution or use position to accumulate.
- Communism slipping into hierarchy: Inuit Greenland hunter praised
  a piece of meat by belittling it — "I don't have much, but there's
  a piece of meat outside that the dogs refused several times."
  Everyone's mouth watered. Saying "thank you" to the Inuit hunter
  suggests the gift was optional, which implies a hierarchy.
- Competitive gift exchange in heroic societies: Celtic nobles —
  whoever received a present too magnificent to match could only
  cut his own throat honorably. Egil the Viking: saw Einar's gift
  of an extraordinary shield and said, "Does he think I'm going to
  stay up all night composing a poem about his shield? Get my horse,
  I'm going to ride after him and kill him." (Einar had ridden far
  enough away; Egil composed the poem.)
- Patron-client relations: in imperial Rome and medieval China,
  being respectable meant spending mornings paying respects to
  patrons. Lorraine Blaxter on French Pyrenees farmers: "rendre
  service" (giving service) = mutual aid. But a particularly great
  favor (finding someone a job) creates a debt that can never be
  repaid in kind — only through deference and token tomatoes.
  "Can he repay the favor, or not?" Both are true: the tomatoes
  are simultaneously a token of unpayable debt AND pretend repayment,
  so both parties can maintain the fiction of equal status.

**The Definition of Debt**:
- Debt = "an agreement between equals to no longer be equals,
  at least for a time." This is its essence.
- Debt requires: (1) two people not seen as fundamentally different
  kinds of being, (2) who are at least potential equals, (3) currently
  NOT in a state of equality, (4) with some conceivable way to
  restore equality.
- While debt remains unpaid: hierarchy takes hold (precedent, no
  reciprocity, like a peasant before a feudal lord).
- European words for "debt" are synonyms for "fault," "sin," or
  "guilt." Ancient Crete: debtors pretended to snatch the money
  from the lender's purse — so that if they defaulted they could be
  charged with violence.
- Debt = "an exchange that has not yet been brought to completion."
- "Please" = "if it pleases you to do this" = "you're under no
  obligation." But there IS a social obligation. "Please" is a polite
  lie that only began during the commercial revolution of the 16th-17th
  centuries — the language of bureaus, shops, and offices.
- "Thank you" → "I will remember what you did" (often untrue).
  Portuguese obrigado = "obliged." French merci = from "mercy" =
  begging for mercy, placing yourself in benefactor's power.
  "You're welcome" / "de rien" = "I am NOT inscribing a debit."
  "My pleasure" = "it was a credit, not a debit — YOU did ME a favor."
- Tiv women's constant gift-giving: constantly recreating society by
  ensuring everyone was always slightly in debt to everyone else.

**Rabelais' "In Praise of Debt" (Panurge)**:
- Panurge (Gargantua and Pantagruel, Book 3, 1540s Paris): "God
  forbid that I should ever be out of debt!" Debt is the basis of
  his philosophy: "Always owe somebody something — he will be
  forever praying God to grant you a long and blessed life."
- The cosmological argument: "A world without debts? Jupiter would
  dispossess Saturn. The Moon would remain dark and bloody. From
  earth no water would be made. The elements would not transmute.
  Faith, Hope and Charity would be banished from the world."
- "Amongst human beings none will save another: 'Help! Fire! I'm
  drowning! Murder!' Nobody will come. Why? Because he has lent
  nothing, and no one owes him anything."
- Graeber's commentary: Panurge is describing the logical conclusion
  of imagining all society as exchange. A universe where everyone
  is exactly squared with everyone else is a universe of pure
  indifference where no one needs anyone.

## Chapter 6: Games with Sex and Death (pp. 152–)

- Reducing human life to exchange has the effect of making women
  disappear from economic history. At the top and bottom of the
  financial system (financiers and gangsters), deals are made much
  like the Nambikwara/Gunwinggu — with sex, drugs, music, food,
  and potential violence playing roles. Neil Bush (George W.'s brother)
  in divorce proceedings: women "mysteriously appeared" at hotel
  doors in Thailand and Hong Kong. Almost certainly daughters of
  indebted parents, contractual debt peons.
- Irish law codes: "cumal" = female slave = unit of account. Bondmaids
  were so plentiful they functioned as currency. Standard economic
  historians don't stop to ask how or why.
- "Covet thy neighbor's wife" in the Ten Commandments: not about
  lust (covered by "thou shalt not commit adultery") but about the
  prospect of taking a woman as a debt-peon — a servant to sweep
  your yard and hang laundry.

**Social Currencies and Human Economies**:
- "Primitive money" / "social currencies" (Graeber's term): NOT crude
  versions of commercial money. Often never used to buy anything.
  Used exclusively to create, maintain, and reorganize relations between
  people: arrange marriages, establish paternity, head off feuds,
  console mourners, seek forgiveness for crimes, negotiate treaties,
  acquire followers.
- "Human economies": economies primarily concerned not with
  accumulation of wealth but with creation, destruction, and
  rearranging of human beings. Commercial economies are the
  relative newcomers.

**Philippe Rospabé's theory**: "Primitive money" was not originally
  a way to pay debts. It is a way of recognizing the existence of
  debts that CANNOT possibly be paid. "Money begins as a substitute
  for life" — a recognition of a life-debt.
- Bridewealth: when a Fijian suitor's family presents a whale tooth,
  they are NOT paying for a woman. They are acknowledging that
  they are asking for something so uniquely valuable that payment
  would be impossible. Only payment for a woman is another woman.
- Tiv of Nigeria: the perfect marriage = exchange of sisters. If you
  marry my sister but I don't want yours, I become "guardian" of your
  sister and can claim her for someone else. Complex webs of wardship
  developed. Using brass rods as bridewealth proxy: 2-3 bundles to
  establish suitor status, more to assuage her mother, 5 more to
  get guardian to accept, more when she gives birth — but the debt
  can never be fully paid. "Everyone knows only a woman can really
  be given in exchange for a woman."
- Wergeld / bloodwealth: same currency used for marriages and for
  murder compensation. Brass rods acknowledge "we owe a life to
  your family" — but nobody suggests they are actually EQUIVALENT
  to a dead relative's life. The Nuer leopard-skin chief who mediates
  blood feuds: victim's family always first refuses ("it would be
  insulting to suggest any cattle could substitute for my son"). Chief
  threatens to curse them if they don't accept. Even after accepting,
  the offense is never forgiven — "at dances, merely bumping into
  the killer's kin may cause a fight."
- Nuer "ghost wife": if a man was murdered before marrying, the 40
  cattle bloodwealth (= standard bridewealth rate) used to acquire
  a "ghost wife" — formally married to the dead man's spirit. Any
  children she produces are the dead man's children. Those boys
  especially are committed to someday avenge his death.
- Iroquois: if a man died, his wife's clan could "put his name upon
  the mat" — send wampum to commission a raid on an enemy
  village. Captive either tortured to death or adopted by throwing
  wampum around his shoulders. He was then given the dead man's
  name, considered to have married his wife, to own his possessions —
  effectively to BE the dead man.
- Key principle: "Money first and foremost acknowledges the
  existence of a debt that cannot be paid."

**Lele of Congo (Mary Douglas, 1950s)**:
- Raffia cloth = small-change social currency. Camwood bars = high
  denomination (100 raffia = 3-5 camwood). Neither used for market
  purchases (no markets). Used for social relations: husband to wife,
  son to father, fines, medical fees, marriage negotiations.
- Matrilineal system: children belong to mother's clan. Blood debt
  system: if a Lele woman died in childbirth → she had committed
  adultery → adulterer responsible. Culprit owed a life → must transfer
  a young woman from his family as a "pawn." Pawnship inherited
  across generations. Girls pledged before birth, even before their
  mothers were of marriageable age.
- Only young women accepted as pawns — to maximize reproduction.
  The entire system functioned as an apparatus for male control over
  women. "Women could only be pawns, never own pawns."
- Village-wife institution: young men collectively bought the right
  to a "village wife" — she was treated like a princess, not required
  to do any household chores (husbands did them), could help herself
  to possessions, was sexually available to the entire age-set (~10-12
  men). Over time she'd settle with fewer husbands. Her children
  belonged to the entire village collectively.
- The crucial insight: in human economies, A = A (one life equals
  one life). It was only when force (village raiding parties) entered
  the picture that A = B (one life = 100 raffia cloths) became possible.
  The price of a slave = 100 raffia cloths. The equation A = B was
  "established at the point of a spear."
- Social currencies almost always made from adornment materials:
  beads, shells, feathers, dog/whale teeth, gold, silver, brass rods
  (used for jewelry). "Money almost always arises first from objects
  used primarily as adornment of the person." Only when governments
  and markets enter do we see utilitarian currencies like barley,
  cheese, tobacco, salt.

## Chapter 7: Honor and Degradation (pp. 191–230)

### Core thesis
- **Honor and degradation are flip sides of the same coin** — both rooted in slavery
- The chapter traces how honor → price → morality via the mechanism of stripping humans from context
- Epigraph: early Sumerian dictionary — ur5 (liver/foundation/interest-bearing debt/slave-woman) — the same word covers all of these

### Olaudah Equiano story
- Born ~1745 in Kingdom of Benin, kidnapped at 11, sold to British slavers (Bight of Biafra → Barbados → Virginia)
- Spent Seven Years' War hauling gunpowder on British frigate; promised freedom multiple times, denied; finally bought own freedom from Quaker merchant in Pennsylvania
- Became merchant, best-selling author, Arctic explorer, leading English abolitionist
- Played key role in British abolition of slave trade (1807)
- **Troubling aspect**: for most of his early life he wasn't opposed to slavery — even briefly bought slaves while saving for his own freedom
- Explanation: Equiano was a man of honor; to be enslaved = stripped of honor; to restore honor you must adopt the standards of the society that surrounds you — including accepting its institutions. This is perhaps the most violent aspect of slavery: restoring one's dignity requires accepting the system that denied it.

### Slavery as Social Death
- Ali 'Abd al-Wahid Wafi (1931 dissertation, Paris): surveyed slavery worldwide
- Four ways to become a slave: (1) war/capture/kidnapping, (2) legal punishment for crimes/debt, (3) paternal sale of children, (4) self-sale
- All have in common: **you become a slave when you would otherwise have died**
- A slave is not someone who owes their master their life — a slave is, in an important sense, **dead**
- Roman law: if a soldier was captured, family read his will and disposed of possessions; if he returned, had to start over, including remarrying
- Orlando Patterson *Slavery and Social Death*: three defining elements:
  1. Slavery is not a moral relation — it is pure violence. Everyone knows this even when dressed in paternalistic language
  2. Slave is alienated from all binding moral relations (family, ancestors, community) — the master-slave bond is the only one that matters
  3. Slave's situation is one of utter degradation — "natal alienation"
- West Africa (Mande): captured warriors offered millet porridge, then given arms to kill themselves. Those who refused were slapped — accepted the contempt that deprived them of personality
- Tiv horror stories about living dead, Haitian zombie stories — all play on the horror of slavery as living death

### Honor as Surplus Dignity
- Honor is NOT dignity — **honor is surplus dignity**
- Heightened consciousness of power that comes from having stripped others of their power/dignity
- This is why men of honor combine ease/self-assurance with extreme jumpiness about slights
- Insults → violence, because honor is zero-sum: your dignity comes from my ability to degrade you
- **Key definition**: honor is "the power to turn others into money" — the ultimate token of honor is the ability to commodify humans

### Medieval Irish *cumal* (slave-girl money)
- Ireland in early Middle Ages: human economy on fringe of expanding commercial one, lively slave trade
- "Ireland has no mineral wealth — foreign luxury goods bought mainly with cattle and people"
- Every person has a formal, legal honor price denominated in **cumals** (slave-girls)
- Kings, bishops, abbots — honor measured in slave-girls; violations compensated in cumals
- A washerwoman who shamed an abbot: he reduced her to a life servant — her permanent disgrace restored his honor
- Honor is zero-sum: degradation of one person = elevation of another's honor
- **Key insight**: Irish jurists had no discomfort putting exact monetary price on human dignity because money was used for social purposes (human economy), not commercial transactions. Money was not used to buy eggs or haircuts — so the dignity-money link wasn't corrupted yet

### The Moral Crisis: When Human Economy Meets Commercial Economy
- Greek: tīme = both "honor" AND "price" — first, no contradiction
- As markets developed, tīme split: honor (aristocratic disdain for money) vs. price (market value)
- Modern Greek: "timi" = honor (open-handed generosity, disregard for money) AND price (per pound of tomatoes) — the split never resolved
- **Mediterranean obsession with sexual propriety** — didn't exist in Irish/Welsh material
  - Why? Because when money used for dignity can also buy prostitutes, the moral landscape collapses
  - The question becomes: how do you signal that your women are NOT for sale?

### Mesopotamia: Origins of Patriarchy
- Early Sumerian texts (3000–2500 BC): women everywhere — female rulers, doctors, merchants, scribes, public officials
- Over the next 1,000 years: women's role erodes; patriarchal pattern emerges — chastity, virginity emphasis, loss of independent legal status
- By 1200 BC: women sequestered in harems, obligatory veiling in some places
- Graeber's argument: **debt explosion turned human relations into potential commodities** — women most exposed — and caused horrified reaction from male "winners" who went to lengths to prove their women couldn't be bought
- Marriage in Sumer: evolved from gift feast → payment to bride's father → "terhatum" (purchase price of a virgin); marriage = "taking possession" of a woman (same word as seizing goods)
- Nuzi: bridewealth = 40 shekels silver = same as price of a slave-girl. This made things "uncomfortably obvious"
- Critical factor: debt. If husband took a loan, wife and children became surety — could be taken as debt pawns
- Prostitution: early Sumerian temples hosted sacred sexual activities; in earliest texts, **non-procreative sex = divine gift, embodiment of civilization**
- Enkidu in Gilgamesh: wild man civilized by temple courtesan (Ishtar priestess) — made human through sex. Already in early versions, ambivalence present.
- Feminist historian **Gerda Lerner**: commercial prostitution grew from pauperization of farmers, debt slavery, sale of daughters; by mid-2nd millennium BC, prostitution common occupation for daughters of poor
- **"Patriarchy" originated as a rejection of great urban civilizations** — pastoral fringes, deserts, steppes = places of displaced, indebted farmers. Resistance in ancient Middle East = politics of exodus. Periodically these tribes swept back as conquerors (bringing strict patriarchal codes from Old Testament tradition).

### Assyrian Veiling Laws (~1400–1100 BC)
- First known reference to veiling in Middle East history
- **First to make policing of social boundaries the responsibility of the state**
- Respectable women, widows, daughters of free Assyrian men → must veil outside
- Prostitutes and slaves → NOT allowed to wear veils
- Punishments directed at prostitutes/slaves who veil (50 lashes + pitch on head; slave girl: ears cut off)
- Respectable women = those "whose bodies could not under any conditions be bought or sold"
- The veil = walking around in a man's domestic sphere, even in public

### Ancient Greece
- Homeric world: honor = treasure + followers; money existed but wasn't used to buy things; no contradiction between tīme/honor and tīme/price
- Commercial markets ~600 BC: coins first used to pay soldiers; by 5th century, agora = both assembly AND marketplace
- **Immediate result**: debt crises ("The poor, together with their wives and children, were enslaved to the rich")
- Greek solution: abolished debt peonage and shipped the poor to military colonies abroad → became conduits for slave trade
- Chattel slavery transformed Greek society: modest citizens could participate in political life → drove aristocracy to elaborate distinctions
- Women in classical Athens: expected to wear veils in public (unlike Persia, Syria)
- **Apollodorus and Nicostratus story** (court speech, ~365 BC):
  - Apollodorus (nouveau-riche, banker father began as slave) and neighbor Nicostratus (aristocratic, straitened means)
  - Nicostratus captured by pirates, held for ransom at Aegina; Apollodorus took 16% interest loan secured by his townhouse to rescue him
  - Nicostratus schemed to have Apollodorus falsely declared "public debtor" to strip his court rights and raid his house
  - **Interpretation**: Nicostratus couldn't bear being indebted to a social inferior — like Egil the Viking who'd kill a friend rather than write a thank-you poem
- **Result in Athens**: extreme moral confusion — debt language colonized all moral language
- Plato's Republic opens with the question: does justice = paying one's debts? Socrates demolishes every answer. The Republic is an extended attempt to find morality beyond commercial logic.
- **Plato's personal story**: captured and sold on auction block at Aegina. Annikeris (Libyan Epicurean philosopher) ransomed him; refused repayment. Plato used the 20 minas to buy land for the Academy. Plato never mentioned Annikeris in his writings — perhaps embarrassed by the debt.

### Ancient Rome: Property and Freedom
- Roman law conquered the world more thoroughly than Roman armies; provides basis of contract, obligation, property, jurisdiction, rights, liberties
- Peculiarity: Roman law defines property (dominium) as **relation between a person and a thing** — characterized by absolute power over it
- Conceptually bizarre — human beings have relations with each other (mutual). You can't have a relation with a thing.
- **Desert island thought experiment**: man alone on island needs no property rights — property is really an understanding *between people* about things, not between a person and a thing
- **Property derived from slavery**: dominium (absolute private property) was a late Republic term — appeared just when hundreds of thousands of captives poured into Italy (~50 BC). Slaves were defined in Roman law as both *persons* AND *res* (things). To imagine a relation between a person and a thing, your starting point has to be a relation between two persons, one of whom is also a thing.
- Etymology: dominium → dominus (master/slave-owner) → domus (household). Related to "domestic." Familia → famulus (slave). A family = all those under the authority of the paterfamilias — originally conceived as absolute. Fathers could whip, torture, sell, or execute children; with slaves, no justification needed at all.
- Roman dominium → modern liberal property theory: liberty = right to do what one likes with one's possessions. Rights treated as a form of property. My right = your obligation.
- "Natural rights theory" (Jean Gerson, ~1400) → ironically embraced by conservatives, not progressives. "For a Gersonian, liberty was property and could be exchanged in the same way as any other property" — sold, swapped, loaned, surrendered.
- This logic was used to justify slavery: if freedom can be sold, nothing intrinsically wrong with debt peonage or slavery. Developed in Antwerp and Lisbon — at the center of the emerging slave trade.
- Thomas Hobbes: government as contract — citizens voluntarily gave up natural liberties to sovereign. This same logic → wage labor: renting our freedom, as slavery = selling it.
- **Self-ownership**: "we own ourselves" casts us as both master and slave simultaneously. Requires the mind-body split to make sense — which cognitive science shows is false. Yet we can't abandon it because our concepts of property, law, and freedom require it.
- Thomas Jefferson: "all men are created equal, endowed with certain inalienable Rights" — directly contradicts the moral basis of slavery. But he didn't create a new conception of rights — he just inserted "not" in various places. "Most of our most precious rights and freedoms are a series of exceptions to an overall moral and legal framework that suggests we shouldn't really have them in the first place."
- Wage labor endures: formal slavery eliminated, but "the idea that you can alienate your liberty, at least temporarily, endures... determines what most of us have to do for most of our waking hours, except usually on weekends."

## Chapter 8: Credit Versus Bullion and the Cycles of History (pp. 246–250+)

### The great cycles of money
- Graeber's central historical framework: **5,000 years of Eurasian history alternates between credit money and bullion money**
- Credit money (virtual) ↔ bullion (metal, physical) — the single most important factor driving the switch: **war**
- Why bullion in war? Gold and silver can be **stolen**. Credit is a record of trust — requires relationship. Bullion requires only accurate scales and confidence someone else will accept it. In zones of widespread violence, simplicity wins.
- Soldiers: access to loot (gold/silver from conquest), constantly moving. A heavily armed itinerant soldier = "the very definition of a poor credit risk." Barter between neighbors is absurd (Mitchell-Innes was right), but barter/bullion between a villager and a passing mercenary makes perfect sense.
- Bullion = "an ingot of gold or silver has served the same role as the contemporary drug dealer's suitcase full of unmarked bills: an object without a history, valuable because it will be accepted anywhere, no questions asked."

### The five cycles (Graeber's framework):
1. **Age of First Agrarian Empires** (3500–800 BC): virtual credit money — Mesopotamia, temples, clay tablets
2. **Axial Age** (800 BC–600 AD): rise of coinage, shift to metal bullion (to be covered in Chapter 9)
3. **Middle Ages** (600–1450 AD): return to virtual credit money (Chapter 10)
4. **Age of Capitalist Empires** (1450–1971 AD): massive switch back to gold and silver bullion; begins with slave trade and colonialism (Chapter 11)
5. **Current age** (~1971–present): virtual money again — began when Nixon severed dollar from gold. Shape of this period "necessarily invisible"

### Coinage invented ~600–500 BC — simultaneously, independently, in three places:
- Northern China (Great Plain), Ganges river valley (India), Aegean Sea (Greece/Lydia)
- Not a technological innovation — each used entirely different technologies
- A **social transformation**: local rulers decided longstanding credit systems were inadequate; began issuing precious metal pieces for day-to-day transactions
- Then ~600 AD, reverse: cash dried up everywhere, return to credit

### Mesopotamia credit system (3500–800 BC):
- Clay tablets inscribed with obligation, sealed in clay envelopes (bullae), marked with borrower's seal
- Creditor kept envelope; broken open on repayment
- Some bullae were **negotiable instruments** — "to the bearer" — could circulate as promissory notes (a 5-shekel tablet = 5-shekel note)
- Promissory notes circulated within merchant guilds or urban neighborhoods — not with strangers
- Tavern-keepers operated on credit; market stalls probably did too
- **Origins of interest**: word for "interest" in most ancient languages = word for "offspring" — possibly from livestock loans, but more likely from commercial loans (temples/palaces forwarding goods to merchants)
- Fixed interest rate vs. profit-sharing: palace bureaucrats distrusted merchant adventurers who might fabricate excuses (shipwrecks, winged snakes) — fixed rate made lies irrelevant
- Herodotus on Persians: "To tell a lie is considered by them the greatest disgrace, and next to that to be in debt … because one in debt must of necessity tell lies."
- **First use of the word "freedom" (amargi)**: King Enmetena of Lagash (2402 BC) — first calculates compound interest Umma owes (4.5 trillion liters of barley — intentionally preposterous), starts war. After winning: publishes first-ever debt cancellation edict: "restored the child to its mother, the mother to its child; canceled all interest due."
- Hammurabi's debt cancellations (Babylon, 1761 BC): "breaking the tablets," raising a torch. Debt pledges freed, cultivation rights restored. The standard list of peasant revolutionary demands: cancel debts, destroy records, redistribute land. Mesopotamian rulers preempted unrest by instituting these reforms themselves as grand cosmic renewal, reenacting Marduk's creation of the universe.
- Egypt: avoided interest-bearing debt for most of its history. Centralized pharaonic bureaucracy substituted taxation for the temple/palace loan system of Mesopotamia. Loans existed but without interest; debt bondage was rare. Later Iron Age → Egyptian debt crises begin. Rosetta Stone = announcement of debt amnesty by Ptolemy V, 196 BC.
- China: no centralized temple/palace accounting → diverse currency forms (cowries, jade, bronze spades/knives). Knotted strings and notched bamboo for credit tabulation. Legend: Emperor Yu and Tang coined money to rescue starving people from debt (selling children) — actually fanciful, but preserves memory of debt-induced social crisis.

## Chapter 9: The Axial Age (800 BC–600 AD) (pp. 258–270+)

### The Axial Age framework
- Karl Jaspers' term: Pythagoras (570–495 BC), Buddha (563–483 BC), Confucius (551–479 BC) were contemporaries; China, India, Greece all saw simultaneous philosophical ferment
- **Graeber's expanded definition**: 800 BC–600 AD — covers birth of all major philosophical traditions AND all major world religions (Zoroastrianism, Prophetic Judaism, Buddhism, Jainism, Hinduism, Confucianism, Taoism, Christianity, Islam)
- **The key connection**: The core of Jaspers' Axial Age exactly corresponds to when coinage was invented; the three places where coins were first invented are also the three epicenters of Axial Age thought (Yellow River, Ganges valley, Aegean)
- This was a period of "checkerboard" of diminutive kingdoms, constant war externally, constant political debate internally; drop-out culture of ascetics and wandering sages

### Coinage: origin by plunder
- First Lydian coins (~600 BC): electrum lumps from Pactolus River, stamped with insignia, quickly monopolized by royal mint
- Indian silver bars: punch-marked, multiple counter-punches — "strongly suggests people used to dealing with more abstract credit instruments"
- Chinese coinage: evolved from social currencies — cast bronze shaped like cowries, knives, spades; governments quickly stepped in
- **How precious metals got democratized**: David Schaps — "Most of it was stolen." War plunder placed gold and silver into soldiers' hands; markets spread it through populations.
- Axial Age armies = new kind: trained professional soldiers (not aristocrats and retainers). Greek hoplite phalanx tactics → mercenaries sought from Egypt to Crimea → needed meaningful payment (not livestock, not promissory notes useless abroad → coins)
- Phoenicians (the "consummate merchants of antiquity") rejected coinage for centuries — continued with ingots and promissory notes until forced to pay Sicilian mercenaries (Carthage's first coins marked "for the people of the camp"). Lesson: great trading nations lost to military powers.
- Fate of Phoenician cities: Sidon destroyed 351 BC (40,000 committed mass suicide), Tyre destroyed by Alexander (10,000 killed, 30,000 sold into slavery), Carthage razed 146 BC by Rome (100,000s killed/enslaved, fields sowed with salt).
- **Military-coinage-slavery complex** (Graeber's term, expanding Geoffrey Ingham's "military-coinage complex"):
  - Coins minted from war spoils → paid to soldiers → required for taxes → markets develop → soldiers need more money → more war → more coins
  - Alexander's army: 120,000 men, required half a ton of silver/day in wages. Melted down Persian temple reserves (an estimated $285 billion in today's terms, released into markets in months).
  - Alexander also destroyed ancient Mesopotamian and Phoenician credit systems by dethesaurizing their silver reserves (basis of credit) and insisting taxes be paid in his own coin.

### Mediterranean (Athens and Rome)
- Athens: early debt crises before coinage even mattered → coinage became solution; coins minted from war spoils distributed as jury fees, assembly fees, direct distributions (famously at Laurium silver mine discovery, 483 BC)
- Rome: parallel history — debt crises between patricians and plebeians, "secessions of the plebs" (commoners camped outside city, threatened mass defection). Eventually: debt bondage outlawed 326 BC, nearly same time as first Roman coinage 338 BC.
- Roman Empire = "vast machine for extraction of precious metals, coining, and distribution to the military" — but only coin-heavy in two zones: Italy/big cities and frontier zones with legions. Elsewhere, older credit systems continued.
- Debt crises never really solved — military expansion just fended off the problem. When expansion stopped: free peasantry eliminated, countryside sank into debt peonage. Plutarch (~100 AD): usurers "sent to Greece with chains and cords."
- Without free peasantry, Roman state had to arm Germanic barbarians → fall of empire.

### India
- Indus Valley collapse ~1600 BC; new urban civilization on Ganges plains ~600 BC
- Checkerboard of Ksatriya republics (warrior caste, lands held in common) and centralized kingdoms; both Buddha and Mahavira born in republics
- Magadha eventually won: controlled mines. Kautilya's *Arthasastra*: "The treasury is based upon mining, the army upon the treasury; he who has army and treasury may conquer the whole wide earth."
- Alexander's men mutinied rather than face Magadha's army (200,000 infantry, 20,000 horses, 4,000 elephants)
- Cash economy born among camp followers: petty traders, prostitutes, hired servants following soldiers
- Kautilya's tricks: pay soldiers generous wages, then replace market hawkers with government agents charging double. Organize prostitutes as trained spies.
- Chattel slavery became commonplace at height of great armies (unlike any other period in Indian history); war captives relocated to government work camps ("remarkably dreary places: veritable work camps, with all forms of festive entertainment officially prohibited")
- Ashoka (273–232 BC): conquered Kalingas 265 BC (hundreds of thousands killed/enslaved); so horrified by carnage he embraced Buddhism, declared nonviolence. Didn't abolish army or slavery, but demobilized much of it, funded Buddhist/Jain monasteries. Coinage declined; sophisticated credit flourished.
- Buddhism and money: monks forbidden to touch gold or silver, but Buddhism has **never formally condemned usury** — rejecting violence while being pro-commerce.

### China (Axial Age)
- Warring States period (475–221 BC) → unified by Qin → immediately overthrown → Han dynasty (206 BC–220 AD) founded by rural constable Liu Bao, first to adopt Confucian ideology and exam system for civil administration
- Same Axial Age pattern as India/Greece: fractured landscape, professional armies, coined money to pay them, chattel slavery on scale never seen before or since in Chinese history
- Difference: China never minted gold or silver coins — cast bronze disks ("cash"), strung together in huge numbers. Large donations to temples required oxcarts of money.
- Chinese philosophy more socially engaged from the start: School of the Tillers (anarchist peasant intellectuals creating egalitarian communities), Mohists (urban artisans, opposed to war, organized military engineers to volunteer against aggressors), Confucians (popular education campaigns)
- Confucius's contemporaries were already speaking of "li" (profit/benefit — literally: sheaf of wheat next to a knife). Lü Buwei story: "return on plowing fields = 10x; return on pearls and jade = 100x; return on establishing a ruler = incalculable." Made an exiled prince emperor of Qin, became first minister.

### The Axial Age Synthesis (Graeber's 8-point framework)
1. Markets → military logic → conquered government itself, defined its purpose
2. Wherever military-coinage-slavery complex emerged: materialist philosophies followed (both in the sense of material forces explaining reality AND material wealth as the ultimate goal)
3. Philosophers reacted by exploring ideas of humanity and soul, seeking new ethical foundations
4. These philosophers made common cause with popular social movements → first popular movements that were also intellectual movements
5. All were first and foremost **peace movements**, rejecting aggressive war as the foundation of politics
6. Attempts to use market logic to rebuild morality (Mohism's "social profit") all foundered. Stronger impulse: imagine a world beyond debt entirely, where social attachments = bondage, body = prison.
7. Rulers eventually adopted these religions as empires reached limits of expansion: Ashoka/Buddhism, Constantine/Christianity, Han emperor Wu-Ti/Confucianism. Only Confucianism succeeded long-term (Chinese empire, 2,000 years). Rome fell but Church endured. Buddhism was mostly driven out of India.
8. **The ideal division of spheres that endures today**: market (selfish acquisition) vs. religion (generosity, charity). "Pure greed and pure generosity are complementary concepts; neither could really be imagined without the other." Both appeared together wherever impersonal cash money appeared.
- Only people who actually abolished slavery in the ancient world were religious sects (Essenes) — by defecting from the social order and forming utopian communities
- Buddhist sangha (monastery) = named after the democratic republics of northern India, preserving consensus-based democratic practice that would otherwise have been forgotten
- Long-term achievement: wars became less brutal, slavery faded to insignificance or nonexistence across most of Eurasia by the Middle Ages

### Materialism I: The Pursuit of Profit
- Impersonal markets born of war → new way of thinking about motivation: **radical simplification** — everyone is pursuing profit/advantage, pretending to care about anything else is theater
- Pre-Axial Age: motives assumed to be complex (love, envy, pride, spite — all mixed together, no assumption that the selfish motive is necessarily the real one)
- Cash transactions with strangers (especially in war zones) really did reduce to "how many X for how many Y" — pure calculation
- "Word 'rational' derives from 'ratio' — how many of X go into Y. Previously used by architects and engineers; with markets, everyone who didn't want to get cheated had to learn it."
- Result: the kind of analysis that made it possible to study human society the same way one studies the movement of celestial bodies — which is exactly what Axial Age philosophy does

### Materialism II: Substance (Coins and Philosophy)
- Maurice Leenhardt (Catholic missionary, New Caledonia, 1920s): asked sculptor Boesoou if he'd grasped the idea of spirit. Boesoou: "Spirit? Bah! We already knew the spirit. What you brought us was the **body**." — The body as something separate from the soul, a mere material prison, was the exotic new idea.
- Axial Age spirituality is built on materialism: the pre-Socratics begin with "What is the world made of?" — Thales (water), Anaximenes (air), Anaximander (apeiron, unlimited abstract substance). All three were from **Miletus** — the first Greek city to mint coins, the commercial center of the region, where cash first replaced credit in everyday transactions.
- Richard Seaford (*Money and the Early Greek Mind*): coins are profoundly new objects — both a lump of metal AND something more (a drachma, exchangeable for anything). This "double-sidedness" (material + abstract) is exactly what Greek philosophy is obsessed with.
- Coins work within a civic community but dissolve into mere metal outside it (in war zones). This tension between community agreement and physical nature = the tension between Form and Matter that haunts all Greek philosophy.
- Greece → "war between Spirit and Flesh, between Idea and Reality" = already inscribed in the nature of coinage

## Chapter 10: The Middle Ages (600–1450 AD) (pp. 291–310+)

### Overview
- Middle Ages = **credit money reasserts itself** after the Axial Age of bullion
- The nexus between war, bullion, and slavery was broken; conquest no longer celebrated as end of political life
- Economic life came under regulation of religious authorities; movement to control/forbid predatory lending
- Counter-intuitive: the Middle Ages was **an improvement** over the terrors of the Axial Age for most people
- Misconception: "reverted to barter" — actually reverted to credit. Absence of coins ≠ absence of money. Barbarian law codes show careful accounts in Roman money even in the Dark Ages.
- Roman Gaul's slave plantations → replaced by manors/churches. Medieval agriculture no less efficient than ancient, and the surplus extraction was far less. "However oppressed medieval serfs might have been, their plight was nothing compared with that of their Axial Age equivalents."

### Medieval India
- After Mauryan Empire → 500 years of Buddhist-supporting kingdoms; coinage declining; armies paid in land grants not salaries; cities shrank to tiny villages
- But: Hindu law-books show **increasing sophistication of credit** (sureties, collateral, mortgages, promissory notes, compound interest) — not a barter economy, but a credit economy
- **Buddhist "inexhaustible treasuries" (perpetual endowments)**: lay supporter donates sum → loaned out at 15% annually → interest earmarked for specific monastic purposes forever. "Created with a document in stone to last as long as the moon and the sun."
- Gold that went out of circulation ended up in religious establishments — hoarded or shaped into divine images
- Ruler Harsa of Kashmir (1089–1101 AD) tried to revive bullion economy: appointed a "Superintendent for the Destruction of the Gods," sent leprous monks to desecrate 4,000+ Buddhist establishments with urine and excrement before melting the statues. Betrayed and killed — held out as example of where revival of old ways leads.
- Caste system solidified: **Dharmaśāstra** (Brahmin law codes 200 BC–400 AD): Vedic debt to gods resurrected but applied only to Brahmins. Laws of Manu: Sudras who listened to sacred texts → molten lead poured into ears. Caste system organized interest rates: Brahmin = 2%/month, Ksatriya 3%, Vaisya 4%, Sudra 5% (24% vs 60% annually). "Bodily interest" = physical labor in creditor's fields until principal cleared (could last generations).
- **Fundamental tension**: debt implies equality between contracting parties → but caste system assumes fundamental hierarchy → these principles are incompatible. This is why peasant uprisings (from Chiapas to Japan) target debt records rather than caste systems: debt is more morally outrageous because it presupposes equality and then betrays it. Deccan riots 1875: indebted farmers seized and destroyed moneylenders' account books.

### China: Buddhism and Finance Capital
- China = opposite extreme: successful Axial Age empire (Confucianism) actually endured; bureaucracy essentially impossible to destroy once established. Chinese monetary theory always chartalist.
- Peasant uprisings: 1.8 per hour in some decades; most famous dynasties (Han, Tang, Song, Ming) were originally peasant insurrections. Chinese statecraft = constant balancing act between feeding cities, keeping nomads at bay, and not triggering rural revolt.
- Government perpetually fighting village moneylenders: peasants fall into debt after disasters → forced to labor in what were once their own lands → threat of rebellion → government reforms. Wang Mang coup (9 AD): nationalized estates, banned slave-holding, established state loan agency (interest-free funeral loans, 3%/month commercial). "He was confident all business transactions would be under his scrutiny and the abuse of usury would be forever eradicated." — It was not.
- Fernand Braudel distinction: markets (C-M-C') = exchanging goods through money. Capitalism (M-C-M') = using money to get more money, requiring monopoly. **China = anti-capitalist market state**: promoted markets, refused to partner with would-be Chinese capitalists, treated merchants like soldiers (necessary but immoral). Result: China maintained highest standard of living in world until ~1820 (past Industrial Revolution).
- **Buddhist Inexhaustible Treasuries as finance capital**: donations → loaned out at interest → earmarked for monastery purposes → surplus invested commercially → monasteries surrounded by oil presses, flour mills, shops, hostels, bonded workers. Gernet: "the world's first genuine forms of concentrated finance capital." Required continual growth (until all sentient beings embrace Dharma = infinite horizon).
- Chinese Buddhism developed elaborate theology of karmic debt: School of Three Stages — every sin of past lives = debt needing to be discharged. Since acquiring wealth necessarily harms other beings, everyone is insolvent. Salvation by donation to the Inexhaustible Treasuries (sins/debts instantly blotted out). "One purchases felicity and sells one's sins, just as in commercial operations."
- Milk-debt theology: mothers transform their flesh and blood into milk; calculated at 180 pecks of milk over first 3 years of life. This debt is infinite — "If you stacked jewels from the ground to the twenty-eighth heaven, it would not compare." Solution: donate to the Inexhaustible Treasury in your mother's name.
- Government repression of monasteries: periodically monasteries grew so rich the government ran out of metal (monks melted coins to build giant copper Buddha statues). 845 AD: 4,600 monasteries razed, 260,000 monks/nuns defrocked, 150,000 temple serfs freed.
- **Graeber's synthesis**: exchange-logic applied to infinity → infinite debt. Things that utterly defy exchange logic (mother's love, god's gift of existence) get first quantified, then declared unpayable, then repaid through donations. "What we treat as eternal in our actual relations with one another vanishes and reappears as an abstraction, an absolute." Ganesha myth: Kartikeya races around universe in 3 years; Ganesha walks around his mother and says "You are the universe to me."








### China: Tally Sticks and Paper Money
- Everyday transactions: coins for strangers; local shopkeepers extended credit; tally sticks (split bamboo, like English hazelwood) for accounts
- "Flying Cash" (806 AD): merchants depositing money with capital bankers, receiving promissory notes redeemable in provinces. Government tried to forbid, then copied and institutionalized.
- Song dynasty (960–1279 AD): local banking running similar operations → de facto paper money
- Bureau of Exchange Medium (1023 AD) + printing press = millions of notes produced in factory settings
- China's paper money "experiment" misrepresented as a failure: the centuries when paper money was in use are considered the most economically dynamic in Chinese history
- Key point: "fiat money" is deceptive — almost all new paper money forms emerged from everyday credit instruments, not government creation. China just had a government powerful and suspicious enough of merchants to take charge.

### Islam: Capital as Credit (Near West)
- For most of the Middle Ages: economic nerve center of the world was Islam, not China or Europe
- Islam is "the West" from a world-historical perspective — Judaism, Christianity, and Islam are three manifestations of the same Western intellectual tradition (centered on Mesopotamia/Levant)
- Confucians: suspicious of strict law, preferred scholar-gentry governance. Islam: embraced law as religious institution; viewed government as unfortunate necessity, potentially corrupt.
- **Islamic legal system banned all the abuses of the Axial Age**: slavery through kidnapping, judicial punishment, debt, sale of children — all forbidden or unenforceable. Usury (any interest) strictly forbidden.
- Yet Islamic commerce flourished immediately: profits still possible through service fees and goods bought on credit priced higher than cash goods. Checks (sakk), promissory notes (ruq'a), letters of credit (suftaja) — all in use by ~900 AD. By 1000, every trader in Basra had a banking account, paid only by check.
- Partnerships replaced interest: one party supplies capital, other carries enterprise; share in profits. "Partnership of good reputation" (sharika al-mafalis) = partnership with no capital, just good names.
- **The merchant as paragon**: Islamic ideal — honor + adventure + harmlessness. Sindbad the Sailor as cultural ideal. No Christian parallel exists.
- **Adam Smith's sources**: Ghazali (1058–1111) and Tusi (1201–1274) both argued exchange is outgrowth of human rationality/speech; both used the same illustration (no one has seen two dogs exchanging bones). Smith's pin factory (18 operations) = Ghazali's needle factory (25 operations). Tusi: division of labor is mutual aid, not competition. "Market is not only founded on, but is an extension of the kind of baseline communism on which any society must ultimately rest."
- Ghazali on money: gold and silver are "useless by themselves, just like stones. Created to circulate, to govern, to facilitate transactions. They are symbols to know the value and grades of goods." Money as "mirror that has no color" — can reflect all colors because it has no particular form of its own. Therefore lending money at interest = "kidnapping a postman."
- The Islamic free-market ideal: markets organized around mosque and bazaar, independent of government. Indian Ocean became effectively a Muslim lake. Malacca: merchants from Swahili, Arab, Egyptian, Ethiopian, Armenian, Indian, Chinese, Southeast Asian quarters — sealed deals "with a handshake and a glance at heaven."
- Key insight: this genuine free market, not backed by state police — based entirely on trust and reputation — could NEVER become what Adam Smith later imagined: purely self-interested individuals competing by any means at hand.

### Christendom (Far West): Commerce, Lending, and War
- Europe late to the Middle Ages; hinterland through most of it — exporter of slaves, raw materials; importer of Chinese silks, Indian calicoes, Arab steel
- Post-Roman Europe: coins disappeared → calculation in Roman money → Carolingian "imaginary money" (pounds, shillings, pence — conceptual accounting system through 17th century)
- Church regulated markets; Scholastics (13th century) adopted Aristotle: money is a mere social convention
- Catholic usury condemnation: initially as harsh as Islam. Biblical grounds absolute (Exodus 22:25; Psalms 15:5; Prophets). Early Church Fathers writing during last great Roman debt crisis.
- St. Basil on usury (Cappadocia, 365 AD): vivid portrait of the moneylender pretending friendship until interest is mentioned — "All is changed; the frown is relaxed." The borrower: money slips away, interest accumulates, forced to hide under the couch at every unexpected knock.
- St. Ambrose's *De Tobia* (Milan, 380 AD): usury = form of violent robbery, even murder. But added "Exception of St. Ambrose": Deuteronomy 23:19–20 permits usury to strangers/foreigners (nokri). "It is only legitimate to do so from those whom it would not be a crime to kill." Later to become enormously important.
- St. Basil's radical position: "God gave us all things in common; the communism of the Apostles was the only proper model for a truly Christian society." Other Church Fathers: communism is the ideal but unrealistic — accept existing property, encourage charity through spiritual arguments.

### Europe (continued): Feudalism, Crusades, and Finance
- Church opposed usury but said nothing against feudal dependency: former debt peons → serfs or vassals. Vassalage was "voluntary" (just as Christians "freely" submit to God). This was consonant with Christianity.
- Commerce itself was suspect: "An honest merchant is a contradiction in terms." Many held you couldn't be both a merchant and a Christian.
- Jewish moneylenders: Christians used them as tools — granted Jews right to charge extravagant interest backed by law, then withdrew protection whenever convenient. King John (1210 AD) ordered all Jews imprisoned and tortured. One wealthy merchant, Abraham of Bristol, had seven molars extracted, one per day, until he paid 10,000 marks. King Henry III (1216–1272) turned victims over to his brother "so that those whom one brother had flayed, the other might embowel." Shakespeare's Merchant of Venice ("pound of flesh") = guilty projection of what was actually being done to Jews, not by them.
- "Exception of Saint Ambrose" found in Gratian's Decretum (canon law, 1140 AD): allowed usury to those with whom Christendom was "literally at war." This complicated Jewish-Christian financial relations enormously.
- By 1100s, Jewish moneylenders displaced by Lombards (northern Italy) and Cahorsins (French town of Cahors) — became notorious rural usurers across Western Europe.
- Church tightening: 1148, mortgage loopholes closed; 1187, merchants forbidden to charge higher prices on credit (going further than Islamic law ever had); 1179, usury made mortal sin, usurers excommunicated and denied Christian burial.
- The word "interesse" (our "interest") = compensation for loss due to late payment. Clever workaround: not a fee for the loan, but a penalty for not repaying on time. Merchant capitalism finally established itself in northern Italian city-states (Venice, Florence, Genoa, Milan) and German Hanseatic League.
- **Knights Templar (1118–1307 AD)**: first pioneers of modern banking — financing the Crusades through mortgages → bills of exchange (modeled on Islamic suftaja, written in secret code) redeemable in Jerusalem. "Christians appear to have first adopted Islamic financial techniques to finance attacks against Islam." Templars destroyed by King Phillip IV (deep in debt to them) — leaders tortured, wealth expropriated.
- Italian banks (Bardi, Peruzzi, Medici): bills of exchange = long-distance money-changing; but quickly morphed into complex currency speculation instruments.
- Venetian model: merchant empire through sugar plantations (staffed by African slaves) and Black Sea slave trade. Genoese "war by subscription" — selling shares in expeditions in exchange for percentage of spoils. Fourth Crusade (1199): knights at tournament of Écry swore to retake Holy Land, commissioned Venetian fleet (50% share of profits) → ended up sacking Christian Constantinople instead. Baldwin II of Constantinople eventually took out a mortgage on his own son.
- **Key insight**: the knight-errant (Sir Percival, Holy Grail) = romanticized image of the traveling merchant — men who set off on lonely ventures into danger, their outcome uncertain. The Grail = abstract value: Richard Wagner was right that it represents a new form of financial abstraction. "Sometimes a plate, sometimes a cup, sometimes a stone." Marc Shell: "a blank check, the ultimate financial abstraction."

### The Symbol / Tally Etymological Connection
- Greek "symbolon" (origin of "symbol") = "tally" — an object broken in half to mark a contract or record a debt
- Chinese "fu" = has almost exactly the same origin. Fu = tally, contract, imperial seal, warrant, passport, credentials, sign of agreement with heaven.
- Evolution parallel: both start as objects broken to record debts → expand to become symbols of divine order. Both paths converge on the same insight: symbols are tokens of agreement between parties, one of whom is fundamentally superior.
- Yin-yang symbol = appears to have developed from the left/right halves of a tally
- Medieval theory of money: Ghazali argued money has NO intrinsic value, and this very lack is the basis of its value — it can "govern" because it has nothing of its own. But unlike Aristotle (money = social convention), Ghazali says money was given by God.
- Medieval synthesis: if the Axial Age was the age of materialism, the Middle Ages were the age of transcendence. True value lies elsewhere, in a domain that can only be approached through study or contemplation. Arguments about wealth → arguments about debt and morality → arguments about the nature of our place in the universe.

## Chapter 11: Age of the Great Capitalist Empires (1450–1971 AD) (pp. 354–370+)

### The turn back to bullion (~1450)
- The era of capitalist empires = another turn of the historical cycle: away from virtual credit → back to gold and silver bullion
- Accompanied by return of: vast empires and professional armies, massive predatory warfare, untrammeled usury and debt peonage, materialist philosophies, scientific creativity, and chattel slavery
- 15th century: century of catastrophe (Black Death decimated cities, commercial economy sagged, knightly classes squabbled) BUT from ordinary workers' perspective, times were never better — plague killed one-third of workforce → wages soared → laws had to be passed banning lowborn from wearing silks
- Festive life reached its heyday: floats, dragons, maypoles, "Abbots of Unreason." Then systematically destroyed by Puritan reformers.
- 1500–1650: prices in England rose 500%, but wages rose slowly → real wages fell to 40% of previous levels

### China abandons paper money (the real origin story)
- The conventional story: Spanish silver from Americas caused European price revolution. Problem: most of that silver didn't stay in Europe. Most gold went to India; overwhelming majority of silver shipped to China.
- **The real story**: China abandoned paper money under Ming dynasty (1368 AD, after overthrowing Mongols). Ming distrusted commerce, promoted agrarian self-sufficiency. Quasi-caste system: farmers, craftsmen, soldiers forbidden to change jobs. Taxes in kind sparked illegal silver mining. Government cracked down → insurrections. Eventually surrendered: stopped issuing paper money, legalized mines, allowed silver as currency, substituted silver tax system. But Chinese mines quickly exhausted. Then Japanese mines. China had to turn to the New World.
- Europe had never produced anything Asians wanted → always paid in specie. Chinese demand for silver sustained the American mines: "Had China not had such a dynamic economy... those mines might have become unprofitable within a few decades." By late 16th century, China importing nearly 50 tons of silver/year (~90% of its silver), rising to 116 tons/year (~97% of silver) by early 17th century.

### The violence of European expansion
- Vasco da Gama (1498): "the principle that the seas should be a zone of peaceful trade came to an immediate end." Portuguese flotillas bombarded every port city, seized strategic points, extorted protection money from unarmed Indian Ocean merchants.
- The conquistadors: driven by debt as much as greed. Hernán Cortés in 1518: deeply in debt, got himself a military commission, borrowed 8,000 pesos from merchant friends "secured on his Indians and estates," burned his boats. After conquering Tenochtitlán (100,000 Aztecs dead), soldiers discovered they received 50–80 pesos each — and immediately had most of that seized again as repayment for equipment and medical care charged at wildly inflated prices. "We were all very deeply in debt." Spanish merchants charged even more inflated prices → Cortés resolved this by sending the most troublesome complainers off to establish colonial settlements (which is how the mines filled with corpses).
- "We are not dealing with a psychology of cold, calculating greed, but of a much more complicated mix of shame and righteous indignation, and of the frantic urgency of debts that would only compound and accumulate."
- Cortés himself: after conquering the greatest empire in the Americas, within 5 years writing the Emperor that he was "in debt for upwards of 500 ounces of gold, without possessing a single peso towards it." Eventually pawned his wife's jewelry to finance California expeditions. Returned to Spain to petition emperor for debt relief.
- Mine conditions (Fray Motolinia): "The bodies of those Indians and of the slaves who died in the mines produced such a stench that it caused a pestilence... along a great part of the road one could scarcely avoid walking over dead bodies or bones."
- Policy evolution: Outright enslavement → Debt peonage (demanding heavy taxes, lending money at interest to those who can't pay, then demanding repayment in labor). Royal efforts to stop this regularly failed because Charles V himself was deeply in debt to Florentine, Genoese, and Neapolitan banking firms — American gold made up ~1/5 of his total revenue.

### The nature of capitalism
- **Capital vs. money**: Capital is not simply money. In the Axial Age, money was a tool of empire — when empires collapsed, the whole apparatus melted away. Under capitalism, the logic of money was granted autonomy; political and military power were reorganized around it.
- The corporation: first major joint-stock corporations were the English and Dutch East India Companies — pursuing the same combination of exploration, conquest, and extraction as the conquistadors. "A structure designed to eliminate all moral imperatives but profit." Executives are "morally bound to ignore" considerations of human welfare because their only responsibility is maximum return.
- Capitalism requires state enforcement: Islamic free markets showed that "purely competitive markets will not develop, and loans at interest will become effectively impossible to collect" without state enforcement of contracts.
- Martin Luther (1524): "Christians are rare in this world; therefore the world needs a strict, hard, temporal government that will compel and constrain the wicked not to rob and to return what they borrow." Money is sin; but without state violence to enforce it, commercial society would collapse. "Let no one think that the world can be ruled without blood; the sword of the ruler must be red and bloody."

## Ch 11 (cont.) — Age of Great Capitalist Empires (pages 371-390)

### Luther, the Peasant Revolt, and the Communism of the Rich
- Luther's reforms unleashed forces beyond his control: radical reformers argued
  poor need not repay usurious loans; proposed revival of Old Testament Jubilee/Sabbatical year
- Peasant War 1525: 100,000+ slaughtered. Rebels represented themselves as
  "simple Christians aiming to restore the true communism of the Gospels"
- Luther's reaction: Old Testament laws no longer binding; 4-5% interest legal;
  collecting interest is sinful but borrowers still have NO right to break the law
- Calvin: rejected blanket ban on usury entirely; by 1650 most Protestants accepted
  reasonable interest provided lenders don't exploit the poor
- "Communism of the rich": aristocracy cooperated when class position threatened;
  merchants closed ranks when it mattered — Graeber's key term for elite solidarity

### Margrave Casimir of Brandenburg-Ansbach — the Psychology of Debt
- His father Friedrich ("somewhat deranged from too much racing and jousting") owed
  200,000 guilders; Casimir staged palace coup in 1515, locked father in castle,
  publicly claimed he couldn't afford 2 guilders for his father's entertainment
- Casimir embraced Luther's reforms in 1521 mainly for Church lands/monastic assets
- Added ~50,000 guilders in gambling debts of his own
- Placed creditors in charge of civil administration → increased exactions on peasants
- His lands in Tauber Valley became epicenter of 1525 revolt
- Initially hedged: assembled 2,000 soldiers but negotiated with rebels "as a Christian brother"
- After Swabian League crushed rebels to south, swung into action: burned villages,
  slaughtered women and children, set up punitive tribunals, seized all looted Church property
- Most savage of all German princes: systematically chopped off fingers of "collaborators,"
  gouged eyes of 58 burghers in Kitzingen "who refused to look at him as their lord"
- His executioner submitted a BILL: "80 beheaded / 69 eyes put out or fingers cut off /
  114½ fl. … [Signed] Augustin, the executioner, who the Kitzingers call 'Master Ouch.'"
- Brother Georg asked Casimir if he was "intending to take up a trade" since he couldn't
  be a feudal overlord if his peasants were all dead
- Graeber: Casimir's psychology — "frantic urgency of having to convert everything around
  oneself into money, and rage and indignation at having been reduced to the sort of person
  who would do so" — embodies something essential about the psychology of debt

### Medieval Village Credit (Craig Muldrew's research)
- Most American gold/silver never reached ordinary farmers — stayed with aristocracy,
  London merchants, or royal treasury; small change almost nonexistent
- Villages ran entirely on credit: shopkeepers issued own token money (lead, leather, wood)
- Typical village: ONLY strangers and "riff-raff" paid cash — people so down on their luck
  no one would extend credit to them
- Everyone simultaneously lender AND debtor; income = promises from other families
- Bi-annual "general reckoning": communities canceled debts against each other in
  great circle; only remainders settled with coin
- "Credit" = reputation for honesty/integrity; financial and moral terms completely merged:
  "worthies," "man of no account," "giving credit to someone's words"
- Marriage "flesh debts": either partner could demand sex; "paying one's debts" acquired
  sexual connotation. Chaucer pun: "taille" (tally) and "tail" in Shipman's Tale

### Criminalization of Debt — Hobbes and Self-Interest
- Margaret Sharples case c. 1660: took cloth from a shop, agreed price, left pawn as security.
  Shopkeeper had second thoughts, returned pawn, commenced legal proceedings. She was hanged.
- As interest-bearing loans became common (1580s), creditors insisted on legal bonds →
  explosion of debt litigation; fear of debtor's prison hung over everyone
- Mr. Coward (kindly shopkeeper): used his credit to help less fortunate → got drawn into
  declining persons' debts → "kept to his house for some time and died of grief and shame"
- Debtor's prisons like Fleet and Marshalsea: aristocrats had manicurists and prostitutes;
  commoners "covered with filth and vermin, suffered to die of hunger and jail fever"
- "The criminalization of debt was the criminalization of the very basis of human society"
- Adam Smith's famous line about butcher/brewer/baker was LITERALLY NOT TRUE when written —
  most English shopkeepers still operated on credit. Smith was painting a utopian picture
  of how he WANTED commerce to work, not describing reality
- "Self-interest" = first appears in English around Hobbes' time, directly borrowed from
  "interesse" (Roman law term for interest payments on a loan)
- Hobbes merely substituted scientific language for Luther's biblical references: same argument
- "Self-interest" originated as euphemism for St. Augustine's "self-love" — the curse of
  the Fall, our insatiable desire for self-gratification. Money that never ceases to grow
  → the same assumption of infinite desires in finite world

### Story of Origins of Capitalism (Graeber's thesis, Ch 11 synthesis)
- NOT: "gradual destruction of traditional communities by impersonal market"
- IS: "story of how an economy of CREDIT was converted into an economy of INTEREST;
  gradual transformation of moral networks by the intrusion of impersonal — and often
  vindictive — power of the state"
- The real origin of "modern banking": Venice 12th c. — compulsory loans on taxpayers
  with 5% interest, bonds made negotiable → market in government debt
- Holland financed independence from Habsburgs (1568-1648) through forced loans + bond issues
- When credit unlatched from real trust between individuals → money could be produced
  by saying it was there → inevitably led to scams → guardians periodically sought to
  latch value back to gold and silver

## Ch 11 (cont.) — Age of Great Capitalist Empires (pages 391-410)

### Bank of England and the Origins of Paper Money
- Bank of England 1694: consortium of 40 London/Edinburgh merchants (already creditors to crown)
  offered King William III £1.2 million loan to fund war against France → in return received
  monopoly on issuing banknotes (promissory notes for the king's debt to them)
- "Paper money was debt money, and debt money was war money"
- Key paradox: financial apparatus of capitalism (central banks, bond markets, stock markets,
  speculative bubbles, securitization) ALL appeared BEFORE factories and wage labor
  → "can the dreams of the system create its body?"

### John Locke vs. Chartalists on Money
- 1690s coin crisis: British silver coins worth more than face value → proper coins vanished
- Proposed solution: recall and restrike at 20-25% lower weight (Chartalist position:
  silver has no intrinsic value; money is state measure)
- John Locke WON the argument: gold/silver have intrinsic value recognized worldwide;
  government stamp merely attests to weight/purity; debasement = treason same as clipping
- Result of following Locke: DISASTER — almost no coinage in circulation, prices/wages collapsed,
  hunger and unrest; only the wealthy were insulated (they had access to banknotes/credit money)
- Britain adopted gold standard in 1717 and maintained it through end of Empire
- Newton (Warden of the Mint) was Locke's advisor for this decision

### Speculative Bubbles — South Sea Bubble
- South Sea Company bought up most of the British national debt; shares skyrocketed
  then collapsed in 1720; described as "too big to fail"
- Charles MacKay: listed 86 schemes, including "A company for the carrying on of an
  undertaking of great advantage, but nobody to know what it is" — man opened office in
  Cornhill, sold 1,000 shares at £2 deposit each, then "set off that same evening for the
  Continent. He was never heard of again."
- Population of London convinced not that money could be made from nothing, but that
  OTHER PEOPLE were foolish enough to believe it — so they actually could make money

### Capitalism's "Secret Scandal" — Never Organized Around Free Labor
- World market system began with search for spices; settled into 3 broad trades:
  arms trade, slave trade, and drug trade (coffee, tea, tobacco, distilled liquor, opium)
- Putumayo scandal 1909-1911: agents of British rubber company in Peru created
  "Heart of Darkness" — exterminated tens of thousands of Huitoto Indians
  ("cannibals") through torture, mutilation, rape
- Parliamentary inquiry: the agents (not the Indians) were the ones deeply in debt to
  the Peruvian company. Indians had no interest in cloth/machetes → agents forced
  loans on them AT GUNPOINT, then tabulated rubber they "owed"
  → Indians massacred for "defaulting" by running away
- "It is the secret scandal of capitalism that at no point has it been organized
  primarily around free labor"
- Americas: mass enslavement → debt peonage → African slavery → indentured service
  (in 1600s, almost as many white debtors as African slaves on southern plantations)
- Russian/Polish peasants were FREE landholders in Middle Ages; became serfs at DAWN
  of capitalism when their lords began selling grain on world market
- Bengal: East India Company institutionalized debt peonage as primary means of production
- Chinese contract laborers built North American railroad; Indian "coolies" built South
  African mines; African contract laborers after slavery replaced by more contract laborers

### Adam Smith's Vision as Utopia (Not Description)
- The picture of "worker who punches clock at 8am and receives regular wages" was a
  UTOPIAN VISION, only gradually put into effect in England/N. America, never the
  main way of organizing production "ever, anywhere"
- Smith imagined away consumer credit, debt, and violence — created a model of a world
  almost entirely free of debt, and therefore, free of guilt and sin
- "The problem with such models… is that, once created, we have a tendency to treat them
  as objective realities, or even fall down before them and start worshipping them as gods"
- Marx was "well aware that there were far more bootblacks, prostitutes, butlers, soldiers,
  pedlars, chimneysweeps, flower girls, street musicians, convicts, nannies, and cab drivers
  in the London of his day than there were factory workers" — his analysis was "as if"
- Period 1825-1975: determined effort to turn Smith's vision into reality — coins/paper money
  finally available in quantities allowing daily life without credit; wages paid on time
- Graeber personal note: "my brother died at the age of 53, having refused to his dying day
  to acquire a credit card" — for working-class families, a wallet of banknotes = freedom

### "Truck System" and Workplace Pilfering
- Factory owners paid workers with tickets/vouchers good only at company stores → employees
  trapped in debt to boss through the shopkeeper
- Rich vocabulary for appropriations from waste/excess: cabbage (cloth), chips (boards <2 feet),
  thrums (warping-bars of looms), buggings, gleanings, potchings, vails, poake, coltage, knockdowns
- "Workplace pilfering" criminalized ~1800 by Samuel Bentham (reforming dockyards):
  workers met this with outraged resistance → whipping and imprisonment
  → Bentham conceived need for tower of constant surveillance → his brother Jeremy
  borrowed the idea for the famous Panopticon
- Royal Admiralty was regularly over a year behind in paying wages of Deptford dockworkers
  → workers tolerated appropriation of "chips" as compensation

## Ch 11 (end) — Capitalism and Apocalypse (pages 411-417)

### Capitalism's Structural Need for Apocalypse
- "We could no more really have a universal world market than we could have a system in
  which everyone who wasn't a capitalist was somehow able to become a respectable, regularly
  paid wage laborer with access to adequate dental care. A world like that has never existed
  and never could exist. The moment that even the prospect of this begins to materialize,
  the whole system starts to come apart."
- South Sea Bubble, tulip mania — the most sober, responsible capitalist regimes generated
  the most bizarre speculative frenzies: "presented with the prospect of its own eternity,
  capitalism simply explodes"
- Most great theorists of capitalism (Marx, Weber, Schumpeter, von Mises) believed capitalism
  would not last more than a generation or two
- Victorian capitalists in Chicago relocated their mansions along the road to the nearest
  military base, convinced the revolution was imminent
- Once fear of social revolution ended (post-WW2) → nuclear holocaust appeared; then → global warming
- Graeber: "it does seem strange that capitalism feels the constant need to imagine, or to
  actually manufacture, the means of its own imminent extinction"
- 2008 crisis: the period leading up to it was precisely when people began to believe
  capitalism would be around forever — result was reckless bubbles that crashed everything

### Cortés and Moctezuma — Gambling for the Universe
- Cortés won much of the Aztec treasure by gambling with the captive Moctezuma,
  playing totoloque (Aztec dice game); he cheated blatantly; Moctezuma just laughed
- Aztec ball game: tiny stone hoops; normal play was accumulation of points, but sending
  the ball through the hoop (near impossible) = instant total victory, ownership of all
  wagered goods, AND right to pillage spectators' clothing
- Historian Inga Clenninden: Moctezuma wasn't gambling for gold (trivial); he was waiting
  for the die to land on its edge — the freak cosmic stroke that would mean total victory
  (or defeat) and explain the extraordinary events around him (strange creatures, epidemics)
- "If Clenninden is right, for Moctezuma, he and Cortés were not simply gambling for gold.
  Gold was trivial. The stakes were the entire universe."
- Cortés continued to cheat; the gods sent no revelation; "the universe was eventually destroyed"
- Graeber: capitalism enshrines the gambler as essential — and uniquely incapable of
  conceiving its own eternity

## Chapter 12 — The Beginning of Something Yet to Be Determined (1971–Present)

### Nixon Shock and the Dollar
- Aug 15, 1971: Nixon announced foreign-held U.S. dollars no longer convertible to gold →
  ended gold standard; initiated free-floating currencies continuing to this day
- Result: gold price skyrocketed to $600/oz by 1980 → massive wealth transfer from poor
  countries (no gold reserves) to rich ones (U.S., Britain); dollar depreciated; persistent inflation
- Federal Reserve gold vault: 33 Liberty Street, Manhattan, 80 feet below street level,
  50 feet below sea level — contains gold for 100+ central banks; "gold stackers" with
  hydraulic lifts shift bars between numbered compartments to settle accounts
- Post-9/11, many New Yorkers' first question was: what happened to the gold?
- Graeber: "Reality has become so odd that it's hard to guess which elements of grand mythic
  fantasies are really fantasy, and which are true"

### Dollar as Reserve Currency — Debt Imperialism
- U.S. currency not issued by the government: issued by private banks under Federal Reserve,
  a public-private hybrid. All dollar bills are "Federal Reserve Notes." Fed pays mint 4 cents
  per bill. Banks then create virtual money at fractional reserve.
- Henry Ford: "If ordinary Americans ever found out how the banking system really worked,
  there would be a revolution tomorrow"
- U.S. T-bonds have replaced gold as world's reserve currency — economist Michael Hudson
  (early 1970s): these bonds "will not have to be repaid, but are to be rolled over indefinitely.
  This feature is the essence of America's free financial ride, a tax imposed at the entire globe's expense"
- Combined effect of low interest + inflation: bonds depreciate → "tribute" (Graeber's term)
- U.S. has 800+ overseas military bases; military spends more than all other nations combined;
  if not for military spending, U.S. would not run a deficit at all
- Saddam Hussein switched oil payments from dollar to euro in 2000; Iran followed in 2001 →
  American bombing and military occupation. "All imperial arrangements do, ultimately, rest on terror."
- Key buyers of U.S. treasury bonds: West Germany (300,000 U.S. troops), Japan, Taiwan,
  South Korea — all U.S. military protectorates. Saudi Arabia + Kuwait keep oil priced in
  dollars — also U.S. military protectorates.
- IMF: "first genuinely global administrative system in human history" — enforces creditor rights,
  NOT debtor protection. Created under U.S. patronage.
- Argentina defaulted 2002 — "and got away with it"
- 2008 crisis: U.S. financial industry ended up with trillions in liabilities it couldn't pay →
  world economy standstill. China became major holder of U.S. treasuries, altering dynamic.

## Chapter 12 (cont.) — 1971-Present (pages 430-450)

### China and the Tribute System
- China has maintained since Han dynasty a tribute system where it showers client states
  with gifts FAR greater than what it receives — to make them "complacent and unwarlike"
- Zheng He expeditions (1405-1433): 7 voyages, treasure fleets carrying silks, porcelain
  as GIFTS to rulers who would recognize Chinese emperor's authority (opposite of Spanish
  treasure fleets which extracted)
- U.S. Cold War policy effectively replicated this: favorable trade terms for Korea, Japan,
  Taiwan, SE Asia — traditional Chinese tributaries — to contain China
- Now: China buying U.S. treasury bonds may be first stage of "reducing the United States
  to something like a traditional Chinese client state"

### Keynesian Era and the Post-War Settlement
- Post-WW2 deal: white working class of N. Atlantic offered unions, social benefits, pensions,
  health care, education — in exchange for setting aside fantasies of changing the system
- Key guarantee: increases in worker productivity would be met by increases in wages (held
  until late 1970s) → rapidly rising productivity AND incomes → consumer economy
- But offer was limited to small slice of world's population; 1945-1975 movements (MLK,
  national liberation in Algeria/Chile, feminism) were demands for inclusion
- "It would appear that capitalism, as a system, simply cannot extend such a deal to everyone"
- Martin Luther King "I Have a Dream" = framed as CHECK CASHING on the Constitution's
  promissory note: "America has given the Negro people a bad check, a check which has
  come back marked 'insufficient funds.'"
- Late 1970s: breakdown of existing order — financial chaos, food riots, oil shock, ecological crisis
  = ways of putting populace on notice that "all deals were off"

### Reagan-Thatcher and the New Settlement
- 1978-2009: new deal. Reagan/Thatcher declared all previous deals off.
  Everyone could have political rights, but these would be economically meaningless.
  Productivity-wages link was severed.
- New offer: rather than euthanize rentiers, everyone could BECOME a rentier (401k, home ownership)
- 1980: U.S. federal usury laws eliminated by Congress (had previously capped interest at 7-10%)
  → payday loans at 6,000% APR became legal. "Problem of loan-sharking was brushed aside
  by making real interest rates of 25%, 50%, even 6,000% annually perfectly legal"
- "Just as the United States had managed to largely get rid of the problem of political corruption
  by making the bribery of legislators effectively legal (redefined as 'lobbying')..."
- "Debt is the new fat" (Margaret Atwood): debt TV shows as religious revival — confession,
  repentance, penance (cut up credit cards), absolution, new dawn
- Double theology: for creditors = supply-side "creation ex nihilo," divine gift; for debtors = sin
- George Gilder's Wealth and Poverty (1981): investors create value from nothing just as God
  creates ex nihilo — "Chance is the foundation of change and the vessel of the divine"
  Pat Robertson: supply-side = "the first truly divine theory of money-creation"

### Microcredit and the Grameen Bank
- "Credit is a human right" — Grameen Bank model: convert social capital of poor into
  growing capital; double-edged
- NGO consultant in Cairo 1995: "Why help them get into debt? Do I really care what they
  use the money for, as long as they pay the loan back?" (borrowers can be imprisoned for default)
- Microcredit in South Asia eventually resembled subprime mortgage crisis: unscrupulous lenders,
  deceptive appraisals, lenders sending goons to seize corrugated tin roofs → epidemic of
  suicides by poor farmers in debt traps from which their families could never escape
- 2008 bailout: when the moment of truth arrived, government chose to treat financiers' imaginary
  money as real, while mortgage holders were left to courts under newly tightened bankruptcy laws

### Graeber's Conclusion — Toward the Next Age
- "The last thirty years have seen the construction of a vast bureaucratic apparatus for the
  creation and maintenance of hopelessness, a giant machine designed, first and foremost,
  to destroy any sense of possible alternative futures"
- Key historical thesis: "The real history of markets is nothing like what we're taught to think it is.
  Earlier markets were spillovers of administrative systems, operated primarily on credit.
  Cash markets arose through war."
- Freedom transformed through Roman institution of slavery: from "ability to make friends,
  to enter into moral relations with others" into "incoherent dreams of absolute power"
- "Who was the first man to look at a house full of objects and immediately assess them only
  in terms of what he could get for them in the market? Surely, he can only have been a thief.
  Burglars, marauding soldiers, then perhaps debt collectors, were the first to see the world this way"
- "Any system that reduces the world to numbers can only be held in place by weapons"
- "It can only operate by continually converting love into debt"
- Middle Ages correctly recognized: when dealing with the Absolute, there can be no such thing as debt
- Two cycles of popular movements: (1) 1945-1978 = demanding rights of national citizenship;
  (2) 1978-2008 = access to capitalism itself
- Middle East parallel: 1st cycle inspired by Marxism; 2nd cycle by radical Islam
  → Iraq has twice before produced great moral/financial innovations: c. 3000 BC (interest-bearing debt)
    and c. 800 AD (first sophisticated commercial system to explicitly REJECT it)
  → Is a third innovation due?
- Market populism: idea that markets can exist outside states; emerged first in Muslim Indian Ocean
  then China's silver revolts; requires market relations to be founded ultimately in honor/trust/
  mutual aid — not sheer calculation
- Graeber's final call: "To begin to free ourselves, the first thing we need to do is to see ourselves
  again as historical actors, as people who can make a difference in the course of world events"

## Conclusion & Afterword (pages 451-463)

### Conclusion — "Perhaps the World Really Does Owe You a Living"
- Von Mises (1930s): "public opinion has always been biased against creditors" because it
  mistakenly thinks creditors = rich and debtors = poor; in modern age of bonds/mortgages/
  savings/pensions, masses of moderate income are actually creditors and rich are principal debtors
- Graeber's critique of Ferguson's Ascent of Money: poverty caused by lack of credit is a
  "democratization of finance" argument. Implies only "industrious poor" deserve help —
  what about the non-industrious poor? "They can go to hell, presumably"
- Graeber: "Cancel all student loan debt? But that would be unfair to all those people who
  struggled for years to pay back their student loans! — this argument makes about as much
  sense as saying it would be 'unfair' to a mugging victim not to mug their neighbors too"
- Final proposal: "Biblical-style Jubilee" canceling both international AND consumer debt
  → not just to relieve suffering but to remind us that "money is not ineffable, that paying
  one's debts is not the essence of morality, that all these things are human arrangements"
- Athens and Rome paradigm: confronted with continual debt crises but never challenged the
  PRINCIPLE of debt — instead legislated around edges, used spoils of empire as subsidies
- "The principle has been exposed as a flagrant lie. We don't 'all' have to pay our debts.
  Only some of us do."
- Final definition: "A debt is just the perversion of a promise. It is a promise corrupted
  by both math and violence."
- "Freedom (real freedom) is the ability to make friends" — and therefore the ability to
  make real promises. Debt corrupts this.
- "No one has the right to tell us what we truly owe"
- Ending plea: "put in a good word for the non-industrious poor. At least they aren't hurting
  anyone. Insofar as the time they are taking off from work is being spent with friends and
  family, enjoying and caring for those they love, they're probably improving the world more
  than we acknowledge. Maybe we should think of them as pioneers of a new economic order."

### Afterword (2014)
- Graeber's aim: influence public debate on matters that really mattered, not just academia
- Marcel Mauss as major inspiration: first to recognize all societies are jumble of contradictory
  principles; combined insights of ancient history with contemporary ethnography; wrote The Gift
  to put to rest the myth of barter — but had virtually no effect on how economics was taught
- Graeber's success: Bank of England issued statement on "The Role of Money in the Modern
  Economy" using IOU story — not the barter myth — "after a century's work, we may well have
  finally managed to do it!"
- Graeber helped plan the Occupy Wall Street occupation of Zuccotti Park (June 2011); largest
  contingent that showed up were "debt refugees"; assemblies around debt were far and away
  the most enthusiastic and attended
- Joined Strike Debt! working group; helped formulate Debt Resistors' Operations Manual and
  Rolling Jubilee
- Adam Smith's theory of moral sentiments (often overlooked): people seek wealth because
  others care more about the well-to-do; but Smith believed people would NOT continue amassing
  wealth beyond "comfortable prominence" — further accumulation was "pointless, even pathological"
- Plutarch anecdote (via Smith): King of Epirus explains all his planned conquests;
  when he finishes: "And what does your Majesty propose to do then?" — King: "Enjoy myself
  with my friends over a bottle." — Favourite: "And what hinders your Majesty from doing so now?"
- Sahlins version: Missionary tells Samoan he should dry copra → sell it → buy machines →
  expand → hire workers → retire → lie on beach all day. Samoan: "...And why would I want to do that?"
  [at which point the Samoan is already doing what the missionary's entire program was designed
  to achieve — lying on the beach]
- Graeber's closing hope: "children born today might live to see the day when there is no longer
  an 'economy'" — when we examine these matters in completely different terms
