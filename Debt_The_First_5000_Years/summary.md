# Debt: The First 5,000 Years
**David Graeber** | Melville House, 2011 (Updated & Expanded 2014) | 534 pp.

---

## Core Argument

The conventional story of money — that barter came first, then coins,
then credit — is a myth invented by economists who never looked at
actual anthropological or historical evidence. In reality, credit came
first, then coins, and barter was mostly a phenomenon of breakdown
between strangers or enemies, never the basis of ordinary economic life.

Debt is not a neutral financial instrument. It is a moral relationship
embedded in power. And the history of civilization is largely the history
of struggles between creditors and debtors — struggles that have produced
slavery, war, empire, religion, and philosophy.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **The Myth of Barter**: No society has ever been found that operated
   primarily through barter. Adam Smith invented this story to explain
   the origin of money, but all historical and anthropological evidence
   contradicts it. Credit systems preceded coinage by thousands of years.

2. **Virtual Money → Bullion → Virtual Money cycles**: Graeber identifies
   5 epochs: Ancient Credit Economies → Axial Age Bullion (600 BC–600 AD)
   → Middle Ages Virtual Credit → Age of Capitalist Empires Bullion → 
   Modern Virtual Money (1971–present). We are now entering a new phase.

3. **The Military-Coinage-Slavery Complex**: Coinage, philosophy, standing
   armies, and mass slavery all appeared simultaneously across Greece,
   India, and China in the Axial Age (600–300 BC) — not coincidentally.
   Coins are instruments of war; soldiers need portable wealth; coinage
   requires enslaved miners.

4. **Debt as Violence**: The precise quantification of debt — what makes
   it different from an obligation or a favor — requires violence or the
   credible threat of it. Nietzsche was right that debt and punishment
   are linked, but wrong about which came first. Debt didn't create
   morality; it perverted it.

5. **Three Moral Frameworks**: Graeber identifies three competing logics
   in all human economies: (1) Communism — from each according to
   ability, to each according to need, the baseline of all human
   relationships; (2) Exchange — strict equivalence, "tit for tat"; and
   (3) Hierarchy — asymmetric obligation maintained through custom.
   Modern capitalism presents exchange as the only valid logic and
   converts the other two into it.

6. **Slavery as Social Death**: Slavery is defined not by labor but by
   the removal of honor — being placed outside the normal web of social
   obligations and debts that constitute personhood. This is why slaves
   can be treated as currency: like coins, they have been stripped of
   history and context.

7. **Capitalism's Secret Scandal**: Capitalism has never been primarily
   organized around free wage labor. From mass enslavement to debt peonage
   to indentured servitude to contract labor, coerced labor has always
   been the rule and free labor the exception. The "free market utopia"
   was always a vision, never a description.

8. **Debt Imperialism**: Since Nixon's 1971 floating of the dollar,
   U.S. T-bonds have replaced gold as the world's reserve currency.
   Foreign nations — particularly those under U.S. military protection —
   must hold dollars, effectively paying tribute that will never be repaid.
   The U.S. national debt is a promise the world knows will not be kept.

9. **The Jubilee**: Great agrarian empires from Sumer to Babylon
   periodically canceled all debts (the "clean slate" or andurarum),
   because allowing debts to compound indefinitely created social
   instability. The Hebrew Jubilee and the Christian Lord's Prayer
   ("forgive us our debts") preserve memories of this institution.
   Graeber's closing proposal is a modern Biblical Jubilee.

10. **A Debt Is a Perverted Promise**: "A debt is just the perversion
    of a promise. It is a promise corrupted by both math and violence."
    Real freedom is the ability to make real promises — and no one has
    the right to tell us what we truly owe.

## Thematic Breakdown

### Origins of Money
- Credit, not barter, preceded coinage. The first "currencies" were
  units of account in Sumerian temple bureaucracies (~3000 BC), used
  to track obligations. Coins appeared ~600 BC in Lydia, simultaneously
  with standing armies. The connection is not coincidental.
- "Primordial debt" theory (state-socialist version): we are all born
  indebted to the cosmos/society — Graeber demolishes this as a
  rationalization for permanent state domination.

### The Axial Age
- ~600–300 BC: coinage, philosophy, and the great religions appeared
  simultaneously in Greece, India, and China. Graeber argues this was
  driven by the Military-Coinage-Slavery Complex: armies needed portable
  pay → coins minted from war booty → taxes demanded in coin → free
  farmers forced to market to get coin → debt → slavery → more mines
  for more silver.
- The philosophers (Buddha, Confucius, Socrates) were responding to
  the moral crisis created by this system: how to live virtuously in a
  world of abstract, impersonal violence?

### The Middle Ages
- Characterized by return to virtual credit money: tally sticks,
  bills of exchange, Islamic suftaja, commercial credit networks.
  Interest was still widely condemned (Islam, Christianity, Buddhism
  all forbade usury) but sophisticated workarounds developed.
- Islamic finance achieved genuine market populism: merchants could
  operate free-market networks spanning Iberia to Southeast Asia
  without state enforcement of contracts, because trust and honor
  were sufficient.
- Buddhist monastery "Inexhaustible Treasuries": offered interest-free
  loans to merchants → the contradiction Graeber identifies between
  Buddhism's anti-materialism and its financial sophistication.

### Age of Capitalist Empires
- The real origin story: Ming China's abandonment of paper money in
  the 15th century created an enormous silver shortage, which drove
  Chinese demand for silver → sustained Spanish American mines →
  supplied the global monetary system.
- Cortés' conquest of Mexico was driven by personal debt: he borrowed
  8,000 pesos, burned his boats, and found that after conquest his own
  soldiers were in debt because equipment was billed back to them.
- Protestant Reformation restructured debt morality: Luther insisted
  debtors must repay, even if creditors sinned by charging interest.
  Peasant War 1525: 100,000+ killed trying to restore "the true
  communism of the Gospels."
- English villages ran entirely on credit through the 16th–17th century;
  Adam Smith's "butcher, brewer, baker" story was not a description but
  a utopian prescription for how commerce should work.

### The Modern Age (1971–Present)
- Nixon's floating of the dollar created a system where U.S. military
  power backs the global monetary system. Countries under U.S. military
  protection must recycle trade surpluses into Treasury bonds that
  will never be repaid.
- Post-WW2 Keynesian settlement: working class got unions, pensions,
  education; productivity-wages link held through late 1970s.
- Reagan-Thatcher: broke the productivity-wages link; eliminated usury
  laws; made 6,000% APR payday loans legal; "democratized finance" by
  turning everyone into a tiny corporation.
- 2008 crisis: financiers' imaginary money was made real through bailouts;
  homeowners were left to harsher bankruptcy laws (tightened two years
  before the crash, "rather suspiciously presciently").

## Contrarian Ideas

- **Communism is the foundation of all human relationships**, not a
  utopian vision. Every time someone asks "can you pass the salt?" or
  helps carry a sofa, they operate on communistic principles. Markets
  and states are built on top of this baseline, not the other way around.

- **The market did not destroy community** — the violent intrusion of
  state-backed debt law into community credit networks destroyed community.
  English villagers saw no contradiction between mutual aid and markets
  until debt became a weapon wielded by the state on behalf of creditors.

- **The "industrious poor" deserve credit** — this framing implies the
  non-industrious poor deserve nothing. Graeber's closing words defend
  those who spend their time with friends and family rather than
  maximizing productivity: "maybe we should think of them as pioneers
  of a new economic order."

- **Freedom as originally conceived was the ability to make friends**,
  not freedom from others. The Roman institution of slavery transformed
  the Western concept of freedom into something negative: freedom from
  interference, freedom to be left alone — which is really just another
  name for isolation.

## Why This Book Matters

*Debt* arrived in 2011 just as Occupy Wall Street was forming, and Graeber
(one of its organizers) helped the movement coalesce around debt as its
central grievance. The book demolished one of the founding myths of
economics — the barter story — so thoroughly that even the Bank of
England quietly rewrote its explanation of money's origins.

More broadly, Graeber accomplished something rare: a work of rigorous
anthropological scholarship that entered popular consciousness and changed
the terms of political debate. By showing that "paying your debts" is not
the essence of morality but a historically specific, politically enforced
arrangement — one that ancient Mesopotamian kings routinely canceled — he
opened a space to imagine things differently.
