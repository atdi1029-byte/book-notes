Key Takeaways

  1. The Generational Diagonal: History is best understood by following cohort-groups along their lifecycle diagonal — the same national events hit different generations at different life phases, producing radically different peer personalities.
  2. Four Recurring Types: Every American generation since the Puritans fits one of four archetypes — Idealist (prophetic), Reactive (picaresque), Civic (heroic), Adaptive (genteel) — arriving in the same fixed sequence.
  3. Two Types of Social Moments: Great turning points are either Secular Crises (reordering the outer world) or Spiritual Awakenings (transforming the inner world), alternating roughly every 40-45 years.
  4. The Cycle Predicts Mood, Not Events: The same triggering event produces radically different national responses depending on which constellational era it falls in.
  5. The "Crisis of 2020": Written in 1991, the authors predicted America would enter a Crisis era around 2004-2025, with peak crisis circa 2020 — matching the American Revolution, Civil War, and WWII constellations.
  6. Eighteen American Generations: From the Puritans (born 1584) to the Millennials (born 1982+), each fits the four-type pattern with only one aberration (the Civil War "skipped a beat").
  7. Response Matters More Than Event: Whether Haymarket or Pearl Harbor — the magnitude of national response depends on the generational constellation, not the severity of the trigger.
  8. The Endowment Cycle: Idealists contribute Principle/Religion/Education; Reactives contribute Liberty/Pragmatism/Survival; Civics contribute Community/Technology/Affluence; Adaptives contribute Pluralism/Expertise/Social Justice.
  9. The "Tilt" Pattern: Each era’s most noticeable neglect occurs in the endowment associated with the generation passing beyond elderhood — explaining why certain values vanish then return.
  10. Boomers as Gray Champions: Aging Boomers would become stern, moralistic "Gray Champions" — visionary elder warrior-priests willing to conscript the young for righteous purpose.
  11. 13ers (Gen X) as Pragmatic Survivors: Alienated and economically squeezed, they would mature into pragmatic midlife crisis managers — like Washington, Truman, and Eisenhower before them.
  12. Millennials as Next Civic Generation: Children of protective Boomers, they would grow into team-oriented, institution-building heroes of the next crisis.
  13. The Civil War Anomaly: The cycle's single major aberration produced a "degenerate" crisis constellation that made the Civil War America's bloodiest and least conclusive conflict.
  14. Cross-Generational Conflict Is Structural: The nastiest feuds always occur between midlife Idealists and rising-adult Reactives — built into the cycle, not accidental.
  15. Generational Cycles as Rejuvenation: The cycle acts as a "balance wheel for human progress," ensuring no dimension of national endowment is pushed too far or neglected too long.
  16. Generational Length Averages 23.4 Years: Spanning 17-33 years across four life phases: Youth (0-21), Rising Adulthood (22-43), Midlife (44-65), Elderhood (66-87).
  17. Peer Personality Over Individual: A generation's "peer personality" describes collective tendencies. Even nonconformists are aware of their nonconformity against the peer norm.

Contrarian Ideas

  1. History is cyclical, not linear: Against the dominant Western belief in progress, Strauss and Howe argue history follows a repeating four-generation rhythm — the future replays the past.
  2. Generational type matters more than period: The same era produces radically different experiences for people at different life stages — "separate generations are aging in place."
  3. The 1960s were not unique: The Consciousness Revolution was the fifth in a series of periodic spiritual awakenings, structurally identical to the Puritan awakening, Great Awakening, and others.
  4. The "Greatest Generation" is structural, not moral: G.I. heroism resulted from their position in the generational constellation (Civic type during Crisis), not inherent superiority.
  5. Generational conflict is beneficial: Recurring tension between types acts as a rejuvenation mechanism, preventing any worldview from calcifying.
  6. Old age won't be what Boomers expect: Rather than G.I.-style "senior citizens," aging Boomers will become austere patriarchs trading material comfort for moral authority.
  7. Every generation produces heroes and villains: The same Idealist archetype that produced Lincoln and FDR also produced zealots who led America into its worst crises.

Author's Key Proposals

  1. Adopt the Generational Diagonal: Abandon age-bracket analysis; study each generation's full lifecycle from birth to death to reveal patterns invisible to conventional history.
  2. Recognize the Four-Type Pattern: Plan for predictable generational moods using the Idealist-Reactive-Civic-Adaptive sequence.
  3. Prepare for the Crisis of 2020: Use the cyclical framework to anticipate the next secular crisis — response depends on generational constellation.
  4. Each Generation Must Play Its Role: Boomers: principled leadership without excess. 13ers: pragmatic survival skills. Millennials: collective institutional rebuilding.
  5. Guard Against Cyclical Dangers: Greatest risk is Idealist elders pushing righteous vision too far — Boomers with weapons of Armageddon are either visionary saviors or humanity's worst nightmare.
  6. Value All Four Types: No type is superior — each contributes an essential endowment (principle, liberty, community, pluralism) that society needs in balance.

Trading & Investing Relevance

  • Crisis timing: The 2004-2025 Crisis era prediction maps onto the 2008 financial crisis and COVID crash — generational mood theory identifies windows of systemic instability.
  • Spending patterns: Civics build institutions (infrastructure), Reactives pursue quick deals (speculation), Idealists drive values-based investing (ESG).
  • Entitlement collision: Aging Boomers vs. underfunded Medicare/Social Security has direct implications for fiscal policy, bonds, and healthcare.
  • Post-crisis rebuilding: The post-2025 Outer-Driven era favors institutional rebuilding and collective projects — potentially bullish for civic-oriented sectors.
  • Long-wave correspondence: The ~80-90-year generational cycle roughly matches Kondratieff waves, suggesting structural connections between generational mood and economic super-cycles.
  • Risk appetite: Crisis eras = collective sacrifice and fiscal expansion; Inner-Driven eras = individualism and speculation. Knowing which era you're in shapes portfolio strategy.

Preface

The Disconnect from History

A 1985 DemoMemo survey found 68% of college graduates reported using history "rarely" or "never" in daily affairs — reflecting what the authors call "two-minute pragmatism" and a deep disconnection from the past. This "today fixation" has rumbled through American society: preference for consumption over savings, debt over taxes, needs of elders over children.

Core Thesis: Generational Rhythm

The book presents "history of the future" through a recurring dynamic of generational behavior. Central claim: this dynamic repeats itself and determines how and when we participate as individuals in social change. If the future replays the past, the past must anticipate the future. The authors trace America from Puritans forward along the "generational diagonal" — the lifecycle course lived by discrete birthyear groups — identifying eighteen generations through four centuries.

"Anyone who claims to possess a vision of the future must present it with due modesty, since no mortal can possibly foresee how fate may twist and turn."

The Four Types Introduced

Four recurring "peer personalities" follow each other in fixed order:

  • G.I. / Civic (born 1901-1924): believers in public harmony, cooperative social discipline, collegial identity — left colossal imprint on institutions
  • Silent / Adaptive (born 1925-1942): other-directed, refined taste for process and expertise, nonjudgmental fairness
  • Boomer / Idealist (born 1943-1960): unique vision, transcendent principle, unyielding opinions
  • 13er / Reactive (born 1961-1981): experienced the "Consciousness Revolution" from a child's perspective; had to grow up fast; produced toughest leaders and most scathingly perceptive artists — but many burn out young

Cross-Generational Tensions

Much stress arises when people expect others to behave in ways their own personalities won't allow. For centuries, Idealist generations invariably come of age mounting symbolic attack against aging Civic elders — then enter midlife in bitter conflict with Reactive next-juniors. The authors' purpose: dispel the illusion of generational sameness and promote reciprocal understanding.

Key intellectual influences: Robert Wohl ("Historical generations are not born. They are made"), Julián Marías, the two Arthur Schlesingers, William McLoughlin, and Samuel Huntington. The proof of their cycle must lie in predictive possibilities — what Karl Popper called "falsifiability."

Chapter 1: People Moving Through Time

The G.I. Generation's Dominance

The 1989 inauguration celebrated the G.I. Generation's last moment of triumph — an unprecedented 30-year presidential monopoly (1961-1990). G.I.s were "confident and rational problem-solvers" across all life phases. The term "senior citizen" emerged only with G.I. presidents. Elderly poverty dropped from the highest bracket (mid-1960s) to well below children — a generational effect, not merely demographic.

"Separate generations are aging in place."

The Railroad Analogy

Lifecycle as a single track with multiple trains (generations) rolling through phase-of-life stations. "Whenever the constellation shifts up by one notch, the behavior and attitudes of each phase of life change character entirely." The constellation ages, shifting up one lifecycle notch roughly every 22 years. The authors propose looking at American life as it has actually been lived by each generation, from childhood through old age — following the "generational diagonal."

Historical Correspondence

Four great secular crises (Glorious Revolution, American Revolution, Civil War, Great Depression/WWII) all occurred at almost exactly the same constellational moment. Five great spiritual awakenings from the Puritans' "City on a Hill" to the Boomers' "Consciousness Revolution" all began at the same constellational moment. Average interval between social moments: 44 years; between same-type moments: ~90 years.

History moves continuously with alternating patterns: public action vs. private introspection, secularism vs. spiritualism, cultural suffocation vs. liberation. The cycle has been constantly turning since the 16th century, showing only one aberration (the Civil War skipped a beat).

"Each generation is a new people." — Alexis de Tocqueville

Chapter 2: Life Along the Generational Diagonal

Cohort-Group Biography

Gail Sheehy's Passages pioneered cohort-group biography: history told from the inside out. Matilda White Riley identified the "life-course fallacy" — the mistake of stitching together different age brackets alive at the same time. Cohort-group membership is involuntary, permanent, and applies to everyone. D-Day empowered young-adult G.I.s but intimidated Silent children; Woodstock brought rapture to 25-year-old Boomers but made 5-year-old 13ers feel the adult world was hysterical.

Evidence for Cohort Effects

  • Voting behavior: 1960s researchers assumed aging produces conservatism; post-1970 arrival of increasingly Democratic 65-to-80-year-olds proved the cohort explanation
  • K. Warner Schaie's Seattle study: aptitude scores correlate more with specific cohort-groups than with age brackets
  • SAT decline (1964-1980): seventeen straight years; CBO discovered a close diagonal match — scores declined for each cohort at all grades

The Troubled 1961-1964 Cohort

Generated America's lowest aptitude-test scores, highest drug/alcohol abuse, highest drunk-driving rates, most violent crime arrest rates. While rates accelerated for 15-to-24-year-olds, they were stabilizing or falling for all brackets over 35 — proving a cohort effect, not a period effect.

Vivid Cohort Examples Across Centuries

The 1633-1647 cohort: most berated colonial children, notorious pirates, half executed for witchcraft. The 1721-1723 cohort: most energetic evangelicals and antislavery activists; half of Continental Congress delegates over 30. The 1809-1821 cohort: vast majority of best-known abolitionists, feminists, commune founders. The 1943 cohort: a disproportionate share of inner-driven figures — Bob Woodward, Jim Morrison, Oliver North, Newt Gingrich.

"How children are raised affects how they later parent. How students are taught affects how they later teach. How youths come of age shapes their later exercise of leadership."

Chapter 3: Belonging to a "Generation"

Defining Generational Length

The authors reject traditional 30-year genealogical spans. Instead: generation = phase of life (~22 years). Four life phases: ELDERHOOD (66-87, stewardship), MIDLIFE (44-65, leadership), RISING ADULTHOOD (22-43, activity), YOUTH (0-21, dependence). All 17 completed generations range from 17-33 years, averaging 23.4 years.

Three Rules of Family Dynamics

  1. Parents distributed across two preceding generations (mothers/fathers typically ages 20-45)
  2. First-wave cohorts have earlier parent generation than last-wave cohorts
  3. Second-succeeding generation exercises especially strong nurturing influence — midlife parent has greater institutional influence than rising-adult parent

Peer Personality

Peer personality = caricature of prototypical member's attributes: collective attitudes about family, sex roles, institutions, politics, religion, lifestyle. A safer basis for generalization than sex, race, region, or age brackets. A generation "is born, lives, and dies" — feels historical urgency and finality. Even nonconformists are aware of their nonconformity against the peer norm.

Three-Part Test

  1. Common age location — chronology relative to historic events (Mannheim's Generationslagerung)
  2. Common beliefs and behavior — objective measures (marriage, crime, fertility, voting)
  3. Perceived membership — society recognizes who belongs

Julius Peterson's typology helps: G.I. examples include Walt Disney (directive), Cronkite (directed), Jack Kerouac (suppressed rebel). UCLA surveys tracked "developing a philosophy of life" falling from 80% to 40% among freshmen while "being very well off financially" rose commensurately.

Chapter 4: The Four-Part Cycle

Social Moments

A social moment is an era (~decade) when people perceive historic events radically altering the social environment. Two types: Secular Crises (reordering outer world) and Spiritual Awakenings (changing inner world). They arrive in intervals of roughly 40-45 years, alternating in type.

The Four Generational Types

  • IDEALIST (dominant, inner-fixated): Grows up indulged after crisis; inspires awakening; fragments into narcissistic rising adults; becomes moralistic midlifers; visionary elders guiding next crisis
  • REACTIVE (recessive): Grows up underprotected during awakening; risk-taking, alienated rising adults; pragmatic midlife leaders during crisis; reclusive elders
  • CIVIC (dominant, outer-fixated): Grows up protected after awakening; heroic rising adults overcoming crisis; sustains institutions as powerful midlifers; busy elders attacked by next awakening
  • ADAPTIVE (recessive): Grows up overprotected during crisis; conformist rising adults; indecisive midlife arbitrators during awakening; sensitive, flexible elders

Four Constellational Eras

  1. AWAKENING ERA: cultural creativity, institutions challenged
  2. INNER-DRIVEN ERA: individualism flourishes, confidence in institutions declines
  3. CRISIS ERA: collective unity against perceived peril, danger overcome
  4. OUTER-DRIVEN ERA: conformity and stability, triumphant ideals secularized

Can the Cycle Exist in a Complex World?

Response determines social moment more than the triggering event itself. Compare WWI vs. WWII: both preceded by foreign acts, but radically different responses due to generational constellation. When society is in a Crisis era (1941), generational forces cooperate for decisive action; during an Awakening era (1967), collective effort faces strong social obstacles.

"They suggest how fortunate America may have been that the world's hour of fascist peril came when it did, and not a quarter century earlier or later."

Chapter 5: The Cycle in America

The Gray Champion

Hawthorne's "Gray Champion": ancient Puritan Simon Bradstreet appears in 1689, stopping British soldiers with commanding presence. The pattern was confirmed in 1775, 1861, and each American crisis — aging, principled Idealists returning to leadership during secular crisis. Hawthorne prophesied the figure would return "whenever the descendants of the Puritans are to show the spirit of their sires."

Eighteen Generations, Five Cycles

Five cycles spanning the 1580s-1980s: Colonial, Revolutionary, Civil War, Great Power, and Millennial. Only one interruption: the Civil War Cycle lacks a Civic-type generation. Average cycle length: 89 years; the Civil War cycle only 64 years.

Five Secular Crises

  1. Armada Crisis (1580-1588): England vs. Spain → English global power
  2. Glorious Revolution (1675-1692): King Philip's War through Salem witch trials
  3. American Revolution (1773-1789): Boston Tea Party through Constitution
  4. Civil War (1857-1865): Dred Scott through Lee's surrender
  5. Great Depression-WWII (1932-1945): FDR's election through VJ-Day

Timing: roughly 85-87 years between each (99 years before Glorious Revolution).

The Civil War Anomaly

Three factors explain why the Civil War Cycle was different: (1) The crisis came early — 51 years after middle Idealist birthyear vs. 62-72 years for other cycles, meaning Transcendentals were still zealous rather than mellowed. (2) Three adult generations allowed dangerous peer instincts to prevail — elderly Compromisers unable to rise above empty process, Transcendentals split into mutually exclusive societies, younger Gilded never outgrew adventurer's lust for battle. (3) The crisis came to an untriumphant end — Union preserved, slaves emancipated, but at enormous cost with no rising Civic generation to build institutions realizing Transcendental visions.

Five Spiritual Awakenings

From the Reformation Awakening (1517) through Puritan, Great, Transcendental, and Missionary awakenings to the Boom "Consciousness Revolution" (1967-1980) — all began when an Idealist generation was coming of age and a Civic generation held elderhood. McLoughlin's definition: "periods of culture revitalization that begin in a general crisis of beliefs and values."

Chapter 6: From Puritans to Millennials

Childhood Images in Film

Films mirror generational mood about children: 1968-1970s demon-child films (Rosemary's Baby, The Exorcist, Omen, Carrie) when 13ers occupied childhood. 1950s bright Disney kids (Shaggy Dog) when Boomers were in childhood. Mid-1980s cuddly-baby films (Three Men and a Baby, Parenthood) when Millennials were arriving.

Cycle Theories of American History

Frank Klingberg: extroversion/introversion in foreign policy (~23-year oscillations). Schlesinger Sr.: public activity/private interest (~16-year periods). Samuel Huntington: "Institutions vs. Ideals" cycle — alternates between "creedal passion" and "creedal action," lining up social moments exactly as the authors do.

The authors' advantage: they examine entire lifecycles, not just political generations, paying attention to nurturing, coming-of-age, midlife, and old-age experiences. Social moments begin ~18 years after mid-cohort birthyear of the younger dominant generation (margin of error: 2.8 years).

Phase-of-Life Oscillations

  • Coming of age: Idealists most attracted to spiritual self-discovery; Civics to early independence
  • Rising adulthood: Idealists narrow sex-role distinctions; Civics widen them; Reactives most risk-prone
  • Midlife: Idealists growing pessimism; Civics growing optimism; Reactives slow down; Adaptives break free
  • Elderhood: Idealists preoccupied with moral judgment; Reactives live least comfortably; Adaptives most comfortably

Chapter 7: The Colonial Cycle

Puritan Generation (Born 1584-1614) — Idealist

Founders of "City Upon a Hill" — zealous reformers who migrated to build a godly commonwealth. John Winthrop's 1630 sermon aboard Arabella: "We shall be as a city upon a Hill." 12,000 Puritan settlers emigrated to Massachusetts during the 1630s, outnumbering all prior colonists. An educational elite with roughly one university alumnus per forty families — a ratio not matched until the late 19th century.

Youth: Indulged in the confident, post-Armada world but grew introspective and morally demanding. Saw moral chaos, became obsessed with sinfulness. Elders ridiculed them by popularizing the word "Puritan." Coming of age: The conversion experience — insisting on "faith" over "works," celebrating dependent relationship to God as "brides of Christ." Apocalyptic strain: "Religion stands on tip-toe in our land." Rising adulthood: Founded nearly everything Americans would remember: Boston, Harvard, representative assembly, their own navy. Group intolerance propelled geographic scattering — a single settlement couldn't accommodate so many infallible prophets.

Midlife: Abandoned fanciful world reform; labored for enforceable moral order at home. Enacted draconian punishments, raged over apathy of younger Cavaliers. Transition from "law of love" to "love of law." Elderhood: Entered old age knowing crisis was coming. Governor Bellingham (75) burned crown letters. Held Massachusetts governorship for 53 years — longest single-generation control in American history. Lived on average into late sixties — longest-lived pre-20th-century generation.

"Insensible to the Puritans' inner fire, the Glorious later revered them as black-clothed statues of unfeeling rectitude."

Cavalier Generation (Born 1615-1647) — Reactive

Rogues and survivors — at worst, unlettered men of crude ambition (rogues, adventurers, witches, pirates, Indian-haters). At best, their perverse defiance of moral authority gave America its first instinct for individual autonomy. A young male servant in 1640s Maryland faced: 2/3 chance of surviving the voyage, 57% of living out indenture, 29% of ever owning land, <1% of becoming a respected planter.

Youth: No one cared about their welfare. In England: orphaned, abandoned, swept into "kidnapping" (word coined at midcentury) onto disease-ridden ships. In New England: Puritan parents castigated youngsters' unsaintliness; child misbehavior declared a capital crime. Rising adulthood: Struck out alone, hustled for wealth. Smuggling, bribing, buccaneering — all viable routes. Midlife: Crisis year 1675 — King Philip's War killed more per capita than any subsequent American war. Cavalier leadership prevailed through realistic diplomacy and reckless courage. Treated younger Glorious with gentleness denied at like age. Striking number died violent deaths.

"I have no confidence in my doings, O wretched I am. What will become of this generation?" — young Michael Wigglesworth

Glorious Generation (Born 1648-1673) — Civic

Came of age during Glorious Revolution; dutiful valor won Indian wars, repelled French, overthrew Stuart tyranny. Cotton Mather wrote 450+ volumes; "laziness" the only sin — activity had to be "social," "useful," and "serve the public." Growing up in an increasingly protective world, they were programmed for achievement. Transformed colonies from savage outpost of 100,000 into wealthy "jewel" with 600,000+ population.

Earned and saved far more than Puritans or Cavaliers; rising land prices gave commanding bargaining power. Dark side: sheer energy eclipsed reflection; social discipline limited spiritual depth. Tightened gender roles and established slavery as institution. Their hubris provoked the next generation's spiritual rebellion.

"Be up and doing. Activity. Activity. This will be most likely followed and rewarded, with triumphant satisfaction." — Benjamin Colman

Enlightenment Generation (Born 1674-1700) — Adaptive

340,000 total; 34% immigrant, 17% slave. Lifecycle defined by two chasms they just missed: the Glorious Revolution (too young to fight) and the Great Awakening (too old to convert). Better mediators than confident leaders; pioneered "freedom of the press" and cultivated "due process." The least geographically mobile generation in American history: only 23% of native New Englanders died more than 16 miles from birthplace.

Included first significant number of doctors/scientists with European credentials. William Byrd II of Virginia epitomized the type: cultured, procedurally minded, uncomfortable with passionate extremes. When the Great Awakening erupted, Jonathan Edwards condemned them as "moral neuters." Samuel Dexter confessed: "ten thousand worlds would I give... to feel and experience what I believe that man does." Most decent, accommodating, and pluralist — if also most colorless — of colonial generations.

Chapter 8: The Revolutionary Cycle

The Battle of Bunker Hill

June 17, 1775 — all four generations present: Awakener elders providing vision; Liberty midlifers providing cunning; Republican rising adults providing heroic valor; Compromise children watching.

Awakening Generation (Born 1701-1723) — Idealist

Inspired the Great Awakening of the 1730s-1740s. Jonathan Edwards led the spiritual transformation: "Sinners in the Hands of an Angry God." Richard Hofstadter likened it to civil rights demonstrations, campus disturbances, and urban riots of the 1960s combined — "a psychological earthquake." Key facts: 250 new churches, 200,000 conversions. "New Side" revivalists averaged age 25; "Old Side" defenders averaged 59. In 1776, eleven of thirteen new states led by Awakeners.

Youth: Raised in secure communities, best-educated children their elders had seen. Turned from distant, busy fathers toward mothers. Many became bookish loners (Franklin, Sherman). Young Edwards kicked off to "solitary places" to reflect on "divine glory in almost everything." Rising adulthood: After the explosion of self-discovery, thirtyish Awakeners drifted into quieter self-perfection. Reversed their parents' lifecycle: values first, then worldly things. Midlife: At last turned principles toward outer world. Organized Committees of Correspondence, led Boston Tea Party. Witherspoon vowed "to prefer war with all its horrors, and even extermination, to slavery." Elderhood: Relinquished power to younger Republicans whom they trusted and loved. Franklin signed memorial for abolition of slavery at age 84.

"Few would doubt that the piety of the Awakening was the main source of the civil millennialism of the American Revolution." — Nathan Hatch

Liberty Generation (Born 1724-1741) — Reactive

The most war-ravaged colonial generation; French and Indian War casualties equivalent of "two twentieth-century world wars in one generation." Consumed more alcohol per capita than any other colonial generation. Coined both "regulator" (vigilante) and "lynch" (Colonel Charles Lynch). Included 35 of 56 Declaration signers but only one-third of Constitution signers. Many leading Patriots were smugglers (Hancock), hopeless debtors (Sears), renegade settlers (Allen).

Youth: During the 1730s-1740s, child-rearing trend was wholesale neglect. The deadliest child-only epidemic in colonial history — the "great throat distemper" (diphtheria) — killed an estimated one child in fifteen. Edwards condemned them as "evil and adulterous generation." Coming of age: Smitten by "privateering fever," thousands enlisted for the French and Indian War; nearly half ended up bilked, crippled, or captured. "They were young people with nothing to do and nowhere to go, and war took care of them." Midlife: Revolution was a practical, defensive goal — prevent "tyranny" and protect "liberties." Excelled at dirty work: privateering, requisitioning, guerrilla tactics.

Elderhood: Prudent, realistic, self-effacing. Fischer: "the generation whose unhappy fate it was to be young in an era when age was respected, and old in a time when youth took the palm." Many met sad fates: Robert Morris died penniless, Thomas Paine a lonely outcast, Daniel Boone penniless.

Emerson on Washington's portrait: "The heavy, leaden eyes turn on you, as the eyes of an ox in a pasture... as if this MAN had absorbed all the serenity of America."

Republican Generation (Born 1742-1766) — Civic

2,100,000 total; 17% immigrant, 17% slave. Majority of leaders both native-born and college-educated. The typical Republican soldier was two inches taller than the typical English redcoat — the most rapid one-generation height climb until the G.I.s. Assumed political power early: delegate share of Continental Congress rose from 7% to 75% (1774-1787). Most aggressively secular: Hamilton on why Constitution didn't mention God: "I declare, we forgot."

Youth: Midlife Awakeners wanted smart, cooperative republican citizens. Parents avoided "corrupt" English schools, sent kids to Awakener-founded academies. Republican kids showed marked preference for fathers over mothers. Coming of age: "Columbia, Columbia, to Glory Arise!" — the most exhilarating rite of passage in American history. Republicans saw themselves as tireless reasoners and builders; the Jeffersonian God was essentially Architect and Builder. Masonic symbolism celebrated the builder: compass, plumbline, carpenter's square.

"'Tis but the morning of the world for us." — Princeton student Hugh Henry Brackenridge

Compromise Generation (Born 1767-1791) — Adaptive

4,200,000 total; 10% immigrant, 15% slave. "Rocked in the Cradle of the Revolution" (Clay): Andrew Jackson (age 12) beaten and jailed by British; William Henry Harrison (age 8) watched Arnold's redcoats use his father's cattle for target practice. Passage into adulthood smooth and seamless — shunning risk, balancing, easing unobtrusively into the adult world.

The "Age of Improvement" (Webster): prosperous careers, canal-building, cotton fortunes — but behind affluence, personal unease and tension between duty and feeling. Southern Compromisers founded dozens of antislavery societies in the 1820s; hopes for gradual emancipation dashed by polarizing Transcendental rhetoric. Younger Emerson assailed "the timid, imitative, and tame" Compromiser leadership. Clay despaired how his generation might "ignobly die" with "the scorn and contempt of mankind; unpitied, unwept, and unremembered."

No American generation ever had a sadder departure — torn between fanaticism of the young and guilty memories of Republican discipline. Seventyish Buchanan, Tyler, and Crittenden resisted fruitless efforts to defer the rush to war.

Chapter 9: The Civil War Cycle

The Anomaly

The Civil War is the cycle's single major aberration. It arrived a half-beat off schedule, producing a "degenerate" crisis constellation with no Civic generation in rising adulthood. The cycle's origin: Andrew Jackson's failed 1824 Presidential bid fired fresh radicalism among coming-of-age Transcendentals. Three generations, not four, drove this cycle's drama: Transcendental midlifers (Idealist, 40-69) wrote the script; Gilded rising adults (Reactive, 19-39) signed up expecting quick adventure; Progressive children (Adaptive, 2-18) watched the ordeal pass over childhood like a dark cloud.

Transcendental Generation (Born 1792-1821) — Idealist

11,000,000 total; 20% immigrant, 13% slave. Emerson recalled his peers: "madmen, madwomen, men with beards, Dunkers, Muggletonians, Come-Outers, Groaners, Agrarians." They erupted against the cultural sterility of their father-built world, preaching feeling over reason, community over society, inner perfection over outer conformity. Whether Abolitionists, "Southrons," Mormons, or Anti-Masons — all agreed each person must act on inner truth that transcends the sensory world.

The most literarily productive American generation of the 19th century: Essays (1841), Civil Disobedience (1849), Uncle Tom's Cabin (1852), "Song of Myself" (1855). Southern "fire-eaters" like Rhett and Yancey refused to apologize for slavery. Garrison: the Constitution was "a covenant with death, an agreement with hell." Notoriously estranged from fathers; Emerson warned of "the terrible judgments of an incensed God." Greatest one-generation political hegemony (90% of governors/Congress in 1860), ending with the largest generational rout in 1868.

"If we could first know where we are, and whither we are tending, we could then better judge what to do, and how to do it." — Abraham Lincoln

Gilded Generation (Born 1822-1842) — Reactive

28% immigrant — largest since colonial times; first large Irish Catholic influx, first Chinese laborers. Of seven million men reaching combat age, ~10% died in the Civil War; per capita casualty rate equal to eight World War IIs combined. Nearly half of war dead buried in unmarked graves. Economic legacy: gross capital formation doubled as share of GNP; sustained 28 consecutive federal surpluses (1866-1893) — reducing national debt by one-third, the only generation to leave behind smaller federal debt than inherited.

Youth: "Children are commercial before they get out of their petticoats." Nearly all looked at the unmerciful indifference of self-absorbed rising adults. Coming of age: Some stayed East in new factories; others followed "Go West, Young Man." Twentyish adventurers cried "Eureka!" chasing Gold Rush mother lodes. Midlife: 1876 Philadelphia Centennial Exposition — Hall of Machines stood for bigness, noise, worldliness. Found ethos in Darwin's "natural selection," Spencer's "social statics," Peirce and James's "pragmatism." Elderhood: Young pressed for Cuba invasion; virtually all aging Gilded luminaries (Twain, Cleveland, Carnegie) urged peace.

"The only population of the kind that the world has ever seen" — Mark Twain on Gold Rush 49ers

Why the Anomaly Matters

Without a Civic rising-adult generation to channel crisis energy into constructive institution-building, the Civil War produced destruction without resolution. The lesson: the cycle's most dangerous moments occur when the constellation is "degenerate" — missing one of its four essential types in the critical life phase.

Chapter 10: The Great Power Cycle

Trinity Test — Four Generational Responses

July 15, 1945: Four generations respond to the atomic bomb:

  • Missionary elder Stimson (77): "Babies satisfactorily born" — Churchill: "This is the Second Coming, in wrath"
  • Lost midlifer Truman (61): took news impassively, ordered Hiroshima — "let the buck stop with him"
  • G.I. rising adult Oppenheimer (41): "If the radiance of a thousand suns were to burst into the sky..."
  • Silent youth Russell Baker (19): "It seems like I'm pulling some monstrous joke on myself"

Progressive Generation (Born 1843-1859) — Adaptive

Vanguard of fledgling experts and social meliorators: Westinghouse (air brake), Edison (arc light), Bell (telephone for deaf). First generation to earn graduate degrees en masse. Obsessive organizers: invented cash register, adding machine, typewriter, time zones, Bureau of Standards. Produced stirring patriotic paeans: Pledge of Allegiance, America the Beautiful, Semper Fidelis, Statue of Liberty inscription.

Lifecycle: keep-your-head-down Civil War teenagers → conformist rising adults → procedural "Progressive Era" reforms (initiatives, referenda, direct election of senators, antitrust). Theodore Roosevelt attacked "men of soft life," led "cult of strenuosity." Many experienced "midlife crises" — the term "middle age" first appeared around 1900. Louis Brandeis warned: "The greatest danger to liberty is an insidious encroachment by men of zeal."

Missionary Generation (Born 1860-1882) — Idealist

45 million total; 23% immigrant. Indulgently raised amid Gilded-era prosperity; horrified by what George Cabot Lodge called "a world of machine-guns and machine-everything-else." Pioneered settlement houses (Addams' Hull House), muckraking, suffrage marches. Feminist cause unprecedented: Isadora Duncan (emancipation by dance), Charlotte Perkins Gilman (assailing This Man-Made World), Margaret Sanger (broadcasting birth control). Whatever their causes, their mental approach remained the same: heroic desire to make the world perfect according to standards welling up from within.

Pushed through Prohibition, women's suffrage, immigration restriction. FDR after Pearl Harbor: "There has never been — there never can be — successful compromise between good and evil." Santayana: "Americans are eminently prophets. They apply morals to public affairs."

"He was the one person I ever knew, anybody, who was never afraid. God, God — how he could take it for us all." — young G.I. Lyndon Johnson on Roosevelt

Lost Generation (Born 1883-1900) — Reactive

America's first generation amid widespread narcotics use. Child labor: nearly one in five aged 10-14 worked for pay; three in five between 15 and 18 employed. From 1900 to 1920, America's homicide rate rose 700%. Lost coined: "underworld," "gangster," "mobster," "racketeer," "moll," "getaway car." At age 20, 50% more suicide-prone than any other generation. Nine million members born abroad — America's largest immigrant generation in absolute numbers. Weakest representation in national leadership posts of any generation.

Youth: In 1897, 8-year-old Virginia O'Hanlon wrote the New York Sun: "Is there a Santa Claus?" Children of the 1890s were America's most tough-minded — growing up fast amid gangs, drugs, saloons. Jacob Riis stepping into a tenement: "the hall is dark and you might stumble over children... kicks and cuffs are their daily diet." Harpo Marx: "School was all wrong. School simply didn't teach you how to be poor and live from day to day." Roosevelt's daughter Alice — smoking, swearing, sipping champagne in the White House — became symbol of children growing up "bad." Lost were America's first generation amid widespread adult-approved narcotics: cocaine in cough syrup, lozenges, and (until 1904) Coca-Cola.

Coming of age: "At 17 we were disillusioned and weary," recalled Malcolm Cowley. In 1913, magazines proclaimed "Sex O'Clock in America." Then came WWI — "the sausage machine," ultimate evidence of elders' colossal blindness. Hemingway, Dos Passos, Cowley, cummings all volunteered as ambulance drivers. Hemingway: "Abstract words such as glory, honor, courage, or hallow were obscene beside concrete names of villages." In 1919-1920, next-elders meted out the Volstead Act, Palmer Raids, and John Sumner's Society for Suppression of Vice.

Midlife: Lost blacks streaming from South (~1.5 million during 1910-1930) touched off cultural explosion: Garveyism, the Harlem Renaissance. Music named after a black idiom for sex — "jazz." Dale Carnegie and Norman Vincent Peale counseled a generation that cared more about success than self-worth. Fitzgerald: "living well is best revenge"; Great Gatsby pined after Daisy — "her voice is full of money." Dorothy Parker and Zelda Fitzgerald disappointed older suffragettes: "The Jazz Age... had no interest at all in politics." Mood darkened in 1927 with the execution of Sacco and Vanzetti. 1918 Influenza had killed about 250,000 Lost — five times the number who died in WWI combat. The Great Depression was the start of a collective midlife hangover.

"There died a myriad... / For an old bitch gone in the teeth, / For a botched civilization." — Ezra Pound

G.I. Generation (Born 1901-1924) — Civic

Lindbergh's transatlantic flight epitomized the G.I. spirit: special, sheltered, optimistic about technology. Superman perfectly captures the G.I. persona: special child, corrupt older Lex Luthor, rocklike manliness, civic mission. Won 99 Nobel Prizes (roughly two-thirds awarded to Americans). Homeownership never more affordable than early 1950s ($3,000 income, 4% mortgage, $7,000 Levittown home). Held White House for thirty years, won nine Presidential elections.

Youth: Child labor rate fell by half — largest one-generation decline ever. John Spargo's Bitter Cry of the Children augured Missionary determination to protect this new generation. Golden era of the high school: glee musicals of Mickey Rooney and Judy Garland. Coming of age: "Ours was the best generation," then Harper's described them as the "Locked-Out Generation" of the Great Depression. Roosevelt's New Deal perfectly suited twentyish mind-set: planning, optimism, collective action. CCC and NYA kept them busy — marching, never complaining, building things that lasted. Pearl Harbor galvanized "good kid" ingot into G.I. steel.

Midlife: Robert Frost at Kennedy's inaugural heralded the coming of an "Augustan Age" — "the torch has been passed to a new generation." The Great 89th Congress (74% G.I.) became the "Grandest Congress of Programs and Entitlements." Then came Vietnam — triggering angry "generation gap" with coming-of-age Boomers. Elderhood: Attacked "myths" of lonely old age; worked to be ever-optimistic "senior citizens." AARP became the largest advocacy organization in nation's history. Half of all federal spending consumed by pensions, medical entitlements, and interest on national debt.

"uncommon valor was a common virtue" — Iwo Jima shrine inscription

Silent Generation (Born 1925-1942) — Adaptive

Forever "betwixt and between" — Admiral Crowe called them a "transitional generation." First wave came of age too late for war-era heroism but in time to encounter a powerful national consensus — against which young rebels like James Dean found themselves "without a cause." 16% of Harvard's Class of '64 joined the Peace Corps (Harvard's top postgraduate destination that year); next year's graduates began criticizing it.

Looked to G.I.s for role models; pursued lifelong mission of refining and humanizing the G.I.-built world. "Other-directed" (David Riesman). Produced America's most refined process experts: MLK Jr., Armstrong, Steinem, Nader. Zero for six in Presidential campaigns since 1968. Their solutions — fairness, openness, due process, expertise — reflect a lack of surefootedness but also keen sense of how humans fall short of grand civic plans.

"During the ferment of the 1960s, a period of the famous 'generation gap,' we occupied, unobtrusively as usual, the gap itself. When nobody over thirty was to be trusted, our age was thirty-something." — Wade Greene

The sexual revolution hit the Silent when most were passing forty, decades after their natural adolescence — causing immense problems in family lives. Benita Eisler labels hers the generation with "a corpse in the trunk." Alvin Toffler foresaw "historic crisis of adaptation." America of the 1980s became Silent-style Tofflerian "ad hocracy" stressing expertise over simplicity, process over result. Today's 50- and 60-year-olds have been less successful in forging national direction than any generation in living memory.

Yet this most considerate of living generations has specialized not in grand bright institutions but in people — life-size people like the statues planned for the Korean War Memorial. Neil Armstrong and Martin Luther King Jr. will likely remain among the most celebrated Americans.

"Deep in my heart, I do believe / We shall overcome someday"

Chapter 11: The Millennial Cycle

Woodstock's Anniversary (1989)

Three generations at the 20th anniversary reveal divergent responses: Boomers (29-46) trying to keep own children from replaying their youthful exuberances; 20th-anniversary reissue of Hair stripped offensive lyrics, turning "Sodomy" into a saxophone solo. 13ers (8-28): the punkish part-timers behind the checkout counter who kept mouths shut while 40-year-olds reveled. Jeffrey Hoogeveen: "the Sixties are history — history that the rest of us don't need repeated and crammed down our throats." Millennials (7 and under): where Boomer tots hitchhiked to Woodstock, Millennial tots traveled in car seats with safety belts.

Boom Generation (Born 1943-1960) — Idealist

First cohort born with G.I. optimism rewarded in victory over fascism. Unlike Silent: no childhood recollection of WWII. Unlike 13ers: all reaching adolescence before draft ended. Mission: not constructing society but justifying and purifying it.

"We are Bomb Babies... We grew up with fallout in our milk." — Ronald Allison, 1967

The Consciousness Revolution (1967-1980): McLoughlin's "Fourth Awakening." Summer of Love, inner-city riots, Kent State. Peaked 1970 when 44% of college students believed violence justified for social change. Ended 1980 with Reagan's election. Waged across the generation gap between Boomers and G.I.s — began within families as revolt against fathers. Most memorable symbols were direct affronts against G.I. constructions: hippie invasion of Disneyland, burning of Isla Vista Bank, Earth Day burial of automobile.

Boomer Perfectionism: Desire for "the best" within personal definition of taste. If couldn't afford house, could afford the very best brand of mustard — "Zen luxuriousness." Gift for deductive logic: better philosophers than scientists, better preachers than builders. Highest-testing Boom cohorts "very late to win a Nobel Prize."

Boom Facts

  • Vietnam: Only one in sixteen ever saw combat; among the rest, two-thirds attributed avoidance to deliberate dodge. 1% committed draft-law felonies — ten times the percentage killed
  • SAT slide: Seventeen-year decline (1946-1963 cohorts) coincided with greatest grade inflation ever measured
  • Sexual revolution: Boomer women doubled rate of premarital sex over Silent (41% to 81%) and tripled propensity to adultery
  • Religion: Church attendance rose 30% during 1980s; seven in ten believe in psychic phenomena; America's fastest-growing church = Assembly of God
  • Economics: Where typical G.I. worker enjoyed 63% inflation-adjusted rise between age 30-40, first-wave Boomer suffered net 1% decline — yet 9-to-1 consider lives more meaningful than fathers'

Youth: G.I. moms applied "democratic discipline" with Dr. Spock who instructed "we need idealistic children." Permissive feeding schedule extended to older kids: "He'll-clean-up-his-room-when-he's-ready-to-have-a-clean-room." Coming of age: Free Speech Movement served first notice of the most emotionally intense youth rebellion in American history. Screaming radicals represented just 10-15% of their generation, but the righteous often prevailed in youth culture. Approaching midlife: "New Puritanism" — fortyish Boomers policing "politically correct" behavior, banning obscene music, promoting "chastity." Smoking or regular drinking becoming "new Scarlet Letters" among ex-flower children.

"A generation that when young trusted nobody over 30 now trusts nobody under 30." — Alan Kors

Thirteenth Generation / Gen X (Born 1961-1981) — Reactive

79 million total; 11% immigrant. Thirteeners, not Boomers, were America's true "children of the 1960s" and especially the 1970s. The Awakening era seemed euphoric to young adults but was a nightmare for children: self-immersed parents, disintegrating homes, culture shifting from G to R ratings. Adult message: "Grow up fast" — they graduated early to "young adult realism." At every phase of life, 13ers encountered a world of more punishing consequence than anything Silent or Boom elders ever knew.

13er Facts

  • Most aborted generation in American history: would-be mothers aborted one fetus in three
  • Divorce: 13er child in 1980s faced twice the risk of parental divorce as Boomer child in mid-1960s, three times a Silent child in 1950
  • Latchkey kids: Mothers with children under 5 in workforce rose from 20% to 47% (1960-1980)
  • Academic decline: First generation since Gilded to be less college-educated than elder siblings
  • Economic decline: Average earnings of young college grads fell 6%; for young high school dropouts, fell 42%
  • Youth homicide: ~2,000 minors murdered in 1988 — twice 1965 despite 6.5 million fewer youths
  • Most heavily incarcerated generation in American history; by 1990, one in four black men age 20-29 in jail, on probation, or on parole

Youth: The most virulently anti-child period in American history. Adults ranked autos ahead of children as necessary for "the good life." Judy Blume's It's Not the End of the World: once-happy family disintegrating amid shouting, slapping, crying. Kenneth Keniston (1977): "The parent is usually a coordinator without voice or authority." Pop culture icons embrace demeaning images: Max Headroom (beheaded, imprisoned in TV), Teenage Mutant Ninja Turtles (flushed down toilet, deformed by radiation), Bart Simpson ("underachiever"). Built a powerful survival instinct around ethos of personal determinism: what matters is how you behave, not who you are inside.

"I hate the idea that you should always protect children. They live in the same world we do." — Judy Blume

Millennial Generation (Born 1982+) — Civic

September 1988: 5- and 6-year-olds arrive at Burrville Elementary wearing brand-new green-and-yellow coats and ties. Boomer school board member Nate Bush: these kids will wear school-issued clothes through high school, "like it or not." Not since the 1910s have adults seen advantage in making kids look alike and work together.

1981: volley of books assaulting adult mistreatment of children — Children Without Childhood, The Disappearance of Childhood, Our Endangered Children. 1984: kids-as-devils movies flopped, marking end of genre. Mid-1980s: cuddly-baby movies replaced them. Tipper Gore's crusade against lyrics; Barbie's doll band changed from the "Rockers" to cleaned-up "Sensations." Grown-up radicals who once shocked their own parents now surprise themselves with strictly perfectionist approach to child nurture.

Safety equipment boom: Gerber drawer latches, stove knob covers, toilet locks, bicycle helmets. States passing laws requiring infant restraints. "Everybody's Baby" Jessica McClure (18 months, trapped in well) captivated the nation. Children treated as more precious than parents. Lowest child-to-parent ratio in American history; only 2% of kids under 18 in families with 5+ children.

NASA official Thomas Paine (1988): "the first Martians are already born and toddling around." Boomer parents setting out to produce kids who are smart, powerful, dutiful — raised not to explore inner world (Boomers figure they handle that) but to achieve and excel at the outer.

Chapter 12: The Past as Prologue

The Four Lifecycle Paradigms

The IDEALIST Lifecycle (Prophetic): Youth: indulged amid secular confidence. Rising Adulthood: spiritual awakening, inner torment, generational "founding." Midlife: moralistic, principled, judgmental. Elderhood: visionary, wise — "Gray Champions" guiding through crisis.

The REACTIVE Lifecycle (Picaresque): Youth: underprotected during awakening. Rising Adulthood: risk-taking, alienated, bad reputation. Midlife: pragmatic leaders during crisis. Elderhood: lonely, caustic, survival-focused.

The CIVIC Lifecycle (Heroic): Youth: increasingly protected. Rising Adulthood: overcome crisis heroically. Midlife: powerful institution-builders, hubristic overreach. Elderhood: busy but culturally frustrated, attacked by next awakening.

The ADAPTIVE Lifecycle (Genteel): Youth: overprotected during crisis. Rising Adulthood: conformist, emulating elders. Midlife: "passage" between outer and inner missions. Elderhood: personally flexible, culturally sensitive, trusting liberals.

The Endowment Cycle

Each generational type has its own vision of the American Dream. National holidays reflect peer personality: Thanksgiving (Idealist Puritans), July Fourth (Reactive Liberty), Memorial Day (Civic G.I.s), MLK Birthday (Adaptive Silent).

The "Tilt" Pattern

"In each era, the most noticeable endowment neglect or reversal occurs in the endowment activity associated with the generation currently passing beyond elderhood."

Mid-1930s: Where were Progressive trust-busters? Too old. 1950s: Culturally sterile "end of ideology" could have used Idealists — but Missionaries were disappearing. Late 1960s-70s: euphoria needed skeptical Reactives — but most Lost had passed.

Each generation develops a lifelong endowment agenda aimed at correcting what society neglected during its youth. The cycle ensures no dimension of progress is pushed too long or lies dormant too long — making it a powerful force for rejuvenation and guarantor of the American Dream.

Social Indicators and the Cycle

Crime rates follow cyclical patterns: rise steeply during Awakening eras, reach cyclical highs during Inner-Driven eras (documented since the 1730s). Substance abuse: rises during Awakening, peaks near its end, falls when Idealists enter midlife. Young Idealists get most euphoria while society looks on indulgently; young Reactives get most blame when crackdown begins. Fertility: tends to rise during Outer-Driven eras; eases during Awakening. Popular music reflects each era's mood: Missionaries' spirituals, Lost jazz, G.I. big-band swing, Silent folk and rock, Boomer acid/Jesus rock, 13er punk.

"As is the generation of leaves, so it is with the generations of men, which alternately come forth and pass away." — Homer

Chapter 13: Completing the Millennial Cycle

Three Scenarios: How America Responds to Crisis

The authors pose a hypothetical: How would America respond to a terrorist attack on New York City? The answer depends entirely on when — during which constellational era:

During Awakening (~1970): America would be "at war with itself more than with the perpetrators." Angry Boomers would blame the government; Silent leaders would be puzzled why foreigners hate America.

During Inner-Driven (~1990s): Caution, conciliation, deferral. Silent cabinet officers would form committees. The nation would "squeeze by undamaged, but leave underlying causes unsolved."

During Crisis (~2020): "Unrecognizable" from today. Boomer leaders would broadly define the threat, demand total defeat. Cooperative Millennial youths would seek orders. The nation would act "promptly and decisively as a single organism."

"History tells us one thing for certain about social behavior: It never remains the same for long. The realistic question is not whether it will change, but how and when."

Boomers Entering Elderhood

Authors predict old Boomers as next embodiment of Hawthorne's "Gray Champions" — combining "the leader and the saint." Not busy "senior citizens" but meditative patriarchs. Looking old will become fashionable. Many will relocate to university communities as "part professor and part pupil."

Healthcare collision: by 2025, younger workers would need to hand over 30-40% of payroll for G.I.-style public support. "History says it is not a war they will wage" — Boomers will trade material comfort for moral authority, recasting Social Security and Medicare. More than 7/10 Boomers don't think Social Security will help them when old — for inner-fixated Boomers, this requires an "adjustment to karma." Not pie-baking grandma but a steel-willed Grand Mother or Grand Father — an ascetic elder glowering down from Sinai.

"Make no mistake: Faced with crisis, this generation of onetime draft resisters will not hesitate, as elder warrior-priests, to conscript young soldiers to fight and die for righteous purpose."
"It is easy to picture aging Boomers as noble, self-sacrificing patriarchs — but just as easy to see these righteous Old Aquarians as the worst nightmare that could ever happen to the world."

13ers in Midlife Crisis

The "sneaky fortune wheel" for a Reactive generation: just when you think you've lost, it tantalizes. Historical pattern: nastiest feuds between midlife Idealists and rising Reactives (Puritans/Cavaliers, Missionaries/Lost, Boomers/13ers). 13ers will be first generation since the Gilded to reach age 40 with lower standard of living than their parents. Never before have public-benefit "safety nets" been tilted so heavily toward retired elders and away from rising adults.

But matured into pragmatic crisis managers who "dart from job to job" and seek "lightning strike at success." Controlling the Boom may emerge as the 13ers' most fateful lifecycle mission — best able to deflect Boomer drift toward apocalyptic visions. A 13er may someday be the general or Presidential adviser who prevents some righteous old Aquarian from "loosing the fateful lightning." Whatever goes wrong in the crisis, 13ers will get more than their share of the blame; whatever goes right, less than their share of the credit.

Millennials as Rising-Adult Heroes

Predicted to come of age during the crisis, channeling collective energy into heroic action — like G.I.s during WWII. Two possible futures: (1) crisis unfolds badly and they become "a smart but hobbled generation," or (2) crisis allows coalescence into genuine Civic type with teamlike spirit and attraction to global ideologies promising material utopias through collective action.

Boomers, having screamed against duty when young, will now demand duty and discipline from post-adolescents. In midlife, Millennials will construct a circa-2030 national mood reminiscent of post-WWII "American High." Scientists design grand projects glorifying the thinker-doer-builder. "Right-stuff" Millennials command manned space flights to nearest planets.

Timing of the Crisis

A cycle is roughly eighty-eight years. Plotting half cycle ahead from Boom Awakening: crisis lasting 2013-2024. Projecting from FDR landslide and Pearl Harbor anniversaries: crisis lasting 2020-2029. At crisis peak: surviving Silent in mid-eighties, Boomers in elderhood, 13ers in midlife, Millennials in rising adulthood. The 2020s will be a turning point in American history and an adrenaline-filled moment of trial. Three of four antecedents ended in triumph; the fourth (Civil War) in moral fatigue.

Risk of early crisis: If crisis comes around 2000-2009, Boomers will be squarely in midlife at peak institutional power — exactly where Transcendentals were when John Brown was planning Harper's Ferry. Boomers can best serve civilization by restraining themselves until twilight years.

Chapter 14: The Beginning of History

The Adams Family: A Generational Drama

Charles Francis Adams: U.S. ambassador to England during the Civil War, published 10-volume biography of grandfather (a President), compiled 12-volume diary of his father (also a President), then saw his son write a biography of him. Grandfather John born 1735; grandson Charles Francis III died 1954 — combined lifespans extending 220 years. Through cross-generational relationships, we communicate across eras of mind-bending length.

The Biblical and Classical Parallels

The Exodus narrative contains four distinct peer personalities matching the cycle:

  1. Aaron/Moses (Idealist): awakened people to the spirit of God, led through wilderness
  2. "Stiff-necked" backsliders (Reactive): golden calf worshipers, "men of little faith"
  3. Joshua's soldiers (Civic): born after Exodus, conquered Canaan through heroic campaigns
  4. Original Judges (Adaptive): invisible children overshadowed by war, political fragmentation in midlife

Homer's Iliad reveals another four-type cycle: Nestor (wise/Idealist), Agamemnon (cunning/Reactive), Odysseus (hubristic/Civic), Telemachus (deferential/Adaptive).

The Cycle's Promise and Warning

The central lesson: each generational type makes its own unique contribution to human progress. Surviving G.I.s: civic example. Elder Silent: other-directed kindness. Midlife Boomers: vision and values. Rising-adult 13ers: pragmatism and defense of individual liberty. Young Millennials: new civic energy and devotion to community.

Less is riding on them than once rode on Franklin and Washington, Lincoln and Grant, or Roosevelt and Eisenhower. Future generations must rely on aging Boomers to build an ethic of community responsibility and principled self-sacrifice. Future generations are counting on 13ers to mellow into guardians of family life and protectors against Idealist excess.

The endowment cycle ensures no dimension of progress lies dormant too long. But the cycle also contains great danger: when Idealist elder zealotry meets weapons of mass destruction, the outcome could be catastrophic. The cycle is a powerful force for rejuvenation — if all generations play their roles well.

"The past is prologue. Let's turn to the future."
