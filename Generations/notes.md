## Preface

### The Disconnect from History

- College graduates list history as the academic subject of **least use** in daily affairs — reflects "two-minute pragmatism" and disconnection from the past
- **"Today fixation"** has rumbled through American society: preference for consumption over savings, debt over taxes, needs of elders over children

### Core Thesis: Generational Rhythm

- Book presents "history of the future" through a **recurring dynamic of generational behavior**
- Central claim: this dynamic **repeats itself** and **determines how and when we participate as individuals in social change**
- If the future replays the past, the past must anticipate the future
- Traces full story of America from Puritans forward along the **"generational diagonal"** — the lifecycle course lived by discrete birthyear groups ("generations")
- Identifies **eighteen generations** through four centuries of American history

### Four Generational Types

Four recurring types of **"peer personalities"** follow each other in fixed order:

- **"G.I."** elders (Civic), born 1901-1924: believers in public harmony and cooperative social discipline; collegial identity left colossal imprint on institutions; see others as ineffectual facsimiles
- **"SILENT"** midlifers (Adaptive), born 1925-1942: other-directed, appreciate mind-sets of those before/behind; highly refined taste for process and expertise; done more than any since **Louis Brandeis** to bring nonjudgmental fairness to American society
- **"BOOMER"** rising adults (Idealist), born 1943-1960: perceive within their circle a unique vision, transcendent principle (like **Winthrop**, **Emerson**); unyielding opinions; generation-as-hypocrite theme no more valid than **Thoreau** at Walden
- **"13ER"** youths (Reactive), born 1961-1981: experienced "Consciousness Revolution" from child's perspective; had to grow up fast amid parental self-immersion; produced toughest leaders, most effective warriors, most scathingly perceptive artists — but many burn out young

### Cross-Generational Tensions

- Much stress arises when people expect others to behave in ways their own personalities won't allow
- For centuries, **"Idealist" generations** invariably come of age mounting symbolic attack against aging **"Civic" elders** — then enter midlife in bitter conflict with **"Reactive" next-juniors**
- Purpose: **dispel the illusion of generational sameness** and promote reciprocal understanding

### The Cycle's Predictive Power

- If authors discovered a cycle, proof must lie in **predictive possibilities** — what **Karl Popper** called "falsifiability"
- Cannot predict specific events; can predict underlying rhythms determining which events are most likely
- During 2010s and early 2020s, American generations will pass deep into **"Crisis Era"** constellation — nation's public life will undergo swift, possibly revolutionary transformation
- **"Crisis of 2020"**: cannot predict specific emergencies, but cycle predicts the constellational mood

> "Anyone who claims to possess a vision of the future must present it with due modesty, since no mortal can possibly foresee how fate may twist and turn."

### Acknowledgments

Key influences: **Robert Wohl** ("Historical generations are not born. They are made"); **Julián Marías** (disciple of **José Ortega y Gasset**); two **Arthur Schlesingers** (oscillating political mood coincides with American progress); **William McLoughlin** (temporal parallels between periodic "awakenings"); **Samuel Huntington** (relationships between awakenings and secular crises)

## Chapter 1: People Moving Through Time

### The G.I. Generation's Dominance

- **1989 Presidential Inauguration**: Bush's parade celebrated G.I. Generation's last moment of triumph
- **30-year presidential monopoly (1961-1990)**: unprecedented single-generation hold on the White House
- G.I.s were "confident and rational problem-solvers" across all life phases
- Term **"senior citizen"** emerged only with the first G.I. presidents
- **Elderly poverty rate** dropped from highest bracket (mid-1960s) to well below children — reflects generational aging, not demographic trends alone

> "Separate generations are aging in place."

### The Four Living Generations and Railroad Analogy

- **Railroad analogy**: Lifecycle as single track with multiple trains (generations) rolling through phase-of-life stations
- **"Whenever the constellation shifts up by one notch, the behavior and attitudes of each phase of life change character entirely."**
- Constellation always aging, shifting up one lifecycle notch roughly every 22 years

### The Generational Diagonal

- **"In this book, we suggest looking at the American lifecycle as it has actually been lived by each generation, from childhood through old age."**
- **"Age location"**: phase-of-life experiences separating youth from adulthood
- Each age location produces a **"peer personality"** — set of collective behavioral traits expressed throughout generation's lifecycle

> "How children are raised affects how they later parent. How students are taught affects how they later teach. How youths come of age shapes their later exercise of leadership."

### Push and Pull Between Generations

- History moves continuously with alternating patterns: public action vs. private introspection, secularism vs. spiritualism, cultural suffocation vs. liberation
- **Startling pattern**: A recurring cycle of four distinct types of peer personalities, arriving in the same repeating sequence
- From 16th century forward, cycle has been constantly turning, showing only one aberration (Civil War skipped a beat)

### Historical Correspondence with Generational Cycles

- **Four great secular crises** (Glorious Revolution, American Revolution, Civil War, Great Depression/WWII) — all occurred at almost exactly the same constellational moment
- **Five great spiritual awakenings** from the Puritans' "City on a Hill" to the Boomers' "Consciousness Revolution" — all began at the same constellational moment
- Average interval between social moments: **44 years**; between same-type moments: **~90 years**

### America as Generational Study

- **Alexis de Tocqueville** (1830s): "each generation is a new people" — America uniquely susceptible to generational flux
- Western Europe shared generational parallels: romantic radicals (1840s), *Blut und Eisen* realists (1860s), *génération du feu* (WWI), *nouvelle résistance* (1968)

## Chapter 2: Life Along the Generational Diagonal

### The Cohort-Group Biography

- **Gail Sheehy's** *Passages* (1976) pioneered **cohort-group biography**: history told from the inside out
- **Sociologist Matthew White Riley** calls the "life-course fallacy": mistake of stitching together all different age brackets alive at the same time
- **Cohort-group membership is involuntary, permanent, applies to everyone** — all members encounter same national events at similar ages

### Age Location Shapes Collective Personality

- **D-Day** empowered young-adult G.I.s but intimidated Silent children; **Woodstock** brought rapture to 25-year-old Boomers, made 5-year-old 13ers feel the adult world was hysterical
- Social institutions reinforce cohort identity: school classrooms, college, reunions, retirement

### Quantitative Research on Cohorts

- **Voting behavior**: 1960s researchers assumed aging produces conservatism; two decades later, post-1970 arrival of increasingly Democratic 65-to-80-year-olds proved the cohort explanation
- **K. Warner Schaie's** Seattle study: aptitude scores correlate more strongly with specific cohort-groups than with age brackets
- **SAT decline (1964-1980)**: seventeen straight years; CBO researchers discovered a close **diagonal match** — scores declined for each cohort at all grades

### Portrait of the 1961-1964 Cohort-Group

- Generated America's lowest aptitude-test scores, highest drug/alcohol abuse, highest drunk-driving rates, most violent crime arrest rates
- While rates accelerated for 15-to-24-year-olds, they were stabilizing or falling for all age brackets over 35 — proving this was a **cohort effect**, not a period effect

### From Cohorts to Generations

Vivid cohort-group examples across centuries:
- **1633-1647**: most berated colonial children, notorious pirates, half executed for witchcraft
- **1721-1723**: most energetic evangelicals and antislavery activists of 18th century; half of Continental Congress delegates over 30
- **1809-1821**: vast majority of best-known abolitionists, feminists, commune founders; two-thirds of Congress at Civil War outbreak
- **1943**: vastly disproportionate number of inner-driven figures: Bob Woodward, Jim Morrison, Oliver North, Newt Gingrich

## Chapter 3: Belonging to a "Generation"

### The Length of a Generation

- Authors reject traditional 30-year genealogical spans; instead: generation = **phase of life** (~22 years)
- **Four life phases**: ELDERHOOD (66-87, stewardship), MIDLIFE (44-65, leadership), RISING ADULTHOOD (22-43, activity), YOUTH (0-21, dependence)
- All 17 completed generations range from 17-33 years, average **23.4 years**

### Three Rules of Family Generational Dynamics

1. **Parents distributed across two preceding generations** (mothers/fathers typically ages 20-45)
2. **First-wave cohorts have earlier parent generation** than last-wave cohorts
3. **Second-succeeding generation exercises especially strong nurturing influence** — midlife parent has greater institutional influence than rising-adult parent

### Peer Personality

**Peer personality** = caricature of prototypical member's attributes: collective attitudes about family, sex roles, institutions, politics, religion, lifestyle

- Safer basis for generalization than sex, race, region, or age brackets
- A generation "is born, lives, and dies" — feels historical urgency and finality
- Even nonconformists are **aware** of their nonconformity against the peer norm

### Three-Part Test for Peer Personality

1. **Common age location** — chronology relative to historic events (**Karl Mannheim's** *Generationslagerung*)
2. **Common beliefs and behavior** — objective measures (marriage, crime, fertility, voting, attitudes)
3. **Perceived membership** — society recognizes who belongs and who doesn't

### "Directive," "Directed," and "Suppressed" Personalities

- **Julius Peterson** typology: G.I. examples — **Walt Disney** (directive), **Cronkite** (directed), **Jack Kerouac** (suppressed rebel)
- UCLA survey: "developing a philosophy of life" fell from 80% to 40% among freshmen; "being very well off financially" rose commensurately

## Chapter 4: The Four-Part Cycle

### Social Moments Define and Shape Generations

- **Social moment** = era (~decade) when people perceive historic events radically altering social environment
- Two types: **SECULAR CRISES** (reordering outer world) and **SPIRITUAL AWAKENINGS** (changing inner world)
- Social moments normally arrive in intervals of roughly **40-45 years**, alternating in type

### Four Generational Types

**1. IDEALIST** (dominant, inner-fixated): Grows up indulged after crisis → inspires awakening → fragments into narcissistic rising adults → cultivates principle as moralistic midlifers → becomes visionary elders guiding next crisis

**2. REACTIVE** (recessive): Grows up underprotected during awakening → matures into risk-taking, alienated rising adults → mellows into pragmatic midlife leaders during crisis → maintains respect as reclusive elders

**3. CIVIC** (dominant, outer-fixated): Grows up increasingly protected after awakening → overcomes crisis as heroic rising adults → sustains institutions as powerful midlifers → becomes busy elders attacked by next awakening

**4. ADAPTIVE** (recessive): Grows up overprotected during crisis → matures into conformist rising adults → produces indecisive midlife arbitrators during awakening → maintains influence as sensitive elders

### Four Constellational Eras

1. **AWAKENING ERA**: cultural creativity, institutions challenged
2. **INNER-DRIVEN ERA**: individualism flourishes, confidence in institutions declines
3. **CRISIS ERA**: collective unity against perceived peril, danger overcome
4. **OUTER-DRIVEN ERA**: conformity and stability, triumphant ideals secularized

### Can the Cycle Exist in a Complicated World?

- **Response determines social moment** more than the triggering event itself
- Compare WWI vs. WWII: both preceded by foreign acts, but radically different responses due to generational constellation
- When society is in Crisis era (1941), generational forces cooperate for decisive action; during Awakening era (1967), collective effort faces strong social obstacles

> "They suggest how fortunate America may have been that the world's hour of fascist peril came when it did, and not a quarter century earlier or later."

## Chapter 5: The Cycle in America

### The Gray Champion Archetype

- **Hawthorne's** "Gray Champion": ancient Puritan **Simon Bradstreet** appears in 1689, stopping British soldiers with commanding presence
- Pattern confirmed in 1775, 1861, and each American crisis — **aging, principled generation returning to leadership during secular crisis**
- These elders had been young idealists during prior awakening, now face "darkness, adversity, and peril" in old age

### Eighteen American Generations

- Five cycles spanning 1580s-1980s: Colonial, Revolutionary, Civil War, Great Power, Millennial
- **Only one interruption**: Civil War Cycle lacks a Civic-type generation
- Average cycle length: **89 years**; Civil War cycle only 64 years

### The Five Secular Crises

1. **Armada Crisis (1580-1588)**: England vs. Spain → English global power
2. **Glorious Revolution (1675-1692)**: King Philip's War through Salem witch trials
3. **American Revolution (1773-1789)**: Boston Tea Party through Constitution
4. **Civil War (1857-1865)**: Dred Scott through Lee's surrender
5. **Great Depression-WWII (1932-1945)**: FDR's election through VJ-Day

**Timing**: roughly **85-87 years** between each (99 years before Glorious Revolution)

### The Civil War Anomaly

**Three factors explaining why the Civil War Cycle was different:**

**1. The crisis came early** — 51 years after middle Idealist birthyear vs. 62-72 years for other cycles. This 10-15 year difference meant Transcendentals were still zealous rather than mellowed; Gilded were less mature as leaders.

**2. Three adult generations allowed dangerous peer instincts to prevail** — elderly Compromisers unable to rise above empty process; Transcendentals split into mutually exclusive societies waging war with apocalyptic finality; younger Gilded never outgrew adventurer's lust for battle. Together produced **nearest approximation of holocaust** America ever experienced.

**3. The crisis came to an untriumphant end** — Union preserved, slaves emancipated, but enormous cost: sectional hatred, impoverishment of South, collapse of Reconstruction into Jim Crow. No rising Civic generation emerged to build institutions realizing Transcendental visions.

### The Five Spiritual Awakenings

1. **Reformation Awakening (1517-1539)**: Martin Luther, Calvin, Knox
2. **Puritan Awakening (1621-1640)**: Winthrop, Cotton, Hutchinson
3. **Great Awakening (1734-1743)**: Edwards, Whitefield, Tennent
4. **Transcendental Awakening (1822-1837)**: Emerson, Nat Turner, Finney
5. **Missionary Awakening (1886-1903)**: Bryan, Addams, DuBois
6. **Boom Awakening (1967-1980)**: "Consciousness Revolution"

**McLoughlin's** definition: "periods of culture revitalization that begin in a general crisis of beliefs and values"

**Key distinction**: Crisis empowers rising-adult Civics with collective confidence; Awakening endows rising-adult Idealists with perpetual ideological mission

## Chapter 6: From Puritans to Millennials and Beyond

### Childhood Images in Film

- **1968-1970s**: demon-child films (*Rosemary's Baby, The Exorcist, Omen, Carrie*) — **13ers** occupied childhood bracket
- **1950s**: bright Disney kids (*Shaggy Dog*) — **Boomers** in childhood
- **Mid-1980s**: cuddly-baby films (*Three Men and a Baby, Parenthood*) — **Millennials** arriving

### Cycle Theories of American History

- **Frank Klingberg**: extroversion/introversion in foreign policy (~23-year oscillations)
- **Arthur Schlesinger Sr.**: public activity/private interest (~16-year periods)
- **Schlesinger Jr.**: predicted 1960s-style liberalism resurgence — authors doubt this (cycle too fast)
- **Samuel Huntington**: "Institutions vs. Ideals" cycle — lines up social moments exactly as authors do; alternates between "creedal passion" and "creedal action"

**Authors' advantage**: examine **entire lifecycles**, not just political generations; pay attention to nurturing, coming-of-age, midlife, and old-age experiences

### Phase-of-Life Oscillations by Generational Type

- **Coming of age**: Idealists most attracted to spiritual self-discovery; Civics to early independence
- **Rising adulthood**: Idealists narrow sex-role distinctions; Civics widen them; Reactives most risk-prone
- **Midlife**: Idealists growing pessimism; Civics growing optimism; Reactives slow down; Adaptives break free
- **Elderhood**: Idealists preoccupied with moral judgment; Reactives live least comfortably; Adaptives most comfortably

### The Predictive Record

- Social moments begin ~18 years after mid-cohort birthyear of younger dominant generation (margin of error: 2.8 years)
- Social moments begin ~64 years after mid-cohort birthyear of elder dominant generation (margin of error: 7.7 years)
- Every social moment has begun shortly after birthyear of recessive-type generation

## PURITAN GENERATION (Born 1584-1614) — Idealist

### Founding Vision

- **John Winthrop's** sermon aboard *Arabella* (1630): "We shall be as a city upon a Hill"
- 12,000 Puritan settlers emigrated to Massachusetts during 1630s — outnumbered all prior colonists
- English-born generation of **boundless spiritual ambition**: forsook Elizabethan fathers, founded holy community

### Key Facts

- Educational elite: roughly one university alumnus per forty families — not matched until late 19th century
- In 1642, Roundheads outnumbered Royalists 2:1 among fiftyish M.P.s; opposite ratio among twentyish M.P.s
- Women assumed conspicuous activism: **Anne Hutchinson** nearly toppled Winthrop; **Anne Bradstreet** most celebrated pre-Transcendental authoress
- Held Massachusetts governorship for **53 years** — longest single-generation control in American history
- Lived on average into **late sixties** — longest-lived pre-20th-century generation

### The Puritan Lifecycle

**YOUTH**: Indulged in confident, optimistic post-Armada world — but grew introspective and morally demanding. Saw moral chaos, became obsessed with sinfulness. Elders ridiculed them by popularizing the word "Puritan."

**COMING OF AGE**: The **conversion experience** — moment when God "ravished" them. Insisting on **"faith" over "works"**, celebrated dependent relationship to God as "brides of Christ." By 1620s, apocalyptic strain: "Religion stands on tip-toe in our land, / Ready to pass to the American strand." Thus began the Great Migration.

**RISING ADULTHOOD**: Founded nearly everything Americans would remember: Boston, Harvard, representative assembly, their own navy. Created entirely **God-focused civilization**. Group intolerance propelled geographic scattering — a single settlement couldn't accommodate so many infallible prophets.

**MIDLIFE**: Abandoned fanciful world reform; labored for **enforceable moral order at home**. Enacted draconian punishments, raged over apathy of younger Cavaliers. Transition from "law of love" to "love of law."

**ELDERHOOD**: Entered old age knowing crisis was coming. Handful still presided in office during 1689 Glorious Revolution. Set unyielding example: **Governor Bellingham** (75) burned crown letters; **John Eliot** (72) protested seeing life's work torn apart. Faced death with "ponderous optimism."

> "Insensible to the Puritans' inner fire, the Glorious later revered them as black-clothed statues of unfeeling rectitude."

## CAVALIER GENERATION (Born 1615-1647) — Reactive

### Character

- Followed Puritans the way skepticism follows belief, egotism follows community, devils follow saints
- At worst: unlettered generation of crude ambition — rogues, adventurers, witches, pirates, Indian-haters
- At best: **perverse defiance of moral authority** gave America its **first instinct for individual autonomy** and "rights" of property and liberty

### Key Facts

- Young male servant in Maryland 1640s: 2/3 chance of surviving voyage, 57% of living out indenture, 29% of ever owning land, <1% of becoming respected planter
- **Largest one-generation decline in educational achievement**: 60% of Chesapeake immigrants couldn't sign their names
- Striking number **died violent deaths**: Mary Dyer (hanged), Bacon's Rebellion leaders (hanged), King Philip (shot), Salem witches (hanged), Captain Kidd (hanged)
- Four — **John Washington, Thomas Jefferson, James Maddison, Andrew Munro** — were great-grandfathers to four of first five U.S. Presidents

### The Cavalier Lifecycle

**YOUTH**: No one cared about their welfare. In England: orphaned, abandoned, swept into "kidnapping" (word coined at midcentury) onto disease-ridden ships. In New England: Puritan parents castigated youngsters' unsaintliness; child misbehavior declared capital crime.

> "I have no confidence in my doings, O wretched I am," wrote young **Michael Wigglesworth**. "What will become of this generation?"

**COMING OF AGE**: Puritans spoke routinely of "corrupt and degenerate rising generation." Accused of "cruelty" and "covetousness," of living "by external considerations only."

**RISING ADULTHOOD**: Struck out alone, hustled for wealth. Land became obsession — **smuggling, bribing, buccaneering** all viable routes to the top. Everyone told the Cavalier he was a loser anyway.

**MIDLIFE**: Crisis year 1675 — **King Philip's War** killed more per capita than any subsequent American war; **Bacon's Rebellion** expanded into civil war. Cavalier leadership prevailed through realistic diplomacy, cunning generalship, reckless courage. Treated younger Glorious with gentleness denied at like age.

**ELDERHOOD**: Marveled that worst was over, attributed success to **dumb luck**. Most died before 45; rest entered old age without wealth or pretense. Never stopped blaming themselves for not measuring up to elders.

---

## GLORIOUS GENERATION (Born 1648-1673) — Civic

- Came of age during Glorious Revolution; dutiful valor won Indian wars, repelled French, overthrew Stuart tyranny
- **Cotton Mather** wrote 450+ volumes; "laziness" the only sin — activity had to be "social," "useful," "serve the public"
- Growing up in increasingly protective world, came of age at moment of triumph, programmed for achievement

> "Be up and doing. Activity. Activity. This will be most likely followed and rewarded, with triumphant satisfaction." — **Benjamin Colman**

- Transformed colonies from savage outpost of 100,000 into wealthy "jewel" with 600,000+ population
- Earned and saved far more than Puritans or Cavaliers; rising land prices gave commanding bargaining power
- **Dark side**: sheer energy eclipsed reflection; social discipline limited spiritual depth
- **"King" Carter**: "Pray God, send the next generation that it may flourish under a set of better polished patriots"

---

## ENLIGHTENMENT GENERATION (Born 1674-1700) — Adaptive

- 340,000 total; 34% immigrant, 17% slave
- Came of age eager to be "better-polished generation" Glorious hoped for
- **Lifecycle defined by two chasms they just missed**: Glorious Revolution (too young to fight) and Great Awakening (too old to convert)
- Better **mediators** than confident leaders; pioneered "freedom of the press," cultivated "due process"
- **Least geographically mobile** generation in American history: only 23% of native New Englanders died more than 16 miles from birthplace
- Included first significant number of doctors/scientists with European credentials; gained more London Royal Society memberships than any other colonial generation
- **Youth**: grew up amid rebellion and war; split universe into dark side (Indians, armies) and safe side (family, smothering Cavalier and Glorious protection)
- **Rising adulthood**: "The Golden Age succeeds the Iron" — era of peace, prosperity, rococo interiors, but guilty obsession over denied catharsis. Became what **Jack Greene** labeled "the new political professionals"
- **Midlife**: Awakening shock — condemned by **Jonathan Edwards** as "moral neuters," sank into guilt; **Samuel Dexter** confessed: "ten thousand worlds would I give... to feel and experience what I believe that man does"
- **Generational whipsaw**: defended civic muscle to elders, then defended moral purity to juniors
- Most decent, accommodating, and pluralist — if also most colorless — of colonial generations

## Chapter 8: The Revolutionary Cycle

### Battle of Bunker Hill (June 17, 1775)

All four generations present: **Awakener** elders providing vision; **Liberty** midlifers providing cunning; **Republican** rising adults providing heroic valor; **Compromise** children watching.

### AWAKENING GENERATION (Born 1701-1723) — Idealist

**The Great Awakening experience**: Coming-of-age catharsis — truth, euphoria, millennial vision, but also hysteria and shattered families. **Richard Hofstadter** likened it to civil rights demonstrations, campus disturbances, and urban riots of 1960s combined — "a psychological earthquake."

**Key facts**: 250 new churches, 200,000 conversions (1730-1745). "New Side" revivalists averaged age 25; "Old Side" defenders averaged 59. Share of church members joining before age 30 rose from one-fifth to two-thirds. In 1776, eleven of thirteen new states led by Awakeners.

**Youth**: Raised in secure communities, best-educated children their elders had seen. Turned from **distant, busy fathers** toward **mothers**. Many became bookish loners (Franklin, Bland, Sherman). Young Edwards kicked off at "solitary places" to reflect on "divine glory in almost everything."

**Rising adulthood**: After explosion of self-discovery, thirtyish Awakeners drifted into quieter self-perfection. Made **poor citizens**: avoided voting, shunned worldly pursuits. Reversed their parents' lifecycle: **values first**, then worldly things.

**Midlife**: At last turned principles toward outer world. In 1760s, expressed horror over America's moral decadence. Organized **Committees of Correspondence**, led Boston Massacre (Attucks) and Boston Tea Party (Samuel Adams). **Witherspoon** vowed "to prefer war with all its horrors, and even extermination, to slavery."

**Elderhood**: Dominated initial revolutionary movement. In Continental Congress, enacted blue laws for "true religion and good morals." Relinquished power to make room for younger Republicans whom they trusted and loved. Franklin signed memorial for abolition of slavery at age 84; suggested Moses parting waters for national seal.

> "Few would doubt that the piety of the Awakening was the main source of the civil millennialism of the American Revolution." — **Nathan Hatch**

### LIBERTY GENERATION (Born 1724-1741) — Reactive

- **Most war-ravaged** colonial generation; French and Indian War casualties equivalent of "two twentieth-century world wars in one generation"
- Consumed **more alcohol per capita** than any other colonial generation; led more violent mobs (1760-1775) than all prior generations combined
- Coined both **"regulator"** (vigilante) and **"lynch"** (Colonel Charles Lynch)
- Included 35 of 56 Declaration signers but only one-third of Constitution signers
- Many leading Patriots were **smugglers** (Hancock), **hopeless debtors** (Sears), **renegade settlers** (Allen)

**Youth**: During 1730s-1740s, child-rearing trend was **wholesale neglect**. Deadliest child-only epidemic in colonial history — **"great throat distemper"** (diphtheria) — killed estimated one child in fifteen. **Edwards** condemned them as "evil and adulterous generation." Kids quickly learned immersed adults at best ignored them, at worst reviled their streetwise realism.

**Coming of age in war**: Colonies fought French and Indian War primarily with Liberty muscle and guts. Smitten by "privateering fever," thousands enlisted; nearly half ended up bilked, crippled, or captured. Royal Navy impressment gangs dragged away young workers; 500-lash whippings enforced compliance. **"They were young people with nothing to do and nowhere to go,"** writes **Pencak**, "and war took care of them."

**Rising adulthood**: From 1750 on, spectacular jump in every measure of social pathology. Many leading Patriots were smugglers, debtors, renegades. **Franklin** called them "white savages"; **Samuel Adams'** remarks "were never favorable to the rising generation." **Boone** declared his secret of happiness: "a good wife, a good gun, and a good horse."

**Midlife**: Revolution was practical, defensive goal — prevent "tyranny" and protect "liberties." Excelled at dirty work: privateering, requisitioning, guerrilla tactics (**"this damned old fox" Francis Marion**). Provided leading opponents to Constitution in 1788 — then relented, not about to stand in way of juniors' energy.

**Elderhood**: Prudent, realistic, self-effacing. **Fischer**: "the generation whose unhappy fate it was to be young in an era when age was respected, and old in a time when youth took the palm." Many met sad fates: **Robert Morris** died penniless, **Thomas Paine** a lonely outcast, **Daniel Boone** penniless. Old **John Adams**: "mausoleums, statues, monuments will never be erected to me."

> **Emerson** on Washington's portrait: "The heavy, leaden eyes turn on you, as the eyes of an ox in a pasture... as if this MAN had absorbed all the serenity of America."

### REPUBLICAN GENERATION (Born 1742-1766) — Civic

- 2,100,000 total; 17% immigrant, 17% slave
- Majority of leaders both **native-born and college-educated** (contrast with helter-skelter Liberty)
- Typical Republican soldier **two inches taller** than typical English redcoat — most rapid one-generation height climb until the G.I.s
- Assumed political power early: delegate share of Continental Congress rose from 7% to 75% (1774-1787)
- **47-year tenure** of national leadership: unmatched by any other generation
- Most **aggressively secular**: most Presidential candidates widely regarded as atheists; Hamilton on why Constitution didn't mention God: **"I declare, we forgot."**

**Youth**: Midlife Awakeners wanted to ensure these kids would be smart, cooperative **republican citizens**. Towns tightened up on begging, gambling, theaters. Parents avoided "corrupt" English schools, sent kids to Awakener-founded academies with practical, optimistic curriculum. Republican kids showed **marked preference for fathers over mothers**: **Marshall** confessed "my father was a greater man than any of his sons."

**Coming of age**: **"Columbia, Columbia, to Glory Arise!"** — most exhilarating rite of passage in American history. Twentyish soldiers confidently followed orders at Bunker Hill, knowing they had won glory in eyes of posterity.

> "'Tis but the morning of the world for us." — Princeton student **Hugh Henry Brackenridge**

- Republicans saw themselves as **tireless reasoners and builders**; Jeffersonian God was essentially **Architect and Builder**, not Puritan Sovereign or Transcendentalist Essence
- **Masonic symbolism** dominated the compass, plumbline, carpenter's square — celebrated the builder; to be "on the square" praised directness and utility

### COMPROMISE GENERATION (Born 1767-1791) — Adaptive

- 4,200,000 total; 10% immigrant, 15% slave
- **"Rocked in the Cradle of the Revolution"** (Clay): **Andrew Jackson** (age 12) beaten and jailed by British; **William Henry Harrison** (age 8) watched Arnold's redcoats use his father's cattle for target practice
- Passage into adulthood smooth and seamless — shunning risk, balancing, easing unobtrusively into adult world
- **"Age of Improvement"** (**Webster**): prosperous careers, canal-building, cotton fortunes — but behind affluence, personal unease
- Felt ambivalence never bothering their parents: tension between duty and feeling, proper social roles and subversive emotions
- Midlife: tried to overcome youthful caution; Jackson, Adams, Van Buren reflect fragmenting "post-heroic" personality
- Southern Compromisers founded dozens of antislavery societies in 1820s; hopes for gradual emancipation dashed by polarizing Transcendental rhetoric — all disbanded by late 1830s
- Younger **Emerson** assailed "the timid, imitative, and tame" Compromiser leadership
- **Clay** despaired how his generation might "ignobly die" with "the scorn and contempt of mankind; unpitied, unwept, and unremembered"
- Seventyish **Buchanan**, **Tyler**, **Crittenden** resisted fruitless efforts to defer rush to war
- **No American generation ever had a sadder departure** — torn between fanaticism of the young and guilty memories of Republican discipline

## Chapter 9: The Civil War Cycle

### Bull Run (July 1861)

- Cycle's origin: failed Presidential bid of **Andrew Jackson** (1824) fired fresh mood of radicalism among coming-of-age Transcendentals
- Extended through Civil War to inglorious end with conformism of "Gilded Age" (1870s-1880s)
- **This cycle is no aberration** — demonstrates how events can turn out badly

Three generations, not four, driving this cycle's drama:

1. **Transcendental** midlifers (Idealist, 40-69): wrote the script; "Fire Eaters" and "Black Republicans" fully prepared to shed younger blood
2. **Gilded** rising adults (Reactive, 19-39): signed up expecting quick adventure; scrappy but later turned bitterly cynical
3. **Progressive** children (Adaptive, 2-18): ordeal passed over childhood like dark cloud; would not see daylight until Lincoln's funeral train

### TRANSCENDENTAL GENERATION (Born 1792-1821) — Idealist

- 11,000,000 total; 20% immigrant, 13% slave
- **Emerson** recalled peers: "madmen, madwomen, men with beards, Dunkers, Muggletonians, Come-Outers, Groaners, Agrarians"
- Erupted against cultural sterility of father-built world: preached feeling over reason, community over society, inner perfection over outer conformity
- Emerson in 1820 charged that father-built world could not produce "not a book, not a speech, a conversation, or a thought worth noticing"
- Whether Abolitionists, "Southrons," Mormons, or Anti-Masons — agreed each person must act on **inner truth that transcends sensory world** — immortalized by Emerson in 1842 as "Transcendentalism"
- Southern "fire-eaters" like **Rhett** and **Yancey** refused to apologize for slavery, found virtue in aggressive empire of chivalry
- **Garrison**: Constitution was "a covenant with death, an agreement with hell"
- Greatest one-generation political hegemony (90% of governors/Congress in 1860), ending with largest generational rout in 1868 — steepest-ever decline in one generation's share of leadership
- Notoriously estranged from fathers: **Emerson** warned of "the terrible judgments of an incensed God"; **Thoreau** (whose mother brought home-cooked meals to Walden) condemned "the mouldering ruins of our ancestors"

### GILDED GENERATION (Born 1822-1842) — Reactive

> "The *only* population of the kind that the world has ever seen" — **Mark Twain** on Gold Rush 49ers

**Key facts**: 28% immigrant (largest since colonial times); first large Irish Catholic influx; first Chinese laborers. Of seven million men reaching combat age, ~10% died in Civil War; per capita casualty rate equal to **eight World War IIs combined**. Nearly half war dead buried in unmarked graves.

**Economic legacy**: gross capital formation doubled as share of GNP; sustained **28 consecutive federal surpluses** (1866-1893) — reducing national debt by one-third, the **only generation to leave behind smaller federal debt than inherited**.

**Critical consensus**: **Beard** despised "cash nexus"; **Parrington** saw "triumphant and unabashed vulgarity"; **Morison**: "When the gilt wore off, one found only base brass."

**Youth**: "Children are commercial before they get out of their petticoats" — **David MacRae**. Nearly all looked at unmerciful indifference of self-absorbed rising adults. Parents complained of toughness; 1849 NY police chief condemned "idle and vicious children... who infest our public thoroughfares."

**Coming of age**: Frenetic years: some stayed East in new factories; others followed "Go West, Young Man" (**Horace Greeley**). Twentyish adventurers cried "Eureka!" chasing Gold Rush mother lodes. North agreed with Lincoln that slavery posed threat to free men; South's "young bloods" and "sons of planters" cried for battle.

**Rising adulthood**: First Civil War years more dashing than bloody. Ultimately bulldog commanders (**Grant**) toughened reputation by throttling "Gentleman Lee." After war, South poet **Timrod** described "beggary, starvation, death, bitter grief, utter want of hope." No southerner of Timrod's generation emerged to national prominence in any sphere.

**Midlife**: 1876 Philadelphia Centennial Exposition — Hall of Machines stood for bigness, noise, worldliness. Found ethos in **Darwin's** "natural selection," **Spencer's** "social statics," **Peirce** and **James's** "pragmatism." Sustained **28 consecutive federal surpluses**; new wealth fortified up-from-nowhere elite.

**Elderhood**: Young pressed for Cuba invasion; virtually all aging Gilded luminaries (Twain, Cleveland, Carnegie) urged peace. **Progressive William Osler** wrote of "uselessness of men above sixty." Many faced involuntary, pensionless retirement. **Twain** in 1905: "I am seventy... and would nestle in the chimney corner."

> Twain's notice for *Huckleberry Finn*: "Persons attempting to find a moral in it will be banished."

### PROGRESSIVE GENERATION (Born 1843-1859) — Adaptive

- Vanguard of fledgling experts and social meliorators: **Westinghouse** (air brake), **Edison** (arc light for visually impaired), **Bell** (telephone for deaf)
- First generation to earn graduate degrees en masse; created Ph.D.-credentialed faculties
- **Obsessive organizers**: invented cash register, adding machine, typewriter, time zones, Bureau of Standards
- Produced stirring patriotic paeans: **Pledge of Allegiance**, *America the Beautiful*, *Semper Fidelis*, Statue of Liberty inscription
- Many experienced "midlife crises" — term **"middle age"** first appeared in popular periodicals around 1900

**Lifecycle**: Keep-your-head-down Civil War teenagers → conformist rising adults → procedural "Progressive Era" reforms (initiatives, referenda, direct election of senators, antitrust, regulatory commissions) → elder statesmen urging calm.

**Midlife shift**: McKinley resolved to follow "best aspirations of the people" by siding with young "jingoes." **Theodore Roosevelt** attacked "men of soft life," led "cult of strenuosity." **Kate Chopin** wrote *The Awakening* protesting "duality of outward existence which conforms, the inward life which questions." Last Progressive President **Wilson** managed WWI surrounded by diplomats, lawyers, statisticians — tried to secure "peace without victory" via League of Nations; younger voters showed little interest.

**Elderhood**: Progressives approached economic sunset with more foresight — began vast expansion in private pension plans. "Reversing age-old custom," notes **Mark Sullivan**, "elders began earnestly to act like juniors." **Louis Brandeis** warned: "The greatest danger to liberty is an insidious encroachment by men of zeal."

> "The muddled state is one of the very sharpest of the realities." — **Henry James**

> "I don't regret a single 'excess' of my responsive youth. I only regret, in my chilled age, certain occasions and possibilities I didn't embrace." — **Henry James**

## Chapter 10: The Great Power Cycle

### Atomic Age Dawn (July 15, 1945)

Four generational responses to Trinity test:
- **Missionary** elder **Stimson** (77): "Babies satisfactorily born" — **Churchill**: "This is the Second Coming, in wrath"
- **Lost** midlifer **Truman** (61): took news impassively, ordered Hiroshima bombed — "let the buck stop with him"
- **G.I.** rising adult **Oppenheimer** (41): "If the radiance of a thousand suns were to burst into the sky..."
- **Silent** youth **Russell Baker** (19): "It seems like I'm pulling some monstrous joke on myself"

### The Missionary Awakening (1886-1903)

- Began with Haymarket Riot and global student missionary movement
- 1890s: agrarian protest, urban labor violence, Bryan's revivalist Presidential campaign
- Gave birth to Bible Belt, Christian socialism, Greenwich Village, Wobblies, women's suffrage
- 1886 Mount Hermon assembly motto: **"The Evangelization of the World in This Generation"**

### The Great Depression-WWII Crisis (1932-1945)

- From economic trough through New Deal ("the Third American Revolution") to VJ-Day
- Rising peer elite began assuming leadership, leading to JFK's "new generation" taking White House in 1961

### MISSIONARY GENERATION (Born 1860-1882) — Idealist

- 45,000,000 total; 23% immigrant; only ~1,000 alive in 1991
- Indulgently raised by midlife Gilded in world of orderly families and capital prosperity; came of age horrified by what **George Cabot Lodge** called "a world of machine-guns and machine-everything-else"
- **William Allen White** on Bryan's campaign: "a fanaticism like the Crusades... the delusion took the form of religious frenzy"
- Pioneered settlement houses (**Addams'** Hull House), muckraking, anarchist communes (**Goldman**, **Berkman**), suffrage marches
- Feminist cause unprecedented: **Isadora Duncan** (emancipation by dance), **Charlotte Perkins Gilman** (assailing *This Man-Made World*), **Margaret Sanger** (broadcasting birth control in *Woman Rebel*)
- Whatever their causes, mental approach remained the same: heroic desire to make world perfect according to standards welling up from within
- Entering midlife around WWI: split between rural evangelicals and urban Social Gospelers, but united by pessimism about nihilistic Lost juniors
- Pushed through Prohibition, women's suffrage, immigration restriction, Red deportation
- **FDR** after Pearl Harbor: "No compromise can end that conflict. There has never been — there never can be — successful compromise between good and evil"

> "He was the one person I ever knew, anybody, who was never afraid. God, God — how he could take it for us all." — young G.I. **Lyndon Johnson** on Roosevelt

- **Howe**: "Early assumptions as to virtue and vice, goodness and evil remained in my mind long after I had tried to discard them. This is, I think, the most characteristic influence of my generation"
- **Santayana**: "Americans are eminently prophets. They apply morals to public affairs. They are men of principles, and fond of stating them"
## Lost Generation Profile

**Born: 1883-1900**
**Type: Reactive**

### Age Location
- **Missionary Awakening** in youth
- **Great Depression-World War II Crisis** in midlife

> "There died a myriad... / For an old bitch gone in the teeth, / For a botched civilization." —**Ezra Pound**

### Overview

- World War I was cruel enough to soldiers in trenches—futile, stalemate-becoming-annihilating
- In 1919-1920, next-elders meted out **Volstead Act** to purge them of liquor, **Palmer Raids** to purge radicals, **John Sumner's Society for Suppression of Vice** to purge pornography
- "Down with middle-aged!" joined **John Dos Passos**, age 24
- These literati would know themselves as the **LOST GENERATION**, scrambling survivors in a world of pomposity and danger
- As 1920s dawned: **John Reed** jailed as "Red" in Moscow, **Harry Truman's** clothing store failing
- Lost set of alienation: **Babe Ruth** (Yankees), **"Scarface" Al Capone** (Chicago), **Irving Berlin** (Tin Pan Alley), writers **F. Scott Fitzgerald**, **Sinclair Lewis**, **Eugene O'Neill**
- **Fitzgerald** described his generation as at once "prewar and postwar"—with rowdy childhood and tired old age as bookends, lifecycle divided roughly in thirds by two world wars

### Lost Facts

- **Child labor**: In 1910, nearly one child in five between age 10 and 14 worked for pay; three in five between 15 and 18 employed
- Many worked in "sweatshops" (word first coined 1892)
- One Lost child in six worked as "newsie"—including **Irving Berlin**, **Jack Dempsey**, **Al Jolson**, **William O. Douglas**, **Groucho Marx**, **Earl Warren**
- Despite tight labor market, union memberships declined from nearly 5 million (1921) to under 3.5 million (1929)
- **Consumer culture**: No other generation purchased such large share of consumption with self-earned income; Lost pocket cash sustained America's child-only retailers
- From 1889 to 1922, Lost sweet tooth propelled doubling in per capita sugar consumption to ~100 pounds annually
- **Education decline**: Share of white children in primary schools dropped from 62% to 54% (1880-1900); for black children, 34% to 31%
- When Lost men took "I.Q." tests during WWI, results convinced scientists draftees had "mental age" of under 12
- In 1970, educational disparity between adults over 25 (12.2 years schooling) and over 65 (8.7 years)—largest ever measured
- **Drug culture**: Lost were America's first generation amid widespread adult-approved narcotics use—cocaine in cough syrup, lozenges, and (until 1904) Coca-Cola
- **Crime**: From 1900 to 1920, America's homicide rate rose **700 percent**
- Lost coined: "underworld," "gangster," "mobster," "racketeer," "moll," "getaway car"
- Also invented lexicon of music and sex: "get hep," "jive," "cat," "cathouse," "floozy," "sugar daddy"
- **1918 Influenza**: Killed about 250,000 Lost—five times the number who died in WWI combat
- **Suicide**: At age 20, Lost were 50% more suicide-prone than last-wave Missionaries; more suicide-prone than next three generations at every phase of life
- **Great Migration**: ~1.5 million black Americans emigrated out of South (1910-1930)—three times prior number since Civil War
- **Immigration**: With nine million members born abroad, Lost is America's largest immigrant generation in absolute numbers
- **Political leadership**: Attained majority Congressional seats later in lifecycle (58 years after first birthyear) than any other generation; weakest representation in national leadership posts
- **Republican leaning**: Most Republican-leaning generation throughout adult lives; chose Willkie in 1940, split for Goldwater in 1964, gave Reagan biggest proportions in 1980/1984
- **Economic hardship**: Hit by Depression during midlife earning years; one age bracket never targeted by New Deal relief; nearly half never lived in house with more than two bedrooms

### Lost Lifecycle

#### Youth

- In 1897, 8-year-old **Virginia O'Hanlon** wrote the *New York Sun*: "Is there a Santa Claus?"
- Children of 1890s were America's most tough-minded ever—growing up fast amid gangs, drugs, saloons, big-city immigration
- Often permissive parenting to point of near-neglect: **George Burns** recalled mother closing door "when house was full"
- **Jacob Riis** stepping into tenement: "the hall is dark and you might stumble over children... kicks and cuffs are their daily diet"
- Unsupervised children surged into labor market—girls as "homeworkers," boys as newsies, bootblacks, coal miners
- **Harpo Marx**: "School was all wrong. School simply didn't teach you how to be poor and live from day to day"
- Roosevelt's daughter **Alice**—smoking, swearing, sipping champagne in White House—became symbol of children growing up "bad"
- **Thomas Wolfe**: "children were without innocence, born old and dull and empty"

#### Coming of Age

- "We have in our unregenerate youth... been forced to become realistic," declared 23-year-old **John Carter**
- "At 17 we were disillusioned and weary," recalled **Malcolm Cowley**
- Most 20-year-olds turned deaf ear toward older radicals like **Jack London** and **Upton Sinclair**
- In 1913, magazines proclaimed "Sex O'Clock in America"—beginning "revolution in morals"
- Then came the **"Flapper of 1915"** who (reported **Mencken**) "has forgotten how to simper"
- These crosscurrents came together in **World War I**—"the sausage machine," ultimate evidence of elders' colossal blindness

#### Rising Adulthood

- **Hemingway**, **Dos Passos**, **Cowley**, **cummings** all volunteered as ambulance drivers
- "Abstract words such as glory, honor, courage, or hallow were obscene beside concrete names of villages" —**Hemingway**
- Lost "Flaming Youth" instinctively turned toward pleasure-seeking
- **Sinclair Lewis** scored salvos in *Main Street* and *Elmer Gantry*
- Triggering "the roar" of Twenties, Lost prompted Missionaries to roar back about "Problem of the Younger Generation"
- **Carter** in 1920 *Atlantic Monthly*: "older generation has certainly pretty well ruined this world before passing it on to us"

#### Midlife

- Uptown, Lost blacks streaming from South touched off cultural explosion: **Garveyism**, **Claude McKay**, **Alain Locke** on birth of "the Negro Age"
- Music named after black idiom for sex—"jazz"—drew crowds and put stamp on era of youthful hedonism
- **Dorothy Parker** and **Zelda Fitzgerald** disappointed older suffragettes: "The Jazz Age... had no interest at all in politics"
- By early forties, Lost entrepreneurs inventing supermarkets, shopping centers, frozen foods, automats
- **Dale Carnegie** and **Norman Vincent Peale** counseled a generation that cared more about success than self-worth
- **Fitzgerald**: "living well is best revenge"; Great Gatsby pined after Daisy—"her voice is full of money"
- Mood darkened in 1927 with execution of **Sacco** and **Vanzetti**
- **Walter Lippmann** in *A Preface to Morals* reflected on his generation's mental and ethical chaos
- **Malcolm Cowley** called 1930 year of "doubt and even defeat"—start of collective midlife hangover: **the Great Depression**
- "Now once more the belt is tight," observed **Fitzgerald**, suspecting his was "generation with no second acts"

## G.I. Generation Profile

**Born: 1901-1924**
**Type: Civic**

### Age Location
- **Great Depression-World War II Crisis** in rising adulthood
- **Boom Awakening** in elderhood

> "uncommon valor was a common virtue" — Inscription on Iwo Jima shrine

### Defining Characteristics

- **Charles Lindbergh's transatlantic flight (1927)** epitomized young G.I. spirit—special, sheltered, optimistic about technology
- By 1950s, G.I.s were "the best damned kids in the world"—victorious soldiers, Minuteman builders, interstate highway engineers, Apollo creators, vaccine developers
- **Henry Malcolm** describes them as **"Prometheus men"** with "implicit belief in progress and in the central role of great men"
- **Superman** perfectly captures G.I. persona: special child, corrupt older Lex Luthor, rocklike manliness, Superpower mission for community good
- Initials **"G.I."** stand for both **"general issue"** and **"government issue"**—this generation's lifecycle stood for both

### Gender Dynamics

- Came of age preferring **crisp sex-role definitions**; matured into father-worshiping, heavily male-fixated generation
- **Betty Friedan** and peers created **"Father Knows Best" culture**

### G.I. Facts

- **Child labor decline**: Rate fell by half during G.I. youth era—largest one-generation decline ever
- First boys/girls whose pin money came from **"allowances"** rather than earnings
- **Health**: From 1900-1924 cohorts, infant/child mortality fell 80%, adult height rose >1 inch, life expectancy at 65 rose 20%
- **Education**: Largest one-generation jump in American history; average schooling rose from 9th to 12th grade, share attending college tripled
- **Scouting**: Boy Scouts (1910), Camp Fire Girls (1910), Girl Scouts (1912), Boys and Girls Clubs (1914)—all founded when first-wave G.I.s reaching puberty
- **Political**: 80% of first-wave G.I. voters opted for FDR in 1932, 85% in 1936—largest single-generation mandates ever; held White House for thirty years, won nine Presidential elections
- **Nobel Prizes**: Won 99 Nobels, roughly two-thirds of all awarded to Americans; dominated physics, chemistry, medicine; won all fourteen economics Nobels through 1989
- **Economic mobility**: Six in seven report facing less difficulty than parents; homeownership never more affordable than early 1950s ($3,000 income, 4% mortgage, $7,000 Levittown home)
- **Elder affluence**: Most affluent elders of 20th century; where Lost elders had highest poverty rate, G.I.s have lowest
- **Federal benefits**: 1965-1989, benefits per elderly person rose fifteen times more rapidly than wages; Social Security paid back entire value of prior contributions within four years of retiring
- 1990 Harvard class of 1940 survey (median net worth $865,000): 88% "fairly or very happy"; 41% "not religious at all"

### The G.I. Lifecycle

#### Youth

- **John Spargo's** *Bitter Cry of the Children* (1904) augured Missionary determination to protect new generation
- "Milk station" movement, pasteurization, Little Mothers' Leagues—major purpose of Prohibition was pushing alcohol away from children
- Popular literature idealized children who were modest, cheerful, deferential; *Literary Digest* demanded "reassertion of parental authority"
- **Golden era of the high school**: Glee musicals of Mickey Rooney and Judy Garland—work hard, play by rules, everybody gets reward
- **"Standardized youth culture"** emerged: teens watched same movies, listened to same radio, picked at 100,000-seat stadia
- By mid-1920s, "kid" shifted from derision to praise

#### Coming of Age

- **"Ours was the best generation,"** said **Gene Shuford**; within years, *Harper's* described them as **"Locked-Out Generation"** of Great Depression
- Roosevelt's New Deal perfectly suited twentyish mind-set: planning, optimism, collective action
- **CCC** and **NYA** kept them busy—marching, never complaining, building things that lasted
- Collective action flourished: winter/spring 1936-1937, nearly half million in **sit-down strikes**
- **Walt Disney's** *Snow White*—innocent maidenhood and cheerful male teamwork defeating brooding witch

> "That is what Pearl Harbor did for this generation, galvanizing 'good kid' ingot into G.I. steel"

- **Rosie the Riveter** comprised only significant female wartime workforce in nation's history
- World War II killed 1.5% of G.I. men; among 97% who emerged uninjured, experience gave confidence to "experience dangers that would test their mettle"

#### Rising Adulthood

> "A good job, a mild future, and a little house big enough for me and my wife"

- Thanks to **G.I. Bill**, two of every five 1950-era housing dollars covered by taxpayers
- Polls showed young adults more stern-minded than elders on Japanese occupation, use of poison gas
- Several (**Kennedy, Nixon, Johnson, Ford**) launched promising political careers
- **William Whyte** catalogued suburban **"social ethic"** in *The Organization Man*—neighborhoods noteworthy mainly for "friendliness"
- Television perfectly expressed G.I. culture: science for man's benefit, relentlessly upbeat, nurturing children

#### Midlife

- Rise to national leadership shaped by childhood mission: clean up squalor left by Lost
- Watershed all-G.I. election of 1960; **Robert Frost** at Kennedy's inaugural heralded coming of **"Augustan Age"**

> "the torch has been passed to a new generation... tempered by war, disciplined by a hard and bitter peace"

- Kennedy brought "vigor" and assembled **"thinker-doers"** like **McNamara** ("the can-do man in the can-do society")
- **"Great 89th" Congress** (74% G.I.) became what Theodore White termed "Grandest Congress of Programs and Entitlements"
- Then came Vietnam—triggering angry **"generation gap"** with coming-of-age Boomers
- **Nixon** despaired America had become "a pitiful, helpless giant"; others (**McNamara, Bundy**) concluded "we were wrong, and the new generation is right"

#### Elderhood

- Attacked "myths" of lonely old age; worked to be ever-optimistic **"senior citizens"**
- **Modern Maturity** would not accept ads mentioning "pain, inflammation, suffer, hurt, ache"
- **1972**: first election where gray-lobby activism became critical; Congress added extra 20% benefit hike—intergenerational transfer dwarfing entire Great Society poverty programs
- **AARP** became largest advocacy organization in nation's history
- Half of all federal spending consumed by pensions, medical entitlements, and interest on national debt
- No elder generation ever so relied upon to foot family bills: in 1987/1988, grandparents accounted for 22% of all toy purchases
- **Erik Erikson** found "indulgent grandparents are associated with societies where grandparents are dissociated with authority"

### G.I. Legacy

> "We've done the work of democracy, day by day" — **George Bush** (1989)

- Whatever they touched, they made bigger—and in their eyes, better
- Patiently endured economic despair that might have driven another to revolution; ably soldiered one war America could not afford to lose
- Produced postwar economic miracle; massive nuclear stockpiles preserved expensive, unnerving peace
- Truly great generation of scientists; their women may have been most dedicated teachers and skillful mothers in American history
- **Yet final ledger includes colossal debits**: unprecedented public/private liabilities, exported assets, depleted resources, environmental harms
- Generation that inherited excess economic capacity is bequeathing fiscally starved economy unable to afford generous agenda
- Offspring of soldiers G.I.s conquered—Japanese and German—today out-competing their own children

## Chapter 10: The Silent Generation (continued)

### Generational Identity and Timing

- **Admiral William Crowe** calls them a "transitional generation," **Rose Franzblau** a "Middle Generation"—forever "betwixt and between"
- Silent boundaries defined less by what they did than by what those older and younger did—and what the Silent themselves just missed
- First wave came of age too late for war-era heroism but in time to encounter a powerful national consensus—against which young rebels like **James Dean** found themselves "without a cause"
- Last wave graduated just before **Benita Eisler's** "great divide" before the fiery Boom Class of 1965
- 16% of Harvard's Class of '64 joined the Peace Corps (Harvard's top postgraduate destination that year)—whereas next year's graduates began criticizing the Peace Corps amid early stirrings of antiestablishment rebellion

### Life Phase Dynamics

- Silent realize they are generational stuffing of a sandwich between the get-it-done G.I. and the self-absorbed Boom
- Looked to G.I.s for role models in rising adulthood—pursued lifelong mission of refining and humanizing the G.I.-built world
- As songwriters, graduate students, and young attorneys, they mentored the Boom Awakening, founding organizations of political dissent that next-juniors later radicalized

> "During the ferment of the 1960s, a period of the famous 'generation gap,' we occupied, unobtrusively as usual, the gap itself. When nobody over thirty was to be trusted, our age was thirty-something." —**Wade Greene**

### Lifecycle Pattern

- The sexual revolution hit the Silent when most were passing forty, decades after their natural adolescence—causing immense problems in family lives
- **Franzblau** (1971): "a generation of jealousies and role reversals"
- In *Birth and Fortune*, **Richard Easterlin** labeled his own peer group "the fortunate ones"—relatively small cohort size gave them an edge
- Silent have enjoyed lifetime of steady rising affluence, suffered relatively few war casualties, and shown twentieth century's lowest rates for almost every social pathology of youth
- Apart from significant number of divorced women who never remarried, Silent lifecycle has been an escalator of prosperity, offering maximum reward for minimum initiative

### Philosophy and Values

- No other living generation could half-believe in **Ellen Goodman's** "Woody Allen school of philosophy: 80 percent of life is showing up"
- Where G.I.s did great things and felt one with history, where Boomers found ravishment within themselves, the Silent have taken great things for granted and looked beyond themselves while worrying that the larger challenges of life are passing them by
- They have been keen on manufacturing points of lifecycle reference around personal rather than historical markers—what various Silent authors labeled "passages," "seasons," "turning points"

### Social Impact and Contributions

- Well aware of their own deficiencies, Silent have spent a lifetime plumbing inner wellsprings older G.I.s seldom felt while maintaining a sense of social obligation Boomers haven't shared
- Their solutions—fairness, openness, due process, expertise—reflect a lack of surefootedness but also keen sense of how humans fall short of grand civic plans or ideal moral standards
- Silent appeals for change have seldom arisen from anger or fury, but rather through self-conscious humanity and tender social conscience

> "Deep in my heart, I do believe / We shall overcome someday"

- Have adopted moral relativism of the skilled arbitrator, mediating arguments between others and reaching out to people of all cultures, races, ages, and handicaps

> "We don't arrive with ready-made conclusions so much as a honed capacity to ask and to listen." —**Wade Greene**

### The Silent Malleability Paradox

- This very malleability left the Silent unable to build secure generational family lives
- **Ellen Goodman**: "If anything has changed in the last generation, it is the erosion of confidence" among parents
- **Benita Eisler** labels hers the generation with "a corpse in the trunk"—the R-rated decade of the 1970s when so much seemed to go wrong coincided precisely with Silent rise to influence

### The 1970s Crisis

- **Alvin Toffler** (*Future Shock*) foresaw "historic crisis of adaptation"—called for "the moderation and regulation of change" with "exact scientific knowledge, expertly applied"
- But as Toffler's peers applied this cult of expertise, they encountered their turbulent passage to midlife—and the "transience index" began exploding upward
- Families splintered, substance abuse moved past euphoria to social damage, American society felt its G.I.-era sensation of belonging fall away
- **Daniel Ellsbergs** pushed to get secrets out, **Phil Donahues** and **Ted Koppels** aimed microphones everywhere
- **Lester Thurow** (*Zero-Sum Society*): improvement in any one group came at expense of another
- **Jerry Brown** called it "the age of limits"—became, in **William Schneider's** words, "the Zeitgeist theme of his generation"

### Reagan Era Transformation

- Elder G.I. won Presidency by ridiculing Silent midlife mind-set
- America of 1980s became Silent-style Tofflerian "ad hocracy" stressing expertise over simplicity, participation over authority, process over result
- "Liberal" transformed from G.I.-style constituency nationalism to Silent-style enervator attuned to multitudinous special interests
- "Conservative" evolved from Lost-style cautious stewardship to faintly hip, high-rolling optimism
- On both sides of political spectrum, Silent would much prefer to discuss processes than outcomes
- America grew more accustomed to deferring or learning to live with problems than to taking aggressive steps to solve them

### Cultural Fragmentation and "End of Empire" Anxieties

- Today's 50- and 60-year-olds have been less successful in forging a sense of national or personal direction than any generation in living memory
- **Robert Bellah** (*Habits of the Heart*): haunted by diffuse disconnectedness in American life
- **Kevin Phillips** describes "End of Empire fixation"; **Barbara Ehrenreich**: white-collar workforce haunted by "fear of falling"; **Jack Kemp** foresees exhilarating prosperity
- What mostly emerges is fuss and detail—world view of such enormous complexity that contributions often lack one redeeming description
- Approaching old age, many still find themselves emotionally obliged to struggling children in what **Robert Grossman** terms "Parenthood II"
- Most aging Silent find themselves wealthier, but more confused as to purpose, than they ever imagined

### The "Grandtravel" Generation

- Travel agents report boom in "grandtravel" (grandparent-grandchild trips)
- New interest in "Elderhostels"—reminiscent of how Silent toured Europe as youths
- Self-styled "kids over 60" formed "SeniorNet" computer network
- In 1989, affluent Social Security beneficiaries formed "21st Century Club" to assign checks to philanthropic trust fund
- **Ralph Nader** mobilizing "Princeton Project '55" to attack "systemic social problems"
- In 1986, Union of International Associations catalogued 10,000 world problems awaiting solution—and the Silent Generation is running out of time

### Historical Memory and Legacy

- Nearly four decades after Korean War, no national memorial exists—fund-raiser complained veterans were being treated "as if they have never lived, never ennobled their time and place"

### Looking Forward

- **Neil Armstrong** and **Martin Luther King, Jr.** will likely remain among the most celebrated Americans
- "Above all, the generation that took America from grinding bulldozers to user-friendly computers, from the circa-1960 'Nuclear Age' into the circa-1990 'Information Age,' has excelled at *personal communication*"
- Have consistently tried to defuse conflict by encouraging people to talk to each other—"From therapeutic T-groups to 'Donahue'-style 'global town meetings'"
- Have been pathbreakers for much of the 1960s-era "consciousness" (music, civil rights, ecology, feminism) for which Boomers too often claim credit
- This most considerate of living generations has specialized not in grand bright institutions but in people—*life-size people* like the statues planned for the Korean War Memorial

## Boom Generation (Born 1943-1960)

### Type: Idealist

### Coming of Age Context

- **1965:** *Time* declared teenagers "on the fringe of a golden era"—predicted they would build blight-proof cities, enrich the underdeveloped world
- Boomer prophecies collapsed one by one as generation became "a surly political powerhouse" capable of sweeping candidates from White House
- **Annie Gottlieb**: Boomers recall "time" as the 1960s, a decade with newfound solidarity over other generations

### Generational Identity

- **First cohort (1943)**: Born with G.I. optimism rewarded in victory over fascism
- **Unlike Silent:** No childhood recollection of WWII; **unlike 13ers:** All reaching adolescence before draft ended
- **1943 "victory babies"** ranked among most self-satisfied in American history
- **VJ-Day forward:** Cultural and spiritual focal point—childhood America-obsessed, youth youth-obsessed, "yuppie" phase yuppie-obsessed
- **Mission:** Not constructing society but **justifying and purifying it**—G.I.s learned basics, Boomers learned **critical thinking**

> "We are Bomb Babies... We grew up with fallout in our milk." —23-year-old Ronald Allison, 1967

- **1980:** 12-point October shift among Boom voters turned Reagan landslide

### The Consciousness Revolution (1967-1980)

- **McLoughlin's "Fourth Awakening"**: began 1967 with Summer of Love, inner-city riots, San Francisco counterculture
- **Peaked 1970** with Kent State and Days of Rage
- Throughout 1970s: Human potential movement, Aquarian Conspiracy, resurgent evangelicalism
- **Ended 1980** as rates of crime and substance abuse peaked among coming-of-age Americans
- Reagan's election (endorsed by Boom) marked acceptance of rising-adult social roles

### Generational Gap and Family Dynamics

- Waged across generation gap between Boomers and G.I.s—began within families as revolt against fathers
- **Keniston**: attachment to mothers; **Malcolm**: oedipal rebellion; **Feuer**: patricide
- Youth fury over Vietnam spread **patriphobia** beyond family—most memorable symbols were direct affronts against G.I. constructions: **hippie invasion of Disneyland, burning of Isla Vista Bank, Earth Day burial of automobile**

### Self and Narcissism

- **Tom Wolfe**: "voyage to the interior"; **Christopher Lasch**: "culture of narcissism"
- Central theme: Rising adulthood through suspended animation, resisting permanent linkages to mates, children, corporations
- **Katharine Ross** (*The Graduate*): Boomers approached life and heard something inside scream *"STOP!"*
- Applied what sounded like pick-and-choose idealism

### Boomer Perfectionism

- Desire for ***the best*** within very personal definition of taste
- If couldn't afford house/family, could at least afford **very best brand of mustard or ice cream**—"Zen luxuriousness" (Katy Butler: maximum enjoyment from minimum consumption)
- **Fixation on self** forged instinct to make judgments according to **wholly internalized standards**, based on immutable principles of right and wrong
- Gift for deductive logic—**better philosophers than scientists, better preachers than builders**
- **Highest-testing Boom cohorts** reached age where scientific achievement peaks—yet "very late to win a Nobel Prize" (Lost, G.I., and Silent won two, four, and six respectively before Boom's first)

> "Like Donald Trump, a prototype Boomer sees himself capable of becoming a titan of whatever world he chooses fully to inhabit." —Authors

### Coming of Age: Spiritual Search

- Sustained compelling urge for perfection and reducing dependence on physical self
- **Drug phase:** Transcendence in first wave, crime in last wave; by 1990 stripped of spiritual trappings—subsequent search for spiritual euphoria: **flocked from drugs to religion**
- Spawned most active era of church formation of the twentieth century

### Gender and Sexuality

- **Hermaphroditic quality**—**Urbanska**: exalted "self-sexuality"
- **Men** intruding into domain of nurturance; **women** invading secular roles once reserved for G.I. males
- Trends made Boomers more independent of each other yet more open to isolation and economic insecurity

### First Wave vs. Last Wave Schism

- **Schism between fortyish modernists/New Agers** and **thirtyish traditionalists/evangelicals**
- **Anti-abortionist Bill Tickel**: "it's just easier to have blanket absolutes"
- **"Victory babies" of mid-to-late 1940s**: Most pampered entirely by G.I.s, came of age during peak parental protectiveness
- **Babies of conformist late 1950s**: Parented mostly by Silent, came of age at point of maximum freedom (some say chaos)
- **Last-wave fared worse** in education, financial security, self-direction; **first-wave fared worse in marital stability**
- But measured by inner-life standards, both ends feel equally serene

### The "Great American Boom"

- **1946:** *Fortune* declared start of "the Great American Boom"—not just fertility, but education, housing, science
- If G.I.s measure worth objectively (by works they leave behind), **Boomers measure themselves subjectively** (by works they see within)
- Many having difficulty matching parents' achievements in economic/family life—yet invariably consider their "consciousness" to be higher

> "If it's by choice and it's not overwhelming, having no money can be a way of entering more deeply into your life." —**Katy Butler**

## Boomer Facts

### Demographics & Family
- **Roughly half of "baby boom"** attributed to unusual fecundity, other half to unusual bunching of family formation
- **Baby bulge 1946-1957** resulted from coincident babymaking of late-nesting G.I.s and early-nesting Silent
- **No generation of American women can match G.I.s for intensity of nurture** in their children's upbringing
- Among preschoolers with working mothers, four in five cared for in own homes—only 2% attended child care

### Youth Pathologies
- **By almost any standard, Boom = generation of worsening trends**: death rates for accidental death rose sharply; drunk driving, suicide, illegitimate births, teen unemployment all doubled or tripled
- Crime rates mounted with each successive cohort—during 1970s, serious youth crime grew **twice as fast** as number of youths
- **Criminals born in 1958** were more likely than those born in 1945 to commit multiple crimes—and **80% more likely to send victims to hospital or morgue**

### Education Statistics
- **17-year SAT slide** spanned nearly entire Boom (1946-1963 cohorts)—yet worst years coincided with greatest grade inflation ever measured
- **1969:** 4% of freshmen claimed straight-A average; **1978:** nearly tripled to 11%
- Average grade rose from C+ to A-; three-fourths of colleges offered alternatives to traditional marking

### Sexual Revolution
- More a women's than men's movement: **Boomer men showed only 3% increase** in sexual activity over Silent
- **Boomer women doubled rate of premarital sex** over Silent (41% to 81%) and **tripled relative propensity to commit adultery**

### Vietnam War
- **Only one Boomer man in sixteen ever saw combat**; among all rest, two-thirds attributed avoidance to deliberate dodge
- **1% of Boomer men committed draft-law felonies**—ten times percentage killed in combat
- **Median soldier age during Vietnam (17)** = lowest in American history
- **Only consensus "lesson"**: war was badly handled by G.I. leaders
- Following Iraq's invasion of Kuwait (1990), Boomers **more inclined than any other generation** to believe sending troops was "the right thing"

### Religion
- During 1980s, overall Boom church attendance rose by nearly 30%
- **America's fastest-growing church = Assembly of God** (membership quadrupled)
- America now has more Muslims than Episcopalians
- **Seven Boomers in ten believe in psychic phenomena** (versus five in ten among elders)
- First-wave Boomers believe more in meditation and reincarnation; last-wavers more in "born-again" conversion

### Economic Position
- Were it not for employed women, Boomer family incomes would be well below what Silent earned at like age
- 40-year-old first-waver earns about 15% more than Silent father at like age, but 30-year-old last-waver has fallen about 10% behind
- Where typical G.I. worker enjoyed 63% inflation-adjusted rise between age 30 and 40, first-wave Boom worker suffered net 1% decline
- Yet Boomers overwhelmingly consider careers better (5-to-1), personal freedoms greater (6-to-1), and lives more meaningful (9-to-1) than their fathers'

## The Boom Lifecycle

### YOUTH: Hothouse Nurture

> "We wanted our children to be inner-directed... It seemed logical to us that fascism and communism... could not really succeed except in countries where children were raised in very authoritarian homes." —**Eda LeShan** (G.I.)

- G.I. dads worked to pay bills; G.I. moms applied "democratic discipline"—dealing with children "thoughtfully, reasonably, and kindly"
- Moms turned to **Dr. Spock**, who mixed science with friendliness and instructed that "we need idealistic children"
- **Permissive feeding schedule** extended to older kids: "He'll-clean-up-his-room-when-he's-ready-to-have-a-clean-room"
- First-wave Boomers passed through public schools in **Sputnik-era peak of institutional confidence**
- **Cheryl Merser**: "Watching over them—God or a saint or a guardian angel... the future looked to be 'the way life was on *The Jetsons*'"
- As successive cohorts passed through childhood, nurturing style leaned more toward tolerance than guidance
- Reform-minded educators insisted adults had as much to learn from youths as vice versa
- **Silent-era couples-only Saturday-night dance nearly vanished**, eclipsed by rock concerts where dateless teenagers danced alone

### COMING OF AGE: Student Rebellion

> "I Am a Student! Do Not Fold, Spindle, or Mutilate!" —Pickets outside Berkeley's Sproul Hall, 1964

- Coffeehouse poets gave way to bullhorn-toting radicals—what Silent activists **Peter Collier and David Horowitz** recall as generational shift: "Those of us who had come of age in the fifties" gave way to "second wave of activists" more likely to heed simple motto: "*Do it!*"
- **Free Speech Movement** served first notice of most emotionally intense and culturally influential youth rebellion in American history

> "I want everyone to see me and say 'There goes an enemy of the state,' because that's what we are, we are at revolution biz." —**James Kunen**

- **Daniel Moynihan**: "Who are these people? I suggest to you they are Christians arrived on the scene of Second Century Rome"
- Screaming radicals represented just **10 to 15% of their generation**, but the righteous often prevailed in youth culture where purity of moral position counted most
- **Keniston**: young radicals had "feelings of loneliness, solitude, and isolation," were profoundly mature by measures of ego strength, yet childlike in social skills
- Collegians could issue "nonnegotiable demands" with little fear—supercharged G.I.-led economy offered good jobs to all comers
- Urban black youths sparked deadly riots; non-college white Boomers **twice as likely as elders to vote for George Wallace** in 1968

### Vietnam War and Youth Rebellion

- Youth frenzy congealed around Vietnam—policy almost perfectly designed to create conflict between rationalist elders and spiritualist juniors
- G.I. leaders waged it as limited, scientifically managed internationalist pest control while avoiding asking for sacrifice from noncombatants
- Boomers had been raised to question, argue, and ultimately disobey orders not comporting with self-felt standards
- **Draft's class bias** created festering divisions between those who went and those who didn't

### 1969: Peak of Youth Rebellion

- **1969:** Vietnam casualties peaked; Boom rebellion turned bloody with eighteen-month spate of radical bombings and shootings
- **Gallup Poll 1970:** 44% of all college students believed violence was justified for social change
- **Rap Brown**: violence "as American as apple pie"
- Killing of six students at **Kent State and Jackson State** briefly mobilized generation in 1970
- **Silent Charles Reich** wrote rapturous best seller about Boom's "Consciousness III" leading to *The Greening of America*

### Post-1973: Grinding Pessimism

- Boomers began heeding their Beatle mentors' "simple words of wisdom: *Let It Be*"
- When economy went sour in 1973, youth mood turned to grinding pessimism
- Last-wavers came of age amid gray generational drizzle of sex, drugs, unemployment
- Boom settled in as more apathetic and illiberal than G.I. parents could have imagined

### RISING ADULTHOOD: Searching for Inner Meaning

> "I have made no plans because I have found no plans worth making... All too many people are just waiting for life rather than living." —*Berkeley Daily Californian*, 1971

- **Exercise faddists** searched for "runner's high"; meditative diet faddists triggered what Levenstein terms "century's Second Golden Age of Food Quackery"
- In athletics, first occupation they gained leverage over: proclaimed "Free agency"—sporting life grew more argumentative and less team-oriented
- Insisted on "meaningful" careers; by mid-1970s America's postwar productivity surge came to abrupt end
- **"New Age Man" with "Manifesto of the Person"**: "our sovereign right of self-discovery"
- **Urbanska** termed personal religion "Sheilaism"—friend Sheila had "this little voice inside" saying "God is whatever I feel"
- **Enter the "yuppie"**: Only ~5% match demographic definition (urban, professional, affluent), but much larger proportion fit subjective definition: self-immersion, impatient desire for personal satisfaction, weak civic instincts

### APPROACHING MIDLIFE: New Puritanism

- Where Silent prided themselves on ability to "grow young," Boomers see themselves as "growing up" to new responsibility—**P.J. O'Rourke's** "new seriousness"
- Surveys show Boomers more apt to believe patience necessary to appreciate value
- **More in parallel than collectively**, Boomers asserting judgments that reflect perfect moral order

> "A generation that when young trusted nobody over 30 now trusts nobody under 30." —**Alan Kors**, history professor

- **"New Puritanism" of circa-1990 America**: Fortyish Boomers policing "politically correct" behavior, passing "anti-ugly" zoning ordinances, banning obscene music, promoting "chastity," supporting novel forms of corporal punishment
- **Smoking or regular drinking** becoming "new Scarlet Letters" among ex-flower children
- **M.A.D.D.'s Candy Lightner** attacks social drinkers as dangerous, even immoral

> "There is no such thing as being too rude to a smoker." —Boom activist

- Now gaining real power, **Boomers do not inherently dislike government**—idea of using state to tell people what to do suits them just fine
- Casting aside Silent preoccupation with process, Boomers (when focused) zero in on essentials, choose between right and wrong, and act
- Boomers of all stripes—**Oliver North** to "Animal Rights" lab bombers—can be easily attracted to lawlessness in pursuit of higher purpose
- **"Earth First!" eco-saboteurs**: "No Compromise in Defense of Mother Earth"
- **Shelby Steele**: blacks should examine "the content of our character" and reject racial preferences as "kindness that kills"
- Oregon's refusal to spend Medicaid on operations considered too expensive: the budgetary result can be less important than the no-pain-no-gain message

### Key Lifecycle Events

- 0-1 (1962): Birth-control pill approved
- 0-4 (1965): "Baby boom" ends; Supreme Court upholds right to contraceptives
- 0-7 (1968): *Rosemary's Baby* begins decade-long popularity of bad-child films

- 9-29 (1990): Rock lyrics censored; U.S. troops to Persian Gulf

### 13ers as True Children of the 1960s-70s

- **Thirteeners, not Boomers**, were America's true "children of the 1960s" and especially the 1970s
- Awakening era seemed euphoric to young adults but was a **nightmare** for children: self-immersed parents, disintegrating homes, culture shifting from G to R ratings, new public-health dangers, "Me Decade" economy tipping away from young
- Adult message: **"Grow up fast"**—they graduated early to "young adult realism," turning into **"proto-adults"** in their early teens
- At every phase of life, 13ers encountered a world of **more punishing consequence** than anything Silent or Boom elders ever knew

### The Inverse Silent Lifecycle

- 13er lifecycle has been direct inverse of Silent generation's experience
- Where Silent passed through childhood in era of parental suffocation and entered adulthood with barriers removed, 13ers faced the opposite
- Where Silent grew up with childlike awe of powerful elders, 13ers acquired **adultlike fatalism** about weakness of elders
- Unlike homogeneous Silent, 13ers coming of age with **sharply diverging personal circumstances** (economists' "spreading bell curve")

### 13er Facts

- **Most aborted generation** in American history: through last-wave birthyears, would-be mothers aborted one fetus in three
- **Divorce**: 13er child in 1980s faced twice the risk of parental divorce as Boomer child in mid-1960s, three times a Silent child in 1950; four-fifths of divorced adults say happier, but majority of children feel otherwise
- **Family complexity**: 1980 only 56% of dependent children lived with two once-married parents; 19% with one parent; one in five had half siblings
- **Latchkey kids**: Between 1960-1980, mothers with children under 5 in workforce rose from 20% to 47%
- **Grade inflation ended**: Youths born 1961 received 10% fewer A's and C's than born 1960
- **Academic comparison** (teachers scoring Boomers vs. 13ers in 43 measures): **Boomers 38, 13ers 4, one tie**; 13ers outscored only in negotiating skills, consumer awareness, adult-interaction skills, and "defenses to prevent extreme dependency"
- **First generation since Gilded** to be less college-educated than elder siblings
- **Economic decline**: Average earnings of young college grads fell 6%; for young high school dropouts, fell **42%**
- **Military**: By 2-to-1, 13er men prefer military to civilian public service; best-educated generation of soldiers in American history
- **Most Republican-leaning youths** in 60-year polling history; in 15 consecutive polls 1981-1988, gave Reagan higher approval than any generation except Lost
- **Disease mortality** lower than Silent at same age, but almost entirely offset by higher accidents, murder, suicide
- **Youth homicide**: ~2,000 minors murdered in 1988—twice 1965 (despite 6.5 million fewer youths); among black males 15-24 in D.C., murder = 47% of all deaths (1987)
- **Fear**: Estimated 135,000 guns brought to classrooms daily; 8% of urban 7th-12th graders miss school monthly from physical fear
- **Suicide**: Committing suicide more frequently than any generation since Lost; ~5,000 children under 18 per year throughout 1980s—largest number and proportion ever
- **Most heavily incarcerated generation** in American history; by 1990, one in four black men age 20-29 in jail, on probation, or on parole

### Economic Distress and Generational Burdens

- **Child poverty**: Since 1974, under-18 bracket has shown highest poverty rates; distinction passed directly from Lost to 13th without touching G.I., Silent, or Boom
- In 1967, young male earners in early twenties made 74% as much as older males; by 1988, ratio fell to **54%**
- Median income of households headed by persons under 25 fell **18%** (1973-1988)
- Homeownership for heads age 18-24 fell by **one-third**; by 1990, three of four young men still living at home—largest proportion since Great Depression
- Older generations deferred **$2 trillion** in consumption through Treasury debt—policy five times more expensive for average 15-year-old than 65-year-old
- In 1990: young 13er couple ($30,000 wage income) paid **$5,055 in taxes** vs. typical retired G.I. couple with same income paying **$1,073**

### Youth: The "Consciousness Revolution" Years

- Most virulently **anti-child period** in American history; childhood **Tom Cruise** recalls as "kind of scattered"
- Silent mothers and fathers increasingly saw children as hindrances to self-exploration
- Adults ranked autos ahead of children as necessary for "the good life"
- *Ourselves and Our Children*: "consider yourself" ranked ahead of "benefiting our children"
- Popular guides emphasized **why-to-dos over what-to-dos**: *doing* right mattered less than parent and child each *feeling* right
- **Marie Winn**: "early-childhood determinism appeared to be a gift from gods" for parents with new wanderlust who could conclude their 6- or 10-year-old could cope well enough
- **Divorce** became central fear of 13er childhood; **Judy Blume's** *It's Not the End of the World*: once-happy family disintegrating amid shouting, slapping, crying

### The Parent Without Authority

> "The parent is usually a coordinator without voice or authority." — **Kenneth Keniston** (1977)

- American child-nurturing style completed two-decade passage from *Father Knows Best* to **Bill Cosby's** *Fatherhood* self-doubt
- Dominant media image: parent as "pal" who "always understands; never gets very angry"
- Silent parents like Cosby's Cliff Huxtable: gentle, communicative; Boomer parents expressed ambivalence, abandoned positive future vision
- Media reinforced view that adults were not especially virtuous, competent, or powerful

> "I hate the idea that you should always protect children. They live in the same world we do." — **Judy Blume**

- *Mad* magazine's **Al Feldstein**: encouraged children to "react to life as you would hack through a jungle"
- Latchkey "Self-Care Guide": "Always pay attention to what is happening around you when you are on the street"

### 13er Self-Perception and Cultural Identity

- Accept being "disrespected," taking perverse fun in being "BAAAD"
- Developed what psychologist **David Elkind** calls the **"patchwork self"**
- First-wavers elbowed aside by Boomers at every turn; last-wavers locked onto Nintendo

> "So many things have already happened in the world that we can't possibly come up with anything else." — 15-year-old **David Peters**

- Pop culture icons embrace demeaning images: **Max Headroom** (beheaded, imprisoned in TV), **Teenage Mutant Ninja Turtles** (flushed down toilet, deformed by radiation), **Bart Simpson** ("underachiever" whom Bennett will "straighten out with soap sandwiches")
- Built **powerful survival instinct** around ethos of **personal determinism**: what matters is how you behave, not who you are inside

### Work Attitudes and Career Patterns

- Leave troubles behind at work, take minds off job when leaving; Boomer bosses see them as "not driven from within," suited for "short-term tasks"
- Look for **quick strikes** over long-term promises; *Wall Street Journal*: "more willing to gamble their careers than earlier generations"
- Best chance: striking out on own, finding small market niche
- **"McJobs" economy**: *American Demographics* predicts fastest-growing fields as cashiers, nurses, janitors, waiters, truck drivers
- Harvard student **Mandy Silber**: "I keep hearing this is the best time of our lives. And I wonder—is it all downhill?"

### Crime and "Wilding"

- New reactionary style of sexism, racism, soulless violence seeped into 13er-penned lyrics
- **Mike Tyson** admitted: "I shot at a lot of people... I liked to see them run, I liked to see them beg"
- When Boomer youths assaulted Silent victims, they claimed mitigating reasons; 13ers who attack Boomer victims condemned as "evil" thugs
- Kids invented word **"wilding"**: "Sometimes they do it for fun, sometimes for money, sometimes they just do it"

### Response to End of Cold War

- East German students poured over Berlin Wall; *Washington Post*: high school kids were "left flat" and "utterly unmoved"
- From 13er perspective: unflinching and realistic—they've tramped through the dirty beach where idealism leads
- **Peter Hart** report: only 12% mentioned voting as attribute of good citizenship; 48% mentioned personal generosity
- 13er civic attitude likened to Lone Ranger: do a good deed, leave a silver bullet, move on
- **Neil Postman**: when 13ers were little, adults gave children "answers to questions they never asked"

### Hidden Strengths

- What 13ers seek: to be left alone, have unjudgmental ear, dial into their style of ironic detachment

> "It's not like some fully bent tongue from hell or anything." — **Miles Orkin**

- This streetwise generation brings seasoned talent for getting the most out of a bad hand
- "Thirteeners may never have glimpsed Nirvana, but they know how to win"

## Chapter 12: The Past as Prologue

### Crisis Era Optimism (1937-1943)

- **"Tomorrow Is Another Day"**—songs like "Somewhere over the Rainbow," 1939 World's Fair
- During crisis: total consensus about society's direction: material abundance, spiritual simplicity, struggle requiring total unity and sacrifice
- **Generational roles**: elders for strategic vision, midlifers for tactical means, rising adults for selfless muscle
- Such moods recur at specific intervals: 1680s, 1770s, (briefly) ~1860, 1930s-1940s

### Four Visions of the Future

- Four visions are component pieces of broader social mood accompanying four **constellation eras**: **AWAKENING, INNER-DRIVEN, CRISIS, OUTER-DRIVEN**
- In each era, mood determined by unique combination of generational types at each life stage

### The Cycle and Progress

- Concept of historical cycle seems to threaten the American Dream of linear progress
- However, **the cycle is perfectly compatible with progress** by any standard—material, spiritual, social, cultural
- All the cycle explains is **when and why different generations apply different standards** in working toward progress

> "Wherever we use the word 'cycle,' the reader may replace it with 'spiral,' with all the opportunity and danger implicit in that word. A spiral turns in a circle while moving upward—or downward."

### Constellational Eras in American History

**Eighteen total eras** across five cycles:

**Colonial Cycle:** Puritan Awakening (1615-1647) → Religious Intolerance (1648-1673) → Glorious Revolution (1674-1700) → Age of Enlightenment (1701-1723)

**Revolutionary Cycle:** Great Awakening (1724-1741) → French and Indian War (1742-1766) → American Revolution (1767-1791) → Era of Good Feeling (1792-1821)

**Civil War Cycle:** Transcendental Awakening (1822-1842) → Inner-Driven (1843-1859) → Civil War (1859) → Reconstruction/Gilded Age (1860-1882)

**Great Power Cycle:** Missionary Awakening (1883-1900) → WWI/Prohibition (1901-1924) → Depression/WWII (1925-1942) → Superpower America (1943-1960)

**Millennial Cycle:** Boom Awakening (1961-1981) → Inner-Driven (1982-)

- Each era typically lasts ~22 years; four-era cycle lasts roughly 80-100 years
- Everyone who lives a normal lifespan experiences every constellational era once, each from different phase-of-life perspective

- **Missionaries**: spirituals and blues (soulful, angry)
- **Lost**: jazz (improvised, naughty)

- **Silent**: folk and rock (subversive, social conscience)
- **Boomers**: inward-seeking, tinged with fury ("acid" and "Jesus" rock)

### Social Indicators and Generational Cycles

**Substance abuse**: rises during Awakening, peaks near its end, falls when Idealists enter midlife. Young Idealists get most euphoria while society looks on indulgently; young Reactives get most blame when crackdown begins.

> "A person growing up in the 1890s and the 1970s would have the image of a drug-tolerating society; in the 1940s—and perhaps the 2000s—a nation that firmly rejects narcotics." — historian **David Musto**

**Fertility**: tends to rise during Outer-Driven eras (Puritans, Awakeners, Transcendentals, Boomers all products of birth booms); eases during Awakening eras

**Immigration**: rises during Awakening, peaks during Inner-Driven, falls during Crisis—reflecting shifting consensus about pluralism and community

### The Four Era Moods

**Awakening Era**: Society transitions into spiritual awakening; focus on inner life intensifies; artistic culture highly innovative; institutions attacked and weakened; wars unlikely and controversial; sex roles narrow; crime and substance abuse rise; child-rearing at minimum protection; defining dialogue between coming-of-age **Idealists** and aging **Civics**

**Inner-Driven Era**: Satisfaction with personal life high; individualism flourishes; cultural life fragments amid growing inequality; confidence in institutions at cyclical nadir; frenetic lifestyles emerge alongside growing seriousness of purpose; perceptions of future darken; wars fought with moral fervor but without consensus; child-rearing moving back toward protection; defining dialogue between rising **Reactives** and midlife **Idealists**

**Crisis Era**: Grim preoccupation with outer-world peril; artistic culture avoids nuance; worldly problems congeal into struggle requiring total consensus and sacrifice; wars fought with fury and efficacy; sex roles widen; substance abuse declines; child-rearing at maximum protection; defining dialogue between coming-of-age **Civics** and aging **Idealists**

**Outer-Driven Era**: Community reaches cyclical peak; individualists ostracized; ideals secularized and institutionalized; emphasis on planning, doing, building

### The Cycle of Generational Types

### The IDEALIST Lifecycle (Prophetic)

**Youth**: Grows up in post-crisis era of vigorous Civic midlifers; indulged by parents in world of secularism and conformism; older generations hope children can bring richer moral dimension

**Rising Adulthood**: Spiritual awakening—challenges elders' behavior as "intolerably deficient in moral worth"; aging Civics cede values agenda; encourages individual autonomy, resists social cooperation

**Midlife**: Retrenches to perfect inner life; gradually reshapes institutions around new values; assumes judgmental stance against younger Reactives; splits emerge between competing moral factions

**Elderhood**: One faction prevails, setting agenda for decisive action; leaders of great moral authority impose will sternly; call on younger generations to sacrifice; society passes through secular crisis

### The REACTIVE Lifecycle (Picaresque)

**Youth**: Powerful Civic elders, moralizing Idealist rising adults; children given little sense of mission; adults view them harshly—at best inconvenient, at worst wicked

**Rising Adulthood**: Strong survival skills and high hopes for individual success; social and economic entrepreneurship; pleasure-seeking and high-risk behavior

**Midlife**: Playing to win but half-expecting to lose; accept wide gaps in personal outcomes; become society's most cunning, pragmatic, colorful public figures; effective crisis managers

**Elderhood**: Undemanding in post-crisis era; compensate for earlier binge by shunning risk; wary conservatives, "not born yesterday"

### The CIVIC Lifecycle (Heroic)

**Youth**: Idealist parents view them as special instrument; child environment perceived as dangerous, pushed toward protection and structure; develop clear collective mission

**Rising Adulthood**: Activity-oriented peer relationships; band together at historic moment guided by elder Idealists and midlife Reactives; successfully shoulder secular crisis; receive generous praise

**Midlife**: Builders and doers; push aside aging Reactives; try to rationalize every sphere of social life; midlife hubris encourages overreach; elicit stormy crusade from coming-of-age Idealists

**Elderhood**: Frustrated that new spiritual agenda saps institutions; detach from cultural trends but retain active public role; seek institutional power and economic reward more than personal respect

### The ADAPTIVE Lifecycle (Genteel)

**Youth**: Secular crisis erupts; Reactive-led families surround with intensely protective nurture; children expected to stay out of harm's way; widening sex-role distinctions

**Rising Adulthood**: Emulate successful adult behavior; not old enough for crisis catharsis, fail to acquire next-elders' self-confidence; probe for fresher role through cult of expertise and gestures of conscience

**Midlife**: Awkward "passage" forced to choose between outer mission of Civics and inner mission of Idealists; compensate for earlier conformism with high-risk political and family behavior; rely on expertise, process, pluralism

**Elderhood**: Personally flexible, culturally sensitive; trusting liberals—believers in inherent goodness of man; adopt agenda of younger Idealists; preserve social conscience, resilient spirit, never stop raising questions

## Chapter 13: Completing the Millennial Cycle

### Historical Sparks and National Crisis Response

- History contains many sparks -- some ignite major conflagrations, others fade without consequence
- Key question: How would America respond to a terrorist attack on New York City?
- Answer depends on **when** the event occurs -- specifically, during which **constellational era**

### Crisis Response During Awakening Era

**Hypothetical scenario**: terrorist threat during last **Awakening** (around 1970)
- Cross-currents of **secular confidence** and **spiritual rebellion**
- **Leaders** in their fifties would downplay the threat as hubristic crisis managers
- **Angry young Boomers** would organize behind symbolic and emotional responses, accuse U.S. government of lying and blame it on "some monstrous institutional plot"
- **Thirtyish Silent** would be puzzled why any foreigner could hate America so much
- America would be "at war with itself more than with the perpetrators"

### Crisis Response During Inner-Driven Era (early 1990s)

- Response would emphasize caution, conciliation, deferral
- **Silent cabinet officers** would consult elderly GIs, form committees, review options, invite public discussion
- Would hardly ruffle young 13ers (many might rush forward to sell/volunteer)
- Nation would "squeeze by the immediate threat undamaged, but leave its underlying causes either unsolved or, at best, mildly ameliorated"

### Crisis Response During Crisis Era (year 2020)

- "Unrecognizable" from today's perspective
- **Boomer leaders** (in sixties) would broadly define the threat, demand total defeat regardless of human/economic sacrifices
- Evacuation would be mannerly, with cooperative **Millennial youths** seeking and accepting orders from elders and pragmatic midlife 13ers
- Nation would act "promptly and decisively as a single organism"

**Key insight**: Impact on history comes less from *act of terrorism itself* than from the *response* it would provoke

### Unpredictability of Future Crises

- Cannot predict specific early twenty-first century troubles: revolution in Latin America, belligerent Third World fundamentalism, AIDS, global warming/ozone depletion, fossil fuel exhaustion, high-tech genetics abuse, trade wars, debt-fueled financial crisis
- Nobody around 1745 and 1910 could have predicted Boston Tea Party or Pearl Harbor Day

### Generational Cycle and Forecasting Social Behavior

- Experts can project technical problems but are helpless before mysteries of human nature: how people will **respond** to those problems
- Energy forecasters assumed Americans in the 21st century would act the same as in the "Me Decade" 1970s
- **Straight-line projections** can be way off the mark -- equivalent of forecasting how the Civil War would be waged by studying the speeches of Henry Clay

> "History tells us one thing for certain about social behavior: It never remains the same for long. The realistic question is not whether it will change, but how and when."

### Qualitative Insight of the Generational Cycle

- The generational cycle helps assess **future constellational moods** and **the peer personalities of today's generations as they age into new phases of life**
- Historical responses to threats at analogous moments: **Chicago Haymarket Riot of 1886** (Awakening), sinking of the **Lusitania in 1915** (Inner-Driven), attack on **Pearl Harbor in 1941** (Crisis)
- In each example, **history determined less by sparklike event itself than by mood of constellational era in which it occurred**

### History Follows Patterns, Not Invariant Outcomes

- Any era can bring forward good or bad leaders, good or bad choices
- **Constellational eras and generational lifecycles follow predictable patterns**, within which each generation has a limited choice of scripts
- During **social moments -- secular crises especially** -- those scripts become more fateful, more determinative of history, more likely to result in triumph or tragedy
- The **Millennial Cycle** already began with the **Boom Awakening** of the late 1960s and 1970s
- The next social moment -- the **"Crisis of 2020"** -- is due to begin when the Millennial Cycle has run about halfway through its course

## GLOSSARY

**COHORT**: All persons born in the same year.

**PHASES OF LIFE**: Twenty-two-year age brackets:
- **Elderhood** (age 66+): stewardship
- **Midlife** (age 44-65): leadership
- **Rising Adulthood** (age 22-43): activity
- **Youth** (age 0-21): dependence

**GENERATION**: A cohort-group whose length approximates a phase of life, with boundaries fixed by peer personality.

**PEER PERSONALITY**: Recognized and determined by (1) common age location; (2) common beliefs and behavior; (3) perceived membership in a common generation.

**SOCIAL MOMENT**: A brief era (~decade) when people perceive historic events radically altering their social environment:
- **Secular Crisis**: reordering the outer world of institutions and public behavior
- **Spiritual Awakening**: changing the inner world of values and private behavior

**DOMINANT GENERATION** (Idealist or Civic): encounters social moments entering rising adulthood and again entering elderhood.
**RECESSIVE GENERATION** (Adaptive or Reactive): encounters social moments entering youth and again entering midlife.

**FOUR GENERATIONAL TYPES** (recurring in fixed order):
- **Idealist**: spiritual awakening in rising adulthood, secular crisis in elderhood
- **Reactive**: spiritual awakening in youth, secular crisis in midlife
- **Civic**: secular crisis in rising adulthood, spiritual awakening in elderhood
- **Adaptive**: secular crisis in youth, spiritual awakening in midlife

**GENERATIONAL CYCLE**: A set of consecutive generations beginning with Idealist and ending with Adaptive; alternately, constellational eras from Awakening to Outer-Driven.

**ENDOWMENT**: Any enduring social legacy, helpful or hurtful, that an older generation passes on to younger generations.

## Appendix A: A Theory of Generations

### Ancient Roots of Generational Thinking

- At dawn of recorded history, **standard measure of cosmic time** was not year or century but **the generation**
- **Hesiod** (8th century B.C.): **five sequential ages of mankind** -- Gold, Silver, Bronze, Heroic, and Iron
- **Old Testament** begins with "Genesis" and measures time with a generational procession

### Family vs. Cohort Generation

- **Family generation**: set of all children "brought into being" by a father -- useful for lineage, but limited for historical context
- **Cohort generation**: everyone "brought into being" at the same historical moment -- shares common age location and peer personality
- **Philip Greven's** *Four Generations* (1970) demonstrated this limitation: his strictly genealogical "fourth generation" stretched births over more than a century

### Modern Emergence of Generational Interest

- Special interest in the cohort generation awaited belief that history had **speed and direction** -- arrived in late 18th/early 19th century
- **Thomas Jefferson** (1789): concluded a generation = ~18.2 years
- **Auguste Comte, John Stuart Mill, Emile Littre**: broadened from political generations to complex drivers behind all social change
- **Mill**: "In the filiation of one generation to another, it is the whole which produces the whole, rather than any part a part"
- **Giuseppe Ferrari**: developed penetrating model of **generational cycles** in 1840s-1870s (received little contemporary recognition)
- **Dilthey** insisted on two separate definitions: (1) span of time and (2) relationship of contemporaneity

### The "Generations Problem" Today

- Since 1920s, no cohort-group has come fully of age without encountering attempts to name it
- **"Baby Boom Generation"** became synonymous with cohort-group whose tastes marketers pay millions to understand
- Central unanswered questions: *What exactly* is a generation? *How* does it develop distinct peer personality? *Why* do they arrive in cycles?
- Proposed lengths vary wildly: Lorenz/Cournot 33 years, Comte/Dilthey 25, Littre 33.3, Jefferson 18.2, Ortega y Gasset 15

### A Journey to Cohortia

- Authors invite readers to mythical land of **Cohortia** -- a traditional society with well-defined, relatively unchanging lifecycle
- Four basic life phase roles (each ~22 years): **Youth** (dependence), **Rising Adults** (activity), **Midlife** (leadership), **Elders** (stewardship)

**Social moments shape generations:**
- Any social moment affects personality differently according to current **phase of life**
- Four adjacent cohort-groups separately coalesce into generations, each with distinct peer personality
- **Length of generations depends on length of phases of life**

### Modern Cohortia: Two Key Assumptions

**First**: A social moment not only shapes personality but forges an **enduring bond of identity** between cohort-group and its role -- an acquired style that redefines how each group will later regard itself

**Second**: A central role acquired during a social moment can extend into the **next life phase but not the one after that** -- even a modern Odysseus cannot sustain society's active role in old age

This creates **two basic types of generational lifecycles**:
- **Dominant generations** (D, F, H): encounter social moments entering rising adulthood; always dominate major social change
- **Recessive generations** (E, G, I): encounter social moments in youth; styles overshadowed by more potent personalities

### Cross-Generational Rejuvenation

> "During a new social moment, each generation will redefine the central role of the phase of life it is entering in a direction that reverses the perceived excesses of that role since the last social moment."

> "Each generation has a formative, nurturing relationship primarily with other two-apart generations (dominant with dominant, recessive with recessive); each tries to cultivate in the second-younger generation a peer personality it perceives as complementary to its own."

### The Four-Part Generational Cycle in the Exodus

Four distinct peer personalities in the biblical narrative:
1. **Aaron/Moses peers** (Idealist): awakened people to the spirit of one true God, led Hebrews through wilderness
2. **"Stiff-necked" backsliders** (Reactive): worshipers of golden calf, "murmurers" and "men of little faith"
3. **Joshua's soldier-peers** (Civic): born after Exodus, came of age in victorious battles, conquered Canaan
4. **Original Judges** (Adaptive): invisible children overshadowed by war, political fragmentation in midlife

- **Remarkable correspondences** across theories and myths spanning **three millennia**
- Four-part generational cycle has never fully emerged from shadowy speculation into established intellectual tradition
- Authors suggest: **"Perhaps, in time, it will."**
