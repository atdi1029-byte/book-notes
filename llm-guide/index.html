1. How LLMs Actually Work

The mental model that changes how you think about everything Claude does.

The One-Sentence Version

A Large Language Model is a program that predicts the next word. That's literally it. Everything Claude does — writing code, explaining concepts, building your apps, having conversations — comes from one ability: given everything written so far, what word most likely comes next?

That probably sounds too simple to explain all the amazing things Claude can do. So let's dig deeper.

Training: How Claude Learned

Before Claude ever talks to you, it went through a massive training process. Think of three phases:

Phase 1: Reading the Internet

Claude was trained on enormous amounts of text — books, websites, code repositories, documentation, academic papers, forums, conversations. Billions and billions of words. During this phase, the model learned patterns: how English grammar works, how JavaScript syntax works, what comes after function, how to structure an HTML file, what a for-loop does, how people explain things, and millions of other patterns.

Analogy
Imagine you read every cookbook ever written — thousands of them. After a while, you'd "know" that after "preheat the oven to" the next word is probably a temperature. You'd know that recipes have ingredients, then steps. You'd know that "fold" means something different in baking than in laundry. Claude does this, but for everything — code, language, math, logic, science, history.

Phase 2: Learning to Be Helpful (RLHF)

Raw text prediction makes a chatbot that just generates plausible-sounding text — not necessarily helpful text. So Anthropic added a second training phase called RLHF (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback). Human trainers rated thousands of Claude's responses: "this one was helpful," "this one was wrong," "this one was too long," "this one nailed it."

Over time, Claude learned not just to predict text, but to predict helpful text. This is why Claude writes clean code with comments, explains what it changed, asks clarifying questions, and tries to match your style — it was rewarded for doing these things during training.

Phase 3: Safety Training

Anthropic also trained Claude to refuse harmful requests, avoid generating dangerous content, and be honest when it doesn't know something. This is why Claude sometimes says "I'm not sure" instead of making something up, and why it won't help with certain requests.

How Prediction Actually Works

When you type "add a delete button to the workout history," Claude doesn't search a database of pre-written code. Instead, it generates its response one token at a time, each one based on everything that came before:

Each token is predicted based on everything before it — your message, instructions, and all previous tokens in the response.

This is why Claude can sometimes start a response well but go off track halfway through. Each prediction depends on the last one. If prediction #47 goes slightly wrong, predictions #48-200 might all follow that wrong direction — like making a wrong turn on a highway and ending up in the wrong city.

Tokens: How Claude Reads and Writes

Claude doesn't read letters or whole words — it reads tokens. A token is a chunk of text, roughly 3/4 of a word. Some examples:

  • hello → 1 token
  • basketball → 2 tokens ("basket" + "ball")
  • localStorage.getItem → ~5 tokens
  • A typical paragraph → ~75-100 tokens
  • Your Zercher index.html → ~25,000 tokens
  • Your Bloom index.html → ~20,000 tokens
  • Your entire CLAUDE.md + MEMORY.md → ~6,000 tokens (loaded every session!)

Why does this matter? Because everything has a token cost. Think of tokens as Claude's currency — it can only spend so many per conversation before running out of space.

What LLMs Are NOT

Understanding what Claude isn't is just as important as understanding what it is:

  • Not a search engine — Claude doesn't look things up in real time. Its knowledge comes from training data (with a cutoff date). When it reads your files, that's using tools, not "knowing" something.
  • Not a database — Claude doesn't store your conversations or remember past sessions (unless you use Memory files). Each conversation starts fresh.
  • Not deterministic — ask Claude the same question twice and you might get different answers. There's randomness built into the prediction process.
  • Not "thinking" — Claude is pattern matching at an incredibly sophisticated level. It produces text that looks like thinking, reasoning, and understanding. The practical effect is similar to thinking (it solves problems, writes working code), but the mechanism is prediction, not cognition.
Key Concept
Claude is the world's best autocomplete.
Incredibly powerful, but fundamentally a prediction engine.

Why Claude Makes Mistakes

Now you can understand why Claude sometimes does strange things:

  • "Hallucinations" — Claude generates plausible-sounding but wrong information. This happens because prediction doesn't require truth — it requires what sounds right based on patterns. "The capital of Australia is Sydney" sounds right (it's not — it's Canberra).
  • Confident mistakes — Claude doesn't "know" it's wrong because it doesn't have a fact-checker. It's generating text that matches patterns. The wrong answer might have a very high probability.
  • Losing track mid-response — Long responses compound prediction errors. Token #500 depends on tokens #1-499. If anything in that chain went slightly wrong, the rest follows the error.
  • Repeating itself — when the context gets long and compressed, Claude may lose track of what it already said and predict the same content again.

Prediction in Action: Summarizing a Book New

Here's a question that trips people up: if Claude is "just predicting the next word," how does it actually read and summarize a 300-page book? Isn't it just making stuff up?

The answer comes down to what's in the context window. When your book notes are sitting in Claude's context, every prediction is conditioned on that actual text. Claude isn't pulling from vague training memories — it's working from the real words right in front of it.

Analogy
Imagine you're taking an open-book exam. You have the textbook right there on your desk. Your answers aren't coming from fuzzy memories of reading it last week — you're looking at the actual pages. That's what Claude does when your notes are in context. The source material dominates the prediction.

Think about how this works step by step. Claude sees your notes plus the instruction "summarize Chapter 5." During training, it saw millions of examples where dense text was followed by concise summaries. So the highest-probability next tokens form a summary of what's actually there — not a copy, not an invention, but a compression based on learned patterns for how to distill ideas.

Each predicted word then shifts what comes next. If Claude starts with "This chapter argues that..." the most probable continuation is a synthesis, not a repetition. Each token steers the next one down the summarization path.

This is why prediction produces surprisingly good analysis. During training, Claude learned:

  • Compression patterns — how to distill 10 paragraphs into 2 sentences without losing the core idea
  • Hierarchical importance — which ideas are central arguments vs. supporting evidence
  • Structural conventions — what a "key takeaway" looks like vs. a "quote" vs. a "framework"

Where It Can Go Wrong

Grounding in context isn't perfect. There are three situations where Claude might fabricate instead of summarize:

  • Gaps in the input — if a page was skipped or a section is missing, Claude doesn't know there's a hole. It smooths over the gap with something plausible but potentially invented. This is exactly why the book project rules say "never skip pages."
  • Context overflow — if the text is too long and gets truncated or compressed, Claude loses access to parts of it. Now it's predicting from incomplete information — like taking that open-book exam but someone ripped out Chapter 7.
  • Over-generalizing — Claude might phrase something slightly different from what the author actually said. Not wrong, but not precise. The prediction engine reaches for the "most likely" phrasing rather than the exact one.

The notes come from the actual pages, not Claude's training memory. Then when building the final HTML, the notes are in context, so the chapter prose is grounded in real content, not hallucination. Every step keeps the source material front and center.

The Transformer Architecture (Optional Deep Dive)

How does the prediction actually happen inside the model? ▶

Claude is built on a transformer architecture — a specific type of neural network invented in 2017 by Google researchers. The key innovation is called attention.

When Claude is predicting the next token, it doesn't weigh all previous tokens equally. The attention mechanism lets Claude "focus" on the most relevant parts of the input. If you said "change the background color in Zercher" 500 tokens ago, the attention mechanism helps Claude focus on "Zercher" and "background color" when generating CSS code, even though there's a lot of other text in between.

The model has billions of "parameters" — numbers that were adjusted during training. These parameters encode the patterns Claude learned. When you hear "Claude Opus" vs "Claude Haiku," the main difference is the number of parameters — more parameters means more capacity for complex patterns, but slower responses.

Model Sizes (Roughly)

  • Haiku — smaller, fast, good for simple tasks. Like asking an intern to do something straightforward.
  • Sonnet — medium, balanced. The daily workhorse for most coding.
  • Opus — largest, most capable, slowest. Best for complex reasoning, architecture decisions, and ambitious tasks. This is what you use in Claude Code.

The reason Opus is better at complex tasks isn't that it "tries harder" — it literally has more parameters to encode more nuanced patterns, so its predictions are more sophisticated.

Embeddings: How Claude "Understands" Meaning

Here's something that'll blow your mind. Claude doesn't store words as text — it converts every token into a list of numbers called an embedding. Each token gets mapped to a point in a high-dimensional space (think: a coordinate on a map, but with hundreds of dimensions instead of just X and Y).

The magic: tokens with similar meanings end up near each other in this space. "Dog" and "puppy" are close together. "Dog" and "refrigerator" are far apart. "JavaScript" and "Python" are closer to each other than either is to "Shakespeare."

Related words cluster together. Claude uses these spatial relationships to understand meaning — "ETF" is closer to "portfolio" than to "puppy."

This is why Claude can follow context across your conversation. When you say "add DCA calculation," Claude's embeddings know that "DCA" is close to "dollar cost averaging," "portfolio," "ETF," and "investment." It activates the right cluster of knowledge automatically. You don't have to explain what DCA means — Claude's embeddings already encode that relationship.

Attention: How Claude Focuses

Remember: Claude sees your entire conversation at once, plus CLAUDE.md, plus any files it read. That could be 100,000 tokens. How does it know which parts matter for the current prediction?

The attention mechanism is the answer. At each prediction step, Claude assigns a "relevance score" to every previous token. High score = "this is relevant to what I'm generating right now." Low score = "this is background noise."

When generating CSS for "change Zercher header color," Claude pays high attention to "Zercher," "header," "color," and the relevant CSS in the file — while ignoring Bloom info, book project rules, and unrelated JS functions.

This is why specificity in your prompts matters. When you say "change the header color in Zercher to #1a2634," the attention mechanism can laser-focus on exactly the right tokens. When you say "change some colors," attention has to spread across many more possibilities, making the prediction less precise.

Multi-Head Attention: Thinking Multiple Things at Once

Claude doesn't just have one attention mechanism — it has many running in parallel, called attention heads. Each head can focus on a different aspect of the input simultaneously:

  • Head 1 might focus on syntax — "we're inside a CSS rule, so the next token should be a CSS property"
  • Head 2 might focus on your request — "user wants header color changed"
  • Head 3 might focus on the existing code — "the current color is #141820"
  • Head 4 might focus on coding conventions — "hex colors are 6 characters"

All of these run at the same time and their outputs combine to produce the final prediction. This is why Claude can juggle multiple constraints simultaneously — following your instructions, matching existing code style, maintaining correct syntax, and producing the right output all at once.

2. The Context Window

This is the single most important concept for how you work with Claude. Understanding it will immediately improve your results.

What It Is

The context window is Claude's working memory. It's the total amount of text Claude can "see" at once. Everything — your messages, Claude's responses, files it read, tool results, your CLAUDE.md instructions — must fit inside this window.

For Claude Opus 4.6 (what you use), the context window is about 200,000 tokens. That sounds like a lot — it's roughly 150,000 words, or about 500 pages of a book. But it fills up faster than you'd think.

What Eats Your Context

Here's a rough breakdown of token costs for things you do every day:

Token costs of common actions ▶
  • A short message from you: ~50-100 tokens
  • A detailed prompt: ~200-500 tokens
  • Claude's typical response: ~500-2000 tokens
  • Reading a small file (100 lines): ~500-1000 tokens
  • Reading Zercher index.html: ~25,000 tokens
  • Reading Bloom index.html: ~20,000 tokens
  • A git diff after changes: ~1,000-5,000 tokens
  • Your CLAUDE.md + MEMORY.md: ~6,000 tokens (loaded every session!)
  • Claude running 5 grep searches: ~2,000-3,000 tokens

So imagine a typical session: you load CLAUDE.md (6K), Claude reads your app (20K), you go back and forth 15 times (30K), Claude reads the file again after compression (20K), more back and forth (20K) — you're already at ~100K tokens, half the window used.

Context Compression

When the window gets close to full, Claude Code automatically compresses older messages. It creates a summary of earlier conversation and throws away the full text.

This is like someone summarizing a meeting: "We discussed the bug in the sync function and decided to use fireGet instead of POST" is the compressed version of a 20-message back-and-forth with code snippets, error messages, and detailed discussion.

The summary keeps the gist but loses the details. This is why:

  • Claude re-reads files it already read (the details were compressed away)
  • Claude forgets specific decisions you made earlier
  • Claude's code quality drops in long sessions (less room for its response)
  • Claude might repeat itself (it doesn't "remember" it already said that)
The #1 Rule for Better Results
Start a fresh conversation for each distinct task. If you've been working on Zercher for an hour and now want to switch to Bloom, open a new session. A clean context focused on one thing will always produce better results than a cluttered one juggling multiple topics.

Why CLAUDE.md Survives Compression

Here's something important: your CLAUDE.md and MEMORY.md are loaded at the start of every conversation, as part of the system context. They're treated as instructions, not conversation history. This means they're never compressed — Claude always has access to your rules.

This is why putting important rules in CLAUDE.md is so much more reliable than saying them in conversation. Anything you say in chat will eventually get compressed. Anything in CLAUDE.md stays forever.

Analogy
CLAUDE.md is like a sticky note on Claude's monitor — it's always visible. Conversation messages are like Post-its on a crowded desk — eventually they get buried under newer ones.

Practical Tips for Context Management

  1. One task per session — new feature = new conversation. Bug fix = new conversation.
  2. Front-load your request — put the most important details in your first message, not spread across 5 messages.
  3. Don't ask Claude to read huge files unless necessary — "read lines 200-300 of index.html" is cheaper than "read index.html."
  4. If Claude starts acting weird, start fresh — it's not a bug, the context is full. A new session fixes it instantly.
  5. Complex tasks that take many messages? Consider breaking them into multiple sessions — do the planning in one, implementation in another.

3. Why Claude Does What It Does

Understanding the instruction stack, temperature, and Claude's "personality" so you can steer it better.

The Instruction Stack

When you type a message, Claude isn't just reading your words. It's reading a whole stack of instructions layered on top of each other. Think of it like a chain of command:

Higher layers take priority. CLAUDE.md rules override your casual conversation if they conflict.

This priority ordering matters. If you casually say "add some extra features" but your CLAUDE.md says "avoid over-engineering, only make changes directly requested," Claude will lean toward the CLAUDE.md instruction because it has higher authority.

This is actually great for you — your CLAUDE.md acts as a safety net that prevents Claude from going off-script even when your prompts are loose.

Temperature: Creativity vs. Predictability

Remember how Claude picks the most probable next token? There's a setting called temperature that controls how strictly Claude follows probability:

  • Low temperature (0.0-0.3) — almost always picks the #1 most probable token. Very predictable, consistent, but can be repetitive. Used for code generation.
  • Medium temperature (0.5-0.7) — usually picks from the top few options. Balanced creativity and accuracy. Used for conversation.
  • High temperature (0.8-1.0) — more willing to pick less likely tokens. More creative and surprising, but more likely to go off-track.

Claude Code uses lower temperature for code (you want predictable, correct code) and a bit higher for conversation. You don't control this directly, but understanding it explains why:

  • Claude's code is usually consistent and formulaic (low temp = safe choices)
  • Claude's explanations might vary if you ask the same question twice (higher temp = different word choices each time)
  • Asking Claude to "be creative" actually works — it nudges the internal temperature behavior toward more diverse outputs

Why Claude Over-Helps

One of Claude's most common behaviors: you ask for one thing, and Claude does that thing plus three more things you didn't ask for. Why?

During training, Claude was rewarded for being thorough. Trainers preferred responses that anticipated needs and went above and beyond. So Claude learned: "when asked to add a button, also add hover effects, accessibility attributes, and a loading state." Helpful in theory, annoying when you want a simple change.

Your CLAUDE.md already fights this with rules like "avoid over-engineering" and "only make changes directly requested." But you can also steer it in conversation:

Gets extras
"add a delete button to workout cards"

Claude adds: button, confirmation dialog, undo toast, animation, aria labels...

Gets exactly what you want
"add a delete button to workout cards. just the button, simple red X, no confirmation dialog."

Claude adds: exactly one red X button.

Over-Helping: Real Examples From Your Apps

This isn't hypothetical — here are patterns you've probably seen Claude do in your projects:

  • You asked: "hide the Need 2 Day button on mobile in the crypto dashboard."
    Claude might also: refactor the entire mobile media query section, add smooth transitions, reorganize the CSS, and "improve" the header layout. When all you wanted was one line: .need-2day-btn { display: none !important; }
  • You asked: "add cloud sync to Roman Chariots."
    Claude might also: add a settings UI for the sync URL, add a manual "Sync Now" button, add a sync status indicator, add retry logic with exponential backoff, add conflict resolution with a diff viewer. When you wanted: fireGet + jsonpFetch, same pattern as Bloom.
  • You asked: "add a daily tip to Roman Chariots."
    Claude might also: add a "favorites" system for tips, a tip history viewer, tip categories, tip sharing, and tip search. When you wanted: one random tip per day, no repeats, that's it.

The fix is always the same: be explicit about scope. "Just this, nothing else" is your best friend. And your CLAUDE.md rule about avoiding over-engineering catches most of this automatically.

Why Claude Apologizes and Hedges

You've probably noticed Claude says things like "I apologize for the confusion" or "you're right, let me fix that." This is also from training — Claude was rewarded for being agreeable and acknowledging mistakes. It's not actually sorry (it doesn't have feelings). It's producing text that matches the pattern of "helpful assistant who acknowledges errors."

This is mostly fine, but occasionally Claude will agree with you when you're wrong, just because it's trained to be agreeable. If you say "I think the bug is in the CSS" and the bug is actually in JavaScript, Claude might start by looking at CSS just to agree with you.

Pro Tip
When debugging, describe the symptoms ("the button doesn't do anything when I tap") rather than your theory ("the CSS is broken"). Let Claude form its own theory about the cause. It's better at diagnosing when it's not trying to confirm your guess.

The Knowledge Cutoff

Claude's training data has a cutoff date (May 2025 for the current model). It doesn't know about events, libraries, or APIs released after that. However, Claude Code has tools that let it:

  • Read your files (so it sees your current code, even if it was written after the cutoff)
  • Run commands (so it can check versions, run tests)
  • Search the web (when needed)

So while Claude's "built-in knowledge" has a cutoff, its access to tools makes it effectively current for the work you do with it.

This is incredibly powerful. It means:

  • Claude follows your CLAUDE.md rules even if you forget to mention them
  • Rules survive across sessions — you write them once, they apply forever
  • You can prevent past mistakes from recurring by adding rules

Your CLAUDE.md is genuinely one of the best I've seen. Here's what makes it effective:

Problem: Descriptions vs. Rules

Some entries describe what an app does instead of telling Claude what to do with it. Claude doesn't need to know that Cadence has "night/day mode toggle" unless that information helps it avoid a mistake.

The CLAUDE.md Test

For every line, ask: "Would Claude make a mistake without this line?"

  • "ALWAYS bump service worker" → YES, Claude would forget. Keep it.
  • "Uses Apps Script backend + fireGet pattern" → MAYBE, Claude could figure this out from reading the code. Consider moving to a topic file.
  • "Categories: personal, errands, work..." → NO, Claude doesn't need this to work on Cadence. Delete it.

CLAUDE.md vs. MEMORY.md vs. Topic Files

You have three layers of persistent instructions:

  • CLAUDE.md — lives in your project root. Loaded every conversation. Best for: project-wide rules, critical do/don't instructions, workflows.
  • MEMORY.md — in your ~/.claude/projects/.../memory/ directory. Also loaded every conversation. Best for: quick-reference context about all your projects. Keep it lean.
  • Topic files — zercher.md, sora.md, etc. Only loaded when Claude reads them. Best for: detailed project info that's only needed when working on that specific app.

The key insight: CLAUDE.md and MEMORY.md eat tokens every single conversation. Topic files only eat tokens when relevant. So put detailed stuff in topic files and keep the always-loaded files minimal.

Zercher: How Cloud Sync Works

This is one of the most important patterns across all your apps. Here's the real cloud sync from Zercher: 

function syncConfigToCloud() {
  if (!syncReady) return;     // ① guard: don't push during initial load
  try {
    const config = {           // ② build payload
      activeProgram: data.activeProgram,
      customPrograms: syncPrograms,
      settings: data.settings,
      prs: data.prs || {}
    };
    const jsonStr = JSON.stringify(config);
    const encoded = encodeURIComponent(jsonStr);

    if (encoded.length < 7000) {   // ③ small enough for one request?
      jsonpFetch(                    // ④ send it!
        SYNC_ENDPOINT + '?action=zercher_save_config&data='
        + encoded, function(){}
      );
    } else {
      pushChunked(jsonStr, 'config'); // ⑤ too big → split into chunks
    }
  } catch(e) { console.warn('Config sync failed', e); }
}

Let's break this down step by step:

  1. ① syncReady guard — When the app first opens, it loads data from the cloud. While it's loading, you don't want it to also push the (possibly empty) local data to the cloud and overwrite good data. The syncReady flag starts as false and only becomes true after the initial load completes. This prevents the "opened app on new phone → wiped all my data" bug.
  2. ② Build the payload — Gathers just the data that needs syncing into a clean object. Not everything gets synced — only the important stuff.
  3. ③ Size check — URLs have a length limit (~8000 characters). If your data is small enough, it fits in a single URL. If not, it gets split into multiple requests (chunks).
  4. ④ jsonpFetch — Sends the data to your Apps Script backend (more on this below).
  5. ⑤ Chunked push — For large data, breaks it into pieces and sends them one at a time, then tells the server to reassemble them.

How jsonpFetch Actually Works

This is the workhorse function that appears in every one of your apps. Let's demystify it: 

function jsonpFetch(url, cb) {
  // ① Create a unique callback name
  const cbName = '_zrCb_' + Date.now()
    + '_' + Math.random().toString(36).substr(2,5);

  // ② Create a script tag
  const script = document.createElement('script');

  // ③ When the script loads, it calls this function
  window[cbName] = function(resp) {
    delete window[cbName]; // clean up
    script.remove();         // remove script tag
    cb(null, resp);         // return data to caller
  };

  // ④ Set the URL with callback parameter
  script.src = url + '&callback=' + cbName;

  // ⑤ Timeout safety net
  setTimeout(function() {
    delete window[cbName];
    script.remove();
    cb('Timeout');
  }, 15000);

  // ⑥ GO! Add script to page, browser fetches it
  document.head.appendChild(script);
}
Why Not Just Use Fetch?
Normal fetch() requests run into CORS restrictions — your phone's browser says "I don't trust this cross-origin request" and blocks it. But browsers never block loading a script tag. JSONP exploits this: your Apps Script wraps the response data in a function call, delivers it as a "script," and the browser runs it happily. It's a clever workaround that's been used for decades. Your fireGet() pattern is even simpler — it creates an invisible image load, which also bypasses CORS.

Common Bug Patterns in Your Apps

Stale cache on phone (most common!) ▶

Symptoms: You pushed changes but your phone shows the old version.

Cause: Service worker version wasn't bumped, so the phone's cached version is still being served.

Fix:

  1. Make sure service worker version was bumped in the push
  2. ADB cachebust: adb shell "am start -a android.intent.action.VIEW -d 'https://SITE/?cachebust=DATE'"

Prevention: Your CLAUDE.md rule "ALWAYS bump service worker" handles this, but double-check before pushing.

CORS / Apps Script silent failure ▶

Symptoms: Data writes work on desktop but silently fail on mobile. No error message — it just doesn't save.

Cause: Apps Script redirects POST requests, and mobile browsers handle CORS differently than desktop.

Fix: Use fireGet() (image-based GET request) instead of POST/fetch for all writes to Apps Script.

Prevention: Every new cloud sync implementation should use fireGet from day one. This is why you have the "EVERY new PWA MUST have fireGet from day one" rule.

localStorage disappearing ▶

Symptoms: App data is gone — workouts, portfolios, budget data vanished.

Cause: Chrome update, storage pressure, or (worst case) someone cleared Chrome data/cache.

Fix: Cloud sync restores data from the server. Every app should have auto-pull on load.

Prevention: Your cloud sync pattern with auto-push, auto-pull, and empty data guards. This is exactly why every app needs cloud sync — localStorage is not reliable long-term storage.

Number parsing issues (NaN) ▶

Symptoms: Prices, totals, or calculations show "NaN" (Not a Number) or "$undefined".

Cause: Usually locale formatting — numbers with commas ("1,234.56") break parseFloat() which expects "1234.56".

Fix: str.replace(/,/g, '') before parsing. Strip commas first, then convert to number.

Prevention: Your CLAUDE.md has this as a common pitfall. Claude should catch this automatically now.

10. How Claude Code Tools Work

Behind every file read, every edit, every git push — there's a tool. Understanding what's happening behind the scenes.

What Are Tools?

Claude on its own can only generate text. It can't read files, edit code, run commands, or interact with your computer. To do those things, Claude uses tools — external functions that it can "call" during a conversation.

When Claude wants to read a file, it doesn't have magic access. It generates a special message that says "I want to use the Read tool on this file." The system executes that tool, gets the file contents, and feeds them back to Claude as text in the context window.

The Tools Claude Uses

When you watch Claude work, you see messages like "Let me read that file" or "I'll run the tests." Behind each of those is a specific tool:

Read Tool

Reads a file from your computer and brings the contents into Claude's context window. When Claude says "Let me read index.html," the entire file (or a portion) gets added to the conversation as tokens.

Context cost: High. A 3,000-line file might be 15,000+ tokens. This is why Claude sometimes reads just a portion of a large file (lines 200-300) instead of the whole thing.

Edit Tool

Makes surgical changes to files. Claude specifies the exact text to find and what to replace it with. It's like find-and-replace but for code. When you see "I'll update the function," Claude is finding the old code and replacing it with new code.

Why it matters: Claude doesn't rewrite your entire file for a small change. It targets just the specific lines that need to change. This is safer — less chance of accidentally breaking something else.

Write Tool

Creates a new file or completely overwrites an existing one. Used when Claude is building something from scratch (like writing an entire new index.html) rather than editing an existing file.

Bash Tool

Runs terminal commands — git push, npm install, adb shell .... This is how Claude pushes your code to GitHub, runs ADB cachebust commands, and executes scripts.

Permission modes: By default, Claude asks your permission before running commands. This is the "approve/deny" prompt you see. In --dangerously-skip-permissions mode, Claude runs everything without asking.

Grep Tool

Searches for text patterns across your files. When Claude needs to find where a function is defined, or all places that reference a variable, it uses Grep. It's like the search function in a text editor, but across your entire project.

Glob Tool

Finds files by name pattern. "Find all HTML files" or "find everything in the Zercher directory." Claude uses this to orient itself in your project — figuring out what files exist before deciding which to read.

The Tool Chain in Action

When you say "add a dark mode toggle to Cadence," here's the full chain of tool calls Claude makes:

  1. Glob — find files in ~/Cadence/ to understand the project structure
  2. Read — read index.html to understand the current code
  3. Edit — add CSS variables for dark mode colors
  4. Edit — add a toggle button in the HTML section
  5. Edit — add JavaScript to handle the toggle and save the preference
  6. Read — read service-worker.js to check the version
  7. Edit — bump the service worker version
  8. Bash — git add . && git commit && git push
  9. Bash — ADB cachebust your phone

Each tool call adds to the context window. A complex feature might involve 15-20 tool calls. This is another reason long sessions degrade — the tool results accumulate and eat context.

Why Claude Asks Permission

Every tool call that could change something on your computer goes through a permission check. This is the "approve/deny" prompt you see when Claude wants to:

  • Edit a file
  • Create a file
  • Run a bash command

Read-only operations (Read, Grep, Glob) usually don't need permission — they're safe because they don't change anything.

When you approve a tool, that approval is usually just for that specific action, not for all future actions. Each edit, each command gets its own check. This is why --dangerously-skip-permissions speeds things up dramatically for trusted workflows — it removes the pause between every action.

What Happens When a Tool Fails

Sometimes tools fail — a file doesn't exist, a command returns an error, a git push gets rejected. When this happens, the error message goes back into Claude's context just like successful results. Claude can then read the error and adjust its approach.

This is why Claude often recovers from errors automatically — it reads the error message, understands what went wrong, and tries a different approach. But sometimes it gets stuck in a loop (trying the same thing repeatedly). That's when you should step in and either describe the issue or start a fresh session.

11. The History of AI

The wild, surprising road from "can machines think?" to Claude building your apps.

The AI you use every day didn't appear out of nowhere. It took 70+ years of breakthroughs, dead ends, hype cycles, and a few key ideas that changed everything. Here's the story.

The 1950s: The Dream Begins

In 1950, Alan Turing published a paper asking "Can machines think?" He proposed what we now call the Turing Test: if you chat with a machine and can't tell it's not human, does it matter whether it "really" thinks? That question still drives AI research today.

In 1956, a group of researchers at Dartmouth College coined the term "Artificial Intelligence" at a summer workshop. They were wildly optimistic. Marvin Minsky, one of the founders, predicted that within a generation, the problem of creating AI would be "substantially solved." They were off by about 60 years.

The 1960s-70s: Early Excitement

The first AI programs were rule-based. ELIZA (1966) was a chatbot that mimicked a therapist by matching patterns in your text and reflecting them back. Type "I feel sad" and it would respond "Why do you feel sad?" People were amazed — and some genuinely believed it understood them. It didn't. It was just pattern matching, no understanding at all.

Researchers built "expert systems" — programs stuffed with hand-written rules like "IF patient has fever AND cough THEN consider flu." These worked for narrow domains but couldn't handle anything outside their rules. You had to manually program every possible scenario. Imagine trying to write rules for every possible conversation — impossible.

The AI Winters: 1970s-80s, 1990s

When the early promises didn't pan out, funding dried up. Twice. These are called the "AI Winters" — periods where the field was considered overhyped and money moved elsewhere. Researchers who worked on AI struggled to get funding or respect. The dream of intelligent machines seemed like science fiction.

The Pattern of AI History
Hype → Overpromise → Disappointment → Winter → Quiet breakthroughs → Sudden explosion.
This cycle repeated multiple times. The current AI boom started around 2017-2022 and it's still accelerating. Whether another winter comes is one of the biggest questions in tech.

The 1990s-2000s: Machine Learning Takes Over

The big shift was moving from hand-written rules to learning from data. Instead of programming "IF fever THEN flu," you show the computer 10,000 patient records and let it figure out the patterns itself. This is machine learning.

In 1997, IBM's Deep Blue beat world chess champion Garry Kasparov. It was a landmark moment, but Deep Blue wasn't really "intelligent" — it was a brute-force calculator that evaluated 200 million chess positions per second. It couldn't hold a conversation or recognize a cat in a photo. It could only play chess.

Machine learning algorithms like Support Vector Machines, Random Forests, and basic neural networks started working well on specific tasks — spam filtering, recommendation systems, image recognition. But they needed humans to hand-pick the right "features" (what to look for in the data). And they still couldn't handle language in any meaningful way.

2012: The Deep Learning Revolution

In 2012, a neural network called AlexNet crushed the competition in an image recognition contest, cutting the error rate nearly in half. The key ingredients: a much deeper network (more layers), training on GPUs (graphics cards repurposed for math), and massive amounts of data.

This kicked off the deep learning era. Suddenly, neural networks could see photos, recognize speech, translate languages. Tech companies poured billions into AI research. Google bought DeepMind. Facebook, Microsoft, Amazon all built AI labs.

In 2016, DeepMind's AlphaGo beat the world champion at Go — a game so complex that brute force couldn't solve it. The machine had to develop something resembling intuition. This shocked the AI community more than chess had. Go was supposed to be decades away from being solved.

2017: "Attention Is All You Need"

This is the paper that changed everything. In 2017, researchers at Google published a paper introducing the Transformer architecture. Before Transformers, language models processed text one word at a time, in order, like reading left to right. They struggled with long texts because by the time they reached the end of a paragraph, they'd forgotten the beginning.

Transformers introduced attention — the ability to look at the entire input at once and figure out which parts matter most for each prediction. When predicting the word after "The cat sat on the ___", a Transformer doesn't just look at "the" — it looks at "cat" and "sat" and weights them appropriately.

This is the architecture that powers Claude, GPT, Gemini, and every major LLM today. The Transformer made it possible to train on billions of words and actually retain long-range patterns. Every AI model you use — including the one writing your apps right now — is built on this 2017 paper.

Why "Attention" Matters to You
When Claude reads your 3,000-line index.html, attention is what lets it connect a CSS variable defined on line 12 to the button that uses it on line 2,800. Without attention, it would forget the variable by line 100. This is why the Transformer was revolutionary — it gave machines memory across long documents.

2018-2022: The GPT Era

OpenAI took the Transformer and scaled it up. GPT-1 (2018) was a proof of concept. GPT-2 (2019) could write convincing paragraphs. OpenAI was so worried about misuse that they initially refused to release the full model. GPT-3 (2020) was a leap — 175 billion parameters, and it could write essays, code, poetry, and carry on conversations that felt eerily human.

Then ChatGPT launched in November 2022 and everything exploded. It reached 100 million users faster than any product in history. Suddenly everyone — not just researchers — was using AI. Your grandmother was asking ChatGPT for recipes. College students were writing papers with it. Programmers were generating code. The age of "AI for everyone" had begun.

2023-2024: The Race & Anthropic's Claude

After ChatGPT, every major tech company raced to build their own LLM. Google launched Gemini. Meta released Llama (open source). And Anthropic — founded by former OpenAI researchers in 2021 — released Claude.

Anthropic was founded specifically because its founders believed AI safety needed to be a priority, not an afterthought. Dario Amodei (CEO) and Daniela Amodei (President) left OpenAI to build a company where safety research and capability research go hand in hand. That's why Claude has strong safety training — it's not just a feature, it's the founding mission.

Claude 1 launched in March 2023. Claude 2 followed in July 2023 with a much larger context window. Claude 3 (Haiku, Sonnet, Opus) arrived in March 2024 with dramatically better coding ability. Then Sonnet 3.5 became the go-to coding model. And now Claude 4.5/4.6 — the model that builds your apps, writes your guides, and manages your entire workflow.

2024-2026: Agents & Tool Use

The latest frontier is giving LLMs the ability to do things, not just talk. Claude Code (what you use every day) is an "agent" — it can read files, edit code, run terminal commands, search the web, and manage git repositories. It's not just predicting text anymore. It's taking actions in the real world.

This is a massive shift. The early chatbots could only generate text. The current models can browse the web, write and execute code, create files, push to GitHub, manage databases, and interact with APIs. When Claude builds your Cadence app, it's reading your existing code, understanding the architecture, writing new features, testing them, committing to git, and deploying to GitHub Pages — all autonomously.

We went from "can machines think?" to "machines are building my apps while I sleep" in about 70 years. And the pace is accelerating.

What's Next?

Nobody knows. Seriously. The pace of progress has surprised even the people building these systems. A few things that are actively being worked on:

  • Longer memory — models that remember past conversations across sessions, not just within one chat
  • Better reasoning — models that can plan multi-step solutions more reliably, with fewer mistakes
  • Multimodal — models that see images, hear audio, and generate both (Claude can already read images)
  • Specialized agents — AI that doesn't just chat but runs businesses, manages projects, and operates autonomously for hours
