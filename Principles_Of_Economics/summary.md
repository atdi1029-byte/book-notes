# Principles of Economics — N. Gregory Mankiw (9th Edition, 2021)

**866 pages | 36 chapters | The most widely used economics textbook in the world**

Mankiw's *Principles of Economics* is the introductory economics textbook that has shaped how millions of students first encounter the discipline. Now in its ninth edition, the book covers the full sweep of economics — from individual consumer choices and firm behavior through labor markets, public finance, monetary policy, and international trade — unified by Mankiw's signature "Ten Principles of Economics" that open the book and echo through every chapter.

The book is organized in two halves. The first eighteen chapters cover **microeconomics**: how markets work, why they sometimes fail, and how government policy affects outcomes. Mankiw builds from supply and demand through elasticity, taxation, market efficiency, externalities, public goods, and the economics of firms — from perfectly competitive price-takers to monopolists, oligopolists, and monopolistic competitors. The micro half closes with labor markets, income inequality, and the theory of consumer choice.

The second half covers **macroeconomics**: how nations measure economic activity (GDP, CPI), what drives long-run growth (productivity, saving, investment), how the financial system and monetary system operate, and how short-run fluctuations arise. Mankiw develops the aggregate demand–aggregate supply model, applies Keynesian fiscal and monetary policy, traces the Phillips curve from its discovery through the Friedman-Phelps revolution and the Volcker disinflation, and closes with six live debates in macroeconomic policy.

What distinguishes Mankiw's approach is clarity without condescension. Complex ideas — comparative advantage, deadweight loss, the money multiplier, crowding out — are built step by step with real-world case studies: Henry Ford's $5-a-day wage, the Wizard of Oz as monetary allegory, JFK's Keynesian tax cuts, the 2008 financial crisis, OPEC's stagflation shocks. The writing is direct and the examples are memorable.

This is not a book that argues for a particular economic worldview. Mankiw presents both sides of every policy debate — minimum wage, trade restrictions, deficit spending, zero inflation targeting — and trusts the reader to weigh the evidence. The result is the closest thing economics has to a common language: the shared vocabulary and framework that every subsequent economics course, policy debate, and financial analysis assumes you already know.
