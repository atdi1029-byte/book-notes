## Chapter 22: Frontiers of Microeconomics

### Asymmetric Information

**Information asymmetry** occurs when one party in a transaction knows more than the other, creating two problems:

**Moral hazard** arises when an agent performs tasks for a principal but cannot be perfectly monitored. Workers may shirk when unsupervised. Employers respond with better monitoring, high wages (efficiency-wage theory), and delayed payment (year-end bonuses). Homeowners with fire insurance buy fewer extinguishers because insurance covers the loss.

**Adverse selection** occurs when the seller knows more about product quality than the buyer. The **lemons problem**: sellers of the worst used cars are most eager to sell, while owners of good cars keep them. Buyers, aware of this, worry about getting a lemon. A car only weeks old sells for thousands less than new because the seller knows something the buyer doesn't. In health insurance, sicker people are more likely to buy coverage, driving up prices and pushing average-health individuals out.

**Signaling** (informed party reveals info) must be costly and less costly for higher-quality parties. Education works because talented people complete it more easily. **Gift giving** as signal: expensive gifts signal love because finding the right gift is easy if you think about her constantly.

> "Now we'll see how much he loves me."

**Screening** (uninformed party induces revelation): car insurance firms offer high-premium/full-coverage vs. low-premium/deductible policies. Risky drivers self-select into full coverage; safe drivers choose deductibles.

### Political Economy

**Condorcet Voting Paradox**: With three options, pairwise majority voting can produce intransitive societal preferences (A beats B, B beats C, but C beats A). The order of voting determines what society "wants."

**Arrow's Impossibility Theorem** (Kenneth Arrow, 1951): No voting system can satisfy unanimity, transitivity, independence of irrelevant alternatives, and no dictators simultaneously.

**Median Voter Theorem**: Majority rule produces the outcome preferred by the median voter. Implication: both parties move toward the center. Minority views receive little weight regardless of intensity.

Politicians are self-interested like everyone else. Corruption impedes development. Don't be surprised when policy fails to resemble textbook ideals.

> "Isn't it the real genius of democracy? . . . The VOTERS are ultimately to blame."

### Behavioral Economics

Real people are **Homo sapiens**, not **Homo economicus**. **Herbert Simon** suggested humans are **satisficers** rather than maximizers.

Systematic mistakes: **overconfidence** (90% confidence intervals contain truth far less than 90% of the time), **vivid observation bias** (one friend's lemon story outweighs Consumer Reports survey of 1,000), **confirmation bias** (both death-penalty supporters and opponents became more certain after reading the same report).

**401(k) default effect**: Far more workers participate when auto-enrolled with opt-out than when they must opt in. Rational maximizers would choose identically either way.

**Ultimatum game**: Player A proposes split of $100; Player B accepts or both get nothing. Theory predicts A offers $1 and B accepts. Reality: B rejects tiny offers, and A typically offers $30-40. People care about fairness even at personal cost.

**Time inconsistency**: People prefer 50 minutes of drudgery in 90 days over 60 minutes in 91 days, but when day 90 arrives, they procrastinate. 76% of Americans say they save too little for retirement.

The unifying theme: **life is messy**. Information, government, and people are all imperfect.

## Chapter 23: Measuring a Nation's Income

### GDP Fundamentals

**GDP** measures two things at once: total income earned and total expenditure on output. Every transaction has a buyer and seller, so income equals expenditure.

**Definition**: Market value of all final goods and services produced within a country in a given period of time.

- Uses market prices; excludes illegal and home-produced items
- Only **final goods** (not intermediate) to avoid double-counting
- Measures current production within geographic borders
- Quarterly data reported at annual rates, seasonally adjusted

**GDP identity**: Y = C + I + G + NX

- **Consumption** (C): 68% of GDP -- household spending except new housing
- **Investment** (I): 17% -- capital goods, residential construction, inventories
- **Government purchases** (G): 18% -- goods/services, NOT transfer payments
- **Net exports** (NX): -3% -- exports minus imports

### Real vs. Nominal GDP

- **Nominal GDP**: valued at current prices (conflates quantity and price changes)
- **Real GDP**: valued at constant base-year prices (isolates quantity changes)
- **GDP deflator** = (Nominal GDP / Real GDP) x 100
- U.S. real GDP grew ~3% per year on average; occasional recessions

### GDP as Well-Being Measure

> "[GDP] measures everything, in short, except that which makes life worthwhile." -- Robert Kennedy (1968)

Kennedy's critique contains truth, but nations with larger GDP can afford better healthcare, education, and more. GDP excludes leisure, environmental quality, volunteer work, and income distribution. International data show GDP per person closely associated with life expectancy, schooling, and life satisfaction.

## Chapter 24: Measuring the Cost of Living

### The Consumer Price Index

**Babe Ruth** earned $80,000 in 1931 -- in 2015 dollars: $1,247,368. Still only one-third of average MLB salary and 4% of Clayton Kershaw's $31M.

**CPI calculation** (five steps): Fix basket, find prices, compute basket cost, choose base year, compute inflation rate. Formula: CPI = (Cost of basket in current year / Cost in base year) x 100.

**Basket breakdown**: Housing 42%, Transportation 16%, Food 15%, Medical 8%, Education 7%, Recreation 6%, Apparel 3%.

**Three CPI problems**: (1) **Substitution bias** -- consumers shift to cheaper goods but fixed basket doesn't reflect this; (2) **New goods** -- more variety makes each dollar more valuable but CPI misses this; (3) **Unmeasured quality change**. Studies concluded CPI overstates inflation by ~1 percentage point (now ~0.5 after reforms).

### GDP Deflator vs. CPI

- GDP deflator covers domestically produced goods; CPI covers goods consumers buy
- GDP deflator uses currently produced goods; CPI uses fixed basket
- Boeing airplane to Air Force: shows in deflator, not CPI. Volvo price hike: shows in CPI, not deflator.

### Real and Nominal Interest Rates

**Real interest rate = Nominal interest rate - Inflation rate**

> "A nickel ain't worth a dime anymore." -- Yogi Berra

In 1970s, nominal rates were high but real rates were negative -- inflation eroded savings faster than interest accumulated.

## Chapter 25: Production and Growth

### The Power of Compound Growth

> "The consequences for human welfare in questions like these are simply staggering: Once one starts to think about them, it is hard to think about anything else." -- Robert Lucas

- U.S. real GDP per person: $4,264 (1870) to $55,860 (2014), ~1.80% annual growth
- China: $762 (1900) to $13,170 (2014), 2.53% growth
- Typical Pakistani in 2014 had roughly same income as typical Briton in 1870

**Productivity** = quantity of goods/services per unit of labor input. One word explains living standard variation: **productivity**.

### Four Determinants of Productivity

1. **Physical capital** (K/L): equipment and structures; a produced factor of production
2. **Human capital** (H/L): knowledge and skills from education/training/experience
3. **Natural resources** (N/L): renewable and nonrenewable; Japan proves they're not necessary
4. **Technological knowledge** (A): society's understanding of best production methods

**Production function**: Y = AF(L, K, H, N). With constant returns to scale: Y/L = AF(1, K/L, H/L, N/L).

### Growth and Public Policy

**Diminishing returns**: As capital rises, extra output from each additional unit falls. Higher saving leads to higher *level* but not permanently higher *growth rate*.

**Catch-up effect**: Poor countries grow faster. U.S. vs. South Korea (1960-1990): similar investment shares, but South Korea grew 6%+ vs. U.S. ~2% because South Korea started with tiny capital stock.

**Education**: Each year of schooling raises wages ~10% in U.S., even more in developing countries. **Brain drain** plagues poor countries when educated workers emigrate.

**Robert Fogel** found improved nutrition accounts for ~30% of British per capita income growth 1790-1980. From 1775 to 1975, average British caloric intake rose 26%, average height rose 3.6 inches.

**Property rights and political stability** are prerequisites -- courts enforce contracts, corruption impedes growth, revolutionary threats deter investment.

**Free trade**: Argentina's total GDP equals Houston's. Imagine Houston banning trade with outsiders. Countries with >80% of population within 100km of coast have GDP per person ~4x that of countries with <20% near coast.

### Population Growth

**Malthus** (1798) predicted mankind doomed to poverty. Wrong: world population sextupled, but living standards soared thanks to technological ingenuity.

**Michael Kremer** showed world growth rates increased with population. After ice-age land bridges flooded (10,000 B.C.), larger isolated regions developed faster. Eurasia-Africa led; Australia and Tasmania lagged. Flinders Island's tiny population died out entirely by 3000 B.C.

**Gordon vs. Mokyr debate**: Robert Gordon (Northwestern) says best innovations behind us, growth will halve. Joel Mokyr (Northwestern) says fast computing opens new pathways and innovation is accelerating.

## Chapter 26: Saving, Investment, and the Financial System

### Financial Institutions

**Financial system**: institutions matching one person's saving with another's investment.

**Bond market**: Bonds have term (maturity), credit risk (default probability), and tax treatment. Junk bonds pay highest rates. Municipal bonds are tax-exempt, so pay lower rates.

**Stock market**: Stocks represent partial ownership (equity finance) vs. bonds (debt finance). Stockholders bear more risk but earn higher returns. **Dow Jones Industrial Average** (30 companies, since 1896) and **S&P 500** are watched as economic indicators.

**Banks**: Take deposits, make loans, facilitate payments as medium of exchange. **Mutual funds**: Allow small investors to diversify. **Index funds** outperform actively managed funds -- with thousands of managers, price reflects true value already.

### Loanable Funds Market

**Supply**: national saving (S). **Demand**: investment (I). **Price**: real interest rate.

**Three policies analyzed**:
1. **Saving incentives** (tax-advantaged accounts): Supply shifts right -> lower interest rate, more investment
2. **Investment tax credit**: Demand shifts right -> higher interest rate, more saving
3. **Budget deficits**: Reduce national saving -> supply shifts left -> higher interest rate, less investment (**crowding out**)

> "When the government reduces national saving by running a budget deficit, the interest rate rises, and investment falls."

### U.S. Government Debt History

- 1836: zero debt; 1945: 107% of GDP (WWII peak)
- Reagan 1980s: tax cuts without matching spending cuts raised debt from 26% to 48% of GDP
- Clinton 1990s: surplus achieved; Bush 2000s: back to deficits (tax cuts, wars, 9/11)
- 2008 crisis: deficits averaged ~9% of GDP (2009-2012), debt rose from 39% to 70%

## Chapter 27: The Basic Tools of Finance

### Present Value

**Present value** of X dollars in N years: X/(1 + r)^N. Higher interest rates make future money less valuable today.

- **Rule of 70**: Variable growing at x% doubles in ~70/x years
- At 1% growth, incomes double in 70 years; at 3%, in just 23 years

> "Albert Einstein once called compounding 'the greatest mathematical discovery of all time.'"

### Risk Management

**Risk aversion**: People dislike losses more than they enjoy equivalent gains (diminishing marginal utility of wealth). Insurance spreads risk efficiently among thousands of shareholders.

### Efficient Markets and Random Walks

**Efficient markets hypothesis**: Stock prices reflect all available information. Changes are unpredictable (random walk). In 15-year period ending 2016, **81% of actively managed stock funds underperformed index funds** by average 0.89 percentage points annually.

> "If the efficient markets hypothesis is correct, then there is little point in spending many hours studying the business page."

**John Maynard Keynes** suggested markets driven by "animal spirits." **Alan Greenspan** questioned "irrational exuberance" in 1990s -- subsequent crash validated the concern.

## Chapter 28: Unemployment

### Measuring Unemployment

BLS surveys ~60,000 households monthly. Adults classified as employed, unemployed, or not in labor force. **Unemployment rate** = Unemployed / Labor force x 100. January 2016: 4.9% (7.8M unemployed out of 158.3M labor force).

**U-6 rate** (broadest measure including discouraged and involuntary part-time): 9.9% vs. official U-3 of 4.9%.

Key insight: **Most spells of unemployment are short, but most unemployment observed at any time is long-term** (3 workers unemployed all year account for 75% of observed unemployment vs. 52 workers unemployed one week each).

### Women's Labor-Force Participation

- 1950: 33% of women in labor force vs. 87% of men
- 2015: 76% of women vs. 69% of men
- Technologies (washing machine, refrigerator), birth control, and changing attitudes drove the shift

### Four Causes of Natural Unemployment

**1. Job search (frictional unemployment)**: Takes time to find best match. Unemployment insurance extends search duration.

**2. Minimum-wage laws**: Set above equilibrium for unskilled workers, creating surplus of labor (structural unemployment).

**3. Unions**: Operate as cartels; threaten strikes. Union workers earn 10-20% more than similar non-union workers. Insiders benefit; outsiders bear costs through unemployment or lower wages in non-union sectors. **Wagner Act (1935)** protects right to organize.

**4. Efficiency wages**: Firms pay above equilibrium voluntarily because it improves health, reduces turnover, attracts better workers, and increases effort.

**Henry Ford's $5-a-day wage (1914)**: Twice the going rate. Turnover fell, absenteeism fell, productivity rose. Ford called it "one of the finest cost-cutting moves we ever made." Assembly lines made worker interdependence critical.

> "Ford and his associates freely declared on many occasions that the high-wage policy turned out to be good business."

## Chapter 29: The Monetary System

### Fractional-Reserve Banking and Money Creation

- **Reserve ratio**: Fraction of deposits held as reserves
- **Money multiplier** = 1/R. With 10% reserve ratio: $100 deposit generates $1,000 of money
- Higher reserve ratio = smaller multiplier

**Leverage amplifies gains and losses**: With leverage ratio of 20 (typical bank), a 5% fall in assets wipes out capital entirely, making bank insolvent.

### The Fed's Tools

1. **Open-market operations** (most common): Buy bonds = inject reserves = increase money supply; sell bonds = reverse
2. **Discount rate**: Interest on Fed loans to banks; lower rate = more borrowing = more money
3. **Reserve requirements**: Rarely changed; disruptive to banks
4. **Interest on reserves** (since October 2008): Higher rate = banks hold more reserves = less lending

**The Fed cannot perfectly control money supply** because it doesn't control household deposits or bank lending decisions.

**Great Depression**: Money supply fell 28% (1929-1933) as bank runs caused deposit withdrawals and banks increased reserves. **FDIC** now prevents runs by guaranteeing deposits.

**Federal funds rate**: Rate banks charge each other for overnight loans. FOMC sets target every ~6 weeks; open-market operations make it happen.

> "When the Fed announces a change in the federal funds rate, it is committing itself to the open-market operations necessary to make that change happen."

## Chapter 30: Money Growth and Inflation

### The Quantity Theory of Money

> "Prices rise when the government prints too much money." -- Ten Principles of Economics

**Quantity equation**: M x V = P x Y (Money x Velocity = Price level x Output)

With velocity relatively stable and output determined by real factors, increases in M produce proportional increases in P. This explains both moderate inflation and hyperinflation.

**Four hyperinflations of the 1920s** (Austria, Hungary, Germany, Poland): Money supply and price level moved almost perfectly in parallel on logarithmic scales.

### Classical Dichotomy and Monetary Neutrality

- **Nominal variables**: Measured in money (dollar prices, wages, nominal GDP)
- **Real variables**: Measured in physical units (real GDP, real wages, relative prices)
- **Monetary neutrality**: Changes in money supply affect nominal but not real variables in the long run
- Short-run non-neutrality is real -- even Hume acknowledged this

### The Inflation Tax

> "The inflation tax is subtler: when government prints money, price level rises, and dollars in your wallet become less valuable."

Hyperinflation example from Bolivia: Teacher Edgar Miranda rushed to convert 25 million pesos to dollars on payday. Within days, his pay's dollar value fell from $50 to $27.

**Fisher Effect**: Nominal interest rate = Real interest rate + Inflation rate. Named after **Irving Fisher (1867-1947)**.

### Six Costs of Inflation

1. **Shoeleather costs**: Resources wasted reducing money holdings
2. **Menu costs**: Costs of repricing
3. **Relative-price variability**: Distorts resource allocation
4. **Tax distortions**: Tax code taxes nominal gains, discouraging saving
5. **Confusion and inconvenience**: Changing unit of account impairs comparison
6. **Arbitrary wealth redistribution**: Unexpected inflation helps debtors, hurts creditors

### The Wizard of Oz as Monetary Allegory

*The Wonderful Wizard of Oz* (L. Frank Baum, 1900) allegorizes the 1890s free-silver debate. Dorothy = American values, Scarecrow = farmers, Tin Woodsman = industrial workers, Cowardly Lion = William Jennings Bryan, Yellow Brick Road = gold standard, Oz = ounce of gold. Dorothy's silver slippers (changed to ruby for Technicolor) represent the silver-money solution.

> "You shall not crucify mankind upon a cross of gold." -- William Jennings Bryan, 1896

## Chapter 31: Open-Economy Macroeconomics: Basic Concepts

### Trade Flows

- **Net exports** (NX) = Exports - Imports (trade balance)
- U.S. trade grew from 4-5% of GDP (1950s) to ~15% (2015)
- Largest partners: China, Canada, Mexico, Japan, Germany

### Capital Flows

**Net capital outflow** (NCO) = Purchase of foreign assets by domestic residents - Purchase of domestic assets by foreigners

**Fundamental identity**: NCO = NX. Trade surplus means capital flowing out; trade deficit means capital flowing in.

**Saving identity** (open economy): S = I + NCO. Two uses for saving: domestic investment and foreign investment.

### Exchange Rates

**Nominal exchange rate**: Rate for trading currencies (e.g., 80 yen per dollar)
**Real exchange rate** = (Nominal rate x Domestic price) / Foreign price

**Purchasing-power parity** (PPP): Exchange rates should equalize purchasing power across countries. Big Mac Index tests this -- predicted and actual rates are "usually in the same ballpark" but not exact.

## Chapter 32: A Macroeconomic Theory of the Open Economy

### Two-Market Model

1. **Loanable funds market**: S = I + NCO; real interest rate is the equilibrating price
2. **Foreign-currency exchange market**: NCO = NX; real exchange rate is the equilibrating price

**NCO links both markets**: Higher domestic interest rate -> more attractive domestic assets -> lower NCO -> less supply of domestic currency in exchange market -> currency appreciates -> net exports fall.

### Capital Flight

When investors lose confidence (Asian crisis 1997, Russia 1998, Argentina 2002): domestic currency depreciates, interest rates spike, net exports increase.

**China's capital flows** (2000-2014): Accumulated ~$4 trillion in foreign reserves by buying U.S. bonds, keeping renminbi cheap, boosting exports. Benefits to U.S.: cheaper goods, lower interest rates. Costs: hurt American producers competing with imports.

> "Just as an individual can go into debt in either a prudent or profligate manner, so can a nation."

## Chapter 33: Aggregate Demand and Aggregate Supply

### Three Facts about Economic Fluctuations

1. **Irregular and unpredictable** -- "business cycle" is misleading
2. **Most macro quantities fluctuate together** -- investment accounts for ~two-thirds of GDP declines despite being one-sixth of GDP
3. **As output falls, unemployment rises**

### The AD-AS Model

**Aggregate-demand curve slopes downward** for three reasons:
1. **Wealth effect**: Lower prices -> real wealth rises -> more consumption
2. **Interest-rate effect**: Lower prices -> less money needed -> lend excess -> lower rates -> more investment
3. **Exchange-rate effect**: Lower prices -> lower rates -> capital flows abroad -> currency depreciates -> more net exports

**Long-run aggregate supply is vertical** at the natural level of output -- the classical dichotomy holds. Shifts from changes in labor, capital, natural resources, and technology.

**Short-run aggregate supply slopes upward** due to:
- **Sticky wages**: Nominal wages slow to adjust; unexpected price drops make production less profitable
- **Sticky prices**: Menu costs delay repricing; firms with higher-than-desired prices cut output
- **Misperceptions**: Suppliers confuse changes in overall prices with changes in relative prices

**Key equation**: Output = Natural level + a(Actual price level - Expected price level)

### AD Shifts: Great Depression and WWII

**Great Depression**: Real GDP fell 27%, unemployment hit 25%, prices fell 22% (1929-1933). Money supply fell 28% as bank runs reversed money creation. Stock prices fell ~90%.

**WWII**: Government purchases increased almost fivefold (1939-1944). Output nearly doubled. Unemployment fell from 17% to 1%.

### The Great Recession (2008-2009)

Housing boom fueled by low rates and **subprime lending** via **securitization** (mortgage-backed securities). Prices doubled 1995-2006, then fell ~30%. Underwater homeowners defaulted; financial institutions holding mortgage-backed securities neared bankruptcy. **Credit crunch** contracted aggregate demand. Real GDP fell 4.2%, unemployment hit 10%.

Policy responses: Fed cut rates to zero, Congress appropriated $700B for bank recapitalization, Obama signed $787B stimulus.

**Olivier Blanchard's lessons**: (1) Macro-prudential tools work better than atomic-bomb interest rate changes; (2) Financial system plumbing matters enormously; (3) Interconnectedness matters.

### Supply Shocks: OPEC and Stagflation

**1970s OPEC shocks**: Oil prices doubled (1973-75), then doubled again (1978-81). Result: **stagflation** -- falling output + rising prices simultaneously. Shifted short-run AS left and Phillips curve right.

**John Maynard Keynes** originated AD-AS thinking in *The General Theory* (1936): "In the long run we are all dead."

## Chapter 34: Monetary and Fiscal Policy on Aggregate Demand

### Theory of Liquidity Preference

**Keynes's theory**: Interest rate adjusts to balance money supply and demand. Money demand slopes down (interest rate = opportunity cost of holding money). Money supply is vertical (set by Fed).

For the U.S., the **interest-rate effect** is the most important reason AD slopes downward.

**Fed increases money supply** -> lower interest rate -> more investment -> AD shifts right. Fed targets the **federal funds rate**; open-market operations achieve the target.

### Fiscal Policy

**Multiplier effect**: Government buys $20B of planes from Boeing -> Boeing pays workers -> workers spend more -> GDP rises by more than $20B. **Spending multiplier** = 1/(1 - MPC).

**Crowding-out effect**: Higher spending -> higher income -> higher money demand -> higher interest rates -> less private investment. Partially offsets the multiplier.

**Tax changes**: Cuts increase disposable income -> more spending -> AD shifts right. Permanent cuts have larger effect than temporary ones.

### Case Study: Keynesians in the White House

**JFK (1961)**: Asked why he advocated tax cuts, replied "To stimulate the economy. Don't you remember your Economics 101?" Advisers **James Tobin** and **Robert Solow** (both future Nobel laureates) were applying Keynes's ideas. Tax cut passed in 1964, followed by robust growth.

> "The stock market has predicted nine out of the past five recessions." -- Paul Samuelson

### Stabilization Policy Debate

**Pro**: Keynes argued fluctuations stem from "animal spirits." Government should adjust policy to stabilize AD. **William McChesney Martin**: "The Fed's job is to take away the punch bowl just as the party gets going."

**Con**: Monetary policy works with ~6-month lag; fiscal policy delayed by political process. Forecasting is imprecise. Policy may destabilize rather than stabilize.

**Automatic stabilizers**: Tax revenues fall automatically in recessions; unemployment benefits rise automatically. These stabilize without deliberate action. A strict balanced-budget amendment would eliminate them.

## Chapter 35: The Short-Run Trade-off between Inflation and Unemployment

### The Phillips Curve

> "Probably the single most important macroeconomic relationship is the Phillips curve." -- George Akerlof

**A.W. Phillips (1958)**: Showed negative correlation between unemployment and wage inflation in U.K. data (1861-1957). **Samuelson and Solow** extended to U.S. and named it the Phillips curve.

The Phillips curve shows combinations of inflation and unemployment arising as AD shifts move the economy along the short-run AS curve.

### Friedman-Phelps and the Natural Rate Hypothesis

**Milton Friedman and Edmund Phelps (1968)**: Predicted the Phillips curve trade-off is only temporary. In the long run, unemployment returns to its natural rate regardless of inflation. The long-run Phillips curve is **vertical**.

**1960s data** seemed to confirm a stable trade-off. But by early 1970s, as expected inflation caught up, unemployment reverted to 5-6% despite high inflation -- exactly as Friedman and Phelps predicted.

### Supply Shocks and the 1970s

OPEC supply shocks shifted the Phillips curve rightward -- **stagflation** (higher unemployment AND higher inflation). Policymakers faced impossible choice: fight inflation (raising unemployment further) or fight unemployment (raising inflation further).

By 1980: inflation >9%, unemployment ~7%. **Jimmy Carter** lost to **Ronald Reagan** partly on this record.

### The Volcker Disinflation

**Paul Volcker** (appointed Fed Chairman 1979) pursued contractionary monetary policy. **Sacrifice ratio** typically estimated at 5 (5% of annual output lost per 1 percentage point of inflation reduced).

Reducing inflation from 10% to 4% implied sacrificing 30% of annual output. Inflation fell to ~4% by 1983-84, but unemployment hit ~10% in 1982-83 -- the deepest recession since the Great Depression.

**Rational expectations** (Robert Lucas, Thomas Sargent, Robert Barro): If government credibly commits to low inflation, expectations adjust immediately and disinflation can be costless. Volcker's disinflation was costly -- but less than predicted, and partly because the public didn't initially believe him.

> "An implication of this view is that inflation can be stopped much more quickly than advocates of the 'momentum' view have indicated." -- Thomas Sargent

### The Greenspan Era (1984-2005)

Relatively mild fluctuations. 1986 OPEC breakdown -> favorable supply shock (falling oil prices). Fed avoided 1960s mistakes by raising rates when unemployment fell below natural rate. Inflation drifted toward zero; unemployment also declined.

## Chapter 36: Six Debates Over Macroeconomic Policy

### Debate 1: Spending Hikes vs. Tax Cuts in Recessions

**Pro spending**: Government purchases add directly to AD; tax cuts may be saved. Obama admin estimated $1 of spending increases GDP by $1.59 vs. $0.99 for tax cuts. **2009 stimulus** ($800B) featured "shovel-ready" projects, aid to states, and extended unemployment benefits.

**Pro tax cuts**: JFK, Reagan, and Johnson all used tax cuts followed by robust growth. Tax cuts alter incentives (supply-side effects) and decentralize spending decisions. Government spending may produce "bridges to nowhere." Consumers anticipating future taxes to repay borrowing may cut current spending.

### Debate 2: Rules vs. Discretion in Monetary Policy

**Pro rules**: Discretion enables **political business cycles** (Nixon pressured Burns in 1972) and **time inconsistency** (promising low inflation then exploiting the short-run trade-off). A 3% money growth rule would eliminate abuse.

**Pro discretion**: Flexibility needed for unforeseen shocks (1930s bank failures, 1970s oil shocks, 1987 crash, 2008 crisis). Fed built credibility through actions in 1990s-2000s. **Inflation targeting** (Fed adopted 2% target in January 2012) is a compromise.

### Debate 3: Zero Inflation

**Pro**: Inflation imposes real costs (shoeleather, menu, tax distortions, arbitrary redistribution). Disinflation is temporary pain for permanent gain. Volcker is now considered a hero.

**Con**: Sacrifice ratio of 5 means going from 4% to 0% costs 20% of a year's output, borne disproportionately by the least-skilled workers. Moderate inflation "greases the wheels" of labor markets (workers resist nominal wage cuts). Positive inflation allows negative real interest rates when needed.

> "As rational individuals, we do not volunteer for a lobotomy to cure a head cold. Yet, as a collectivity, we routinely prescribe the economic equivalent." -- Alan Blinder

### Debate 4: Balancing the Budget

**Pro**: Government debt of ~$41,000 per person burdens future taxpayers. Deficits reduce national saving, raise interest rates, crowd out investment. Justifiable only for wars and recessions.

**Con**: $41,000 is only ~2% of lifetime earnings. Government can sustain debt growing slower than GDP (~5%/year). Forward-looking parents offset debt by saving more. Cutting education to balance the budget could make future generations worse off.

### Debate 5: Tax Reform to Encourage Saving

**Pro**: Tax code heavily penalizes saving. $1,000 at 10% for 45 years: $72,900 without tax, only $13,800 with 40% tax on interest. Double taxation of corporate income (corporate tax + dividend tax). A **consumption tax** would exempt saving entirely.

**Con**: Tax breaks for saving favor the wealthy. **Income effect** may offset **substitution effect** (higher returns mean you need to save less to hit targets). Could just raise public saving by reducing the deficit instead.

### Conclusion

> If they sound like they are offering you free lunch, you should look for the hidden price tag. Few if any policies come with benefits but no costs.
