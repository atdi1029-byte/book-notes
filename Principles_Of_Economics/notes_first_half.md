## Chapter 1: Ten Principles of Economics

### How People Make Decisions

**Principle 1: People Face Trade-offs**
- "Guns and butter" — more defense spending = less for consumer goods
- Environmental regulations: cleaner air vs. higher costs that reduce incomes of firms, workers, customers
- **Efficiency vs. equality**: society gets maximum from scarce resources vs. benefits distributed uniformly
- Policies redistributing income (welfare, unemployment insurance, progressive income tax) trade efficiency for equality — "the pie gets smaller" as work incentives diminish
- Acknowledging trade-offs doesn't eliminate them, but helps understand available options

**Principle 2: The Cost of Something Is What You Give Up to Get It**
- **Opportunity cost**: whatever must be given up to obtain an item
- College costs: biggest cost is foregone wages, not tuition
- Athletes who can earn millions face especially high opportunity costs for college

**Principle 3: Rational People Think at the Margin**
- **Marginal change**: small incremental adjustment to existing plan
- Decisions rarely black-and-white — not "fast or pig?" but "extra spoonful of potatoes?"
- Airline example: 200-seat plane costs $100,000 total, average cost $500/seat — but marginal cost of standby passenger is tiny (can of soda) — sell ticket for $300 and profit
- Cell phone example: $90 bill for 100 minutes ($0.50/min average + $40 fixed); 10-min call doesn't cost $9 — marginal cost only $5, marginal benefit $7, so make the call
- Water vs. diamonds paradox: water essential but marginal benefit of extra cup is small; diamonds rare so marginal benefit is large

**Principle 4: People Respond to Incentives**
- One economist: "People respond to incentives. The rest is commentary."
- Higher apple prices: people buy fewer apples, orchards hire more workers
- Gasoline taxes: high in Europe → Europeans drive smaller cars; low in U.S. → Americans drive larger cars
- **Sam Peltzman** (1975): seat belt laws → fewer deaths per accident but more accidents — drivers feel safer, drive less carefully, more pedestrian deaths
- **Ralph Nader's** *Unsafe at Any Speed* (1965) generated the safety movement
- Key lesson: when analyzing any policy, consider not only direct effects but indirect effects working through incentives

**Principle 5: Trade Can Make Everyone Better Off**
- Trade allows specialization in what each person/country does best
- Countries are partners in world economy, not just competitors

> "For $5 a week you can watch baseball without being nagged to cut the grass!"

**Principle 6: Markets Are Usually a Good Way to Organize Economic Activity**
- Collapse of communism (late 1980s-early 1990s) showed central planning fails
- **Market economy**: decentralized decisions of firms and households
- **Adam Smith** (1776, *The Wealth of Nations*): "invisible hand" guides self-interest toward desirable outcomes
- Prices reflect both value to society and cost of production

> "Man has almost constant occasion for the help of his brethren, and it is in vain for him to expect it from their benevolence only."

- Central planners failed because they tried to run economy with one hand tied behind their backs — the invisible hand of the marketplace

**Adam Smith** (1723-1790): Scottish philosopher and economist
- *Wealth of Nations* published in 1776, same year as Declaration of Independence
- Both documents share premise: individuals usually best left to their own devices

**Case Study: Adam Smith Would Have Loved Uber**
- Local governments impose strict taxi controls: medallions, price regulations, police enforcement
- **Uber** (2009): app connecting passengers and drivers, technically not taxis
- Surge pricing during demand spikes (rainstorms, New Year's Eve) increases supply when most needed
- 2014 survey: every single economist agreed Uber increased consumer well-being; 85% said surge pricing also helped consumers

**Principle 7: Governments Can Sometimes Improve Market Outcomes**
- **Property rights** essential — farmers won't grow crops if they expect theft; restaurants won't serve if customers don't pay; film companies won't produce if piracy is unchecked
- **Market failure**: externalities (pollution) and market power (monopoly well owner charging excessive prices)
- World's best basketball player earns more than world's best chess player — invisible hand does not ensure everyone has sufficient food, clothing, or healthcare
- Government *can* improve outcomes does not mean it always *will* — political process is far from perfect

### How the Economy as a Whole Works

**Principle 8: Standard of Living Depends on Productivity**
- 2014: average American ~$55,000; Mexican ~$17,000; Nigerian ~$6,000
- U.S. incomes grow ~2%/year — doubles every 35 years, eightfold over past century
- **Productivity**: quantity of goods/services per unit of labor input

**Principle 9: Prices Rise When Government Prints Too Much Money**
- Germany 1921: newspaper cost 0.30 marks; November 1922: 70,000,000 marks
- 1970s U.S.: prices more than doubled; President **Gerald Ford** called inflation "public enemy number one"
- In almost all cases of large inflation, culprit is growth in quantity of money

**Principle 10: Short-Run Trade-off between Inflation and Unemployment**
- Increasing money stimulates spending → firms hire more → lower unemployment
- Over a year or two, many policies push inflation and unemployment in opposite directions
- **Business cycle**: irregular fluctuations in economic activity
- By changing spending, taxes, and money supply, policymakers influence short-run combination of inflation and unemployment
- **Obama** 2008-2009: stimulus package (reduced taxes + increased spending) plus **Federal Reserve** money supply increase to combat deep downturn caused by bad bets on housing market

## Chapter 2: Thinking Like an Economist

### The Economist as Scientist
- Scientific method: observation, theory, testing — like physics or biology
- **Einstein**: "The whole of science is nothing more than a refinement of everyday thinking"
- Economists cannot run experiments easily — rely on **natural experiments** from history

### Assumptions and Models
- Physicist assumes marble falls in vacuum; economist assumes two countries, two goods
- Different assumptions for different questions: short-run (prices fixed) vs. long-run (prices flexible)

### The Circular-Flow Diagram
- **Two decision makers**: firms and households
- **Two markets**: goods/services (households buy, firms sell) and factors of production (households sell labor/land/capital, firms buy)
- Inner loop = flows of inputs and outputs; outer loop = flows of dollars
- Follow a dollar: your wallet → Starbucks (revenue) → landlord rent or worker wages (income) → back in someone's wallet → cycle repeats

### The Production Possibilities Frontier
- Graph showing output combinations possible given available resources and technology
- Example: economy produces only cars and computers
- Points on frontier = **efficient**; inside = **inefficient** (e.g., widespread unemployment); outside = infeasible
- Moving from point A to B: 100 more cars costs 200 fewer computers → opportunity cost of each car = 2 computers
- **Bowed outward shape**: opportunity cost increases because resources not equally suited for all activities
- Moving autoworkers to computer industry adds little to computer output (they're not good at it)
- Technological advance shifts frontier outward — society can enjoy more of both goods

### Microeconomics vs. Macroeconomics
- **Microeconomics**: households/firms in specific markets
- **Macroeconomics**: economy-wide phenomena (inflation, unemployment, growth)

### Why Economists Disagree
- Differences in **scientific judgments** and **values**
- Rent control: economists largely oppose, but cities implement anyway
- Trade barriers: almost all economists oppose tariffs, yet presidents restrict imports

### Keynes on Economics

> The master-economist must be mathematician, historian, statesman, philosopher — must contemplate particular and general simultaneously.

### Pitfalls in Economic Reasoning
- **Omitted variables**: cigarette lighters correlate with cancer, but smoking is the real cause — government study might wrongly discourage lighter ownership
- **Reverse causality**: more police correlate with more crime — **Association of American Anarchists** might argue police cause crime, but actually cities expecting more crime hire more police
- **Broken window fallacy** (Robert McTeer): when resources shift from one use to another (window repair), it doesn't create net new spending — just different activity

## Chapter 3: Interdependence and the Gains from Trade

### The Frank and Ruby Parable
- **Frank** (farmer): 60 min/oz meat, 15 min/oz potatoes
- **Ruby** (rancher): 20 min/oz meat, 10 min/oz potatoes
- Ruby has **absolute advantage** in both — better at everything

### Absolute vs. Comparative Advantage
- **Absolute advantage**: ability to produce using fewer inputs — Ruby has this in both goods
- **Comparative advantage**: ability to produce at lower opportunity cost
- Frank's opportunity cost of 1 oz potatoes = 1/4 oz meat; Ruby's = 1/2 oz meat
- **Frank has comparative advantage in potatoes; Ruby in meat**
- One person can have absolute advantage in both goods, but **impossible to have comparative advantage in both**
- Opportunity cost of one good is the inverse of opportunity cost of the other

### Specialization and Trade
- Without trade: Frank produces 16 oz potatoes + 4 oz meat; Ruby produces 24 oz potatoes + 12 oz meat
- With specialization and trade: Frank gets 17 oz potatoes + 5 oz meat; Ruby gets 27 oz potatoes + 13 oz meat
- Both gain 1 oz of each food — total production rises (potatoes 40→44, meat 16→18)
- Trade at 3 oz potatoes per 1 oz meat — between the two opportunity costs

> For both parties to gain, trade price must lie between the two opportunity costs.

### Applications
- **Serena Williams mowing lawn**: absolute advantage (faster mower) but opportunity cost = $30,000 (TV commercial in 2 hours); hire Forrest Gump (opportunity cost $50 from McDonald's wages)
- **U.S.-Japan trade**: U.S. comparative advantage in food (better farmland), Japan in cars; through specialization, both can have more food AND more cars
- Trade is **not like war** — it's not zero-sum

**Adam Smith** (1776): "It is a maxim of every prudent master of a family, never to attempt to make at home what it will cost him more to make than to buy."

**David Ricardo** (1817, *Principles of Political Economy and Taxation*): developed comparative advantage with wine/cloth and England/Portugal example; opposed Britain's **Corn Laws** as Parliament member

- **Emily Oster** (Brown University): applies comparative advantage to household chores — divide based on relative skill, not absolute skill; some task-splitting efficient even when one person is better at everything
- Survey: 100% of economists agree trade with China makes most Americans better off

## Chapter 4: The Market Forces of Supply and Demand

### Markets and Competition
- **Competitive market**: so many buyers/sellers that each has negligible price impact
- **Perfectly competitive**: identical goods + many buyers/sellers = **price takers**
- **Monopoly**: one seller who sets price

### Demand
- **Quantity demanded**: amount buyers are willing and able to purchase
- **Law of demand**: price rises → quantity demanded falls (other things equal)
- **Demand curve** slopes downward; **market demand** = horizontal sum of individual demands
- **Demand shifters**: income, prices of related goods, tastes, expectations, number of buyers
- **Normal good**: demand rises with income; **Inferior good**: demand falls with income (e.g., bus rides when you can afford a car)
- **Substitutes**: price of one rises → demand for other rises (hot dogs/hamburgers); **Complements**: price of one rises → demand for other falls (gasoline/automobiles)
- Critical distinction: **movement along** the demand curve (price changes) vs. **shift** of the curve (non-price factors change)
- Cigarette example: 10% price increase → 4% reduction in quantity demanded (movement); anti-smoking campaign shifts entire curve left
- Studies show tobacco and marijuana are **complements** rather than substitutes

### Supply
- **Quantity supplied**: amount sellers willing and able to sell
- **Law of supply**: price rises → quantity supplied rises
- **Supply shifters**: input prices (sugar, labor), technology (mechanized ice-cream machines), expectations, number of sellers

### Equilibrium
- **Equilibrium price** (market-clearing price): balances quantity supplied and demanded
- Ice cream example: equilibrium at $2.00 per cone, 7 cones traded
- **Surplus** (price above equilibrium): sellers cut prices; **Shortage** (price below equilibrium): buyers bid up prices
- **Law of supply and demand**: price adjusts to bring quantities into balance

### Three-Step Analysis
1. Does event shift supply, demand, or both?
2. Which direction?
3. How does shift change equilibrium price and quantity?

- Hot weather: demand shifts right → price and quantity both rise
- Hurricane destroys sugar crop: supply shifts left → price rises, quantity falls
- Both together: price definitely rises, quantity effect ambiguous (depends on relative magnitude)

> "If an invisible hand guides market economies, then the price system is the baton that the invisible hand uses to conduct the economic orchestra."

### Price Gouging Debate
- After disasters, higher prices discourage hoarding, encourage conservation, increase supply
- $4 Coke, $6.99 batteries in Brooklyn after storms — **Governor Chris Christie** threatened penalties
- Pro-market view: price spikes distribute scarce goods more efficiently and encourage increased supply
- Anti-gouging laws often result in more empty shelves, not fewer

## Chapter 5: Elasticity and Its Application

### Price Elasticity of Demand
- **Formula**: % change in quantity demanded / % change in price
- **Elastic** (>1): quantity responds substantially; **Inelastic** (<1): responds slightly; **Unit elastic** (=1): equal proportional changes
- Example: 10% price increase → 20% quantity decrease = elasticity of 2
- **Midpoint method**: divides by average of initial/final values — gives same answer regardless of direction
- **Extreme cases**: perfectly inelastic (vertical curve, elasticity = 0); perfectly elastic (horizontal curve, elasticity = infinity)

### Determinants of Elasticity
- **Close substitutes** → more elastic (butter vs. margarine); eggs have no close substitute → inelastic
- **Necessities** → inelastic (doctor visits); **luxuries** → elastic (sailboats)
- **Narrow markets** → more elastic (vanilla ice cream vs. food vs. ice cream)
- **Longer time horizons** → more elastic
- Gasoline: 10% price increase → 2.5% consumption reduction after 1 year, but 6% after 5 years

### Real-World Elasticities
- Eggs: 0.1; Healthcare: 0.2; Cigarettes: 0.4; Housing: 0.7; Beef: 1.6; Restaurant meals: 2.3; Mountain Dew: 4.4

### Total Revenue and Elasticity
- **Total revenue** = P x Q
- **Inelastic demand**: price increase raises revenue
- **Elastic demand**: price increase reduces revenue
- Along linear demand curve, elasticity varies: elastic at high prices, inelastic at low prices

### Other Elasticities
- **Income elasticity**: % change in quantity / % change in income (positive = normal good, negative = inferior)
- **Cross-price elasticity**: positive = substitutes, negative = complements

### Price Elasticity of Supply
- **Formula**: % change in quantity supplied / % change in price
- Key determinant: flexibility of production; beachfront land (inelastic — can't produce more) vs. manufactured goods (elastic — run factories longer)
- Supply more elastic in long run than short run (firms can build new factories, new firms enter)
- At low output: high elasticity (idle capacity); at full capacity: elasticity drops sharply

### Three Applications

**Good News for Farming = Bad News for Farmers**
- **Kansas State University** discovers wheat hybrid raising production 20% per acre
- Supply shifts right → price falls substantially; demand for food is inelastic → total revenue falls from $300 to $220
- Two hundred years ago most Americans lived on farms; today fewer than 3 million (2% of labor force), producing 5x more than in 1950
- Policy pays farmers NOT to plant — with inelastic demand, smaller crop = greater revenue
- No single farmer can leave land fallow (takes market price as given), but collectively all benefit

> "Improvement in farm technology can be bad for farmers because it makes farmers increasingly unnecessary, but it is surely good for consumers who pay less for food."

**Why OPEC Failed to Keep Oil Prices High**
- OPEC raised oil prices: $3/barrel (1972) → $11 (1974) → $35 (1981)
- Short run: supply/demand both inelastic → large price increase works
- Long run: both more elastic — producers increase exploration, consumers buy fuel-efficient cars, switch to public transit
- 1982-1985: price declined ~10%/year; 1986: cooperation collapsed, price plunged 45% to $13
- By 1990: price (inflation-adjusted) back where it started in 1970
- Post-2000 price fluctuations driven more by global demand booms/busts and fracking technology than OPEC restrictions

**Drug Interdiction vs. Drug Education**
- Interdiction shifts supply left → price rises → with inelastic demand, total drug spending INCREASES → addicts need even more cash → more crime
- Education shifts demand left → price falls, quantity falls → total spending and crime both fall
- Time horizon matters: demand may be inelastic short-run (established addicts don't quit) but more elastic long-run (higher prices discourage youth experimentation)

> "According to an old quip, even a parrot can become an economist simply by learning to say 'supply and demand.'"

## Chapter 6: Supply, Demand, and Government Policies

### Price Ceilings
- **Price ceiling**: legal maximum price; **non-binding** (above equilibrium) has no effect; **binding** (below equilibrium) creates shortage
- Rationing under ceilings: long lines, discrimination by seller bias — neither desirable

> "When the government imposes a binding price ceiling on a competitive market, a shortage of the good arises, and sellers must ration the scarce goods among the large number of potential buyers."

- **1973 gas lines**: OPEC raised crude oil prices; U.S. price ceiling on gasoline prevented market adjustment → severe shortages and long lines. Eventually laws repealed — lawmakers learned they were partly responsible
- **Rent control**: short-run small shortage (supply/demand inelastic); long-run massive shortage as landlords stop building/maintaining, low rents encourage everyone to seek own apartment
- Under rent control with waiting lists, landlords lose incentive to maintain buildings — why bother when people are lined up to move in?

> "One economist called rent control 'the best way to destroy a city, other than bombing.'"

- 95% of economists disagree that rent control increases quantity and quality of affordable housing

### Price Floors
- **Price floor**: legal minimum price; **binding** (above equilibrium) creates surplus
- **Minimum wage**: U.S. first established via **Fair Labor Standards Act of 1938**; federal rate $7.25/hour in 2015
- Greatest impact on **teenage labor market** — least skilled, least experienced
- 10% minimum wage increase → 1-3% drop in teenage employment
- Higher minimum wage can cause teens to drop out of school for jobs, displacing other teens who already dropped out
- 2015: $7.25/hour × 40 hours × 52 weeks = $15,080, barely above poverty line for family of four
- Opponents: not all minimum-wage workers are poor — many are middle-class teenagers
- Alternative: **rent/wage subsidies** and **earned income tax credit** raise living standards without discouraging hiring

### Tax Incidence
- **Tax incidence**: who actually bears the burden of a tax
- **Key insight**: taxes on sellers and taxes on buyers produce identical outcomes
- Tax creates wedge between buyer price and seller price — $0.50 ice cream tax
- Ice cream bowl analogy: whether $0.50 goes directly from buyer's pocket into bowl, or from pocket to seller to bowl, doesn't matter
- Example: $0.50 tax on sellers → price rises from $3.00 to $3.30; buyers pay $0.30 more, sellers keep $2.80 ($0.20 less) — both bear burden

> "A tax burden falls more heavily on the side of the market that is less elastic."

- Elastic side has better alternatives and can leave the market more easily → bears less burden
- **FICA** (15.3%): Congress split 50/50 between firms and workers, but labor supply is much less elastic than demand → workers bear most of it regardless of legal split
- **1990 luxury tax on yachts**: elastic demand (millionaires can buy houses instead) + inelastic supply (yacht builders can't easily switch careers) = burden fell on middle-class yacht builders → Congress repealed most luxury taxes in 1993

## Chapter 7: Consumers, Producers, and the Efficiency of Markets

### Consumer Surplus
- **Willingness to pay**: maximum buyer will pay for a good
- **Consumer surplus** = willingness to pay - amount actually paid
- Elvis Presley album auction: Taylor (WTP $100) wins at $80; surplus = $20
- Measured as area below demand curve and above price
- Lower prices raise surplus: (1) existing buyers pay less, (2) new buyers enter market

### Producer Surplus
- **Cost**: value of everything seller must give up to produce a good (including opportunity cost)
- **Producer surplus** = amount received - seller's cost
- House painting example: Andy (cost $500) hired at $600; surplus = $100
- Area below price and above supply curve

> "The area below the price and above the supply curve measures the producer surplus in a market."

### Market Efficiency
- **Total surplus** = consumer surplus + producer surplus = value to buyers - cost to sellers
- Amount paid cancels out (transfer from buyer to seller)
- **Equilibrium maximizes total surplus** — three insights:
  1. Free markets allocate supply to buyers who value goods most (measured by WTP)
  2. Free markets allocate demand to sellers who produce at lowest cost
  3. Free markets produce quantity maximizing total surplus
- **Laissez-faire**: "let people do as they will"
- Benevolent social planner would need to know every buyer's value and every seller's cost — practically impossible; centrally planned economies never work well

> "These three insights tell us that the market outcome makes the sum of consumer and producer surplus as large as it can be."

### Market Failure Exceptions
- **Market power**: monopoly keeps prices above competitive level
- **Externalities**: side effects on bystanders (agricultural pesticides affect nearby residents)

### Case Study: Market for Organs
- Government imposed price ceiling of zero → chronic organ shortage, thousands die annually waiting
- People born with two kidneys usually need only one — obvious gains from trade
- 57% of economists favor establishing a market for kidneys (IGM panel, 2014)
- Critics worry market would benefit rich at expense of poor; defenders question whether current system is fair either

### San Francisco Parking Experiment
- **Donald Shoup** (UCLA): electronic meters with embedded sensors; raise prices on crowded blocks, lower on empty ones
- Goal: at least one empty spot per block — the invisible hand parking your car

## Chapter 8: Application: The Costs of Taxation

> "Taxes are what we pay for civilized society." — Oliver Wendell Holmes, Jr.

### Deadweight Loss
- Tax creates wedge between buyer and seller prices → market shrinks below optimum
- Government revenue = T × Q; lost surplus beyond revenue = **deadweight loss** (areas C + E)
- Tax reduces consumer surplus (B + C) and producer surplus (D + E); government gains B + D; net loss = C + E
- **Mike and Mei example**: Mike cleans Mei's house weekly for $100 (his cost $80, her value $120, surplus $40); $50 tax makes trade impossible — both lose $20, government collects nothing — pure deadweight loss of $40
- Core insight: taxes prevent buyers and sellers from realizing gains from trade

### Elasticity Determines Deadweight Loss Size
- **Inelastic** supply/demand = small deadweight loss (quantity doesn't change much)
- **Elastic** supply/demand = large deadweight loss (quantity responds substantially)
- General principle: greater elasticities produce greater deadweight losses

### The Deadweight Loss Debate
- U.S. **marginal tax rate** on labor approximately 40% (Social Security, Medicare, federal + state income taxes combined)
- Labor supply inelastic view: most people work full-time regardless → small deadweight loss
- Labor supply elastic view: overtime decisions, second earners comparing wages to childcare, retirement timing, underground economy → large deadweight loss

### The Laffer Curve
- **Arthur Laffer** (1974): revenue as function of tax rate — inverted U-shape
- **Reagan** story: 1940s wartime surtax hit 90% — "You could only make four pictures and then you were in the top bracket. So we all quit working after four pictures and went off to the country"
- Deadweight loss rises with **square** of tax size (double tax → 4x deadweight loss; triple → 9x)
- Reagan cut taxes to encourage work (**supply-side economics**)
- Most economists skeptical U.S. was on wrong side of Laffer curve in 1980s
- Sweden (early 1980s, ~80% marginal rate) more plausible case
- Economist survey (2012): 96% disagree that tax cuts would raise revenue within five years

## Chapter 9: Application: International Trade

### The Isoland Parable
- Imaginary country **Isoland** — initially isolated by government decree
- **World price** comparison to domestic price determines trade direction
- If world price > domestic price → country has comparative advantage → becomes **exporter**
- If world price < domestic price → country lacks comparative advantage → becomes **importer**

### Winners and Losers
- **Exporting country**: domestic price rises to world price; producers gain (B + C + D), consumers lose (B); net gain = area D
- **Importing country**: domestic price falls to world price; consumers gain (B + D), producers lose (B); net gain = area D

> "Trade raises the economic well-being of a nation in the sense that the gains of the winners exceed the losses of the losers."

### Tariffs and Quotas
- **Tariff**: tax on imports → raises domestic price → deadweight loss (areas D + F) from overproduction and underconsumption
- **Import quota**: same price/quantity effects as tariff, but surplus goes to license holders, not government
- U.S. pressured Japan to "voluntarily" limit car exports — licenses went to Japanese firms, surplus accrued abroad
- Quotas worse: at least tariff produces revenue for domestic government

### Benefits of Trade Beyond Supply/Demand
- Increased **variety** of goods
- **Economies of scale** from access to larger markets
- Increased **competition** (limits market power)
- Enhanced **flow of ideas** and technology

### Arguments Against Free Trade
- **Jobs argument**: trade creates and destroys jobs — gains from comparative advantage, not absolute advantage
- **National security**: sometimes legitimate, often abused by firms seeking protection
- **Infant industry**: "picking winners" extraordinarily difficult; temporary protection becomes permanent
- **Unfair competition**: foreign subsidies? Isoland should send a thank-you note for cheap goods
- **Bargaining chip**: threat may not work, leaving country worse off

### Trade Agreements
- **NAFTA** (1993): U.S., Mexico, Canada
- **GATT/WTO**: reduced average tariffs from ~40% post-WWII to ~5% today

### The Smuggler Parable
- Isolandian "inventor" turns wheat into textiles (actually smuggling wheat abroad for textiles)
- When exposed and jailed, living standards fall — he was just practicing international trade

## Chapter 10: Externalities

### Externalities Defined
- **Externality**: when a person's activity influences well-being of bystander without compensation
- **Negative externality**: adverse impact (automobile exhaust, barking dogs, dioxin from paper mills)
- **Positive externality**: beneficial impact (restored historic buildings, research into new technologies)

### Negative Externalities
- **Social cost** = private cost + external costs on third parties → social-cost curve lies above supply curve
- Aluminum production: each unit emits smoke; market produces Q_MARKET but socially optimal is Q_OPTIMUM (smaller)
- **Internalizing the externality**: tax on producers = external cost shifts supply to social-cost curve → optimal quantity

### Positive Externalities
- **Social value** = private value + external benefits → social-value curve lies above demand curve
- Education: private benefit (higher wages) + external benefits (informed voters, lower crime, technological advances)
- Market underproduces: optimal quantity larger than market quantity
- Solution: subsidies (education heavily subsidized through public schools and scholarships)
- **Technology spillovers**: one firm's R&D benefits others — debate over **industrial policy** (subsidize computer chips over potato chips?)

> "Negative externalities lead markets to produce more than socially desirable. Positive externalities lead markets to produce less."

### Policy Approaches

**Corrective Taxes (Pigovian taxes)** — after **Arthur Pigou (1877-1959)**
- Ideal corrective tax = external cost from negative externality
- Paper mill can reduce pollution at lower cost than steel mill: tax allows paper mill to reduce more, steel mill less — both optimize
- Regulation forces equal reductions regardless of cost differences → less efficient
- Unlike most taxes, corrective taxes **enhance efficiency** — move allocation closer to social optimum
- Also raise revenue AND incentivize development of cleaner technologies

**Gasoline Tax Case Study**: three negative externalities from driving:
- **Congestion**: encourages public transit, carpooling, living closer to work
- **Accidents**: SUV drivers 5x more likely to kill others in crashes
- **Pollution**: primary cause of global warming
- 2007 study: optimal corrective gas tax = $2.28/gallon (2005 dollars); actual U.S. tax only ~50 cents
- European countries have much higher gas taxes — yet politically unpopular everywhere

**Tradable Pollution Permits**
- **EPA** issues limited permits; firms trade on open market
- Same outcome as corrective tax: pollution allocated to lowest-reduction-cost firms
- Initial permit distribution doesn't affect final allocation (efficient regardless)
- **Success**: 1990 Clean Air Act SO₂ permit system reduced acid rain cost-effectively
- **Senator Edmund Muskie**: "We cannot give anyone the option of polluting for a fee"
- Economists' response: clean environment is a normal good — richer countries afford more; eliminating all pollution would require reversing technological progress

### Private Solutions

**Private Solutions**:
- Moral codes ("Do unto others") = internalize externalities
- Charities (**Sierra Club** for environment; college donations for education externality)
- Business integration (apple grower buys beehives — or beekeeper buys orchard — internalizes cross-pollination externality)

**Coase Theorem** (after **Ronald Coase**): if parties bargain without cost, they solve externalities efficiently regardless of initial rights distribution
- Dick's barking dog ($500 benefit) vs. Jane's disturbance ($800 cost)
- If Dick has right to keep dog: Jane offers $600 to remove it (Dick accepts — $600 > $500)
- If Jane has right to quiet: Dick won't pay $800+ (benefit only $500)
- Either way: efficient outcome (dog goes) — only who pays whom differs

> "Whatever the initial distribution of rights, the interested parties can reach a bargain in which everyone is better off and the outcome is efficient."

- Fails when **transaction costs** high (lawyers, translators) or **many parties** involved (factory polluting lake used by many fishermen)

## Chapter 11: Public Goods and Common Resources

### Four Types of Goods
- **Private goods**: excludable + rival (ice cream)
- **Public goods**: not excludable + not rival (national defense)
- **Common resources**: not excludable + rival (fish in ocean)
- **Club goods**: excludable + not rival (cable TV)

### Public Goods and the Free-Rider Problem
- **Free rider** receives benefit without paying
- Fireworks example: $5,000 total value, $1,000 cost, but private entrepreneur can't sell tickets
- **National defense**: $748 billion (2014), $2,346 per person
- **Basic research**: general knowledge is public good; government funds via NIH, NSF
- **Cost-benefit analysis**: no price signals available — value of human life ~$10 million (wage differential studies)

### The Tragedy of the Commons
- Medieval town: families add sheep, overgraze common land until barren, wool industry collapses
- Each family's incentive: add more sheep; but each addition reduces land quality for others
- **Aristotle**: "What is common to many is taken least care of, for all men have greater regard for what is their own"
- Solutions: regulation, taxes, permits, or convert to private property (England's enclosure movement, 17th century)

**Important Common Resources:**
- **Congested roads**: tolls as corrective tax; London/Stockholm found congestion pricing very effective; **State Route 167** (WA) and **I-15** (CA) use embedded sensors for variable real-time tolling
- **Fish/wildlife**: North American buffalo fell from 60 million to 400 by 1900

**Case Study: Why the Cow Is Not Extinct**
- Great demand for beef, yet cows face no extinction threat
- Cattle are **private good** (live on ranches — owners preserve); elephants are **common resource** (roam freely — each poacher has incentive to kill as many as possible)
- **Kenya/Tanzania/Uganda**: made killing elephants illegal — hard to enforce, battle increasingly violent
- **Botswana/Malawi/Namibia/Zimbabwe**: made elephants private good (people can kill on own property) → landowners have incentive to preserve → populations rising
- With private ownership and profit motive, African elephant might someday be as safe as the cow

### Property Rights as the Key
- Market failures arise when property rights not well established
- Factory pollutes too much because no one charges for pollution it emits
- Government can help: define property rights (pollution permits), regulate behavior (antipollution laws), or provide goods markets fail to supply (national defense)

## Chapter 12: The Design of the Tax System

### Revenue Sources
- **Federal**: individual income taxes (largest), payroll taxes (Social Security/Medicare), corporate income taxes
- **State/local**: sales taxes, property taxes (40%+ of revenue), state income taxes
- **Federal revenue sharing**: redistributes funds from high-income to low-income states

### Taxes and Efficiency

> "An efficient tax system is one that imposes small deadweight losses and small administrative burdens."

- Two costs beyond payment: **deadweight losses** (distorted incentives) + **administrative burden** (compliance time, lawyers, accountants)
- Tax loopholes: some deliberate (municipal bond preference helps state/local borrowing), some accidental
- Resources devoted to compliance = pure deadweight loss — government gets only revenue, taxpayer loses both payment and time/money

**Marginal vs. Average Tax Rate**
- **Average tax rate**: total taxes / total income
- **Marginal tax rate**: tax on additional dollar of income
- Example: 20% on first $50,000 + 50% above $50,000; person earning $60,000 pays $15,000
  - Average rate: 25%; marginal rate: 50%
- Marginal rate determines deadweight loss — rational people think at the margin
- **Lump-sum tax**: most efficient (zero marginal rate, no distortion) but most unfair (poor and rich pay same)

### Income vs. Consumption Tax
- Income tax discourages saving: $1,000 at 8% for 40 years → $21,720 without tax, only $10,290 with 25% tax on interest
- Consumption tax: tax what people spend, not earn — savings not taxed until withdrawn
- Already partially implemented via 401(k)s, IRAs
- European **VAT** (value-added tax) collects at each production stage
- **Alan Greenspan** (Fed Chairman, 2005): advocated consumption tax to encourage saving and capital formation

### Taxes and Equity

> "Don't tax you. Don't tax me. Tax that fella behind the tree." — Senator Russell Long

- **Benefits principle**: pay based on benefits received
- **Ability-to-pay principle**: pay based on capacity
- **Vertical equity**: richer pay more; **horizontal equity**: similar pay similar
- **Proportional** (flat), **regressive**, and **progressive** tax systems
- U.S. federal system is progressive: poorest quintile 1.9%, richest 23.4%, top 1% at 29.0%

### Corporate Income Tax
- Popular because corporations seem like impersonal entities
- But people pay all taxes — burden falls on owners, workers, customers
- **Flypaper theory** misleading: burden doesn't stick where it first lands

### Tax Policy History
- **Reagan** (1980): cut top rate from 50% to 28% (efficiency focus)
- **Clinton** (1993): raised top rate to ~40% (equity focus)
- **Bush**: reduced to 35%; **Obama**: restored to ~40%

## Chapter 13: The Costs of Production

### Production Function
- **Diminishing marginal product**: each additional worker adds less output
- Production function gets flatter; total-cost curve gets steeper

### Cost Measures
- **Fixed costs**: don't vary with output (rent)
- **Variable costs**: change with output (materials, labor)
- **TC = FC + VC**; **ATC = TC/Q**; **MC = ΔTC/ΔQ**
- **U-shaped ATC**: fixed costs spread thin at low output, diminishing returns dominate at high output
- **Efficient scale**: quantity minimizing ATC
- **MC crosses ATC at its minimum** — like GPA and marginal grade

### Short Run vs. Long Run
- Short run: factory size fixed; long run: can build/close factories
- **Economies of scale**: ATC falls with output (specialization)
- **Diseconomies of scale**: ATC rises with output (coordination problems)

> Adam Smith's pin factory: specialization → thousands of pins per worker per day

## Chapter 14: Firms in Competitive Markets

### Revenue
- **Average revenue** = price; **marginal revenue** = price (for competitive firms)
- **Profit maximization**: produce where **MR = MC** (which equals P for competitive firms)
- **Firm's supply curve = MC curve above AVC** (short run) or **above ATC** (long run)

### Shutdown vs. Exit
- **Shutdown** (temporary): if P < AVC — sunk costs irrelevant
- **Exit** (permanent): if P < ATC
- **Sunk cost principle**: "Don't cry over spilt milk"

### Long-Run Equilibrium
- Entry/exit drives economic profit to zero: **P = minimum ATC = MC**
- **Zero economic profit ≠ zero accounting profit** — includes opportunity costs
- Long-run supply curve is **horizontal** at minimum ATC (or upward-sloping with limited resources or differing costs)

> "At the end of this process of entry and exit, firms that remain must be making zero economic profit."

## Chapter 15: Monopoly

### Why Monopolies Arise
1. **Monopoly resources**: DeBeers controlled ~80% of world diamonds
2. **Government regulation**: patents (20-year exclusive right), copyrights
3. **Natural monopoly**: single firm at lower cost due to economies of scale (water distribution, bridges)

### Monopoly Revenue and Pricing
- Monopolist faces downward-sloping market demand → **MR < P**
- Two effects: **output effect** (more sold) vs. **price effect** (price falls on all units)
- Profit maximization: MR = MC, but **P > MR = MC**
- **Profit** = (P - ATC) x Q

> "In competitive markets, price equals marginal cost. In monopolized markets, price exceeds marginal cost."

### Welfare Cost
- Monopoly produces less than socially efficient quantity
- **Deadweight loss** = triangle between demand and MC curves
- Monopoly like a private tax collector — but profit goes to firm, not government
- **Prozac** example: patent expired 2001, generic sells at 1/3 the price

### Price Discrimination
- Selling same good at different prices to different customers
- **Readalot Publishing**: $30 to die-hard fans, $5 to casual readers → profit rises from $1M to $3M, eliminates deadweight loss
- **Perfect price discrimination**: each customer charged willingness to pay → efficient but all surplus goes to producer
- Examples: movie tickets (age), airlines (Saturday stay), coupons, financial aid, quantity discounts

---

## Chapter 16: Monopolistic Competition

### Characteristics
- Many sellers, differentiated products, free entry/exit
- Examples: books, restaurants, clothing, computer games

### Long-Run Equilibrium
- Entry/exit drive profit to zero: **P = ATC** (like competition)
- But **P > MC** (like monopoly) — downward-sloping demand
- **Tangency condition**: demand curve tangent to ATC curve
- **Excess capacity**: firms produce below efficient scale
- **Markup**: firms eager for additional customers (unlike competitive firms)

### Advertising
- ~2% of revenue economy-wide; 10-20% for differentiated consumer goods
- **Critics**: manipulates tastes, reduces competition
- **Defenders**: provides information, intensifies competition
- **Lee Benham (1972)**: states allowing optometrist advertising had 20% lower eyeglasses prices
- **Quality signaling**: willingness to spend on advertising itself signals quality (Kellogg vs. shredded newspaper cereal)
- **Brand names**: provide information and incentive to maintain quality (McDonald's consistency)

## Chapter 17: Oligopoly

### Duopoly Example
- Jack and Jill own water wells, MC = 0
- **Monopoly outcome**: 60 gallons at $60, profit $3,600 (split $1,800 each)
- **Nash equilibrium**: each produces 40 gallons, total 80 at $40, profit $1,600 each
- **Competitive outcome**: 120 gallons at $0

> "Oligopoly price is less than monopoly price but greater than competitive price."

- As oligopoly grows larger, price effect diminishes → approaches competitive outcome

### The Prisoners' Dilemma
- **Bonnie and Clyde**: confessing is dominant strategy for each → both get 8 years (could get 1 year each with cooperation)
- Oligopoly = same structure: each firm's dominant strategy is to overproduce
- **OPEC**: $3/barrel (1972) → $35 (1981), then cooperation collapsed → $13 (1986)
- Arms races, common resource depletion follow same logic

### Cooperation in Repeated Games
- **Tit-for-tat** (Robert Axelrod's tournament winner): start cooperative, then mirror opponent's last move
- Threat of future punishment can sustain cooperation

### Antitrust Policy
- **Sherman Act (1890)**: made price-fixing criminal conspiracy
- **Clayton Act (1914)**: triple damages for antitrust violations

> "People of the same trade seldom meet together, but the conversation ends in a conspiracy against the public." — Adam Smith

- **Robert Crandall** (American Airlines) to Howard Putnam (Braniff): "Raise your fares 20%. I'll raise mine the next morning." — Putnam gave tape to Justice Department
- **Resale price maintenance**: may solve free-rider problem among retailers
- **Predatory pricing**: skeptics argue predator bears 80% of losses
- **Microsoft case** (1998): tying browser to Windows; settled 2002 without breakup

## Chapter 18: The Markets for the Factors of Production

### Labor Demand
- **Derived demand**: demand for labor comes from demand for the good produced
- **Value of marginal product** = marginal product x output price
- Firm hires until value of marginal product = wage
- **Value-of-marginal-product curve = labor-demand curve**
- Demand shifts with output price, technology, supply of other factors

### Labor Supply
- Trade-off between work and leisure — opportunity cost of leisure = wages
- **Women in workforce**: 34% (1950) → 57% (2015)
- **Immigration** increases labor supply — Israeli-Palestinian example: Palestinian workers cut by half → wages rose ~50%

### Productivity and Wages
- 1960-2015: productivity grew 2.0%/year, real wages 1.8%/year
- 1960-1973: both grew 2.7% — exact match
- 1973-1995: productivity slowdown → wage slowdown
- Post-1995 "new economy": technology-driven productivity acceleration

### Land and Capital
- Same theory: rental price = value of marginal product
- **Black Death** (14th century): 1/3 of population died → wages doubled, rents fell 50%+
- Factors used together: loss of one factor affects earnings of all others

## Chapter 19: Earnings and Discrimination

### Compensating Differentials
- Wage differences offset non-monetary job characteristics
- Coal miners paid more (dangerous); night shift paid more; professors paid less (rewarding work)

### Human Capital
- Education and training raise marginal product → higher wages
- College premium: men 42% (1974) → 81% (2014); women 35% → 71%
- Two explanations: **international trade** (unskilled labor cheap abroad) and **skill-biased technological change** (computers)
- 88% of economists agree technology is leading reason for rising inequality

### Discrimination
- Wage gaps don't prove discrimination — human capital, experience, occupation differ
- **Bertrand and Mullainathan** study: "Emily" received 50% more callbacks than "Lakisha" on identical resumes
- Competitive markets create **profit motive** to hire underpriced workers
- **Segregated streetcars**: companies opposed segregation laws — "separation was costly"

> "The railroad company did not initiate the segregation policy... their primary motives were economic."

- **Customer discrimination** can sustain wage gaps (basketball attendance study)
- **Government mandates** can also sustain discrimination (apartheid)

## Chapter 20: Income Inequality and Poverty

> "The inherent vice of capitalism is the unequal sharing of blessings. The inherent virtue of socialism is the equal sharing of miseries." — Winston Churchill

### U.S. Inequality
- Top quintile: 48.9% of all income (2014); bottom quintile: 3.6%
- Top 5% received 20.8% — more than bottom 40% combined
- 1935-1970: became more equal; 1970-2014: trend reversed
- U.S. more unequal than most advanced countries, similar to China

### Poverty
- 14.8% below poverty line (2014); fell from 22.4% (1959) to 11.1% (1973), then stalled
- **Black: 26.2%**; Hispanic: 23.6%; Female-headed households: 33.1%
- **In-kind transfers** (food stamps, Medicaid) not counted in standard measures
- **Life cycle** and **transitory income** overstate true inequality
- **Cox and Alm** (2008): income ratio top/bottom = 15x; but consumption per person = only 2.1x
- **Economic mobility**: 1 in 4 families below poverty line at least one year per decade, but fewer than 3% poor for 8+ years
- 4 of 5 millionaires are self-made

### Political Philosophy of Redistribution

**Utilitarianism** (Bentham, Mill): maximize total utility
- **Diminishing marginal utility**: extra dollar worth more to poor person
- But redistribution reduces work incentives — "leaky bucket" parable
- Government stops short of complete equality

**Liberalism** (John Rawls, *A Theory of Justice*, 1971)
- **Veil of ignorance**: design society without knowing your position
- **Maximin criterion**: maximize well-being of worst-off person
- Still allows inequality for incentive purposes

**Libertarianism** (Robert Nozick, *Anarchy, State, and Utopia*, 1974)
- Evaluate the **process**, not the **outcome**
- If income achieved fairly, distribution is just regardless of inequality

> "We are not in the position of children who have been given portions of pie by someone who now makes last minute adjustments." — Nozick

### Antipoverty Policies
- **Minimum wage**: poorly targeted (many are middle-class teenagers)
- **Welfare** (TANF, SSI): 1996 reform imposed 5-year lifetime limit
- **Negative income tax / EITC**: supplements low-income workers without requiring "need"
- **In-kind transfers**: ensure basic necessities but less efficient than cash
- **Work disincentive problem**: benefit phase-outs create effective marginal tax rates up to 100%+
- **Plato** recommended max 4:1 income ratio; modern society far exceeds this

## Chapter 21: The Theory of Consumer Choice

### Budget Constraint
- Shows affordable consumption bundles given income and prices
- **Slope = relative price** of two goods (opportunity cost)
- Pizza ($10) and Pepsi ($2): slope = 5 liters per pizza

### Indifference Curves
- Show bundles giving equal satisfaction
- **Marginal rate of substitution (MRS)**: rate consumer trades one good for another
- Four properties: higher preferred, downward-sloping, don't cross, bowed inward
- **Perfect substitutes**: straight lines (nickels and dimes)
- **Perfect complements**: right angles (left and right shoes)

### Optimization
- Consumer chooses point where indifference curve is tangent to budget constraint
- At optimum: **MRS = relative price** = ratio of marginal utilities
- **MUx/Px = MUy/Py** — marginal utility per dollar equalized across goods

### Income and Substitution Effects
- **Substitution effect**: price change makes one good relatively cheaper → buy more of it
- **Income effect**: price change alters purchasing power → buy more or less of both
- For normal goods: both effects increase quantity when price falls
- For inferior goods: effects conflict

### Applications

**Giffen Goods**: price increase raises quantity demanded
- Income effect dominates substitution effect for strongly inferior goods
- **Jensen and Miller** (2008): found Giffen behavior for rice among poor Chinese households

**Labor Supply**
- Higher wages: substitution effect → work more; income effect → work less (afford more leisure)
- **Historical evidence**: workweeks shortened as wages rose over century — income effect dominates
- Lottery winners: 25% quit working within a year (>$50,000 winnings); 40% quit (>$1 million)
- **Andrew Carnegie**: inherited wealth "deadens talents and energies"

**Saving and Interest Rates**
- Higher interest rates: substitution effect → save more; income effect → save less
- Ambiguous result — economists disagree on net effect
- Makes tax policy for encouraging saving uncertain
