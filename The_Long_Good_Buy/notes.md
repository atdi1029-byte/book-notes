# The Long Good Buy - Peter C. Oppenheimer
## Notes

### Preface & Introduction (pp. 1-30)

- Book is about economic and financial market cycles and the factors that affect them
- Despite enormous changes since mid-1980s (Cold War ended, globalisation, tech revolution, inflation collapsed), repeated patterns persist in financial markets
- Over 35 years: inflation expectations collapsed, US 10-year bonds from 11% to 2%, Fed funds from 8% to 1.5%, quarter of global govt bonds have negative yield
- Cray-2 supercomputer (1985) had similar processing power to iPhone 4
- Three major recessions since 1980s, plus multiple financial crises: S&L crisis 1986, Black Monday 1987, Japanese bubble 1986-92, Mexican crisis 1984, Asian/Russian/Argentine emerging market crises 1997-2002, ERM crisis 1992, tech collapse 2000, global financial crisis 2007-09, European sovereign debt crisis 2010-11
- Book aims not to predict but to identify signals and relationships between economic and financial cycles
- Key cycle theories: Kitchin (40-month, commodities/inventories), Juglar (7-11 years, capital investment), Kuznets (15-25 years, incomes), Kondratiev (50-60 years, technology)
- Fisher (1933) and Keynes (1936) examined interaction between real economy and financial sector
- Financial cycle is part of business cycle; private sector balance sheets are important triggers
- Efficient market hypothesis (Fama 1970) vs. Shiller's finding that P/E ratios provide guide to future long-term returns
- Risk-taking appetite is key channel through which policy affects cycles (Borio 2013)
- Fear, greed, crowd behaviour transcend specific periods - patterns repeat even under very different circumstances
- Howard Marks quote: "Economics and markets have never moved in a straight line in the past, and neither will they do so in the future"
- Core thesis: if equity investors hold 5+ years and recognise signs of bubbles/inflection points, they can benefit from the "long good buy"

- Roger Ferguson (former Fed vice chairman): "Detecting a bubble appears to require judgment based on scant evidence. It entails asserting knowledge of the fundamental value of the assets in question"
- Richard Thaler and Cass Sunstein's "Nudge" (2008) — behavioural economics impact on public policy. Thaler won Nobel Prize 2017
- Key insight: despite all changes, recognising signs of excess (pessimism OR optimism) and prospects of inflection points can help generate higher returns

### Chapter 2: Returns over the Long Run (pp. 47-60)

- Total return for US equities since 1860 has averaged about 10% per year across 1-year to 20-year holding periods
- US 10-year government bonds: averaged 5-6% returns over same periods
- Jeremy Siegel "Stocks for the Long Run" (1994): real returns remarkably stable — 7.0% per year from 1802-1870, 6.6% from 1871-1925, 7.2% since 1926
- Equity volatility (1-year standard deviation) roughly 3x that of bonds (10% vs 3%)
- Over 1-year periods: equities have negative returns 28% of the time vs 18% for bonds
- Over 5-year periods: equities negative 11%, bonds only 1%
- Over 10-year periods: equities negative only 3%, bonds 0%
- Over 20-year periods: neither equities nor bonds have ever had negative returns
- Key insight: for investors who can hold 5+ years, equities provide "the long good buy"
- 10-year rolling real returns chart shows worst periods: equities bought at start of WWI, WWII, late 1960s (before inflation spike), and tech bubble top (2000-2003) — lowest real returns in 100 years
- Best periods: 1920s boom, post-war 1950s reconstruction, 1980s/1990s (low/falling rates after large bear markets)
- Bond returns since 1980s have been truly remarkable historically — US Treasuries bought at peak of inflation cycle had 10%+ annualised real returns for 10 years, 7%+ for 20 years
- $1,000 invested in US govt bonds in 1980 would be worth ~$6,000 in real terms by time of writing
- Bonds bought in early 1990s: ~5% real return for 20 years — returns investors "used to hope for in equities"
- Austria launched 100-year bond at just 1.1% yield — extraordinary times
- Quarter of global government bonds have negative yield

**Equity Risk Premium (ERP)**
- Ex post ERP = actual achieved return of equities vs bonds over time
- Required ERP = expected future premium investors demand for taking equity risk vs safe bonds
- Mehra and Prescott (1985) "equity risk premium puzzle": 1889-1978, average real stock return ~7%, bonds ~1%, giving ERP of 6%+ — far too high to explain by standard risk aversion models
- Bernstein (1997): because valuations changed (P/E from ~10x to ~20x), ex post ERP overstates future expected premium
- Fama and French (2002): used discounted dividend model (DDM), found expected ERP averaged about 3%
- Shiller's "Irrational Exuberance" (2000): introduced CAPE (cyclically adjusted P/E using 10 years trailing earnings) as better predictor of returns than standard P/E
- ERP varies dramatically over periods depending on starting valuation: very negative after 1920s bubble, extraordinarily high in post-war 1950s-60s (low valuations), negative again after tech bubble and before financial crisis, very high after March 2009 trough

**The Power of Dividends**
- Since early 1970s, ~75% of S&P 500 total return attributable to reinvested dividends and compounding
- 1880-1980: US dividend payout ratio averaged 78% of earnings, dividend yield averaged 4.8%
- Buybacks only explicitly permitted by SEC since Rule 10b-18 in 1982
- Since 2000: dividend yield averaged 1.9%, buyback yield 2.0%. Combined 4% annual return = double investment in <18 years with zero price appreciation
- In European markets (STOXX Europe 600): 80% of 20-year rolling returns come from dividends (index heavy in mature industries: oil, banks, utilities, telecoms)
- Europe and Japan similar in price returns, but Europe outperforms Japan in total returns because Japan pays much less in dividends

**Factors That Affect Returns**
- Two key factors: timing of investment and valuation at time of investment
- Market timing: from beginning of 2009, buy-and-hold investor would have seen ~250% gain (~12% annualised)
- Avoiding best month each year since 1900 → ~2% annual return; avoiding worst month → ~18% annual return (~80% higher than fully invested)
- 60/40 portfolio: ~2% if missing best months, 12%+ if avoiding worst months
- Worst years: falls of 20-40%; best years: rises of 40-60%
- Most worst years occur near economic stress (recessions, sharply rising rates)
- S&P 500 best years: 1862 (+67%), 1933 (+53%), 1954 (+52%), 1879 (+50%)
- S&P 500 worst years: 1931 (-44%), 2008 (-37%), 1937 (-35%), 1907 (-30%)
- Bonds best year: 1982 (+39%); bonds worst year: 1931 (-13%)

**Valuations and Returns**
- Great valuation peaks (1929, 1968, 1999) followed by very poor risk-adjusted returns; great troughs (1931, 1974, 2008) followed by strong returns
- Shiller CAPE P/E vs 10-year forward returns: R² = 0.70 (very high predictive power over 10 years)
- R² = 0.20 for 2 years, 0.40 for 5 years, 0.60 for 20 years — valuation is better predictor at medium/long term
- Real yield gap (dividend yield minus real bond yield) as proxy for relative valuation: higher relative valuation for equities → lower future relative returns
- Main period where valuation failed as predictor: mid-1990s (equities didn't look cheap vs bonds but massively outperformed — reflecting onset of tech bubble)

**Diversification and the 60/40 Portfolio**
- 60/40 bull/bear markets since 1900: median bull ~3 years, +53% total real return (15% annualised); median bear ~1.5 years, -25% real (22% annualised loss)
- Strongest balanced portfolio period: 1920s, +360% over 9 years
- Post-financial crisis produced longest balanced bull market: 9+ years, ~10% real annualised
- Average 60/40 bull market: +81% (22% annualised) over 3.5 years

### Chapter 3: The Equities Cycle — Identifying the Phases (pp. 67-80)

- Equity markets move in cycles averaging ~8 years in the US (trough to peak)
- Returns split into four distinct phases, each driven by different factors

**THE FOUR PHASES (Oppenheimer's key framework):**

1. **Despair Phase** (avg 16 months): Bear market, peak to trough. Driven by falling valuations (P/E contraction) as market anticipates deteriorating macro. Earnings still modestly rising but prices fall ~43%. Valuations contract ~40%.

2. **Hope Phase** (avg 9 months): Market rebounds from trough. HIGHEST RETURNS of the cycle (40-50% price appreciation, annualising at 60%+ in US). Driven entirely by P/E expansion — earnings still falling (-9%). Starts when second derivative improves (data stops deteriorating). Best time to buy but also hardest psychologically — macro data still weak.

3. **Growth Phase** (avg 49 months): Longest phase. Earnings growth materialises (+62%). But returns are modest (+16%) because investors already prepaid during hope phase. P/E multiples actually CONTRACT ~30% as earnings catch up to prices.

4. **Optimism Phase** (avg 23 months): Final phase. Valuations rise again (+7 P/E points), outstripping earnings growth (-4%). Second-best returns (+62%). FOMO dominates. Sets stage for next correction.

**Key insights from the four phases:**
- 36% of total returns come from hope phase (just 9 months!)
- 51% from optimism phase
- Only 13% from the much longer growth phase
- Earnings growth is NOT when stock returns are earned — returns are earned in ANTICIPATION of growth
- Profits are still falling during the hope phase when returns are highest

**Cycle-by-cycle history:**
- 1970s: Structural bear market. Dow peaked 1972, not surpassed until November 1982. Rising inflation pushed up rates, crushed P/E (contracted 42-52%). Oil supply shock embedded in wage inflation. Growth phase was one of the longest on record.
- Early 1980s: Strong cycle driven by falling inflation, falling rates, declining ERP, substantial P/E expansion.
- 1990s: "Great Moderation" — solid growth, low inflation, low rates. Globalisation, Soviet collapse, China opening, central bank independence. Very strong cycle.
- 2000-2007: Best earnings growth cycle but lowest returns — much profit was illusory financial sector earnings from leverage. Ended with subprime crisis.
- 2008-now (post-crisis): Longest cycle, distorted by rolling crises (European sovereign debt 2010-11, EM/commodity 2015-16). Marked by QE, historically low rates, weak profit growth but rising valuations. Significant dispersion — US vastly outperformed Europe/EM. Technology driving much higher profits than rest of market.

**Mini/High-Frequency Cycles:**
- Within longer investment cycles, shorter industrial cycles driven by inventories and policy
- ISM/PMI as high-frequency proxy for economic cycle
- Post-2009 growth phase had THREE mini industrial cycles
- Best equity returns: when ISM below 50 but inflecting upward (recovery phase) — mirrors hope phase logic
- Worst phase: ISM below 50 and contracting (despair equivalent)
- Slowdown (ISM above 50 but falling): lacklustre/flat returns

**Bond Yields and the Cycle:**
- Returns in all ISM phases are higher when bond yields are falling
- Structurally falling bond yields (1980s) generated higher long-term returns than rising yields (1970s)
- Steepening yield curve → supportive monetary policy
- Inverted yield curve → restrictive policy, warning sign
- But short-term: rising yields from very low levels with improving growth can be positive for equities (confidence that policy is working)

### Chapter 4: Asset Returns through the Cycle (pp. 80-...)

- Different asset classes respond differently to growth and inflation changes across the cycle
- Late recession: defensive assets outperform (gold, long-term bonds benefit from lower policy rates and falling inflation expectations)
- Early recovery (second derivative improving): equities rebound sharply, gold and bonds underperform
- Financial assets (priced off future expectations) outperform "real" assets (priced off current supply/demand) in early recovery
- Late expansion: higher-beta equities (EM) dominate; commodities more neutral; fixed income underperforms (higher risk tolerance, higher inflation)
- Early recession: defensive assets outperform, oil still does well; cyclical/high-beta equities underperform most. Corporate debt does best in late recession as bond yields fall and growth risks moderate
- Inflation response: high and rising inflation bad for both equities and bonds. Rising inflation → higher bond yields via monetary tightening. Since 1990s, inflation low/stable = less important driver
- 1970s stagflation: equities AND bonds declined together — commodities were the key diversifier
- When inflation >3%, changes in inflation drove large rotations between equities, bonds, and real assets

**Assets across the Investment Cycle (Exhibit 4.2 — key data):**
- S&P 500 average annualised by phase: Despair -44%, Hope +69%, Growth +2%, Optimism +48%
- Hope phase: equities outperformed bonds and commodities in ALL six cycles since 1973
- Growth phase: commodities tend to lead — outperformed equities and bonds in 4 of 6 cycles. Commodities driven by actual supply/demand (not expectations) so they outperform when real growth materialises
- Optimism phase: equities outperform again in most cycles

**Bond Yields and Equities — The Complex Relationship:**
- Gordon growth model (DDM): Dividend yield + growth = Risk-free Rate + ERP
- If bond yields fall, dividend yield should fall (equity price rises) — BUT only if growth expectations unchanged. If lower yields reflect lower growth, no benefit to equities; ERP may rise, pushing prices DOWN
- Post-tech bubble: negative equity/bond correlation became norm — lower bond yields = lower growth expectations = bad for equities
- Historically positive correlation (bonds up = equities up) was norm. Turned negative after tech bubble and especially after GFC
- Correlation between equity/bond prices depends heavily on yield LEVEL:
  - Yields above 4-5%: positive correlation (falling bond prices = rising yields signal inflation → both fall)
  - Yields below 4-5%: NEGATIVE correlation (falling yields signal recession fears → equities fall too, bonds rise as safe haven)
- Factors determining impact of rising yields on equities:
  1. Point in cycle — earlier is more immune
  2. Speed of adjustment — slower is better
  3. Level of yields — historically UST 10y at 5%+ definitively bad for equities
  4. Valuation of equities — cheap = more immune
  5. Drivers — real or nominal, inflation-led rises easier to digest
- Sharp yield moves (>20bp weekly) have coincided with negative equity returns
- Average US equity performance during 17 rising yield periods: S&P +9% price return, average yield rise 145bp over 8 months

**Structural Shifts in Equity/Bond Valuation:**
- George Ross Goobey (1956): Imperial Tobacco pension fund manager who gave famous speech arguing pension funds should invest in equities for inflation-linked growth. Allocated entire pension fund to equities — started "cult of the equity"
- Goobey quote: "I know that people will say: 'Well, things are never going to be the same again', but... it has happened again, and again"
- Annualised real total return US equities 1956-2000: 7% — Goobey was right
- Post-tech bubble: "reverse yield gap" reversed again — dividend yields rose above bond yields as confidence in equities collapsed, deleveraging ensued
- Early 1990s: investors got 4.5% cash yield on equities vs 8% on 10-year bonds
- Post-GFC (10 years after): 5%+ cash yield on equities vs <2% on government bonds — massive structural shift in relative attractiveness
- Germany: dividend + buyback yield above 4% vs 10-year bund yield below 0%

### Chapter 5: Investment Styles over the Cycle (pp. 99-...)

- Generalising at micro level (individual companies, sectors) is riskier than broad market
- Sector cyclicality can change over time: chemicals shifted from bulk commodities to higher-value specialty products (coatings, adhesives, agrichemicals); tech sector now combines very cyclical semiconductors with less cyclical software
- Food producers: demand used to be highly stable but now more cyclical due to supermarket own-brand competition

**Four-quadrant sector framework (Exhibit 5.1):**
- Cyclical Growth: Technology, Industrials, Luxury — high growth, high economic sensitivity
- Cyclical Value: Commodities, Auto & Parts, Financials — low valuation, high sensitivity
- Defensive Growth: Retail, Health Care, Food & Beverage — faster growth, lower sensitivity
- Defensive Value: Utilities, Telecoms, Real Estate — low growth, low sensitivity, cheap
- Cyclical value best environment: accelerating growth + rising inflation/rates (operational leverage from high fixed costs boosts margins)
- Defensive growth best environment: weaker growth + lower rates (long-duration cash flows benefit from lower discount rates)
- Most cyclical sectors by EPS beta to GDP: Basic Resources, Autos & Parts, Technology
- Most defensive: Utilities, Telecoms, Real Estate, Food/Bev/Tobacco

**Cyclical vs Defensive across the Four Phases:**
- Despair: worst for cyclicals (avg -25% vs defensives), ~30% relative underperformance
- Hope: best for cyclicals (avg +16% outperformance, median +12%)
- Growth: most ambiguous — mini cycles within create varied outcomes
- Optimism: cyclicals generally outperform (rising valuations, higher risk tolerance)
- Cyclical/defensive performance closely tracks ISM/PMI levels

**Bond yields and cyclical/defensive:**
- Lower bond yields → cyclical underperformance (weaker growth signal)
- Rising bond yields → cyclical outperformance (stronger growth signal + inflation benefits operational leverage)
- Rate of change matters too: best for cyclicals = ISM below 50 but rising + bond yields rising (recovery)
- Worst for cyclicals: ISM below 50 and falling + bond yields falling (contraction)

**Value vs Growth Companies:**
- MSCI growth: classified by long-term/short-term forward EPS growth, current internal growth, historical EPS/sales trends (5 variables)
- MSCI value: book value to price, 12-month forward earnings to price, dividend yield (3 variables)
- Value tends to be more cyclical; growth tends to overlap with defensive
- Value vs growth across phases: LESS clear pattern than cyclical/defensive. Only consistent finding: value UNDERPERFORMS in optimism phase (avg -15%). Growth stocks get premium when investors are most confident and willing to extrapolate
- Graham and Dodd (1934): first identified "value premium" — low P/E stocks outperform over time
- Fama and French (1998): value outperformed growth by 7.68% per year (1975-1995) in 12 of 13 global markets

**Duration — key to value/growth relationship:**
- Equity duration (Macaulay 1938): time until investors receive cash flows. Tech = long duration (pay off far future). Utilities = short duration (immediate dividends)
- Long-duration stocks benefit MORE from falling rates; short-duration less sensitive
- 1980-2007: generally negative relationship between bond yields and value/growth (falling yields → strong growth, value benefited from positive macro)
- Late 1990s: sharp rotation to growth as low rates seen as beneficial to long-duration "new economy" companies
- Post-tech bubble (2000-2007): value premium reasserted as "old economy" restructured, growth stocks collapsed from extreme valuations
- Post-GFC (2007+): relationship reversed AGAIN — lower bond yields associated with growth outperformance. Value stocks crushed by ultra-low rates environment

**Four drivers of post-GFC style relationships:**
1. Tech earnings far outpaced rest of market; banks suffered from low/negative rates hampering loan margins
2. Even sector-neutral growth vs value: growth outperformed due to increased scarcity of sales growth (low nominal growth/inflation)
3. Relentless fall in bond yields: lower yields = greater benefit to long-duration (growth) companies, especially "disruptors" in mature industries
4. Higher risk premium: greater perceived risks to growth, deflation, geopolitics → investors pay premium for predictable/stable returns ("bond-like equities" — infrastructure, utilities with fixed contracts)

### Chapter 6: Bear Necessities — The Nature and Shape of Bear Markets (pp. 119-...)

- Bear markets are natural/inevitable part of investment cycle but vary enormously
- Timing crucial: selling too early is as costly as staying — losses in first 3 months of bear ≈ gains in final months of preceding bull
- 27 bear markets (≥20% decline) in S&P 500 since 1835; 10 in post-war period
- Most bear markets last ~2 years but some much longer

**Three triggers:**
1. Rising rates/inflation + recession fear
2. Exogenous shock (war, oil shock, EM crisis, technical dislocation)
3. Bursting of asset bubble / unwinding structural imbalances

**THREE TYPES OF BEAR MARKETS (Oppenheimer's key taxonomy):**

1. **Cyclical bear**: Rising rates, impending recession, expected profit falls. Most common type. Average: 27 months, -31% decline, 50 months to recover nominally
2. **Event-driven bear**: One-off shock (war, oil, EM crisis, technical). Short-lived rise in uncertainty/ERP. Average: 9 months, -29% decline, 15 months to recover — shortest and fastest to recover
3. **Structural bear**: Unwinding of major imbalances, financial bubbles, often deflation. DEEPEST and LONGEST. Average: 42 months, -57% decline, 111 months (9+ years!) to recover nominally

**Key bear market data (Exhibit 6.2 — comprehensive table):**
- S&P fell 86% Sep 1929-Jun 1932 (structural) — took 266 months (22 years!) to recover in nominal terms, 284 months in real terms
- 2007-2009 (structural): -57% over 17 months, 49 months to recover nominally, 55 in real
- 1973-1974 (structural): -48% over 21 months, 69 months nominal recovery, 154 months real (12+ years — inflation eroded nominal recovery)
- 1987 Black Monday (event-driven): -34% in just 3.3 months, recovered in 20 months
- Average of ALL bear markets: 28 months, -38%, 60 months to recover

**US bear market triggers since 1960s (Exhibit 6.1):**
- 1961-62: "Kennedy Slide" — rising rates, Cold War tension. No recession
- 1966: Inflation from Johnson's Great Society; Fed raised rates ~1.5% in one year. No recession
- 1968-70: Vietnam + inflation; Fed raised from 4% to 9%. Recession Dec 69-Nov 70
- 1973-74: Bretton Woods collapse, Nixon Shock, oil from $3 to $12. Recession Nov 73-Mar 75
- 1980-82: "Volcker crash" — second oil crisis, Fed raised from 9% to 19% in six months. Recession
- 1987: Black Monday flash crash — programme trading, currency tensions. No recession
- 1990: Gulf War, oil doubled. Recession Jul 90-Mar 91
- 2000-02: Dotcom bubble, Enron, 9/11. Recession Mar-Nov 2001
- 2007-09: Housing bubble, subprime, CDS collapse. Recession Dec 07-Jun 09

### Chapter 6 (continued): Bear Market Types in Detail (pp. 121-140)

**Cyclical Bear Markets:**
- Relate to standard economic downturn triggered by tighter monetary policy
- Key distinction: equity/bond prices respond to falling interest rates and leading indicators during cyclical bears
- Broadly a MONETARY phenomenon — generally reaches trough 3-6 months after first rate cut
- Markets recover before end of recession because financial prices anticipate recovery from falling rates
- Average cyclical bear: ~30% fall, ~27 months duration, 4+ years to recover nominally, 6 years real
- Average monthly volatility peak-to-trough: 14% (vs 20% for structural)
- Profitability: short-lived declines; profits start recovering ~10 months after bear market ends
- Before: strong economic growth, rising interest rates
- After: rate cuts with rapid response, profits recovering quickly, equity market responding to falling rates
- Cyclical bears often global (depend on economic cycle), but not always synchronised — e.g., US equity market decoupled from Europe in 1991

**Event-Driven Bear Markets:**
- Not triggered by economic cycle/rising rates — result of unexpected exogenous event (political shock, oil price spike)
- Raises required risk premium even if equities weren't expensive
- Usually short-lived, not associated with fundamental shift in economic/corporate conditions
- Average decline: 29% (similar to cyclical). But duration only ~9 months (vs 2 years cyclical), recovery to previous peak in just over 1 year
- Definitional problem: sometimes event triggers follow-on waves (e.g., 1973 oil crisis wasn't purely event-driven — led to inflation/rate rises)
- Russian debt default & Asian crises 1997-98: resulted in global monetary easing when domestic demand was strong → cost of capital fell, import costs fell, margins boosted → valuations expanded → set scene for tech boom and bust of 2000
- Event-driven bears typically emerge with fairly modest inflation; stable monetary environment prevented events from causing sustained bear

**Structural Bear Markets:**
- Preceded by financial bubbles, rising interest rates or tightening credit, acute overvaluation
- Accompanied by major imbalances: significant increase in private sector debt
- Recovery dictated by unwinding of imbalances, NOT simply by monetary easing
- Much deeper and sharper than cyclical; recovery typically takes about a DECADE
- High volatility prominent during both decline and recovery
- Average: falls of over 50%, lasting ~4 years, 8-10 years to recoup losses
- Annualised recovery rate not materially different from cyclical — just takes much longer, with more rallies and false starts
- Usually result of misallocation of resources: new technology cycle + falls in cost of capital → savings/investment imbalance → rising predisposition to shocks
- Often coexist with large current account or budget deficits + high corporate/consumer debt

**Exhibit 6.3 — US imbalances shifted from private to public sector post-2008:**
- Pre-crisis: households & corporations (incl. financials) debt/GDP peaked ~270% around 2008
- Post-crisis: private debt fell while govt debt/GDP rose from ~35% to ~80%; Fed holdings rose from ~$500bn to ~$4,500bn
- Debt hasn't disappeared — shifted from private sector (vulnerable) to public sector (less vulnerable)

**UK structural bear early 1990s:** serious imbalances, deep recession, sharp property/equity falls. Process speeded up when sterling crashed out of ERM — devaluation acted as sharp economic adjustment. Easier for small open economies (UK, Sweden) than large closed economies (US)

**Structural bear characteristics summary:**
- More savage in magnitude and duration
- Recovery takes much longer
- Associated with ongoing structural economic problems

**Interest Rate Cuts Have Less Impact on Structural Bears:**
- Rising rates NOT usually the trigger for structural bears (unlike cyclical)
- Many structural bears preceded by VERY LOW interest rates and inflation — the factor that helped the boom in the first place
- Since rising rates aren't the cause, falling rates aren't the cure
- Recovery owes more to availability/demand for money than the price of money
- Structural bears don't typically end until future returns on capital rise sufficiently to boost investment
- Excess capacity must unwind first — can take longer than rate cuts

**Price Shocks — Deflation is Common in Structural Bears:**
- Price shock (inflationary or deflationary) common during structural bears; more often deflation
- Deflationary forces are by-product of falls in cost of capital and overinvestment
- Susceptibility to price shocks slows recovery, lengthens time for return on capital to rise

**Belief in a "New Era" / New Valuations:**
- Many great structural bears preceded by financial bubbles and "new era" belief
- Greenspan testimony Feb 26, 1997: "Regrettably, history is strewn with visions of such 'new eras' that, in the end, have proven to be a mirage"

**High Levels of Debt:**
- Rising corporate, personal, and government debt often associated with structural bears
- BIS study (Borio and Lowe 2002): examined 34 countries over 40 years — rapid debt growth is the SINGLE BEST LEADING INDICATOR of financial crises

**Equity Market Leadership Becoming Narrow:**
- "Nifty Fifty" enthusiasm late 1960s: top 50 companies raced ahead while rest failed to gain; then collapsed MORE than market in 1970s bear
- Same in Japan late 1980s (banks/property dominated)
- Late 1990s S&P: rose avg 25%/yr 1994-1999 but >half of constituents FELL during that period
- 1994-1996: two-thirds of stocks rose only 10%, in line with post-WWII average
- By 1999: 5 companies with biggest market value increase accounted for 42% of total market increase; top 100 accounted for 139% of increase (vs avg 87% since 1967)
- Europe end of 1999: top 20 companies by market cap = ~30% of total market

**High Volatility:**
- Structural bears feature high volatility during both price declines AND recovery
- More rallies and false starts than cyclical bears

**Bear Markets and Corporate Profits (Exhibit 6.5):**
- Since 1960, average EPS actually INCREASED by 5% during bear markets — but distorted by:
  1. EPS doesn't fall (or falls little) in event-driven bears — these are valuation/derisking events
  2. EPS decline period doesn't coincide with bear market dates — market anticipates
- Excluding event-driven bears: average EPS fall = 19%, similar to average price fall of 40% (excluding event-driven)

- EPS declines lag start of bear market by 5 months on average — "prices start to fall 5 months before EPS does"
- Market rallies late in cycle even as profits may have peaked (optimism phase)

**2007 Financial Crisis — A Structural Bear with a Difference:**
- Had hallmarks of structural bear: rising imbalances, bubble (but in real estate, not stocks)
- Less "new era" belief in equities than prior structural bears
- What set it apart: the POLICY RESPONSE — rapid rate cuts + QE
- Lower risk-free rates triggered search for yield in bonds, pushed up present value of future income streams
- Very low inflation + unprecedented policy → extended cycle and rising valuations
- Resulted in sharper rebound in equity/asset prices than typical structural bear

**Finding an Indicator to Flag Bear Market Risk:**
- Three main problems: (1) all bear markets are unique, (2) many false negatives, (3) equity prices anticipate the future — hard to find anything that leads them
- Goldman Sachs tested 40+ variables across macro, market-based, and technical categories
- Most variables unreliable — no consistent pattern, lagged market, or too volatile
- No single bear market signalled by ALL indicators; no single indicator has 100% hit rate
- Most consistently useful pre-bear indicators: measures of UNEMPLOYMENT and VALUATION
- Technical variables (positioning, sentiment surveys) particularly poor — tended to lag

**Typical Conditions Prior to Bear Markets:**
- Most common: deteriorating growth momentum + policy tightening + high valuation
- "At the very least, in combination they may provide valuable information after the peak of the market on whether a bear market bounce is genuinely the start of a bigger fall rather than a shorter correction"

**Six key bear market indicators:**
1. **Unemployment**: very low unemployment consistent feature pre-bear; combining low unemployment + high valuation = useful signal for potential risk
2. **Inflation**: rising inflation important contributor to past recessions/bears (tightens policy). Peak of inflation lags equity market peak. Post-GFC: lack of inflation supported longer cycle + less volatility
3. **Yield curve**: tighter policy → flat/inverted curve. Flat curves (prior to inversion) also followed by low returns/bears. QE/falling inflation expectations weakened signal. Prefer 3-month to 10-year measure; 0-6 quarter forward spread better than back-end. Combining flat/inverted curve + high valuation = useful bear indicator
4. **Growth momentum at a high**: strong/accelerating growth tends to be followed by lower returns when pace moderates. Highest returns = ISM low but recovering; lowest = ISM low and deteriorating (-1.6%/month). ISM in highest quartile → followed by lower returns
5. **Valuation**: high valuations feature of most bear markets but rarely the trigger alone. Valuation can stay high for long periods. When combined with other fundamental triggers, bear risks elevated
6. **Private sector financial balance**: total income minus total spending of households/firms — measure of financial overheating risk. Selected over credit growth or home prices for empirical track record and intuitive appeal

**A Framework for Anticipating Bear Markets (Exhibit 6.8):**
- Combination of all six indicators provides reasonable signal for bear market risk
- No single indicator reliable alone but combination works
- Bear market risk indicator plotted against MSCI world equities since 1955
- When indicator close to highs → some indication of risk; close to lows → opportunities
- Also provides information about prospective 5-year returns
- Has been reasonably successful highlighting turning points at extremes

### Chapter 7: Bull's Eye — The Nature and Shape of Bull Markets (pp. 139-140+)

- Bull markets = three phases of cycle not associated with sustained drawdowns: hope, growth, optimism
- Can vary enormously: some long/strong with rising valuations; others flat/trendless where return comes from dividend/earnings growth

**The "Super Cycle" Secular Bull Market:**
- Long-term upward trend in equities comes in phases, not straight lines
- Three long "super cycles" or secular bull markets since WWII:
  1. **1945-1968: Post-War Boom** ("Golden Age of Capitalism")
  2. **1982-2000: Disinflation Era**
  3. **Post-2009** (implied)
- Each punctuated by sharp drawdowns/mini bears but driven by specific structural factors that persisted even during corrections

**1945-1968 Post-War Boom:**
- Marshall Plan (European Recovery Plan) boosted growth, reduced unemployment
- Strong productivity growth in Europe and East Asia; post-war baby boom strengthened demand
- Equity risk premium declined as risks to global system faded
- New international institutions: IMF, World Bank, Bretton Woods monetary system, GATT (1948), UNCTAD (1964)
- Kennedy Round of GATT: cuts to trade tariffs averaging 35-40% on many items — "the most important trade and tariff negotiation ever held"
- Nifty Fifty emerged 1960s: IBM, Xerox, Texas Instruments, Burroughs, Merck, Pfizer, Eli Lilley, Avon, McDonald's, Polaroid, Kodak
- By 1972: P/E on Polaroid 90x, McDonald's 85x, Walt Disney 82x. Average P/E for Nifty Fifty ~45x (vs S&P at 33x). Top 5 biggest at 35.5x
- US dollar became overvalued under Bretton Woods fixed exchange rate system
- Johnson's Great Society + Vietnam spending put pressure on gold standard
- Nixon 1971: "temporary" suspension of dollar's convertibility into gold → Nifty Fifty bubble burst
- Equity markets plateaued ~1966; US peak came 1968. Real decline 1966-1982: 75%
- 1973: Watergate, Arab-Israeli War, OPEC oil embargo → further instability
- End of 1970s: sharp rallies but enthusiasm killed by rate hikes (Fed raised discount rate to all-time high of 14%)
- 1981: inflation, high unemployment, economic stagnation → further lows

**1982-2000: The Start of Disinflation:**
- Fall in inflation = key driver of this secular bull
- Investors suffered "money illusion" after 1970s inflation — two errors: (1) capitalised future earnings at nominal rate, (2) failed to account for gains from depreciating real value of nominal liabilities
- Volcker credit crunch: Fed tightened from 1977, took rates from ~10% to close to 20%
- From that point, inflation fell globally → vigorous recovery, confidence, valuations rising
- Aug 1982 - Dec 1999: compound real return on DJIA = 15%/year (well above long-run average or earnings/book value growth)
- Most of this secular bull = VALUATION EXPANSION pushing up both equity and bond returns
- 1980s deregulation/reform: Reagan's Economic Recovery Act of 1981 (top income tax from 70% to 28% by 1986), partial repeal of Glass-Steagall (1933), privatisation programme in UK under Thatcher

### Chapter 7: Bull's Eye — The Nature and Shape of Bull Markets (pp. 139-148)

**2009 Onwards — Start of QE and the "Great Moderation":**
- S&P 500 collapsed 57% from 2007 peak; started longest bull market in history
- Strength of recovery partly function of scale of preceding decline
- US housing collapse: >$1 trillion in sub-prime mortgages outstanding, huge loss of household wealth
- Between 2007-2010, median household wealth in US fell 44%, to levels below 1969
- Fed Chairman Bernanke: "too-big-to-fail financial institutions were both a source (though by no means the only source) of the crisis and among the primary impediments to policymakers' efforts to contain it"
- March 2009: Fed announced $1 trillion in newly created dollars on back of govt and mortgage bonds → QE critical in triggering stock market rebound
- Second major contributor: ascent of large technology companies, becoming largest sector in US equity market

**UK privatisation context:** companies in public ownership = 12% of GDP in 1979, only ~2% by 1997. France Telecom: $7.1bn IPO in 1997, $10.4bn secondary offering a year later
- 1982-2000 secular bull interrupted by: S&L crisis early 1980s, 1987 crash, 1994 bond crisis (30yr yields rose ~200bp in 9 months), 1998 Asia crisis — still "super cycle" because structural factors persisted
- Fall of Berlin Wall 1989 → Dax surged 30% (Oct 89 - Jul 90)
- More integrated global economy → decline in discount rate; end of Cold War reduced equity risk premium
- 1998 Asia crisis → decisive policy response → looser money → propelled tech bubble of late 1990s → bust in 2000

- Highest annualised returns come AFTER the deepest bear markets

- Definition: no bear market, no bull market with >25% return, over less than 2 years

### Chapter 8: Blowing Bubbles — Signs of Excess (pp. 149-160+)

- Bubbles can be concentrated in single industry/asset class or broad-based across whole market
- Recovery from bursting bubbles can take a long time
- No precise definition of a bubble; difficulty of detection widely commented on
- Former Federal Board vice chairman: "even with the benefit of hindsight, statistical tests attempting to confirm the existence of bubbles in historical episodes can remain inconclusive"
- Working definition: "rapid acceleration in prices and valuations that makes an unrealistic claim on future growth and returns"
- Not all strong price rises = bubbles. Problems start when rapid price increase creates seemingly virtuous cycle → attracts new investors → excess capital
- Fear of missing out (FOMO): "the more a theme is spoken about and the greater the attention it receives, the greater the interest from investors"

**Charles Mackay (1841):** "men think in herds; it will be seen that they go mad in herds, while they recover their senses slowly, one by one"

**Robert Shiller, *Irrational Exuberance* (2000):** bubble = "a situation in which news of price increases spurs investor enthusiasm which spreads by psychological contagion from person to person, in the process amplifying stories that might justify the price increase and bring in a larger and larger class of investors, who, despite doubts about the real value of the investment, are drawn to it partly through envy of others' successes and partly through a gambler's excitement"

**Case and Shiller (2003):** pre-2008 housing crisis, 83-95% of buyers in 2003 expected annual housing price growth of ~9% over next 10 years — well above long-run averages

**Famous bubbles through history:**
1. 1630s: Tulip mania, Holland
2. 1720: South Sea bubble (UK) and Mississippi bubble (France)
3. 1790s: Canal mania, UK
4. 1840s: Railway bubble, UK
5. 1873: Railway bubble, US
6. 1920s: Stock market boom, US
7. 1980s: Land and stock bubble, Japan
8. 1990s: Technology bubble, global
9. 2007: Housing/banking bubble, US and Europe

**Spectacular Price Appreciation ... and Collapse:**

**Tulip Mania (1630s):** Nov 1636 - Feb 1637, some bulbs increased 20x. Single bulb = value of luxury townhouse. Financial innovation played role: futures market developed for bulbs. Most contracts paid by credit notes → system vulnerable to contagion. Lower-quality plain bulbs (which attracted smaller speculators) never recovered from crash

**South Sea Bubble (1720):** Shares went from £128 (Jan 1720) to £1,050 (end of June 1720), collapsed to £175 by September. British Parliament's Bubble Act gave South Sea Company Royal Charter, enhancing credibility. Mississippi Company in France: stock rose 6,200% then collapsed 99%

**UK Railway Bubble (1840s):** By 1850, railway shares fallen avg 85% from peak; total value of shares less than half the capital spent on them. Celebrities/politicians invested: Brontë sisters, John Stuart Mill, Charles Darwin, Benjamin Disraeli. King George I invested in South Sea, Isaac Newton reportedly lost £20,000 (~£3m today). In 1845 an author wrote "A short and sure guide to railroad speculation" praising railways as safest investment — shortly before epic collapse. Britain's railway track: 98 miles (1830) → 104,333 miles (1860). Returns failed to match expectations despite real transformational impact

**US Railway Boom (1870s):** Post-Civil War expansion. Bank loans between 1868-1873 increased 7x faster than deposits. Collapse → "Long Depression" (worst economic downturn until Great Depression). Over 7 years after 1873 panic, ~half of factories closed. Stock/railway prices fell 60% (1873-1878). Took a decade for investment to pick up

**1929 Crash:** Black Monday Oct 28 — DJIA fell 13% (already down 6% since early Sept), then another 12% following days. Index didn't recover to previous peak until November 1954 (Ferguson 2005). At lows: dividend yield on DJIA reached 9.5%. NYSE seat sold for $17,000 (vs peak of $650,000 in 1929)

**Japan Bubble (1980s):** BoJ cut rates from 5% to 2.5% by early 1987. Plaza Accord 1985 depreciated dollar vs yen. Japanese companies bought Rockefeller Center, golf courses in Hawaii/California. Imperial Palace in Tokyo reportedly worth more than entire value of France or California. Land value in Japan 1988 = 4x value of all US land (US 25x larger). ¥10,000 note dropped in Ginza district worth less than the ground it covered. Capital gains on stocks/land = 452% of nominal GDP (1986-89); subsequent losses = 159% of nominal GDP (1990-93). Mitsui, Sumitomo, Mitsubishi, C Itoh all had higher sales than General Motors. Tokkin accounts (1984): companies could trade securities tax-free on capital gains; corporate gains from Tokkin funds rose from ¥240bn (1985) to ¥952bn (1987). Nearly half of individuals seeking help from Japan Credit Counselling Association in Tokyo (1989) had 11-20 credit cards. Nikkei rose ~20%/yr from 1981-1990 (5x increase)

**Tech Bubble (1990s-2000):** Yahoo IPO April 1996: $13 → $33 in single day (>doubled). In 1999 Qualcomm rose 2,619%. Thirteen large cap stocks rose >1,000%; another seven rose >900%. Nasdaq increased 5x between 1995-2000, reaching P/E of 200x (higher than even Japan's 70x). April 2000: Nasdaq lost 34% in 1 month. Priceline fell 94%. By Oct 2009 trough, Nasdaq had fallen nearly 80%. By end of 2002 downturn: $5 trillion in market cap lost. Nasdaq-100 dropped to 1,114 (down 78% from peak)

**Shanghai composite:** rose 165% (61% annualised) June 2013 - June 2015, then collapsed 48% through March 2016

**Belief in a "New Era" ... This Time Is Different:**
- Key characteristic: belief that something has fundamentally changed — new technology, innovation, growth opportunity
- Schumpeter: speculation often occurs at start of new industry
- Study of 51 major innovations (1825-2000): bubbles in equity prices evident in 73% of cases. Magnitude increases with radicalness of innovation, potential for network effects, and public visibility at time of commercialisation
- Innovation was important even in South Sea and Mississippi bubbles: both companies issued shares in exchange for government debt (converting national debt into equity — a financial innovation). Both had exclusive rights to exploit resources (tobacco, slave trade)
- First publicly traded insurance companies emerged from Bubble Act in Britain — limited liability insurance changed nature of risk-sharing, increasing appetite for risky ventures
- Atlantic trade routes opened by maritime navigation advances → one of first major forms of globalisation

**Canal boom (1770s-1790s):** First canal opened 1767 by Duke of Bridgewater. Return on capital in canals fell from pre-bubble peak of 50% to just 5%; quarter century later only 25% of canals could pay dividends

**1920s US boom:** Radio penetration grew from negligible to nearly 1/3 of US homes by end of decade. RCA shares: $5 → $500, then collapsed 98% (1929-32), didn't return to highs for 30 years. AT&T had become govt-approved monopoly by 1913. In 1915, first transcontinental telephone call (NY-SF, 3,400 miles). "American system" of cooperative labour relations + Prohibition → boosted productivity expectations. 1920s features repeated in Japan 1990s: too much easy money + belief in productivity improvements

**Personal anecdote (Oppenheimer):** one of his first job offers was with a leading Japanese bank — "when I told people about the offer most thought my future would be secure working for a Japanese bank, at the cutting edge of global finance." Same pattern later shifted to China: *When China Rules the World* (2009) by Martin Jacques

**PC revolution 1980s:** IBM facilitated PC commercialisation in 1981. PC demand boomed, hundreds of manufacturers. 1983: Atari, Texas Instruments, Coleco announced losses. Commodore, Columbia Data Systems, Eagle Computer went bust. Surviving stocks took years to recover — same pattern as post-railway-boom

**Technology bubble and communication costs:** Cost of 3-min telephone call NY-London fell from $4.37 (1990, in 2000 dollars) to $0.40 in 2000

**Deregulation and Financial Innovation:**
- Light touch regulation/deregulation = common ingredient in bubble buildup
- UK railway: repeal of Bubble Act in 1825 (introduced after South Sea collapse) made it easier to register companies and attract public investment. By 1845, railway permit applications went directly to House of Commons committees — but many parliamentarians were investors, so huge numbers of permits approved. By 1846: 272 Acts of Parliament setting up new railway companies
- 1920s US: Federal Reserve established 1913 → greater investor confidence; Coolidge relaxed antitrust laws → wave of mergers
- Japan 1980s: Ministry of Finance allowed warrant bonds in London (1981) — warrants gave option to buy shares, rising stock prices made warrants valuable, enabling companies to borrow at very low rates. More borrowing → more warrants → more demand for stock = perceived virtuous cycle. Also Tokkin accounts (1984) = tax-free trading vehicles
- 1990s-2000s: derivative market growth — notional amounts of derivatives in interest rates and currency grew 457% (1994-2000), equivalent to 452% growth from 2001-2007
- 2007 housing bubble: not really evident in stock market valuations but collapse caused huge stock falls. Light regulation of financial institutions + financial product innovation (MBS, CDOs) were key ingredients
- Carlota Perez (2009): "masters of the universe" label for financial geniuses "were seen as powerful innovators, spreading risk and somehow magically evaporating it in the vast complexity of the financial galaxy"
- CDOs valued on "mark to market" basis → when housing fell, credit markets collapsed → market illiquidity → bank write-downs

**Easy Credit:**
- Rapid growth of new entrants in 1873 US railway bubble facilitated by easy money and exchange banks offering loans against collateral

### Chapter 8 (continued): Blowing Bubbles — Signs of Excess (pp. 161-170)

**Easy Credit (continued):**
- 1873 US railway bubble: railway companies allowed private investors to buy on margin with only 10% deposit; railway had right to call rest of capital at any time — exacerbating fallout
- US rail track grew from 35,000 to 70,000 miles (1865-1873), with 18,000 miles laid in 1873 alone
- Of 364 railway operators in 1872, only 194 paid a dividend
- John Crooke and Company (famous financier) went bankrupt in 1873 trying to build second transatlantic railway (Northern Pacific) — triggered chain of corporate failures, brokerage collapses; NYSE closed for 12 days
- Galbraith (1955): explosion in margin borrowing = significant cause of 1929 crash; also argued significant contributor to 1987 crash
- Cheap credit central to Japanese bubble: in 1998 world's 10 largest banks were ALL Japanese, using cost-of-capital advantages to capture global market share. By late 1980s combined market cap of largest 13 Japanese banks > 5x the top 50 banks globally. Today top 4 banks by assets are Chinese
- Dotcom bubble: record capital flowed into Nasdaq from 1997. By 1999, 39% of all venture capital went to internet companies; 295 of 457 IPOs were internet-related; 91 more internet IPOs in Q1 2000 alone

**New Valuation Approaches:**
- 1920s: academics argued stocks no riskier than bonds but offered greater returns; emphasis on compound growth in equities
- Charles Dice, *New Levels in the Stock Market*: argued prices were too low, hadn't priced in "triple revolutions in production, distribution and finance"
- Benjamin Graham, *The Intelligent Investor* (1949): "old standards of valuation are no longer applicable" due to Fed's attempts to avoid depression via low rates
- Japan 1990s: equity P/E surge; equity yield spread implied expected nominal GDP growth of 8% — "highly improbable" given low inflation and demographics
- *The Economist* (April 15, 1989): "What Japanese investors have become aware of is the dramatic way Japan's blue chip companies have changed the sources of their earnings through restructuring. This has made their profits too erratic to give any meaning to rigid measures such as a P/E ratio"
- Cooper, Dimitrov and Rau (2001): companies that changed names to include ".com" or internet-related terms in late 1990s saw average 53% stock price increase in following days — even if company had little IT activity

**Accounting Problems and Scandals:**
- Post-bubble accounting revelations = recurring feature across history
- Arthur Smith (1848), *The Bubble of the Age; or, the Fallacy of Railway Investment, Railway Accounts, and Railway Dividends*: argued railway companies had been paying dividends out of CAPITAL, not revenue. "The dividends of every railway company since the introduction of locomotive power have been paid by charging sums to capital which should be credited to the revenue account"
- George Hudson (MP) — failed due to fraudulent practice of paying dividends out of capital (same as South Sea bubble)
- Graham and Dodd (1934) in *Security Analysis*: "in 1928 and 1929 there occurred a wholesale and disastrous relaxation of the standards of safety previously observed by the exhibit houses of issue"
- Japan 1990s: "Zaitech" (manufactured accounts) allowed companies to manipulate assets → accounting scandals. Summer 1991: exposed secret payments of >$1 billion from securities firms to select clients (to reimburse trading losses). Nomura Securities (world's biggest brokerage) worked to manipulate Tokyu Corp stock. Banks like Tokai and Kyowa-Saitama issued fictitious certificates of deposit as "collateral" for real estate loans
- Graham and Dodd (1934): "instead of judging the market by established standards of value, the new era based its standards of value upon the market price"
- Enron: *Fortune* named it America's most innovative company 6 years running (1996-2001). Filed bankruptcy Dec 2, 2001; audited balance sheet had understated long-term debt by $25 billion
- WorldCom: filed $3.8 billion in expenses as capital investment + $3.3 billion in irregular reserve manipulation

**Summary — Common Features of Bubbles:**
1. Belief in a "new era" or technology
2. Deregulation and financial innovation
3. Easy availability of credit and financial conditions
4. Justification of new valuation measures
5. The emergence of accounting scandals and irregularities

These are warning signs that a bull market is turning into a bubble that could lead to severe structural bear market

### Chapter 9: How the Cycle Has Changed Post the Financial Crisis (pp. 171-180+)

**Part III begins: "Lessons for the Future: A Focus on the Post-Financial Crisis Era; What Has Changed and What It Means for Investors"**

- Post-2007-2009 environment "particularly unusual" — traditional patterns and relationships between economic and financial markets have broken down in some cases

**Wave 1 — US (2007-2009):**
- Housing market collapse → broader credit crunch → Lehman Brothers bankruptcy → TARP + QE
- Sub-prime mortgages, banking problems
- Stresses extended to European banks (very highly levered, heavily exposed to southern European real estate)

- Collapse in commodity prices and EM equities, China at epicentre
- Aug 2015: China devalued currency against USD
- Brent oil: nearly $100/barrel (summer 2014) → $46 (January 2016)
- Europe hit again (large EM exposure); US experienced milder/shorter correction — seen as safe haven

**2016 turning point:** all global equity markets moved higher together. For first time in cycle, large share of returns came from profit growth (not just valuation expansion). MSCI AC World recorded one of highest risk-adjusted returns since mid-1980s

**The Unusual Gap between Financial Markets and Economies:**
- Post-crisis cycle: economic cycle much LONGER than normal but much WEAKER
- US at time of writing: longest economic expansion in 150 years
- But recovery much slower than average post-1950 recessions (Exhibit 9.2)
- Slow growth post-GFC even more pronounced in Europe (impact of sovereign debt + banking crises)
- Consistent with historical pattern: recessions driven by housing/banking collapses → slower/weaker recoveries
- Federal Reserve Board of San Francisco study of ~200 recessions since 1850: "a recession and recovery path associated with a financial crisis peak is likely to be much more prolonged and more painful than that found after a normal peak"
- Romer and Romer (2017): OECD panel — GDP typically ~9 percentage points lower 5 years after extreme financial crisis
- US recovery pace very similar to Japan early 1990s (banking/real estate collapse) — but US pulled ahead more recently due to more aggressive policy

**Striking paradox:** WEAKEST economic recovery but STRONGEST financial market recovery
- S&P 500 recorded longest rally ever — over a decade-long boom from March 2009
- Exhibit 9.5: US recouped 100% of previous high within 4 years of 2009 crisis; after 1929 US and 1990 Japan, still at ~50% after same period
- Much of equity recovery = function of loose financial conditions, zero rates, QE — recovery much sharper than after similarly deep bears in past

**All Boats Were Lifted by the Liquidity Wave:**
- Common driver across all asset classes: falling risk-free rates → rising valuations
- Gap between "inflation" in real economy vs financial assets has been notable
- Exhibit 9.6: asset prices (S&P 500, European HY, US HY, MSCI World, etc.) rose 100-300%+ since Jan 2009; real economy measures (wages, CPI, house prices, commodities) rose only 0-50%
- Post-financial-crisis period generated longest and strongest bull market in standard balanced portfolio (60% US equities / 40% US govt bonds)

- Revenue growth weaker: roughly half of typical proportion (partly due to lower inflation)

### Chapter 9 (continued): How the Cycle Has Changed Post the Financial Crisis (pp. 181-200)

**Lower Inflation and Interest Rates (continued):**
- Exhibit 9.8: real bond yields (10yr nominal minus current inflation) turned NEGATIVE — US and Germany both below zero by late 2010s, after being 4-8% through 1960s-1990s
- Possible explanations for low real rates:
  1. Excess of savings over investment driven equilibrium real rates down (Summers 2015 secular stagnation hypothesis: chronically weak aggregate demand + ultra-low policy rates kept desired saving above investment)
  2. Global saving glut (Bernanke 2005) + shortage of safe assets (Caballero and Farhi 2017): EM excess savings → current account surpluses → flowed into advanced economies, depressing real rates
  3. Slower economic growth + lower inflation (demographics, rapid technological disruption)
- Forward market inflation measures also fallen; Phillips curve has flattened
- Since 2000s: more effective central bank forward guidance → lower/more stable inflation expectations
- QE has also been responsible for suppressing yields

**A Downtrend in Global Growth Expectations:**
- Significant fall in long-term growth rates since financial crisis — reflected in long-term forecasts AND in corporate sales/EPS growth
- 10-year rolling average of sales growth has fallen across US, Europe, World
- Developed world revenue growth rate converging toward levels Japan has experienced since late 1980s bubble collapse

**The Fall in Unemployment and Rise in Employment:**
- Labour market surprisingly strong despite slow GDP growth
- US, UK, Germany, Japan: unemployment fell to levels unseen for 40-50 years
- US employment grew for more months without contraction than ever before (Exhibit 9.10: >120 months by 2019)
- Explanations: weaker welfare states, lower taxes making employment more attractive, significant increase in female labour participation, less union power/collective bargaining, ageing populations, rise in new labour market entrants
- Technology helped labour market grow and become more flexible: cost of filling a vacancy fell 80% in past 10 years (*The Economist*); people using internet for job search reduced time unemployed by up to 25%
- Despite rising employment, wages and inflation remained very low — key unusual feature
- Labour share of nonfarm business output fell from ~66% (1947-early 2000s) to ~56% (Exhibit 9.11) — ongoing fall in labour share of GDP, rise in profit share of GDP

**The Rise in Profit Margins:**
- Relentless rise in corporate margins offset weakening sales growth
- Drivers: lack of labour pricing power (growing power of technology), rapid rise in margins at faster-growing tech companies, growing globalisation
- German example: low wage inflation despite low unemployment because workers pushing for higher wages risk jobs shifting to central Europe
- Growing gap between margins in economy as a whole vs stock market specifically (Exhibit 9.12)
- US profit share of GDP has been FALLING while S&P net income margins have been RISING — divergence explained by: (1) 2017 US tax cuts benefited large international companies in stock market, (2) stock market has much higher proportion of large tech companies with higher margins
- Risk: rising wages starting to wear down margins; if valuations stop rising and margins peak, lower sales growth → lower earnings growth → lower returns

**Falling Volatility of Macro Variables:**
- Volatility of GDP growth, inflation, and unemployment has declined, especially since 1980s (Exhibit 9.13)
- Independence of central banks + globalisation (post-Soviet collapse) → lower macro volatility
- 1990s "Great Moderation" ended with tech bubble, but since then macro volatility fell again
- Typical recession drivers (industrial shocks, oil shocks, inflationary overheating) have become less threatening
- Current cycle likely to be even longer absent significant rate rises, financial bubbles, or macro imbalances
- Volatility of company earnings (EBITDA) also fallen (Exhibit 9.14: median S&P 500 company 10-year EBITDA growth variability fell from ~38 in late 1980s to ~20 by late 2010s)
- Historically profit growth was very cyclical; since financial crisis it's been relatively low but much more STABLE (Exhibit 9.15)
- Lower volatility of financial assets should make cycles more predictable; anchoring of inflation + low rates plausibly make cycles much longer
- Positive factor: private sector imbalances much smaller → more resilient to shocks, reducing risk of private sector deleveraging

**The Rising Influence of Technology:**
- Dramatic growth of tech companies disrupting traditional industries (retail, restaurants, taxis, hotels, banking) → distribution of profits has diminished vs past cycles
- Tech sector: dramatic rise in profits since crisis. World excluding tech: strong improvement as economy recovered in 2016, but only returned to pre-crisis EPS levels. Tech meanwhile surged far above (Exhibit 9.16: world tech earnings ~250 indexed vs world ex-tech ~100, from base of 100 in Jan 2009)
- Resulted in much wider dispersion of returns between winners and losers

**The Extraordinary Gap between Growth and Value:**
- Post-financial-crisis: value has significantly underperformed growth — more pronounced than ever
- Five reasons:
  1. **Growth has been scarce and therefore highly valued.** Revenue growth trended down; proportion of companies with high growth (>8% annual revenue) has fallen while low growth (<4%) has risen (Exhibit 9.18)
  2. **Lower bond yields enhanced value of growth vs value** due to longer "duration" of growth stocks and their sensitivity to lower rates (Exhibit 9.19: MSCI AC World value vs growth closely tracks US 10yr bond yield since 2007)
  3. **Lower yields boosted defensives relative to cyclicals.** Many cyclical sectors have low P/E (value); defensives offer better or more predictable growth (Exhibit 9.20: cyclicals vs defensives tracks bond yield)
  4. **Lower yields increased value of "quality" — low volatility, strong balance sheets.** Premium for companies with stability/predictability in future revenue streams (Exhibit 9.21: low vol stocks outperformed as yields fell)
  5. **Shift favouring growth impacted regional performance.** US = growth market (high concentration of fast-growing companies); Europe = value market (low-growth, "cheaper" companies in mature industries). S&P 500 vs Euro Stoxx: no clear trend 1990-2007, then persistent US outperformance post-crisis. Europe's relative performance mirrored value vs growth factor (Exhibit 9.22)

**Regional EPS divergence (Exhibit 9.23):**
- Since pre-crisis EPS peak: S&P 500 EPS up 88%, MXAPJ up 17%, TOPIX up 12%, SXXP (Europe) up only 4%
- If Europe had same sector weighting as US (more tech, less banks): EPS growth would have been ~53% (not 4%) — gap roughly halves when adjusting for sector composition

**Lessons from Japan:**
- Japan post-bubble (1990s) is closest precedent for post-GFC trends: stock collapse + bond boom + declining interest rates
- Key difference: Japan's bubble much larger (Nikkei peak P/E ~60x vs much lower pre-2007)
- Growth scarcity impacted Japanese stock market similarly to post-GFC world:
  - Lack of yield in bonds → high dividend yield stocks more attractive
  - Few Japanese companies were shareholder-friendly; paying dividends = good sign
  - Growth vs value factor performance in Japan over past 20-30 years similar to global pattern (value outperforming until ~2007, then underperforming)
- In Japan: exporters (enjoying strong external demand in buoyant markets) persistently outperformed banks (Exhibit 9.24). Same pattern in European markets over past decade
- "Growth defensives" (consumer staples, health care) outperformed in Japan since 1990s; same true in Europe. But regulated/higher-yielding defensives had less clear pattern in Japan; in Europe regulation + lack of pricing power → underperformance
- Banks underperformed in both post-crisis Europe and post-bubble Japan; Italian banks performed even worse than Japanese banks post-bubble

**Chapter 9 Conclusion — Key structural changes since the financial crisis:**

**Chapter 9 Conclusion — Summary of key structural changes post-crisis:**
- Unusual length of economic cycle (longest in US for nearly 150 years)
- Relatively weak economic cycle in nominal and real GDP growth → unusually aggressive monetary easing + QE
- Despite rate cuts, long-term growth expectations have moderated; average revenue growth across corporate sector in Western economies has slowed
- Despite weaker than average economic/profits growth, financial markets unusually strong — both fixed income (policy rates/inflation moderated) and equities (lower rates pushed up valuations)
- Term premia and inflation expectations collapsed; bond yields at record lows globally and in many individual economies
- Slow growth + record low rates → income and growth scarce → secular shift toward low volatility, quality, and growth assets, plus anything generating yield pickup (e.g., high yield corporate credit)
- Financial crisis recovery overlaid with huge secular/super-cycle shift in technology → rapid concentration of revenues and profits in a relatively small number of very large companies, many in the US. This + stronger domestic economy → US equity market's superior relative returns

### Chapter 10: Below Zero — The Impact of Ultra-Low Bond Yields (pp. 205-220)

- Collapse in long-term bond yields in US and UK is extraordinary by historical standards
- UK bond yields at lowest levels since 1700; US yields lowest since 1880s (Exhibit 10.1)
- ~25% of government debt globally has NEGATIVE yield — investor actually pays the government to take their money
- Even a quarter of investment grade corporate bonds have negative yield
- "The idea of paying to lend money is an odd concept"

**Three reasons for ultra-low/negative bond yields:**

**1. Central bank policy:**
- Global financial crisis triggered global effort to push rates down rapidly — learned from mistakes of slower action (Japan late 1980s, US 1930s)
- "Anchoring" of rates cemented longer-term rates through QE programmes
- QE affects yields two ways: (a) "signalling effect" — buying govt debt signals rates staying lower than otherwise; (b) "portfolio rebalancing" — central bank buying govt securities pushes investors into riskier assets for acceptable returns → pushes down yields on corporate bonds, riskier bonds, longer-duration bonds
- Most studies conclude Fed's QE had "economically and statistically significant effects" on Treasury yields; similar conclusions for other countries

**2. Falling inflation expectations:**
- Inflation expectations declined materially since start of 21st century (post-tech collapse) and remained stable (Exhibit 10.2: US 5y5y inflation swap fell from ~3.5% in 2005 to ~2-2.5%; Europe fell from ~2.5% to ~1.2%)
- Japan and Europe: inflation expectations fallen most sharply; both have large proportion of world's negative-yielding bonds
- Japan's negative interest rate policy (2016) further pushed down medium-term inflation expectations
- Negative policy rates in Europe had spillover effect on bond markets elsewhere including US
- ECB QE + negative German Bund yields had meaningful impact on sovereign spreads: Greek 10yr yields spiked above 50% during sovereign debt crisis (2011), again briefly in 2015, then converged toward US levels as euro breakup fears faded

**3. Collapse of term premium:**
- Yield on default-free govt bond = sum of expected policy rates + term premium
- Term premium compensates for two risks: (a) unexpected inflation eroding real value of fixed nominal payments; (b) risk of recession
- When inflation expected to be high or uncertain → higher term premium demanded
- Recessions → lower expected wealth → higher risk aversion → investors demand more compensation for risky assets but LOWER premium for safe fixed income
- Term premium collapse contributed to falling yields alongside lower rate expectations

**Zero Rates and Equity Valuations:**
- "Yield gap" = S&P 500 earnings yield (inverse of P/E) minus 10yr Treasury yield
- Relationship between bond/equity yields has changed over time; correlation not constant
- Generally positive relationship over very long periods pre-crisis; negative since financial crisis
- Exhibit 10.5: S&P 500 earnings yield and 10yr Treasury tracked closely 1976-2000s, then diverged post-crisis
- Since crisis: bond yields fell relentlessly but gap with equity earnings yield INCREASED — equity P/E is LOWER (earnings yield higher) than might have been expected given falls in risk-free rates
- Effect even more striking in Europe with negative govt bond yields
- At start of crisis: German 10yr Bund yield ~4.5% (same as US). Since then, Bund yield went negative while equity cash yield (dividend + buyback) rose steadily
- Gap between equity cash yield and bond yield at record high (Exhibit 10.6)
- Early 1990s: equity cash yield ~4% while bonds yielded ~8%. Now: bonds below 1.5% but equity cash yield over 5%. Difference = significant decline in long-term growth expectations
- US: gap not as wide (reflecting stronger long-term US earnings growth expectations vs Europe)

**Zero Rates and Growth Expectations:**
- Yield gap between bonds and equities = approximate equity risk premium (ERP)
- Gordon Growth Model: Bond yield + ERP = Dividend yield + long-term growth
- If bond yield = zero or negative: ERP = (or exceeds) cost of equity = dividend yield + long-term growth
- Europe example: dividend yield ~4%, long-term earnings growth ~2% (1% real GDP + 1% inflation) → ERP = at least 6% with zero/negative bond yield
- Implication of zero rates: investors require HIGHER future return from equities because zero rates increase uncertainty about future path AND are associated with lower long-term growth
- Ex post ERP: ~3.5% in post-war period (US since 1950s), measured over 10-year periods of equity performance vs bonds
- Using 3.5% ERP in Gordon Growth Model with current market levels: implied long-term dividend growth is close to ZERO or slightly negative for Europe (Exhibit 10.8)
- If ERP = 8%: implied long-term nominal earnings growth = 4.7% (~2.7% real + 2% inflation)
- Implied/expected long-term growth has fallen continually over past decade
- Key tension: lower bond yields might imply lower discount rate → higher valuations, BUT slowdown in long-term growth offsets this effect — lower expected growth = lower expected long-term cash flows/profit growth

**Zero Rates: Backing Out Future Growth:**
- Japan has achieved roughly zero nominal GDP over recent decades; negative bond yield environment in Europe suggests similar future there
- Consensus medium-term GDP growth forecasts have trended down despite powerful monetary easing + QE + (in US) fiscal spending
- Exhibit 10.10: long-term (6-10yr) real GDP growth forecasts — Europe ~1.4%, US ~2.1%, Japan ~0.7%, China ~5.1% (all trending down)
- Top-line corporate sales growth trended down; rest of world converging toward Japan's level
- Europe and Japan where yields fallen most → where growth expectations lowest; where highest share of negative-yielding bonds
- Counter-intuitive finding: despite theory suggesting lower rates → higher equity valuations, P/E ratios in Europe and Japan are at SAME level and BELOW US (which has higher bond yields)
- Explanation: negative yields in Japan and Germany reflect lower long-term growth expectations (Exhibit 10.12: Europe and Japan have similar forward P/Es; both below S&P 500)
- Slowing rate of long-term corporate earnings growth (already present in Japan for 20 years) is now emerging in Europe as bond yields go below zero

**Impact on Banks:**
- Zero/negative rates = strong headwind for banks (weak loan growth + negative rates)
- Study of 6,558 banks from 33 OECD countries (2012-2016): introduction of zero interest rate policy REDUCED bank lending
- Japanese banks underperformed fairly consistently since end of their financial crisis (1990) and start of low growth/negative rates
- Similar pattern emerging in European banks since 2008 crisis + weak growth + negative rates
- Italian banks performed even more poorly than Japanese banks post-bubble

## Chapter 10 (continued from Part 2): Zero Rates and Demographics / Demand for Risk Assets

### Zero Rates and Demographics
- Half of Japan's market has been growing slowly for 20 years; in Europe the shift to low-growth is more recent
- Lower bond yields partly a function of structural demographic factors
- Life cycle investment hypothesis (Modigliani and Brumberg 1980): people borrow more when young, save more when old
- Growing proportion of old/middle-aged people → more demand for income-generating safe assets (gov bonds) → pushes prices up and yields down
- The MY ratio (middle-aged to young people ratio) helps explain level of long-term interest rates
- Demographics reduced equilibrium interest rate by at least 1.5 percentage points between 1990 and 2014 (Carvalho, Ferro, Nechio 2016)
- Population declines projected in both Europe and Japan in coming decades, faster in Japan

### Zero Rates and Demand for Risk Assets
- Zero/negative rates affect preference for risk assets among pension funds and insurance companies
- For a typical defined-benefit pension plan: a 100-basis-point drop in long-term bond yields → immediate increase of liabilities ~20%
- OECD warned about "excessive search for yield" by pension funds and insurance companies trying to match promised returns
- US institutions have taken on more risk as risk-free rates and funding rates have fallen
- Up to a third of funds' total risk was related to underfunding and low interest rates between 2002 and 2016
- Reaching for yield not confined to institutions — applies to individual investors too
- Companies with large future pension liabilities heavily affected by crisis and subsequent rate falls
- For insurance companies: fall in rates threatens guaranteed yields of life assurance contracts, makes them less resilient in downturns
- In Europe: high risk weighting applied to equities for regulatory purposes makes it harder to increase equity exposure
- Increased demand for bonds (to hedge interest rate and liability risks) → further downward pressure on bond yields → worsens funding problem — a vicious cycle
- European pensions and insurance companies have continued to focus on debt investments even as yields fell below zero

### Chapter 10 Conclusions on Zero/Negative Bond Yields
1. Collapse in global bond yields since financial crisis unprecedented — about one-quarter of all government bonds had negative yield
2. Bond yields at zero bound do NOT necessarily benefit equities — lower yields have pushed up the required equity risk premium
3. Zero/negative yields can make the cycle less volatile BUT leave equities much more sensitive to long-term growth expectations — a shock causing recession could have much greater negative impact on valuations than past cycles
4. Pension funds and insurance companies vulnerable to liability mismatching as yields fall to/below zero — can result in taking too much risk OR more demand for bonds (further lowering yields)

## Chapter 11: The Impact of Technology on the Cycle

### Opening Framework
- Economic cycle since 2008: weaker but longer than usual
- Equity market cycle: stronger than usual
- Slowdown in nominal GDP + lower inflation → lacklustre corporate earnings progression
- Exception: technology sector — strongest profit growth since financial crisis
- Digital revolution = "third Industrial Revolution"
- Widening gap between winners and losers beneath surface of equity market

### Capital-Light vs Capital-Intensive Sectors
- Non-capital-intensive sectors have dramatically outperformed capital-intensive sectors since the financial crisis (Exhibit 11.1)
- Capital-intensive: forestry, paper, industrial metals, mining, autos, construction, oil equipment, telecoms, electricity, gas, utilities
- Non-capital-intensive: beverages, food, household goods, tobacco, healthcare, pharma, biotech, software, computer services, tech hardware

### The Ascent of Technology and Historical Parallels

#### The Printing Press (1454) — First Great Data Revolution
- 90% of world's data generated in past two years (SINTEF 2013)
- Over half of world's population now has internet access — grown from virtually nothing in <30 years
- Printing press (1454) fuelled information explosion analogous to recent data explosion
- Before printing press: information was handwritten manuscripts, controlled by the Church
- Number of books published: 0 → ~3 million/year by 1550 in Europe
- By 1800: 600 million books published total
- Price of books collapsed as production costs fell

#### The Railway Revolution
- 1830: England had 98 miles of railway track
- 1840: ~1,500 miles
- 1849: ~6,000 miles linking all major cities
- Cheap money + revolutionary technology → surge in investment
- Railway tracks enabled telegraph infrastructure growth in the 1840s
- Within 10 years: sending telegrams became part of everyday life
- By mid-1860s: London connected to New York; messages sent London-Bombay within minutes
- AT&T born in 1885 from telegram/telegraph companies

#### Radio
- Between 1923 and 1930: 60% of US families purchased radios
- 1920: US broadcast radio dominated by KDKA
- By 1922: 600 radio stations had opened across the US

#### Electricity and Oil (20th Century)
- US in 1900: just 5% of mechanical power from electricity (up from 1% in 1890)
- By 1920s: electricity reached half of all companies and close to half of households
- Real price of electricity fell ~80% between 1900 and 1920

### Technology: Disruption and Adaptation
- Investors often assume technology will displace existing industry, but often it's additive
- Railways: feared horses would be obsolete, but railways actually INCREASED demand for horses ("first mile problem")
- Same pattern today: internet food ordering needs delivery by motorbikes, bicycles, cars
- Digital watches (1970s): expected to kill mechanical watches — instead Swiss watchmakers rebranded for quality/nostalgia, CHF 21.8 billion revenue in 2018
- Video technology (1980s) and DVDs (1997): expected to kill cinema — instead cinema reinvented, global ticket sales reached record $41.7 billion in 2018
- Vinyl records making comeback: 4 million+ chart-eligible albums sold in UK in 2018

### Technology and Growth in the Cycle
- Paradox: tech boom + low economic/productivity growth
- Historical precedent: previous tech waves also resulted in SLOWER initial productivity growth
- James Watt's efficient engine (1774) → first successful steam locomotive not until 1812 → British output per capita didn't accelerate until 1830s
- Electrical age innovations (1880s) → substantial productivity gains not until 1920s
- Robert Solow (1987): "you can see the computer age everywhere except in the productivity statistics"
- 1990s: dramatic improvement in productivity, concerns faded
- Post-financial crisis: productivity weakness revived the debate

#### Mismeasurement Problem
- Goldman Sachs economists analyzed unused iPhone prices on eBay: 20-40% price declines near new model rollouts → significant quality improvements
- Gap between secondary-market prices and telephone hardware CPI implies ~8% annual quality improvement
- Estimated potential understatement of annual consumption growth: +0.05 to +0.15 percentage points over last decade
- Combined mismeasurement of US GDP growth: currently between 2/3 pp and 3/4 pp per year, up from about 1/4 pp two decades ago
- Weak post-crisis economic growth may be partly explained by mismeasurement of technology's impact

### Three Categories of Technology Opportunities
1. **Companies that invent/innovate** (printing press, radio, TV)
   - Not all innovators/first movers succeed
   - 30 US manufacturers produced 2,500 motor vehicles in 1899; 485 companies entered next decade → now dominated by 3 conglomerates
   - Between 1939 and today: 220+ TV manufacturers made TVs for US market; only ~23 still make sets today
2. **Companies that create infrastructure** (railways, oil, power, search engines)
   - Network companies can be highly dominant but hard to predict which will survive
   - AOL was early internet provider but lost to Google; Myspace lost to Facebook
3. **Companies that utilize innovations to disrupt/displace incumbents** (platforms/marketplaces)
   - Platform/digital marketplace success driven by network effects
   - The Economist: "Size begets size: the more sellers Amazon, say, can attract, the more buyers will shop there, which attracts more sellers"
- Winners ultimately determined by: timing, good management, and financing

#### Historical Monopoly/Dominance Examples

  - In 1975: AT&T was one of just 2 companies with >5% of GICS telecom industry sales
  - By 1996: 9 publicly listed US telecom companies with >5% of industry sales
- **IBM**: ~70% share of mainframe market
  - DOJ antitrust lawsuit filed January 1969; lasted 13 years; dropped January 1982
  - Despite no formal judgement, regulatory risk kicked off steady decline in sales growth and margins
- **Microsoft**: OS ran on well over 90% of consumer devices in 2000
  - *US vs. Microsoft* filed May 1998; judge ordered breakup June 2000
  - Reversed on appeal June 2001; settled with consent decree
  - Average quarterly y/y sales growth fell from 40% (1988-2000) to 10% (2001-2018)
- **Google**: over 90% market share in internet searches; next competitor Bing at 3.2%

#### Biggest S&P 500 Company Through History
- **1955-1973: General Motors** — earnings >10% of S&P 500 ("golden age of capitalism")
- **1974-1988: IBM** — peaked at 7.6% of market cap ("age of mainframes")
- **1989-1992: Exxon** — peaked at 2.7% (spin-off from Standard Oil)
- **1993-1997: GE** — peaked at 3.5%
- **1998-2000: Microsoft** — peaked at 4.9% ("age of software")
- **2000-2005: GE (again)** — peaked at 3.5%
- **2006-2011: Exxon (again)** — peaked at 5.2% (Bank of America and Citigroup briefly biggest 2006-2007)
- **2012-today: Apple (sometimes Microsoft)** — peaked at 5.0%

- Dominant companies in previous periods were bigger as % of market than today
- But current dominant companies are far larger in absolute dollar terms — AT&T before breakup was ~$47 billion (~$120 billion inflation-adjusted)
- The reach and earnings power of current dominant companies are much larger than historical precedents

### Technology and the Widening Gaps Between Winners and Losers

#### Gap 1: Wages vs Profits (Labour Share Decline)
- Total factor productivity (TFP) growth and capital deepening accounted for most of the average within-industry decline in labour share in OECD countries between 1990 and 2007
- Labour share of GDP in US trending downwards since WWII, but particularly sharp fall since the financial crisis
- Technology not the only reason — also austerity, QE impact, corporate buybacks
- Technology has pushed down prices of books, clothes, toys, electrical goods — benefiting low-income consumers

#### Gap 2: Growth vs Value Companies
- High-growth companies (many in tech) have significantly outperformed "cheap" companies (low P/E, high dividend yield) this cycle
- Growth vs value outperformance driven by multiple factors:
  - Ongoing weakness of banks post-crisis
  - Ultra-low/negative interest rates creating headwinds for bank profits
  - Secular fall in bond yields alongside inflation → important contributory factor
- Growth companies = "long duration" — their net present value is MORE sensitive to interest rate changes than value companies (in more mature, slower-growing industries)
- In a period of falling interest rates: positive impact on NPV of tech companies is higher than for value companies

## Summary and Conclusions

### What We Can Learn from the Past
- Returns depend on time horizon and starting valuation — the longer the holding period, the more likely volatility-adjusted returns will rise
- Equities bought at top of tech bubble (2000) achieved among the worst 10-year holding returns in over 100 years
- Japanese stock market (Nikkei 225) remains ~45% below its 1989 peak, 30 years later
- S&P 500 did not return to its 1929 level until 1955
- Great valuation peaks (1929, 1968, 1999) → poor risk-adjusted returns
- Great valuation troughs (1930, 1973, 2008) → very strong returns
- Average annualised total return for US equities since 1860: ~10% (over any 1-20 year horizon)
- 10-year government bonds: average return 5-6% over same holding periods
- Over longer term, investors are generally rewarded for taking risk

### Four Phases of the Equity Cycle (Summary)
1. **Despair phase**: market peak to trough (bear market)
2. **Hope phase**: short period (avg 10 months US, 16 months Europe); highest returns achieved; starts when macro data still depressed
3. **Growth phase**: longest period (avg 39 months US, 29 months Europe); earnings growth generates and drives returns
4. **Optimism phase**: final part of cycle; investors become complacent; valuations rise again and outstrip earnings growth; typically 25 months in US

### Bear Markets Summary
- Worst annual post-war S&P return: -26.5% (1974); best: +52% (1954)
- Three types: cyclical, event-driven, structural
- Cyclical and event-driven: ~30% price falls
- Structural: ~50% price falls
- Event-driven: avg 7 months
- Cyclical: avg 26 months
- Structural: avg 3.5 years
- Event-driven and cyclical: revert to previous highs in ~1 year
- Structural: take avg 10 years to return to previous highs

### Bull Markets Summary
- Average US bull market: prices rise >130% over 4 years, annualising ~25%
- Secular bull markets: driven by sustained valuation increases (post-war boom 1945-1968; disinflation/Cold War collapse 1982-2000)
- Cyclical bull markets split into:
  - **Skinny and flat**: low volatility, low returns, narrow trading range
  - **Fat and flat**: high volatility, low returns, strong rallies and corrections within

### What We Can Learn from the Present
- Post-financial-crisis cycle differs from past: longest US cycle in well over a century
- Inflation expectations moderated; bond yields at record lows
- UK long-term bond yields at lowest since 1700
- $14 trillion+ in government bonds with negative yields
- Technology has driven widening gap between winners and losers in profit growth and returns
- Low growth + low inflation + low yields → scarcity of income AND growth
- Search for yield in fixed income and credit markets
- Growth factor outperforming value within equities
- Higher premium for quality (stronger balance sheets, less cyclical sensitivity)
- These conditions likely to persist unless growth and inflation expectations revert to pre-crisis levels

### Zero Rates Impact (Reiterated in Conclusions)
- QE has increased valuations, suggesting lower future returns
- Bond yields at zero bound don't necessarily benefit equities
- Japan and Europe experience: lower yields → higher required equity risk premium
- Zero/negative yields → less volatile cycle BUT more sensitivity to growth shocks
- Pension funds and insurance companies vulnerable to liability mismatching

### Technology Impact (Reiterated in Conclusions)
- 90% of world's data generated in past 2 years
- Rapid distributional impact across relative winners and losers
- Amazon, Apple, Microsoft: combined market cap larger than annual GDP of Africa (54 countries)
- Technology = dominant US equity sector
- Sector dominance evolution: financials (1800-1850s) → transport (1850s-1910s) → energy (1920s-1970s) → technology (post-2008)
- IBM became biggest S&P stock 1974; Microsoft biggest 1998; Apple biggest 2012

### What We Can Expect in the Future
- **Valuations matter**: high valuations → lower future returns, and vice versa
- Post-crisis combination of low-inflation products but high-inflation financial assets = partly function of falling interest rates
- Real interest rate decline reflects: ageing populations, excess savings, technology impact, globalisation, aggressive central bank easing
- Lower real yields + lower growth → more elongated economic cycle BUT economies/companies/investors more dependent on continuation of these conditions
- Scope to cut rates in face of shocks is much more limited → harder to recover from downturns
- Government borrowing and fiscal expansion increasingly tempting given low funding costs
- But if borrowing drives stronger growth → could raise inflation expectations and rates → derating of financial assets
- **Scenario 1**: Growth recovers to pre-crisis pace → would drive long-term rates much higher → derating of financial assets, painful bear market in equities AND bonds
- **Scenario 2**: Growth/inflation/rates remain subdued (Japan-like) → lower volatility BUT lower returns; harder to generate required returns given ageing populations and pension/healthcare liabilities
- **Climate change**: perhaps the greatest challenge; costly to decarbonise but provides significant investment opportunities
- Green tech progress: past 8 years — wind power costs down 65%, solar costs down 85%, battery costs down 70%
- Within 15 years: renewable electricity fully competitive with fossil fuels; power systems 80-90% reliant on intermittent renewables possible
- **Final message**: "Over the long run, even accepting the fluctuations caused by cycles, investing can be extremely profitable... if they can hold their investments for at least 5 years and, especially, if they can recognise the signs of bubbles and of changes in the cycle, they really can enjoy a 'long good buy'"
