# A History of Interest Rates, 4th Edition
**Authors:** Sidney Homer & Richard Sylla
**Published:** 2005 (original 1963)
**Pages:** 733

---

## Summary

*A History of Interest Rates* is the definitive reference work on interest rates across all civilizations and eras. Homer first published it in 1963; Sylla updated it to 2005 in the 4th edition. The book covers 5,000 years of lending, from Babylonian grain loans under Hammurabi's Code to the post-2001 Fed easing cycle. It is not theoretical economics — it is empirical financial history, marshaling thousands of data points to trace the rise and fall of borrowing costs as a mirror of human civilization.

---

## Key Themes

### 1. Long Secular Decline
Interest rates have trended **downward for 700+ years** across Western civilization, from roughly 8% in 13th-century merchant credit to 2–3% mid-20th-century bond yields. This is not smooth — three major secular reversals occurred: late 18th century, early 20th century, and the great 1946–1981 bear bond market. But the half-century minimums from each era trace a downward arc from medieval times to the modern era.

### 2. The 1946–1981 Anomaly
The postwar bear bond market was the biggest reversal in modern financial history. U.S. prime corporate bond yields rose from ~2.5% (1946) to 15–16% (1981). The same pattern appeared in nearly every developed country, though the magnitude differed. This was driven by unexpected inflation — investors in the 1950s and 60s bought bonds at low nominal yields without demanding inflation compensation, slowly learning a painful lesson. The 1981 peaks in most countries exceeded anything seen in centuries.

### 3. Switzerland as the Outlier
Switzerland is the singular example of a country that maintained low interest rates consistently from WWII onward. Swiss bond yields peaked at 7.15% in 1974 and then declined even before 1981 — the only country where the second peak (1981) was *lower* than the first (1974). Swiss franc stability and massive capital inflows made it the safe-haven anchor of global rates.

### 4. Wars and Inflation Destroy Creditors
Every major war or hyperinflation episode wiped out bondholders. Germany's Weimar hyperinflation (1922–1923) sent call money to 10,950% annualized. Russia's 1917 hyperinflation destroyed the ruble's value 5 billion-fold over a few years. France and Italy lost 99%+ of their currency's dollar value by 1960, yet their bond yields (in nominal terms) barely tracked this loss for decades.

### 5. Geographic Convergence and Divergence
Rates across countries tend to move in the same direction but with wildly different magnitudes. England set the global norm from Napoleonic times until WWII, then became a country of high yields. The U.S. declined from high rates in the early 19th century to the lowest rates by 1900, then rose again to the highest in the developed world by 1981. Japan maintained chronically high rates until the 1975 liberalization, then dropped to among the lowest. Switzerland became the lowest consistently since the 1950s.

## Chapter Summaries

**Ancient World (Chs. 1–5):** Babylonia, Greece, Rome, Egypt. Rates 6–33% for quality credits, 100%+ for consumer loans. Pattern of civilization: rates decline as commerce flourishes, spike as the civilization falls.

**Medieval to Renaissance (Chs. 6–10):** Church usury prohibitions drove loans underground but didn't eliminate them. Italian city-states (Florence, Venice, Genoa) pioneered modern finance. Venice issued 5% perpetuals at par by 14th century. Antwerp and Amsterdam later became the world's low-rate centers.

**Dutch Republic (Ch. 11):** First truly modern capital market. Dutch Republic borrowing rates fell from 8% to 3% (1640–1700). Provided the blueprint that England copied. Amsterdam was the 17th-century financial center of the world.

**England 18th–19th century (Chs. 12–14):** Consols invented; government credit transformed from 8% (1694) to 2.5% (1896 low). Bank of England bank rate as monetary policy instrument. Britain as the global low-rate benchmark for 200 years.

**United States (Chs. 15–18):** Colonial era very high rates; post-Civil War dramatic decline; by 1900 U.S. rivaled England as low-rate leader; 20th c post-WWII bear market unprecedented; 1981 peak (15.32% on 10-yr Treasury) highest ever recorded; 1981–2003 bull market produced ~18%/yr holding period returns on long bonds.

**France, Netherlands, Belgium, Germany (Chs. 19–20):** France lost 99%+ of franc value; yields tracked European pattern but with extreme post-WWII spikes. Germany's Weimar hyperinflation is the most dramatic episode in the book (call money 10,950% annualized). Post-WWII West Germany became a paragon of monetary stability.

**Scandinavia, Switzerland, Other Europe (Ch. 21):** Switzerland: perennial low-rate leader post-WWII. Sweden: all-time low 2.34% in 1938, eight years before U.S./UK bottomed. Denmark: government bond yields above 20% in 1982. Norway: yields peaked in 1987, after 1981 peak elsewhere.

**Canada (Ch. 22):** Little autonomous rate history; closely tracked U.S. with modest premium; Bank of Canada not established until 1935.

**Summary Analysis (Ch. 23):** The seven-century downtrend in minimum rates is the book's most important finding. Short rates more volatile and policy-driven; long rates better reflect economic preferences of populations.

**Japan (Ch. 24):** Chronically high rates until post-1975 deregulation; then among lowest in world. Bubble economy peaked 1989; rates fell to historically unprecedented lows in 1990s–2000s.

**Sterling Area (Ch. 25):** Australia, NZ, South Africa, India, Pakistan. All followed British patterns with varying degrees of local modification. Australia had wild 19th-century frontier rates (300–1300%/yr for moneylenders) before converging to Western norms.

**Russia (Ch. 26):** Centuries of currency debasement. Soviet interest rates are not market-comparable. Hyperinflation 1917–1924 destroyed the ruble (50 billion old rubles = 1 new 1924 ruble).

**China (Ch. 27):** Historically among the highest-rate nations. Consumer loans at 72–120%/yr were standard for millennia. 1948 hyperinflation saw prices doubling daily.

**Latin America (Ch. 28):** Rates consistently above European levels. 1980s saw triple- and quadruple-digit rates in Brazil, Argentina, and others. Argentine lending rate hit 14,177% annualized in April 1989.

**Turn of the Millennium 1990–2005 (Ch. 29):** U.S. bull bond market 1981–2003 completed; 10-year Treasury fell from 15.32% to 3.33%. Fed credibility established. International rate convergence accelerated. Japan and Switzerland maintained lowest long rates. Real estate bubble beginning to form as mortgage rates hit levels last seen in the late 1950s.

## Key Facts and Data Points

- Interest rates have ranged from 10,000% (Berlin 1923) to 0.01% (New York) — a ratio of 1,000,000 to 1
- 7-century downtrend in Western minimum interest rates from 8% (13th c) to ~2.3% (mid-20th c)
- U.S. 10-year Treasury peak: 15.32% (September 1981)
- U.S. 10-year Treasury bull market low: 3.33% (June 2003)
- British consol yield: lowest decennial average 2.47% (late 19th century)
- Weimar Germany call money: 10,950% annualized (November 1923)
- Soviet ruble destroyed: 50 billion old rubles = 1 new 1924 ruble
- Argentine interest rate: 14,177% annualized (April 1989)
- Chinese farmer average loan rate (1933): 34% (money), 85% (seed)
- Danish government bond peak yield: above 20% (1982)

## Why It Matters

This book is the bible of long-term financial perspective. It demonstrates that the "normal" interest rate environment of any given era is shaped by centuries of accumulation — the financial reputation of a nation, the stability of its currency, the credibility of its government, and the sophistication of its capital markets. No single decade makes sense in isolation. The 1981 bond market peak is incomprehensible without the 35-year buildup from 1946. The Swiss low rates are incomprehensible without two centuries of Swiss neutrality and monetary discipline. Homer and Sylla built the longest and most comprehensive interest rate database in existence, and the patterns it reveals — secular declines interrupted by catastrophic reversals, geographic convergence, the persistent premium of political risk — remain as relevant today as when the first edition appeared in 1963.
