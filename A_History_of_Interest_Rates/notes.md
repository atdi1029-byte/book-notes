## INTRODUCTION (pp. 1–16)

- 1970s–early 1980s produced the highest long-term prime rates ever recorded in modern capital markets — above any rate seen in the US since 1800, England since 1700, Holland since 1600
- Since 1981, rates have declined back to mid-20th-century levels
- Rate charts mirror the "rise and fall of nations" — wars, political crises, economic calamities visible at a glance
- Eugen von Böhm-Bawerk (Austrian economist, ~1900): "cultural level of a nation is mirrored by its rate of interest" — higher intelligence and moral strength = lower rates. At his time, long-term prime rates in Western nations were 2.5–3.5%
- Homer interprets this: moral/cultural strength → financial strength → low rates
- Ancient civilizations (Babylonia, Greece, Rome) all show the same pattern: rates decline as the civilization thrives, then spike as it declines and falls
- Western Europe shows a long declining trend from the Middle Ages to the mid-20th century

**Famous Rate Extremes:**
- 10,000% high in Berlin (hyperinflation, Weimar Germany); 0.01% low in New York — range = 1,000,000 to 1
- Jan 2, 1990: Argentine banks offering 600% per month
- Classical Athens loan sharks: 48% per MONTH (576%/year uncompounded)
- Theophrastus's usurer (d. 287 BC): 25% per DAY = 9,125%/year
- NY loan sharks: $4 loaned Monday for $5 Friday = theoretically 1,300%/year

**Hammurabi's Code (~1800 BC):**
- Max 33⅓%/year for grain loans; max 20%/year for silver loans by weight
- All loans required written contracts witnessed by officials
- If illegal rate collected by subterfuge → principal of debt canceled
- Personal slavery for debt limited to 3 years

**Laws of Solon (~600 BC, Athens):**
- Removed ALL limits on interest rate (opposite of Hammurabi)
- Reduced/canceled many existing debts; permitted hypothecation
- Forbade personal slavery for debt; endured for centuries

**Rome's Twelve Tables (~450 BC):**
- Max 8⅓%/year; fourfold damages for violations; personal slavery permitted but physical wellbeing protected

**Medieval curiosities:**
- 12th century England: personal loans 52–120%/year; same time Netherlands: real property loans 8–10%
- Baldwin II (King of Jerusalem): hypothecated his beard as collateral
- Emperor of Constantinople: borrowed on security of the Crown of Thorns; King Louis IX of France redeemed collateral when he defaulted
- 14th century Venice 5% bonds sold OVER par; same period Frederick the Fair of Austria borrowing at 80%
- 15th century Charles VIII of France: 100% for war loan in Italy; Italian merchants borrowed 5–10%
- 17th century Holland: refunded 8⅓% state debt at 3¾%; Dutch merchants as low as 1¾%; same era Crown of Spain paying 40% short-term

**Scope and methodology:**
- 3000 BC to 2005 AD; focused on HIGH-GRADE credits in each era
- Ancient era: propertied individuals > governments (no large corporations)
- Book prefers market yields over nominal rates when both available
- "Interest rate is a ratio of like to like" — comparability holds better over centuries than most prices
- Long-term free market rates = "fever chart of the economic and political health" of a nation
- Each generation normalizes the rates it grew up with; students of history won't be surprised by volatility

**Why Sidney Homer wrote it:**
- Could find no comprehensive history of interest rates anywhere
- Started in 1930s on Wall Street: needed historical context; original research on American bond yields, expanded to England, then Continent, then backward through antiquity
- 1st ed. 1963; 2nd ed. 1977; 3rd ed. 1991/1996; 4th ed. 2005

## PART ONE: ANCIENT TIMES

### Chapter 1: Prehistoric and Primitive Credit and Interest (pp. 17–24)

### Chapter 1: Prehistoric and Primitive Credit and Interest (pp. 17–24)

- Credit preceded coinage by over 2,000 years — Sumerian documents from 3000 BC show systematic loans of grain and metal
- Origins: productive loans of seeds and animals naturally generated interest (the increment, the calf, the harvest surplus)
- Non-productive loans (food to friends) rarely charged interest — the interest concept arose from the productivity of seed loans
- Distinction: loan of identifiable object (tool, animal) must return the SAME thing; loan of commodity (grain, money) returns equivalent — required standards of measurement → may have led to the development of primitive weights and money
- Word origins: "capital" and "pecuniary" from Latin "pecus" = flock/cattle; Sumerian "mas" = calves AND interest; Egyptian "ms" = interest, from verb "to give birth" — interest was conceptualized as the offspring of money

**Primitive interest rates from ethnological records:**
- India (backward districts): grain loans repaid double at harvest = 100% × 12/months; coin loans 24–36%/year
- Indo-China (early 20th c.): rice loans 50% per season
- Philippines (Ifugao tribe): rice loaned double at next harvest; pig loans required return of two pigs; 3 pesos borrowed → 24 pesos owed 3 years later
- Banks Islands (SW Pacific): shell money loans 100% for any period; forced loans on unwilling borrowers repayable with interest under threat of severe penalties
- Kwakiutl (Vancouver Island): blanket loans → 80% annual for 6-month loans, 100% for 1-year; young man starts by borrowing to repay double, then lends to relatives at 300%; "potlatch" forced loans so widespread they were prohibited by the Canadian government
- Kirghiz of Siberia: horse and sheep money loaned at 100%/year

**Four fundamental types of credit that persist throughout history:**
1. Long-term productive loans
2. Short-term working capital loans
3. Nonproductive consumption loans
4. Loans to governments

**Key insight:** Early historic rates already codified at 20–50%/year — the legal maxima and enforcement machinery suggest even higher "loan shark" rates would otherwise be common. Lower rates existed between wealthy capitalists.

### Chapter 2: Mesopotamia: Sumer, Babylonia, and Assyria (pp. 25–31)

**Four periods:**
1. Early Sumer ~3000–1900 BC (before first Babylonian dynasty)
2. Babylonian Empire 1900–732 BC
3. Assyrian domination 732–625 BC
4. Neo-Babylonian Empire 625–539 BC; then Persian rule 539–333 BC

**Financial infrastructure:**
- Two monetary standards: grain (rural) and silver (urban) — values varied, requiring state intervention on exchange rates
- All credit transactions recorded in legal documents witnessed before officials and deposited in temples
- Hammurabi's Code (~1800 BC): regulated land, labor, credit, rentals; creditors must wait until after harvest; crop failure cancels interest for that year; fixed legal maximum rates

**Hammurabi's Code specifics:**
- Established legal maxima that lasted 1,200+ years: 33⅓%/year for grain loans, 20%/year for silver loans
- Max only changed ONCE in 2,000 years (Neo-Babylonian: grain brought down to 20% to match silver)
- Subterfuge to collect higher rate → principal of debt canceled
- Pledging of wife, children, slaves for debt permitted but max 3 years servitude
- Wife's signature required for loan contracts; women's property rights protected
- Holder of house loan could live in the house in lieu of collecting interest

**Temple banking:**
- Temples (especially Marduk at Babylon, Sun God Shamash at Sippar) were chief bankers
- Made some loans to poor WITHOUT interest; other loans at below-maximum rates
- God Shamash at Sippar lent barley at 20% and silver at 6¼% — far below the 33⅓% maximum
- Temple of Marduk even lent money to slaves to buy their freedom
- Banking operations including deposits, transfers, bills of exchange dating back to 3rd millennium BC

**Rate history:**
- Sumerian period 3000–1900 BC: customary 33⅓% for grain, 20% for silver; examples of silver loans at 25%; state sometimes lent at 12%
- Babylonian period 1900–732 BC: same maxima; range in practice 10–25% on silver, 20–33⅓% on grain
- Assyrian period 732–625 BC: less sophisticated than Babylon; more collateral required; interest-free loans common but with heavy penalties (40%, 100%, 141% penalty rates); Temple of Arbela charged 25%; one example at 40%
- Neo-Babylonian 625–539 BC: grain max reduced to 20%; actual loans mostly at 16⅔–20%; banking house Egibi at 20%
- After Persian conquest 539 BC: ~40% became common as Mesopotamia lost independence and economic leadership shifted to Greece

**Actual loan documents (translated):**
- ~2000 BC: "Two shekels of silver... borrowed by Mas-Schamach... from the sun-priestess Amat-Schamach... he will pay the Sun-God's interest... at time of harvest"
- ~1800 BC: "Ka-enlilla borrowed one mina of silver from Bur-Sin on the interest of the temple of Shamash"
- 595 BC: half-mina at 16⅔%/year; all property pledged as security
- 542 BC: Banking house of Egibi lent ½ mina at 20%/year

**Bills of exchange and early banking:**
- Exchange bills known from time of Hammurabi, some payable to bearer, some on demand
- Documentary orders (remittances), deposit banking, money-changing, payment on behalf of others without cash deposit
- After 600 BC: private banking firms like Egibi Sons and Murashu operated like sophisticated merchant banks

### Chapter 3: Greece (pp. 32–43)

**Background:**
- Bronze Age Aegean (2400–1200 BC): left no interest rate records; cattle as first standard of value
- Dorian invasion ~1200 BC destroyed Minoan-Mycenaean civilization → "Dark Ages" / Iron Age
- Homer's epics describe feudal economy: "gifts" between chieftains = primitive credit; ox-based standard of value
- Solon's reforms 594 BC forced by debt crisis: canceled debts, freed slaves, REMOVED all limits on interest rate, forbade personal slavery for debt, devalued the drachma ~25%

**Financial development:**
- Official coinage: first in Lydia 7th century BC (electrum coins under King Gyges ~686 BC); King Croesus of Lydia first pure gold ingots (560–546 BC); Greeks coined silver
- In classical Attica: 1 talent = 60 minas; 1 mina = 100 drachmas; 1 drachma = 6 oboli
- After 450 BC investment in productive capital became common — wealthy Athenians held land, rental housing, slaves let out for hire, business investments, loans at interest
- Athens' "owls" (silver coins): even during Peloponnesian War disaster Athens refused to debase them; remained accepted currency in Mediterranean for 600 years
- After Alexander's conquests (~325 BC): vast Persian gold distributed → money stock multiplied several-fold → prices rose and interest rates DECLINED

**Sea loans ("bottomry loans"):**
- Lender advanced against hull or cargo; if shipwreck, borrower owed NOTHING; if voyage completed, repaid principal + premium
- Rates: 30% wartime, 22½% peacetime for Athens–Bosphorus round trip; earned twice/year so 40–60%/season common
- Not really "interest" — more like insurance premium or risk partnership

**Public finance:**
- Greek states rarely borrowed in the classical period — accumulated treasure instead
- Direct taxes considered "servile" and unknown in 5th–4th centuries BC
- Athens borrowed from Temple of Athena — "religious fiction," money was really the war reserve; interest nominal and rarely paid
- Greek city-state credit generally poor — rated below wealthy citizens
- State fortifications hypothecated as security; public revenues pledged
- 205 BC: Miletus experimented with LIFE ANNUITIES — paid 360 drachmas/year for 10% of principal loaned (10% annuity rate); proved popular

**Greek interest rates (all silver loans):**
- Temple of Delos: 10% on ALL loans, 5th–2nd centuries BC; loans to individuals secured by land, usually 5 years; once ran 33 years and once 55 years (due to delinquency); initially below "normal," eventually became above normal by 2nd century BC → temple had difficulty placing funds
- Normal loans (personal loans to men of substance): ranges by century discussed
- Interest quoted monthly; here converted to annual rates (×12)
- Private banking ("trapezitai"): by 4th century BC handled deposits, loans, foreign remittances, letters of credit, money orders, honored checks, kept complete books
- Demosthenes (~385–322 BC): lent one talent to city of Oreos at 12% secured by all public revenues

### Chapter 4: Rome (pp. 44–56)

**The Republic background:**
- Romans were farmer-soldiers who left banking and commerce largely to foreigners (mainly Greeks)
- Cato: "How much worse the money lender was considered by our forefathers than the thief" — yet Plutarch says Cato himself secretly invested in mercantile loans
- Standard money: cattle/animals → raw bronze/copper → silver (by 2nd century BC); Republican Rome coined NO gold
- Legal maxima from Twelve Tables (~443 BC): 8⅓%/year for loans; fourfold damages for violations

**Republican rate history:**
- 88 BC: Sulla raised legal limit to 12% (old 8⅓% had fallen into disuse)
- 86 BC: Valerian law remitted ¾ of all debts; state debts repaid only 25 cents on the dollar
- 74–69 BC: Lucullus reformed Asiatic debts: limited interest to 12%, accumulated interest could not exceed principal, creditors limited to ¼ of debtor's income
- 54 BC: rates in Rome at "customary" 6%; would rise to 8% during political campaigns
- 4% available at some points for safe loans — only risky loans demanded the legal maximum of 12%
- Brutus (44 BC): charged city of Salamis and King of Cappadocia 48% through agents; Cicero as governor of Cilicia was shocked, reminded Brutus the legal rate was 12%

**Crisis of 33 AD:**
- Prosecution of bankers for overcharging → banks called more loans → credit contraction
- Crisis resolved when treasury withdrew large sums and loaned them out for THREE YEARS WITHOUT INTEREST (quantitative easing, ancient version)

**Roman rate range:**
- Best credits: 4–6% under Augustus; rose to 12% during crises; "normal" 6% for most of early Empire
- 4th century legal limit increased slightly to 12½% (currency change reason)
- Nerva and Trajan (96–117 AD): land loans at 5% to fund feeding of poor children ("alimenta")

**Byzantine rates:**
- Justinian's Code (6th century) reduced 12–12½% limit to 4–8% depending on creditor status:
  - Bankers: 8%/year max
  - Ordinary citizens: 6%/year max
  - Senators and "illustrious creditors": 4%/year max
  - Maritime loans limited: 12% per VOYAGE (previously unlimited)
  - Church/foundation loans: 3%/year
  - Accumulated interest could not exceed principal
- 9th century: limits raised back to 11⅛%

**Egyptian rates:**
- "Normal" rate of 12% quoted repeatedly across centuries — may have been a traditional limit
- Grain loans at various higher rates; pawnbroker rates above and below legal limits
- 9th century BC papyrus: 100% rate (borrowed 5 deben, repay 10 deben in one year)
- Under Roman rule: 12% established as legal max; compound interest forbidden; accumulated interest cannot exceed principal

**Key observation — ancient vs. modern:**
- Risk factor in ancient loans is overstressed — margins of security often very large, terms short, sanctions very severe (personal slavery)
- Real difference from modern times: INCONVENIENCE of lending — no institutional intermediaries; mechanics of individual-to-individual lending is complex and burdensome
- No large organized money markets → rates were "traditional and stable" rather than fluctuating daily
- A US 6% tradition lasted 2 centuries; usury limits stable until 1960s — ancient stability is understandable

### Chapter 5: A Summary and Analysis of Ancient Interest Rates (pp. 57–66)

**Overall trend:**
- Three great ancient cultures (Babylonia, Greece, Rome) ALL showed the same "vast saucer" pattern: rates declined from earliest history until late commercial development, then rose again during political breakdown
- Babylonian rates generally higher than Greek; Greek generally higher than Roman
- Rome had lowest rates in all of antiquity: 4% was quoted in the early Empire — within range of modern prime rates
- Greek rates were most stable over long periods; Roman rates became volatile in 100 BC–200 AD (something like a true money market was developing)
- After 3rd century AD, "period of relatively low interest rates was ended for Western Europe for a thousand years or more"

**Trend explanation:**
- After Alexander's conquests: vast Persian gold distributed → Mediterranean money stock multiplied several-fold → interest rates DECLINED
- Trade expansion also increased demand and supply of goods
- When Rome finally absorbed Greek financial skills (Greek bankers = Roman bankers effectively), rates reached their lowest in antiquity

**Data limitations:**
- No daily or annual time series — only "century-by-century" snapshots
- Three types: (1) spot rates on specific transactions, (2) historians' estimates of "normal" rates, (3) legal limits
- "Normal" loans probably closest to modern personal loans at small banks far from money market centers — traditional, stable

## PART TWO: MEDIEVAL AND RENAISSANCE EUROPE

### Chapter 6: Usury Doctrines and Their Effect on European Credit Forms and Interest (pp. 67–79)

**The prohibition:**
- Council of Nicea (325 AD): first canon prohibiting usury by clerics
- Capitularies of Charlemagne (~800 AD): first time STATE (not just Church) forbade usury to EVERYONE; defined usury as "where more is asked than is given"
- 850: lay usurers excommunicated by Synod of Pavia
- 1139: Second Lateran Council prohibited usury; usurers to be held infamous
- Pope Alexander III (1159–1181): credit sales at price above cash price = usury; manifest usurers excommunicated
- 1210: William of Auxerre: "a usurious will makes the usurer" — mental usury is a sin; comparison to sin of unlawful desire
- St. Thomas Aquinas (citing Aristotle): "money is sterile" — breeding money from money is unnatural

**The key distinction — usury vs. interest:**
- "Interest" (from Latin intereo = "to be lost") was NOT profit but compensation for loss
- Origins from Roman law: difference between lender's current position and where he'd be if he hadn't lent = "damages"
- Term "interesse" became standard ~1220 as compensation or penalty for delayed repayment
- Permitted bases for interest: compensation for lender's actual loss, wages for effort in making loans, profit foregone, loss incurred in raising funds to lend

**The credit forms that developed to avoid/evade usury prohibition:**

1. **Pawnshops (manifest usurers):** Legal maxima of 32½%–43½% for private pawnshops; tolerated as "necessary evils"; Jews and Lombards dominated; states licensed them and collected fees
   - First public mons pietatis (1461 Perugia): charged only 6% vs. private 32½%–43½%; Pope Paul II approved
   - Belgian montes: census sales at 6¼% secured by business; permitted to charge 15%; continued to French Revolution

2. **Forced state loans (Venice, Florence, Genoa):** Loans organized as mons (government fund); shares traded on bourse; interest paid "as gift and interest"; low rates justified (no one would voluntarily lend otherwise)

3. **Partnerships/societas:** Profit from commerce accepted; "triple contracts" (also called "five-percent contracts") eventually approved — partner insured against loss + promised fixed return; controversial but accepted

4. **Census/annuity:** "An obligation to pay an annual return from fruitful property"; life census, perpetual census, temporary census — resembled mortgages and annuities; monasteries invested in these; states and cities financed through census sales; 1425 and 1455: Popes Martin V and Calixtus III authorized

5. **Bills of exchange:** Combined remittance with credit; profit hidden in exchange rate differential; "dry exchange" made it more like a pure loan; provided credit without explicit usury violation

6. **Deposit banking:** By 13th century Italy, deposits at variable profit rates accepted; "a banker's social standing in 13th-century Florence was probably at least as good as in 20th-century New York"

**The Reformation's break:**
- Martin Luther: eventually concluded 5–6% not usury; 8% permissible for redeemable land securities; condemned 60% rates as causing "fearful inflation and woes"
- Zwingli: no strict biblical prohibition; condemned only professional usurers
- John Calvin (1509–1564): attacked Aristotle's "money is sterile" doctrine; scripture forbids only BITING usury; Golden Rule is the test; Geneva fixed max at 5% (1547)
- 1650: in Protestant countries, economic effects of interest discussed by mercantilists; John Locke: "price of the hire of money cannot be regulated"
- Catholic Church final acceptance: between 1822–1836, Holy Office decreed all interest allowed by law may be taken; Pope Pius XII (1950): bankers "earn their livelihood honestly"

**Key insight:**
- The anti-usury prohibition was NOT just an ecclesiastical debate nor a dead letter
- It genuinely forced development of ALTERNATIVE credit forms: bills of exchange, census, partnership contracts, etc.
- These forms survived and became permanent features of modern finance BECAUSE they evolved under the pressure of prohibition

### Chapter 7: The Dark Ages (pp. 80–86)

**Economic collapse and revival:**
- Arab conquest 622–711 AD: Mediterranean sea trade almost completely closed; only southern Italy, Adriatic, and Aegean ports remained open; Charlemagne's empire essentially landlocked
- Vikings dominated North Sea and Channel; 9th century = low ebb of European economic life; merchant class nearly disappeared; serfdom general; Church monopolized literacy and finance
- Venice: uniquely survived — refugees on Adriatic sand islands, trade with Constantinople and even Arab markets; Doges themselves engaged in commerce
- 10th century = "century of transition": signs of revival; Arab power weakening; Vikings turned to trade; Flemish wool manufacture; population began increasing after mid-10th century
- Charlemagne devised new silver coinage (denier, sou, pound) — suited to small agricultural economy; his currency subdivisions survived to the 20th century

**Credit data:**
- No specific interest rates can be cited for the dark age period — complete gap
- Church was the indispensable moneylender; advanced to nobles against "land gage" (live gage if payments reduced principal, "dead gage"/mortgage if not)

### Chapter 8: Late Medieval Times (pp. 87–101)

**12th Century:**
- Trade revival: Crusades good for Venetian and Genoese shipping; Champagne fairs developed (6 per year, 6 weeks each); Italian merchant-bankers dominated international finance
- English moneylending largely in Jewish hands at 43⅓%/year (2 pence/pound/week) for good security; 80–120%/year for poor security
- Netherlands commercial/official loans: 10–16%/year; Genoa: 20% for commercial loans by banks
- Netherlands real estate (census/mortgage): 8–10%

**13th Century:**
- Commerce reached highest medieval level; Mongol conquest opened Asia to European trade; Marco Polo era
- Florence: public debt rate fell from 15% → 10% → 5% by 1390
- Venice prestiti (forced government bonds, 5% nominal): market traded at 60–75 of par (yields 6⅝–8⅜%) during wars; occasionally above par during peace; first modern government bond market
- Venice Camera del Frumento (grain office/proto-central-bank) paid 8–12% for short loans (1285–1289); set 10% max in 1289
- Best commercial credits in Italy: 20–25%; Champagne fairs 15–20%; Netherlands 10–16%
- Princes' credit: far below merchants'; Emperor Frederick II paid 30–40%; princely rate with good collateral much less (Countess Jeanne paid 18% with good security in 1221)
- Pawnshop legal limits: England 43⅓%, Provence 300%, Germany 173%

**14th Century — the calamities:**
- Black Death killed ⅓–⅔ of population in parts of Europe; Hundred Years' War; both English and French kings defaulted on debts; most Italian banks broke
- 1374 Venice reform: banks forbidden to trade in speculative commodities; 1403: required to hold 2/5 of assets in public debt; bank examiners appointed
- Frederick the Fair of Austria: borrowed at 80%
- King of Naples (1319): borrowed at 30% from Florentines; with jewels as collateral: 15%
- Philip IV of France defaulted on bankers by expelling them and canceling debts; destroyed Order of Knights Templar (had become a banking organization)
- Commercial rates Italy: 7–15%; Venice late century: 5% became the rule
- Venetian prestiti: detailed market history — prices from 92½ (1375) to 19–43 (1381) after war with Genoa; back to 60–66 by 1400

**Key insight — princes vs. towns:**
- Towns had perpetual credit (could pledge wealth of burghers indefinitely, sources of revenue systematic); merchants had realizable assets
- Princes could not bind their successors; credit depended on youth, health, military success; with death or bad faith, creditors lost everything → hence much higher rates for princes
- "Lend not to him who is mightier than thou" (Ecclesiastes VIII:13)

### Chapter 9: The Renaissance (pp. 102–131)

**15th Century:**
- Century of transition: printing invented; worldwide discovery; Columbus 1492; Turks took Constantinople 1453
- Medici Bank at Florence (1430–1480): greatest financial organization in Europe; chief bank for the Curia; paid 10% → 7¼% → 5% on deposits
- Florence public debt rate forced down from 15% (13th c.) → 10% → 5% (by 1370)
- Charles VIII of France (1494) war loan: paid 42–100%/year depending on source
- Commercial rates in Italy: declining to 5–8%; 5% becoming the "five percent contract" standard
- Venice Prestiti final chapter: 1482 new Monte Nuovo at 5%; heavy issues 1495 → price to 80 (6.25%) → 52 or 9.6% (1500) → 74 or 6.76% (1502)

**16th Century:**
- Antwerp dominated European finance; Exchange had 5000 members; 500 ships/day
- "Price revolution": commodity prices in northern Italy rose 2½× from 1550–1620; attributed to influx of American gold and silver
- Spanish Crown became Europe's largest debtor; six bankruptcies in one century: 1557, 1575, 1596, 1607, 1627, 1647
- English Crown at Antwerp: 1546–1558 rates 12–14%; Elizabeth I (1558–1603) first to enforce 10% legal max
- Fugger loans to Hapsburgs: mostly 10–14%; emergency loans at 24–52%
- Genoa Bank of St. George discount rates (for 4–5 year deferred dividends): 4–6% in 1520s–30s; declining to 3½–4% in 1546; then back to 4–6% range through 1570s; then down to 2½–2⅞% by 1590s — the lowest prime rates yet seen in northern Europe
- France (Lyons): commercial bills 8–14%/year; King of France short-term loans 12–16%
- Province of Holland long-term perpetual annuities: ~12% in 1500, 8⅓% in 1552, declining to 6⅙% by 1570

**17th Century — the great contrast:**
- Spain: serial bankruptcies ~every 20 years (1607, 1627, 1649); revenues pledged 5–10 years in advance; operating on copper standard; never recovered
- France: Italian bankers ruined; floating debt at 15–60%; Colbert reformed (1665): set 5% max, said "banker should behave toward a finance minister like a soldier toward a general"; founded State Savings Bank 1674 paying 5%
- Germany: Thirty Years' War 1618–1648 — irreparable loss; Fuggers ruined by Spanish defaults
- England: goldsmiths became bankers; 1672 Charles II "stopped the Exchequer" (stopped payment on floating debt); settled at 6% perpetual annuities — last English forced loan; Bank of England founded 1694 at 8%; discounted trade bills 1694–1699 at 4½–6%
- England legal max: 10% (1571–1624) → 8% (1624–1651) → 6% (1651–1714)
- Holland: THE great success; funded debt, no forced loans; 65,000 people in one province alone held Dutch state annuities; series of conversions drove rates DOWN:
  - 1610s: 6¼%; 1630s: 5%; 1640s: 4%; 1650s: 3%; by 1700: DOWN to 2% and even 1¾%!
  - Amsterdam merchants borrowed at 3–4½%, lent to England/France at 6%+
- Elsken Jorisdochter's 1624 bond: perpetual 6¼% dike bond, still paying 2½% as of 1957; presented to New York Stock Exchange in 1938 which collected interest

**Extraordinary detail:** The oldest continuously-paying bond in the world, a Dutch dike bond from 1624, was still paying interest in 1957.

### Chapter 10: Summary and Analysis of Medieval and Renaissance Interest Rates (pp. 132–139)

**Overall trend: long secular DECLINE from 12th century to 17th century:**
- 12th century: commercial bills 20%; short-term loans 43–120% for personal; long-term real estate 8–10%
- 13th century: commercial 10–25%; deposits 10–20%; long-term real estate 8–10%
- 14th century: commercial 5–25%; deposits 5–12%
- 15th century: commercial 5–8%; deposits 5%; census/mortgage 5–6%
- 16th century: commercial bills at Antwerp 4–13%; Holland long-term 8⅓%→6⅙%
- 17th century: Holland long-term 2–3%; Amsterdam commercial 3–4½%

**Key structural insight:**
- Medieval European finance was essentially ONE money market dominated by Italians until the 17th century — rates across countries not as different as they appear
- In the 17th century, markets split by national boundaries
- Low rates in England and Holland; disorganized markets in Antwerp and Italy following Spanish Crown defaults

**Why Holland succeeded:**
- No expensive court; frugal people saved and invested; all wars defensive, right at home; navy = merchant marine (profit-generating); democratic consent → ability to pledge entire future surplus of the people; complete honesty of administration

**The evolution of public finance:**
- Italian city-states (Venice, Genoa) pioneered marketable government securities — key innovation was making forced loans TRADEABLE on bourse
- Dutch perpetual annuities → adopted by England after 1689
- "Consent of the propertied public was the essential ingredient" — democratic governments could mobilize public resources; Spain failed absolutely

## PART THREE: MODERN EUROPE AND NORTH AMERICA TO 1900

### Chapter 11: England in the Eighteenth Century (pp. 145–163)

**The founding of the Bank of England and the modern credit system:**
- England in 1689 had no money market, no substantial bank, no organized national debt — King William III came from Holland and brought "Dutch finance"
- London goldsmiths issued bearer receipts for cash → birth of the modern bank note; developed "inland bills of exchange" at explicit interest rates
- 1692: First long-term national debt — £1 million as life annuities at 10% until 1700, then 7% (plus tontine benefits to survivors who outlived others); 1693: another million at 10% for 16 years with lottery benefits (total cost 14%)
- 1694: Bank of England founded — £1.2 million loaned to government at 8%; subscribers received right to incorporate as "Governor and Company of the Bank of England"; entire subscription filled in 12 DAYS; commenced operations January 1, 1695 with 54 staff in the house of the Company of Grocers
- Bank opened books June 21, 1694; subscription filled within 12 days

**English 18th century rate history:**
- 1700–1750: Long-term rates declined from 6–8% to 3% (consols)
- Famous "consols" created 1751 by Henry Pelham (Chancellor of Exchequer): converted most national debt to 3% consolidated annuities; originally paying 4% until 1750, then 3½% until 1757, then 3%
- 1755: consols above par (under 3%); then Seven Years War 1756 → dropped below 80 (3.75%); war loans at 4.90%
- 1775 American Independence War: consols hit 54 (5.56%); national debt doubled to £244 million
- 1793 Napoleonic Wars: consols hit 47¼ (6.35%) — highest yield in consol history until 1961!
- 1796: Famous "Loyalty Loan" — 5% annuities at 89% par = 5.62% yield
- Bank of England discount rate: FIXED at 5% from 1746 to 1822 — never moved, even when consols yielded 3%
- South Sea Bubble (1720): stock from £128 to £1000 and back to £135; Chancellor of Exchequer imprisoned; "Bubble Act" passed

**The transformation:**
- 1689: national debt under £1 million, all temporary
- 1815 (end of Napoleonic Wars): £900 million national debt
- England became world power by using its national credit
- Two English improvements on Dutch finance: (1) complete disclosure of size/terms of every issue; (2) uniformity through large interchangeable issues
- Government bonds ("the funds") set the going rate; usury limit of 5% on private loans put ceiling on private competition
- When funds yielded ~3%, private credit expanded; when ~5%, government pre-empted all savings

**Key insight:** Low long-term government rates were the engine of industrial revolution — canals, turnpikes, mortgages for agricultural enclosure all financed when consols were high-priced (low-yielding).

### Chapter 12: Europe in the Eighteenth Century (pp. 164–177)

**France — the contrast case:**
- John Law's Banque Générale (1716): initial success — note issue made acceptable for taxes; interest rates fell to 4½% and briefly 2–3%; commodity prices rose 88% as circulating medium tripled in 4 years
- Mississippi Bubble: company stock rose tenfold, then crashed; result: "profound popular distrust of paper money and big banks" that lasted for a century; France returned to coin
- French rentes: 5% was the "magic rate" throughout most of 17th and 18th centuries; lower and higher rates were temporary
- Revolutionary decade: 5% rentes priced at 6⅛–36⅛ (yields 82–14%); in 1798 mean price 16⅝ → yield 30% (if full interest paid) or 10% (if only ⅓ paid); in 1799 rentes ranged 7–22½
- After Napoleon: rentes recovered to 44 (7.35% yield) by 1801, then 93⅜ (5.38%) by 1807
- Napoleon's reforms: established budget, increased taxes, refunded debt into 5% rentes; rarely borrowed; followed "pay-as-you-go" policy by exploiting conquered countries

**Holland — the declining hegemon:**
- 18th century: Dutch turned from trade to finance as England took over commercial leadership
- Amsterdam Exchange: by end of century, 110 different securities traded; pioneered futures, margins, short sales, "counterminers" (shorts) and "lovers" (longs)
- Long-term rates: provinces typically 2½–3%; 1762 market yields Holland/Utrecht bonds 2.51–2.84%; Republic's own bonds paying 3% nominal
- Short-term commercial: 1¾–2% early century; 2–3% most of century; rose to 3–4% by 1775; then 4–8% during French occupation (1795)
- 1798 calamity: Holland's 2½% bonds at 36–39 (yield 6.95–6.42%); East India Company debentures at 20–25 (yield 10–8%)

**Other European countries:**
- Germany/Italy: 5% still "normal" for census annuities; sometimes 4%
- Basel (later 18th c.): rates fell from 5% to 4%; debtors threatened to repay; local governments tried to keep rates up but failed
- Belgium: public pawnshops still charging 15% through French Revolution
- Sweden (Bank of Sweden founded 1656, oldest central bank): deposit rate 6% (1668), loan rates 8–10%; gradually reduced following Dutch example; by 1741 down to 4½% for loans on precious metals
- Russia: 5% recognized by law as legal maximum but actual rates 8–10%

**The 18th-century European pattern:**
- England: achieved modern credit structure; Bank of England's long discount rate stability (5% from 1746–1822) contrasted with volatile long-term yields
- France: repeated failures and inflations despite persistent demand for rentes from savers
- Holland: declining but still with lowest rates in Europe until French occupation
- Key contrast: "democratic" governments (England, Dutch Republic) could mobilize public savings; autocratic governments (France, Spain) could not


## Chapter 13: England in the Nineteenth Century (continued)

### Short-Term Interest Rates
- 5% usury law capped quoted short rates 1714–1833; during Napoleonic Wars, discount houses charged commissions to get effective 5.5–6%
- 1833: short bills exempted from usury law → flexible discount rate policy became possible
- 1839: first deliberate monetary policy via bank rate manipulation
- Bank rate pattern: 1850s–1860s put up to 7–10% in emergencies, then quickly depressed to 2–3%
- Late 19th c: open market discount rate usually below 2%, fell below 1% in final decade
- With funds yielding less than 2.5%, Britain had achieved "Dutch finance"

### Key Episodes
**The 1860s and the Overend Gurney Crisis (1866)**
- Consol yields: 3.11% low to 3.54% high — only 12 points price range
- Bank rate range: 2–10% in same decade; open market discount: 1.30–10.29%
- May 10, 1866: Overend Gurney & Co. (London's largest discount house, half of bill market) failed for £5M
- Bank rate raised to 10% for unprecedented 3 months
- Yet consols barely moved: May yield 3.48% vs April 3.46% — funds were remarkably stable

**The Conversion of 1888**
- Background: 2½% annuities had risen from 78½ in 1880 to 97¼ in 1888 (yield 3.19% → 2.56%)
- Chancellor Goschen's strategy: offered holders new consols at 3% for one year, 2¾% for 14 years, 2½% thereafter
- Bank of England held rate at 2–3% to facilitate conversion; Governor given baronetcy after success
- Result: 96% of £591M accepted the new consols; national debt funded at near-2.5%

**The Crest: 1892–1900**
- New 2¾–2½% consols advanced from 95 in 1892 to 113⅞ in 1896 (yield 2.21%) — all-time high price
- 1894–1895: bank rate down to 2% for 2+ years (unprecedented); open market below 1%
- 1896: true peak of century-long bull market
- By 1920: consols down to 44¼, yielding 5.65%
- Those 2½% consols still outstanding; mid-1970s fell below 14 to yield 18%; late 1980s recovered below 10%

### Investment Market Diversity
- Non-speculative domestic bonds: 3.5–4% when funds yielded ~3%
- Colonial sterling bonds: wider yields reflecting quality/risk spectrum
- London Stock Exchange: £1.2B listed in 1843 (63% British govt); by 1875 £4.5B (British govt only 13%)
- 1866: Benjamin Disraeli paid 40% for personal loans; farmers borrowed at 50%
- Courts' usury ceiling: ~48% (post-repeal of usury law)

## Chapter 14: France in the Nineteenth Century

### General Background
- Political crises drove interest rate spikes: 1830–1831, 1848–1849, 1870–1871 — each crisis corresponded to regime change
- Despite political instability, 19th century = orderly finance and great economic growth
- Bank of France founded 1800 by Napoleon; didn't achieve monopoly of note issue until later; not a bankers' bank initially
- Napoleon reformed budget, restrained borrowing; 5% rentes rose from below 10% of nominal in 1797 to high of 93% by 1807
- French frugal savers financed industrialization of France AND Russia (large sums lost on Russian bonds)
- French provinces got lower rates by 1870s–1880s through branch banking expansion

### Long-Term Interest Rates (Rentes)
- Rentes: perpetual annuities descending from medieval census annuities
- 4 nominal series outstanding most of century: 3%, 4%, 4½%, 5% — yield differentials significant
- Investors preferred discount issues (protected against redemption) → lower yield premiums at discount
- 1797: 5% rentes at 6⅛–36⅛ (near bankruptcy); 1799: consolidated at 14¾, yielding ~34%
- 1801: rentes recovered to 68 high, yielding 7.35%
- 1815: 5% rentes at 52¼ = 9.6% yield; 1816 new 5s issued at 57¼ = 8.73%
- 1824: converted to 4½s at 100 and 3s at discount; by 1829, 5s at 110⅝; 3s at 80, yielding 3.75%
- Revolution of 1830: 3s plunged to 46 (6.55%), 5s to 74⅞ (6.66%) — 34–41% price declines
- Swift recovery: by 1833, 5s at 105½, 3s at 80½ = 3.73%
- Early 1840s: period of lowest yields in the century's first half
- 1848 revolution: 3s cratered to 32½ = 9.25%! New 5s issued at 75¼ = 6.65%; 3s at 46.40 = 6.48%
- 1851–1853: swift recovery, 3s back to 86 = 3.48%
- 1853–1866: only sustained rise in century (Second Empire military expansion; debt up 2/3)
- Franco-Prussian War 1870–1871: 3s fell from 75⅛ high to 50⅜ = 5.97% — 33% price decline
- 1871 war indemnity of 5 billion francs: floated at 6.06% and 5.91%, both heavily oversubscribed
- 1873–1900: near-steady decline; 3s from 50⅜ in 1871 to 87¼ = 3.45% in 1880; 105¼ = 2.86% in 1897 (century low)
- France funded at 3%; Britain at 2½% — both starting from same 1815 high, similar decline magnitude

### Short-Term Interest Rates
- Bank of France established at 6% in 1800; reduced to 4% in a few years; held there until 1852
- Only flexible discount rate adopted 1852 (13 years after Britain)
- First half-century rigid at 4%: 1830 revolution brought zero fluctuation; 1848 revolution: only 4→5% and back
- Open-market discount rates regularly reported after 1862; always averaged below Bank of France's rate
- 1830s: with bank at 4%, good commercial paper privately discounted at 2–3%
- 1860s: French open-market average 3.37% vs British 4.31%
- 1890s: both French and British averaged 2.09% open-market
- Bank of France relied on large metallic reserves; Bank of England relied on rate to protect small reserves
- French provincial rates much higher: 15–20% for landowners at mid-century; Bordeaux wine harvest season 15%, other seasons 5%
- Small consumer credit: pawnshops and moneylenders at 20–30%

## Chapter 15: Other European Countries in the Nineteenth Century

### The Netherlands
- 1815: Belgium reunited with Dutch provinces; 1830: Belgian revolution, separation
- Dutch financial system rebuilt in European mold but Dutch long rates were above British all century
- 1762: Province of Holland 2½% perpetuals at 99½ (yield 2.51%); 1798: down to 36 (yield 6.95%)
- 1814: Dutch 2½% perpetuals: 38 high / 30½ low = yields 6.56–8.20%
- 1830: high of 66⅝ = 3.75%; Belgian war sent them down to 34 (7.35%) then back to 65¼ in 1845
- 1848 crisis: 60 → 34 (7.35%) again; recovered to 66½ by 1852
- 1852–1870: irregular decline, then Franco-Prussian War low of 48½ = 5.15%
- 1872–1895: near-steady rise to 96 = 2.60%; end of century 79½ = 3.15%
- Closest to British yields: 1888–1892, when Britain first achieved 2½% nominal on large debt
- Dutch discount rate to 3% in 1815 (British at 5%); 1½% briefly in 1828; never below 2% again this century
- Dutch short rates below British early in century; after 1853, never reached British low of 2%
- Key comparison: both long and short lowest for Britain and Holland, 1894–1897:
  Britain: long 2.25%, short 0.75%; Holland: long 2.75%, short 2.25%

### Belgium
- Kingdom established 1831; no wars, missed 1848 revolutions; became 4th-ranking European manufacturer
- Multiple rente series (2½%–5%) with significant yield differentials
- High nominal rate rentes never sold to yield much below their nominal rate — fear of redemption
- 1831: 2½% rentes at 38–39 = 6.41–6.58% (right after independence war)
- 1845: 2½s at 60 = 4.17% — high prices for 15–20 years
- 1848: 2½s crashed to 25½ = 9.8% (lowest ever); French 5s simultaneously at 10%!
- 1848–1894: almost uninterrupted decline — most sustained decline of any European country
- 1894: 2½s at 99 = 2.52% — only 6 basis points above consols (vs 504 bp spread in 1848)
- Post-1870: Belgian rates declined faster than French and Dutch despite continental crises
- Short rates: Belgian open-market 4.59% in 1850s (British 3.84%); declined to 2.29% in 1890s

### Germany
- Two key dates: 1815 (reorganization after Napoleon) and 1870 (unification into German Empire)
- Reichsbank organized 1876; unified 9 currencies; Berlin as leading financial center
- Mortgage banks (Pfandbriefe system) key German institution since 1770; agricultural cooperative banks
- Germany = relatively high interest rate country in 19th century
- 1815: Prussian 4s at avg 81⅛ = 4.94–5.14%; rose to high of 5.87% in 1821 (unusual — other countries declining)
- 1830: Prussian 4s at 97⅞ = 4.09%; 1844: new 3½s floated at 101¼ = 3.45% (century low until 1890s)
- 1848 revolutions: 3½s plunged to 76⅛ = 4.60%
- 1870: wars with Denmark (1864), Austria (1866), France (1870) kept yields high; new 4s issued at 82¼ = 4.87%
- 1870–1896: sharp decline; computed average fell from 4.26% (1878) to 3.35% (1896) — lowest of century
- German yields always substantially above British; differential tended to widen after 1845
- Short rates: Berlin very volatile early (3–10% in 1820s when France fixed at 4%, Britain at 4–5%)
- After 1853: Berlin never reached British low of 2%

### Switzerland
- No unified central bank until 1905; 4 independent financial centers (Geneva, Basel, Zurich, St. Gallen)
- Swiss rates in moderate range 3–6%; never declined to extreme lows of other countries
- Unusual: last decade of 19th c rates ROSE (avg 3.78%) while all others declined
- Swiss rates: decade averages 4.14% (1837–39), 4.11% (1840s), 4.11% (1850s), 4.57% (1860s), 3.35% (1880s)

### Sweden
- Riksbank (oldest central bank in world, founded 1656) not truly central bank until 1897
- Swedish discount rates remained high all century; never reached very low levels
- 1890s: Sweden RAISED rates (avg 4.55%) unlike other European countries
- Long-term bonds: 1855 first quoted at 4%; highest rate 5.96% in 1868; low of 3.64% in 1890
- Swedish short rates always above Swedish long rates — unusual positive yield curve all century

## Chapter 16: The United States in the 18th and 19th Centuries

### Colonial and Revolutionary Background
- Colonists brought English 6% usury tradition; 6% legal max spread to most colonies (Virginia: 5%)
- Hard money very scarce; barter common; Indian shells, tobacco, rice used as money
- 1700: only ~$1M specie in all colonies; 1775: ~$12M
- Colonial loan banks charged 5%; private bankers charged 8% (led to public protest in Pennsylvania)
- 1690: Massachusetts first bills of credit for military expenses
- Continental currency: $449M total issued vs ~$12M in specie → 100:1 depreciation by 1781
- Revolutionary War foreign loans: Dutch bankers demanded large discounts/commissions

### Alexander Hamilton's Refunding (1790–1794)
- Three issues created: 6s of 1790 ($30M), Deferred 6s of 1790 ($14.6M, 6% after 1800), 3s of 1790 ($19.7M) = $64.3M total
- Old debt redeemed at par plus accrued interest; not a forced conversion
- 1791 price of 6s: ~70 (yield 8.57%); 1799 new 8s issued at highest nominal rate in US history until then
- 1798 crisis (undeclared war with France): 6s of 1790 sold as low as 75 = 8% yield
- 1802–1809: decade of sharply declining yields; debt paid from $86M (1804) to $57M (1809)

### War of 1812 Finance (1810–1829)
- First Bank of US deprived of charter 1811 → no sure source of credit
- 1812: 6s of 1812 at 100; by 1814: 6s of 1814 sold as low as 65 in state bank notes = 9.25% yield
- 1814: highest yields for long-term American government bonds in 19th century
- Peace 1815: dramatic recovery; by 1817 6s up 16–28 points to yield 5.30%
- 1824–1825: 4½s issued at 100; by 1835: entire national debt paid off
- Note: Massachusetts bonds suffered far less from War of 1812 than federal bonds

### Zero Debt Era and Rebuilding (1830–1859)
- 1835: federal debt entirely wiped out — no government bonds for 7 years
- 1841 new debt: 5½s at 100 and 6s at 100 (market initially weak)
- 1842: 6s of 1842 at 97½–100 = 6%; but 1843 market improved → 5s of 1843 at 101–103¾ = 4.82–4.95%
- 1847–1848: Mexican War loans; 6s of 1847 and 1848 (20-year term) at 102–104; heavily oversubscribed
- 1853: those 6s of 1847–1868 sold at 122 — Treasury regretted, investors loved the 20-year 6s

### Civil War Finance (1860–1879)
- National debt: from $60M to $2,675M during Civil War
- Two distortions: (1) greenback depreciation vs gold, (2) national bank demand for government bonds
- Government bonds distorted 1863–1918 — municipal bond yields better index for this period
- 1860: government tried 5s at 101; post-election panic → Treasury forced to issue notes at 10–12%
- 1861 low: 6s of 1848 sold to 86 = 6.98%
- Jay Cooke as bond selling agent (3/8 of 1% commission, 2,500 subagents) hugely successful
- Civil War bond types: long-term 5–20s (6%), interest-bearing notes, legal tender (greenback) notes
- 1864: gold at $2.33 in greenbacks; 6s of 1848 rose to 145 (speculation on gold redemption)
- 1865–1866: large postwar refunding; 6% 5–20s at ~102½; effort to contract currency begins
- Confederate bonds: 8s of 1861 at 100; Cotton Loan 6s; specie price as low as 5 in 1864–1865; became valueless
- 1870 Refunding Act: authorized 5s/4½s/4s for great postwar refunding
- 1878–1880: $739M "4s of 1907" sold at 100 — giant refunding

### Last Two Decades of 19th Century
- 1873–1899: great 25-year bull market in prime bonds
- New England municipal yields: 5.67% (1873) → 4.15% (1879) → 3.35% (1889) → 3.07% (1899)
- Railroad bond yields: 6.50% (1873) → 3.07% (1899) — railroad bonds first truly "high grade" by 1889
- US achieved "Dutch finance": long-term yields fell to 3–3.5% range
- American yields never reached British levels in 19th c; US didn't fall below British yields until after WWI
- Government bond premiums: 4½s of 1891 (issued at 100) rose to avg 114¾ in 1882 = 2.65% to redemption; 118½ high in 1887 = nothing to redemption
- Low-rate refundings: 3½s at 100 (1881), 3s at 100 (1882), Funded 2s at 100 (1891)

### Short-Term Rates — Extreme Volatility
- Call money 1899: ranged from 1% per annum to 186% per annum in SAME YEAR
- Commercial paper ranged 3.5–36% in century
- No lender of last resort, no pooled reserves → panic rates whenever demand exceeded supply
- Decennial averages of commercial paper: fell from ~10% in 1830s to ~5% by 1890s
- Short rates averaged far ABOVE long rates throughout 19th c — opposite of European easy-money decades
- Federal Reserve organized 1914–1917 ended erratic upswings; after 1921 short rates averaged BELOW long

### Western Frontier Rates (Risk and Eccentric Rates)
- 1850 San Diego: Bandini borrowed $10,000 at 4% per month = 48%/year
- 1852 California: 96%/year mortgage interest caused ranches to be lost
- 1848–1868 California: commercial rates 24–120%/year, savings banks paid 15%
- Indiana before 1833: ~50%/year common rate by contract
- Utah 1850s–60s: Brigham Young set savings rate at 10%
- Montana 1879: "18% was the lowest rate known in Montana"
- Illinois 6s 1841–42: crashed from 62½ to 14½ = 41% yield; recovered 550% from lows by 1853
- Virginia 6s: 95 in 1860 → 36 in 1861 (16⅔%); in postwar decline to 22 in 1876
- California 7s: paid gold during Civil War; rose to 167 high in 1864!

### US vs Britain Comparison
- British yields always below American throughout century
- Wide US-UK differential 1830s–1870s → encouraged heavy British investment in America
- After 1870 both declined together; US decline larger especially in 1870s
- Americans did not achieve British yield levels until after WWI

### Banking System Evolution
- 1862: 1,496 state banks, 7,000 different note types, 5,500 varieties of altered/fraudulent notes
- National Banking Acts 1863–1865: uniform currency secured by government bonds
- Treasury surplus 1880s used to buy back bonds at high premiums
- No lender of last resort until Fed; banking panics 1819, 1837, 1857, 1873, 1884, 1893

## Chapter 17: The United States in the Twentieth Century: 1900–1945

### The Giant N Pattern
- 20th c US bond yields form "gigantic N": declined 25 years → rose 20 years (peak 1920) → declined 26 years (trough 1946) → rose 35 years (peak 1981)
- After 1981: yields declined but remained at levels not seen before 1974

### Summary of Three Bond Market Cycles
**First bear bond market: 1899–1920**
- 1899 absolute low: municipal/corporate bond yields 3.07–3.20%
- 1900–1902: government 2s sold at 109⅝ = 1.55% yield (circulation privilege)
- 1907 panic: call money rose from 1 to 125% — last call rate over 30% in this history
- 1914 WWI outbreak: NYSE closed July 31–December 12; emergency currency prevented call > 10%
- Federal Reserve opened doors late 1914; brought rediscount rate 6→3% by 1916
- 1917 US entry into war: corporate bond yields rose 3.98% → 4.98% in 9 months
- Liberty Loans: First 3½s (1917), Second 4s (1917), Third 4¼s (1918), Fourth 4¼s (1918)
- Those who sold Liberty Bonds below par condemned as "unpatriotic"
- 1920 peak: corporate bonds 5.56% (highest until 1967); commercial paper avg 7.50%; call money hit 25%
- Fed held 7% rediscount rate through 1920 deflation; 18M Liberty Bond subscribers, 14M sold out

**Great bull bond market: 1920–1946**
- By 1928 nearly half of 26-year yield decline was complete
- 1920s boom: private debt doubled; $4.5B foreign bonds purchased; short rates far below pre-WWI
- 1928–1929: rediscount raised 3½→6%; call money 3½→20%; corporate yields 4.04→4.59%
- Stock crash Oct 1929: bond market rose; corporate yields fell 4.59→3.99% by May 1931
- 1931: Fed raised rates in traditional fashion (gold outflow crisis) → bond market turned sharply
- 1932: call money fell to 1%; commercial paper 1½%; Treasury bills 0.08%
- 1934: corporate yields below 4%; 1936: new all-time low at 2.95%
- 1936–1937: Fed doubled reserve requirements → 6-point bond decline, brief
- 1938: corporate yields 2.94%; T-bills at 0.001%, occasionally at negative yields
- WWII finance: Treasury pegged rates — T-bills 3/8%, 1-year certificates 7/8%, long bonds 2½%
- Fed bought "whatever required" to maintain schedule
- 1945–1946: tap 2½s rose to 106⅛ = 2.12% and bank-eligible 2½s to 109⅝ = 1.93% — all-time low
- 1946 absolute bottom: prime corporate 2.37%; long municipals 0.90%
- "Riding the yield curve" profitable sport: 2½ bonds pegged at 100 would rise as they shortened maturity

### Investment Market Changes 1900–1945
- Perpetual bonds and 100-year railroad bonds "all but vanished" in 20th c
- Income tax created special demand for tax-exempt municipals (distorts yields)
- Insurance companies, pension funds became dominant holders — "captive buyers"
- T-bills displaced call loans and commercial paper from 1930s onward
- Commercial paper pre-Fed: could spike to 18–36% for a few days; call money >100% briefly

### Short-Term Rate Structure
- 1900–1913: commercial paper 5.71% avg; call money volatile 1–75%
- Fed created (1914): brought commercial paper avg 6.20% (1913) → 3.84% (1916)
- 1920: commercial paper avg 7.50% (annual avg — highest until 1969)
- Before Fed: short rates averaged ABOVE long rates; after 1921: short rates averaged BELOW long rates
- Corporate yield curve: mostly negative (short > long) 1900–1930; positive (normal) after 1930

## Chapter 18: The United States in the Twentieth Century: 1946–1990

### The Greatest Bear Bond Market: 1946–1981
- Prime corporate bond yields: 2.46% (1946 low) → 15.49% (1981 peak) = 1303 basis points
- New issue peak: 16.5% industrials, 18.0% utilities
- Duration: 35 years — by far the longest bear bond market in US history
- A constant-maturity 2½% 30-year bond would have lost 83% in price over this period
- Broke decisively outside the "traditional band" that had prevailed for centuries

**Key episodes:**
- 1951 Treasury-Fed Accord: freed Fed from obligation to support government security prices
- Long govts declined 4–7 pts; corporates 7 pts; municipals 13% after accord
- 1957 peak: corporate avg 4.57%; then sharp 12½% rally in 2–4 months
- 1958: "unprecedented speculation" in govt bonds → flight from bonds to stocks, dollars to property
- 1959 high: corporate yields 4.65% (monthly avg); rediscount rate 4% (high since 1930)
- Treasury limited by law to 4¼% max yield on long-term bonds
- 1961–1965: unusual stability — corporate yields near 4.50% for 5 consecutive years

**The Great Inflation and Record Yields:**
- 1965: dangerous inflationary spiral began
- 1971 wage/price controls; relaxed 1973 — no lasting effect
- 1974 double-digit inflation; OPEC oil shock 1973
- 1979 Oct: Paul Volcker's new Fed policy — target monetary aggregates, let rates seek market level
- 1979–1982: unprecedented volatility
- Corporate yields: 9.93% (Sept 1979) → 12.96% (Mar 1980) → 10.58% (Jun 1980) → 15.49% (Sep 1981)
- Federal funds: peaked 19.10% (June 1981); annual avg 16.38% in 1981
- Commercial bank prime rate: peaked 21½% (Dec 1980); annual avg 18.87% in 1981
- T-bills: 16.30% (May 1981); annual avg 14.03% in 1981
- UST peak: 15¾% 20-year bonds sold at near par to yield 15.78% — highest government yield in 200-year history
- 1987 stock market crash: Oct 19 crash > 20%; yields had peaked Oct at 10.52%; Fed provided liquidity
- End of decade: yields still at levels "not seen before 1974"

### Term Structure of Interest Rates
- 19th c: commercial paper averaged far ABOVE long bond yields
- Early 20th c: same negative yield curve (short above long)
- After 1930: positive yield curves (normal — long above short) became the rule
- 1940–1946: rare period when short and long rates moved in opposite directions
- 1960: yield curve turned negative again (then-considered-high rates)
- Post-1981: generally positive except 30-year sometimes below 20-year

### Risk Spreads and Market Structure
- Depression 1930s: Aaa-Baa spreads enormous; 1920s and recent decades: narrow
- 1938–1941: T-bills occasionally at negative yields (property tax exemption)
- 1970s: Penn Central and NYC defaults created new investor conservatism
- 1980s: growth of junk bonds (LBO mania) → quality distribution of corporate bonds declined
- Municipal bond yields relative to corporate: tied to tax rates; Reagan cuts widened spread

## Chapter 19: England in the Twentieth Century

### Pattern: The "M" Shape
- English 20th c long-term yields form letter "M": rose 1896→1920, declined 1920→1946, rose 1946→1974, declined 1974→1989+
- US pattern similar but Britain's peak in 1974, US peak in 1981
- Correlation of UK/US bond markets much closer in 20th c than in 19th c

### Key Data Points
- 1897: consols at low of 2.21%; US prime corporates 3.25% (over 100 bp spread)
- 1900: spread narrowed to ~50 bp
- 1907: consols yielded above 3% for first time since 1888
- 1912: consols at 72½ = 3.45% — highest yield since 1848
- 1914 pre-war: financing at 3.96%; 1915: 4.50%; 1917: 5.33%
- 1920: consols at 43⅝ = 5.73% — 62% price decline from 1897 high (largest in history until 1961)
- 1920s: bank rate avg 4.82% (highest since Napoleonic Wars); consols avg 4.63% — unique high-rate peacetime decade
- 1931: pound off gold; bank rate cut 6→2%; consols rocketed from 49½ → 78½ in months
- 1932: giant 5% War Loan converted to perpetual 3½% War Loans at 99
- 1935: consols at 94⅜ = 2.65% (not so high since 1902)
- 1939: consols at 61 = 4.10%
- WWII: England fought a "3% war" (vs 4½–5% in WWI); bank rate held at 2%; open market ~1%
- 1946 Labour forced: consols to 99⅝ = 2.51% — "Daltons" (undated 2½% Treasury stock issued at 100)
- 1952 monetary orthodoxy: bank rate 2→4%; consols to 55 = 4.55%
- 1957 Suez/run on pound: bank rate to 7% (first since 1920); consols to 45 = 5.56%
- 1961: consols to new all-time low of 36¼ = 6.90%
- 1964–1974: consols from 43⅝ to 13⅞ = 18% yield — bank rate hit 13%; bill rate 15½%
- Inflation exceeded 30% at times; doubts about viability of entire British economy
- British long yields peaked 1974, US peaked 1981 — UK turned corner earlier
- Peak nominal yield: 15½% Treasury Loan maturing 1998 (1977)

### Inflation-Indexed Bonds (1981 Innovation)
- British government introduced "index-linked gilts" in 1981
- Pay fixed real rate + principal increased annually by retail price index
- Real yield at issue: <3%; rose to 3–4% range in mid/late 1980s
- Advantage: mid-1980s could borrow at ~8% total vs 12–13% on non-indexed gilts
- Largest use of this innovation by any government — came from financial desperation
- Did NOT catch on widely in other leading countries

### Short-Term Rates
- 1800–1860s: short rates averaged above consol yields
- Late 19th c easy-money: short averaged below consols
- Early 20th c: short again above consols
- 1920s–1940s: short far below consols (1930s decennial avg far below any earlier)
- 1980s: short rates averaged well above long rates again (EMS defense)
- Short rates peaked 1980 — 6 years AFTER long rate peak in 1974

### National Debt Growth
- WWI: debt rose 11-fold; only 28% of war expenses from taxes
- WWII: debt rose 175%; 48.5% from taxes (much better); "3% war"
- Post-1965: debt nearly doubled; £61B (1975) → £117B (1980) → £205B (1987)

## Chapter 20: Europe in the Twentieth Century (France, Netherlands, Belgium, Germany, Italy)

### France
- Franc lost 99%+ of dollar value in 20th century — mostly before 1958
- French rentiers wiped out by inflation; contrast with 19th century stability
- Despite 99% currency devaluation, French rentes fluctuated within 19th-century RANGE through 1960
- 1950s: yields approximately stable at "high" range
- Early 1960s: yields declined
- Post-1968 crisis: rose to extreme highs — nearly 17% in 1981
- Post-1960: France enjoyed political stability → inflation restrained, franc sustained
- Issued bonds indexed to gold, foreign currencies, equities in 1950s (escape from franc)


### Netherlands
- Historically low rates through 20th c — tradition of stability
- Dutch yields broke out of historical band to 11.55% in 1981
- Returned to lower levels by mid-1980s

### Belgium
- Belgian franc followed French franc until WWII
- Unusual stability 1946–1964 despite wider European volatility
- Rose to 13.5%+ in 1981–1982 peak

### Germany
- Weimar hyperinflation: Reichsbank discount rate 90%, call money 10,950% annualized Nov 1923
- Mark circulation +300,000% from July–October 1923
- Post-WWII West Germany: paragon of monetary stability
- German yields peaked below British/Italian yields in 1981

### Italy
- Long bond yields 9.05% in 1873; Italian domestic events drove rates more than foreign trends until 1912
- After 1912, Italy conformed to general Western interest-rate pattern
- Won WWI, lost WWII — both wars followed by currency debasement and high rates
- Italian lira lost 99%+ of dollar value through 1960 (like French franc)
- 1924–1945: swift rise 1925–26 after inflation check; decline to 1934; WWII low 3.22%
- Post-WWII: bond yields rose rapidly — 5.73% (1950) → 6.81% (1957) → 5.24% (1960)
- 1970s: Italian peaks BELOW British yields; 1980s Italian inflation persisted → yields soared absolutely and relatively
- Short-term: Italian discount rate LOW in 1950s/60s despite global rises (unusual)
- 1970s–1980s: discount rate finally joined worldwide procession to high rates

## Chapter 21: Europe in the 20th Century — Switzerland, Austria, Scandinavia, Ireland, Iberia, Turkey

### Switzerland
- Symbol of neutrality, monetary stability, safe haven capital
- Franc stable: devalued only once (1936); by 1990 worth 65 US cents vs 20 cents in 1900
- Pre-WWII: Switzerland high rates by European standards; reversed after WWII
- Swiss long bond yields rose to 7% annual avg in 1920 — above belligerent countries
- Swiss SHORT rates 4.56–5% in 1920 — far below Swiss bond yields (unusual positive curve)
- From 1910–1920: bond yields +330bp vs private discount rate only +120bp (short rates less volatile, unusual)
- Post-WWII: Swiss bond yields FELL while others rose — unique divergence
- All-century low: 2.55% annual average in 1953 — well below any other country
- 1974 peak: 7.15% — well below most other countries
- 1974–1979: yields declined sharply as capital sought safe haven from inflation
- 1981 peak BELOW 1974 peak — only country with this pattern
- Swiss short rates became lowest in Western world since 1950s; consistently below others

### Austria
- Closely paralleled German interest-rate history through WWII
- Post-WWII: moderate rate path, peak 6.75% in 1980–1981, only once above 10% annually (1981)
- Became country of relative stability and low rates

### Sweden
- Neutral in both wars; shared some economic instability with devaluations 1931, 1949
- Historical all-time low bond yield: 2.34% in 1938 — EIGHT years before American/English lows
- Post-WWII: renewed period of low rates, but limited rally after 1981 compared to others
- Discount rate peaked 12% in 1981; consumer prices doubled 1971–1979, doubled again 1979–1988

### Norway
- Low interest rate policy despite dangerous inflation — delayed but didn't prevent record yields
- Bond yields peaked 13.56% in 1987 — AFTER 1981 peak elsewhere
- Discount rate held below 3.5% until 1969 despite Swedish rate rising to 7%

### Denmark
- Comparatively high rates throughout 1930–1990
- Government bond yields peaked above 20% in 1982 — one of highest in Europe
- Sharp contrast with Norway's low yields until mid-1980s
- Discount rate peaked 11% in 1979–1981

### Ireland
- Very close financial ties to UK throughout period
- Discount rate rose to 12.75% (1973), 16.5% (1979 and 1981)
- Irish bond yields generally above English yields from 1965 onward

### Portugal
- Authoritarian regime (1930s–1970s) kept interest rates artificially low
- Discount rate held at 2% throughout WWII and post-war; unusually stable
- Post-revolution (early 1970s): rates climbed rapidly to high levels
- Pre-revolution: remarkable price stability; cost of living barely moved while Spain's doubled
- Represents controlled markets under authoritarian regimes, not market-determined rates

### Spain
- Never well financially developed; historically isolated economically
- Civil War 1930s: highest discount rate of covered countries that decade
- 1957–1960: sharp rise during economic reform period
- Post-Franco freer markets: discount rate reached peak of 21.40% (Bank of Spain, early 1980s)

### Turkey
- Consistently high interest rates by European standards
- 1980s: extraordinary inflation — 1988 price level nearly 20× its 1979 level
- Discount rate 26% in 1980, 54% in 1989 — altogether atypical of European pattern

## Chapter 22: Canada in the 20th Century

- Canada had little autonomous interest-rate history until post-WWI
- Relied on foreign capital (chiefly English) until WWI forced domestic financing
- Bank of Canada not organized until 1935; modern money market not until 1950s
- Canadian bond yields closely paralleled U.S. yields throughout century
- Almost always higher than U.S. yields; much lower than UK yields from 1950s onward
- Canadian-UK differential shifted dramatically: from 87bp above England (1900s) → below England (1940s) → far below (1950s-70s)
- 1980s: Canadian yields rose more than U.S. → spread widened; Canadian advantage over Britain nearly disappeared
- Canadian short rates: no organized money market until 1950s; at times volatile, usury laws caused dislocations (1959 bill rate >6% caused bank lending difficulties)

## Chapter 23: Summary and Analysis — Europe and North America Since 1700

### Suprasecular trend of long-term rates
- Half-century minimum interest rates have trended DOWN for seven centuries (since 1200)
- Three temporary reversals: late 16th–early 17th c, late 18th–early 19th c, and post-WWII to 1974/1981
- Lowest level in history: mid-20th century U.S. taxable government bond decennial average
- 1970s–1980s: alarming break — Swiss 1970s average 5.11% (lowest) alone exceeds all earlier half-century minimums
- Question: are 1970s–1980s highs a new secular reversal or temporary?

### Long-term secular trends
- Secular rises: (1) 1737–1798; (2) 1898–1920; (3) 1946–1981
- Secular declines interspersed between these periods
- Post-WWII rise (1946–1981) the most severe — in some countries carried yields to new all-time highs

### Country comparisons
- England held lowest long rates from Napoleonic era until 20th century; then reversed
- U.S.: highest long rates early 19th c → lowest by early 20th c → among highest by 1970s–80s
- Netherlands: declined from high post-Napoleonic → close to lowest by WWI → moderate post-WWII
- France: recovered in early 19th c → very low by 1900 → among highest post-WWII
- Germany: consistently 2.5–3% above minimum — transformed from high-yield to relatively low-yield country (unchanged differential, but "Germany moved relatively" because others rose more)
- Switzerland: highest in early 20th c → lowest in world by 1950s onward; ONLY country staying within historical low-yield range

### Short-term rates summary
- Short rates started HIGHER and ended LOWER than long rates over centuries
- Short rates in 20th c far below long rates — unique to 20th century
- Short rates far more influenced by monetary policy and market structure; long rates better reflect economic preferences of large numbers of people
- Switzerland: lowest short rates in Western world since 1950s
- U.S.: went from highest short rates in early 19th c to among the lowest → then rose again post-1950

## Chapter 24: Japan

### Background
- Japan entered modern world in 1867 under Emperor Meiji; rapidly adopted Western financial institutions
- Bank of Japan established 1882
- Pre-1970s: Japanese financial system heavily regulated, segmented; companies relied on banks for external finance; bond/money markets insignificant
- High personal savings encouraged via tax-exempt interest accounts
- Financial revolution post-1975: deregulation, open markets, active bond/money markets

### Interest rate history
- Pre-WWI: short rates HIGH by European standards and RISING (contrary to European downtrend)
- WWI era: rates narrowed toward European range
- 1930s: declined with Western rates but never as low; lowest call money 2.51%, official discount 3.29%, bank loan rate still 5.95%
- Post-WWII: rates rose — 1949: corporate debenture yields 10.02%
- 1973–1975 oil shock: discount rate 9%, call money 12.57%, corporate debentures 12.52%
- Post-1975 liberalization: rates declined to WWII-era lows
- 1979 second oil shock: brief rise; then unlike other countries, Japan avoided double-digit inflation
- Post-1980: Japanese rates among lowest in world — only Switzerland lower
- 1990s: bubble economy collapse drove rates to some of the lowest levels of any country in modern history

### Australia
- Frontier penal colony: by 1800s "prime rate" 10–15%; loan shark rates 329–1300%+ annualized
- 1850s: prime rates came down to 6%; further decline post-gold rush
- Post-1930 modern series: trends followed general Western pattern with few exceptions

- Closely parallel to Australia until 1969; then remarkably independent course

- Followed Commonwealth trends; became country of high rates in 1980s amid political turmoil

### Pakistan
- Rates similar to India; both rose less than European/American rates in 1970s–1980s

## Chapter 26: Russia

### Historical overview
- Centuries of currency debasement: copper coins worth 5 rubles → 312 ruble coins (1645–1676)
- 1769 paper assignat: depreciated 75% by 1810; devalued 71% in 1839 reforms
- Gold standard adopted 1897 — lasted barely 20 years (WWI ended it)
- 1917 Revolution hyperinflation: ruble lost 99.9% of 1913 value by 1920; commodity prices +4.9 million% from 1913 to 1921
- Currency reforms: 10,000 czarist rubles → 1 "1921" ruble; 100 "1921" → 1 "1922"; 50 billion czarist → 1 "1924" ruble
- 1923 peak: ruble depreciating at rate of 5% per HOUR
- Soviet banking: Gosbank (1921) as state bank; interest rates served socialistic function, not market function
- Interest rates under communism not comparable to free market rates: low official rates by design, unofficial/informal rates unrecorded


### Soviet Interest Rate History (continued)
- Imperial Russia: legal max 5% (unenforced); best rates 8–10% in 18th c; still 8–9% for mortgages in 1911
- Pre-WWI industrialization: joint-stock banks, market discount rates 5.60–8.04% annualized (1900–1908)
- Gosbank rates in hyperinflation: 8–12% range in early 1920s; gradually lowered through 1930s
- Post-WWII: official rates NOT permitted to rise when Western rates rose; rigid administered rates
- Savings banks paid 6–9% in 1930s (exempt from income tax) — high by Western standards at the time
- Government bonds sold to public were "voluntary in name only" — subscription equivalent to 2-weeks' wages

## Chapter 27: China

### Historical background
- Credit in China traditionally = consumer/personal loans at high rates; commercial credit rare
- Copper coins with hole ("cash") standard for 2,000+ years; large transactions settled in silver/gold by weight
- Paper money experiments began ~AD 1000 ("flying money"); overissue led to depreciation
- Buddhist monastery pawnshops operated by AD 200–300; private pawnshops by AD 800; peaked 18th c
- Shansi (draft) banks: originated 18th c to safely remit funds across China; collapsed in 1911 revolution
- "Chop loans" = daily interbank overnight credit rate set at clearinghouses in major cities
- Four government banks established post-1911 to handle state finances

### Interest rates
- Han dynasty (200 BC–AD 220): moneylending returned 20–33% (33% considered too greedy)
- Tang dynasty (618–907): legal limits declining from 100% → 60% → lower; loans 6–10%/month = 72–120% annual
- "Young lamb interest" (13th c): 9.6% government loan → reloaned at 100%/yr → 1024× in 10 years
- "Seal print money" (18th c evasion): charged full interest on original principal while repaying in installments
- 1930s rural China survey: 70%+ households burdened by cash debt; average loan at 34% (money), 85% (seed)
- 1927–1937: pawnshops charged 20–72%; farm mortgages 20%+
- Treaty ports (late 19th c): European banks brought rates down to 6–10% — considered HIGH by European standards
- 1946 hyperinflation: central bank offered 180% on deposits; 1-month T-bills at 234–291%
- 1948: prices doubling every day at peak; Communist government set 6% max rate (ignored)
- Post-1949 Communist China: administrative rates similar to Soviet model; deposit/loan rates by decree, not market

## Chapter 28: Latin America

- Consistently high rates vs. European/North American throughout history
- 1980s: quadruple-digit rates in some countries — unprecedented outside of German/Russian/Chinese hyperinflations

### Chile
- Trend declined 1878–1905; rose to 1915; very high throughout 20th century
- 1930s: 7.79–11.82% bond yields when Europe's were declining
- Post-WWII: monetary unit lost 95% of dollar value between 1945–1960; cost of living rose 50-fold
- 1975: bank loan rate 185% (amid 7% central bank discount rate — massive gap between official and market)
- Bank loan rates 30–64% annually throughout 1980s

### Brazil
- Brazilian bond yields declined early 20th c, ran counter to international trend
- No easy-money period in 1930s; yields stayed moderate
- Military government indexed bonds to CPI — made comparable yield quotation impossible
- Official discount rate: 6% (1948–1958), 8–35% (1964–1979), triple digits (1983), quadruple digits at times (1989)

### Argentina
- Only Latin American country to experience truly low yields (3.11–3.26% in mid-1940s)
- T-bill rate declined from 2.40% (1936) to 0.56% (1944) — below major financial centers
- 1970s–1980s hyperinflation: over 400%/yr inflation; lending rate reached over 1000% in 1985
- April 1989 annualized rate: 14,177% (reported)

### Uruguay
- Most conservative Latin American market; yields followed European more than Latin American patterns
- Bank loan rates 60–110% in 1976–1989

### Mexico
- Discount rate held at 4.50% from 1943–1975; bank loans usually above 10%
- Bank loan rate climbed to 92% in 1987; fell back by 1988–1989

### Peru
- Discount rate held unchanged at 9.5% from 1960 through 1975; then climbed to 72% (1985)
- Bond yields 11.5% in 1959 — highest in Latin America that decade

### Colombia
- Discount rate 30% in 1979; maintained through 1980s
- Had both highest (early 1930s, near 14%) and lowest (early 1950s, below most of Latin America) yields

## Chapter 29: Turn of the Millennium — 1990–2005

### Political and economic background
- Soviet Union collapsed; Cold War ended; Germany reunified; all by 1990s
- Japan's bubble economy peaked Nikkei 38,916 on Dec 29, 1989; by 2005 hovered ~11,000 (<30% of peak)
- U.S. Dow Jones: 2,753 (1989) → 10,500 (2005) — nearly fourfold
- EU real growth 1995–2004: 19%; Japan: 15%; U.S.: 45%; UK: 32%; Canada: 35%
- Germany grew only 13% from 1995 to 2004; Ireland doubled in size

### The Bull Bond Market 1981–2003
- 10-year Treasury peak: 15.32% in September 1981; fell to 3.33% in June 2003
- Total yield decline ~1000bp; bond prices more than doubled
- Hypothetical 12%, 20-year Treasury at 1981 peak: priced at 85.85 → 201.72 at 2003 low
- Holding period return ~18% annualized — nearly twice the 20th-century stock market average of 9–10%
- Bull market included 7 reversals of 100–300bp, but secular trend steadily lower
- $1.8B of 15¾ of 2001 issued near peak — investor who bought Nov 1981 and sold Aug 1995 earned 18%+

### Fed policy narrative 1990–2005
- 1989–1990: Fed tightened → soft landing attempt disrupted by Gulf War, S&L crisis, LDC debt problems
- Federal funds rate: peaked ~10% (1989), fell to 3% (1992–1993), lowest in nearly 30 years
- Bond yields: corporate 9.56% → 6.66% (1993); governments 8.89% → 5.33% (1993)
- 1994: Fed reversed — 7 tightenings, funds rate 3% → 6% over one year
- 1994 bond carnage: prime corporates 6.66% → 8.68%; 10-year gov 5.33% → 7.96%
- 1995: Fed credibility established → massive bond rally; 10-yr fell 231bp in months
- 1996–2000: Tech boom, productivity surge, GDP growth 3.7–4.5%, unemployment from 6.1% → 4%
- 1998 LTCM crisis: Russia defaulted; Fed bailed out LTCM; brief rate cut; no meltdown
- 1998–2000 dot-com bubble: Fed tightened, 10-yr rose from 4.53% (Oct 1998) → 6.66% (Jan 2000)
- 2001 recession: Fed cut funds rate 6.5% → 1% (by mid-2003) — held there one year
- 10-year Treasury: 6.66% (Jan 2000) → 3.33% (June 2003) — cut exactly in half
- 2004–2005: Fed raised funds rate from 1% to 3%; long rates barely responded → "conundrum"
- Asset speculation shifted from stocks to real estate as long rates stayed low

### TIPS and inflation expectations
- TIPS introduced 1997; yield represents real interest rate; spread over nominal = inflation expectation
- TIPS investors systematically underestimated inflation by ~50bp → Treasury overpaid on TIPS
- Fed credibility on inflation control interpreted as key reason for low inflation expectations
- Inflation expectations rose ~1% between 2003–2005

### International rate comparisons 1980–2004
- G-7 long bond yields (1980–1989 averages): Italy 14.46%, England 11.18%, France 11.75%, Canada 11.72%, Germany 7.53%, Japan 6.50%, Switzerland 4.69%
- By 2000–2004: convergence especially in euro zone; Japan and Switzerland lowest at 1–3%
- Italy 20.56% (1981) → 4.26% (2004); Japan 9.22% (1980) → 1.50% (2004)
- Eurodollar-CD spread: 199bp in 1969 (peak of regulation) → virtually eliminated by 1990s (deregulation)
- Emerging markets: narrowing range 1990–2004 but high volatility
  - Argentina, Brazil: astounding highs at start of 1990s; financial crisis effects visible in rates
  - Singapore, Hong Kong, South Korea: rates close to European/Japanese levels by 2000s
  - Turkey, Russia, Argentina: persistently high rates with extreme crises
