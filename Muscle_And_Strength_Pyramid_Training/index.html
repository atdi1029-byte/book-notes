Glossary

Training Volume

Volume: Total training stimulus, best measured as number of hard sets per muscle group per week (not volume load).
Volume Load: Sets × reps × load. Useful for tracking within a program but poor for comparing programs because high-rep sets inflate the number disproportionately.
MEV (Minimum Effective Volume): The fewest sets per week that still produces measurable progress. Roughly 10 sets/muscle group for most intermediates.
MAV (Maximum Adaptive Volume): The sweet spot — the volume that produces the most adaptation given adequate recovery. Roughly 10–20 sets/muscle/week for most.
MRV (Maximum Recoverable Volume): The most volume you can do and still recover before the next session. Exceeding it leads to nonfunctional overreaching.
MV (Maintenance Volume): Minimum sets needed to hold existing muscle during a cut or deload. Lower than MEV.
Sets: The primary unit of volume. One set = one round of reps at a given load, taken to or near failure.
Reps: Individual repetitions within a set.

Intensity & Load

Intensity of Load: The weight used, expressed as % of your 1-rep max (1RM) or as a rep max (RM). E.g., 80% 1RM or an 8RM.
Intensity of Effort: How close to failure a set is taken. Expressed via RPE or RIR. Separating "intensity" into these two meanings prevents confusion.
1RM (One-Rep Max): The maximum load you can lift for exactly one full, technical repetition. The reference point for all percentage-based loading.
% 1RM: Load expressed as a percentage of your 1RM. Approximate guide: 95% ≈ 2 reps, 90% ≈ 3–4, 85% ≈ 5–6, 80% ≈ 7–8, 75% ≈ 10, 70% ≈ 12–13.
Rep Max (RM): The maximum load for a given rep target. A 6RM is the heaviest weight you can lift for exactly 6 reps. More practical than % 1RM for most bodybuilders.
RPE (Rate of Perceived Exertion): A 1–10 scale. In training context, specifically based on reps-in-reserve (RIR). Developed by Mike Tuchscherer; validated by Helms and Zourdos.
RIR (Reps in Reserve): How many more reps you could have done after a set. RPE 8 = 2 reps left in the tank.
Technical Failure: Point at which form breaks down noticeably, even if another rep might be physically possible. Recommended stopping point for compound lifts.
Muscular Failure: Complete inability to move the weight. Should be avoided routinely on squats, deadlifts, and overhead presses due to injury risk and disproportionate fatigue.

Periodization

Microcycle: One week of training. The building block of all programming.
Mesocycle: A training block of 4–8 weeks with a consistent stimulus goal (e.g., accumulation or intensification).
Macrocycle: A full training season or year, composed of multiple mesocycles.
Linear Periodization (LP): Volume decreases as intensity increases across a macrocycle. The traditional "phases" model: hypertrophy → strength → power → taper.
Block Periodization: Shorter, focused blocks (4–6 weeks each): Accumulation → Intensification → Realization. Minimizes detraining between phases. Better for athletes who compete multiple times per year.
DUP (Daily Undulating Periodization): Rep ranges and intensity zones change each training session within a week (e.g., hypertrophy day, strength day, power day).
WUP (Weekly Undulating Periodization): Rep ranges rotate week-to-week rather than day-to-day.
Wave Loading: An intermediate progression strategy where reps decrease by 1–2 per week while load increases. After 3 weeks, restart from the higher-rep end at a heavier weight.
Deload: A planned week of ~50% normal volume at similar intensity. Allows fatigue to dissipate while fitness is maintained. Different from a taper in that it's mid-training, not pre-competition.
Taper: Volume reduction (50–67%) immediately before competition to peak performance. Intensity maintained or slightly increased.
Intro Week: A ramp-up week (~75% of planned volume, 1 RPE lower) used before a new volume block to let the body acclimate. Used after an intensification block, not after a deload.

Physiology & Adaptation

Hypertrophy: Increase in muscle cross-sectional area through an increase in the size of individual muscle fibers. Driven primarily by mechanical tension and metabolic stress.
Progressive Overload: The gradual increase in training stimulus over time. The foundational driver of both strength and hypertrophy. The entire purpose of periodization is to plan and sustain this increase.
SRA (Stimulus-Recovery-Adaptation): The cycle by which training breaks tissue down, recovery rebuilds it stronger, and adaptation leaves you better than before. Frequency and volume should align with this cycle.
Fitness-Fatigue Model: Performance = Fitness minus Fatigue. Both fitness and fatigue are created by training. Fatigue dissipates faster than fitness, which is why deloads expose strength that was there all along.
Functional Overreaching: Intentionally accumulating fatigue beyond a comfortable level during a mesocycle. When followed by a deload, performance rebounds higher than the starting point. The desired outcome of a well-designed mesocycle.
Nonfunctional Overreaching: Volume continues past the point where it can be recovered from. Performance returns only to baseline after a deload — no supercompensation. Wasted training stimulus.
Overtraining Syndrome: Fatigue so severe that fitness itself degrades. Takes weeks to months to resolve. Rare in resistance training but seen in extreme competition prep and high-volume CrossFit athletes.
Impulse (F×T): Force multiplied by time. The complete mechanical stimulus — more accurate than time-under- tension alone because it accounts for the magnitude of force, not just its duration.

Program Design

Adherence: Consistently following a program over time. The most important variable — more important than which specific program you follow.
Autoregulation: Adjusting training load, volume, or exercise selection in real-time based on how you feel that day. Structured flexibility. The practical solution to variable life stress.
APS (Antagonist Paired Sets): Performing a set for one muscle group, then immediately performing a set for the opposing muscle group during what would otherwise be the rest period. E.g., bench press + row. Saves time without sacrificing performance.
Drop Sets: Performing a set to failure, then immediately reducing the load and continuing to failure, repeating one or more times. Time-efficient but hard to track. Best on accessory exercises.
Rest-Pause Sets: Performing a set to failure, resting 20–30 seconds, going to failure again, repeating until a target rep count is reached. More trackable than drop sets. Induces more fatigue per session than straight sets.
Double Progression: An isolation exercise progression model: add reps each session within a target range (e.g., 12–15). Once you hit the top of the range for all sets, increase load and restart from the bottom.
Specificity: Adaptations are specific to the stimulus. Getting stronger at the squat primarily requires squatting. Training with heavy loads improves performance with heavy loads more than with light loads, and vice versa.

Introduction: The Pyramid Model

Most lifters spend years asking the wrong questions — "Is 5×5 better than 3×10?" "Should I do a push-pull-legs or an upper-lower split?" These questions treat programs as fixed objects to be selected rather than as the product of manipulating a handful of variables intelligently. Eric Helms, Andy Morgan, and Andrea Valdez built this book around a single organizing insight: not all training variables are created equal. Some matter enormously, others barely at all, and confusing the hierarchy is why most lifters plateau unnecessarily.

The pyramid arranges six levels of training variables from most to least important. The base is widest and most critical. The apex is narrowest and least consequential. Roughly 80% of your progress comes from getting Levels 1 through 3 right. Levels 5 and 6 — rest periods and lifting tempo — are mostly about avoiding mistakes rather than unlocking gains.

The six-level hierarchy — base is most important, apex least. Periodization wraps the entire pyramid as an overarching organizational concept.

One key framing the authors return to throughout the book: "Programs are not distinct things — they are the manipulation of volume, intensity, and frequency over time." Every popular program you've heard of — 5/3/1, GZCLP, PHUL, Texas Method — is simply a particular arrangement of the same underlying variables. Understanding what those variables do, why, and in what order they matter is the whole point of this book.

Level 1: Adherence

Adherence sits at the base of the pyramid for a simple reason: nothing else matters if you can't follow the program. The best periodization scheme in the world, executed inconsistently for six months, will produce worse results than a mediocre program followed perfectly. Helms frames adherence around three conditions, which he calls the REF framework: the program must be Realistic, Enjoyable, and Flexible.

Realistic means matching the program to your actual life. A four- day program that requires 90-minute sessions is useless if you have three available hours a week. Enjoyable doesn't mean training should always feel fun — it means you should be able to sustain the approach without dreading every session. If you hate barbell back squats, front squats or leg press are better exercises for you than the textbook-optimal choice, because you'll actually do them. Flexible means the program can absorb disruption — missed sessions, heavy work weeks, travel — without collapsing. A program with zero slack is fragile.

The research backs this up in a non-obvious way. A 2010 study by McNamara and Stearne had one group follow a fixed periodization order and another group choose easy, moderate, or hard sessions based on how they felt that day. Same total volume over twelve weeks. The flexible group outperformed the fixed group in strength. A 2017 study by Colquhoun et al. found similar results with daily undulating periodization — lifters who chose their own weekly rep-range order based on perceived readiness showed better adherence and better outcomes than those locked into a fixed sequence.

Life stress and training stress aren't separate systems. A 2008 study by Bartholomew et al. found that people experiencing more negative life events during a training program adapted less well, even holding training variables constant. Your body processes work stress, sleep deprivation, relationship strain, and heavy squats through overlapping recovery systems. This is the scientific basis for autoregulation: structured flexibility isn't an excuse to slack off, it's a rational response to the way recovery actually works.

On injury: resistance training is considerably safer than most sports. Bodybuilding produces roughly 0.24–1.0 injuries per 1,000 training hours. Powerlifting and Olympic lifting range from 2.4 to 3.3 per 1,000 hours. Basketball sits at 8.5 to 11.1. Injuries do happen, but the solution is modified training rather than complete rest — altering range of motion, reducing load, finding pain-free movement patterns, or using blood flow restriction (BFR) at ~20% 1RM to maintain hypertrophic stimulus at minimal joint load.

The interference effect — the idea that cardio undermines strength gains — is real but modest. It slows progress rather than stopping it. The practical mitigation is simple: lift first, then do cardio. Separating them by six hours is better; a full day between is best. Helms cites Robineau et al. (2016) showing the effect scales with recovery duration between concurrent training sessions. If you enjoy running or cycling outside the gym, you don't need to stop — you just need to sequence it intelligently.

Level 2: Volume

Volume is the primary driver of hypertrophy and a key variable for strength. The best way to count it is hard sets per muscle group per week — not volume load (sets × reps × load), which is misleading because high-rep sets inflate the number without producing proportionally more stimulus. A set of 25 reps and a set of 10 reps produce similar hypertrophy per set (Schoenfeld et al., 2015), but the volume load difference looks enormous.

The dose-response relationship is real but non-linear. A 2017 meta- analysis by Schoenfeld et al. showed that 10+ sets per muscle per week produces meaningfully more hypertrophy than fewer. A 2017 meta-analysis by Ralston et al. found 5–12+ sets per week optimal for strength. But past roughly 20 sets per week, the returns diminish and recovery becomes the constraint. Gonzalez-Badillo et al. (2005) demonstrated this cleanly with competitive weightlifters: a moderate volume group (2,481 reps over 10 weeks) outperformed both low (1,923 reps) and high (3,030 reps) volume groups for strength gains. More is not always better; more than you can recover from is worse.

Heaselgrave et al. (2018) provided a concrete illustration of the U-shaped curve. Trained males performed curls, rows, and pulldowns at either 9, 18, or 27 sets per week for biceps. The 18-set group showed meaningfully greater biceps growth than either the 9 or the 27-set group. The 27-set group essentially trained themselves into junk volume — sets that consumed recovery resources without producing additional adaptation.

Advanced 16–20 sets/muscle/week 4–5×/week Block periodize; deload regularly

The fitness-fatigue model explains why training feels worse before it feels better. Fitness — your actual capability — builds gradually. Fatigue, also a product of training, masks that fitness and suppresses acute performance. Performance = Fitness − Fatigue. A well-designed mesocycle intentionally builds fatigue over 3–6 weeks (functional overreaching), then a deload allows fatigue to dissipate while fitness is maintained. Performance rebounds higher than it started — this is supercompensation. If you skip the deload and keep piling on volume, you enter nonfunctional overreaching: fatigue stays, fitness stalls, and the deload only returns you to baseline.

The practical rule for managing volume is straightforward: start at the low end of the range, add 1–2 sets per muscle group when you plateau on multiple lifts with all recovery factors optimized, and reduce by ~20% if you're constantly needing deloads after just 1–2 mesocycles. Volume is a dial, not a lever — move it in small increments and observe the response before moving it again.

Level 2: Intensity

Intensity means two different things in training, and conflating them causes enormous confusion. Intensity of load is the weight used — expressed as % of 1RM or as a rep max. Intensity of effort is how close to failure a set is taken — expressed via RPE or RIR. Both matter, but they matter differently for strength versus hypertrophy, and for different exercise types.

The RPE/RIR scale was developed by Mike Tuchscherer of Reactive Training Systems and validated by Helms and Mike Zourdos at Florida Atlantic University and SPRINZ. RPE 10 means failure — no more reps or load increase is possible. RPE 8 means 2 reps left, with the possibility of a third. RPE 7 means 3 reps clearly remain. The scale allows athletes to self-regulate load in real time, accounting for day-to-day variation in recovery, stress, and sleep that rigid percentage-based loading ignores entirely.

A large source of confusion around % 1RM is that it has huge inter-individual variability at sub-90% loads. Pareja-Blanco et al. (2018) had trained males do an AMRAP at 70% 1RM on the back squat. The rep range across subjects was 9 to 26 reps — both answers to "what is 70% 1RM?" This is why RPE alongside % 1RM is more useful than % 1RM alone.

For strength, training with heavier loads matters more because strength is specific to load — getting strong at an 8RM transfers less to your 1RM than getting strong at a 3RM. Three mechanisms drive strength gains: muscle mass and structural adaptation, neuromuscular efficiency, and motor pattern acquisition. The intensity recommendations for strength are two-thirds to three-quarters of weekly volume in the 1–6 rep range at RPE 5–10, with the remainder in the 6–15 range.

For hypertrophy, load matters less than effort. Both high (above 60% 1RM) and low (below 60% 1RM) loads produce comparable hypertrophy when effort and volume are equated (Schoenfeld et al., 2017 meta-analysis). The floor appears to be around 20% 1RM — sets at this load produce less hypertrophy than 40–80% 1RM even when taken to failure, because extremely high-rep sets become more endurance than resistance training. The hypertrophy intensity recommendation is two-thirds to three-quarters of volume in the 6–12 rep range, with the remainder split between heavier (1–6) and lighter (12–20) work.

The biggest mistake around intensity is taking compound lifts to absolute muscular failure. A 2016 meta-analysis by Davies et al. found no strength advantage to training to failure versus stopping short of it. Going to failure on set one of three drops total reps: 8, 6, 5 = 19 total reps. Stopping at RPE 8 produces 7, 7, 7 = 21 total reps. Izquierdo et al. (2006) found that groups training to failure showed overtraining biomarkers with no additional strength gain compared to the non-failure group. Save failure for isolation exercises at the end of sessions where the fatigue doesn't cascade into subsequent sets.

Level 2: Frequency

Frequency is not a stimulus in itself — it is an organizational tool. The question isn't "how many days should I train?" but "given my weekly volume target, how do I distribute it across sessions to maximize recovery and skill development?" All the work in one session is not equal to the same work spread across several sessions.

Two mechanisms make higher frequency superior. First, skill acquisition: compound movements like the squat, deadlift, and bench press are technically demanding. Cramming all weekly volume into one session means the later sets are done in a fatigued, degraded state that reinforces poor movement patterns. Spreading volume across sessions lets each rep be executed with higher quality. Second, SRA (stimulus-recovery-adaptation) cycle alignment: muscle protein synthesis peaks 24–48 hours after a session. Training a muscle twice per week gives you two synthesis windows per week instead of one. More synthesis opportunities means more accumulated growth over time.

The research is consistent. Schoenfeld et al. (2015) compared a full-body 3×/week program to a traditional body-part split with the same total exercises, sets, reps, and loads. The full-body group showed significantly greater hypertrophy. Raastad et al. (2012) showed that the Norwegian powerlifting team — 6 sessions/week with the same total weekly volume split across 6 days — beat a 3-sessions/week group for both strength and hypertrophy. Multiple meta-analyses by Schoenfeld (2016), Grgic (2018), and Greg Nuckols (2018) all show that ≥2×/week per muscle group is superior to 1×/week, independent of total volume.

The minimum effective frequency is 2× per muscle group per week. Beyond that, the optimal number depends on your total weekly volume, session length, and recovery capacity. The practical approach: calculate the total sets you need per muscle, estimate how long those sets take, and distribute them across enough sessions to keep each session manageable. Three to six total training days per week covers the vast majority of lifters from novice to advanced.

Level 3: Progression & Periodization

Progressive overload is the foundational principle: the training stimulus must increase over time for adaptation to continue. Level 3 is about how that progression is structured across a training career. The answer depends entirely on training age — not years in the gym, but rate of progress. Novices progress session-to-session. Intermediates progress week-to-week. Advanced athletes progress month-to-month. This classification comes from Mark Rippetoe's Practical Programming and underpins the entire periodization section.

Novice linear progression is the simplest and most efficient method: add weight to the bar every session. For squats and deadlifts, that's +10 lbs per week. For bench press and upper body movements, +5 lbs per week. When you fail to complete target reps two sessions in a row, drop the load 10%, return to the stalled weight the following session. If you stall again, halve the increment. Stalling on most lifts despite these resets is the signal to graduate to intermediate programming.

Intermediate wave loading (linear periodization within a mesocycle) adds one layer of complexity: reps decrease by one per week while load increases, then the wave resets from the higher-rep end at a heavier weight. A three-week wave might look like 3×8, then 3×7, then 3×6 — each week slightly heavier. For isolation exercises, double progression is simpler: choose a rep range of, say, 12–15. Add reps each session. Once you hit 3×15, increase the load and drop back to 3×12.

Advanced block periodization structures the year into distinct mesocycles with specific goals. The Accumulation Block (6 weeks) prioritizes muscle mass and work capacity: sets build from 12 per muscle per week up to 20–22, RPE stays in the 5–8 range, rep ranges stay moderate (6–12 for hypertrophy, 4–6 for strength focus). The Intensification Block (4 weeks) converts that work capacity into maximal strength: sets drop from 18 back to 12 per week, RPE climbs to 9–9.5 by the final week, rep ranges drop to 2–5 for three-quarters of volume. The Realization Block (1–2 weeks) is the taper: volume halved, intensity maintained, RPE descending to allow full expression of the fitness built in the two prior blocks.

Daily Undulating Periodization (DUP) varies rep ranges and intensity each session within a week. A classic DUP week might have a hypertrophy day, a strength day, and a power day. Zourdos et al. (2016) found that a modified HPS order (Hypertrophy → Power → Strength) outperformed the traditional HSP order because placing light power work between the most fatiguing day and the most important day allowed better recovery before the strength session.

Deloads are mandatory, not optional. The reactive deload checklist: if two or more of the following are true — dreading the gym, sleeping worse than normal, lifts declining, life stress elevated, aches and pains worsening — take a deload week. That means roughly 50% of normal volume at similar intensity. Even without any warning signs, a mandatory deload every third mesocycle prevents gradual fatigue accumulation that escapes the checklist. Fatigue is an insidious accumulator.

Competition tapering follows the same principles: cut volume 50–67%, maintain or slightly increase intensity, and use a "primer session" 24–48 hours before the meet (light singles at RPE 4–5 on competition lifts only — no accessories). Tsoukos et al. (2018) showed that a light resistance session 24–48 hours before competition improves performance on meet day. Attempt selection strategy: open at your current 3RM, take your current 2RM as the second attempt, and attempt a conservative PR on the third. Build the total, not individual records.

Level 4: Exercise Selection

Exercise selection matters more for strength than for hypertrophy. For strength athletes, specificity is nearly everything: the most direct path to a bigger squat is squatting. Powerlifters should direct 50–75% of their total training volume to the competition lifts (squat, bench, deadlift), with the remaining 25–50% as accessories. Bodybuilders need 1–2 compound movements per major muscle group and 1–3 isolation exercises, but the specific exercises are far more interchangeable than the volume and intensity applied to them.

The evidence for specificity is granular. Schoenfeld et al. (2014) showed that training at 10RM loads produces less 1RM improvement than training at 3RM loads with the same total volume — load specificity matters. A 1998 study of a 20-week bench/leg press/curl program found that arm hypertrophy appeared at 10 weeks, but trunk and leg hypertrophy didn't appear until 20 weeks. The explanation is skill-before- hypertrophy: the curl is mastered quickly, so overload and growth start early; compound lower-body movements take longer to learn, delaying hypertrophic stimulus.

Variety and consistency exist in tension, and both matter. Fonseca et al. (2014) showed that a group doing four lower-body exercises (Smith machine squat, leg press, deadlift, and lunges) gained more strength on the Smith squat than a group doing only the Smith squat with equal total sets — because accessory work eliminated the "weakest link" that otherwise limits the main lift. It also produced more uniform quad development across all four heads, since each movement emphasizes different joint angles and activates different muscle compartments preferentially.

Rauch et al. (2017) studied autoregulated exercise selection in trained lifters over nine weeks. The group that chose their own exercises within loading zones gained more upper-body strength and more lean mass than the fixed-exercise group. The autoregulated group didn't abandon proficiency — they still trained each movement 4–14 times in nine weeks — but they naturally chose compound movements more often, which increased per-muscle volume and frequency. The practical rule: keep your main compound movements constant for the full macrocycle. Rotate isolation exercises as often as mesocycle-to-mesocycle.

Range of motion is unambiguous: full ROM produces more hypertrophy than partial, even though partial ROM allows heavier loads. Bloomquist et al. (2013) on squats, Pinto et al. (2012) on bicep curls, and McMahon et al. (2014) on mixed leg training all found full ROM superior. Partial ROM training transfers only to partial ROM performance. Train the full range you have and work to expand it.

The mind-muscle connection has genuine utility on isolation exercises: focusing attention on the target muscle demonstrably improves activation. But above approximately 80% 1RM on compound lifts, the effect disappears (Snyder and Fry, 2012). When the weight is that heavy, all available motor units must contribute to complete the rep. Focusing on the lat during a heavy deadlift pulls cognitive resources from the neural drive that's actually moving the bar.

Sticking points are not where you think they are. What appears as a stall at a particular point in the range of motion is actually a failure that began earlier — the bar has already decelerated by the time it becomes visible. Pausing at the visible sticking point therefore trains you to produce less force at exactly the wrong position. Better approaches include technique variants that "punish" the fault (front squats for athletes whose hips shoot up too early), accommodating resistance (bands or chains that increase resistance at lockout to train through the sticking region), and simply getting stronger overall through progressive overload in the full competition pattern.

Level 5: Rest Periods

The case for short rest periods was built on a foundation that collapsed under scrutiny. The hypothesis was that short rest intervals (60–90 seconds) maximize the acute post-exercise growth hormone spike, and this spike drives hypertrophy. The problem: West and Phillips (2010) showed that the hormone response was caused by the high-volume training that produced growth, not a cause of the growth itself. Ehrnborg et al. (2005) demonstrated that supraphysiological growth hormone at doping-equivalent levels for a full month produced no significant hypertrophy in otherwise healthy adults. The mechanism was always wrong.

The direct evidence against short rest is equally clear. A 2009 study by Buresh et al. compared 1-minute versus 2.5-minute rest periods. The short-rest group had higher acute hormone responses. The long-rest group had more muscle growth. The reason: longer rest allowed heavier loads on subsequent sets, producing more total mechanical stimulus. Reviews by Henselmans and Schoenfeld (2014) and systematic reviews by Grgic et al. (2018) found no evidence of superior hypertrophy from shorter rest — and if anything, longer rest either matched or surpassed shorter rest.

Antagonist Paired Sets (APS) are the practical resolution to the tension between rest length and time efficiency. Pair a compound push with a compound pull — bench press with barbell row, for example — and perform one set of each as the "rest" for the other. The agonist muscle is passively recovering while the antagonist works. Robbins et al. (2010) showed APS groups performed more total volume load than traditional groups, and at minimum APS doesn't hurt performance. The result: shorter sessions without sacrificed volume. Do not apply APS to squats, deadlifts, or other full-body exercises — the cardiovascular demand is too high to maintain performance.

Drop sets and rest-pause sets are time-efficient tools for accessories when the gym is crowded or the session is running long. Ozaki et al. (2018) showed that one working set plus four drops (80→65→50→40→30% 1RM, all to failure) produced similar strength and size gains to 3 straight sets with 3-minute rest — in roughly 2 minutes of actual training time versus 7. The trade-off is higher acute fatigue and more soreness, so drop sets belong at the end of sessions and on muscles with at least a full rest day before the next training session.

Level 6: Lifting Tempo

Lifting tempo sits at the apex of the pyramid — the variable that matters least. Getting it wrong costs you slightly. Getting it right adds very little. The practical rule is simple: control the eccentric, accelerate the concentric with maximal intent. Everything interesting about tempo research follows from understanding why.

The concept of time under tension (TUT) is widely cited as the mechanism for hypertrophy. Deliberately slow reps extend TUT. But TUT ignores the magnitude of the force being applied. The more complete metric is impulse — Force multiplied by Time (the area under the force-time curve). Mohamad et al. (2012) compared 3×12 at 70% 1RM to 6×12 at 35% 1RM, volume-equated, both with maximal movement intent. The 35% group had longer TUT and higher velocity. The 70% group had approximately 25% greater total impulse despite shorter TUT. Greater impulse is greater stimulus. Doubling rep count to compensate for halving the load doesn't work.

Slow tempo directly reduces volume. Headley et al. (2011) had subjects do AMRAPs at 75% 1RM with either a 2-second eccentric or a 4-second eccentric. The 4-second group produced 1.5 fewer reps per set and approximately 10% less total work performed. The slow eccentric fatigued the muscle before the concentric even began. The pattern mirrors short rest periods: sacrificing a major variable (volume, mechanical tension) to optimize a minor one.

Gonzalez-Badillo et al. (2014) tested maximal intent versus half-speed concentric on the bench press with matched loads. The maximal-intent group achieved approximately twice the strength gains. The mechanism is motor learning: attempting to accelerate the bar maximally recruits higher-threshold motor units even when the bar moves slowly due to the weight. Deliberate slow training teaches the nervous system to produce force gradually. That's the opposite of what strength and power development require.

A 2015 meta-analysis by Schoenfeld et al. found that repetition duration has no effect on hypertrophy for reps lasting under 10 seconds. At and above 10 seconds per rep, the effect becomes negative. Slow training is not a technique for advanced hypertrophy — it is a beginner coaching tool for learning movement patterns, and a rehabilitation technique for tendon pain (2–4 second tempo in both directions, 60–85% 1RM, per Lim and Wong, 2018).

Quick Start Guide to Program Building

The book's Quick Start Guide reduces everything to six steps, each corresponding to a pyramid level. This is the practical synthesis — where the science becomes a program.

Step 1 — Choose training days (Adherence). Most lifters train 3–5 days per week, hitting each muscle or movement 2–4 times. Two days per week can work for novices with scheduling constraints. More days means shorter individual sessions. Common split options: 2 days = Full Body; 3 days = Full Body ×3 or Lower/Upper/Full; 4 days = Upper/Lower ×2; 5 days = Legs/Push/Pull + 2 Full Body; 6 days = Legs/Push/Pull ×2.

Step 2 — Set volume, intensity, and frequency (Level 2). Start with 10–12 sets per muscle per week as a novice, 13–15 as intermediate, 16–20 as advanced. For hypertrophy: most compound work at 3–10 reps for free weights, 6–15 reps for machines, 8–20 for isolations. For strength: main lift strength work at 1–5 reps RPE 7–10, volume work at 4–8 reps RPE 5–8. Frequency minimum: 2×/week per movement.

Step 3 — Plan progression (Level 3). Novices: linear load progression session-to-session (+10 lbs squat/deadlift per week, +5 lbs upper body). Intermediates: wave loading for compounds, double progression for isolations. Advanced: block periodization with wave loading within blocks. Always plan deloads — reactive (via checklist) or mandatory (every 3rd mesocycle).

Step 4 — Select exercises (Level 4). One or two primary compounds per movement pattern, stable across the macrocycle. One to three isolations per muscle group, rotatable between mesocycles. Choose movements that are pain-free, suit your biomechanics, and match your goal (competition specificity for powerlifters, uniform development for bodybuilders). Order compounds first; isolations last.

Step 5 — Set rest periods (Level 5). Straight sets: ≥2.5 min for compounds, ≥1.5 min for isolations. Use APS for upper push/pull pairs and for antagonist isolations to reduce session time. Drop sets and rest-pause for time-pressed accessory work only.

Step 6 — Tempo (Level 6). Controlled eccentric (don't let gravity drop the bar). Maximal-intent concentric (try to accelerate the load, even if it moves slowly). That's it.

Sample Programs Overview

The book provides six complete sample programs: Novice, Intermediate, and Advanced versions for both Powerlifting and Bodybuilding. These are teaching tools demonstrating how the pyramid principles translate into actual weekly schedules — not universal prescriptions. The authors explicitly warn against treating them as plug-and-play solutions.

The Novice Powerlifting Program runs 3–4 days per week using a daily undulating model within the week: a hypertrophy day, a power/technique day, and a strength day. Main lifts use % 1RM (established via 3–5RM test and a calculator); accessories use RPE from the start. Volume is at the low end of the guidelines — roughly 10–12 sets per muscle per week — with about half coming from competition lifts and half from accessories. Progression is linear: +10 lbs per week for squat and deadlift, +5 lbs for everything else. Miss a session's reps: repeat. Miss two sessions in a row: drop 10%.

The Novice Bodybuilding Program is a 4-day lower/upper split. Days 1 and 3 are lower body; days 2 and 4 are upper body. The first session of each pair is strength-focused (lower reps, higher load); the second is hypertrophy-focused (moderate reps, moderate load). APS pairs are built in for upper body and antagonist isolations. Same progression rules as the powerlifting novice.

The Intermediate Powerlifting Program runs 4 days per week and introduces wave loading: reps decrease one per week (e.g., 9 → 8 → 7) as load increases, then the cycle resets at a heavier starting weight three weeks later. The main lifts use both % 1RM as a starting point and RPE to calibrate actual loading — RPE 7 on the hypertrophy day, RPE 5 on the technique day, RPE 8 on the strength day. Volume sits at 13–15 sets per muscle per week. Competition prep is built directly into the template: four weeks out, the strength day rep ranges drop to 1–3; meet week is a primer session at RPE 4–5.

The Advanced Powerlifting Program is a 6-day program using block periodization. An Accumulation mesocycle (higher reps, more accessories, RPE 5–8) alternates with an Intensification mesocycle (lower reps, fewer accessories, RPE approaching 9–10). Testing protocols after each block — either AMRAP at 85% for strength estimation or a full mock meet — provide regular progress data without relying on arbitrary 1RM days. RPE ranges replace single target RPEs, allowing load to respond to daily variation.

The Intermediate and Advanced Bodybuilding Programs follow the same block structure adapted for hypertrophy goals. The intermediate runs 5 days (lower/upper split plus a third dedicated day); the advanced runs 6 days (alternating lower/upper, with daily undulating rep ranges within the week). Both programs use wave loading for compound movements and double progression for isolations. The key distinction from the powerlifting programs is exercise breadth — more isolation work, less competition-lift specificity, and greater attention to lagging muscle groups.

For lifters who compete in both sports — powerbuilders — the recommendation is to run the powerlifting template most of the year, adding 2–3 sets of 8–15 reps for triceps, biceps, calves, hamstring curls (short head of bicep femoris only crosses the knee and is undertrained by squats and deadlifts), and leg extensions (rectus femoris). Then, 3–6 months out from bodybuilding competition, switch to the bodybuilding template with competition lifts substituted wherever variants are specified.

Programming Takeaways

These are the practical applications — how to actually use the pyramid to build and troubleshoot your own training programs.

Start Low, Progress Slow

The most common mistake in self-programmed training is starting with too much volume. The dose-response curve for volume is non-linear — the first 10 sets per muscle per week produce the most adaptation per set. Sets 15 through 20 produce less. Sets above 20 may actively slow progress. Start at 10–12 sets per muscle per week regardless of how experienced you feel, run a full mesocycle, assess your recovery and progress, then add 1–2 sets per muscle if results are good. Resist the urge to add everything at once.

Choose Your Rep Ranges by Goal and Exercise Type

Rep ranges are not arbitrary. For strength, you need enough work in the 1–6 rep zone to develop the neuromuscular patterns and motor skills that heavy loads require. Two-thirds to three-quarters of your volume there, the rest in moderate reps. For hypertrophy, the 6–12 rep range is the sweet spot for free weight compounds — heavy enough for meaningful tension, light enough to accumulate adequate volume per session. Machine work and isolations can extend into the 12–20 range productively. The problem with programming exclusively in one zone is you miss adaptations that only occur at other rep ranges.

Use the Deload Checklist Every Mesocycle

A deload is not a sign of weakness — it's the mechanism that converts accumulated fatigue into visible performance gains. Run the checklist at the end of every mesocycle: dreading the gym, sleeping worse than normal, lifts declining, life stress elevated, aches and pains worsening. Two or more "yes" answers mean take a deload week — approximately 50% of normal volume at similar intensity. Even if all answers are "no," take a mandatory deload every third mesocycle. Fatigue accumulates invisibly before it manifests in symptoms.

Build Your Main Compound Lifts and Stick with Them

The single biggest driver of long-term progress in both strength and hypertrophy is getting increasingly strong on a small number of compound movements trained repeatedly. The specificity principle is not a limitation — it's the mechanism. Squats, deadlifts, bench press, overhead press, and rows should be the fixed anchors of your program across a full training macrocycle. Isolation exercises can rotate between mesocycles to address variety and weak points. Exercise novelty provides a temporary stimulus bump but costs you the progressive overload advantage that only comes from deep familiarity with a movement.

Autoregulate Load with RPE

Use percentage of 1RM to set a starting load, then adjust based on that first set's RPE for all subsequent sets. If 80% of your estimated 1RM lands at RPE 6 instead of RPE 7, add a small amount of load. If it lands at RPE 9, reduce load. Over time, RPE tracking tells you whether your strength is trending up or down, provides ongoing progress data without formal testing, and makes your training automatically harder on good days and appropriately lighter on hard days. Novices should track RPE for the first few months without using it to adjust load — calibration takes time.

Adjust Volume When Cutting

A caloric deficit is a systemic stressor that reduces recovery capacity. During a cut, step down one volume category (advanced → intermediate, intermediate → novice range) in the first third of the diet, or sooner if the cut is aggressive. Switch from reactive deloads to automatic deloads after every mesocycle — the checklist becomes unreliable because being tired, cranky, and unmotivated is just what cutting feels like, not a training overreach signal. Use all available autoregulation strategies: flexible training days, RPE-based loading, exercise selection adjustments.

Key Takeaways

The Pyramid Hierarchy Is the Core Insight

Most training debates are arguments about the apex while ignoring the base. Slow versus fast tempo, 90-second versus 3-minute rest, cables versus free weights — these variables reside at levels 5 and 6 of the pyramid and account for perhaps 5–10% of outcomes. Volume, intensity, frequency, and progression at levels 2 and 3 determine 80%+ of results. Get the hierarchy right and most arguments about training dissolve.

Adherence Beats Optimization

An optimal program abandoned at month two produces worse outcomes than a suboptimal program followed consistently for a year. The research on flexible periodization isn't merely theoretical — lifters who adapt their training to life circumstances outperform those locked into rigid programming. This is not permission to train randomly; it's recognition that consistency, not perfection, is the primary training variable.

Volume Is a Dial, Not a Lever

More sets produce more adaptation only up to a point. Past approximately 20 sets per muscle per week, incremental volume more often increases fatigue than stimulates new growth. The dose-response relationship is inverted U-shaped. Managing volume intelligently — starting low, increasing incrementally, and periodically reducing to restore sensitivity — produces more long-term progress than maximizing volume at every phase.

RPE + Percentage Is Better Than Percentage Alone

A 70% 1RM back squat produces anywhere from 9 to 26 reps in trained individuals. Percentage-based programming creates an illusion of precision while ignoring individual variation in muscle fiber composition, daily recovery state, and training adaptation. RPE alongside percentage bridges the gap — the percentage sets the ballpark, the RPE calibrates the actual loading to what your body is capable of that specific day.

Fatigue Dissipates Faster Than Fitness

The entire logic of the deload, taper, and peaking protocol rests on this asymmetry. Fitness, once built, is durable. Fatigue, acute and chronic, fades within days to a week. This is why a well-timed deload before a competition — or simply before resuming hard training — produces genuine strength expression, not just rest. The fitness was always there; fatigue was hiding it.

Strength and Hypertrophy Are Deeply Related

Bigger muscles become stronger muscles. Stronger muscles can be trained with more volume and load, producing more hypertrophy. The two goals are not opposites; they reinforce each other over a career. Tracking strength on primary compound lifts is the best proxy for progressive overload in hypertrophy programs, because you can't reliably measure a centimeter of quad growth in any given month, but you can measure whether your squat went up.

Contrarian Ideas

Short Rest Periods Are Counterproductive for Hypertrophy

Conventional wisdom: short rest intervals maximize the hormonal stimulus for muscle growth. Reality: the hormone spike is a consequence of high training volume, not a driver of hypertrophy. Growth hormone at supraphysiological, doping-level doses for a full month produces no significant muscle growth. Short rest intervals reduce performance on subsequent sets, decrease total volume, and produce equal or worse hypertrophic outcomes than longer rest in every well-controlled study. The entire rationale for "the pump" as a primary training goal is built on a mechanism that doesn't work.

Slow Training Reduces Gains, It Doesn't Increase Them

Conventional wisdom: time under tension drives hypertrophy, therefore slower reps produce more growth. Reality: what matters is force multiplied by time (impulse), not time alone. Slow tempos reduce reps per set, reduce total volume, and reduce the peak force produced in the set. Maximal intent to accelerate the bar — even when it moves slowly due to heavy load — produces twice the strength gains of deliberate slow concentric velocity. Slow training produces less of the stimulus, not more.

More Volume Does Not Always Mean More Muscle

Conventional wisdom: the more you train, the more you grow. Reality: the dose-response relationship for volume is non-linear. Heaselgrave et al. (2018) found 18 sets per week for biceps outperformed both 9 sets and 27 sets. The group doing the most volume showed less growth than the group doing a moderate amount. Past a threshold that varies by individual and training phase, additional sets consume recovery resources without producing proportional adaptation. The goal is the minimum effective dose that drives progress, not maximum tolerable volume.

Training to Failure Every Set Is Suboptimal

Conventional wisdom: pushing to absolute failure maximizes the training stimulus. Reality: training to failure drops total reps across a session (8, 6, 5 = 19 reps versus 7, 7, 7 = 21 reps at RPE 8). Izquierdo et al. (2006) found failure training produced overtraining biomarkers with no additional strength gain. Moran-Navarro et al. (2017) found failure training requires 24–48 additional hours of recovery for the same net stimulus. The research suggests stopping 1–3 reps short of failure for most compound sets is both safer and more productive.

Exercise Rotation Impedes Hypertrophy

Conventional wisdom: "muscle confusion" — constantly changing exercises — prevents adaptation plateaus. Reality: the initial strength gains on any new exercise are primarily neurological, not hypertrophic. While you're learning a movement, you cannot produce sufficient overload to drive meaningful muscle growth. The 1998 bench/leg press/curl study found no trunk or leg hypertrophy for the first ten weeks of training — only after the movements were mastered did growth begin. Constant rotation keeps you permanently in the neurological adaptation phase, perpetually delaying hypertrophic stimulus.

Proposals & Frameworks

The REF Framework for Adherence

Every training program should satisfy three conditions before it is adopted. Realistic: matches actual schedule, time constraints, and life demands. Enjoyable: sustainable without dreading every session; suboptimal-but-followed beats optimal-but-abandoned. Flexible: can absorb disruption (missed sessions, travel, stress) without collapsing. Any program that fails one or more of these conditions will eventually be abandoned and should be redesigned.

RPE/RIR Auto-Regulation System

Use percentage of 1RM to set a starting load; use RPE to calibrate every subsequent set in real time. RPE 10 = failure. RPE 8 = 2 reps left. RPE 7 = 3 reps left. Prescribe sets as, for example, "3×5 at 80% RPE 7–8" — the percentage gives the ballpark, the RPE range gives the day-to-day adjustment. Over time, RPE tracking functions as an ongoing strength test, revealing whether fitness is trending up, flat, or down without requiring formal 1RM tests.

The Reactive Deload Checklist

At the end of every mesocycle, ask: (1) Am I dreading the gym? (2) Am I sleeping worse than normal? (3) Are my lifts declining? (4) Is life stress higher than usual? (5) Are aches and pains worse than normal? Two or more "yes" answers: take a deload week (~50% of normal volume at similar intensity). Zero or one "yes": proceed to the next mesocycle. Regardless of checklist score: mandatory deload every third mesocycle.

Block Periodization Structure

Accumulation → Intensification → Realization. Each block has a primary goal. Volume builds in Accumulation (12–22 sets/week, RPE 5–8, moderate rep ranges). Volume reduces as intensity climbs in Intensification (12–18 sets/week, RPE 8–9.5, lower rep ranges). The Realization block is a 1–2 week taper: volume halved, intensity maintained, RPE descending to allow full fitness expression. Repeat with slightly higher starting loads each macrocycle.

Attempt Selection for Powerlifting Competition

Open at your current 3RM (high confidence of success). Second attempt: current 2RM (extension of warmup quality). Third attempt: next incremental PR (conservative, not heroic). The goal is to build the largest total possible — missing attempts costs the total and disrupts momentum. Only pursue a single-lift record attempt when it doesn't jeopardize the total.

What Didn't Fit

Blood Flow Restriction (BFR) Training

BFR training uses a cuff or wrap to partially restrict blood flow to a limb, then performs light-load training — typically around 20% of 1RM — that produces significant hypertrophic stimulus at loads far too light to cause mechanical tension via conventional means. Helms mentions this specifically in the context of injury management: when a joint or structural issue prevents loading at normal training intensities, BFR can maintain hypertrophic stimulus at loads that won't aggravate the injury. The mechanism involves metabolic accumulation and cell swelling as the primary drivers rather than mechanical tension, making it a legitimate tool in the rehabilitation and maintenance context. It won't replace heavy training for healthy athletes, but it's a far better option than simply stopping training during injury recovery.

The Norwegian Powerlifting Study

The Raastad et al. (2012) Norwegian frequency experiment is one of the most striking examples of frequency research in practice. The Norwegian national powerlifting team was split into two groups: one trained each lift three times per week (standard competition preparation), the other trained six times per week with identical total weekly volume simply divided across more sessions. After the study period, the higher-frequency group showed meaningfully greater strength and hypertrophy gains. The finding is particularly compelling because the subjects were already elite, experienced athletes — the population for whom further gains are hardest to produce and most meaningful. Same volume, more sessions, better outcome.

Accommodating Resistance — Bands and Chains

Accommodating resistance uses elastic bands or heavy chains to make a barbell exercise harder at the top of the movement (lockout) while lighter at the bottom (where leverage is worst). The theory is that this better matches the strength curve of many exercises. Helms is measured in his assessment: a 2018 meta-analysis by dos Santos et al. found no overall advantage of accommodating resistance over traditional resistance for strength development. The primary practical use is for equipped powerlifters simulating the assistance of their suit or wraps in raw training, or for speed work (50–60% 1RM plus bands) where maintaining resistance at lockout prevents the natural deceleration that typically occurs when the bar is moving fast. For most raw lifters, it's a complexity that adds little.

The "Dual Athlete" Dilemma

Powerbuilders — athletes who compete in both powerlifting and bodybuilding — face a genuine programming problem: the two sports have different exercise priorities, different periodization demands, and different peak timing requirements. Helms' solution is sequential rather than simultaneous. For most of the year, run the powerlifting template. The strength base transfers to bodybuilding because bigger muscles are also stronger. Then, 3–6 months before a bodybuilding competition, shift to the bodybuilding template with the competition lifts substituted wherever variants appear in the program. Attempting to run both templates simultaneously dilutes the specificity of both. The physiques of elite powerbuilders like Matt Ogus demonstrate that sequential prioritization works.

Exercise Reference

Every exercise mentioned in the book, organized by movement pattern. The breadth here reflects the book's emphasis on accessory work and exercise variety as tools for addressing weak points and structural limitations — not just the competition lifts.

Lower Body — Squat Pattern

Back Squat (High-Bar) — bar sits higher on the traps, more upright torso, greater quad emphasis. Preferred for bodybuilders and athletes with shorter femurs.
Back Squat (Low-Bar) — bar sits in the rear deltoid shelf, more forward torso lean, greater posterior chain emphasis. Most common in raw powerlifting.
Front Squat — bar on front deltoids, very upright torso, maximum quad activation, less lumbar stress. Useful for athletes with long femurs who struggle with low-bar mechanics.
Zercher Squat — bar held in the crook of the elbows, high demand on upper back and core, upright torso. Less common; useful as a squat variant.
Safety Bar Squat — cambered bar with handles transfers bar weight to front, reducing shoulder mobility demands. Good pain-free alternative for athletes with shoulder or wrist issues.
Pause Squat — static pause at the bottom of the squat, typically 2–3 seconds. Eliminates the stretch- shortening cycle benefit, training the concentric from a dead stop. Useful for technique refinement.
Leg Press — machine-based bilateral lower body push. Lower lumbar fatigue than barbell squats. Useful for athletes with biomechanical limitations for barbell squatting, or as a secondary quad developer.
Hack Squat — machine or barbell variant with heels elevated, emphasizing quads. Good quad isolation alternative to leg press.
Smith Machine Squat — fixed bar path; useful for technique learning but less specific than free bar squatting for competition strength.
Bulgarian Split Squat — rear foot elevated, front-leg bilateral imbalance correction. High quad and glute demand. Useful for addressing left-right strength asymmetries.
Lunges — forward, reverse, or walking. Multiple quad heads and glute activation. Fonseca et al. (2014) showed lunges + squats + leg press produced more uniform quad development than squats alone.
Step-Ups — unilateral leg press movement. Develops single-leg strength and coordination. Lower injury risk than Bulgarian split squats.
Single-Leg Squat (Pistol) — full unilateral squat. Very high coordination and strength demand. Primarily useful for ensuring equal development between legs.

Lower Body — Hip Hinge & Posterior Chain

Conventional Deadlift — hip-width stance, hands outside legs. Develops the full posterior chain. The primary strength lift in powerlifting.
Sumo Deadlift — wide stance, hands inside legs, more upright torso. Lower lumbar fatigue than conventional; preferred by some athletes for biomechanical reasons.
Romanian Deadlift (RDL) — hip-hinge with controlled eccentric to mid-shin, minimal knee bend. Primary hamstring and glute developer. Preferred for higher-rep hypertrophy work (6+ reps).
Stiff-Leg Deadlift — similar to RDL but with less hip push-back and more lower back involvement.
Good Morning — bar on back, hinge at the hip with relatively straight legs. Heavy lumbar and hamstring demand. Useful as a deadlift accessory for lower back and posterior chain development.
Glute Ham Raise (GHR) — prone, knee-driven hip extension using a GHR machine. Primary developer of hamstring and glute in a hip extension pattern with minimal lumbar load.
Weighted Back Extension — hyperextension bench, loaded with plates or barbell. Trains erectors and glutes. Much lower lumbar fatigue per set than deadlifts.
Reverse Hyperextension — prone on a reverse hyper machine, legs raised in hip extension. Trains posterior chain with spinal decompression. Useful for athletes managing lower back fatigue.
Barbell Hip Thrust — back on a bench, bar across hips, maximum glute contraction at lockout. Primary glute developer; does not train hamstrings through full range.
Glute Bridge — floor version of hip thrust, lower range of motion. Good introductory exercise for hip extension pattern.
Cable Pull-Through — cable between legs, hip hinge pattern. Teaches hip extension mechanics with lower load than barbell variants.

Upper Body — Push

Flat Barbell Bench Press — the primary horizontal push in powerlifting. Trains chest, anterior delts, and triceps. Competition standard.
Incline Barbell/DB Bench Press — shifts emphasis to upper chest and anterior delts. Middle delts contribute more to incline than flat pressing.
Close-Grip Bench Press (CGBP) — hands shoulder-width or slightly narrower. Greater tricep emphasis, reduced chest and shoulder contribution. Primary bench accessory for powerlifters developing tricep lockout strength.
Dumbbell Bench Press — greater range of motion than barbell, independent arm movement. Useful for addressing left-right imbalances.
Overhead Press (OHP) — standing or seated barbell press. Primary developer of anterior and middle delts and triceps. The fourth competition lift in strongman; standard accessory in powerlifting.
Push Press — leg-drive assisted OHP. Allows heavier loading than strict OHP; primary Olympic lifting accessory.
Dips — bodyweight or weighted. Close-grip variant is primarily tricep; wider grip is more chest. Good pushing volume without additional spinal compression.
Board Press — bench press performed with boards on the chest to reduce range of motion. Specifically trains the lockout portion of the bench. Primarily used in equipped powerlifting.

Upper Body — Pull

Barbell Row (Pendlay Row) — bar from floor each rep, horizontal pull. High lat and mid-back activation. The Pendlay variant requires a reset between reps for stricter form.
Seal Row (Bench Row) — chest on an elevated bench, removes lower back stabilization demand. Allows full lat engagement without lumbar fatigue.
Cable Row — adjustable angle cable pull. Consistent tension throughout range of motion. Good for mid-back isolation without lumbar involvement.
Single-Arm Dumbbell Row — unilateral row with knee and hand braced. Good range of motion, addresses left-right imbalances.
Chest-Supported Row — incline bench support removes lumbar stabilization. Useful when lower back is fatigued from heavy squat/deadlift work.
Lat Pulldown — machine-based vertical pull. Good lat development; instruction from an expert improves lat activation in novices (Snyder and Leech, 2009).
Chin-Ups — underhand grip vertical pull. Higher bicep involvement than pull-ups. Weighted chin-ups are a primary upper body compound for strength athletes.
Pull-Ups — overhand grip vertical pull. Greater lat emphasis than chin-ups. Band-assisted for beginners.
Face Pull — cable pulling toward the face, external rotation component. Targets rear delts, external rotators, and upper traps. Useful for shoulder health.
DB/Cable Pullover — horizontal plane lat stretch and contraction, also trains triceps and chest. Useful for lat isolation without vertical pulling patterns.

Isolation Exercises

Bicep Curls — barbell, dumbbell, cable, or machine. Primary bicep developer.
Tricep Pushdown — cable or machine. Tricep isolation in an elbow extension pattern. Primary tricep developer for bodybuilders.
Overhead Tricep Extension — dumbbell or cable, tricep in a stretched position. Trains the long head of the tricep that is understimulated by pushing movements.
Leg Extension — machine-based quad isolation. The only exercise that isolates the rectus femoris in knee extension. Useful for bodybuilders and powerlifters seeking additional quad development.
Leg Curl (Lying/Seated) — machine-based hamstring isolation. The short head of the bicep femoris only crosses the knee (not the hip), so it is undertrained by deadlifts and squats — leg curls provide the missing stimulus.
Lateral Raise — dumbbell or cable, arm abduction. Primary middle delt developer. Middle delts contribute to all vertical and horizontal pulling, but direct lateral raises are needed for visible lateral delt development.
Front Raise — anterior delt isolation. Less commonly needed because anterior delts receive heavy indirect training from all pressing.
Calf Raise (Standing/Seated/Leg Press) — standing: primarily gastrocnemius. Seated: primarily soleus. Both heads are needed for complete lower leg development.
Cable Fly / DB Fly — chest isolation, emphasizes the stretch and adduction portion of chest training. Trained across incline, flat, and decline angles for complete chest development.

Specialty & Technique Variations

Pause Deadlift — pause below the knee to practice keeping the bar close and maintaining back position.
Deficit Deadlift — standing on a plate, greater ROM than conventional. Useful for improving off-the- floor strength.
Block Pull / Rack Pull — pulling from elevated position, reduced ROM. Allows heavier loading for overload at specific portions of the pull.
Speed Work (50–60% + bands/chains) — maintains resistance at lockout, preventing the deceleration phase. Primarily used in Westside-influenced programming.
Isometric Holds Against Pins — maximal isometric contraction at a specific joint angle. Theoretically trains force-deficit points, but exact implementation requires motion capture to locate precisely.
Grip Holds — near-lockout deadlift from pins at 90–110% of 1RM, held for 10–20 seconds. Used by advanced powerlifters to develop static grip strength.
Single-Arm Bodyweight Hang — grip endurance with spinal decompression. Useful on days when the spine is already compressed from heavy squat/deadlift work.

Covers volume, intensity, frequency, progression, periodization, exercise selection, rest periods, and tempo with specific numerical recommendations, sample programs at three training levels (novice, intermediate, advanced), and practical tools including the RPE/RIR scale, deload checklist, and a complete Quick Start Guide for building your own program.

About the Authors

Eric Helms, PhD, CSCS holds a doctorate in strength and conditioning from Auckland University of Technology (AUT) at SPRINZ, where he also completed research on RPE-based autoregulation in powerlifting. He is a certified strength and conditioning specialist, a natural pro bodybuilder (PNBA), and has competed at the international level in raw powerlifting under the IPF. Through his coaching organization 3D Muscle Journey, he has worked with hundreds of competitive natural bodybuilders and powerlifters, combining an unusually complete picture of the science and the practice. He has published multiple peer-reviewed papers on training and nutrition, is a co-editor of the MASS research review with Greg Nuckols and Mike Zourdos, and is widely regarded as one of the most rigorous evidence-based voices in the fitness industry.

Andy Morgan, BS runs RippedBody.com, one of the most respected evidence-based nutrition and training platforms for physique athletes. He works primarily with Japanese athletes and serves as a cultural and practical bridge between research-based fitness and real-world competitive preparation. His book The Last Shred covers contest preparation in detail. Andrea Valdez, MS holds a Master's degree in Exercise Physiology and is a 3D Muscle Journey coach. She brings practical coaching experience, academic rigor, and deep expertise in evidence-based programming for female athletes and natural bodybuilders.

Why This Book Matters

Most fitness books solve a narrow problem: they provide a specific program. The Muscle and Strength Pyramid solves a broader one — it teaches you to understand the principles behind any program, which means you can design, evaluate, and troubleshoot your own training indefinitely. This is the difference between being handed a fish and being taught to fish, but with research citations.

The book's lasting contribution is the pyramid framework itself: the explicit, evidence-backed hierarchy that tells you which variables to optimize first and which to worry about last. In an industry saturated with marketing dressed as science, and genuine scientists who can't write accessibly, this book is one of the rare documents that is both rigorously evidence- based and practically actionable. The RPE/RIR system it helped popularize is now standard in competitive powerlifting and increasingly common in evidence-based coaching generally. The sample programs remain among the most thoughtfully designed freely-available templates in the sport.
