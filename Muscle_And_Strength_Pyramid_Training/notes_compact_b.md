## LEVEL 4: EXERCISE SELECTION

### Specificity
- Pure strength athletes: 50–75% of volume from competition lifts (squat/bench/deadlift for powerlifters)
- Accessory work: 25–50% of total volume
- Hypertrophy: 1–2 compound + 1–3 isolation per muscle group
- Compound main lifts stay static across mesocycles; isolation movements can rotate more freely

### More Exercises > One Exercise (Key Study)
- 2014 study (Fonseca et al.): group doing Smith machine squat + leg press + deadlift + lunges
  gained more strength on Smith squat than group that only did Smith squat with equal total sets/reps
- Why: compound movements have a "weakest link" — accessory work develops lagging muscles
  that otherwise limit the main lift
- Also: four-exercise group showed more uniform quad development across all four heads
- **Mechanism**: muscle is compartmentalized (e.g., four heads of quads, sternal vs. clavicular pec head);
  different compartments preferentially activated by different joint angles/positions

### Autoregulated Exercise Selection (2017 study, Rauch et al.)
- Trained lifters who chose their own exercises (within loading zones) made more upper-body
  strength gains and more lean mass than fixed-exercise group over 9 weeks
- Autoregulated group still trained each movement 4–14 times in 9 weeks (didn't lose proficiency)
- They chose compound movements more often → higher volume/frequency per muscle group
- **Practical rule**: main compound lifts (upper push, upper pull, squat, hinge) stay for full macrocycle;
  isolations can rotate session-to-session or mesocycle-to-mesocycle

### Compound vs. Isolation
- Compound lifts: train multiple muscle groups simultaneously → time efficient, allow heavier loading,
  enable precise progress tracking over time
- Isolation exercises: needed for uniform/symmetrical development and to address weak points
- Novices: achieve global hypertrophy from compounds alone; isolation de-emphasized early
- Advanced: isolation increasingly important as weak points emerge

### Weak Points: Hypertrophy
- Structural weak points (narrow ribcage, long femurs) may require targeted accessory work
- Long femurs → forward lean in squat → poor quad activation, greater lumbar stress
  → better to use leg press or front squat + RDL for such athletes
- Technique issues can create uneven activation (e.g., biceps dominating rows instead of lats)
- **Helms personal example**: used straps/thumbless grip + waist-directed rowing to engage lats;
  prior to this, had t-taper instead of v-taper
- Expert instruction on lat pulldowns shown to improve latissimus activation in novices [Snyder & Leech, 2009]

### Weak Points: Strength (Powerlifters)
- "Weakest link in the chain" principle: if grip fails deadlift before back/legs, train grip directly
  (e.g., rack holds at near-lockout with 90–110% of max, timed 10–20 sec holds)
- Long-femur low-bar squatter: poor quad development from squats → add front squat or leg press
- Bench pain with high volume: limit comp bench freq/volume, supplement with CGBP, military press,
  or dumbbell press
- Volume dedicated to accessory work: can fluctuate **25–50%** of total training volume

### Sticking Points and Sticking Regions
- **Pauses**: pausing AT the visible sticking point is counterproductive
  (the bar has already decelerated before you see it stop; pausing reduces force at that spot)
- **Valid uses of pauses**: breaking movement into chunks for motor learning (pause below-knee deadlift
  to keep bar close; pause squat to practice eccentric-concentric transition; longer bench pauses
  before competition to practice from dead stop)
- **Isometric training** at force-deficit point: theoretically valid but exact point is hard to find
  without motion capture
- **Technique variants** that "punish" faults: front squat for "squat mornings" (hips shooting up)
- **Accommodating resistance (bands/chains)**: increases resistance as bar approaches lockout
  - Meta-analysis (dos Santos et al., 2018): no overall advantage of accommodating vs. traditional resistance
  - Most useful for equipped lifters mimicking suit/wrap assistance in raw training
  - Speed work (50–60% + bands): prevents braking phase by maintaining resistance at lockout

### Mind-Muscle Connection
- Focus on target muscle enhances activation at lighter loads
- At ≥80% 1RM on compound lifts: mind-muscle connection effect disappears — all muscles must
  maximally contribute to complete the rep [Snyder & Fry, 2012]
- Practical: use mind-muscle focus for isolation exercises and during technique learning;
  not for heavy compound sets

### Exercise Order
- Perform compound barbell exercises first — highest technical demand, most fatigue-generating,
  highest injury risk, most stimulus per rep [Simao et al., 2012]
- Exception: if a muscle group is a weak point and fatiguing it first won't impair compound lifts,
  train it first (e.g., biceps before bench, calves before squat/deadlift)

### Range of Motion
- Full ROM → greater hypertrophy than partial ROM, even though partial allows heavier loads
  - Squat partial vs. full ROM: full ROM superior [Bloomquist et al., 2013]
  - Biceps curls: full ROM superior [Pinto et al., 2012]
  - Mixed leg training: full ROM superior [McMahon et al., 2014]
- Partial ROM training → only partial transfer (stronger at partial, not full)
- Full ROM training → transfer to partial ROM as well
- **Rule**: train with the full ROM you currently have; gradually increase it over time via stretching
  and small ROM increments in the gym

### Summary Table: Exercise Selection
| Goal | Structure |
|------|-----------|
| Strength (Powerlifter) | 50–75% competition lifts; 25–50% accessory |
| Hypertrophy (Bodybuilder) | 1–2 compound/muscle group; 1–3 isolation/muscle group |

### Key References (Exercise Selection)
- Fonseca et al. (2014) — changes in exercise > loading scheme for strength
- Rauch et al. (2017) — autoregulated exercise selection; lean mass gains
- Snyder & Leech (2009) — expert instruction improves lat activation
- Snyder & Fry (2012) — mind-muscle effect disappears at ≥80% 1RM
- Bloomquist et al. (2013) — full vs. partial squat ROM
- Kompf & Arandjelovic (2016) — sticking points review
- dos Santos et al. (2018) — accommodating resistance meta-analysis

## LEVEL 5: REST PERIODS

### The Hormone Hypothesis (Debunked)
- Late 1980s–early 2000s: short rest intervals thought to maximize post-exercise hormone (GH) spike
  → believed to drive hypertrophy
- Problem: the hormone response was caused by the high-volume training that caused growth,
  not the cause of the growth itself [West & Phillips, 2010]
- Growth hormone at supraphysiological levels (doping-equivalent) for 1 full month → no significant
  hypertrophy effect [Ehrnborg et al., 2005]
- Study: 1 min vs. 2.5 min rest → shorter rest = higher acute hormone response BUT more muscle
  growth in the longer rest group [Buresh et al., 2009]
  - Why: longer rest group used heavier loads (sets taken to failure)

### Metabolic Fatigue Argument (Weak)
- Very short rest → fewer reps on subsequent sets → less total volume
- "Throwing baby out with bathwater": sacrificed volume for metabolic stress
- One study showed superiority of 1 min rest [Villanueva et al., 2015], but used submaximal
  training (no failure) — confound: cumulative fatigue created more stress per set, not fewer reps
- More volume with heavier load always better than restricted rest if both options available

### Muscle Damage and Rest Periods
- Muscle damage from short rest intervals: inconsistent and highly individual
- Noticeable muscle damage NOT required for muscle growth [Flann et al., 2011]
- Excessive muscle damage can reduce force production and interfere with hypertrophy [Paulsen et al., 2012]
- Damage occurs naturally from progressive training — no need to seek it out

### The Evidence: No Advantage to Short Rest for Hypertrophy
- De Souza et al. (2010): no significant difference in muscle CSA between 2-min and 30-sec rest
- Ahtiainen et al. (2005): 2 min vs. 5 min, matched volumes → no significant difference at 6 months
- Schoenfeld et al. (2014): powerlifting style (3 min rest) vs. bodybuilding style (90 sec rest),
  matched volumes → no significant difference in muscle thickness
- Helms et al. (2015) review: "No investigation to date has found variations in rest periods
  between 1 to 5 minutes to alter the hypertrophic response"
- Henselmans & Schoenfeld (2014) review: "No study has demonstrated greater muscle hypertrophy
  with shorter compared with longer rest intervals... if anything, longer rest is superior"
- Grgic et al. (2018) systematic reviews: shorter rest still produced robust responses BUT
  longer rest appeared superior for both strength and hypertrophy

### Practical Rest Period Recommendations
- **Default**: rest until ready to perform at your best
- **Minimum guidelines for straight sets**:
  - Isolation/small muscle groups: ≥1.5 minutes
  - Compound lifts: ≥2.5 minutes
- **If you tend to under-rest**: actually clock your rest periods

### Antagonist Paired Sets (APS)
- **Definition**: pair a set on one muscle group with a set on the opposing muscle group
  during the rest interval (e.g., bench press + seal row; leg extension + leg curl; bicep curl + tricep extension)
- Different from same-muscle supersets (which sacrifice volume and tension for metabolic fatigue)
- **Mechanism**: while performing the antagonist exercise, the agonist is passively recovering;
  active ROM of antagonist may produce active recovery or potentiation effect
- Study (Robbins et al., 2010): APS bench/bench pull group performed more volume load than traditional group
- Review (Robbins et al., 2010): APS at minimum doesn't harm performance; may improve it
- Maia et al. (2014): 1-minute rest between isolation APS → more reps vs. traditional

#### APS Implementation:
- **Compound push + pull (e.g., bench + row)**: ~2 min rest between each exercise, ~3–4 min per pairing
- **Isolation pairs (e.g., leg curl + leg extension)**: ~1 min rest between each exercise
- **Autoregulate**: if not ready after 2 min for compounds, wait longer

#### When NOT to use APS:
- Squats, deadlifts, deadlift variants, lunges — full-body exercises with high cardiovascular demand
  (Ciccone et al., 2014: doing bench + rows during squat rest intervals reduced squat reps)
- Powerlifters should be cautious even with bench (leg drive makes it quasi-full-body)
- When gym is crowded and holding two pieces of equipment is impractical

### Drop Sets
- Perform set to failure at given load → immediately reduce load and continue to failure → repeat
- Study (Ozaki et al., 2018): 1 set + 4 drops (80→65→50→40→30% 1RM, all to failure, ~2 min total)
  vs. 3×80% with 3-min rest (~7 min total) → similar strength and size gains, more reps in drop set
- **Time-efficient but tracking-difficult**: can't compare drop set volume to straight set volume
- Progress within drop-set sessions by: more reps at each drop, or same reps at higher loads
- Best use: accessory exercises when time-pressed; not for main compound lifts

### Rest-Pause Sets
- Select load, go to failure, rest 20–30 seconds, go to failure again, repeat until target reps hit
- Example: 100 lb goal for 15 reps → 9 reps + rest 30s → 5 reps + rest 30s → 1 rep
- Effective for strength and hypertrophy [Prestes et al., 2017]
- More trackable than drop sets (same load throughout)
- Induces more fatigue than straight sets at matched volume/load [Moran-Navarro et al., 2017]:
  3×10 to failure → 24–48 hours extra recovery needed vs. 6×5 with same load (5 reps short of failure)
- **Best use**: accessory movements; schedule on days furthest from next session for that muscle (e.g., Friday
  on Mon/Wed/Fri split)

### Rest Period Summary
| Scenario | Rest Period |
|----------|-------------|
| Straight sets, isolation | ≥1.5 minutes |
| Straight sets, compound | ≥2.5 minutes |
| APS, compound | ~2 min between each exercise |
| APS, isolation | ~1 min between each exercise |
| Drop/rest-pause sets | Use for accessories only; more fatigue = more recovery needed |

### Key References (Rest Periods)
- Buresh et al. (2009) — longer rest → more muscle growth despite lower hormone response
- Henselmans & Schoenfeld (2014) — rest period review; longer rest superior or neutral
- Grgic et al. (2018 ×2) — systematic reviews on rest, strength, and hypertrophy
- Robbins et al. (2010) — APS volume load advantage
- Ozaki et al. (2018) — drop sets comparable to 3 straight sets
- Prestes et al. (2017) — rest-pause vs. traditional; similar adaptations
- Moran-Navarro et al. (2017) — failure training → 24–48h extra recovery

## LEVEL 6 (APEX): LIFTING TEMPO

### The Least Important Level
- Tempo is the lowest-priority variable in the pyramid; getting it wrong slows progress slightly
  but is far less consequential than Levels 1–4

### Strength and Tempo
- Load-velocity relationship: heavier load → slower movement (not a choice — it's physics)
- Well-trained lifters can grind through very slow 1RM attempts; novices cannot
- For strength: control the eccentric (to get in position), then attempt to **accelerate the
  concentric as fast as possible** (even if bar moves slowly due to weight)

### Eccentric Muscle Actions
- Eccentric = lowering phase; muscle lengthens while producing tension (not a contraction in traditional sense)
- Eccentric is passive and uses less energy than concentric; you are 20–40% stronger eccentrically
- **Why eccentrics are effective in lab**: they allow heavier loading → more tension + more volume
  (not because of slow speed per se)
- In the real gym: max load is determined by concentric strength (the weakest link)
  → slowing the eccentric doesn't give you access to heavier loads → benefit of eccentric training
  doesn't transfer via slow tempo alone

### Time Under Tension — The Missing Piece
- "Time under tension" ignores magnitude of tension
- **Impulse** = Force × Time (area under the force-time curve) — the more complete metric
- Study (Mohamad et al., 2012): 3×12 at 70% 1RM vs. 6×12 at 35% 1RM (equal volume, both maximal intent)
  - 35% group: longer time under tension, higher velocity, higher power
  - 70% group: **~25% greater total impulse** despite fewer reps and shorter TUT
  - Greater impulse = greater stimulus for hypertrophy and strength

### Slow Tempo Reduces Volume
- Study (Headley et al., 2011): AMRAPs with 75% 1RM at 2/0/2 tempo vs. 4/0/2 tempo
  - 4-sec eccentric → reduced 1RM (fatigue from slow lowering before concentric)
  - 4-sec eccentric → 1.5 fewer reps in AMRAPs AND ~10% less total work performed
  - Slow tempo forced lower loads → less total volume
- Pattern mirrors short rest periods: focusing on the minor thing (slow tempo/short rest)
  at the expense of the major things (volume, load, tension)

### The Evidence Against Slow Training
- Meta-analysis (Schoenfeld et al., 2015): repetition duration has NO effect on hypertrophy
  until reps last ≥10 seconds; at that point, negative effect on muscle growth
- Gonzalez-Badillo et al. (2014): maximal intent to accelerate concentric → **~2× strength gains**
  vs. deliberate half-speed concentric on bench press
- Nearly all traditional vs. slow-speed resistance training studies favor traditional speed [refs 7–11]

### Practical Tempo Guidelines
1. **Don't intentionally slow the tempo** (unless novice learning form)
2. **Control the eccentric** — don't let gravity drop the weight; you are actively producing tension
3. **Forceful, intentional concentric** — try to accelerate the load (F = ma)
4. For strength: controlled eccentric gets you into position; slow eccentric may be warranted
   (e.g., bench press: slower lowering → easier press command from referee)
5. **Powerlifters**: deadlift — okay to let bar descend faster on eccentric (some don't do true
   eccentric at all in training to reduce volume)

### Exceptions to Rule
- Slow, heavy resistance (2–4 sec concentric + eccentric, 60–85% 1RM) can help with
  **tendon pain and rehab** [Lim & Wong, 2018] — but Helms notes this is outside scope of practice

### Supramaximal Eccentric Training
- Using loads >1RM on eccentric (e.g., concentric with 2 limbs, eccentric with 1 limb on machine)
- Theory: overload stimulus for hypertrophy + strength (handling >concentric 1RM)
- Meta-analysis (Schoenfeld et al., 2017): 15 studies — no significant difference vs. traditional training
  for hypertrophy (nonsignificant trend toward slight benefit)
- Meta-analysis (Buskard et al., 2018): 5 studies — no significant difference for strength either
- **Verdict**: not recommended for most lifters; difficult to implement, hard to track, increases
  soreness, potentially dangerous with free weights

### Tempo Summary
| Goal | Eccentric | Concentric |
|------|-----------|------------|
| Hypertrophy | Controlled (not dropped) | Maximal intent to accelerate |
| Strength | Controlled (for positioning) | Maximal intent to accelerate |
| Rehab/tendon pain | 2–4 sec deliberate | 2–4 sec deliberate |
| Exception | Powerlifter deadlift (may drop) | Maximum effort |

### Key References (Tempo)
- Mohamad et al. (2012) — impulse study; 70% vs. 35% 1RM volume-equated
- Headley et al. (2011) — slow tempo reduces work performed by ~10%
- Schoenfeld et al. (2015) — rep duration meta-analysis; ≥10s reps negative
- Gonzalez-Badillo et al. (2014) — maximal intent = 2× strength gains vs. half-speed
- Lim & Wong (2018) — slow tempo for tendon pain (rehab context)

## QUICK START GUIDE TO PROGRAM BUILDING

### Step 1: Adherence — Choose Training Days
- 2–6 days/week; most people: **3–5 days/week, 2–4× frequency per muscle/movement**
- Novice: can make 2 days work; after novice phase, minimum 3 days/week
- More frequency → shorter sessions per day; less frequency → longer sessions
- **Splits available by days/week**:
  - 2 days: Full Body only
  - 3 days: Full Body ×3, or Lower/Upper/Full
  - 4 days: Upper/Lower ×2, or Legs/Push/Pull/Full
  - 5 days: Legs/Push/Pull + 2 Full Body (or variations)
  - 6 days: Legs/Push/Pull ×2, or Full Body ×6

### Step 2: Volume, Intensity, Frequency
- **Volume**: 10–20 sets/muscle group/week
  - Novice: 10–12 sets/week, 2–3× frequency
  - Intermediate: 13–15 sets/week, 3–4× frequency
  - Advanced: 16–20 sets/week, 4–5× frequency
- **Intensity by exercise type (hypertrophy)**:
  - Lower free weight compound (squat, DL, RDL): 3–10 reps, RPE 6–9
  - Lower machine compound (leg press, hack squat): 6–12 reps, RPE 6–9
  - Lower isolation (calf, leg ext, leg curl): 8–20 reps, RPE 7–10
  - Upper free weight compound (OHP, bench, BB row): 3–10 reps, RPE 6–9
  - Upper machine compound (lat pulldown, cable row): 6–15 reps, RPE 6–10
  - Upper isolation (lateral raise, pushdown, etc.): 8–20 reps, RPE 5–10
- **Intensity by exercise type (strength)**:
  - Main lift strength (1–5 reps): RPE 7–10
  - Main lift volume (4–8 reps): RPE 5–8
  - Main lift technique/power (1–3 reps): RPE 4–7
  - Compound accessory (OHP, good morning, BB row): 4–8 reps, RPE ~5
  - Machine/isolation accessory: 8–15 reps, RPE 5–10
- **Frequency**: minimum 2×/week per muscle group or movement pattern

### Step 3: Progression
- **Novice (compounds)**: linear load increases session to session (same sets/reps, add weight)
  - Squat/DL: +10 lb (~5 kg) per week; Bench/other upper: +5 lb (~2.5 kg) per week
- **Intermediate (compounds)**: wave loading (linear periodized): 
  - 3-week waves; reps decrease 1/week while load increases
  - Squat/DL/HH/LP: +10 lb per wave cycle; all other: +5 lb per wave cycle
- **Intermediate/Advanced (isolation)**: double progression
  - Add reps within range until top of range achieved for all sets, then increase load and repeat
- **Advanced (compounds)**: block periodized + wave loading within blocks
  - Accumulation block (W1–6): sets increase 15→20/week, reps 10–20 (6–12 for free weight lower body), RPE 6–8
  - Intensification block (W7–10): sets stay at 14, reps wave 12→6 (10→4 for lower body), RPE 8–10
  - Taper + test or rinse and repeat

### Step 4: Exercise Selection
- **Volume counting**: primary trained muscles = full credit; secondary/indirect = full credit
  (but don't rely entirely on indirect volume)
- **Movement patterns and muscles trained**:
  - Squat variants: Quads (primary), Glutes, Erectors (if free weight)
  - Hip hinge (DL, RDL, good morning, back extension): Glutes, Hams, Erectors (primary),
    Scapular retractors
  - Vertical pull (chin/pulldown): Lats, Bis (primary), Rear delts
  - Vertical push (OHP): Anterior delts, Tris (primary), Middle delts
  - Horizontal pull (row): Lats, Scapular retractors (primary), Rear delts, Bis, Middle delts
  - Horizontal push (bench/press): Chest, Anterior delts (primary), Tris (CG/dips), Middle delts (incline)
  - Horizontal hip extension (hip thrust/glute bridge): Glutes, Hams (primary)
  - Pullover (DB/cable/BB): Lats, Tris, Chest
  - Fly (cable/DB): Chest (primary), Anterior delts
  - Isolation: target muscle only

- **Powerlifter categories**:
  - Upper body push: bench + variants, horizontal/vertical push, triceps
  - Upper body pull: deadlift + variants, horizontal/vertical pull
  - Lower body: squat + variants, deadlift, single-leg work

### Step 5: Rest Periods + Step 6: Tempo
- Set up APS for upper body push/pull pairs; also for leg extension/curl and bicep/tricep
- Time-pressed: use drop sets or rest-pause on accessories
- Control eccentric; forceful concentric; rest as needed for performance

### Autoregulation Strategies
- **Flexible training days**: works best with 2–3 sessions/week (more off days than training days)
- **Flexible off days**: works best with 4+ sessions/week (fewer off days than training days)
- **Load**: use %1RM as starting point; adjust based on RPE for all subsequent sets
  - Novices: track RPE without using it to adjust load for first few months
- **Deload assessment** (after each mesocycle):
  - Dreading gym? Sleep suffering? Aches/pains? Performance declining? Mood changes?
  - 0–1 "yes": start next meso
  - 2+ "yes": take deload week
  - Only aches/pains: high-rep week (possibly with BFR)
  - Mandatory deload every 3rd mesocycle regardless of checklist score

### Training Adjustments When Cutting
1. Step down one volume category (e.g., advanced → intermediate volume)
   — do this ~first third into the diet; only if aggressive/prolonged cut
2. Switch from deload-by-checklist to automatic deload after every mesocycle
3. Use all autoregulation strategies (load, exercise, days off)

## SAMPLE PROGRAMS: OVERVIEW

### Warming Up
- **Recommended warm-up structure**: submaximal aerobic (optional) → general dynamic movement prep →
  sport-specific warm-up sets
- **Static stretching before training**: can acutely reduce muscular performance; data on injury
  prevention is mixed
  - Short duration (<60 sec, not to point of discomfort): no performance decrease; minimal flexibility gain
  - Foam rolling (self-myofascial release): increases ROM without decreasing force production
  - Dynamic warm-up after static stretching negates performance decrement
- **When static stretch IS appropriate**: for muscles not being trained that day (e.g., pec/delt stretch
  before low-bar squats); or to address specific mobility limitation for that day's exercise
- **Sample warm-up**:
  - 10 leg swings front-to-back, 10 side-to-side, 10 arm circles forward/backward, 10 cross-body arm slaps, 10 walking lunges with torso rotation
  - Warm-up sets: 50% → 60% → 70% → 90% × 2 working weight (reps decrease as weight increases)

### Program Design Philosophy
- These sample programs are primarily **learning tools**, not guaranteed-optimal programs
- Individuality is key; jumping into any pre-written program cold is not ideal
- Read only the section relevant to your experience/goal

## NOVICE POWERLIFTING PROGRAM

### Structure
- **3-day or 4-day option**
- 3-day: Day 1 = hypertrophy (higher rep, moderate load); Day 2 = power/strength (low rep, heavy DL);
  Day 3 = strength (low rep, heavy squat + bench)
- 4-day: same main lift structure on Days 1–3; Day 4 = dedicated accessory day
- Daily undulating model within the week (hypertrophy → power → strength days)
- Simple single-progression model: increase load session to session (no complex periodization needed)

### Volume
- Lower end of guidelines: 10–12 sets/muscle group/week
- ~½ volume from competition lifts; ~½ from accessories
- ~⅓ volume as heavy work; ~⅔ lighter work
- (DL volume counted toward both lower body and upper body pull)

### Loading
- Main lifts: %1RM (establish via 3–5RM test → 1RM calculator)
- Accessories: use RPE from start
- Always track RPE on main lifts as a learning tool (even when using %1RM)
- Target RPE on all novice sets: **"First Set RPE 8"** (should complete all sets without missing reps)

### Novice Progression Rules
- Squat/DL: +10 lb (~5 kg)/week; Bench/other: +5 lb (~2.5 kg)/week; accessories: smallest available increment
- If miss reps one week: repeat same weights next week
- Miss two weeks in a row: **decrease load 10%** the next week; return to stalled weight the week after
- If stall again: halve the load increase increment (squats/DL → +5 lb; bench → +2 lb microload or every other session)
- If stalling on majority of lifts even with smaller increments: graduate to intermediate
- Do NOT train to absolute failure on compounds (squats, DL, bench, OHP, front squat, RDL);
  stop at technical failure

## NOVICE BODYBUILDING PROGRAM

### Structure
- **4-day only** (no 3-day option); lower/upper split
- Days 1/3 = lower body; Days 2/4 = upper body
- Day 1/2 = strength focus (heavier, lower reps); Day 3/4 = hypertrophy focus (moderate, higher reps)
- APS available on Days 2, 3, 4 for: horizontal/vertical push+pull pairs; triceps+biceps; leg curl+extension

### Volume
- 10–12 sets/muscle group/week (calves/hams slightly below; glutes slightly above due to compound overlap)
- ~65% moderate loads; ~35% split between heavy and light

### Progression
- Same linear load increase rules as Novice Powerlifting

## INTERMEDIATE POWERLIFTING PROGRAM

### Structure
- **4-day program** (S / B / S+B / D+B or similar split)
- Day 1: squat variation (upright variant if low-bar athlete) + bench — moderate volume, higher reps
- Day 2: heavy technique work (competition lifts, RPE 5 — 4–6 reps shy of failure)
- Day 3: strength work — squat + bench (RPE 8 on strength sets)
- Day 4: strength work — deadlift + bench variation (ideally rest day between Day 3 and 4)
- Daily undulating model continues; wave loading applied week-to-week

### Volume
- 13–15 sets/week (upper push, upper pull, lower body)
- ~70% heavy loading; ~30% lighter
- ~50% competition lifts; ~50% accessories
- Bench variant on Day 4: close-grip if wide-grip competitor (or weak-point specific variant)

### Loading/Progression
- Wave loading: reps decrease 1/week as load increases over 3-week cycle
  - Example: 9 reps @ 67.5% → 8 reps @ 70% → 7 reps @ 72.5% (all at RPE 7 first set)
- Main lifts + variants: RPE 7 on Day 1; RPE 5 on Day 2 (technique); RPE 8 on Days 3/4
- Accessories: first set RPE 8
- Squat/DL: +10 lb per 3-week cycle; all other: +5 lb per cycle
- After 3-week cycle: assess deload checklist; if needed, deload Week 4
  - Deload: lowest reps + loads from previous 3 weeks, at 2/3 the sets (3–4 sets → 2; 5–6 → 3)
- Next cycle starts +10 lb (or +5 lb) heavier; repeat pattern
- Can make accumulation or intensification version by adjusting rep ranges (e.g., 4–6 @ 80–85% for more volume;
  2–4 @ 85–90% for more intensity)

### Competition Prep (Intermediate)
- 4 weeks out: change main lift reps on strength days from 3–5 to 1–3
- Week 3 (1 week before comp): deload accessories; swap bench variant with comp bench on Day 4;
  Day 4 becomes squat + bench + deadlift for singles (work to opener)
- Week of comp: deload everything; Day 3 = 2 singles @ RPE 4 on squat/bench, 1 single @ RPE 4 on DL;
  perform Day 3 24–48 hours before meet; Day 4 = competition

## ADVANCED POWERLIFTING PROGRAM

### Structure
- **6-day program**: daily undulating model across the week
- Block periodized: distinct **Accumulation** and **Intensification** mesocycles (each 3 weeks)
- Accumulation: more accessories, higher reps, lower intensities; 50% competition lifts / 50% accessories;
  ~56% heavy reps / ~44% lighter
- Intensification: fewer accessories, lower reps, higher loads; 75% competition lifts / 25% accessories;
  ~80% heavy reps / ~20% lighter
- Overall advanced program lands in middle of Level 2 VIF guidelines
- Linear wave loading within blocks; specificity increases across full macrocycle

### Loading (Advanced)
- RPE **ranges** (not single "first set RPE") — e.g., RPE 6.5–8.5 for accumulation work
- %1RM used as starting point; first set RPE adjusts subsequent sets up or down
- Two purposes: (1) individualized load; (2) track strength gains without formal testing

### Advanced Testing/Competition Protocols
- **AMRAP testing after accumulation**: modified deload → AMRAP at 85% or new 4–6RM
- **1RM testing after intensification**: modified deload → mock meet (3 attempts per competition lift)
- **Competition peak**: accumulation → intensification → modified deload → opener practice (87.5–92.5% of 1RM,
  RPE 7.5–8.5, 1 week out) → competition taper

## INTERMEDIATE BODYBUILDING PROGRAM

### Structure
- **5-day program**: Days 1–2 = Lower + Upper (strength focus); Days 3–5 = Legs/Push/Pull
- Frequency: 2×/week per muscle group (same as novice)
- 5 days allows more total volume without crushing individual sessions
- Wave loading for compounds; double progression for isolations
- ~65% moderate loads / ~35% heavy or light (same as novice but more total volume)
- Volume lands in 10–20 sets/week range; arms appear high due to indirect work from compounds

---

## ADVANCED BODYBUILDING PROGRAM

### Structure
- **6-day program**: alternating Lower/Upper ×3 each week
- Daily undulating within pairs: Days 1+2 = higher reps/volume/RPE; Days 3+4 = lower volume/RPE;
  Days 5+6 = strength focus
- Block periodized (Accumulation + Intensification), similar to advanced powerlifting
- Differences: same exercise selection in both blocks; only rep ranges and loads change
- Accumulation → more moderate-intensity reps; Intensification → more heavy loading
- Sets remain same in both blocks (unlike quick start guide example which shows sets changing)
- **Testing week** after Intensification Week 3 (optional; every 8–16 weeks)

## SAMPLE PROGRAM NOTES: ACCESSORY EXERCISE GUIDANCE

### Vertical/Horizontal Pulls
- Horizontal row: choose cable/single-arm DB/chest-supported/seal row — avoid lumbar fatigue
- Vertical pull: chin-ups/pull-ups if able; else lat pulldown; band-assisted if not strong enough
- Heavy lower-rep pull: use free weight/bodyweight option (Pendlay row, seal row, weighted chins)

### Squat Variants
- Any barbell free weight squat (high-bar, low-bar, front, Zercher, safety bar, pause, accommodating)
- Choose: pain-free, low injury risk, suits biomechanics
- Long femur/high forward lean → prefer front/high-bar/safety bar for more upright position
- If barbell squat impossible: leg press variant acceptable

### Hip Hinge Variants
- Glute ham raises, weighted back extensions, reverse hypers, barbell hip thrusts, cable pull-through
- Preferred for reducing lumbar fatigue relative to deadlifts
- Deadlift variant can substitute (higher lumbar fatigue risk)

### Deadlift Variants (Bodybuilding Programs)
- Conventional, sumo, Romanian, good morning, partial ROM, or accommodating resistance
- Sumo: not ultra-wide for bodybuilders — moderately wide with upright torso
- Higher reps (6+): RDL or good morning recommended (controlled eccentric built in)

### Bench Press Variants (Powerlifting Programs)
- Close-grip (not extremely close — no narrower than push-up grip width), board press, flat-back,
  feet-up, 2-count pause, accommodating resistance
- Close-grip if wide-grip competitor; or weak-point specific variant

### Single-Leg Squat Variants
- Primarily to ensure equal development + coordination between legs (injury prevention)
- Preferred: Bulgarian split squat, lunges, step-ups, single-leg squat (bodyweight or loaded)
- Machine option (single-leg press): ensures equal force production but less coordination benefit

### Grip Work (Advanced Powerlifting Only)
- 3 sets of timed holds; 10–20 sec with 90–110% of max (barbell from rack near lockout)
  OR single-arm bodyweight hang for time (good choice when spine is compressed/fatigued)
- Powerlifter needs static grip strength specifically (squeezing won't transfer well)

### No Direct Ab Work or Shrugs in Bodybuilding Programs
- Reasoning: when program includes free-weight rows, squats, deadlifts, OHP — abs and upper traps
  are adequately stimulated; adding direct work shows no reliable improvement in experienced lifters
- Exception: if abs or traps are genuinely lagging, add 2–3 sets/week direct work

### Substitutions
- Acceptable if: same movement pattern, same muscle groups, rational basis
- Important to preserve program's designed balance (compounds count for indirect overlap)

### Dual Athletes ("Powerbuilders")
- **Bodybuilder competing in powerlifting**: run bodybuilding programs; select competition lifts
  wherever variants are prescribed; use slightly lower rep/higher load final mesocycle before meet;
  competition taper same as intermediate/advanced powerlifting
- **Powerlifter competing in bodybuilding**: run powerlifting programs; add 2–3 sets × 8–15 reps
  for triceps, biceps, calves isolations; add hamstring curls (short head of bicep femoris only
  crosses knee — understimuated by squats/DLs); add leg extensions (rectus femoris)
- **True powerbuilder**: when offseason from BB, follow powerlifter-who-competes-in-BB approach;
  3–6 months out from BB prep, switch to BB-who-competes-in-PL approach
- **Novices should not compete** — wait until intermediate level
