# The Rise and Fall of the Great Powers — Compacted Notes (Chapters 1–6)

## Introduction: The Core Argument

- Book traces how Great Powers have risen and fallen *relative to each other* over five centuries
- The triumph or collapse of any Great Power results from **three interacting factors**: (1) lengthy fighting by armed forces, (2) efficient wartime use of productive economic resources, (3) the state's economy rising or falling *relative to rivals in the decades preceding war*
- **Peacetime economic trajectory matters as much as wartime military performance**
- Circular relationship: wealth underwrites military power; military power acquires and protects wealth — but if too large a share goes to military, national power weakens long-term
- **Strategic overextension** — conquering too much or waging too many wars — risks outweighing the benefits of expansion, especially during economic decline
- **Lag time**: an expanding power prefers growth over armaments; decades later it has accumulated overseas obligations and faces rising rivals — defense spending rises even as relative position weakens
- Great Powers in relative decline instinctively spend more on security, diverting resources from investment — compounding the long-term dilemma (Spain 1630s, Britain 1910, US/USSR 1980s)
- Coalition wars are won by the side with more productive resources — true across every major conflict since 1500

> "whether a nation be today mighty and rich or not depends not on the abundance or security of its power and riches, but principally on whether its neighbors possess more or less of it." — von Hornigk

- **Relative, not absolute**: Netherlands in mid-18th century was richer than a century earlier, yet much less of a Great Power because France and Britain had grown more

## Chapter 1: The Rise of the Western World

### Ming China

- Most advanced civilization of premodern times; population 100-130 million vs. Europe's 50-55 million
- Technological precocity: movable type, paper money, magnetic compass, gunpowder, iron output of ~125,000 tons/year in the 11th century — far larger than British output seven centuries later
- 1420 Ming navy: 1,350 combat vessels including 400 floating fortresses; **Admiral Cheng Ho** led seven expeditions (1405-1433) with treasure ships ~400 feet long
- **1433**: last expedition; **1436**: imperial edict banned seagoing ships; later banned ships with more than two masts
- Confucian bureaucracy distrusted merchants, confiscated property, restricted printing, let canals decay, abandoned ironworks
- In 1736 — just as **Abraham Darby's** ironworks at Coalbrookdale were beginning to boom — the blast furnaces of Honan were abandoned entirely

### The Muslim World

- **Ottoman Empire**: finest siege train of the age; by 1500s domains stretched from Crimea to the Levant; **Suleiman** besieging Vienna by 1529
- After 1566: thirteen incompetent sultans in succession; centralized, despotic, hostile to initiative and commerce
- "An idiot sultan could paralyze the Ottoman Empire in the way that a pope or Holy Roman Emperor could never do for all Europe"
- Printing press forbidden; guilds checked innovation; in 1580 janissaries destroyed a state observatory alleging it caused plague
- **Mughal Empire**: Muslim elite atop vast poverty-stricken Hindu majority; taxation known as "eating" — population received nothing in return

### Two Outsiders — Japan and Russia

- **Tokugawa Japan**: 1636 banned oceangoing vessels; Japanese forbidden to sail; trade restricted to one Dutch ship at Deshima; Christians murdered; merchants never permitted social/political prominence — military ossified until **Perry's** "black ships" (1853)
- **Russia**: gunpowder expansion enabled "empire" growth to Pacific by 1638; but foreigners segregated, serfdom enforced, social modernization firmly blocked; unlike other empires, would eventually become a world power

### The "European Miracle"

- Why did scattered, relatively unsophisticated peoples of western Eurasia generate unstoppable development? Kennedy's answer: **political fragmentation** from geography
- No enormous plains for horsemen; no great river valleys creating conquerable peasants — decentralized power emerged naturally
- Differentiated climate → differentiated products → **bulk trade** → banking → predictability of economic conditions
- No uniform authority could suppress commercial development; persecution of merchants backfired — they moved and took expertise elsewhere
- Arms race dynamic: political decentralization → primitive arms race → constant weapons improvement; in China, Japan, Mughal India, once cannon monopoly established authority, incentive to improve vanished
- Counter-fortification (raised earthworks absorbing cannonballs) prevented any single European power from achieving decisive conquest
- Key innovation: **siting cannon on sailing vessels** — the gunned sailing ship as the decisive maritime instrument

## Chapter 2: The Habsburg Bid for Mastery, 1519-1659

### The Meaning and Chronology of the Struggle

- Two root causes of intensified warfare after 1500: (1) the **Reformation** fracturing Christendom, (2) the **Habsburg dynastic combination** — territories from Gibraltar to Hungary
- **Charles V** inherited all four dynastic streams simultaneously: Duke of Burgundy at 15, King of Spain 1516, Holy Roman Emperor 1519
- Three great phases: Italian Wars (to 1559), Netherlands Revolt and Armada (1560s-1609), Thirty Years War (1618-1648)
- **Spanish Armada** (1588): failed; cost 10 million ducats; two later attempts also failed
- **Thirty Years War**: Bohemian revolt lit the fuse; **Gustavus Adolphus** of Sweden invaded; Olivares planned triple invasion of France: "Either all is lost, or else Castile will be head of the world"
- **Treaty of Westphalia** (1648) + **Treaty of the Pyrenees** (1659): Habsburg predominance ended; European plurality confirmed

### Strengths and Weaknesses of the Habsburg Bloc

- Five revenue sources: Castile (most reliable), Italian states, Low Countries, American empire, banking houses
- Greatest military asset: the **Spanish tercio** — most effective infantry in Europe from early 16th century through mid-Thirty Years War
- Three factors in Habsburg failure:
  1. **Military revolution**: army sizes exploded from 30,000 (pre-1529) to 300,000 (1625); costs spiraled; Philip II defaulted repeatedly; at death debts = 100 million ducats
  2. **Strategic overextension**: too many enemies, too many fronts; "Spain resembled a large bear in the pit: more powerful than any of the dogs attacking it, but never able to deal with all opponents"
  3. **Failure to mobilize resources**: congeries of territories each with own privileges; Castile as "milch cow" — entire fiscal system rested on ~6 million Castilian peasants
- Between 1566-1654, Spain sent **218 million ducats** to the Netherlands — more than total crown receipts from the Indies (121 million)

> "The flood of precious metals from the Indies was to Spain as water on a roof — it poured on and then was drained away."

### International Comparisons

- **France**: latent advantage of 16 million population (2x Spain, 4x England); **Sully** restored solvency; but Richelieu's war doubled the taille; France and Spain were "punch-drunk boxers" by 1650s
- **England**: Elizabeth recognized limits; maintained independence by diplomacy and "cold war" at sea; never declared bankruptcy — left James I in debt but solvent
- **Sweden**: transformed by foreign entrepreneurs (**Louis de Geer**) and **Gustavus Adolphus**; army was parasitic — financed by "contributions" from German states, unsustainable from Sweden's own base
- **Dutch Republic**: tiny cluster of provinces became a Great Power through commercial-financial strength; could borrow more cheaply than any rival because it **reliably repaid debts**; Holland's war costs: 960,000 florins (1579) → 10 million annually by early 17th century

### War, Money, and the Nation-State

- War was the most urgent driver of nation-state formation; up to **three-quarters of government expenditure** went to war or debt repayment
- **Army of Flanders mutinied 46 times** between 1572 and 1607 — lack of pay as dangerous as enemy action
- The anti-Habsburg coalition succeeded by slightly better balancing material base against military power, recognizing that "the trader and the manufacturer and the farmer were as important as the cavalry officer and the pikeman"

## Chapter 3: Finance, Geography, and the Winning of Wars, 1660-1815

### The "Financial Revolution"

- Seven major Anglo-French wars (1689-1815) were wars of endurance — victory went to the coalition with greatest capacity to sustain credit
- Cost escalated from millions to tens of millions to occasionally £100 million a year
- **Dutch model**: merchants who were part of government demanded financial rectitude; Amsterdam became world's financial capital
- **Britain vs. France**: Britain held decisive financial advantages despite France's larger population:
  - Taxes mostly "invisible" (excise, customs); no internal tolls; authorized by elected Parliament
  - **Bank of England** (1694) enabled long-term loans with guaranteed interest
  - British government bonds increasingly attractive to Dutch investors

> "Credit was the principal advantage that England hath over France." — Bishop Berkeley

- **British Wartime Expenditure 1688-1815**: Total £1,657.9M in the Napoleonic Wars alone, of which £440.3M raised on money markets
- **France's structural failures**: no proper system of public finance; cluster of middlemen each taking a cut; every attempt to create a national bank was resisted
- By late 1780s: France's national debt similar to Britain's but annual interest payments nearly **double** — link between national bankruptcy and the French Revolution was direct

### Geopolitics

- Geography means strategic location: could a power concentrate on one front or fight on several? Was it a land power, sea power, or hybrid?
- **Dutch geographic trap**: forced to expand army against Louis XIV → drained resources from navy and commerce; 75% of defense spending went to army, leaving maritime campaigns to Britain
- **France as hybrid**: energies divided between continental and maritime ambitions — "fell between stools" in every war except 1778-1783
- **Britain's Janus-faced strategy**: one face toward the Continent to balance power, one face toward the sea to extend maritime dominance; subsidized continental allies while using Navy to control trade

### The Winning of Wars, 1660-1763

- **Battle of Poltava** (1709): Russian army wiped out Sweden's force; Sweden fell to second-order power
- **Seven Years War**: three decisive Anglo-Prussian advantages: leadership (Frederick + Pitt), financial staying power, military/naval expertise
- British overseas trade *increased every year* of the Seven Years War — commerce funded the war in real time
- Britain spent over £160 million, of which £60 million raised on money markets

> "In the present state of Europe it is colonies, trade and in consequence sea power, which must determine the balance of power upon the continent." — Choiseul

### The Winning of Wars, 1763-1815

- Napoleonic Wars: Britain supreme at sea, France supreme on land — neither could deliver a knockout blow
- **Continental System** never applied consistently enough; British total exports rose throughout the war
- Britain's **Industrial Revolution** as strategic asset: pig-iron output 68,000 tons (1788) → 325,000 tons (1811); customs/excise revenue tripled
- Napoleon's war finance was systematic **plunder**: Prussia paid 311M francs after Jena; "War would support war"

> "My power depends on my glory and my glories on the victories I have won. Conquest has made me what I am and only conquest can enable me to hold my position." — Napoleon

- **Russian campaign**: 600,000+ men, ~270,000 killed, 200,000 captured; **Battle of Leipzig** (1813): 195,000 French overwhelmed by 365,000 allied troops bankrolled by British subsidies
- Post-1815: rough parity on land among five Great Powers, but Britain held near-monopoly of naval power, reinforced by economic lead

## Chapter 4: Industrialization and the Shifting Global Balances, 1815-1885

### The Eclipse of the Non-European World

- Industrial Revolution: substitution of inanimate for animate sources of power; power-driven looms produced 20x the output of a hand worker
- **Relative Shares of World Manufacturing Output**: Third World fell from 73.0% (1750) to 11.0% (1900); China from 32.8% to 6.2%; India from 24.5% to 1.7%
- India suffered absolute deindustrialization: cotton fabric imports 1M yards (1814) → 995M yards (1870)
- Europeans occupied/controlled 35% of world's land surface in 1800 → 84%+ by 1914
- **Battle of Omdurman** (1898): Maxims destroyed 11,000 Dervishes at cost of 48 British troops

### Britain as Hegemon?

- At zenith (~1860): UK produced 53% of world's iron, 50% of coal; industrial capacity = 40-45% of world potential
- With 2% of world population: responsible for 1/5 of world's commerce but 2/5 of trade in manufactured goods
- Defense spending only 2-3% of GNP for 50 years after 1815 — least mobilized for war since early Stuarts
- Three realms of strength: naval supremacy, colonial empire expanding in a power-political vacuum, financial hegemony (capital exports £75M/year by 1870-1875)
- Hidden dangers: Britain's capital exports were financing the very industries that would allow rivals to overtake it; increasing dependence on international trade made it vulnerable in wartime

### The "Middle Powers"

- **Prussia**: least of the Great Powers post-1815; humiliated at Oelmuetz (1850); but best educational system, formidable general staff studying "railways and rifles"
- **Habsburg Empire**: central fulcrum of 1815 balance; extraordinary staying power but ethnic incoherence — army mobilization order in 15 different languages; military budget cut drastically one year before war with Prussia
- **France**: constrained middle power; population growth slower than any Great Power throughout 19th century; classic hybrid torn between European and non-European interests

### The Crimean War and the Erosion of Russian Power

- Russia's post-1815 peak: 800,000-man army, gendarme of Europe — but per capita GNP falling relative to Britain
- Crimean War exposed: flintlock muskets with 200-yard range vs. Allied rifles at 1,000 yards; no railways south of Moscow — supplies took 3 months vs. 3 weeks by sea for Allies
- Crown Council warned (Jan 1856): if war continued, state would go **bankrupt** — forced negotiated peace

### The United States and the Civil War

- By April 1861, US had surpassed Germany and Russia in manufacturing output; iron 830,000 tons vs. Russia's 350,000; railway mileage 30x greater
- North won through resources: 110,000 manufacturing establishments vs. South's 18,000; Pennsylvania pig iron alone 580,000 tons vs. Confederacy's 36,700
- Confederate financial collapse via inflation; Northern output surged — the "American way of war" (mobilizing massive industrial-technological potential) was forged here

### The Wars of German Unification

- Prussian military revolution: short-service system, **Moltke's** General Staff, learning culture
- **Franco-Prussian War** (1870-71): German states combined had larger population than France, more railway miles, coal 2.5x France's; **Krupp** steel symbolized industrial-military integration
- "Europe had lost a mistress and gained a master" — Germany became a "half-hegemonic power"

## Chapter 5: The Coming of a Bipolar World, Part One, 1885-1918

### The Shifting Balance of World Forces

- By 1895-1900, observers sensed US and Russia would be future world powers; Joseph Chamberlain: "the tendency of the time is to throw all power into the hands of the greater empires"
- UK share of world manufacturing: **22.9%** (1880) → **13.6%** (1913); US surged from 14.7% to 23.6%

### The Position of the Powers, 1885-1914

- **Italy**: illusion of Great Power status; 37.6% illiteracy rate; no coal; world manufacturing share actually *dropped*; military disasters at Adowa (1896) and Caporetto (1917)
- **Japan**: Meiji Restoration transformed a feudal society; national slogan *fukoku kyohei* ("rich country, strong army"); Russo-Japanese War victory (1904-05) but near bankruptcy by war's end
- **Germany**: population 49M→66M; steel 17.6M tons (more than Britain, France, Russia *combined*); Siemens and AEG dominated European electrical industry; Bayer/Hoechst produced 90% of world's industrial dyes — but "born encircled," growth threatened multiple Great Powers simultaneously
- **Austria-Hungary**: world manufacturing share only 4.5%; mobilization order in 15 languages; "trying to act the part of a great power with the resources of a second-rank one"
- **France**: industrial potential only 40% of Germany's by 1914; population gap decisive; but rebuilt position diplomatically through alliances with Russia and Britain
- **Britain**: "the weary Titan staggering under the too vast orb of its fate"; UK world manufacturing share fell from 22.9% to 13.6%; strategic overextension across every continent simultaneously
- **Russia**: fourth-largest industrial power but per capita industrialization less than 1/4 of Germany's; 80% of population in agriculture; Stolypin: "Give the state twenty years of peace and you will not recognize Russia" — but the war came too early
- **United States**: national income $37 billion (vs. Britain $11B, Germany $12B); coal, pig-iron, steel, energy consumption dwarfing all rivals; but spending only 1% of GNP on defense, smaller army than Serbia

### Alliances and the Drift to War, 1890-1914

- Alliance "rigidification": fixed military alliances in peacetime, rarely seen before Bismarck
- Franco-Russian Dual Alliance (1894) matched Triple Alliance; Anglo-Japanese Alliance (1902); Entente Cordiale (1904); Anglo-Russian entente (1907)
- War plans like "dominoes" — coalition structure virtually guaranteed one local conflict would cascade
- Both at sea and on land, a swift victory was technically impossible — but all general staffs assumed speed was of the essence

### Total War and the Power Balances, 1914-1918

- Allied superiority was "significant rather than overwhelming"; Franco-Russian alone against Central Powers in a long war: hard to see how they could have won
- Germany's advantages: inner lines, defensible western positions, intelligent staff rethinking warfare faster than anyone
- **Russia's collapse**: isolated, could not secure meaningful aid from allies; czarist fiscal self-destruction (abolished spirits trade losing 1/3 of revenue, printed money); casualties by end of 1916: 3.6 million dead/wounded + 2.1 million captured
- **Britain as banker**: $4.3 billion in inter-Allied credits by April 1917; but increasingly dependent on American markets
- **US entry** (April 1917): military significance minimal for 12-15 months but productive strength unmatched — 2.5x Germany's industrial output
- Total Allies: $57.7B expenditure / 40.7 million mobilized vs. Central Powers: $24.7B / 25.1 million
- Germany's advantages could not over the long run outweigh the Allies' massive superiority in economic muscle

## Chapter 6: The Coming of a Bipolar World, Part Two, 1919-1942

### The Postwar International Order

- WWI cost ~8 million combat dead, 7 million permanently disabled; total casualties ~60 million; material cost ~$260 billion
- The three rising powers all withdrew: Germany defeated, Russia collapsed into Bolshevik isolation, USA retreated into isolationism — leaving 1920s affairs focused on weakened France and Britain
- France's "search for security": Little Entente, alliances with Poland/Czechoslovakia/Romania; but 1919 settlement could not create ethnically coherent states
- Financial chaos: interlocking debts; no European country could return to gold standard; real center of world finance migrated to the US but America was structurally ill-suited for that role
- Wall Street Crash triggered chain reaction: by summer 1932 industrial production in many countries half of 1928; world trade shrunk by one-third
- International system fragmented into rival currency blocs; democracies in no frame of mind to cooperate against territorial revisionism

### The Challengers

- **Italy under Mussolini**: diplomatic prominence masked economic weakness; only 2.8% of world manufacturing by 1938; structurally dependent on imported coal, oil, rubber — 80% passing through Gibraltar or Suez; main battle tank only 3.5 tons; air force claimed 8,500 planes but only 454 bombers and 129 fighters of first-rate quality
- **Japan**: manufacturing production index 176 (1920) → 552 (1938) vs. world average 93→183; but near-total reliance on petroleum imports; finance minister **Takahashi** assassinated by militarists when he sought to moderate military spending; attacking the US seemed folly yet not striking meant economic strangulation
- **Germany**: by 1938, 52% of government expenditure and 17% of GNP poured into armaments — more than Britain, France, and US *combined*; but held only 1% of world gold reserves; raw material dependence acute; economic strain created temptation for conquest as fiscal relief — Czech national bank assets, ores, and arms industry were seized immediately

### The Offstage Superpowers

- **USSR**: manufacturing down to 13% of 1913 levels by 1920; Stalin's collectivization drove private consumption to 51-52% of GNP; human cost reckoned in millions; but by late 1930s industrial output surpassed France, Japan, Italy and probably Britain; purges eliminated 90% of generals and 80% of colonels — USSR actually *weaker* militarily at end of 1930s than five years earlier
- **United States**: produced larger output than the other six Great Powers *taken together* in the 1920s; but GNP plummeted during Depression; share of world manufacturing fell from 43.3% (1929) to 28.7% (1938); held **41.7% of world war potential** in 1937 while spending only 1.5% on defense; two-thirds of American steel plants were *idle* — other powers running at full capacity

### The Unfolding Crisis, 1931-1942

- Manchurian crisis (1931): League shown impotent; three democracies incapable of united action
- Appeasement logic: Britain's chiefs of staff said it was impossible to safeguard interests "against Germany, Italy and Japan at the same time"
- Munich (1938): Hitler the only leader actually prepared to fight; gave him more than he might have gained by war
- **Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact** (August 1939): enhanced Germany's strategic position; made war over Poland virtually inevitable
- **Blitzkrieg**: combined arms, tactical air power, decentralized offensive warfare — shattered larger but poorly organized Allied forces in 1940
- **Operation Barbarossa** (June 1941): Germany's fatal overreach — killing or capturing 3 million Russians in four months didn't solve the problem; "This was a limitless war, and the Third Reich was not properly equipped to fight it"
- **Pearl Harbor** (December 1941): Japan's navy calculated warship strength would fall from 70% of US (1941) to 30% (1944) — giving powerful incentive to strike before the sleeping giant awakened
- Both Germany and Japan understood the arithmetic; both gambled on early decisive victories before Allied productive superiority could be mobilized

## Chapter 7: Stability and Change in a Bipolar World, 1943–1980

### "The Proper Application of Overwhelming Force"

- On news of U.S. entry into war, **Churchill** rejoiced:
  > "Hitler's fate was sealed. Mussolini's fate was sealed. As for
  > the Japanese, they would be ground to powder. All the rest was
  > merely the proper application of overwhelming force."
- Confidence appeared premature through mid-1943: Japan overran
  Pacific/SE Asia; **Wehrmacht** resumed eastern offensive toward
  Caucasus; **Rommel** pushed within 55 miles of Alexandria;
  U-boats at peak lethality; strategic bombing failing
- Decisive fact: once the **Grand Alliance** fully mobilized,
  productive superiority over the Axis was insurmountable

**Pacific Theater turning points:**
- **Nimitz**'s carriers blunted Japan at **Coral Sea** (May 1942)
  and **Midway** (June 1942) — primacy of naval air power
- By Gilberts invasion (late 1943): four fast-carrier task forces
  (twelve carriers); Japan still had ~1M soldiers in China +
  780,000 in Manchuria, unable to withdraw due to American
  submarine campaign

**North Africa / Europe:**
- El Alamein (Oct 1942): **Montgomery** attacked with 6x tanks,
  3x troops, near-complete air superiority
- **Eisenhower**'s 100,000-man force landed in French North Africa;
  German-Italian forces surrendered May 1943
- **Doenitz** withdrew U-boat wolf packs from North Atlantic;
  long-range **P-51 Mustang** (Dec 1943) broke Luftwaffe's
  ability to defend Reich airspace

**Eastern Front:**
- **General Halder** (Aug 1941): "We reckoned with about 200
  enemy divisions. Now we have already counted 360."
- Germans claimed 3M+ Russian casualties in first five months —
  Red Army still had **4.2 million men** in field armies
- **Kursk** (summer 1943): Red Army countered with 34 armored
  divisions (~4,000 vehicles vs. 2,700); smashed Wehrmacht
- By Jan 1945: Soviet superiority "fivefold in manpower,
  fivefold in armor, over sevenfold in artillery, and seventeen
  times the German strength in the air"

### German and Japanese Strategic Errors

- **Economics vs. strategy** (paraphrasing **Clausewitz**):
  economic power stood in the same relationship to combat as
  the swordsmith's craft to fencing — necessary but not
  sufficient
- German blunders compounded material disadvantage: pouring
  reinforcements into N. Africa just in time to be captured;
  brutal treatment of **Ukrainian** minorities who initially
  welcomed escape from Stalinism; assumed **Enigma**
  unbreakable; ideological refusal to employ German women in
  munitions (while all Allied nations exploited this labor);
  **"polycratic chaos"** — army, SS, Gauleiter, economics
  ministry all rivaling, preventing coherent resource allocation
- Japan pursued "continental" strategy (army-dominant); only 11
  divisions for Pacific vs. 13 in Manchuria and 22 in China;
  even as Nimitz closed in 1945, reinforcements "far too tardy
  and far too small"
- **Imperial Japanese Navy** errors after **Yamamoto**'s death:
  admirals still "wedded to the battleship" — sought a second
  **Tsushima**; submarines wasted as fleet scouts instead of
  attacking Allied supply lines; failed to develop convoy
  systems, ASW, or escort carriers — built *zero* destroyer
  escorts 1941–43 vs. Americans' 331 ships; completely lost
  the intelligence/codes/decrypts battle

### Counterfactual: Could the Axis Have Won?

- Even if Moscow fell Dec 1941, USSR had massive reserves east
- Despite Barbarossa losses (coal -57%, pig iron -68%), Russia
  produced **4,000 more aircraft than Germany in 1941** and
  **10,000 more in 1942**
- Allied shipping losses: 8.3M tons (1942), 4M (1943) — but
  *new launchings* of 7M and 9M tons respectively
- U.S. by mid-1942 launching vessels **faster than U-boats
  could sink them**

**Key production data:**
- Aircraft (1943): Allies 151,761 vs. Axis 43,100
- Aircraft (1944): Allies 167,654 vs. Axis 67,987
- D-Day: Germans 319 aircraft vs. Allied 12,837 in the west
- Arms output ($bn, 1944 dollars): 1943 Allies $62.5 vs.
  Axis $18.3; U.S. arms rose **eightfold** 1941–43
- By 1943–44: U.S. producing **one ship per day** and **one
  aircraft every five minutes**

> "Once again, in a protracted and full-scale coalition war,
> the countries with the deepest purse had prevailed."

### The Atomic Bomb in Context

- Hiroshima was a watershed — but in 1945 was *one of several*
  tools: submarine campaign strangling Japan, B-29 fire raids
  (Tokyo, March 9: ~185,000 casualties), planned invasion
- Only the **United States** had the resources to simultaneously
  wage two large conventional wars AND invest ~$2B in an
  experimental weapon that might not work

### The New Strategic Landscape

- **Tocqueville**'s 1835 bipolar forecast finally realized;
  former great powers eclipsed; the international order moved
  "from one system to another" (**DePorte**)

**American Power in 1945 — Unprecedented Scale:**
- GNP rose from $88.6B (1939) to $135B (1945, constant dollars)
- Industrial expansion 1940–44: >15%/year; productive plant
  grew ~50%; war production surged from 2% to 40% of output
  yet civilian sector *also* grew — U.S. the **only Great Power
  that became richer** because of the war
- Gold reserves: **$20B** (two-thirds of world total of $33B)
- Produced more than half world manufacturing output
- Military: 12.5M personnel; Navy 1,200 major warships;
  2,000+ heavy bombers; monopoly on atomic bombs

**The Post-1945 Outward Thrust:**
- By 1945: 69 divisions in Europe, 26 in Asia/Pacific, zero
  in continental U.S.
- Like Britain after 1815: "new frontiers of insecurity"
  hardened informal influence into formal commitments
- **IMF**, **World Bank**, **GATT** — hammered out 1942–46;
  nations wanting reconstruction funds obliged to conform to
  American requirements; only growing fear of **Soviet
  influence** prompted the **Marshall Plan**

**Soviet Power in 1945:**
- Red Army smashed **506 German divisions**; 10 of 13.6M German
  casualties fell on the eastern front
- Stalin shifted dozens of divisions to Far East before Reich's
  collapse — crushed Japan's Kwantung Army within days of
  Hiroshima
- Territorial gains: Finland, Poland, Baltic states, East
  Prussia, Bessarabia; satellite states: Poland, East Germany,
  Czechoslovakia, Hungary, Romania, Bulgaria; in Far East:
  Manchuria, North Korea, Sakhalin
- But devastating cost: ~20–25 million Soviet citizens died
  premature deaths 1941–45; material destruction of occupied
  territory staggering: "Of 11.6 million horses, 7 million
  killed or taken away; 20 of 23 million pigs. 137,000
  tractors, 49,000 grain combines destroyed. 65,000 km of
  railway track, half of all railway bridges. People lived
  in holes in the ground."
- USSR stripped its German zone of all movable assets and
  extracted reparations from Romania, Finland, Poland

### Geopolitical Confirmation: Mackinder Vindicated

- Soviet empire appeared to confirm **Halford Mackinder's**
  prediction: a military power controlling the Eurasian
  **"Heartland"** would threaten global balance
- **Molotov** (1946): "One cannot decide now *any* serious
  problems of international relations without the USSR"
- Two superpowers both committed to **globalist thinking** —
  clash of interests inevitable

### Soviet Postwar Reconstruction and Militarization

- USSR 1945: a **military giant** but economically poor;
  with Lend-Lease cut off, reverted to post-1928 forced
  growth from own resources
- Heavy industry output nearly doubled 1945–50 — but much
  was recovery to prewar levels; agriculture remained
  chronically broken under Stalin's vendetta against peasants
- **Red Army** reduced by two-thirds — still 175 divisions,
  25,000 tanks, 19,000 aircraft; MiG-15 by 1947–48; captured
  German scientists developed guided missiles; **Beria** put
  in charge of atomic program the day after Hiroshima;
  navy transformed with new heavy cruisers and oceangoing
  submarines
- Stalinist repression renewed: returning POWs shot; **Zhukov**
  removed; Leningrad party purged 1948; censorship extended
  into natural sciences, biology, linguistics; same
  "tightening" imposed on eastern European satellites

- France: national income halved; franc slid from 50/$1 to
  420/$1 by 1949
- Britain: the only major state fighting start to finish;
  Churchill's "Big Three" status; yet **Correlli Barnett**:
  "victory was not synonymous with the preservation of
  British power"; export trade withered to **31% of 1938
  figures**; **Keynes's "financial Dunkirk" memorandum**
  warned of colossal trade gap; **Attlee government** tried
  to create welfare state AND maintain global bases; 1947
  withdrawal from India, Palestine, Greece — repositioning,
  not abandonment

### The Passing of the European Age — Statistical Evidence

- U.S. GNP surged 50%+ in real terms during war; Europe's
  (ex-USSR) fell ~25%; Europe's share of world manufacturing
  by 1953 only **26%** vs. U.S. at **44.7%**
- Military spending 1950: U.S. $14.5B / 1.38M personnel;
  USSR $15.5B / 4.3M men; Britain, France, Italy *combined*
  defense spending < one-fifth of U.S.

### Nuclear Weapons and Ideology

- Nuclear weapons gave any possessor capability for mass
  destruction; Britain and France built arsenals — same logic
  that drove pre-1914 Dreadnought building: a reflection of
  **weakness, not strength**
- **Ideology** cemented bipolar world; both Liberalism and
  Communism were universal ideologies — "mutually exclusive"
- **Truman Doctrine** (March 1947): framed world as binary
  choice between free institutions and minority tyranny;
  both Liberalism and Communism were universal ideologies —
  "mutually exclusive"; the whole world became an arena where
  ideological quarrel and power advantage were inseparable
- Cold War ideology served **domestic functions** too:
  discrediting political rivals, justifying repression,
  sustaining military budgets on both sides

### The Cold War and the Third World

- Cold War initially about remaking European boundaries;
  underneath, still about the "German problem" — whoever
  controlled Germany controlled Europe
- Stalin's paranoid demand for security drove policy;
  **Poland's fate** — Katyn massacre, disapproval of Warsaw
  uprising, imposition of puppet regime — the key signal
- **Containment strategy** (**George Kennan**): two elements:
  (1) *Negative*: military support for key regions;
  (2) *Positive* (more crucial): massive economic aid to
  rebuild Europe and Japan — make prosperity the obvious path
- **Kennan's five centers**: U.S., USSR, Britain, Germany,
  Japan — keep last three in western camp = permanent Soviet
  inferiority in "correlation of forces"
- **Marshall Plan** offered to *all* European nations including
  Communist states; **Molotov's walkout** from Paris talks +
  Russian pressure on Poland and Czechoslovakia not to apply
  → Europe divided more sharply
- Eastern Europe: **Cominform** established 1947; Prague
  Communist coup 1948; satellites forced into **Comecon** —
  "simply a new piece of machinery for milking the satellites"

**Cold War Escalation 1946–48:**
- Communists expelled from French government (April 1947);
  same in Italy a month later; Soviet refusal to join IMF
  confirmed rejection of postwar liberal economic order
- **NATO** did militarily what the Marshall Plan did
  economically: deepened division into two entrenched blocs
- **Brezhnev Doctrine**: any country that had become Communist
  could not abandon it; Soviet enforcement: East Germany
  rising (1953), Hungary (1956), **Berlin Wall** (1961),
  Czechoslovakia crushed (1968)

**Berlin Crisis and NATO:**
- Berlin Blockade (1948–49): Allies flew supplies eleven months;
  U.S. moved B-29 bombers to British airfields
- NATO on signing day: only **100,000 U.S. troops** in Europe
  (vs. 3M in 1945); only 12 divisions in Europe; NATO in
  early years reflected political symbolism more than military
  reality — historic shift in American diplomatic tradition

**Far East escalation:**
- "Loss" of China (1949) most traumatic for U.S.; **Joe
  McCarthy**'s red-baiting, **John Foster Dulles** and **Dean
  Rusk** forcing Truman to act more assertively
- Korean War: ~$50B, 54,000+ dead; **MacArthur's** Inchon
  provoked Chinese intervention (Oct/Nov 1950); stalemate
  forced trench warfare reminiscent of 1914–18
- Korea reversed abandonment of **Chiang Kai-shek**; Taiwan
  protected by U.S. fleet; Dulles shifted to military-
  territorial guarantees
- Key 1951 treaties: Philippines defense, **ANZUS**, Japan
  peace treaty + security pact keeping U.S. forces in Japan

**Iran and the "Northern Tier":**
- First extra-European dispute: tripartite occupation of
  **Iran** (1941–43); Moscow failed to withdraw garrison 1946;
  U.S./British protests forced Soviet withdrawal — "the
  meaning of containment before the doctrine was enunciated"
- Pattern spread: Greek civil war, Soviet pressure on Turkey
  → British 1947 declaration it could no longer guarantee
  those nations → **Truman Doctrine**

### The Arms Race

**Defense expenditures (Table 37 highlights, $bn):**
- U.S.: $10.9 (1948) → $33.3 (1951, Korean surge) → $52.3
  (1962, Berlin/Cuba) → $80.7 (1968, Vietnam)
- USSR: $13.1 (1948) → $20.1 (1951) → $54.7 (1963) → $89.8
  (1969)
- China: $2.0 (1948) → $23.7 (1970) — modest but steady
- European powers modest relative to superpowers — pattern
  "conveys the impression of a bipolar world"

**The Nuclear Arms Race: From Monopoly to MAD:**
- 1949: Russia's atomic test — well before western estimates;
  broke U.S. monopoly
- **"Bomber gap"** scare: Soviet **Bison** long-range bombers
  supposedly in vast numbers; reflected U.S. Air Force tendency
  to exaggerate
- **NSC-68** (Jan. 1950): imperative to reduce dependence on
  atomic weapons; U.S. ground forces in Europe tripled 1950–53
- H-bomb: U.S. tested "super" bomb; Russia tested nine months
  later (1953); **"missile gap"** panic after *Sputnik* (1957)
  → U.S. committed massive resources to ICBMs
- Result: **MAD** (Mutually Assured Destruction) — Churchill's
  "mutual balance of terror"; only superpowers counted

**The Naval Arms Race:**
- Soviet buildup the key stimulus: **Khrushchev** halted
  surface program, saw no point in expensive warships in
  missile age; three events changed thinking — Suez (1956),
  Lebanon landing (1958), Cuba naval cordon (1962)
- **Admiral Gorschkov**: without powerful navy, USSR at serious
  disadvantage → massive Red Navy expansion challenging
  western maritime dominance

**Alliance building and American overstretch:**
- By 1970, U.S. had "more than 1,000,000 soldiers in 30
  countries, mutual defense treaties with 42 nations,
  furnishing aid to nearly 100 nations"
- Alliance chain: **Rio Pact**, **NATO**, **ANZUS**, **SEATO**,
  **Baghdad Pact/CENTO**, bilateral treaties with Japan,
  South Korea, Taiwan, Philippines
- > "This has become a very small planet. We have to be
  > concerned with all of it." — **Dean Rusk**, 1965
- Domestic logic: once Korea was declared "not vital" and then
  attacked, every non-commitment became an invitation to
  Communist advance

**Soviet outreach under Khrushchev:**
- After Stalin's death, wanted USSR "admired rather than
  feared"; concessions: withdrew from Austria, returned
  Porkkala to Finland, Port Arthur to China
- Third World moves: 1953 trade agreement with **India**;
  1955 offer to build **Bhilai steel plant**; funded
  Egypt's **Aswan Dam** (replacing U.S.); 1960 first trade
  agreement with **Castro's Cuba**

### The Non-Aligned Movement

- **Bandung Conference** (April 1955): first major Third World
  gathering; UN General Assembly transformed from ~50 to 100+
  states with dominant Afro-Asian bloc
- Key figures: **Tito** (paradigm case of defiance), **Nasser**
  (accepted Soviet aid but treated home-grown Communists badly),
  **Nehru** (symbolic moderate)
- Soviet "gains" regularly attended by "losses": Iraq coup
  suppressed Communists; Indonesia's army wiped out CP with
  great ferocity; Guinea sent Russian ambassador home 1961;
  **Sadat** expelled 21,000 Russian advisers 1972 — major blow
- "Universalist" message of both superpowers would not be
  automatically accepted by other cultures

### The Fissuring of the Bipolar World

**Superpower military dominance 1974:**
- U.S. defense: $85B; USSR: $109B — both 3–4x China ($26B),
  8–10x leading European states
- Nuclear delivery vehicles (1974): ICBMs — U.S. 1,054 /
  USSR 1,575; SLBMs — U.S. 656 / USSR 720

**Arms control as arms race continuation:**
- **Nuclear test-ban treaty (1963)**; **SALT I (1972)** capped
  ICBMs; SALT II signed 1979 but never ratified by Senate
- Key insight: banning one weapon redirected resources to
  another — **MIRVs**, missile-carrying submarines, theater
  weapons (**Pershing II**, **cruise missiles** vs. **SS-20**)

**Soviet Third World expansion post-1960:**
- Red Navy broke into regions of western monopoly: Mediterranean,
  Indian Ocean, West Africa; Arab-Israeli wars drove Syria,
  Libya, Iraq toward Moscow; **Afghanistan invasion (1979)**
  — first Soviet expansion outside E. Europe since WWII

**The Sino-Soviet Split** — Kennedy called it the single
**most important strategic event since 1945:**
- Moscow half-hearted about CCP — as late as 1946–48 tried to
  balance CCP against **Chiang Kai-shek**; feared China as
  "a competing pole of attraction"
- Mao resented Stalin's meager aid (always for a price
  emphasizing Russian privileges); suspicious of Khrushchev's
  détente as betrayal
- 1959: Khrushchev canceled atomic agreement with Beijing,
  gave India larger loans than China ever received; by 1960
  split open at World Communist Parties meeting
- 1962–63: Mao denounced Russia for backing down over Cuba
  AND signing Nuclear Test Ban; USSR cut all aid to China
- 1964: China exploded first atomic bomb; Mao demanded Russia
  be reduced by **1.5 million square kilometers**
- Soviet divisions on Chinese frontier: 15 (1967) → 21 (1969)
  → 30 (1970) → **44 (1972)** after serious clash at
  **Damansky (Chenpao) island, March 1969**; a quarter of
  Soviet air force redeployed east; Moscow hinted at
  preemptive strike on **Lop Nor** nuclear installation

**De Gaulle's Challenge:**
- Resented treatment as less-than-equal; rejected Kennedy's
  Polaris offer; 1966: pulled France out of NATO military
  structure, expelled NATO HQ, closed American bases
- > "Hooray for France—since this morning she is stronger
  > and prouder." — de Gaulle, on first French atomic test
- Limitations: France only 3.5% of world manufacturing (1963)

**Willy Brandt's Ostpolitik (1969–73):**
- Reconciliation with Russia, Poland, Czechoslovakia, and
  especially E. Germany — accepting 1945 boundaries as
  permanent; western investment flowed across Iron Curtain;
  led to **Helsinki Accords (1975)** on human rights

### The Vietnam War

- U.S. 50–100x more productive than N. Vietnam; had firepower
  to "bomb the enemy back into the stone age" — but constraints
  prevented this superiority from being effective
- Troop levels peaked at **542,000 in 1969**; Clausewitzian
  mismatch: N. Vietnamese fighting for deeply held beliefs;
  S. Vietnamese regime "corrupt, unpopular, in a distinct
  minority"
- **Ho Chi Minh** declared willingness to lose men at ten to
  one — and after Tet (1968) proved it
- Long-term consequences: Vietnam delayed recognition of the
  Sino-Soviet split by perhaps a decade; while U.S. poured
  money into Vietnam, USSR achieved rough **strategic nuclear
  parity** and built a global naval force; by 1978, "national
  security expenditures" only 5% of GNP — lowest in 30 years;
  may have produced salutary shock to hubris — analogous to
  Russia's **Crimean War** or Britain's **Boer War** shock

### Nixon-Kissinger Revolution

- **Nixon**: "unique combination of ideological rigidity with
  political pragmatism" — could bomb N. Vietnam massively in
  1972 AND journey to China to bury the hatchet with Mao
- **Kissinger** modeled on **Metternich**, **Castlereagh**,
  **Bismarck**: "The beginning of wisdom in human as well as
  international affairs was knowing when to stop."
- Bismarckian aphorism: "The hostility between China and the
  Soviet Union served our purposes best if we maintained
  closer relations with each side than they did with each
  other."
- 1973: Kissinger identified five key regions; welcomed
  **concert of powers** as "a safer world and a better world"
  than a bipolar zero-sum system
- Effects: Japan established formal relations with PRC; Kremlin
  concluded SALT I; 1973 Arab-Israeli War: Kissinger's shuttle
  diplomacy froze Russia out of Middle East
- **Watergate** swept Nixon out; Congress slashed defense,
  cut all aid to S. Vietnam (Feb 1975, months before they
  fell); **War Powers Act** pared presidential authority;
  blocked CIA funds for Angola

### Carter and Reagan

- **Carter**: Wilsonian belief in "fairer" global order; Camp
  David agreement (1978) a real achievement; but foundered on
  internal inconsistencies — berated right-wing regimes while
  supporting **Mobutu**, the Shah; **Soviet invasion of
  Afghanistan (Dec 1979)**: Carter withdrew SALT II, began
  balance-of-power politics he had condemned
- **Reagan**: Manichaean worldview; defense budget more than
  doubled in five years; foundered on real-world complexity —
  jungle interventions blocked by Congress; nuclear "prevail"
  talk produced mass unease in Europe; Grenada invasion
  resembled "a Gilbert and Sullivan farce"
- USSR's structural problems deepening: importing foreign grain
  AND technology; eastern European satellites increasingly
  disaffected; Afghanistan became military quagmire; if any
  superpower had truly "lost China," it was **Russia**

### The Changing Economic Balances, 1950 to 1980

- **Bairoch**: post-WWII growth "a totally unprecedented rate";
  world manufacturing output 1953–73 grew at **6% per year**;
  accumulated output 1953–73 equaled the entire century and
  a half from 1800–1953
- World trade in **manufactured goods exceeded primary produce**
  for first time in **1957**

**The Japanese Miracle:**
- GDP grew **10.5% per year** 1950–73 — far exceeding any
  other industrialized nation; post-1973 oil shock still ran
  **almost twice** competitors' growth rates
- Automobile share: **1% (1960) → 23% (1984)**; shipyards
  producing over half world tonnage; steel matched total U.S.
  output; by late 1970s Japan's GNP equaled **UK and France
  combined** — more than half America's
- Ironically kick-started by U.S. defense spending in Korean
  War — **Toyota** was near bankruptcy until rescued by U.S.
  Defense Department truck orders
- Key drivers: fanatical quality control, universal education,
  **MITI** coordination ("nursed new industries while
  coordinating the orderly run-down of aging ones"), low
  defense spending, high personal savings rate, large
  *zaibatsu* (giant firms)

> "Within one generation, its share of world manufacturing
> output had risen from around 2–3 percent to around 10
> percent — and was not stopping."

**China — Political Giant, Economic Laggard:**
- 1953: only 2.3% of world manufacturing, industrial potential
  equal to only 71% of Britain's in 1900; overwhelmingly
  peasant population
- **Five-Year Plan (1953)** imitated Stalinist heavy industry;
  **Great Leap Forward** catastrophic; **Cultural Revolution**
  disgraced experts; Sino-Soviet split halted Russian aid
- Recovery: by 1980 steel output 37M tons (exceeding Britain
  or France); energy consumption twice any European state;
  world manufacturing share 3.9% (1973) → 5.0% (1980),
  closing on W. Germany's share

**Western Europe — The Recovery:**
- European GDP growth 1950–70: **5.5%/year** (4.4% per capita);
  industrial production at **7.1%/year**; Europe's share of
  world output rose from 37% to 41%; per capita income in one
  generation rose as much as in the previous 150 years
- Post-1945 "new Europeans" (**Monnet, Spaak, Hallstein**)
  built cooperative structures; **Marshall Plan** "a shot in
  the arm at a critical time"; U.S. defense umbrella freed
  Europe from excessive armaments spending
- **Italy** ("Italian Miracle"): GNP grew **nearly three times
  as fast** after 1948 as interwar; **FIAT** dominated domestic
  auto market; Italian refrigerators outsold all others in
  Europe by the 1960s; but north-south gap remained chronic
- West German *Wirtschaftswunder*: industrial production rose
  **110%** between 1948–52; steel from zero (1946) to 34M tons
  (1960); GNP: $32B (1952) → $89B (1962, largest in Europe)
  → $600B+ by late 1970s; per capita income $1,186 (1960) →
  **$10,837** (1979) — ahead of U.S. average of $9,595;
  brought in "guest workers" to meet labor demand; deutsche
  mark became de facto reserve currency
- Britain: **"The English disease"** — militant trade unionism,
  poor management, negative cultural attitudes toward
  entrepreneurship; share of world manufacturing **8.6%**
  (1953) → **4.0%** (1980); share of world trade **19.8%**
  (1955) → **8.7%** (1976); **Suez 1956** revealed inability
  to act militarily against American wishes
- France: growth from low base spectacular — industrial output
  index 95 (1953) → 259 (1973); **Common Market** protected
  agriculture AND gave large market; automobile production
  +1000%, aluminum +600% (1949–69); France's outsized
  influence derived from "national-cultural assertiveness"

**USSR — Impressive Gains, Structural Decline:**
- Steel: 12.3M tons (1945) → **148M tons** (1980) — world's
  largest; but growth rate fell from double digits to 3–4% by
  late 1970s
- 1980: American farm worker fed **65 people**; Soviet farm
  worker fed **8**
- Eastern European satellites mirrored problems; options for
  reform all constrained

**EEC as Collective Power (by 1983):**
- World's largest importer and exporter; largest currency and
  gold reserves; manufactured more automobiles (34%) than
  Japan (24%) or U.S. (23%); total population ~272M
- But early integration hopes foundered on nationalism; oil
  shock "took the steam out of earlier optimism"

**American Relative Decline:**
- Key caveat: the 1945 position was unprecedented and
  artificial — some decline was mathematically inevitable
- U.S. share of world manufacturing: ~50% (1945) → 44.7%
  (1953) → 31.5% (1980)
- U.S. share of world GNP: 25.9% (1960) → 21.5% (1980)
- Automobile example: 52% of world's cars (1960); by 1980
  only 23% — but *absolute* production actually rose
- Gold reserves: **68%** of world (1950) → **27%** (1973)
- **Kennedy** and **Johnson**: increased military spending +
  domestic expenditures, refused to raise taxes → deficits,
  inflation, uncompetitiveness; **de Gaulle** attacked U.S.
  "export of inflation"
- **Bretton Woods** collapsed when leading pillar could bear
  strains no more; productivity growth fell: 2.4% (1965–72)
  → 1.6% (1972–77) → 0.2% (1977–82)
- Federal deficits sucked foreign cash in, pushing dollar
  artificially high, turning U.S. from **net lender to net
  borrower**

## Chapter 8: To the Twenty-first Century

### History and Speculation

- Chapter explicitly **provisional and conjectural** — based
  on plausible assumptions, not guarantees
- Two central theses restated: (1) uneven economic growth
  impacts military power; (2) uneven growth has long-term
  impacts on strategic position — Engels: "nothing is more
  dependent on economic conditions than the army and navy"
- Victory in Great Power wars always goes to the side with
  greatest material resources; correlation over centuries
  is undeniable

### Two Major Forward-Looking Trends

**Trend 1 — Rise of the Pacific Basin:**
- Asian-Pacific GDP (ex-U.S.): 7.8% of world (1960) → 16.4%
  (1982); projected 20%+ by 2000
- U.S. trade with Asia-Pacific: 48% of U.S.-Europe trade
  (1960) → **122%** (1983)

**Trend 2 — Spiraling Cost of Weapons:**
- Bombers: **200x** more expensive than WWII; fighters **100x**;
  carriers **20x**; tanks **15x**; submarines: $5,500/ton
  (WWII) → $1.6M/ton (Trident)
- By 1984: world arms imports ($35B) exceeded world grain
  trade ($33B); world military spending ~$940B
- Historical parallel: excessive military top-heaviness
  afflicted **Philip II's Spain**, **Nicholas II's Russia**,
  and **Hitler's Germany**

> "A large military establishment may look imposing; but if
> not resting upon a firm foundation, it runs the risk of
> future collapse."

### The Guns-vs.-Butter Tension

- U.S. and USSR each spending **$250B+/year** on defense,
  heading to $300B+; **Worldwatch Institute**: "militarization
  of the world economy" advancing faster than for a generation
- Arms spending costs nations not just capital but **scientists,
  engineers, and R&D personnel** who would otherwise fuel
  commercial/export growth
- Two models as poles:
  - **"Trading states"** (Japan, Hong Kong, Switzerland):
    minimize defense, emphasize peaceful trading, free
    resources for consumption and investment
  - **"Militarized" economies** (USSR, Vietnam, Israel):
    allocate 10%+ of GNP to defense; suffer manifest
    diversion from productive ends
- Nations with **extensive overseas obligations** face the
  sharpest dilemma; aging populations further complicate

### The Threefold Challenge

- The **threefold feat** demanded of all governments heading
  into 21st century: (1) provide military security; (2) satisfy
  socioeconomic needs; (3) ensure sustained growth
- Achieving all three simultaneously is very difficult;
  failing the third — growth — inevitably leads to relative
  eclipse over the long term

> "It is hard to imagine, but a country whose productivity
> growth lags 1 percent behind other countries over one
> century can turn, as England did, from the world's
> indisputed industrial leader into the mediocre economy
> it is today."

- No perfect solution; best achievable is **rough harmony**
  among the three aims, shaped by national circumstances

---

### China's Balancing Act

- Poorest of major Powers but leadership evolving a grand
  strategy "more coherent and forward-looking" than Moscow,
  Washington, or Tokyo
- 1979 border war with Vietnam exposed PLA weaknesses:
  ~26,000 Chinese killed, 37,000 wounded
- **Deng Xiaoping** committed to modernizing — analogized to
  Colbert's France, Frederick the Great, or Meiji Japan:
  developing power by every pragmatic means
- PLA reduced from 4.2 to 3 million (counterintuitively an
  enhancement — many were mere support troops); military ranks
  restored (abolished by Mao as "bourgeois"); conscription
  introduced; academies reopened
- Navy modernized: new destroyers, fast-attack craft; 107
  conventional submarines by 1985 (third-largest fleet);
  tested ICBMs with 7,000-nautical-mile range; 1981 rocket
  launched three space satellites (multiple-warhead indicator);
  1980 naval exercise: 18-vessel task force on 8,000-mile
  South Pacific mission — first significant Chinese sea-power
  projection since **Cheng Ho** in the early 15th century
- Critical gaps remain: lack of metallic technology, advanced
  jet engines, radar; spending only one-eighth of superpower
  defense budgets; Moscow deploys ~50 divisions east of Urals
  vs. 70+ PLA divisions — a "rough equivalence"

**China's economic trajectory:**
- Since 1952: industry grew ~10%/year, agriculture 3%, overall
  GNP 5–6% — remarkably including Great Leap Forward and
  Cultural Revolution averages
- Under Deng's reforms (post-1978): agriculture 8%, industry
  12%; between 1979–83, 800 million rural Chinese increased
  incomes ~70%; "In 1985, Chinese produced 100 million more
  tons of grain than a decade earlier"
- China feeds 1 billion on only 250 million acres (vs. U.S.
  400 million acres for 230 million people)
- Savings and investment rate consistently above 30% of GNP
  since 1970; Cultural Revolution closed universities for
  nearly a decade — China only now recovering scientific cadre

**Defense spending deliberately suppressed:**
- **"Four modernizations"**: agriculture, industry, science,
  defense — defense explicitly *last*; defense share fell
  from ~17.4% (1971) to ~7.5% (1985) — stark contrast to
  Soviet obsession with military security
- Core logic: long-term security assured only when output
  multiplied many times over
- *The Economist*: if 8% growth maintained, China would
  surpass British/Italian GNP before 2000 and exceed any
  European power by 2020
- Beijing joined **World Bank** and **IMF** not as ideological
  conversion but as hard-nosed calculation that multilateral
  access better preserves independence than bilateral deals

> **Jonathan Pollack**: "China has very shrewdly used its
> available resources… China must be judged as a candidate
> superpower in its own right."

### The Japanese Dilemma

**The "Scissors Effect" threatening Japan:**
- Blade 1: Asian NIC competition (lower labor costs)
- Blade 2: Western protectionism (U.S. trade deficit with
  Japan: $62B in fiscal 1986)

**Japan's structural advantages:**
1. **Terms of trade windfall**: imports 99% of oil, 92% of
   iron, 100% of copper — falling commodity prices save
   billions; energy dependence cut 25% in one decade
2. **High-technology pivot**: systematically exiting textiles,
   shipbuilding for computing, robotics, biotech, ceramics,
   nuclear power; in semiconductors (with MITI support) drove
   most U.S. firms out of 16k, 64k, and 256k RAM production;
   entering **"fifth generation" supercomputers** —
   American experts "stunned"; **Mitsubishi**–Westinghouse and
   **Japanese Aircraft Development Corp.**–Boeing partnerships
   described as "Faustian bargains"
3. **R&D surge**: rising from 2% to expected 3.5% of GNP by
   1990; if military R&D excluded, Japan already devotes as
   many man-hours as U.S.; will lead in *nonmilitary* R&D by
   early 1990s; crucially industry-funded and commercially
   targeted
4. **High savings**: tax system encourages saving vs. U.S.
   system encouraging borrowing; weak pensions force
   individuals to save; banks awash with low-interest capital
5. **Protected home market**: formal/informal barriers; cultural
   pride and complex distribution networks
6. **Workforce quality**: 15-year-olds markedly superior in
   math/science; produces ~50% more engineers than U.S.;
   **David Halberstam**: emulating Japan requires copying
   its *social system* — "a much harder competition than
   the political-military competition with the Soviet Union"

**Japan as World Creditor Nation:**
- Personal savings over 20% of wages; by 1985 average household
  savings exceeded average annual income
- Capital outflow: $17.7B (1983) → $49.7B (1984) → $64.5B
  (1985) — world's largest net creditor; Nomura Research
  forecast: gross overseas assets to exceed **$1 trillion
  by 1995**; much going into U.S. Treasury bonds to finance
  Reagan-era deficits — if recalled could unsettle finance
  "just as in 1929"

**Long-term trajectory:**
- In 1951 Japan's GNP was 1/3 of Britain's and 1/20th of
  U.S.'s; within 30 years doubled Britain's and approached
  half the U.S.'s; **Herman Kahn** and **Ezra Vogel** argued
  Japan would be "number one" economically
- Kennedy's verdict: "destined to get much richer" barring
  war, ecological catastrophe, or 1930s-style protectionism

**Japan's military paradox:**
- 1983 defense: **$11.6B** vs. U.S. $239B; per capita: $98
  vs. $1,023 for Americans
- If Japan matched NATO European average (~3–4% of GNP):
  world's third-largest military power, $50B+/year
- But will resist: **anti-war sentiment** (A-bomb trauma,
  memory of 1930s militarism); Article 9 constitutional bar;
  economic logic (businessmen oppose public spending); regional
  alarm from Russia, China, Korea, Philippines, Australia
- ***Zenhoi heiwa gaiko*** ("omnidirectional peaceful diplomacy")
- Neighbors fear Japan creating new **"Greater East Asia
  Co-Prosperity Sphere"** — sells manufactures, imports only
  raw materials; **Deng Xiaoping** (1979): "America's trade
  with China must come equal to Japan's" — to prevent "the
  imperialism of free trade"
- Deepest worry rarely discussed: what if U.S. withdraws
  from Asia? What if China dominates the region? The
  trading-state model has no answer.

> "Commercial expertise and financial wealth sometimes no
> longer suffice in the anarchic world of international
> power politics." — Kennedy

### The EEC — Potential and Problems

- Of five main power concentrations, only Europe is not a
  sovereign state — that is its defining problem
- Analogy: EEC circa 1987 resembles the **German Federation**
  mid-19th century — a customs union proving economically
  successful but unable to translate potential into power
- 12-member population ~320M (1.5x U.S.); total GNP roughly
  equal to USA; largest trading bloc in the world
- If whole EEC spent 7% of GNP on defense: expenditure would
  equal the two superpowers combined
- But armed forces suffer multiple languages, different weapons;
  political fragmentation; **"Eurosclerosis"** — unemployment
  leaped from 5.9M to 10.2M between 1978 and 1982

**West Germany — Strengths and Anxieties:**
- Largest EEC producer of steel, chemicals, electrical goods,
  autos; trade surplus second only to Japan's; deutsche mark
  used as reserve currency; engineering management culture
  (vs. American finance emphasis)
- **Angst**: EEC farm subsidies drain German taxpayers; high
  unemployment keeps social-security payments high; **steep
  birthrate fall** will enlarge pensioner share
- **Reunification dilemma**: 62M West + 17M East Germans
  logically belong together; unified Germany's GNP dangerously
  close to USSR's; a trading magnet for all eastern Europe
- **Soviet veto**: East Germany is the strategic barrier —
  USSR could not accept unified Germany with 660,000+ troops
  on its western flank; result: "suspended animation"
- **Bundeswehr**: 335,000 troops, 645,000 trained reserves —
  Europe's highest-quality conventional force; declining
  birthrate makes full strength hard to maintain
- Since **Willy Brandt**: pursued *détente* to calm fears of
  German resurgence; shuns "out-of-area" capability

**Britain — Manufacturing Collapse and R&D Trap:**
- Trade balance on manufactures in deficit **for first time
  since the Romans invaded** (per *The Economist*)
- **North Sea oil** paradox: turned sterling into a
  "petrocurrency," pushing value unrealistically high,
  making exports uncompetitive; when oil runs out,
  manufacturing capacity already scrapped
- For every $1 on R&D in Britain, $1.50 in Germany, $3 in
  Japan, $8 in U.S.; and **50% of British R&D went to
  defense** vs. Germany's 9% and Japan's near-zero
- Still maintained 65,000 troops in Germany plus garrisons in
  Belize, Cyprus, Gibraltar, Hong Kong, Falklands, Brunei
- Britain spent 5.5% of GNP on defense — more than any
  European NATO partner except Greece; **BAOR**: 55,000 troops,
  600 tanks, nearly $4B annually; **Trident** costs rising
  monthly; weapon prices rising 6–10% faster than inflation
- Divergence between shrunken economy and overextended posture
  more extreme than any major Power except Russia

**France — Similar Dilemma, Better Concealed:**
- Built nuclear "triad" (land, aircraft, submarine); asserting
  that if deterrence fails, all missiles fire at Russia
  immediately; kept independent of NATO command structure
- **Raymond Aron**: Gaullist assertiveness possible only
  because France was sheltered behind U.S. nuclear guarantee
- Nuclear triad absorbed ~30% of defense budget; France
  spending 4.2% of GNP on defense; nuclear credibility
  problem: France's own promise to go nuclear on behalf of
  W. Germany is doubted by West Germans themselves
- **SDI** threat: if superpowers develop missile defense,
  France's modest nuclear force rendered useless
- Growing Soviet threat pushed France toward closer NATO
  alignment — **"New Atlanticism"**

- Kennedy's verdict on Europe: if EEC acts as unified power,
  it could improve; if not — "which, given human nature, is
  the more plausible outcome — its relative decline seems
  destined to continue"

### The Soviet Union and Its "Contradictions"

- Kennedy uses Marxist term deliberately and ironically:
  widening gap between stated goals and methods

> **Gorbachev** (1986): "Difficulties began to build up in the
> economy in the 1970s… Acceleration of the country's
> socio-economic development is the key to all our problems."

**Agricultural failure:**
- Once one of two largest grain exporters; now imports tens
  of millions of tons annually
- Agriculture consumes ~30% of Soviet investment (vs. 5% in
  U.S.); productivity one-seventh that of American farming
- Private plots = only 4% of arable land but produce ~25%
  of total crop output — devastating indictment
- Meat costing $4/lb to produce sells for 80 cents; ~20% of
  grain and ~50% of potatoes perish due to poor storage

**Industrial stagnation:**
- USSR outproduces U.S. in steel, cement, fertilizers — yet
  "paradox of industrial plenty in the midst of consumer
  poverty"; factories cannot go bankrupt

**Energy crisis:**
- USSR used 1,490 kg coal equivalent per $1,000 GDP vs.
  W. Germany's 565; oil output dropped 1984–85 for first
  time since WWII; extraction costs rose 70% per ton;
  **Chernobyl** (1986) severely set back nuclear plans

**High-technology gap:**
- Falling behind in robotics, supercomputers, lasers, telecom
- Core contradiction: computers thrive in open societies but
  threaten Soviet monopoly on information; senior scientists
  forbidden from using copying machines personally —
  departments staffed by the **KGB**

**Demographic crisis:**
- Life expectancy for Soviet man: ~60 years — six years less
  than mid-1960s; infant mortality 3x U.S. rate; alcoholism
  at "fantastic levels"
- Central Asian republics have fertility 3x Slavic peoples;
  Russian population share projected to fall below 50% by 2000
- Labor force growth 1980–90: only 5.99M vs. 24.2M prior decade

**Two political obstacles to reform:**
- (1) **Entrenched party elite** — "the Soviet Union needs its
  inefficiencies to remain Soviet"
- (2) **Defense burden**: 11–13% of GNP (CIA revised estimate);
  roughly **twice** U.S. share even under Reagan buildup;
  military siphons trained manpower and capital; "guns will
  always come before butter and, if need be, before growth"

**Soviet military — real but overstated:**
- Massive modernization across all branches is real; but should
  not be presented as omnipotent
- **Technology gap**: Russia's advantage greatest when weapons
  technology *standardizes* (quantity over quality); whenever
  technology spirals upward emphasizing quality, Russian
  advantage **diminishes**; Soviet weaponry repeatedly
  outclassed by U.S. hardware in surrogate conflicts
- **SDI** compounds the problem: even imperfect SDI could
  protect missile sites, forcing Soviets to produce many more
  warheads; SDI-derived technologies (lasers, supercomputers,
  precision guidance) could dominate *conventional* warfare;
  **Marshal Ogarkov** warned of awful consequences of failing
  to match western technology
- **Demographic threat**: recruits from **Turkestan** — poorly
  versed in Russian, low mechanical/electronic competence,
  sometimes strongly influenced by Islam; officer corps and
  rocket forces overwhelmingly Slavic; like **Austro-Hungarian**
  and **Czarist** empires, an unresolved nationality problem;
  Ukraine historically disloyal; 48M Muslims along unstable
  borders with Turkey, Iran, Afghanistan

**Nuclear balance (Table 47, 1986):**

| Delivery type    | U.S.    | USSR     |
|------------------|---------|----------|
| ICBM-borne       | 2,118   | 6,420    |
| SLBM-borne       | 5,536   | 2,787+   |
| Aircraft-borne   | 2,520   | 680      |
| **Total**        | **10,174** | **9,987+** |

- Kennedy's key argument: nuclear arsenals essentially
  **unusable** — any exchange would inflict damage serving
  no political purpose; **Robert Jervis**: "a rational
  strategy for the employment of nuclear weapons is a
  contradiction in terms"
- **SLBM invulnerability** the decisive fact: submarine-launched
  missiles in undetectable craft ensure mutual deterrence
  remains stable

**Conventional balance — Warsaw Pact reliability:**
- 1985 British Defence White Paper: European NATO provides
  90% of ready manpower in Europe, 85% of tanks, 95% of
  artillery; NATO allies spend **six times more** on defense
  than Russia's Warsaw Pact allies
- But Warsaw Pact unreliable: **Solidarity** in Poland;
  East Germany seeking closer ties with Bonn; Hungarian
  "creeping capitalism"; Polish forces "can hardly be
  reckoned as addition to pact's strength"; four-fifths of
  combat power is Russian alone
- **Two-front nightmare**: ~50 divisions and 13,000 tanks on
  Chinese border; Trans-Siberian Railway vulnerable to rocket
  strike; Russia's age-old expansion across Asia has
  effectively *halted*

**Naval weakness (Table 48):**

| Category           | Warsaw Pact | NATO   |
|--------------------|-------------|--------|
| Nuclear submarines | 105         | 97     |
| Major surface ships| 187         | 376    |
| Naval aircraft     | 807         | 2,533  |

- 75% of Red Navy personnel conscripts; many surface warships
  20+ years old; Soviet navy's probable real purpose: forming
  "ocean bastion" to protect nuclear-missile submarines
- Overseas positions (Vietnam, Ethiopia, Cuba) expensive and
  exposed; USSR's trade/investment in Third World minuscule
  compared to the West

> "Without its massive military power, it counts for little;
> *with* its massive military power, it makes others feel
> insecure and hurts its own economic prospects. A grim
> dilemma."

- **None** of the overextended empires surveyed (Ottoman,
  Spanish, Napoleonic, British) ever retreated gracefully
  without being defeated or so weakened that withdrawal
  was politically unavoidable

### The United States: The Problem of Number One in Relative Decline

- Two distinctions from Soviet case: (1) U.S. absolute strength
  still much larger than USSR's; (2) America's laissez-faire
  society gives better capacity to readjust — but only if
  leadership understands the larger processes

**Imperial overstretch:**
- Sum total of U.S. global interests far larger than power to
  defend them all simultaneously
- Like **Imperial Spain around 1600** or **British Empire
  around 1900**: inheritor of vast commitments made when
  capacity seemed more assured
- By 1985: **520,000 military personnel abroad** — more than
  British Empire peacetime deployments at height
- **Joint Chiefs chairman** (late 1983): "mismatch" between
  forces and strategy "is greater now than ever before"

**Regional dilemmas:**
- Middle East: world's surplus oil, Soviet penetration threat,
  Israel/Arab contradiction = policy paralysis
- Latin America: debt crisis; **Mexico** — economic bankruptcy,
  illegal migration, drug trade — "worse than the Polish
  crisis for the USSR"
- East Asia: largest share of world population and growing
  U.S. trade; hidden contradictions (build PRC relationship
  without abandoning Taiwan; push Japan on defense without
  alarming neighbors)
- NATO: 50–60% of U.S. general-purpose forces allocated;
  Europe's total population and income now larger than USA's,
  yet lower defense spending share

**Military procurement problems:**
- Reagan spent 75% more on aircraft than Carter but acquired
  only 9% more planes — "fewer and fewer, more and more
  expensive" spiral; if conventional war consumed early losses,
  replacement at WWII speeds impossible
- **Interservice rivalry** more entrenched than most armed
  forces; "waste, fraud and abuse" rooted in lack of
  competitive bidding in **military-industrial complex**
- **Wrong-war problem**: nuclear deterrence, large conventional
  war vs. Warsaw Pact, and Third World proxy conflicts each
  demand different weapons mixes

**Grand strategy incoherence:**
- State Department wants firm responses; Defense (post-Lebanon)
  reluctant to commit forces; Pentagon made unilateral decisions
  (SDI, abandoning SALT II) without consulting allies
- Pro-Israel lobby repeatedly undercuts strategic interest in
  supporting pro-Western Arab states
- Structural obstacles: electoral cycle paralyzes foreign policy
  every two years; lobbyists and PACs prejudice every change;
  mass media simplifies complex issues; constitutional division
  of powers designed for isolated republic, not global
  superpower requiring swift decisions

**Economic decline:**
- High-tech trade surplus plunged from $27B (1980) to $4B
  (1985); visible trade deficit: **$160B** (twelve months to
  May 1986)
- U.S. transformed from **world's largest creditor** to
  world's largest debtor in a few years
- Federal deficit: $59.6B (1980) → $202.8B (1985); national
  debt: $914B → $1,823B; interest: $52.5B → $129B
- Historical parallel: only other peacetime Great Power that
  so increased debt was **France in the 1780s** — fiscal crisis
  contributed to political collapse
- U.S. channels most R&D into military; Japan and W. Germany
  into **commercial R&D** — scientists drawn to Pentagon not
  designing better consumer products
- **Eurodollar market** alone has volume "at least 25 times
  that of world trade" — dislocation of capital flows from
  goods trade is a new destabilizing phenomenon

**U.S. Federal Deficit, Debt, and Interest ($bn):**

| Year | Deficit | Debt      | Interest |
|------|---------|-----------|----------|
| 1980 | $59.6   | $914.3    | $52.5    |
| 1983 | $195.4  | $1,381.9  | $87.8    |
| 1985 | $202.8  | $1,823.1  | $129.0   |

- Projections: if trends continued, national debt ~$13
  *trillion* by 2000; interest ~$1.5 *trillion*

**Social/political consensus at risk:**
- Earnings gap between rich and poor larger than any other
  advanced society; political stability rested on overall
  growth offering betterment to the majority
- Demographic shift (differentiated birthrates) +
  deindustrialization (loss of high-wage manufacturing,
  replaced by low-wage services) may undermine consensus
- Warning: a polity that raises defense spending AND slashes
  social spending risks political backlash — the three-way
  tension between **defense, consumption, and investment**
  has no easy resolution

**Defense spending vs. economic growth:**
- Defense was 10% of GNP under Eisenhower, 9% under Kennedy —
  but U.S. share of global production was *twice* today's
- If U.S. devotes 7%+ while Japan allocates far less, Japan
  has proportionally more for civilian investment

**The Edwardian Britain parallel:**
- American anxiety mirrors the "national efficiency" movement
  in Edwardian Britain — broad debate over growing
  uncompetitiveness
- **G. K. Chesterton**: "A strong man does not worry about
  bodily efficiency; only when he weakens does he begin to
  talk about health"
- What **Hewins** said of British industrial decay in 1904
  now applies to U.S. decline in shipbuilding, mining,
  semiconductors

**The imperial overstretch dilemma:**
- As global superpower, requires far larger defense forces
  than regional powers — just as imperial Spain needed a
  bigger army and Victorian Britain a bigger navy
- **The arms race trap**: heavy armament brings short-term
  security but erodes commercial competitiveness; low
  investment leaves the U.S. feeling vulnerable everywhere
- The more perceptive decision-makers recognize Soviet
  armament spending is debilitating *their* economy too —
  real question: "whose economy will decline **fastest**
  relative to Japan, China, etc.?"
- Historical pattern: as leading power's economic strength
  ebbs, growing threats compel *more* military spending,
  which squeezes investment → slower growth → heavier taxes
  → domestic splits → **downward spiral**

**The "natural share" argument:**
- U.S. will not collapse to obscurity — too large and
  homogeneous; British analogy flawed (Britain's natural
  share ~3–4% vs. peak of ~25%)
- U.S. natural share ~16–18% of world wealth; peaked at
  ~40%+ in 1945 due to extraordinary wartime circumstances;
  present moment is ebbing back toward natural share
- Even at natural share, U.S. will remain very significant
  in a **multipolar world** simply because of its size
- Goal: not preserve 1945 position (impossible) but **manage**
  the erosion so it is slow and smooth — not accelerated by
  policies offering only short-term advantage
- **Walter Lippmann**: bring "into balance the nation's
  commitments and the nation's power"
- **Pierre Hassner**: U.S. remains "the decisive actor in
  every type of balance and issue" — because it is linchpin
  of western alliance and center of global economy

## Epilogue

### Core Thesis Restated

- After 500 years: **wealth and power are always relative**;
  international balances can never be still
- Outcome of great coalition wars consistently confirms
  economic shifts already underway — war reveals and ratifies
  the redistribution of productive power

### Does Rising/Falling Always Lead to War?

- **Mackinder**: "the great wars of history are the outcome
  of the unequal growth of nations"
- Nuclear weapons may have broken this pattern (MAD); but may
  only ensure future conflicts remain *conventional*
- **Norman Angell's** *The Great Illusion* (1910) argued war
  would be ruinous — published as European staffs finalized
  war plans. History refutes confident predictions of peace.

### The Emerging Multipolar Pattern

- **First**: power will shift from the five largest
  concentrations (U.S., USSR, China, Japan, EEC) to more
  nations — but no new entrant will join this pentarchy soon
- **Second**: productive balances tilting away from Russia and
  U.S., toward Japan and China; PRC still far behind but
  growing fastest
- **Third**: military bipolarity slowly eroding; at nuclear
  level China, France, Britain building up; at conventional
  level steady Chinese buildup; hypothetical West German–
  French combination would be enormous

### The Permanent Dilemma

- **Friedrich List's** *Nationaloekonomie*: "War or the very
  possibility of war makes the establishment of a manufacturing
  power an indispensable requirement for a nation of the first
  rank"
- Yet the paradox: devoting manufacturing power to "unproductive"
  armaments erodes the economic base — especially vs. states
  concentrating on productive investment
- **Adam Smith** school: keep defense expenditures low;
  **List** school: the state must possess greater instruments
  of force; both acknowledged it was genuinely a difficult
  choice
- Ideally "profit" and "power" go hand in hand — in practice
  statesmen are constantly forced to choose between buying
  security now (at cost to economy) or keeping defense low
  (risking exploitation by rivals)

### "The Law of the Increasing Cost of War"

- Cost escalation alarming for all governments — cynic's
  forecast: even a single aircraft in 2020 could consume
  entire U.S. Air Force budget
- Great Powers face two simultaneous structural challenges:
  (1) **Uneven economic growth** — some become stronger
  relative to others; (2) **The military-security trap** —
  must choose between immediate military security and longer-
  term economic security; no universally applicable rule exists
- Neglecting defense → vulnerable to exploitation; overspending
  → **imperial overstretch**, like "an old man attempting to
  work beyond his natural strength"
- Already visible: USSR, U.S., and Britain all showing signs
  of overstretch; China and W. Germany consciously *avoiding*
  excessive military spending because they judge it would
  hurt long-term growth

### Final Observation

- Each large Power grapples with age-old dilemmas of rise
  and fall, technological innovation, spiraling weapons costs
- **Bismarck**: all Powers are traveling on "the stream of
  Time," which they can "neither create nor direct," but
  upon which they can "steer with more or less skill"

> "How they emerge from that voyage depends, to a large
> degree, upon the wisdom of the governments in Washington,
> Moscow, Tokyo, Peking, and the various European capitals."
