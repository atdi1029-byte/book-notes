# The Rise and Fall of the Great Powers — Summary

**Author:** Paul Kennedy
**Published:** 1987 (Vintage Books, Random House)
**Pages:** ~677 (text) + endnotes/bibliography

## Core Thesis

The rise and fall of Great Powers is driven by the interaction between economic change and military conflict. A nation's productive economic base determines its long-term military strength. Powers that overextend militarily relative to their economic capacity inevitably decline, while rising powers that invest in productive capacity rather than defense spending eventually surpass them. This pattern has repeated across five centuries, from Habsburg Spain to Cold War America.

## Key Arguments

1. **Economics precedes military power**: Shifts in trade flows and manufacturing shares herald the rise of new Great Powers, sometimes decades before the military balance tips.

2. **Strategic overextension kills empires**: When military commitments outstrip economic capacity — as with Spain, France under Napoleon, Britain after 1900, and potentially the US and USSR — decline follows.

3. **Lag time**: There is always a delay between economic and military trajectories. A rising power prefers growth over armaments; a declining power looks strong for decades after the economic rot has set in.

4. **Coalition wars are decided by resources**: In prolonged great-power conflicts, victory goes to the side with the greater productive base — true from the anti-Habsburg wars through both World Wars.

5. **Political fragmentation breeds innovation**: Europe's lack of centralized authority created competitive pressures that drove constant military, commercial, and technological advancement — unlike centralized empires (China, Ottoman, Mughal) that could suppress innovation by decree.

## Structure

The book is organized in three parts spanning 500 years:

**Part I: Strategy and Economics in the Preindustrial World (1500-1815)**
- Why Europe surpassed Ming China, the Ottoman Empire, and other civilizations
- The Habsburg bid for mastery and its failure through overextension
- How Britain's financial revolution and island geography made it the dominant power by 1815

**Part II: Strategy and Economics in the Industrial Era (1815-1942)**
- How industrialization shifted global balances, eclipsing the non-European world
- Britain's rise to zenith and slow relative decline as Germany and the US industrialized
- The transition from a multipolar to a bipolar world through two world wars

**Part III: Strategy and Economics Today and Tomorrow (1943-1987)**
- The Cold War bipolar system and its evolution
- The relative decline of both superpowers as economic power dispersed
- Kennedy's controversial prediction of American "imperial overstretch"

## What Makes This Book Important

Kennedy demonstrated with exhaustive historical evidence that no Great Power remains dominant forever. The same pattern — economic rise, military expansion, overextension, relative decline — has played out with Spain, France, Britain, and (he predicted) would play out with the United States and Soviet Union. The book became one of the most debated works of the late Cold War era, particularly for its argument that America's military commitments were outstripping its economic base.

## The Through-Line

From Ming China burning its fleet to Habsburg Spain bankrupting itself on the Army of Flanders, from Napoleon's plunder-based war finance to Britain's "weary Titan" moment, the lesson is the same: **productive power is the foundation of everything**. Military strength, diplomatic influence, and imperial reach all rest on the economy. When a nation forgets this — spending too much on defense, neglecting investment, fighting too many wars on too many fronts — the decline may be invisible for a generation, but it is already underway.
