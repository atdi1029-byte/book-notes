Ming China

In 1500, no observer surveying the world’s civilizations would have predicted European dominance. The most impressive society on earth was Ming China — a unified empire of 100 to 130 million people governed by a sophisticated Confucian bureaucracy that had survived Mongol domination and reasserted itself with quiet authority. Chinese cities dwarfed their European counterparts. The empire’s internal trade routes were as extensive as anything in the West, and its cultural achievements — printing, paper money, gunpowder, the magnetic compass — had been borrowed by Europeans for centuries.

The numbers alone were staggering. In the eleventh century, north China’s iron industry produced roughly 125,000 tons per year — a figure that far exceeded British iron output at the dawn of the Industrial Revolution, seven centuries later. By 1420, the Ming navy comprised 1,350 combat vessels, including 400 large floating fortresses and 250 long-range cruising ships. No European fleet came close. Between 1405 and 1433, Admiral Cheng Ho led seven extraordinary expeditions with hundreds of ships and tens of thousands of men, reaching Malacca, Ceylon, the Red Sea, and Zanzibar. His treasure ships displaced over 1,500 tons — dwarfing the Portuguese caravels that would later round the Cape of Good Hope. China could have “discovered” Portugal decades before Henry the Navigator pushed south of Ceuta.

“The Chinese apparently never plundered nor murdered — unlike the Portuguese, Dutch, and other European invaders.” — Kennedy

Then China turned inward. The last Cheng Ho expedition sailed in 1433. Three years later, an imperial edict banned the construction of seagoing ships. Later decrees forbade vessels with more than two masts. The great warships rotted in their harbors. There was a plausible strategic rationale — Mongol pressure on the northern frontier made a large navy seem an expensive luxury — but the decision was never reconsidered, even when Japanese pirates ravaged the coast or Portuguese vessels appeared off China’s shores.

The deeper cause was the conservatism of the Confucian bureaucracy, heightened during the Ming period by resentment at Mongol-forced changes. The mandarins distrusted merchants. They routinely confiscated commercial property, banned foreign trade, restricted printing to scholarly works, and let canals, ironworks, and astronomical instruments fall into disuse. Chinese cities never developed the autonomy of their Western counterparts — when the emperor’s court moved, the capital city moved with it. Merchants who acquired wealth invested in land and education, not in proto-industrial development.

The Confucian code treated warfare as deplorable and armed forces as necessary only against barbarian threats. State-driven technology could be started and stopped by bureaucratic decree. Paper currency was discontinued. The iron industry gradually declined. In 1736 — just as Abraham Darby’s ironworks at Coalbrookdale were beginning to boom — the blast furnaces of Honan and Hopei were abandoned entirely. They had been great before the Conqueror landed at Hastings. They would not resume production until the twentieth century.

Ming China was far less vigorous than under the Sung dynasty four centuries earlier. Agricultural improvements could not keep pace with population growth. Even the replacement of the Ming by the more vigorous Manchu dynasty in 1644 could not halt the relative decline. The lesson was devastating in its simplicity: a civilization that possessed every conceivable advantage — population, technology, wealth, military power — surrendered its lead because a centralized bureaucracy had the authority to shut down innovation by decree. In Europe, no such authority existed.

The Ming story is the book’s first and most vivid illustration of Kennedy’s central theme: power is relative, not absolute. China did not collapse. It did not become poor. It simply stopped advancing while others accelerated. The iron furnaces went cold not because of invasion or plague but because mandarins who despised commerce decided they were unnecessary. By the time anyone thought to relight them, Europe had passed China and would not look back for three centuries.

There is something almost eerie about this reversal. A nation that invented gunpowder, printing, and the compass — the three technologies that would reshape the world — chose to suppress their development. A navy that could have circumnavigated Africa was ordered to rot. An iron industry that dwarfed anything in Europe was deliberately abandoned. The Ming case establishes the pattern that will repeat throughout the book: the greatest threat to a dominant power is not external enemies but internal decisions to stop competing.

The Muslim World

While China turned inward, the Ottoman Empire was the most rapidly expanding force in world affairs. In the sixteenth century, Muslim states were on the march everywhere — Safavid Persia resurging under Abbas the Great, Babur establishing the Mughal Empire in India, Muslim khanates controlling the Silk Road. The Ottoman Turks possessed the finest siege train of the age. By the 1520s their domains stretched from the Crimea to Egypt, and Suleiman the Magnificent was besieging Vienna itself.

The Ottoman military machine was formidable. The janissary system — recruiting Christian youth from the Balkans into a dedicated, uniformed corps — produced soldiers of exceptional quality. Ottoman tolerance drew talent from across the empire: a Hungarian served as Mehmet’s chief gun-caster at the siege of Constantinople. By 1600, Constantinople had 500,000 inhabitants. The empire was more than a military machine — it unified official faith, culture, and language over an area exceeding ancient Rome.

But the Ottoman decline, when it came, mirrored China’s with unsettling precision. After Suleiman’s death in 1566, thirteen incompetent sultans followed in succession. The empire suffered from strategic overextension — large armies in central Europe, a navy in the Mediterranean, troops in North Africa and the Aegean and Crimea, all at once. The Shi’ite- Sunni split created a permanent eastern front against Persia. Without territorial expansion and booty, janissaries turned to internal plunder.

The systemic failures were identical to Ming China’s: a centralized, despotic orthodoxy hostile to initiative and commerce. Merchants, nearly all foreigners, were subjected to unpredictable taxes and property seizure. The printing press was forbidden because it could disseminate dangerous opinions. Guilds were supported to check innovation. In 1580, janissaries destroyed a state observatory, alleging it had caused a plague. After the naval defeat at Lepanto, the Ottomans did not build larger European-style vessels. Their cannon remained heavy and immobile while European artillery grew lighter and deadlier with each generation.

“An idiot sultan could paralyze the Ottoman Empire in the way that a pope or Holy Roman Emperor could never do for all Europe.” — Kennedy

The Mughal Empire presented an even starker case. Despite brilliant courts and sophisticated banking, the system was a plunder machine at its core. Muslim rulers sat atop a vast poverty-stricken Hindu majority. Tax collectors preyed on peasants regardless of harvest — taxation was known as “eating.” The population received nothing in return: no famine relief, no infrastructure, no protection. Hindu religious taboos against killing rodents and insects worsened food losses, while the caste system throttled initiative. Kennedy’s verdict was blunt: “All this makes the Ming dynasty appear benign, almost progressive, by comparison.”

What connects these Muslim empires to Ming China is the structural argument: centralized authority that could suppress commerce, ban innovation, and extract wealth without reinvesting it will eventually fall behind competitors who cannot do these things. The Ottoman and Mughal empires did not lack intelligence, bravery, or resources. They lacked the competitive pressure that forced European states to innovate or die. When a single sultan or emperor could shut down an observatory or forbid a printing press, the entire civilization lost its capacity to adapt.

Kennedy draws an explicit parallel between Ottoman strategic overextension — armies simultaneously in Hungary, the Mediterranean, North Africa, and Persia — and the Habsburg overextension he will analyze in the next chapter. The mechanism is identical: a power that fights on too many fronts exhausts its resources before any single enemy can be decisively defeated. The difference is that Europe’s political fragmentation prevented any single state from suffering this fate permanently. In the Ottoman system, there was only one center of power, and when it failed, nothing could compensate.

The Muslim world’s decline was not inevitable. Under different leadership — a reform-minded sultan, a bureaucracy willing to adopt the printing press, a military establishment open to lighter artillery — the trajectory could have been different. But the very centralization that made these empires so formidable in their rise became the mechanism of their decline. Power concentrated in a single point is power that can be switched off from a single point.

Two Outsiders — Japan and Russia

Japan and Russia occupied peculiar positions in the sixteenth-century world — geographically peripheral, culturally distinct, and developing along paths that would only become globally significant centuries later. Japan was a feudal archipelago of warring clans where the emperor was a cipher and constant inter-clan feuding resembled Scottish highland strife. Its geography gave it one crucial advantage: insularity. No overland invasion force could reach it, a protection China could never enjoy.

Sixteenth-century Japan was paradoxically more open than Ming China. Japanese pirates raided Chinese and Korean coasts while other Japanese traded eagerly with the Portuguese and Dutch. Firearms concentrated power rapidly: the warlord Hideyoshi used imported European armaments to consolidate control and even attempted to conquer Korea twice before his death in 1598. The Tokugawa clan completed the consolidation, establishing a shogunate that would last two and a half centuries.

Then Japan made a choice that echoed Ming China. In 1636, construction of oceangoing vessels was stopped. Japanese were forbidden to sail the high seas. Trade with Europeans was restricted to a single Dutch ship permitted to dock at Deshima in Nagasaki harbor. All Christians, foreign and native, were murdered. The motive was not Confucian conservatism but raw political control — the Tokugawa shoguns saw foreigners, Christians, and feudal rivals as subversive threats. Feudal lords were required to spend half the year in the capital; their families were held as virtual hostages.

The result was two centuries of enforced stasis. Merchants and bankers grew wealthier but were never permitted social or political prominence, as they were in Italy, the Netherlands, and Britain. The military system ossified completely. When Commodore Perry’s “black ships” appeared in Tokyo Bay in 1853, Japan could only acquiesce. The Tokugawa isolation was broken not by internal evolution but by external force — a humiliation that would fuel Japan’s extraordinary modernization drive after the Meiji Restoration.

Russia followed a fundamentally different path. The Kingdom of Muscovy was geographically isolated but deeply influenced by its European inheritance, particularly through the Russian Orthodox Church. Western muskets and cannon solved Russia’s ancient vulnerability to Asian horsemen, enabling a “gunpowder empire” expansion of breathtaking scope. By 1556, Russian troops had reached the Caspian Sea. By 1638, explorers had reached the Pacific coast.

But westward expansion was blocked by Swedes and Poles who also possessed firearms. Russia borrowed enough from Europe to maintain military strength but firmly resisted Western social and political modernization. Foreigners in Russia were segregated from natives to prevent subversive influences. Ivan the Terrible purged restive nobles, but the systemic weaknesses remained: military absolutism, education monopolized by the Church, serfdom, and an unpredictable bureaucracy that discouraged commerce.

Unlike the Ming, Ottoman, or Mughal empires, czarist Russia would survive and eventually become a world power. Its vast territory provided strategic depth that no invasion could overcome. Its population, though poor and largely serf, could be mobilized in enormous numbers. Russia’s gunpowder advantage over steppe peoples gave it access to new manpower, land, and resources with each eastward push. But in 1500, few western Europeans knew more about Russian rulers than they did about the legendary Prester John.

The contrast between Japan and Russia illuminates Kennedy’s argument about geography and political choice. Japan chose isolation from a position of cultural sophistication and military competence — and paid for it with two centuries of technological stagnation. Russia chose partial engagement with Europe — borrowing military technology while resisting social change — and gradually accumulated the territorial and demographic mass that would make it a superpower. Both stories demonstrate that a nation’s trajectory is shaped not just by its resources but by the choices its leaders make about engagement with the wider world.

The “European Miracle”

Why did the scattered, relatively unsophisticated peoples of western Eurasia generate unstoppable economic and technological development to become the world’s dominant civilization? This is the central question of the book’s opening chapter, and Kennedy’s answer rests on a single structural fact: political fragmentation. Europe had always been politically divided, and this was not an accident. Its geography made unity virtually impossible.

There were no enormous plains where horsemen could impose dominion over vast populations, as in Central Asia. No great river valleys created masses of easily conquerable peasants, as the Yangtze and Ganges did. Instead, mountain ranges, forests, and peninsulas separated population centers into natural political units. The result was decentralized power — local kingdoms, marcher lordships, highland clans, lowland town confederations — and no single authority that could impose uniform policies across the continent.

This fragmentation, which looked like weakness compared to the great Asian empires, turned out to be Europe’s decisive advantage. Differentiated climates produced differentiated products — timber, grain, wine, wool, herrings — which drove bulk trade rather than luxury caravans. Many navigable rivers and surrounding seas enabled flourishing maritime commerce. Long-distance trade led naturally to bills of exchange, credit facilities, and international banking — a basic predictability of economic conditions that private traders had rarely enjoyed anywhere else in the world.

No central authority in Europe could suppress commercial development. A Rhineland baron who overtaxed travelers found trade routes going elsewhere, taking his revenues with them. A monarch who repudiated debts found it nearly impossible to raise loans when war threatened. Persecution of merchants — Jewish traders, Flemish textile workers, Huguenots — backfired spectacularly: they simply moved and took their expertise to competing states eager to receive them. Gradually, European regimes entered a symbiotic relationship with the market economy, providing domestic order and non-arbitrary legal systems in exchange for tax revenue.

This political pluralism generated a primitive arms race among city-states and then kingdoms. Italian city-states’ merchant-funded armies demanded value for money, driving innovation. Free-market competition forced condottieri to compete for contracts and artisans to improve weapons. Only in Europe did sustained competitive pressure drive constant improvement in gunpowder, artillery, barrel shapes, and mobile gun carriages. In China, Japan, and Mughal India, once a cannon monopoly had established central authority, the incentive to improve vanished.

A critical development preserved this pluralism. Within a quarter-century of the French invasion of Italy in 1494, Italian engineers discovered that raised earthworks inside city walls could absorb cannonballs. Sophisticated bastion fortifications with crossfire from muskets and cannon became nearly insuperable. This counter- fortification prevented any single European power from achieving decisive conquest — the gunpowder revolution that unified Japan and India was structurally blocked in Europe.

The final piece of the puzzle was the gunned sailing ship. Around 1400–1450, Chinese and Turkish naval power might have seemed the future of maritime development. What differed was Europe’s sustained ability to rationalize exploration and mobilize resources. The Portuguese systematically collected geographical data. Genoese merchant houses funded Atlantic ventures. The innovation of mounting bronze cannon on stoutly built, round-hulled, three-masted sailing vessels gave European warships a power that no Mediterranean galley or Arab dhow could match. As soon as the first Portuguese vessels rounded the Cape of Good Hope, a new era had begun — though its full consequences would take centuries to unfold.

Kennedy’s summary of Europe’s advantage is characteristically precise: it was structural, not accidental. The combination of economic laissez-faire, political and military pluralism, and intellectual liberty existed nowhere else. “Europe’s greatest advantage was that it had fewer disadvantages than the other civilizations.” None of this mix existed in Ming China, the Ottoman Empire, or any other society examined. They appeared to stand still while Europe advanced — not because Europeans were inherently superior, but because their political system made stagnation impossible and innovation mandatory.

The Habsburg Bid: Meaning and Chronology of the Struggle

By the sixteenth century, Europe’s internal competition was lifting the continent above all other regions — but which European state would dominate the rest remained an open question. For roughly 150 years after 1500, the Habsburg family threatened to become the predominant power in Europe. Through a spectacular series of dynastic marriages, Charles V inherited an empire stretching from Gibraltar to Hungary, from Sicily to Amsterdam — the largest agglomeration of territories since Charlemagne. He was Duke of Burgundy at fifteen, King of Spain at sixteen, Holy Roman Emperor at nineteen.

Two forces drove the intensified warfare that followed. The first was the Reformation — Martin Luther’s revolt against papal authority in 1517 fractured Christendom into a transnational religious struggle that divided Europe north from south and city middle classes from feudal orders. The second was the Habsburg dynastic combination itself, which alarmed every other major power. France saw itself encircled. German princes feared Charles could impose his will inside Germany. Even the popes opposed Habsburg accumulation.

The struggle played out in three great phases. The Italian Wars, culminating in the Treaty of Cateau-Cambrésis in 1559, saw France and Spain exhaust themselves in a contest neither could win. Francis I was actually captured at the Battle of Pavia in 1525, but the war continued for another three decades. The second phase centered on the Netherlands Revolt and the Spanish Armada period. Philip II’s religious intolerance and heavy taxation provoked Dutch resistance that Spain could never crush. The Armada’s failure in 1588 was a financial as well as naval disaster, costing ten million ducats.

The final and most devastating phase was the Thirty Years War (1618–1648), which began with a Bohemian Protestant revolt and escalated into a continental conflagration. Gustavus Adolphus of Sweden invaded Germany in 1630 with perhaps the best army in the world. Cardinal Richelieu of France intervened openly in 1635. Count-Duke Olivares of Spain planned a triple invasion of France, proclaiming: “ Either all is lost, or else Castile will be head of the world.” Portuguese revolt in 1640 and Catalan rebellion drained Spanish resources. Dutch forces struck at Brazil, Angola, and Ceylon — making this arguably the first global war.

“Henceforth, like the double-headed black eagle in the imperial coat of arms, the Habsburgs had two heads at Vienna and at Madrid, looking east and west.” — historian Mamatey

The Peace of Westphalia in 1648 and the Treaty of the Pyrenees in 1659 together confirmed that the age of Habsburg predominance was over. European plurality — five or six major states in rough equilibrium — was established as a permanent feature of the continent’s political landscape. The terms were not particularly harsh, but they revealed an irreversible truth: no single dynasty could unite Europe by force.

The Habsburg failure is Kennedy’s first great case study in imperial overstretch, and it establishes the template for everything that follows. Spain had the largest army, the richest colonies, the most feared infantry in Europe, and access to the silver mines of Peru. It should have won. That it didn’t — that 140 years of effort only confirmed the status quo — is the puzzle the next section explains.

What makes the Habsburg story particularly resonant is that the failure was not due to any single catastrophic defeat. There was no Waterloo, no Stalingrad. Spain simply bled out over fourteen decades, fighting everywhere and winning nowhere decisively. Each peace bought a few years of recovery before the next war began. Each war cost more than the last. The trajectory was downward from the start, but the decline was so gradual that Spanish statesmen never recognized the moment when retreat became the rational choice.

Strengths and Weaknesses of the Habsburg Bloc

The scale of Habsburg resources makes their failure all the more instructive. Roughly one-quarter of early modern Europe’s population lived in Habsburg territory. The empire drew revenue from five major sources: Castile, the most reliable direct tax base; the Italian states, the richest trading regions in Europe; the Low Countries, the second great commercial nexus; the American empire, with its “royal fifth” of silver and gold; and the great banking houses of southern Germany and Italy. In the postfeudal age, cash and credit were absolutely essential — ships, provisions, and mercenary wages all required direct payment in a market economy.

The military assets were equally impressive. The Spanish tercio — integrated regiments of up to 3,000 pikemen, swordsmen, and arquebusiers introduced by Gonzalo de Córdoba — was the most effective infantry formation in Europe from the early sixteenth century through the mid-Thirty Years War. At Nördlingen in 1634, Spanish tercios resisted fifteen charges by the Swedish army before advancing to crush the enemy. Even at Rocroi in 1643 — the battle traditionally seen as marking Spain’s military eclipse — the remaining tercios fought grimly when surrounded, breaking only because the ranks were now filled with German, Italian, and Irish mercenaries rather than Castilians.

Three factors combined to defeat these formidable resources. The first was the military revolution that transformed warfare between 1520 and 1660. Infantry supplanted cavalry as the decisive arm. Sophisticated bastion fortifications required enormous numbers of troops. Army sizes exploded: no state fielded more than 30,000 effectives before 1529; by 1625, Philip IV commanded 300,000 men. General inflation compounded the cost — food prices quintupled and industrial prices tripled between 1500 and 1630. The Habsburgs faced bankruptcy repeatedly: Philip II defaulted in 1557, 1575, and 1596. At his death in 1598, debts totaled 100 million ducats, with interest payments consuming two-thirds of all revenues.

“What other fate was due to a nation which, although providing formidable fighters, was directed by governments which consistently spent two or three times more than the ordinary revenues provided?” — Kennedy

The second factor was strategic overextension. The Habsburgs had too much to do, too many enemies, too many fronts. Forces were dispersed across Sicily, Italy, North Africa, the Netherlands, the New World, and Germany simultaneously. Kennedy’s analogy is vivid: “Spain resembled a large bear in the pit: more powerful than any of the dogs attacking it, but never able to deal with all of its opponents.” Unlike France, England, or even the Ottomans, Spain never enjoyed sustained periods of peace and recovery. It lurched from one enemy directly to the next, with adversaries consciously aiding each other diplomatically and commercially.

The Netherlands was the most ruinous front. Between 1566 and 1654, Spain sent at least 218 million ducats to the military treasury in the Low Countries — considerably more than the total crown receipts from the Indies over the same period. The Army of Flanders consumed roughly one-quarter of total Spanish government spending decade after decade. Yet abandoning the Netherlands was politically impossible: Spanish statesmen perceived an interconnected strategic whole where losing any one piece would trigger a domino collapse.

The third factor was Spain’s failure to mobilize resources efficiently. The Habsburg empire was a congeries of territories, each with its own privileges and no central administration. Taxpayers in Sicily paid for Mediterranean fleets but refused to fund Netherlands wars. The entire fiscal system rested on Castile — roughly six million peasants and merchants bearing the weight of a global empire. Even within Castile, Basque provinces were immune from key taxes. American silver provided only one-quarter to one-third of what Castile itself generated at peak.

Spain’s economic policies actively undermined its own power base. The Counter-Reformation and continuous warfare strengthened religious and military elements while weakening commercial ones. Jews and Moriscos were expelled, losing skilled communities. Universities closed contacts with foreign institutions. Shipyards were ordered to build only large warships, excluding more useful trading vessels. By 1640, three-quarters of goods in Spanish ports arrived in Dutch ships — enriching Spain’s most dangerous enemies. Interest-bearing government bonds diverted investment from trade and industry. By 1543, fully 65% of ordinary revenue went to bond interest alone.

“The flood of precious metals from the Indies was to Spain as water on a roof — it poured on and then was drained away.” — contemporary observer

The core failure was that Spain never recognized the importance of preserving the economic underpinnings of military power. The causes of decline existed for decades before they became visible in the 1640s. Habsburg Austria, by contrast, achieved a consolidation of its dynastic lands and eventually reemerged as a European great power in the later seventeenth century — precisely because it was forced to retrench rather than overextend.

International Comparisons

Habsburg failure was relative, not absolute. Every European state faced the military revolution’s challenge of financing ever-larger forces. What distinguished the anti-Habsburg powers was not immunity from fiscal strain but a slightly better balance between resources and commitments. France, with its population of sixteen million — twice Spain’s and four times England’s — had the raw material for dominance, but internal divisions and Richelieu’s desperate war finance left it nearly as exhausted as Spain by 1659. France and Spain by mid-century were “punch-drunk boxers,” near financial collapse, unable to finish each other off.

England under Elizabeth I demonstrated a different model. Elizabeth recognized her country’s limits with exceptional clarity. England could not match Spain or France directly, so she maintained independence through diplomacy and economical “cold war” at sea. She built a healthy surplus in her first twenty-five years, which she needed when war with Spain finally came. Naval commanders Hawkins, Raleigh, and Drake urged intercepting Spanish silver and raiding colonies. Elizabeth committed troops to the Netherlands not from love of Dutch rebels but because, as she argued, “whenever the last day of France came it would also be the eve of the destruction of England.”

Sweden offered a cautionary tale about the limits of military genius without economic depth. Foreign entrepreneurs, especially the Dutch industrialist Louis de Geer, transformed Sweden into Europe’s largest iron and copper producer. Gustavus Adolphus created a national army based on conscription, new tactics, and mobile light artillery. By 1630, he had perhaps the best army in the world. But Sweden’s war machine was parasitic — financed by “contributions” extracted from German states rather than from Sweden’s own meager economic base. Post-1648, its trans-Baltic possessions cost more to defend than the campaigns had produced.

“For a generation Sweden had been drunk with victory and bloated with booty: Charles XI led her back into the grey light of everyday existence, gave her policies appropriate to her resources.” — Professor Roberts

The Dutch Republic presented the starkest contrast of all. A tiny cluster of seven heterogeneous provinces with no natural-resource advantages became a Great Power through pure commercial-financial strength. Dutch military and naval dominance were consequences of this wealth, not its cause. Holland’s war costs rose from 960,000 florins in 1579 to ten million annually by the early seventeenth century, and the Dutch economy grew fast enough to make this burden bearable. The government could borrow more cheaply than any rival because it reliably repaid debts — a compounding advantage in every war.

Kennedy draws a crucial distinction between powers that built military strength on economic foundations and those that tried to sustain military strength despite economic weakness. The Dutch and English models worked because commercial prosperity generated the tax base that funded military expansion. The Spanish model failed because military spending consumed the economic base that should have sustained it. The Swedish model collapsed because military glory rested on plunder rather than production. These distinctions would replay across the next five centuries.

The international comparison also reveals that timing matters enormously. France could have dominated Europe if it had not been torn apart by religious civil war at precisely the moment Spain was weakening. England might have played a larger continental role if its Crown-Parliament conflict had not paralyzed the country during the Thirty Years War. Sweden might have sustained its military pre-eminence if its economy had been large enough to absorb the costs of garrison duty across the Baltic. In each case, internal political dynamics determined whether latent economic potential could be converted into effective military power.

The most important lesson from this comparative analysis is that no amount of battlefield brilliance can compensate for an inadequate economic base over the long term. Swedish generals were among the finest in Europe. Spanish infantry was unmatched for a century. Dutch naval tactics revolutionized maritime warfare. But in a prolonged struggle, the side that could sustain its forces financially outlasted the side that could not — even if the latter won more individual battles.

War, Money, and the Nation-State

The post-1450 birth of the nation-state was inseparable from the demands of modern warfare. Centralization of authority, expanded taxation, and elaborate bureaucratic machinery all arose primarily to fund armies. In Elizabeth I’s last years and Philip II’s Spain, up to three-quarters of all government expenditure went to war or debt repayment for past wars. War was not merely an activity of the state — it was the primary reason the modern state existed.

Military machines of the era were deeply inefficient. Ragtag troops, disloyal mercenaries, inadequate supply, unstandardized weapons, and rampant corruption were the norm, not the exception. The Army of Flanders mutinied forty-six times between 1572 and 1607. Gustavus Adolphus’s celebrated mobile campaigns in Germany were driven not by strategic vision but by the desperate need to find food and fodder for 150,000 men.

“History knows many more armies ruined by want and disorder than by the efforts of their enemies; and I have witnessed how all the enterprises which were embarked on in my day were lacking for that reason alone.” — Richelieu, Testament Politique

No state, however prosperous, could pay immediately for a prolonged war. The gap between income and expenditure was always closed by loans — from private bankers like the Fuggers or later from organized government bond markets. But spiraling costs repeatedly forced defaults and currency debasements, which in turn destroyed credit and made the next war even harder to finance. The pattern was vicious and self-reinforcing.

Kennedy’s central argument for this period is that the Habsburg failure was not absolute but relative. All states were under severe strain. Victory was a marginal, relative achievement. The anti-Habsburg coalition succeeded by slightly better balancing its material base against military power, recognizing — however imperfectly — that the trader, the manufacturer, and the farmer were as important as the cavalry officer and the pikeman. As the Duke of Wellington would later say of another close-run contest, it had been a “damned close-run thing.” Most great contests are.

This section establishes what will become the book’s recurring refrain: the relationship between wealth and power is not static but dynamic. A state that invests in its productive capacity builds the foundation for future military strength. A state that diverts too much to current military needs undermines the foundation on which that military rests. The challenge for every Great Power is finding the balance point — spending enough on defense to survive the present without mortgaging the future. Spain failed this test. The powers that replaced it would eventually face the same dilemma.

The Habsburg era also demonstrates that coalition warfare favors the economically stronger side over time. Individual battles can be won by tactical genius or sheer courage, but prolonged wars are won by the coalition that can keep its armies in the field, replace losses, and maintain political cohesion. This principle will hold true through every subsequent chapter of the book, from the Anglo-French wars of the eighteenth century to the World Wars of the twentieth.

Perhaps most importantly, this section shows that the relationship between domestic political structure and military effectiveness is intimate and inescapable. States with representative institutions — where merchants had a voice in policy and taxation was negotiated rather than imposed — proved more resilient than absolutist regimes. This was not because democracy is inherently more efficient, but because consultation created legitimacy, and legitimacy made taxation sustainable over the long term. The Dutch merchants who voluntarily funded their own defense could sustain costs that would have provoked revolt in Castile.

The “Financial Revolution”

Between Louis XIV’s assumption of power in 1661 and Waterloo in 1815, the older powers — the Ottoman Empire, Spain, the Netherlands, Sweden — fell permanently to second rank. Five major states emerged: France, the Habsburg Empire, Prussia, Britain, and Russia. The wars that determined their relative positions were wars of endurance, and endurance depended on credit. The seven major Anglo-French wars spanning 1689 to 1815 drove military costs from millions of pounds in the sixteenth century to tens of millions in the late seventeenth to occasionally one hundred million a year during the Napoleonic conflict.

The United Provinces led the way in public credit. Dutch merchants who were part of the government demanded the same financial rectitude as in a joint-stock company. The States General raised taxes efficiently and kept interest rates low, making long-term funded state debt seem perfectly normal. Amsterdam became the world’s financial capital: clearing bills, exchanging currency, providing credit to foreign governments. Amsterdam’s surplus capital flowed into foreign government bonds — the interest rate each country paid functioned as a market rating of its economic potential and creditworthiness.

But the Dutch model contained a trap. Steady returns from government loans shifted the economy toward rentier status, discouraging bankers from industrial risk capital. Excise duties needed to service the debt made wages uncompetitive. The nation that invented modern public finance was slowly consumed by its own success.

The critical comparison was between Britain and France. The older view — Britain as an industrial giant, France as a backward ancien régime — is an oversimplification. France had a larger population, greater agricultural revenues, and considerable armaments production. Its dirigiste regime seemed more coherent than Westminster party politics. Yet Britain held decisive financial advantages. British taxes were mostly “invisible” — excise on goods, customs duties — and authorized by an elected Parliament, giving them legitimacy. There were no internal tolls strangling domestic commerce. Light peacetime taxation preserved private savings for investment capital and created a vast reserve of taxable wealth for wartime.

“Credit was the principal advantage that England hath over France.” — Bishop Berkeley

The creation of the Bank of England in 1694 — initially a wartime expedient — enabled long-term loans raised efficiently with guaranteed interest repayment. The growth of the national debt, the stock exchange, and “country banks” expanded the money supply without severe inflation. After the South Sea Bubble of 1720, British financial administration remained “more honest, as well as more efficient, than that of any other in Europe” for the rest of the century. Even Dutch investors, traditionally suspicious of British policy, found London government bonds increasingly attractive.

France, by contrast, had no proper system of public finance. From the Middle Ages, financial operations were managed by a cluster of middlemen: municipal governments, clergy, provincial estates, tax farmers. Each collector took a cut before passing money upward. No national accounting existed until Necker’s reforms in the 1770s. French surplus capital flowed toward purchasing offices or annuities rather than investing in business. Every attempt to create a French national bank was resisted by those profiting from the existing system.

The consequences were stark. By the late 1780s, France’s national debt was similar to Britain’s in total size, but its annual interest payments were nearly double — fourteen million pounds versus seven million. Britain could borrow at 3%; France paid twice that. Calonne’s desperate attempt at tax reform triggered the Assembly of Notables, then the calling of the States General in 1789, then the collapse of the entire regime. The link between national bankruptcy and the French Revolution was direct.

Kennedy’s analysis of the financial revolution establishes a principle that will echo through every subsequent chapter: in prolonged great-power wars, the side that can borrow more cheaply, tax more broadly, and spend more sustainably will outlast its opponent — regardless of which side has the larger army or the more brilliant generals. Money is not everything in war, but without it, everything else eventually fails.

Geopolitics

Finance alone did not determine outcomes. Amsterdam was the world’s greatest financial center, yet the Dutch Republic declined as a Great Power. Russia was economically backward and capital-starved, yet grew steadily in European influence. The missing variable was geography — not climate or raw materials, but strategic location. Could a power concentrate on one front, or must it fight on several? Did it share borders with weak or powerful states? Was it a land power, a sea power, or a hybrid — and what did each posture cost?

The Dutch Republic illustrates the geographic trap. By the late seventeenth century, its geographic vulnerabilities had become decisive. Louis XIV’s France posed a more dangerous land threat than Spain had a century earlier. The Dutch were forced to expand their army to 93,000 by 1693 and garrison southern border fortresses, draining resources from the navy and from commerce. William III’s English alliance simultaneously saved the United Provinces and contributed to its decline as an independent power. Forced to devote 75% of defense spending to the army, the Dutch left maritime and colonial campaigns to Britain — which captured the commercial benefits.

France suffered from being a hybrid power, its energies perpetually divided between continental aims and maritime ambitions. Under Louis XIV, French strength rested on indigenous resources: a large homogeneous territory, agricultural self-sufficiency, and a population of twenty million. The army grew from 30,000 in 1659 to 350,000 by 1710. But France was massively strong defensively while hemmed in by geographic barriers and rival powers in every direction. Every war involving maritime powers split French energies between Flanders, Germany, and Italy on one hand, and the Channel, West Indies, Canada, and Indian Ocean on the other.

“The France of the ancien régime remained always the greatest of the European states; but it was not big enough or efficiently organized enough to be a ‘superpower,’ and, restricted on land and diverted by sea, it could not prevail against the coalition which its ambitions inevitably aroused.” — Kennedy

Britain’s strategic position was unique. Alfred Thayer Mahan argued that an island nation freed from land defense could concentrate all effort seaward — an inherent advantage over continental states. This was partly correct: the shift of trade routes from the Mediterranean to the Atlantic after 1500 hugely benefited an island on Europe’s western flank. The “virtuous triangle” of trade, colonies, navy, and more trade reinforced itself. But Britain could not afford to ignore the continent. A France dominant on land, as in 1806–1812, could deliver devastating blows to British manufacturing. Britain’s solution was a Janus-faced strategy: one face toward the Continent to balance power through subsidies and alliances, one face toward the sea to extend maritime dominance.

Russia and the fledgling United States shared a structural advantage that no European continental power could match: geographic invulnerability. Both expanded across vast frontiers but faced no militarily advanced societies threatening their home base. Both could challenge European powers while remaining insulated from the main battle zones. By the 1790s, observers were already predicting a great future role for both. De Tocqueville would later argue that Russia and America seemed “marked out by the will of Heaven to sway the destinies of half the globe.”

The key insight of this section is that finance and geography frequently interacted. A power’s geographic position determined how much of its finances went to unproductive frontier defense versus commercial expansion. The Dutch financial system could not compensate for the geographic squeeze between Britain and France. Russia — poor, geographically peripheral — grew in influence precisely because its vast territory reduced vulnerability to the kind of multi-front pressures that crippled France and the Netherlands. To explain outcomes in the wars of 1660–1815, neither finance nor geography alone suffices. Both must be analyzed together.

The Winning of Wars, 1660–1763

The century between the Peace of Westphalia and the Treaty of Paris saw the five-power system tested in a series of increasingly global conflicts. The pattern was consistent: coalitions formed around whichever power threatened to dominate, and the side with the deeper purse and better geographic position eventually prevailed. The most dramatic early demonstration was Sweden’s collapse. Charles XII failed to crush the Russian army despite brilliant campaigning. At the Battle of Poltava in July 1709, the Russian force was vastly superior in numbers and position. Sweden’s army was wiped out. Charles fled to Turkish exile. By the 1721 Peace of Nystad, Sweden had lost its Baltic provinces and fallen permanently to second-rank status.

The Seven Years War (1756–1763) was the century’s decisive contest and a textbook illustration of Kennedy’s thesis. The Franco-Austro-Russian coalition looked stronger on paper — decidedly bigger militarily. But the Anglo-Prussian combination possessed three decisive advantages: leadership, financial staying power, and military-naval expertise. Frederick the Great harnessed Prussia’s full energies with brilliant generalship, destroying French and Austrian armies at Rossbach and Leuthen. William Pitt the Elder executed a coherent grand strategy across all theaters simultaneously.

The financial dimension was decisive. At the war’s height, Britain maintained over 120 ships of the line, more than 200,000 soldiers on its pay lists, and subsidized Prussia — all while British overseas trade increased every year of the war. The Seven Years War cost the Exchequer over £160 million, of which £60 million was raised on money markets. By contrast, all other combatants’ economies suffered heavily.

“In the present state of Europe it is colonies, trade and in consequence sea power, which must determine the balance of power upon the continent. The House of Austria, Russia, the King of Prussia are only powers of the second rank, as are all those which cannot go to war unless subsidized by the trading powers.” — Choiseul, French minister

Admiral Anson directed a Royal Navy blockade of France’s Atlantic ports in all weathers, throttling French maritime trade, protecting British commerce, and critically preventing reinforcement of French troops in the West Indies, Canada, and India. The year 1759 became the annus mirabilis: French colonies fell into British hands globally while Anglo-German troops routed two French armies at Minden. The settlement of 1762–1763 confirmed Britain as the clear beneficiary, with gains in the West Indies, West Africa, India, and supremacy in North America.

Prussia survived the war only because Czarina Elizabeth died in 1762, and her successor immediately withdrew from the conflict. Frederick had lost 60,000 soldiers in 1759 alone and was near collapse. The lesson was double-edged: Prussian military genius could hold off a continent, but only with British financial support and a measure of extraordinary luck. When those props were removed, as they would be against Napoleon, Prussia fell in weeks.

France lost disastrously overseas and failed in Europe. Its “lackluster military performance suggested the center of gravity had shifted from western Europe to the east.” Evidence: France was simply disregarded during the first partition of Poland in 1772. The hybrid power’s inability to concentrate on either land or sea had produced failure in both domains.

The Winning of Wars, 1763–1815

The Napoleonic Wars presented the ultimate test of the relationship between economic power and military force. The strategic dilemma was stark: Britain was supreme at sea, France supreme on land, and neither could deliver a knockout blow. British sea control could not destroy French hegemony in Europe. Napoleon’s land mastery could not force Britain to surrender. The conflict became a war of attrition in which each side tried to strangle the other’s economy while maintaining its own.

Napoleon’s Continental System — the Berlin and Milan Decrees of 1806–1807 banning all British trade with Europe — was a logical strategy against an island nation dependent on commerce. Britain suffered real pain: the crises of 1808 and 1811–1812 piled up manufactured goods in warehouses, London docks overflowed, and unemployment rose. But the system never applied consistently enough to take full effect. Spain’s revolt in 1808 broke the blockade. Russia’s break with Napoleon in December 1810 reopened Baltic trade. Massive smuggling persisted from Heligoland to Salonika. New markets in Asia, Africa, the West Indies, and Latin America compensated for European losses. British total exports actually rose throughout the war: £21.7 million in 1794–96 to £44.4 million in 1814–16.

The reason the Continental System failed was that Britain was deep into the Industrial Revolution, which Napoleon could not match. War spending stimulated iron, steel, coal, and timber production. Pig-iron output surged from 68,000 tons in 1788 to 325,000 tons in 1811. Cotton, virtually a new industry before the war, became Britain’s greatest export by 1815. The fiscal capacity expanded with the economy: customs and excise revenue tripled; income and property taxes, introduced in 1799, produced £14.6 million by 1815. Over the entire period 1793–1815, Britain raised £1.217 billion in taxes and an additional £440 million in loans without exhausting its credit.

Napoleon’s war finance rested on a fundamentally different — and ultimately unsustainable — model: systematic plunder. The domaine extraordinaire confiscated property in conquered lands, stripped enemy museums and treasuries, imposed massive war indemnities, and required satellite states to quarter French regiments. Prussia paid 311 million francs after Jena — half of France’s ordinary annual revenue. In Italy between 1805 and 1812, about half of all taxes raised went to France. “War would support war” — the system worked brilliantly as long as Napoleon kept winning.

“My power depends on my glory and my glories on the victories I have won. My power will fail if I do not feed it on new glories and new victories. Conquest has made me what I am and only conquest can enable me to hold my position.” — Napoleon

The structural contradiction was inescapable: a nation proclaiming liberty, fraternity, and equality was conquering non-French populations, taxing them, conscripting their youth, and distorting their trade. The system depended on unbroken military success. Spain’s revolt in 1808 created Napoleon’s “Spanish ulcer” — 300,000 French troops pinned down by guerrilla warfare, unable to suppress resistance. The Russian campaign of 1812 was the fatal overreach: 600,000 men entered Russia, of whom only 270,000 were French. Roughly 270,000 were killed and 200,000 captured.

The endgame demonstrated the iron logic of coalition warfare backed by economic superiority. At Leipzig in October 1813, 195,000 French were overwhelmed by 365,000 allied troops, the coalition economically underpinned by British subsidies of £11 million that year alone, plus 125,000 muskets and 218 artillery pieces shipped from Britain. The Vienna settlement surrounded France with strong buffer states and established the principle of “containment and reciprocal compensation.”

Post-1815, Britain held a near-monopoly of naval power reinforced by its economic lead. As Gneisenau, the Prussian general, observed with grudging admiration: “Great Britain has no greater obligation than to this ruffian. For through the events which he has brought about, England’s greatness, prosperity, and wealth have risen high. She is mistress of the sea and neither in this dominion nor in world trade has she now a single rival to fear.”

The Napoleonic Wars confirmed every major theme of Kennedy’s argument. Plunder-based finance cannot sustain prolonged warfare against a commercially productive enemy. Geographic overextension — fighting in Spain, Russia, Germany, and against the Royal Navy simultaneously — guarantees exhaustion. Coalition warfare favors the side with the deeper economic reserves. And the lag between economic and military power works both ways: Napoleon’s empire looked invincible for a decade, but its economic foundations were rotting from the start.

The Eclipse of the Non-European World

The Industrial Revolution transformed the relationship between Europe and the rest of the world from one of rough parity to one of overwhelming dominance. The core change was the substitution of inanimate for animate sources of power — heat converted into work through machines that were “rapid, regular, precise, tireless.” A power-driven loom produced twenty times the output of a hand worker. A spinning “mule” had two hundred times the capacity of a spinning wheel. A single railway engine replaced hundreds of packhorses.

For the first time in history, productivity gains were self-sustaining. Previous economic improvements had always been consumed by population growth — the Malthusian trap that had governed human civilization for millennia. Now both the economy and technological knowledge were growing fast enough to generate a continuing flow of investment and innovation that lifted beyond visible limits the ceiling on human welfare. In Britain, population quadrupled between 1801 and 1911 while national product rose fourteenfold. Real wages increased 80% in the second half of the nineteenth century alone.

The impact on the non-European world was devastating. Kennedy presents two tables that tell the story with brutal clarity. In 1750, China held 32.8% of world manufacturing output and India held 24.5% — together accounting for well over half the global total. By 1900, China’s share had fallen to 6.2% and India’s to 1.7%. Meanwhile, Europe’s share rose from 23.2% to 62.0%, and the United States surged from 0.1% to 23.6%. Per capita industrialization in the Third World actually declined — from 7 in 1750 to 2 in 1900, on an index where the UK in 1900 equaled 100.

India suffered absolute deindustrialization. After the East India Company’s trade monopoly ended in 1813, British cotton fabric imports into India exploded from one million yards in 1814 to 995 million yards by 1870, destroying traditional domestic producers. Large population growth in China and India reduced per capita income from one generation to the next. The firepower revolution — breechloaders, Gatling guns, Maxims, and steam-driven ironclads — made indigenous resistance in open terrain nearly impossible. At the Battle of Omdurman in 1898, Kitchener’s forces destroyed 11,000 Dervishes at the cost of only 48 British troops.

Europeans occupied or controlled 35% of the world’s land surface in 1800, 67% by 1878, and over 84% by 1914. By the end of the century, the leading nations possessed resources fifty or a hundred times greater than those at the bottom. The global dominance of the West, implicit since da Gama’s day, now knew few limits. Kennedy’s numbers make the argument inescapable: the Industrial Revolution did not merely give Europe an advantage. It created a chasm that would take the rest of the world a century or more to begin closing.

Britain as Hegemon?

Britain entered 1815 already globally preeminent through naval mastery, financial credit, commercial expertise, and alliance diplomacy. The Industrial Revolution enhanced an already supremely successful state, turning it into a qualitatively different kind of power. Between 1760 and 1830, the UK was responsible for roughly two-thirds of Europe’s industrial growth. Its share of world manufacturing output rose from 1.9% in 1750 to 9.5% in 1800 to a peak of 19.9% in 1860 — achieved despite the spread of industry to other countries.

At its zenith around 1860, Britain produced 53% of the world’s iron and 50% of its coal and lignite. It consumed nearly half the world’s raw cotton output. With just 2% of world population and 10% of Europe’s, the UK possessed industrial capacity equal to 40–45% of world potential. Energy consumption was five times that of the United States or Prussia, six times France’s, and 155 times Russia’s. Britain was responsible for one-fifth of world commerce but two-fifths of trade in manufactured goods, and more than a third of the world’s merchant marine flew the British flag.

“The plains of North America and Russia are our corn fields; Chicago and Odessa our granaries; Canada and the Baltic are our timber forests… Peru sends her silver, and the gold of South Africa and Australia flows to London.” — Jevons, economist, 1865

Yet Britain was not a traditional hegemon. Laissez-faire ideology, drawing on Adam Smith and especially Richard Cobden’s disciples, treated armed forces as “unproductive” expenditure to be minimized. Post-1815 demilitarization was extreme: defense spending consumed only 2–3% of GNP for fifty years. Central government expenditure stayed below 10% of GNP. Mercantilist protections were dismantled, tariffs abolished, Navigation Acts repealed. Britain’s army was tiny compared to continental forces — effectively the least mobilized for war of any time since the early Stuarts.

Britain’s real strength lay in three domains. First, naval supremacy: after Trafalgar, no country or combination seriously challenged the Royal Navy, which was at times equal in fighting power to the next three or four navies combined. Second, colonial empire expanding in a power-political vacuum — growing at roughly 100,000 square miles per year between 1815 and 1865, with no serious European rival in most of the tropics. Third, financial hegemony: capital exports surged from £6 million per year after Waterloo to £75 million per year by the 1870s, largely reinvested overseas in a virtuous upward spiral. London became the center of the gold standard and international payments.

Kennedy identifies two hidden structural dangers invisible to mid-Victorians. First, Britain’s capital exports were financing the very industries and railways that would allow rivals — the United States, Germany, Russia — to exploit their vast natural resources and eventually overtake Britain. Industrialization equalized the ability to exploit indigenous resources, favoring large land-based states over smaller peripheral naval-commercial ones. Second, increasing dependence on international trade and finance made Britain uniquely vulnerable in wartime. Exports represented one-fifth of national income by mid-century. In a major war, export markets could collapse, food and raw material imports could be cut off, and the London-based financial system could freeze.

Like all hegemons at the peak, the British believed their position was both natural and destined to continue. Bernard Porter’s metaphor captured the reality: Britain was “the first frogspawn egg to grow legs, the first tadpole to change into a frog, the first frog to hop out of the pond” — ahead of all others, but not immune to being caught. The rude shock was still some way in the future, but the structural foundations of relative decline were already being laid.

The “Middle Powers”

The half-century after Napoleon’s fall saw the great European powers in an unusual equilibrium. No state sought continental hegemony. The Concert of Europe managed disputes through collective diplomacy. Economic and technological change had far less dramatic impact on continental Europe than on Britain — two decades of Napoleonic war had left lasting damage, and the pace of industrialization was glacial compared to the British Midlands.

Prussia was the least of the Great Powers, greatly augmented territorially by the acquisition of the Rhineland but seemingly less impressive than under Frederick the Great. Early nineteenth-century Prussia produced only 50,000 tons of iron annually, eclipsed by Britain, France, Russia, and the Habsburg Empire. The humiliation at Oelmütz in 1850, where Prussia demobilized its army and abandoned unification schemes to avoid war with Austria backed by Russia, confirmed its second-tier status. But hidden strengths were accumulating: the best educational system in Europe, an efficient administration, and a formidable general staff studying the military implications of railways and rifles.

The Habsburg Empire served as the central fulcrum of the 1815 balance, checking French ambitions in western Europe, preserving the German status quo, and blocking Russian Balkan penetration. Its staying power was extraordinary — surviving the Reformation, the Turks, the French Revolution, and the revolutions of 1848–1849. But in an age of national consciousness, Austria was an anachronism. Every other Great Power had an ethnic or religious majority providing cohesion. The Austrian Emperor ruled eight million Germans, sixteen million Slavs, five million Hungarians, five million Italians, and two million Romanians. “What sort of nation did that make? The answer is none at all.”

France was significantly stronger than either Prussia or the Habsburg Empire but remained a “middle power” because strategic, diplomatic, and economic circumstances prevented it from gaining a decisive lead. The 1815 containment system blocked every traditional French expansion route. Population growth was slower than any other Great Power throughout the century. British manufacturing output, level with France’s at the century’s start, reached 251% of France’s by 1860. France remained a classic hybrid power, torn between European and colonial interests, checking all other powers but dominating none.

Russia appeared the most powerful of all: an 800,000-man army after 1815, the self-appointed “gendarme of Europe.” But the appearance concealed a deepening relative decline. Russia’s total GNP grew, but overwhelmingly through population increase rather than productivity growth. Per capita GNP fell from roughly half of Britain’s in 1830 to roughly one-quarter by 1890. Iron production doubled in the early nineteenth century; Britain’s increased thirtyfold. By 1850, Russia had only 500 miles of railroad versus the United States’ 8,500.

The military strength concealed the economic weakness — until it didn’t. The Crimean War would expose the gap with devastating clarity, revealing that the gendarme of Europe was built on a foundation of serfdom, inadequate transport, and a fiscal system that could not sustain modern warfare against industrialized opponents.

The Crimean War and the Erosion of Russian Power

The Crimean War of 1854–1855 was the moment when Russia’s apparent strength collided with its actual weakness. On paper, the Russian army was the largest in Europe, and Czar Nicholas I had used it with devastating effect to crush the Hungarian revolution of 1849. But the war against Britain and France exposed every structural flaw in the Russian system with merciless precision.

The technological gap was stark. Russian flintlock muskets had an effective range of 200 yards. Allied rifles were accurate at 1,000 yards. The disparity in casualties was inevitable and enormous. Russia had no railways south of Moscow: supply wagons crossed hundreds of miles of steppe, taking three months to reach the Crimea. Allied troops reached the theater by sea from France and England in three weeks. Equipment stocks collapsed: Russia began the war with one million guns stockpiled; by the end of 1855, only 90,000 remained. Powder and shot were even worse.

The fiscal dimension sealed Russia’s fate. Military expenditure rose from 220 million rubles in 1853 to roughly 500 million in 1854–55. The ruble’s international value collapsed. Printed paper money caused large-scale inflation and peasant unrest. In January 1856, the Crown Council delivered its verdict: if Russia continued, the state would go bankrupt. Russia was forced to negotiate a humiliating peace.

The defeat drove radical reforms under Alexander II: abolition of serfdom, railway construction, industrial development. But Kennedy poses the critical question: could this modernization keep pace with the explosive industrial growth in the Ruhr, Silesia, and Pittsburgh? Could it match the Prussian “military revolution” that emphasized qualitative over quantitative national strength? The answer, as subsequent chapters would show, was no. Russia’s European standing was substantially reduced from the eminence it had held in 1815 and 1848, and every future exposure to modern warfare — the Russo-Japanese War, World War I — would reveal the same gap between mass and efficiency.

The United States and the Civil War

By April 1861, the United States had already surpassed Germany and Russia in world manufacturing output and was close to overtaking France. The numbers reveal a stunning asymmetry: American iron production stood at 830,000 tons versus Russia’s 350,000. Energy from modern fuel was fifteen times greater. Railway mileage was thirty times larger. Yet the U.S. army numbered only 26,000 men versus Russia’s 862,000. The gap between economic and military indices of the two continent-sized states was wider at that moment than at any other.

The Civil War was, Kennedy argues, the first real industrialized “total war” on proto-twentieth-century lines. Both sides committed to fight to the finish across vast distances. The front stretched from Virginia to the Mississippi and beyond. The Union naval blockade covered a coastline as long as Hamburg to Genoa.

The North won through overwhelming economic superiority. The numbers were crushing: 110,000 manufacturing establishments in the North versus 18,000 in the South. Pennsylvania’s pig iron alone — 580,000 tons — dwarfed the Confederacy’s total of 36,700 tons. The North produced roughly 1.7 million rifles during the war. The Union Navy grew to 671 warships by December 1864, of which 236 had been built since the war’s start.

The Confederacy’s financial collapse was as decisive as its industrial weakness. The South’s peacetime income came from cotton exports, which the blockade cut off. Few banks, little liquid capital, and negligible taxable wealth forced the Confederate treasury to resort to the printing press, producing rampant inflation that undermined public will to continue. The North, by contrast, could raise money through taxation and loans. Northern agricultural output surged during the war alongside munitions and railway-building. By war’s end, Union soldiers were probably the best-fed army in history.

Professor Weigley’s phrase captures what was forged here: the “American way of war” — mobilizing and deploying massive industrial-technological potential to crush the enemy. This pattern would replay in both World Wars and define American military strategy for the next century and a half.

The Wars of German Unification

The Crimean War had undermined the Concert of Europe. Russia needed years to recover. Britain retreated to imperial and domestic concerns. Europe was left dominated, artificially, by Napoleon III’s France. Into this vacuum stepped Prussia, with a military revolution that would transform the continent.

The Prussian system rested on three interlocking innovations. First, a short-service system — three years in the regular army plus four in the reserve plus Landwehr duty — which produced a far larger front-line force relative to population than any other Great Power. Second, the General Staff under Helmuth von Moltke, which recruited the brightest academy graduates to plan future conflicts in peacetime, supervised railway deployment, and trained officers to act independently within broad operational guidelines. Third, a learning culture that studied its own mistakes: artillery weakness exposed in 1866 led to adoption of the Krupp breechloader that proved decisive in 1870.

Bismarck’s diplomacy ensured no Great Power intervened while Moltke dispatched three armies to converge on the Austrian position in Bohemia. The Austro-Prussian War of 1866 was over in seven weeks. The Franco-Prussian War of 1870–71 appeared more evenly matched on paper — France had a larger population, an experienced army, the best rifle in the world, and a secret weapon, the mitrailleuse. But French mobilization was chaotic. Within fifteen days, three German armies totaling 300,000 men were advancing. At Sedan in September 1870, the French army surrendered and Napoleon III was taken prisoner.

“The military system of a nation is not an independent section of the social system but an aspect of it in its entirety.” — Michael Howard

Behind the Prussian victory lay a society better equipped for modern warfare. The German states combined had a larger population than France. They had more railway miles, better arranged for military purposes. Coal output was two and a half times France’s. The Krupp steel and armaments combine symbolized the integration of industry and military power. High levels of primary and technical education, an unrivaled university and scientific establishment, and a bureaucratic culture that prized efficiency — all gave Germany advantages that France’s elan could not overcome.

“Europe had lost a mistress and gained a master.” Bismarck’s Germany dominated European affairs for two decades, but Kennedy labels it a “half-hegemonic power” — dominant but constrained from further territorial aggression. The question was whether post-Bismarckian leadership would respect those constraints. The answer, as the next chapter reveals, was no.

The Shifting Balance of World Forces

By the winter of 1884–1885, when the Great Powers met in Berlin to carve up West Africa, the old European order was at its zenith. Japan was not even invited. The United States attended but remained off the main international stage. Russian General Dragimirov declared that “Far Eastern affairs are decided in Europe.” Within three decades, Europe would be tearing itself apart, and within sixty years, its future would be decided in Washington and Moscow.

Many acute observers by 1895–1900 sensed the direction of change. Lord Salisbury admitted the world was dividing into “living” and “dying” powers. Joseph Chamberlain declared that “the tendency of the time is to throw all power into the hands of the greater empires.” Admiral Tirpitz urged the Kaiser to build a navy so Germany would be one of “four World Powers: Russia, England, America and Germany.” Leo Amery cut to the heart of the matter: “The successful powers will be those who have the greatest industrial base.”

The statistical picture told the story with devastating clarity. Britain’s share of world manufacturing output fell from 22.9% in 1880 to 13.6% by 1913 — still large in absolute terms but shrinking rapidly in relative weight. The United States surged from 14.7% to 23.6%. Germany grew from 8.5% to 14.8%. Russia, despite its backwardness, maintained 7.6% to 8.2%. Japan rose from virtually nothing to a notable 1.2%. The “Vasco da Gama era” of four centuries of European world dominance was ending even before 1914.

Technology diffusion accelerated the convergence. Within five years of the 1879 invention of basic steel processing, eighty-four converters were operating across Europe, and the process had crossed the Atlantic. Kennedy’s key caveat: military potential does not equal military power. An economic giant may choose to be a military pygmy. A poor state can organize society to be a formidable military force. But in the era of industrialized warfare, the link between economics and strategy was tightening inexorably.

The Position of the Powers, 1885–1914

Kennedy surveys each Great Power through the lens of Correlli Barnett’s framework: national power consists not only in armed forces but in economic and technological resources, the quality of foreign policy, the efficiency of social and political organization, and above all in the people themselves — their skills, energy, discipline, beliefs, myths, and illusions.

Italy was the illusion of Great Power status made flesh. United Italy had thirty million people and a respectable army and navy on paper, but an illiteracy rate of 37.6%, no coal, and a total industrial base comparable to peasant Spain. World manufacturing share actually dropped from 2.5% to 2.4% between 1900 and 1913. Every other Great Power had two to three times Italy’s industrial muscle; Germany and Britain had six times; the United States had over thirteen times. Italian national loyalties were familial, local, and regional — never genuinely national. Military disasters confirmed the gap: Adowa in 1896, where Italy became the only European army defeated by an African society in that era, and Caporetto in 1917.

Japan represented the opposite trajectory: a feudal society transformed in a single generation by sheer force of will. The Meiji Restoration from 1868 was state-driven modernization at a pace that made Colbert and Frederick the Great look leisurely. The national slogan was fukoku kyohei — “rich country, strong army” — economic and military power deliberately fused. The Russo-Japanese War of 1904–05, with Admiral Togo’s decisive victory at Tsushima, announced Japan as a Great Power. But the victory came against a backward enemy disadvantaged by immense distance, with financing from American and British loans. Japan was near bankruptcy by war’s end.

Germany was the most consequential of the newcomers. By 1914 its population was 66 million, its steel output 17.6 million tons — more than Britain, France, and Russia combined. Siemens and AEG dominated European electrical industry. Bayer and Hoechst produced 90% of the world’s industrial dyes. Illiteracy among German army recruits was one per thousand. But Germany was, in one scholar’s phrase, “born encircled”. Rising at the center of Europe meant growth simultaneously threatened multiple Great Powers. Every overseas expansion conflicted with existing spheres.

Austria-Hungary held only 4.5% of world manufacturing, yet tried to act as a Great Power. Its mobilization order in 1914 was issued in fifteen different languages. The heart of the matter was that Austria-Hungary was trying to act the part of a great power with the resources of a second-rank one. France had industrial potential only 40% of Germany’s but rebuilt its diplomatic position brilliantly through alliances with Russia and Britain. Russia was the fourth-largest industrial power but with per capita industrialization less than one-quarter of Germany’s and 80% of its population in agriculture. Stolypin boasted in 1909: “Give the state twenty years of peace and you will not recognize Russia.” But the war came too early.

The United States dwarfed everyone. National income was $37 billion — triple Germany’s, six times France’s, five times Russia’s. Coal output exceeded Britain’s. Pig-iron production was larger than Germany, Britain, and France combined. Andrew Carnegie was producing more steel than all of England when he sold out to J.P. Morgan’s U.S. Steel Corporation. Yet America spent less than 1% of GNP on defense and maintained an army smaller than Serbia’s. Herbert Spencer’s distinction applied perfectly: the United States was an “industrial” society while Russia was a “military” society — and the industrial society was six times more productive.

Alliances and the Drift to War, 1890–1914

The third element in understanding how the Great Power system changed was what Felix Gilbert called the “rigidification” of alliance blocs — fixed military alliances in peacetime, rarely seen before Bismarck. By the 1890s, most governments assumed the next great war would be fought as coalition warfare. Bismarck’s elaborate treaty architecture — the Austro-German Alliance of 1879, the Triple Alliance of 1882, the secret Reinsurance Treaty with Russia — was designed to keep all options open and all potential enemies off-balance. After Bismarck’s dismissal in 1890, his successors let the Reinsurance Treaty lapse, pushing Russia toward France.

The Franco-Russian Dual Alliance of 1894 created the essential counterweight. France wanted Alsace-Lorraine back; Russia wanted eastern expansion. Both were deterred by Germany. No other partner was available. The ideological gulf — bourgeois France and czarist Russia — was bridged by mutual fear. The Entente Cordiale of 1904 resolved Anglo-French colonial differences. The Anglo-Russian entente of 1907 eliminated Asian quarrels. By 1907, Germany genuinely faced “encirclement” — though Kennedy argues this was largely self-inflicted through Weltpolitik, the naval buildup, and chaotic diplomacy.

The war plans looked like dominoes. Germany’s Schlieffen Plan required a massive assault on France through Belgium regardless of the specific cause of war. France’s Plan XVII called for a headlong rush into Alsace-Lorraine. Russia pledged ever-swifter mobilization westward. All general staffs assumed speed was of the essence — once crisis seemed likely, mobilization had to begin immediately, and diplomats would lose control. Both at sea and on land, a swift victory was technically impossible — yet every plan assumed one.

The July 1914 crisis cascaded exactly as the structure predicted. Austria’s ultimatum to Serbia triggered Russian mobilization. The Schlieffen Plan activated automatically. Britain entered when Germany invaded Belgium. Coalition structure guaranteed what individual rationality would have prevented: a continental war that no single government wanted and none could stop.

Total War and the Power Balances, 1914–1918

The fundamental question of the war was simple: could the Allies’ economic superiority overcome the Central Powers’ military and geographic advantages before exhaustion set in? Germany and Austria-Hungary operated on efficient internal lines with superior industrial technology. Franco-Russian forces were larger but less coordinated and industrially weaker. Britain’s entry tilted the balance, bringing naval blockade, global financial resources, and eventually dominion and Indian manpower — but the war was going to be long.

Trench warfare produced a stalemate that no amount of “big pushes” could break. Quick- firing artillery and machine guns made offensive warfare suicidal. Defenders could always mobilize reserves faster than attackers could exploit a breach. Four miles deep was the typical depth of enemy-held defenses, and no army knew how to get through without catastrophic casualties. The Somme cost 400,000 British casualties. Passchendaele cost another 300,000. Neither achieved strategic results.

Russia’s collapse was the war’s first decisive turn. Isolated from its allies, unable to secure meaningful economic or military aid, Russia used up ammunition stocks ten times faster than prewar estimates. Its transport system could not simultaneously move shells to the front, food to the cities, and Allied supplies from Murmansk. The czarist regime abolished the spirits trade — losing a third of its revenue — refused to raise income taxes on the wealthy, and turned to the printing press. By 1917, casualties totaled 3.6 million dead, sick, or wounded plus 2.1 million captured. The army was “a huge, weary, shabby, and ill-fed mob of angry men united by their common thirst for peace.”

American entry in April 1917 transformed the equation. U.S. military significance was minimal for twelve to fifteen months — the army was less prepared than any European force in 1914. But American productive strength was unmatched: two and a half times Germany’s industrial output. The United States could launch merchant ships in hundreds, build destroyers in three months, and produce half the world’s food exports. With the U.S. added and Russia subtracted, the Allied bloc held 51.7% of world manufacturing versus the Central Powers’ 19.2%.

Ludendorff’s spring 1918 offensives were Germany’s final gamble — mobilize everything remaining for one decisive western strike before 300,000 American troops per month made the odds impossible. The initial successes were spectacular: the British Fifth Army was crushed, German forces drove within thirty-seven miles of Paris. But Germany had overextended. It inflicted 240,000 British and 92,000 French casualties in the first two blows — but lost 348,000 of its own. By October, only 2.5 million Germans remained in the west, facing Allied forces that were now superior in men, artillery, tanks, and aircraft. From mid-July, Foch orchestrated relentless offensives that broke the German position.

The war’s statistics confirmed Kennedy’s thesis with mathematical precision. Total Allied expenditure: $57.7 billion. Total Central Powers expenditure: $24.7 billion. Allied forces mobilized: 40.7 million. Central Powers: 25.1 million. Germany’s advantages — inner lines, army quality, territorial occupation, Russia’s isolation — could not over the long run outweigh the Allies’ massive superiority in economic muscle. As in every previous coalition war, the side with more productive resources prevailed.

The Postwar International Order

The human cost of the First World War defied comprehension: roughly eight million combat dead, seven million permanently disabled, fifteen million seriously wounded, and an estimated sixty million total casualties when civilians were included. Material destruction reached approximately $260 billion — about six and a half times the sum of all national debt accumulated in the world from the end of the eighteenth century to the war’s eve. European industrial output remained 7% below 1913 levels as late as 1920. Russian industrial output stood at 13% of its prewar figure.

The Paris settlement of 1919 created a cluster of new nation-states — Poland, Czechoslovakia, Austria, Hungary, Yugoslavia, Finland, and the Baltic republics — replacing three empires. Germany lost Alsace-Lorraine, its colonies, its military, and was saddled with enormous reparations. But the deeper problem was structural: the three rising powers predicted since the 1890s — Germany, Russia, and the United States — all withdrew from the center. Germany was defeated. Russia collapsed into Bolshevik isolation. America retreated into isolationism. The 1920s were dominated by France and Britain — both badly hurt by the war — in a “curiously artificial” Euro-centered world.

The stability was illusory. Financial chaos — interlocking war debts, inflated currencies, the absence of a global lender of last resort — created a system that functioned only as long as American credit flowed freely. When the Wall Street Crash of October 1929 triggered a chain reaction, the entire structure collapsed. By summer 1932, industrial production in many countries had fallen to half of 1928 levels. World trade shrank by one-third. The world economy dissolved into rival currency blocs, and democracies were in no frame of mind to cooperate in handling territorial revisionism.

At the heart of the postwar instability lay the German question, unresolved and intensified. Germany in 1919 was still potentially an immense Great Power: larger population than France, iron-and-steel capacity three times France’s, internal communications and industries intact. Very few Germans were not revisionists in some form; the only question was timing and method. Hitler’s rise in 1933 merely intensified an existing drive.

The Challengers

Kennedy examines three “challenger” states — Italy, Japan, and Germany — each dissatisfied with the postwar order and each pursuing aggressive revisionism by the 1930s. What distinguished them from the status quo powers was not just ideology but the mismatch between ambition and economic capacity.

Mussolini’s Italy achieved remarkable diplomatic prominence: guarantor of the Locarno agreement, signatory of the Munich settlement, conqueror of Abyssinia. But behind the facade lay a semideveloped economy with only 2.8% of world manufacturing by 1938. Italy was structurally dependent on imported coal, oil, rubber, and copper — 80% of which passed through Gibraltar or Suez, much of it carried in British ships. Its main battle tank weighed 3.5 tons with two machine guns and no radio. Its air force claimed 8,500 planes; investigation found only 454 bombers and 129 fighters of first-rate quality.

“Rarely in the history of human conflict has it been argued that the entry of an additional foe would hurt one’s enemy more than oneself; but Mussolini’s Italy was, in that way at least, unique.” — Kennedy

Japan was a far more formidable challenger. Its manufacturing production index soared from 176 in 1920 to 552 by 1938, a growth rate second only to the Soviet Union. The Imperial Japanese Navy trained intensively for day and night fighting. Army officers embodied the bushido spirit — soldiers literally fought to the last man while others merely talked of it. But Japan was nearly totally reliant on petroleum imports, and finance minister Takahashi was assassinated by militarists when he sought to moderate military spending.

The arithmetic of a war with the United States was terrifying: the U.S. had nearly twice Japan’s population, seventeen times its national income, five times as much steel, and eighty times as many motor vehicles. Japan’s navy calculated its warship strength would fall from 70% of the U.S. Navy in 1941 to 30% by 1944. This gave Japan a powerful incentive to strike before the window closed — and a virtual guarantee of defeat if the war lasted more than two years.

Nazi Germany was by far the most dangerous challenger. By 1938, 52% of government expenditure and 17% of GNP flowed into armaments — more than Britain, France, and the United States combined. But Germany held only 1% of world gold and financial reserves. Raw material dependence was acute: rich only in coal, Germany needed vast imports of iron ore, copper, petroleum, and rubber. The economic strain created what Tim Mason called “the dreadful logic” of German development under National Socialism: the only solution to the tensions produced by dictatorship and rearmament was more dictatorship and more rearmament. “A war for the plunder of manpower and materials lay square in the dreadful logic.”

The Offstage Superpowers

While the challengers overreached, the two future superpowers stood offstage, their true potential concealed by ideology and isolation. The Soviet Union was reduced to 13% of its 1913 industrial output by 1920. Stalin’s response was forced collectivization — driving private consumption down to 51–52% of GNP, lower than even the Nazis achieved. The human cost was measured in millions: horses fell from 33.5 million to 16.6 million between 1928 and 1935; the 1933 famine killed uncounted masses. But by the late 1930s, Soviet industrial output had surpassed France, Japan, and Italy, and probably overtaken Britain. Coal production reached 128 million tons. Steel reached 17.7 million tons. Graduate engineers rose from 47,000 to 289,900. Enormous new factories were built beyond the Urals, invulnerable to Western attack.

Then Stalin’s purges eliminated 90% of all generals and 80% of colonels. Tukhachevsky and the modern warfare enthusiasts were wiped out. The USSR was actually weaker militarily at the end of the 1930s than five years earlier, even as Germany and Japan grew more threatening. The Red Army had badly deteriorated, as the catastrophic 1939–40 Winter War against Finland revealed.

The United States presented the mirror image: overwhelming economic potential squandered by political isolation and economic self-destruction. In the 1920s, America produced a larger output than the other six Great Powers taken together. Then the Depression struck. GNP plummeted from $98.4 billion in 1929 to barely half that. The U.S. share of world manufacturing fell from 43.3% to 28.7% by 1938. Fifteen million were unemployed.

Yet even at its Depression nadir, the United States held 41.7% of world war potential while spending only 1.5% on defense. Two-thirds of American steel plants were idle — other powers were running at full capacity. The sleeping giant was not structurally weak; it was merely asleep. Both Berlin and Tokyo understood this arithmetic perfectly, which gave them powerful incentives to strike before the giant awakened. Japan’s navy calculated that its warship strength relative to the U.S. would decline steadily from 70% in 1941 to 30% by 1944. The window was closing.

The Unfolding Crisis, 1931–1942

The 1930s unfolded as a sequence of crises that the status quo powers were structurally unable to meet. Japan’s seizure of Manchuria in 1931 succeeded precisely because no Great Power could act — Britain was in economic crisis, France feared diverting resources from the German problem, the U.S. was consumed by Depression. The League of Nations was shown to be impotent. Hitler watched Japan’s revisionism succeed as a useful precedent.

Britain’s strategic dilemma was acute. Japan was the more immediate danger; Germany was the greater long-term threat. It was impossible to be strong in both theaters simultaneously. Appeasement was rational given strategic overstretch — the chiefs of staff warned it was impossible to safeguard interests “against Germany, Italy and Japan at the same time” — even if badly executed. Chamberlain’s real flaw was not appeasement itself but the fact that Hitler was fundamentally unappeasable.

Munich in September 1938 gave Hitler more than he might have gained by war. Czechoslovakia was eliminated as a European military force. Germany acquired Czech armaments, factories, and raw materials. Stalin’s suspicion of the West deepened dramatically. By the time the Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact was signed in August 1939, war over Poland was virtually inevitable.

The war’s opening confirmed German operational superiority. Blitzkrieg — combined arms, tactical air power, decentralized offensive warfare — shattered larger but poorly organized Allied forces. France fell in six weeks. But Operation Barbarossa in June 1941 proved the fatal overreach. Germany killed or captured three million Russians in four months — and it didn’t matter. Russia could absorb appalling losses and still function. “This was a limitless war, and the Third Reich, for all its imposing successes and operational brilliance, was not properly equipped to fight it.”

Pearl Harbor in December 1941 brought the sleeping giant into the war. Both turning points — Barbarossa and Pearl Harbor — confirmed the central theme of the entire book. Germany and Japan had gambled on rapid, decisive victories before Allied productive superiority could be mobilized. Both achieved spectacular initial successes. Both ultimately confronted the same immovable reality: in a prolonged war, the side with the greater economic base will prevail.

The Proper Application of Overwhelming Force

When news reached Churchill that the United States had entered the war, he rejoiced: “ Hitler’s fate was sealed. Mussolini’s fate was sealed. As for the Japanese, they would be ground to powder. All the rest was merely the proper application of overwhelming force.” The confidence appeared premature through mid-1943, with Japan overrunning the Pacific, the Wehrmacht driving toward the Caucasus, Rommel within fifty-five miles of Alexandria, and U-boats at peak lethality. But once the Grand Alliance fully mobilized, productive superiority became insurmountable.

The numbers tell the story. By 1943, Allied aircraft production reached 151,761 versus the Axis’s 43,100. By 1944, the ratio widened to 167,654 versus 67,987. On D-Day, the Luftwaffe managed 319 aircraft against 12,837 Allied planes in the west. American arms output rose eightfold between 1941 and 1943. By 1943–44, the United States was producing one ship per day and one aircraft every five minutes. Allied arms production in 1943 totaled $62.5 billion (1944 dollars) versus the Axis’s $18.3 billion.

The Eastern Front revealed the most dramatic application of productive superiority. General Halder noted in August 1941: “We reckoned with about 200 enemy divisions. Now we have already counted 360.” The Germans claimed over three million Russian casualties in the first five months, yet the Red Army still had 4.2 million men in field armies. Despite losing 57% of coal production and 68% of pig-iron capacity to German occupation, Russia produced 4,000 more aircraft than Germany in 1941 and 10,000 more in 1942. At Kursk in 1943, the Red Army deployed roughly 4,000 armored vehicles against 2,700 German. By January 1945, Soviet superiority was fivefold in manpower and armor, sevenfold in artillery, and seventeenfold in the air.

German and Japanese strategic errors compounded the material disadvantage. Germany’s “polycratic chaos” — army, SS, Gauleiter, and economics ministry all competing — prevented coherent resource allocation. Ideological refusal to employ German women in munitions while all Allied nations exploited this labor force was characteristic. Japan pursued a continental strategy driven by the army, keeping thirteen divisions in Manchuria and twenty-two in China while only eleven defended the Pacific against Nimitz’s advancing fleet.

“Once again, in a protracted and full-scale coalition war, the countries with the deepest purse had prevailed.” — Kennedy

The American achievement was unique in human history. The United States was the only Great Power that became richer because of the war. GNP rose from $88.6 billion in 1939 to $135 billion in 1945 in constant dollars. Industrial expansion ran at over 15% per year between 1940 and 1944. Productive plant grew roughly 50%. War production surged from 2% to 40% of total output, yet the civilian sector also grew. By 1945, the United States held $20 billion in gold reserves — two-thirds of the world total — and produced more than half of world manufacturing output.

The former great powers were shattered. Germany’s national income in 1946 was less than one-third of 1938. Japan’s real income stood at 57% of prewar. Italy’s GNP reverted to its 1911 level. France’s national income was halved. Britain, the only major state fighting from start to finish, saw its export trade wither to 31% of 1938 figures. Keynes’s “financial Dunkirk” memorandum warned of a colossal trade gap. The European age in world affairs was over.

The New Strategic Landscape

Tocqueville’s 1835 prediction of a bipolar world dominated by Russia and America was finally realized. The geopolitical confirmation was dramatic: Soviet forces occupied everything from the Elbe to the Pacific, while American power projected from western Europe to Japan. Molotov declared in 1946: “One cannot decide now any serious problems of international relations without the USSR.” Two superpowers, both committed to globalist thinking, found a clash of interests inevitable.

American power in 1945 reached a scale without historical precedent: 12.5 million military personnel, a navy of 1,200 major warships, over 2,000 heavy bombers, and a monopoly on atomic weapons. By 1945, sixty-nine divisions were stationed in Europe and twenty-six in the Asia-Pacific, with zero in the continental United States. Like Britain after 1815, new “frontiers of insecurity” hardened informal influence into formal commitments. The IMF, World Bank, and GATT institutionalized American economic primacy. Only growing fear of Soviet influence prompted the Marshall Plan.

The Soviet Union was simultaneously a military giant and an economic wreck. The Red Army had smashed 506 German divisions — ten of Germany’s 13.6 million casualties fell on the Eastern Front. But twenty to twenty-five million Soviet citizens died premature deaths between 1941 and 1945. Material destruction was staggering: of 11.6 million horses, seven million killed or taken; 137,000 tractors and 49,000 grain combines destroyed; 65,000 kilometers of railway track obliterated. People lived in holes in the ground. Stalin reverted to post-1928 forced growth from the USSR’s own resources, doubling heavy industry output by 1950 — but much was mere recovery to prewar levels.

Nuclear weapons gave any possessor the capability for mass destruction. Britain and France built arsenals for the same reason nations had built dreadnoughts before 1914: as a reflection of weakness, not strength. Ideology cemented the bipolar world. The Truman Doctrine of March 1947 framed the globe as a binary choice between free institutions and minority tyranny. Both liberalism and communism were universal ideologies, “mutually exclusive.” The whole world became an arena where ideological quarrel and power advantage were inseparable.

The Cold War and the Third World

The Cold War began as a contest over European boundaries — fundamentally still about the “German problem,” since whoever controlled Germany controlled Europe. George Kennan’s containment strategy had two elements: the negative side provided military support for key regions; the positive side, which Kennan considered more crucial, offered massive economic aid to rebuild Europe and Japan, making prosperity the obvious path. Kennan identified five critical centers of industrial-military power — the United States, the Soviet Union, Britain, Germany, and Japan — and argued that keeping the last three in the Western camp would guarantee permanent Soviet inferiority in the “correlation of forces.”

The Marshall Plan was offered to all European nations, including Communist states. Molotov’s walkout from the Paris talks, combined with Russian pressure on Poland and Czechoslovakia not to apply, divided Europe more sharply than any previous act. NATO did militarily what the Marshall Plan did economically: deepened the division into two entrenched blocs. The pattern then spread globally through alliance chains — Rio Pact, ANZUS, SEATO, Baghdad Pact, and bilateral treaties with Japan, South Korea, Taiwan, and the Philippines. By 1970, the United States had more than one million soldiers in thirty countries, mutual defense treaties with forty-two nations, and was furnishing aid to nearly one hundred.

The nuclear arms race escalated relentlessly. Russia broke the American atomic monopoly in 1949, well before Western estimates. The hydrogen bomb followed for both sides. Sputnik in 1957 triggered “missile gap” panic and massive American ICBM investment. The result was MAD — Mutually Assured Destruction — Churchill’s “mutual balance of terror.” Arms control negotiations continued, but banning one weapon category merely redirected resources to another: MIRVs, submarine-launched missiles, theater weapons.

Vietnam demonstrated the limits of economic and military superiority. The United States was fifty to one hundred times more productive than North Vietnam, with firepower to “bomb the enemy back into the stone age.” But constraints prevented this superiority from being decisive. Troop levels peaked at 542,000. Ho Chi Minh declared willingness to lose men at ten to one — and proved it after Tet. The long-term consequences were severe: while America poured money into Vietnam, the Soviet Union achieved rough strategic nuclear parity and built a global naval force. Kennedy compares Vietnam’s shock to Britain’s Boer War or Russia’s Crimean War — a salutary exposure of imperial hubris.

The Fissuring of the Bipolar World

The rigidly bipolar world of the early Cold War began to crack from within. The most important strategic event since 1945, Kennedy argues, was the Sino-Soviet split. Moscow had always been half-hearted about the Chinese Communists, fearing China as a competing pole of attraction. Mao resented Stalin’s meager aid and Khrushchev’s détente as betrayal. By 1960, the split was open. By 1964, China had exploded its first atomic bomb. Soviet divisions on the Chinese frontier grew from fifteen in 1967 to forty-four by 1972, after serious military clashes along the Ussuri River. Moscow hinted at a preemptive strike on China’s nuclear installations.

De Gaulle challenged American leadership from within the Western alliance, pulling France out of NATO’s military structure in 1966, expelling NATO headquarters, and closing American bases. Willy Brandt’s Ostpolitik reconciled West Germany with its eastern neighbors, accepting 1945 boundaries as permanent and enabling Western investment to flow across the Iron Curtain. The Nixon-Kissinger revolution exploited the Sino-Soviet split with brilliant pragmatism — bombing North Vietnam massively in 1972 and journeying to China to bury the hatchet with Mao in the same year. Kissinger modeled himself on Metternich, Castlereagh, and Bismarck, welcoming a concert of powers as “a safer world and a better world” than a bipolar zero-sum system.

Reagan’s attempt to reassert bipolar dominance through a massive defense buildup — more than doubling the budget in five years — foundered on real-world complexity. Jungle interventions were blocked by Congress. Nuclear “prevail” talk produced mass unease in Europe. Meanwhile, the Soviet Union’s structural problems deepened: importing foreign grain and technology, satellites increasingly disaffected, Afghanistan becoming a military quagmire. If any superpower had truly “lost China,” it was Russia.

The Changing Economic Balances, 1950–1980

The postwar economic transformation was unprecedented. World manufacturing output from 1953 to 1973 grew at 6% per year — accumulated output in those two decades equaled the entire century and a half from 1800 to 1953. The Japanese miracle was the most spectacular: GDP grew at 10.5% per year from 1950 to 1973. By the late 1970s, Japan’s GNP equaled Britain and France combined, and was more than half of America’s. Ironically, the miracle was kick-started by American defense spending during the Korean War — Toyota was near bankruptcy until rescued by U.S. Defense Department truck orders.

Western Europe recovered with equal drama. European GDP growth averaged 5.5% per year from 1950 to 1970. Per capita income rose in one generation as much as in the previous 150 years. West Germany’s Wirtschaftswunder was extraordinary: steel production went from zero in 1946 to 34 million tons by 1960; per capita income reached $10,837 by 1979, ahead of the American average. By 1983, the EEC was the world’s largest importer and exporter, with the largest currency and gold reserves, manufacturing more automobiles than Japan or the United States.

American relative decline was mathematically inevitable from the artificially high 1945 base — some erosion was simply the rest of the world recovering. But the pattern went beyond natural adjustment. The U.S. share of world manufacturing fell from roughly 50% in 1945 to 31.5% by 1980. Gold reserves dropped from 68% of the world total to 27%. Productivity growth slowed from 2.4% in the late 1960s to 0.2% by the early 1980s. The Bretton Woods system collapsed when its leading pillar could bear the strain no more. Federal deficits sucked in foreign cash, pushing the dollar artificially high and turning the United States from the world’s largest net lender to its largest net borrower.

“Is it ‘conceivable’ that in ten or twenty years’ time the relative strength of the imperialist powers will have remained unchanged? Absolutely inconceivable.” — Lenin, 1917–18

The Soviet Union showed impressive gains concealing structural decline. Steel production reached 148 million tons by 1980 — the world’s largest. But growth rates fell from double digits to 3–4% by the late 1970s. An American farm worker fed sixty-five people; a Soviet farm worker fed eight. By 1980, the shares of gross world product told the emerging multipolar story: the United States at 21.5%, the EEC at 22.5%, Japan at 9.0%, China at 4.5%, the USSR at 11.4%, and developing countries at 14.8%. The bipolar world was giving way to something more diffuse, more unpredictable, and more historically normal.

History and Speculation

Kennedy’s final chapter is explicitly provisional and conjectural, applying the book’s historical patterns to the late-1980s world. Two central theses are restated: uneven economic growth impacts military power, and uneven growth has long-term consequences for strategic position. Two major trends dominate the forward view. First, the rise of the Pacific Basin — Asian-Pacific GDP rose from 7.8% of the world total in 1960 to 16.4% by 1982, with projections above 20% by 2000. U.S. trade with the Asia-Pacific, which had been 48% of U.S.-Europe trade in 1960, reached 122% by 1983. Second, the spiraling cost of weapons — bombers 200 times more expensive than in World War II, fighters 100 times, tanks 15 times. By 1984, world arms imports exceeded world grain trade.

Every government heading into the twenty-first century faced a threefold challenge: provide military security, satisfy socioeconomic needs, and ensure sustained economic growth. Achieving all three simultaneously was very difficult. Failing the third — growth — inevitably led to relative eclipse. No perfect solution existed; the best achievable was rough harmony among the three aims. As Kennedy observed: a country whose productivity growth lags 1% behind others over a century can turn, as England did, from the world’s undisputed industrial leader into a mediocre economy.

China’s Balancing Act

China was the poorest of the major powers, but Deng Xiaoping was evolving a grand strategy that Kennedy judged “more coherent and forward-looking” than Moscow’s, Washington’s, or Tokyo’s. The approach was analogous to Colbert’s France or Meiji Japan: developing national power by every pragmatic means available. The PLA was reduced from 4.2 to 3 million men — counterintuitively an enhancement, since many had been mere support troops. Military academies reopened, conscription was introduced, and the navy was modernized with new destroyers and fast-attack craft.

In 1980, a Chinese naval task force conducted an 8,000-mile South Pacific exercise — the first significant Chinese sea-power projection since Admiral Cheng Ho in the early fifteenth century. The symbolism was unmistakable: five and a half centuries after Ming China had burned its fleet, the nation was returning to the sea.

The “four modernizations” placed defense explicitly last, after agriculture, industry, and science. Defense spending fell from roughly 17.4% of the budget in 1971 to 7.5% by 1985 — a stark contrast to Soviet obsession with military security. The core logic was that long-term security could only be assured when output had been multiplied many times over. Under Deng’s reforms, agriculture grew at 8% and industry at 12%. Between 1979 and 1983, 800 million rural Chinese increased their incomes by roughly 70%. Savings and investment consistently exceeded 30% of GNP. The Economist projected that if 8% growth were maintained, China would surpass British and Italian GNP before 2000.

The Japanese Dilemma

Japan faced what Kennedy called a “ scissors effect”: one blade was competition from Asian newly industrializing countries with lower labor costs; the other was Western protectionism driven by Japan’s enormous trade surpluses. The U.S. trade deficit with Japan reached $62 billion in fiscal 1986. But Japan’s structural advantages were formidable: systematic migration from textiles and shipbuilding to computing, robotics, and biotechnology; R&D spending rising toward 3.5% of GNP with nearly all of it commercially rather than militarily targeted; personal savings rates above 20% of wages; and a workforce whose fifteen-year-olds were markedly superior in math and science to their American peers.

By 1985, Japan was the world’s largest net creditor nation, with capital outflows reaching $64.5 billion — much of it flowing into U.S. Treasury bonds to finance Reagan-era deficits. In 1951, Japan’s GNP had been one-third of Britain’s and one-twentieth of America’s. Within thirty years it had doubled Britain’s and approached half the American total.

The military paradox was acute. Japan spent only $11.6 billion on defense in 1983 versus America’s $239 billion. If Japan matched the NATO European average of 3–4% of GNP, it would become the world’s third-largest military power overnight. But deep anti-war sentiment, constitutional restrictions, and the economic logic of keeping defense spending low all militated against rearmament. Kennedy posed the question that haunted Japanese strategists: what happens if the United States withdraws from Asia, or if China dominates the region? “Commercial expertise and financial wealth sometimes no longer suffice in the anarchic world of international power politics.”

The EEC — Potential and Problems

Of the five main power concentrations, only Europe was not a sovereign state — and that was its defining problem. Kennedy drew an analogy to the German Federation of the mid-nineteenth century: a customs union proving economically successful but unable to translate collective potential into unified power. The twelve-member EEC had a population of roughly 320 million, a total GNP roughly equal to America’s, and was the largest trading bloc in the world. If the entire EEC spent 7% of GNP on defense, its expenditure would equal the two superpowers combined. But armed forces suffered from multiple languages, different weapons systems, and political fragmentation. “Eurosclerosis” — high unemployment, rigid labor markets, ballooning welfare costs — sapped dynamism.

West Germany was the engine: largest producer of steel, chemicals, electrical goods, and automobiles in Europe, with a trade surplus second only to Japan’s. But the reunification dilemma loomed: a unified Germany’s GNP would approach the Soviet Union’s, which Moscow could never accept. Britain was in manufacturing decline, its trade balance on manufactures falling into deficit “for the first time since the Romans invaded,” with 50% of R&D going to defense versus Germany’s 9% and Japan’s near-zero. Kennedy’s verdict on Europe: if the EEC acted as a unified power, its position could improve; if not — “which, given human nature, is the more plausible outcome — its relative decline seems destined to continue.”

The Soviet Union and Its “Contradictions”

Kennedy used the Marxist term deliberately and ironically: the widening gap between stated goals and actual results. Gorbachev himself acknowledged in 1986 that “difficulties began to build up in the economy in the 1970s” and that “acceleration of the country’s socio-economic development is the key to all our problems.” The litany of failure was comprehensive.

Agricultural disaster: the USSR, once one of the two largest grain exporters, was now importing tens of millions of tons annually. Agriculture consumed roughly 30% of Soviet investment versus 5% in America, yet productivity was one-seventh of American farming. Private plots constituting only 4% of arable land produced roughly 25% of total crop output — a devastating indictment of collectivization. Energy efficiency was abysmal: the USSR used 1,490 kilograms of coal equivalent per $1,000 of GDP versus West Germany’s 565.

The high-technology gap was widening. The Soviet Union was falling behind in robotics, supercomputers, lasers, and telecommunications. The core contradiction was structural: computers thrive in open societies but threaten the Soviet monopoly on information. Senior scientists were forbidden from personally using copying machines — departments were staffed by the KGB. Demographically, life expectancy for Soviet men had fallen to roughly sixty years, six years less than the mid-1960s. Infant mortality was three times the American rate. Alcoholism reached “fantastic levels.”

The defense burden was crushing. At 11–13% of GNP, roughly twice the American share even under Reagan’s buildup, military spending siphoned trained manpower and capital from productive uses. “Guns will always come before butter and, if need be, before growth.” Without its massive military power, the Soviet Union counted for little in world affairs. With its massive military power, it made others feel insecure and hurt its own economic prospects. Kennedy called it “a grim dilemma” and noted that none of the overextended empires he had surveyed — Ottoman, Spanish, Napoleonic, British — had ever retreated gracefully without being defeated or so weakened that withdrawal became politically unavoidable.

The United States: The Problem of Number One in Relative Decline

Kennedy’s most controversial chapter drew the parallel between America’s late-twentieth- century position and the trajectories of previous dominant powers. Like imperial Spain around 1600 or the British Empire around 1900, the United States was the inheritor of vast global commitments made when its relative economic capacity seemed more assured. By 1985, 520,000 American military personnel were stationed abroad — more than the British Empire’s peacetime deployments at their height. The Joint Chiefs chairman acknowledged in late 1983 that the “mismatch” between forces and strategy was “greater now than ever before.”

The economic indicators were troubling. The high-tech trade surplus plunged from $27 billion in 1980 to $4 billion by 1985. The visible trade deficit reached $160 billion. The United States transformed from the world’s largest creditor to the world’s largest debtor in a few years. The federal deficit soared from $59.6 billion in 1980 to $202.8 billion in 1985, with national debt nearly doubling and interest payments reaching $129 billion. Kennedy noted that the only other peacetime Great Power to so increase its debt was France in the 1780s — and that fiscal crisis contributed to political collapse.

The parallel with Edwardian Britain was explicit. American anxiety mirrored the “national efficiency” movement that had gripped Britain at the turn of the century. G.K. Chesterton’s observation applied: “A strong man does not worry about bodily efficiency; only when he weakens does he begin to talk about health.” What Hewins had said of British industrial decay in 1904 now applied to American decline in shipbuilding, mining, and semiconductors.

Yet Kennedy was careful to distinguish the American case from inevitable collapse. The United States would not decline to obscurity — it was too large and homogeneous. The British analogy was imperfect: Britain’s natural share of world output was perhaps 3–4%, versus its peak of roughly 25%. America’s natural share was 16–18%, versus a wartime peak above 40%. The present moment was not collapse but a gradual ebb back toward a natural share. The goal was not to preserve the 1945 position — that was impossible — but to manage the erosion so it was slow and smooth rather than accelerated by policies offering only short-term advantage.

“Bring into balance the nation’s commitments and the nation’s power.” — Walter Lippmann

The imperial overstretch dilemma was structural and inescapable. As global superpower, the United States required far larger defense forces than regional powers — just as imperial Spain needed a bigger army and Victorian Britain a bigger navy. Heavy armament brought short-term security but eroded commercial competitiveness. Low investment left America feeling vulnerable everywhere. The more perceptive decision-makers recognized that Soviet armament spending was debilitating their economy too. The real question was whose economy would decline fastest relative to Japan, China, and the rest. Bismarck’s metaphor provided the final word: all powers were traveling on “the stream of Time,” which they could neither create nor direct, but upon which they could “steer with more or less skill.”

Lessons

The same patterns repeat across 500 years

Economic power always precedes military power, and military power always follows economic decline. This is the master pattern of the book. Spain, France, Britain, and the Soviet Union all experienced it. The lag between economic peak and military peak can be decades — long enough to create the illusion of permanent strength — but the correction always comes. A nation’s army fights with the wealth of yesterday; its economy determines the army of tomorrow.

Overextension is the universal killer. Habsburg Spain, Napoleonic France, Victorian Britain, imperial Japan, Nazi Germany, and Cold War America all faced the same structural trap: commitments that grew faster than the resources to sustain them. The mechanism is always the same — each new obligation seems individually defensible, but collectively they exhaust the treasury and force the state into a defensive posture that accelerates decline.

The nation that innovates financially wins the war. The Dutch bond market, the Bank of England, American wartime industrial mobilization — in every era, the side that found new ways to fund warfare outlasted the side that relied on traditional revenue or outright plunder. Napoleon’s domaine extraordinaire was brilliant but unsustainable. Britain’s ability to borrow at 3% while France paid 6% was worth more than any number of regiments.

Centralization looks powerful but loses over time. Ming China, the Ottoman Empire, Mughal India, and the Soviet Union all demonstrate that centralized authority capable of suppressing innovation by decree will eventually fall behind competitors who cannot suppress innovation because no single authority exists to do so. Europe’s fragmentation was its greatest strength precisely because it was nobody’s choice.

Coalition wars are won by the deeper purse. Every major conflict in the book — the anti-Habsburg wars, the Seven Years War, the Napoleonic Wars, both World Wars — was decided by the coalition with superior productive resources. Individual battles can be won by genius or courage, but prolonged wars are won by the side that can keep its armies in the field, replace losses, and maintain political cohesion. The pattern has no exceptions in Kennedy’s five-century survey.

The lag time creates dangerous illusions. A power in relative decline continues to look strong for decades after the economic foundations have eroded. Spanish tercios were still the finest infantry in Europe when Spain’s economy was already hollowed out. Britain remained the world’s dominant naval power for decades after Germany and America had surpassed its industrial base. This lag tempts leaders into believing the decline is manageable or reversible through military spending alone — which only accelerates the underlying erosion.

Geography is destiny, but not in the way people think. The advantage is not resources or location but strategic insulation. Russia and America became superpowers partly because their continental mass made them nearly unconquerable. The Dutch, despite financial genius, could not overcome the geographic squeeze between Britain and France. Japan’s insularity protected it from overland invasion but limited its ability to project power without ruinous naval expense.

Internal decisions matter more than external threats. Ming China burned its fleet. The Ottoman Empire destroyed its observatory. Spain expelled its Jews and Moriscos. Britain let its industrial base erode while investing overseas. America accumulated federal deficits while cutting taxes and raising military spending. In every case, the critical choices were made at home, not imposed from abroad. The greatest enemy of a dominant power is not the rising challenger but its own failure to adapt.

Trading & Investing Lessons

Imperial Overstretch Is Overleveraging

Kennedy’s central concept maps directly to markets. A nation that takes on more military commitments than its economy can sustain is doing exactly what a trader does when leveraged beyond their risk capacity. Each individual position looks manageable, but collectively they create a fragility that guarantees failure when conditions turn. Spain’s multiple fronts are a portfolio of correlated positions with no hedge.

The Lag Between Fundamentals and Price

Kennedy’s “lag time” between economic decline and military decline is the same phenomenon traders see when a stock trades above fair value on momentum. Britain looked invincible at Queen Victoria’s Diamond Jubilee in 1897 — yet its industrial base had been eroding for two decades. The market’s version: a company reporting record revenue while market share quietly shrinks. The chart looks great until it doesn’t.

Diversification Beats Concentration

Europe’s political fragmentation — the engine of its rise — is essentially diversification. No single ruler could make a catastrophic decision that destroyed the whole system. Ming China was a concentrated bet on centralized management. When the managers turned inward, the entire civilization stagnated. A diversified portfolio of competing states generated constant innovation and self-correction.

The “Plunder Model” Doesn’t Scale

Napoleon’s war finance — systematic plunder of conquered territories — produced spectacular short-term returns but was inherently unsustainable. In market terms: extractive strategies (pump-and-dump, value-destructive acquisitions, exploitative pricing) generate near-term profits but destroy the ecosystem that produced them. Britain’s model of funding wars through productive commerce is the equivalent of compound growth.

Credit Quality Is the Ultimate Edge

Britain could borrow at 3% while France paid 6%. This single differential was worth more than any fleet or army. In markets, credit quality determines cost of capital, which determines competitive advantage in every deal, every acquisition, every expansion. The Dutch Republic became a Great Power through the simple mechanism of reliably repaying its debts. The lesson for portfolio management: protect your credit rating and your reputation above all else, because they compound silently over decades.

Mean Reversion Is Inevitable

Kennedy’s argument about America’s “natural share” of world output is pure mean reversion. The 1945 peak was an anomaly created by the destruction of every other industrial economy. The subsequent decline was not collapse but regression to a natural level. In trading: extreme moves revert. Sectors that capture outsized market share eventually face competition. Countries that dominate world output eventually see challengers industrialize. The question is never whether reversion will happen but how fast and how far.

Never Bet Against the Bigger Economy in a Long War

If Kennedy’s book has one iron law, it is this: in prolonged coalition conflicts, the side with the greater productive base always wins. Germany in both World Wars, Japan in WWII, Spain across fourteen decades — all lost despite superior tactics and early victories. In market terms: don’t fight the trend. Don’t short the economy with the deeper balance sheet. Time is always on the side of productive capacity.

Key Takeaways

Productive power is the foundation of everything

Military strength, diplomatic influence, and imperial reach all rest on the economy. When a nation neglects this foundation — spending too much on defense, fighting too many wars, suppressing commerce — decline follows with mathematical certainty, even if the lag can span generations.

Power is always relative, never absolute

The Netherlands in the eighteenth century was richer than it had been in the seventeenth, yet much less powerful because France and Britain had grown faster. Britain in 1987 had far greater absolute wealth than at its Victorian peak, yet its share of world product had shrunk from 25% to 3%. What matters is not how strong you are, but how strong you are compared to your rivals.

Strategic overextension is the common cause of imperial decline

From Habsburg Spain to Cold War America, the pattern is identical: commitments accumulate faster than the resources to sustain them. Each obligation appears individually defensible. Collectively, they exhaust the state.

Coalition wars are always won by the economically stronger side

This holds across every major conflict in the book’s five-century survey, without exception.

Financial innovation is as decisive as military innovation

The Bank of England, Dutch bond markets, American wartime industrial mobilization — the ability to fund war sustainably has been more decisive than any single weapon or tactic.

Political fragmentation drives innovation more reliably than centralized control

Europe outpaced every other civilization because no single authority could suppress competition. This insight applies to technology, commerce, science, and military development.

The lag between economic and military decline creates dangerous illusions

A state looks strong for decades after the rot has set in. This tempts leaders into believing the decline is manageable through military spending alone — the exact response that accelerates it.

Internal decisions matter more than external threats

Every major decline in the book was driven primarily by domestic choices: Ming China burning its fleet, Spain expelling its commercial classes, Britain underinvesting in industry, America accumulating deficits. The greatest threat to any dominant power is itself.

Contrarian Ideas

Britain’s “hegemony” was never as dominant as assumed

Kennedy argues that Britain’s overall GNP was probably never the world’s largest — China and Russia had vastly more aggregate output from peasant populations. Britain’s dominance was in per capita industrial output and finance, not in total economic weight. The question mark after “Britain as Hegemon?” is deliberate.

Napoleon’s Continental System was rational, not foolish

Conventional history treats the Continental System as a blunder. Kennedy argues it was a logical strategy against an island economy dependent on trade. The failure was one of execution — Spain’s revolt, Russia’s defection, massive smuggling — not conception.

Appeasement was rational for an overstretched empire

Kennedy defends appeasement not morally but strategically. Britain’s chiefs of staff were right that they could not fight Germany, Italy, and Japan simultaneously with available resources. Chamberlain’s error was not the logic of buying time but the assumption that Hitler could be satisfied with incremental adjustments.

Germany was “born encircled” — its aggression was partly structural

Kennedy argues that Germany’s geographic position at the center of Europe meant that any growth automatically threatened multiple Great Powers. The encirclement was not merely a response to aggression but partly a structural consequence of Germany’s location and scale.

Nuclear weapons may not have prevented great-power war — they may only ensure it stays conventional

Kennedy notes that Norman Angell’s The Great Illusion argued in 1910 that war was too economically ruinous to occur — and was published as European general staffs finalized their war plans. History refutes confident predictions of peace.

Proposals & Frameworks

The Three-Factor Framework for Great Power Decline

Kennedy identifies three interacting forces that determine whether a Great Power rises or falls: (1) the productive economic base relative to rivals, (2) geographic position and the cost of defending it, and (3) the efficiency with which resources are mobilized for war. All three must be analyzed together; any single factor considered in isolation produces misleading conclusions.

The Threefold Challenge for Modern States

Every government faces three simultaneous imperatives: (1) provide military security, (2) satisfy socioeconomic needs of the population, (3) ensure sustained economic growth. Failing the third inevitably leads to relative eclipse. No perfect solution exists — the best achievable is rough harmony among the three.

The “Trading State” vs. “Militarized Economy” Spectrum

Kennedy identifies two poles: trading states (Japan, Switzerland, Hong Kong) that minimize defense spending and emphasize peaceful commerce, freeing resources for consumption and investment; and militarized economies (USSR, Israel, Vietnam) that allocate 10%+ of GNP to defense at manifest cost to productive output. The challenge for superpowers is that their global commitments push them toward the militarized end even as their economic health requires the trading-state model.

The “Natural Share” Concept

Each power has a natural share of world output determined by its size, population, resources, and institutional quality. Wartime peaks and postwar troughs represent departures from this natural share. Long-term policy should aim not at preserving artificial peaks but at managing the transition back to the natural level smoothly and without acceleration by self-defeating policies.

What Didn’t Fit

The 49 Statistical Tables

Kennedy’s argument rests on an extraordinary foundation of quantitative data: 49 statistical tables covering everything from military manpower in the 1470s to nuclear warhead counts in 1986. The two most important recurring metrics are relative shares of world manufacturing output and per capita levels of industrialization. These tables are not decoration — they are the argument. Kennedy treats shifts in manufacturing share as leading indicators of military power, and the historical record consistently validates this approach. The tables showing China’s collapse from 32.8% of world manufacturing in 1750 to 6.2% by 1900, or Britain’s decline from 22.9% in 1880 to 13.6% by 1913, carry more argumentative weight than any narrative passage.

The Army of Flanders’s 46 Mutinies

Between 1572 and 1607, the Spanish Army of Flanders mutinied forty-six times — not because of cowardice or disloyalty, but because the troops were not paid. Spain poured 218 million ducats into the Netherlands over this period, more than total crown receipts from the Indies, yet could not keep soldiers in the field reliably. This single detail captures the entire dysfunction of the Habsburg system: wealth flowing in from the New World, consumed by military obligations in Europe, generating resentment at every stage, and never sufficient. The army that fought at Nördlingen and Rocroi was the same army that routinely turned on its own officers for lack of pay.

The Cheng Ho Expeditions and the Lost Opportunity

Admiral Cheng Ho’s seven expeditions between 1405 and 1433 are among history’s great what-ifs. His treasure ships displaced over 1,500 tons and reached as far as East Africa. China could have “discovered” Portugal decades before Portuguese navigators pushed south. But the Confucian bureaucracy saw maritime commerce as beneath contempt, and after 1433, the ships rotted in their harbors. When the Portuguese arrived off China’s coast, the navy that could have overwhelmed them no longer existed. Kennedy’s observation that China’s retreat was never reconsidered even when Japanese pirates ravaged the coastline is one of the book’s most haunting details.

Stolypin’s Prophecy

Russian Prime Minister Stolypin boasted in 1909: “Give the state twenty years of internal and external peace and you will not recognize Russia.” He was almost certainly right — Russian industrialization was accelerating impressively. But the war came in 1914, only five years into Stolypin’s requested two decades. Kennedy’s analysis of Russia’s “relative power curve” shows it was just rising from its post-1894 low point in 1914, while Germany’s was near its peak. The timing could not have been worse for Russia.

Toyota’s Korean War Rescue

Japan’s postwar economic miracle, which produced 10.5% annual GDP growth for two decades, was ironically kick-started by American military spending. Toyota was near bankruptcy in 1950 until rescued by U.S. Defense Department truck orders during the Korean War. The company that came to symbolize Japanese manufacturing supremacy — and American industrial decline — was literally saved by American defense procurement. Kennedy notes the irony without comment, but the detail perfectly illustrates the law of unintended consequences in great-power economics.

About the Author

Paul Kennedy (born 1945) is a British-born historian who has spent most of his career at Yale University, where he is the J. Richardson Dilworth Professor of History and Director of International Security Studies. Born in Wallsend, Tyneside, he was educated at Newcastle University and St. Antony’s College, Oxford. Before Yale, he taught at the University of East Anglia, where he established himself as an authority on British naval and imperial history.

Kennedy’s earlier works include The Rise and Fall of British Naval Mastery (1976) and The Rise of the Anglo-German Antagonism, 1860–1914 (1980). The Rise and Fall of the Great Powers was his breakthrough to global prominence, spending months on the New York Times bestseller list and generating intense debate in policy circles. He followed it with Preparing for the Twenty-First Century (1993) and The Parliament of Man: The Past, Present, and Future of the United Nations (2006).

Kennedy is distinguished by his ability to synthesize vast quantities of historical, economic, and military data into accessible narrative. His work bridges the gap between academic history and public policy debate, making him one of the most influential historians of the late twentieth century. He continues to write and teach at Yale.

Why This Book Matters

The Rise and Fall of the Great Powers was published in 1987, two years before the Berlin Wall fell and four years before the Soviet Union dissolved. Kennedy’s prediction that Soviet military spending was eroding its economic base proved spectacularly correct — the USSR collapsed precisely as his framework would have predicted. His parallel argument about American “imperial overstretch” remains the most debated thesis in modern strategic studies, with defenders and critics still arguing over whether the United States in the twenty-first century is repeating the trajectories of Habsburg Spain and Victorian Britain.

The book’s lasting significance lies not in any specific prediction but in its analytical framework. Kennedy demonstrated with five centuries of evidence that the relationship between economic productivity and military power is not optional or incidental but fundamental and inescapable. Nations that forget this — that spend beyond their means, fight on too many fronts, or suppress the commercial activity that funds their defense — decline. The pattern has no exceptions in five hundred years of recorded history.

In an era when China’s rise, American fiscal deficits, and the spiraling cost of military technology dominate strategic thinking, Kennedy’s framework is more relevant than when he first proposed it. The specific numbers have changed. The underlying dynamics have not. Any serious discussion of great-power competition in the twenty-first century begins where this book began: with the recognition that wealth and power are always relative, international balances can never be still, and the stream of time carries all nations toward consequences they can neither predict nor prevent — only navigate with more or less skill.
