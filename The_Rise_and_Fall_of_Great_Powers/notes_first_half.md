# The Rise and Fall of the Great Powers — Compacted Notes (Chapters 1–6)

## Introduction: The Core Argument

- Book traces how Great Powers have risen and fallen *relative to each other* over five centuries
- The triumph or collapse of any Great Power results from **three interacting factors**: (1) lengthy fighting by armed forces, (2) efficient wartime use of productive economic resources, (3) the state's economy rising or falling *relative to rivals in the decades preceding war*
- **Peacetime economic trajectory matters as much as wartime military performance**
- Circular relationship: wealth underwrites military power; military power acquires and protects wealth — but if too large a share goes to military, national power weakens long-term
- **Strategic overextension** — conquering too much or waging too many wars — risks outweighing the benefits of expansion, especially during economic decline
- **Lag time**: an expanding power prefers growth over armaments; decades later it has accumulated overseas obligations and faces rising rivals — defense spending rises even as relative position weakens
- Great Powers in relative decline instinctively spend more on security, diverting resources from investment — compounding the long-term dilemma (Spain 1630s, Britain 1910, US/USSR 1980s)
- Coalition wars are won by the side with more productive resources — true across every major conflict since 1500

> "whether a nation be today mighty and rich or not depends not on the abundance or security of its power and riches, but principally on whether its neighbors possess more or less of it." — von Hornigk

- **Relative, not absolute**: Netherlands in mid-18th century was richer than a century earlier, yet much less of a Great Power because France and Britain had grown more

## Chapter 1: The Rise of the Western World

### Ming China

- Most advanced civilization of premodern times; population 100-130 million vs. Europe's 50-55 million
- Technological precocity: movable type, paper money, magnetic compass, gunpowder, iron output of ~125,000 tons/year in the 11th century — far larger than British output seven centuries later
- 1420 Ming navy: 1,350 combat vessels including 400 floating fortresses; **Admiral Cheng Ho** led seven expeditions (1405-1433) with treasure ships ~400 feet long
- **1433**: last expedition; **1436**: imperial edict banned seagoing ships; later banned ships with more than two masts
- Confucian bureaucracy distrusted merchants, confiscated property, restricted printing, let canals decay, abandoned ironworks
- In 1736 — just as **Abraham Darby's** ironworks at Coalbrookdale were beginning to boom — the blast furnaces of Honan were abandoned entirely

### The Muslim World

- **Ottoman Empire**: finest siege train of the age; by 1500s domains stretched from Crimea to the Levant; **Suleiman** besieging Vienna by 1529
- After 1566: thirteen incompetent sultans in succession; centralized, despotic, hostile to initiative and commerce
- "An idiot sultan could paralyze the Ottoman Empire in the way that a pope or Holy Roman Emperor could never do for all Europe"
- Printing press forbidden; guilds checked innovation; in 1580 janissaries destroyed a state observatory alleging it caused plague
- **Mughal Empire**: Muslim elite atop vast poverty-stricken Hindu majority; taxation known as "eating" — population received nothing in return

### Two Outsiders — Japan and Russia

- **Tokugawa Japan**: 1636 banned oceangoing vessels; Japanese forbidden to sail; trade restricted to one Dutch ship at Deshima; Christians murdered; merchants never permitted social/political prominence — military ossified until **Perry's** "black ships" (1853)
- **Russia**: gunpowder expansion enabled "empire" growth to Pacific by 1638; but foreigners segregated, serfdom enforced, social modernization firmly blocked; unlike other empires, would eventually become a world power

### The "European Miracle"

- Why did scattered, relatively unsophisticated peoples of western Eurasia generate unstoppable development? Kennedy's answer: **political fragmentation** from geography
- No enormous plains for horsemen; no great river valleys creating conquerable peasants — decentralized power emerged naturally
- Differentiated climate → differentiated products → **bulk trade** → banking → predictability of economic conditions
- No uniform authority could suppress commercial development; persecution of merchants backfired — they moved and took expertise elsewhere
- Arms race dynamic: political decentralization → primitive arms race → constant weapons improvement; in China, Japan, Mughal India, once cannon monopoly established authority, incentive to improve vanished
- Counter-fortification (raised earthworks absorbing cannonballs) prevented any single European power from achieving decisive conquest
- Key innovation: **siting cannon on sailing vessels** — the gunned sailing ship as the decisive maritime instrument

## Chapter 2: The Habsburg Bid for Mastery, 1519-1659

### The Meaning and Chronology of the Struggle

- Two root causes of intensified warfare after 1500: (1) the **Reformation** fracturing Christendom, (2) the **Habsburg dynastic combination** — territories from Gibraltar to Hungary
- **Charles V** inherited all four dynastic streams simultaneously: Duke of Burgundy at 15, King of Spain 1516, Holy Roman Emperor 1519
- Three great phases: Italian Wars (to 1559), Netherlands Revolt and Armada (1560s-1609), Thirty Years War (1618-1648)
- **Spanish Armada** (1588): failed; cost 10 million ducats; two later attempts also failed
- **Thirty Years War**: Bohemian revolt lit the fuse; **Gustavus Adolphus** of Sweden invaded; Olivares planned triple invasion of France: "Either all is lost, or else Castile will be head of the world"
- **Treaty of Westphalia** (1648) + **Treaty of the Pyrenees** (1659): Habsburg predominance ended; European plurality confirmed

### Strengths and Weaknesses of the Habsburg Bloc

- Five revenue sources: Castile (most reliable), Italian states, Low Countries, American empire, banking houses
- Greatest military asset: the **Spanish tercio** — most effective infantry in Europe from early 16th century through mid-Thirty Years War
- Three factors in Habsburg failure:
  1. **Military revolution**: army sizes exploded from 30,000 (pre-1529) to 300,000 (1625); costs spiraled; Philip II defaulted repeatedly; at death debts = 100 million ducats
  2. **Strategic overextension**: too many enemies, too many fronts; "Spain resembled a large bear in the pit: more powerful than any of the dogs attacking it, but never able to deal with all opponents"
  3. **Failure to mobilize resources**: congeries of territories each with own privileges; Castile as "milch cow" — entire fiscal system rested on ~6 million Castilian peasants
- Between 1566-1654, Spain sent **218 million ducats** to the Netherlands — more than total crown receipts from the Indies (121 million)

> "The flood of precious metals from the Indies was to Spain as water on a roof — it poured on and then was drained away."

### International Comparisons

- **France**: latent advantage of 16 million population (2x Spain, 4x England); **Sully** restored solvency; but Richelieu's war doubled the taille; France and Spain were "punch-drunk boxers" by 1650s
- **England**: Elizabeth recognized limits; maintained independence by diplomacy and "cold war" at sea; never declared bankruptcy — left James I in debt but solvent
- **Sweden**: transformed by foreign entrepreneurs (**Louis de Geer**) and **Gustavus Adolphus**; army was parasitic — financed by "contributions" from German states, unsustainable from Sweden's own base
- **Dutch Republic**: tiny cluster of provinces became a Great Power through commercial-financial strength; could borrow more cheaply than any rival because it **reliably repaid debts**; Holland's war costs: 960,000 florins (1579) → 10 million annually by early 17th century

### War, Money, and the Nation-State

- War was the most urgent driver of nation-state formation; up to **three-quarters of government expenditure** went to war or debt repayment
- **Army of Flanders mutinied 46 times** between 1572 and 1607 — lack of pay as dangerous as enemy action
- The anti-Habsburg coalition succeeded by slightly better balancing material base against military power, recognizing that "the trader and the manufacturer and the farmer were as important as the cavalry officer and the pikeman"

## Chapter 3: Finance, Geography, and the Winning of Wars, 1660-1815

### The "Financial Revolution"

- Seven major Anglo-French wars (1689-1815) were wars of endurance — victory went to the coalition with greatest capacity to sustain credit
- Cost escalated from millions to tens of millions to occasionally £100 million a year
- **Dutch model**: merchants who were part of government demanded financial rectitude; Amsterdam became world's financial capital
- **Britain vs. France**: Britain held decisive financial advantages despite France's larger population:
  - Taxes mostly "invisible" (excise, customs); no internal tolls; authorized by elected Parliament
  - **Bank of England** (1694) enabled long-term loans with guaranteed interest
  - British government bonds increasingly attractive to Dutch investors

> "Credit was the principal advantage that England hath over France." — Bishop Berkeley

- **British Wartime Expenditure 1688-1815**: Total £1,657.9M in the Napoleonic Wars alone, of which £440.3M raised on money markets
- **France's structural failures**: no proper system of public finance; cluster of middlemen each taking a cut; every attempt to create a national bank was resisted
- By late 1780s: France's national debt similar to Britain's but annual interest payments nearly **double** — link between national bankruptcy and the French Revolution was direct

### Geopolitics

- Geography means strategic location: could a power concentrate on one front or fight on several? Was it a land power, sea power, or hybrid?
- **Dutch geographic trap**: forced to expand army against Louis XIV → drained resources from navy and commerce; 75% of defense spending went to army, leaving maritime campaigns to Britain
- **France as hybrid**: energies divided between continental and maritime ambitions — "fell between stools" in every war except 1778-1783
- **Britain's Janus-faced strategy**: one face toward the Continent to balance power, one face toward the sea to extend maritime dominance; subsidized continental allies while using Navy to control trade

### The Winning of Wars, 1660-1763

- **Battle of Poltava** (1709): Russian army wiped out Sweden's force; Sweden fell to second-order power
- **Seven Years War**: three decisive Anglo-Prussian advantages: leadership (Frederick + Pitt), financial staying power, military/naval expertise
- British overseas trade *increased every year* of the Seven Years War — commerce funded the war in real time
- Britain spent over £160 million, of which £60 million raised on money markets

> "In the present state of Europe it is colonies, trade and in consequence sea power, which must determine the balance of power upon the continent." — Choiseul

### The Winning of Wars, 1763-1815

- Napoleonic Wars: Britain supreme at sea, France supreme on land — neither could deliver a knockout blow
- **Continental System** never applied consistently enough; British total exports rose throughout the war
- Britain's **Industrial Revolution** as strategic asset: pig-iron output 68,000 tons (1788) → 325,000 tons (1811); customs/excise revenue tripled
- Napoleon's war finance was systematic **plunder**: Prussia paid 311M francs after Jena; "War would support war"

> "My power depends on my glory and my glories on the victories I have won. Conquest has made me what I am and only conquest can enable me to hold my position." — Napoleon

- **Russian campaign**: 600,000+ men, ~270,000 killed, 200,000 captured; **Battle of Leipzig** (1813): 195,000 French overwhelmed by 365,000 allied troops bankrolled by British subsidies
- Post-1815: rough parity on land among five Great Powers, but Britain held near-monopoly of naval power, reinforced by economic lead

## Chapter 4: Industrialization and the Shifting Global Balances, 1815-1885

### The Eclipse of the Non-European World

- Industrial Revolution: substitution of inanimate for animate sources of power; power-driven looms produced 20x the output of a hand worker
- **Relative Shares of World Manufacturing Output**: Third World fell from 73.0% (1750) to 11.0% (1900); China from 32.8% to 6.2%; India from 24.5% to 1.7%
- India suffered absolute deindustrialization: cotton fabric imports 1M yards (1814) → 995M yards (1870)
- Europeans occupied/controlled 35% of world's land surface in 1800 → 84%+ by 1914
- **Battle of Omdurman** (1898): Maxims destroyed 11,000 Dervishes at cost of 48 British troops

### Britain as Hegemon?

- At zenith (~1860): UK produced 53% of world's iron, 50% of coal; industrial capacity = 40-45% of world potential
- With 2% of world population: responsible for 1/5 of world's commerce but 2/5 of trade in manufactured goods
- Defense spending only 2-3% of GNP for 50 years after 1815 — least mobilized for war since early Stuarts
- Three realms of strength: naval supremacy, colonial empire expanding in a power-political vacuum, financial hegemony (capital exports £75M/year by 1870-1875)
- Hidden dangers: Britain's capital exports were financing the very industries that would allow rivals to overtake it; increasing dependence on international trade made it vulnerable in wartime

### The "Middle Powers"

- **Prussia**: least of the Great Powers post-1815; humiliated at Oelmuetz (1850); but best educational system, formidable general staff studying "railways and rifles"
- **Habsburg Empire**: central fulcrum of 1815 balance; extraordinary staying power but ethnic incoherence — army mobilization order in 15 different languages; military budget cut drastically one year before war with Prussia
- **France**: constrained middle power; population growth slower than any Great Power throughout 19th century; classic hybrid torn between European and non-European interests

### The Crimean War and the Erosion of Russian Power

- Russia's post-1815 peak: 800,000-man army, gendarme of Europe — but per capita GNP falling relative to Britain
- Crimean War exposed: flintlock muskets with 200-yard range vs. Allied rifles at 1,000 yards; no railways south of Moscow — supplies took 3 months vs. 3 weeks by sea for Allies
- Crown Council warned (Jan 1856): if war continued, state would go **bankrupt** — forced negotiated peace

### The United States and the Civil War

- By April 1861, US had surpassed Germany and Russia in manufacturing output; iron 830,000 tons vs. Russia's 350,000; railway mileage 30x greater
- North won through resources: 110,000 manufacturing establishments vs. South's 18,000; Pennsylvania pig iron alone 580,000 tons vs. Confederacy's 36,700
- Confederate financial collapse via inflation; Northern output surged — the "American way of war" (mobilizing massive industrial-technological potential) was forged here

### The Wars of German Unification

- Prussian military revolution: short-service system, **Moltke's** General Staff, learning culture
- **Franco-Prussian War** (1870-71): German states combined had larger population than France, more railway miles, coal 2.5x France's; **Krupp** steel symbolized industrial-military integration
- "Europe had lost a mistress and gained a master" — Germany became a "half-hegemonic power"

## Chapter 5: The Coming of a Bipolar World, Part One, 1885-1918

### The Shifting Balance of World Forces

- By 1895-1900, observers sensed US and Russia would be future world powers; Joseph Chamberlain: "the tendency of the time is to throw all power into the hands of the greater empires"
- UK share of world manufacturing: **22.9%** (1880) → **13.6%** (1913); US surged from 14.7% to 23.6%

### The Position of the Powers, 1885-1914

- **Italy**: illusion of Great Power status; 37.6% illiteracy rate; no coal; world manufacturing share actually *dropped*; military disasters at Adowa (1896) and Caporetto (1917)
- **Japan**: Meiji Restoration transformed a feudal society; national slogan *fukoku kyohei* ("rich country, strong army"); Russo-Japanese War victory (1904-05) but near bankruptcy by war's end
- **Germany**: population 49M→66M; steel 17.6M tons (more than Britain, France, Russia *combined*); Siemens and AEG dominated European electrical industry; Bayer/Hoechst produced 90% of world's industrial dyes — but "born encircled," growth threatened multiple Great Powers simultaneously
- **Austria-Hungary**: world manufacturing share only 4.5%; mobilization order in 15 languages; "trying to act the part of a great power with the resources of a second-rank one"
- **France**: industrial potential only 40% of Germany's by 1914; population gap decisive; but rebuilt position diplomatically through alliances with Russia and Britain
- **Britain**: "the weary Titan staggering under the too vast orb of its fate"; UK world manufacturing share fell from 22.9% to 13.6%; strategic overextension across every continent simultaneously
- **Russia**: fourth-largest industrial power but per capita industrialization less than 1/4 of Germany's; 80% of population in agriculture; Stolypin: "Give the state twenty years of peace and you will not recognize Russia" — but the war came too early
- **United States**: national income $37 billion (vs. Britain $11B, Germany $12B); coal, pig-iron, steel, energy consumption dwarfing all rivals; but spending only 1% of GNP on defense, smaller army than Serbia

### Alliances and the Drift to War, 1890-1914

- Alliance "rigidification": fixed military alliances in peacetime, rarely seen before Bismarck
- Franco-Russian Dual Alliance (1894) matched Triple Alliance; Anglo-Japanese Alliance (1902); Entente Cordiale (1904); Anglo-Russian entente (1907)
- War plans like "dominoes" — coalition structure virtually guaranteed one local conflict would cascade
- Both at sea and on land, a swift victory was technically impossible — but all general staffs assumed speed was of the essence

### Total War and the Power Balances, 1914-1918

- Allied superiority was "significant rather than overwhelming"; Franco-Russian alone against Central Powers in a long war: hard to see how they could have won
- Germany's advantages: inner lines, defensible western positions, intelligent staff rethinking warfare faster than anyone
- **Russia's collapse**: isolated, could not secure meaningful aid from allies; czarist fiscal self-destruction (abolished spirits trade losing 1/3 of revenue, printed money); casualties by end of 1916: 3.6 million dead/wounded + 2.1 million captured
- **Britain as banker**: $4.3 billion in inter-Allied credits by April 1917; but increasingly dependent on American markets
- **US entry** (April 1917): military significance minimal for 12-15 months but productive strength unmatched — 2.5x Germany's industrial output
- Total Allies: $57.7B expenditure / 40.7 million mobilized vs. Central Powers: $24.7B / 25.1 million
- Germany's advantages could not over the long run outweigh the Allies' massive superiority in economic muscle

## Chapter 6: The Coming of a Bipolar World, Part Two, 1919-1942

### The Postwar International Order

- WWI cost ~8 million combat dead, 7 million permanently disabled; total casualties ~60 million; material cost ~$260 billion
- The three rising powers all withdrew: Germany defeated, Russia collapsed into Bolshevik isolation, USA retreated into isolationism — leaving 1920s affairs focused on weakened France and Britain
- France's "search for security": Little Entente, alliances with Poland/Czechoslovakia/Romania; but 1919 settlement could not create ethnically coherent states
- Financial chaos: interlocking debts; no European country could return to gold standard; real center of world finance migrated to the US but America was structurally ill-suited for that role
- Wall Street Crash triggered chain reaction: by summer 1932 industrial production in many countries half of 1928; world trade shrunk by one-third
- International system fragmented into rival currency blocs; democracies in no frame of mind to cooperate against territorial revisionism

### The Challengers

- **Italy under Mussolini**: diplomatic prominence masked economic weakness; only 2.8% of world manufacturing by 1938; structurally dependent on imported coal, oil, rubber — 80% passing through Gibraltar or Suez; main battle tank only 3.5 tons; air force claimed 8,500 planes but only 454 bombers and 129 fighters of first-rate quality
- **Japan**: manufacturing production index 176 (1920) → 552 (1938) vs. world average 93→183; but near-total reliance on petroleum imports; finance minister **Takahashi** assassinated by militarists when he sought to moderate military spending; attacking the US seemed folly yet not striking meant economic strangulation
- **Germany**: by 1938, 52% of government expenditure and 17% of GNP poured into armaments — more than Britain, France, and US *combined*; but held only 1% of world gold reserves; raw material dependence acute; economic strain created temptation for conquest as fiscal relief — Czech national bank assets, ores, and arms industry were seized immediately

### The Offstage Superpowers

- **USSR**: manufacturing down to 13% of 1913 levels by 1920; Stalin's collectivization drove private consumption to 51-52% of GNP; human cost reckoned in millions; but by late 1930s industrial output surpassed France, Japan, Italy and probably Britain; purges eliminated 90% of generals and 80% of colonels — USSR actually *weaker* militarily at end of 1930s than five years earlier
- **United States**: produced larger output than the other six Great Powers *taken together* in the 1920s; but GNP plummeted during Depression; share of world manufacturing fell from 43.3% (1929) to 28.7% (1938); held **41.7% of world war potential** in 1937 while spending only 1.5% on defense; two-thirds of American steel plants were *idle* — other powers running at full capacity

### The Unfolding Crisis, 1931-1942

- Manchurian crisis (1931): League shown impotent; three democracies incapable of united action
- Appeasement logic: Britain's chiefs of staff said it was impossible to safeguard interests "against Germany, Italy and Japan at the same time"
- Munich (1938): Hitler the only leader actually prepared to fight; gave him more than he might have gained by war
- **Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact** (August 1939): enhanced Germany's strategic position; made war over Poland virtually inevitable
- **Blitzkrieg**: combined arms, tactical air power, decentralized offensive warfare — shattered larger but poorly organized Allied forces in 1940
- **Operation Barbarossa** (June 1941): Germany's fatal overreach — killing or capturing 3 million Russians in four months didn't solve the problem; "This was a limitless war, and the Third Reich was not properly equipped to fight it"
- **Pearl Harbor** (December 1941): Japan's navy calculated warship strength would fall from 70% of US (1941) to 30% (1944) — giving powerful incentive to strike before the sleeping giant awakened
- Both Germany and Japan understood the arithmetic; both gambled on early decisive victories before Allied productive superiority could be mobilized
