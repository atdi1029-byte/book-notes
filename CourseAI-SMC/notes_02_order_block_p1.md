## Core Concept: What is an Order Block?

An Order Block is a supply/demand zone where institutional
participants entered the market in bulk — "buying wholesale"
(comprar no atacado). It originates from the **Law of Cause
and Effect**: consolidation (cause) produces directional
movement (effect). The market spends most of its time moving
laterally, then produces impulses up or down.

On a higher timeframe, what appears as a single candle (the
Order Block) is actually a consolidation zone visible on
lower timeframes. This is the **fractal nature** of markets —
smaller timeframes combine to form the movements of larger
timeframes. When you see an Order Block on the 1H chart, drop
to the 5m and you'll almost always find a consolidation
(lateralization) inside it.

**Key insight**: The Order Block IS the cause. The strong
movement that follows IS the effect. Without understanding
cause and effect, you cannot understand Order Blocks.

## Validity Principles (Requirements)

For an Order Block to be **minimally valid**, two conditions
must be met:

### 1. Strong Movement (Movimento Forte)
The impulse coming out of the block must show genuine
institutional interest. Three characteristics define a
strong movement:
- **Full-bodied candles** (candos mais cheios) — large
  real bodies, not wicks
- **Velocity** — the movement happens quickly, not
  dragging sideways
- **Steep angle** — price moves almost vertically, not
  at a shallow/lateral angle

A movement that drifts sideways, is slow, and has small
bodies does NOT show interest. When price truly moves in
one direction with conviction, institutional participation
is present.

### 2. Break of Structure (BMS)
The strong movement must break a structure — renew a high,
break a low, break a valid swing point. Without this,
the block lacks confirmation.

**BMS** (Break of Market Structure) is the general term
for any structure break. It divides into two specific types:

#### CHoCH (Change of Character)
- Breaks a **valid swing low** in an uptrend (bearish)
- Breaks a **valid swing high** in a downtrend (bullish)
- Represents a change in market character/direction
- Example: price is trending up, leaves a swing low,
  then breaks below that swing low → CHoCH

#### BOS (Break of Structure / Pivot)
- **Renewal of highs** in an uptrend (bullish continuation)
- **Renewal of lows** in a downtrend (bearish continuation)
- Classic 1-2-3 pivot structure
- Example: price makes a low, retraces up, then makes
  a new lower low → BOS (bearish)

**Relationship**: BMS = CHoCH + BOS. When someone says
"the Order Block needs BMS," they mean it needs either
a CHoCH or a BOS — any type of structure break.

## Quality Principles (Enhancements, Not Requirements)

These are NOT required for validity but significantly
improve the quality of an Order Block setup:

### 1. No RSI Divergence
When price renews a high and RSI also renews its high
(no divergence), it indicates a "healthy" move. This is
a quality enhancement, not a validity requirement. Having
no divergence adds confidence; having divergence is a
warning sign but doesn't automatically invalidate the block.

### 2. Liquidity Capture Position
An Order Block located at a position where liquidity was
swept is considered stronger. When price sweeps equal highs
or equal lows (capturing stop losses), then reverses with
a strong move, the block at that extreme tends to be
more reliable.

This is related to the concept of **POI (Point of Interest)**
in traditional SMC: an Order Block located at a micro-
manipulation of price (liquidity sweep).

### 3. Trend Alignment (Favor da Tendência)
Order Blocks that align with the prevailing trend are
generally better. Trading WITH the trend is always an
additional positive factor.

## Order Block Types

### Bullish Order Block (Demand Zone)
- Located at the **bottom** of a strong upward movement
- Specifically: the **last bearish candle** before the
  strong bullish impulse
- If the last bearish candle is very small, extend the
  zone to include the last 2 bearish candles
- Price returns to this zone → buy opportunity

### Bearish Order Block (Supply Zone)
- Located at the **top** of a strong downward movement
- Specifically: the **last bullish candle** before the
  strong bearish impulse
- Price returns to this zone → sell opportunity

## Common Beginner Mistake: Counter-Trend Blocks

The instructor emphasizes this is the BIGGEST mistake
beginners make. New traders learn to identify Order Blocks,
then trade them AGAINST the trend:

**Bad example**: Price is in a downtrend, falling in steps.
Trader finds an old demand block far below current price.
Price eventually drops to it. Trader buys. Price blasts
right through the block and keeps falling.

**Better approach**: Trade Order Blocks that form DURING
the trend, as continuation entries:
- In an uptrend: buy the demand blocks that form at each
  pullback as price stair-steps higher
- In a downtrend: sell the supply blocks that form at
  each pullback as price stair-steps lower

These continuation blocks are far more reliable than
trying to catch a reversal at an old counter-trend block.

**Exception**: Counter-trend blocks CAN work when there is
strong confluent context — multiple concepts aligned
(liquidity sweep + volume profile VPOC + higher timeframe
block + accumulation pattern + CHoCH confirmation). But
this requires advanced reading, not just "there's a block
here, I'll buy."

## Trading Order Blocks with Confirmation

Even when an Order Block is valid, it doesn't automatically
mean you should trade it. The instructor distinguishes
between:

1. **Identifying** the Order Block (what this lesson teaches)
2. **Deciding whether to trade it** (requires context —
   covered in future lessons)

For counter-trend blocks that DO have strong context, the
ideal approach is:
- Wait for price to reach the block zone
- Watch for **accumulation** to form on top of the block
  (on a lower timeframe)
- Look for a **liquidity sweep** within that accumulation
- Wait for a **CHoCH with interest** (momentum shift)
- Enter on the retest of the CHoCH

This confirmation approach is covered in the POI lessons.

## Market Flow and Macro Control

**Operating with the flow** means:
- Following the BMS direction on the higher timeframe
- Following the sustained trend direction
- Aligning with the macro control of the market

**ICOF concept** (briefly mentioned): When price transitions
from a disequilibrium state (trend) to an equilibrium state
(lateral movement), this represents a change of character
in market nature. Within this lateral movement, signs of
buyer or seller control may emerge:
- Price spending more time near the top of the range
- Resistance being tested multiple times
- Gradual buildup of demand pressure

When these signs appear and price breaks out, the new
trend's Order Blocks (continuation blocks) become
high-quality trading opportunities — even though you're
technically trading against the OLD trend, you're trading
WITH the new macro control.

## Key Vocabulary

| Term | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| **Order Block (OB)** | Supply/demand zone from institutional bulk entry |
| **BMS** | Break of Market Structure (general term) |
| **CHoCH** | Change of Character — break of valid swing high/low |
| **BOS** | Break of Structure — renewal of high/low (pivot) |
| **POI** | Point of Interest — OB at a liquidity manipulation |
| **Bullish OB** | Demand zone (last bearish candle before impulse up) |
| **Bearish OB** | Supply zone (last bullish candle before impulse down) |

| **Lateralização** | Consolidation / sideways movement (the "cause") |
| **Fractalidade** | Fractal nature — same patterns across timeframes |
| **ICOF** | Change of state: trend → lateral (disequilibrium → equilibrium) |
| **VPOC** | Volume Profile Point of Control |
