Glossary

Term Definition
Order Block (OB) A supply or demand zone created by institutional bulk entry — "buying wholesale." On lower timeframes it appears as a consolidation; on higher timeframes as a single candle zone.
BMS Break of Market Structure — the general term for any structural break. Encompasses both CHoCH and BOS.
CHoCH Change of Character — breaking a valid swing high or swing low, signaling a potential reversal in market direction.
BOS Break of Structure — renewal of a high (bullish) or low (bearish) in a classic 1-2-3 pivot pattern. Continuation signal.
POI Point of Interest — an Order Block located at a micro-manipulation of price, typically a liquidity sweep. A higher-quality OB.
Movimento Forte Strong movement — characterized by full-bodied candles, velocity, and a steep angle. Indicates institutional participation.
Lateralização Consolidation / sideways movement — the "cause" that precedes a directional "effect." Hidden inside every Order Block on a lower timeframe.
ICOF Change of state from disequilibrium (trend) to equilibrium (lateral). When market nature shifts, new control emerges within the range.
VPOC Volume Profile Point of Control — the price level with the highest traded volume. When it aligns with an OB, adds quality to the zone.
Bullish OB Demand zone — the last bearish candle before a strong upward impulse.
Bearish OB Supply zone — the last bullish candle before a strong downward impulse.

What Is an Order Block?

An Order Block is a zone on the chart where institutional players entered the market in bulk — the instructor calls it "comprar no atacado" (buying wholesale). Unlike retail traders who scale in gradually, institutions concentrate their entries into a block of orders, creating a zone of intense supply or demand that leaves a visible footprint on the chart. When price returns to this zone later, the remaining unfilled orders often cause price to react, making it a high-probability area for trade entries.

The concept is deceptively simple on the surface: find the last bearish candle before a strong bullish move (demand block), or the last bullish candle before a strong bearish move (supply block). But the instructor spends the entire 44-minute lesson building the reasoning behind this identification, because without understanding WHY order blocks work, traders will misidentify them, trade them in the wrong context, and lose money consistently.

Every sustained price movement — every strong impulse that actually goes somewhere — is caused by institutional participation. When you see price drifting sideways with small candles, going nowhere, that is the absence of institutional interest. When you see price ripping in one direction with full-bodied candles, covering distance quickly at a steep angle, institutions are involved. The Order Block is where they placed their bets, and the strong move that follows is the proof that those bets were real.

The Law of Cause and Effect

Everything in SMC flows from one foundational principle: the Law of Cause and Effect. The instructor opens the lesson with this, calling it "the first point of everything." The cause is consolidation — sideways price action where the market is building energy, accumulating or distributing positions. The effect is the directional move that follows — the impulse that shoots price up or down. Markets spend the majority of their time in the cause phase (lateral), punctuated by relatively brief effect phases (trending).

This law is the engine behind Order Blocks. Every Order Block is a cause. The consolidation happening inside the block — visible on lower timeframes — is the accumulation or distribution of institutional positions. The strong move that explodes out of the block is the effect: the market responding to the imbalance that was built during the cause. If you see a strong move without a preceding cause, you should be skeptical. If you see a cause without a subsequent effect, the block is not validated. The two are inseparable.

Cause → Effect Pattern

Price rises → moves sideways (cause) → rises again (effect) → moves sideways (cause) → drops (effect). Each sideways phase on a higher timeframe is potentially an Order Block. Drop to a lower timeframe and you will see the consolidation that formed it.

Fractal Markets & Timeframes

The instructor spends significant time on why an Order Block looks like a single zone on one timeframe but reveals its internal structure on another. Markets are fractal — the same patterns repeat across every timeframe. Smaller timeframe candles combine to form the candles of larger timeframes. The consolidation patterns on the 5-minute chart become the Order Block candles on the 1-hour chart. This is not abstract theory; it is a mechanical reality of how charts are constructed.

He demonstrates this live: starting on the 5-minute chart where a clear consolidation zone is visible — price moving sideways, building a range. Switching to the 15-minute chart, the consolidation is still visible but starting to compress. On the 30-minute chart, it has disappeared entirely, replaced by what looks like a single bearish candle followed by a strong bullish impulse — the classic Order Block appearance. The lesson is that what you identify as an Order Block on a higher timeframe ALWAYS contains a consolidation on a lower timeframe. If you cannot find that consolidation when you drill down, you may be misidentifying the block.

This fractal perspective also explains why experienced traders use multiple timeframes. You identify the Order Block on the higher timeframe (where it appears as a clean zone), then drop to the lower timeframe to understand what is happening inside it — where the consolidation boundaries are, where the institutional entry likely concentrated. This gives you a more precise entry and tighter stop placement.

Validity Principles REQUIRED

The instructor makes a crucial distinction that many SMC courses gloss over: validity principles versus quality principles. Validity is binary — the block either meets these criteria or it does not count as an Order Block at all. Quality is a spectrum — additional factors that make a valid block stronger or weaker. Conflating the two leads to either taking every block (ignoring quality) or adding so many requirements that you never take a trade (treating quality as validity).

1. Strong Movement (Movimento Forte)

The impulse that comes out of the Order Block must demonstrate genuine institutional interest. Three observable characteristics define a strong movement:

The Three Signs of Institutional Interest

Full-bodied candles (candos mais cheios) — large real bodies relative to wicks. The bodies show actual buying or selling pressure, not indecision.

Velocity — the movement happens quickly. Price covers distance in few candles rather than grinding slowly over many bars. A move that takes 50 candles to travel the same distance as one that takes 8 candles is fundamentally different in character.

Steep angle — price moves almost vertically, not at a shallow or lateral slope. A 70-80 degree impulse shows conviction; a 20-30 degree drift shows indifference. The instructor draws the contrast: a move that "falls almost straight down" versus one that "drifts sideways, barely moving" — only the first qualifies.

When all three characteristics are present, you can be confident that institutional participants drove the move. When even one is missing, the "move" may just be noise — retail traders pushing price around without conviction, creating zones that will not hold on retest.

2. Break of Structure (BMS)

The strong movement must break a structural level — it must renew a high, break a low, violate a valid swing point. A move can be strong in terms of candle bodies and speed, but if it does not actually break any structure, it has not proven itself. The structure break is the market's confirmation that the move is real and sustained, not just a brief spike that reverses immediately.

The instructor illustrates with a comparison: price rises strongly toward a previous high. In Scenario A, it approaches the high but stalls, barely touching it with a small wick before falling back — that is NOT a valid structure break. In Scenario B, price blasts through the high with a full-bodied candle and continues higher — that IS a valid break. "It renews properly, it goes up there, it flows." The conviction of the break matters.

BMS, CHoCH, and BOS Explained

This is the section where many beginners get confused by terminology, and the instructor addresses it head-on. BMS (Break of Market Structure) is the umbrella term — it simply means "a structure break happened." But BMS comes in two flavors, and understanding the difference is essential for reading market intent.

CHoCH — Change of Character

CHoCH occurs when price breaks a valid swing point against the prevailing direction. In an uptrend, price leaves swing lows as it climbs; when price breaks below one of those swing lows, that is a CHoCH — a signal that the market's character may be changing from bullish to bearish. In a downtrend, the opposite: breaking above a valid swing high signals potential shift to bullish character.

CHoCH is inherently a reversal signal. When an Order Block produces a strong move that results in a CHoCH, you are looking at a potential trend change. The block at the origin of that CHoCH move tends to be significant because it represents the point where institutions decided to reverse the market's direction.

BOS — Break of Structure (Pivot)

BOS is the continuation counterpart to CHoCH. It follows the classic 1-2-3 pivot pattern: in a downtrend, price makes a low (1), retraces up (2), then makes a new lower low (3) — that renewal of the low is a BOS. In an uptrend: higher high (1), pullback (2), new higher high (3). BOS confirms that the existing trend is continuing with institutional backing.

When an Order Block produces a strong move that results in a BOS, you are looking at a continuation setup. The block formed during a pullback, and the move out of it renewed the trend's extreme — this is the bread-and-butter of trend-following with SMC.

BMS = CHoCH + BOS

The Relationship

"An Order Block needs BMS to be valid" — this is a correct statement. It means the block needs EITHER a CHoCH OR a BOS. Both are types of BMS. When you hear "BMS," think "structure break of any kind." When you need to specify which kind, use CHoCH (reversal) or BOS (continuation).

Bullish vs Bearish Order Blocks

Bullish Order Block (Demand Zone)

A Bullish OB sits at the bottom of a strong upward movement. Specifically, it is the last bearish candle (or last downward candle) before the strong bullish impulse begins. This is where institutions placed their buy orders in bulk before driving price higher. When price returns to this zone on a pullback, those same institutions — or others recognizing the zone — tend to buy again, creating a bounce.

If the last bearish candle is unusually small (a tiny body, a doji), the instructor advises extending the zone to include the last two bearish candles. This is a practical reading adjustment, not a rule change — you are simply acknowledging that the institutional footprint may span more than one candle in that specific price action context.

Bearish Order Block (Supply Zone)

The mirror image: a Bearish OB sits at the top of a strong downward movement. It is the last bullish candle before the strong bearish impulse. Institutions sold or shorted in bulk here, and the zone becomes resistance on any subsequent rally back to the area. Same rules apply — if the last bullish candle is tiny, consider extending to the last two.

Quality Principles ENHANCEMENT

Once a block meets the validity requirements (strong move + BMS), these additional factors determine how good the setup is. None of them are required, but each one present adds confidence.

1. No RSI Divergence

The instructor pulls up the RSI on a live chart example. When price makes a new high and RSI also makes a new high (no divergence), the move is "healthy" — momentum confirms price. When RSI diverges (price makes a higher high but RSI makes a lower high), it is a warning sign that momentum is fading. No divergence on the move that validates the block is a quality enhancement — not a dealbreaker, but a confidence booster.

2. Liquidity Capture Position

An Order Block that sits at a position where liquidity was captured is stronger. The classic setup: equal highs form above (creating a pool of stop losses), price sweeps through those highs (triggering the stops and capturing liquidity), then reverses hard. The Order Block at the extreme of that sweep — where the reversal originated — benefits from the liquidity capture. It is both a valid block AND a POI (Point of Interest) in traditional SMC terminology.

3. Trend Alignment

Blocks that align with the prevailing trend direction carry more weight. This is not a hard rule — counter- trend blocks can work with strong confluent context — but trend-aligned blocks have one extra factor in their favor. The instructor's emphasis: "It is one more positive point. Being against a clear trend is a negative point, but sometimes there are so many other positive factors that it is worth the risk, depending on the scenario."

Trading with the Trend

The instructor repeatedly returns to one theme: context is everything. An Order Block is not a buy or sell signal in isolation — it is one piece of a puzzle. The puzzle is context: the reunion of multiple concepts, well-configured, aligned in the same location, forming a genuine opportunity. Trend direction is one piece. RSI behavior is another. Liquidity capture is another. Volume Profile confirmation is another. When multiple pieces align, the probability tilts in your favor. When only one piece (the block exists) is present, you are gambling.

"A context is a reunion of concepts. Several good concepts, well-configured, reunited in the same location — they form a beautiful trade opportunity. Whether in favor or against the trend." — Instructor

That said, trading with the trend provides an inherent advantage. In an uptrend where price is stair-stepping higher, each pullback creates a new bullish Order Block. Buying these continuation blocks as price pulls back into them is far more reliable than trying to catch a reversal at an old block from a previous trend. The trend provides the wind at your back — you need fewer confirming factors because the macro direction is already helping you.

The Biggest Beginner Mistake

Counter-trend block trading is the most common mistake the instructor sees in new SMC traders — and one he admits making himself early on.

The pattern is always the same: a trader learns to identify Order Blocks, finds an old demand zone far below current price, and waits for price to "come back to it." Price has been trending down, creating supply blocks on the way, but the trader ignores all of those in favor of the one demand block that sits at the bottom. Price eventually reaches it and the trader buys — only to watch price blast right through the zone and keep falling.

The insidious part is that this strategy works sometimes. One out of several attempts, price does bounce from the old block, and the trader takes that as validation. "You keep doing it wrong because every now and then you relive that one good experience," the instructor says. The occasional success reinforces a fundamentally flawed approach.

The correct approach is to trade the continuation blocks that form WITH the trend:

Continuation vs Reversal Blocks

In an uptrend: buy the demand blocks that form at each pullback as price stair-steps higher. Each new block has the trend behind it.

In a downtrend: sell the supply blocks that form at each rally as price stair-steps lower. The macro direction supports each entry.

Counter-trend: only when there is overwhelming confluence — liquidity sweep, volume profile alignment, higher-timeframe block, accumulation pattern, and CHoCH confirmation all present. This is an advanced setup, not a beginner strategy.

Confirmation Approach

Even when a counter-trend block has strong context, the instructor recommends a confirmation-based entry rather than a blind limit order at the zone. The process he outlines:

Wait for price to reach the Order Block zone. On a lower timeframe (e.g., 5-minute), watch for an accumulation to form on top of the block — price moving sideways, building a base. Within that accumulation, look for a liquidity sweep — price dips below the range, triggering stops, then reclaims. Then wait for a CHoCH with interest — a momentum shift where price breaks a swing high within the accumulation, confirming that buyers have taken control. Enter on the retest of that CHoCH level.

This approach sacrifices some of the potential move (you will not catch the exact bottom) in exchange for significantly higher win rates. You are not predicting that the block will hold — you are waiting for the market to SHOW you that it is holding, then entering with confirmation.

Market Flow & Macro Control

The instructor introduces a nuanced concept toward the end of the lesson: the difference between trading with the trend and trading with the macro control. These are not always the same thing, and understanding the distinction separates intermediate from advanced traders.

Consider a market that has been in a strong downtrend. At some point, price stops making new lows and begins to move sideways — it enters a lateral range. This is the ICOF (change of state): the market transitions from disequilibrium (trending) to equilibrium (ranging). Within this range, signs of buyer control may emerge: price spends more time near the top, resistance gets tested repeatedly, demand pressure builds.

When price eventually breaks out of the range to the upside, a new trend begins. The Order Blocks that form during this early uptrend are excellent continuation entries — even though you are technically trading "against the trend" if you define trend by the prior downtrend. You are trading WITH the new macro control that was established during the lateral phase. The instructor emphasizes: "You are going in favor of market control, which is the part where the lateralization showed clear buyer dominance."

This is why rigid "only trade with the trend" rules can be limiting. The better framework is: trade with the flow — the current directional intent of the dominant players, whether that aligns with the historical trend or represents a fresh shift in control.

Key Takeaways

Order Block = Institutional Footprint

Every Order Block is a zone where institutions entered in bulk. The consolidation (cause) is hidden inside on lower timeframes. The strong move (effect) is the proof. Without the strong move, you have no proof.

Validity is Binary, Quality is a Spectrum

Two requirements for validity: strong movement + break of structure (BMS). Everything else — RSI confirmation, liquidity capture, trend alignment — adds quality but is not required. Do not conflate the two categories.

BMS Taxonomy

BMS is the umbrella term. CHoCH (reversal) and BOS (continuation) are the two specific types. When someone says "the block needs BMS," they mean either CHoCH or BOS — any structure break that confirms the move was real.

Trend-Aligned Continuation Blocks Win

Trading Order Blocks that form during pullbacks within an existing trend is far more reliable than trying to catch reversals at old counter-trend blocks. The occasional counter-trend success is a trap that reinforces bad habits.

Context Over Concept

An Order Block alone is not a trade signal. Context — the reunion of multiple aligned concepts — determines whether a block is worth trading. No amount of technical validity replaces the need for contextual reading of the market environment.

Flow Over Rigid Trend Rules

"Trade with the trend" is a simplification. The more accurate principle is "trade with the flow" — the current directional intent of dominant players, which may be a fresh shift emerging from a lateral phase rather than the historical trend direction.
