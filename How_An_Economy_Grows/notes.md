## Introduction

### The Failure of Modern Economics

- Economics has lagged behind all other sciences in predictive power and achievement
- If economists had NASA's forecasting skill, the Galileo mission "would have missed its orbit of Saturn, but in all likelihood the rocket would have turned downward on lift-off, bored though the Earth's crust, and exploded somewhere deep in the magma"
- **2007 crisis**: Very few economists saw the biggest economic catastrophe in three generations coming; three years later, their remedies seem "frankly ridiculous"

> "We are told that we must go deeper into debt to fix our debt crisis, and that we must spend in order prosper."

### Keynesianism vs. Austrian Economics

- The disconnect stems from near-universal acceptance of **John Maynard Keynes**' theories
- **Keynes**: Early-twentieth century English academic who made simple ideas seem hopelessly complex
- Proposed **economic duality**: one set of laws for micro level (individuals/families), another for macro level (nations/governments)
- Similar to quantum mechanics: different physical laws for small particles vs. everything else

### Historical Context of Keynesian Triumph

- Keynes's work emerged at the tail end of the most expansive economic period in world history (19th-early 20th century)
- **Free market capitalism** of the United States was unique in its preference for individual rights and limited government
- But decentralizing elements of capitalism threatened rigid power structures worldwide
- Capitalist expansion created visible wealth/poverty extremes, leading progressives to seek more "equitable" alternatives
- **Keynes gave cover to central authorities and social utopians** who believed economic activity needed planning from above

### The Core Keynesian Idea

- Governments could smooth out free market volatility by **expanding money supply and running large budget deficits** when times were tough
- This offered politicians pain-free solutions: promise to increase employment and boost growth without raising taxes or cutting services
- **Keynesianism = economic equivalent of miracle weight-loss programs** — irrational but soothing, attractive on campaign trail

> "Keynesianism permits governments to pretend that they have the power to raise living standards with the whir of a printing press."

### Keynesianism's Institutional Advantages

- **Pro-government bias**: Keynesians more likely to receive top government economic appointments than Austrians
- Universities producing finance ministers and Treasury secretaries gained prestige, favoring professors who supported Keynesian ideas
- **Financial institutions** (banks, investment firms) prefer Keynesian environment of easy money and loose credit
- Belief that government should backstop investments helps financial firms pry open pocketbooks of skittish investors
- **Self-fulfilling mutual admiration society**: top economists inbred with loyalty to Keynesian principles

### Austrian School Opposition

- When Keynesians burst onto scene (1920s-30s), they clashed with the **"Austrian School"** (followers of **Ludwig von Mises**)
- **Austrians argued**: recessions are necessary to compensate for unwise decisions during booms
- Booms created by false signals from government "stimulating" economies with low interest rates
- **Keynesians**: mitigate the busts
- **Austrians**: prevent artificial booms

### The Great Depression Myth

- Keynesians take as gospel that their policies ended the Great Depression
- Many argue that without government stimulus (including WWII expenditures), we would never have recovered
- **Missing from analysis**: Depression was longest and most severe crisis in modern history AND the first ever dealt with using full range of Keynesian tools
- **Whether interventions were cause or cure is apparently a debate no serious "economist" thought worth having**

### The Astrologer Analogy

- With Keynesians controlling economics departments, financial ministries, and investment banks, "it's as if we have entrusted astrologers instead of astronomers to calculate orbital velocities of celestial bodies"
- No matter how often economists flub missions, how many rockets explode, **no one questions their models**

### The Central Argument

- Most people assume economists are clueless because economics is "so vast, vast, murky, and illogical that true predictive power is beyond even the best and most educated minds"
- **But what if**: the economic duality proposed by Keynesians doesn't exist?
- **What if**: economics is much simpler than that?
- **What if**: what is good for the goose is good for the gander?
- **What if**: it were equally impossible for a family or a nation to spend its way to prosperity?

### Peter Schiff's Advantage

- Schiff accurately forecasted the 2008 crash not due to superior intelligence, but because of **solid and fundamental understanding of basic economic principles**
- His father **Irwin Schiff** provided him with the basic tool kit through stories, allegories, and thought experiments
- One of those stories serves as the basis for this book

### Irwin Schiff: Background

- **Irwin Schiff**: figure of renown associated with national movement to resist federal income tax
- For 35+ years challenged IRS methods, arguing income tax is enforced in violation of Constitution's taxing clauses, 16th Amendment, and revenue laws
- Has written many books on the subject, openly challenged federal government in court
- At 82, remains incarcerated in federal prison, paying heavy personal price for his activities

### Irwin's Economic Awakening

- Born 1928 in New Haven, Connecticut, eighth child of lower-middle-class immigrant family
- Father was union man; entire extended family enthusiastically supported **Roosevelt's New Deal**
- Entered University of Connecticut in 1946 to study economics
- Nothing in background or temperament suggested he would reject dominant orthodoxy and embrace out-of-fashion Austrians — **but he did**

### Irwin's Conversion Process

- Had power of original thinking, combined with outsized belief in himself
- Sensed that lessons he was learning didn't fully mesh with reality
- Dug deeper into full spectrum of economic theory across books by libertarian thinkers like **Henry Hazlitt** and **Henry Grady Weaver**
- Conversion was gradual (took full decade of 1950s to complete)
- Eventually emerged as full-blooded believer in **sound money, limited government, low taxes, personal responsibility**
- By 1964, enthusiastically supported **Barry Goldwater** for president

### Bretton Woods and the Gold Standard

- At **1944 Bretton Woods Monetary Conference**: United States persuaded world nations to back currencies with dollars instead of gold
- US pledged to exchange one ounce of gold for every 35 dollars and owned 80% of world's gold
- Arrangement widely accepted

### The Gold Drain Crisis

- 40 years of monetary inflation by Keynesian money managers at Federal Reserve caused pegged gold price to be severely undervalued
- This mismatch led to the **"gold drain"**: mass run by foreign governments (led by France in 1965) to redeem U.S. Federal Reserve Notes for gold
- Foreign governments quickly depleting U.S. reserves at 1932 prices

### Johnson Administration Response (1968)

- **President Lyndon Johnson's** economic advisors argued gold drain resulted not from bargain basement prices, but because foreign governments feared U.S. gold reserves were insufficient to back domestically held notes AND redeem foreign notes
- **Solution**: Remove required 25% gold backing from domestic dollars so reserves would be available for foreign dollar holders
- Presumably this would assuage foreign concerns and stop the gold hemorrhage
- **Irwin Schiff thought their reasoning was absurd**

### Irwin's Letter to Senator Tower

- Sent letter to **Texas Senator John Tower** (committee member reviewing gold issue)
- Explained US faced two choices: **deflate prices** to align with 1932 gold price, or **raise gold** to align with 1968 prices
- To adjust for 40 years of Keynesian inflation, America had to either deflate prices or devalue the dollar

### Irwin's Recommendation

- Argued **deflation would be most responsible course** — would restore lost purchasing power of the dollar
- Understood economists erroneously view falling prices as catastrophe
- Understood governments have natural preference for inflation (as will be explored in the book)
- Given these biases, argued authorities could at least **acknowledge prior debasement and officially devalue the dollar against gold**
- In such scenario, gold would have to be priced at **$105 per ounce**

### Irwin's Warning

- Feared a much more likely and dangerous third option: **government would do nothing** (which was precisely what they chose)
- "Then as now, the choice was between **facing the music or deferring the problem to future generations**. They deferred, and we are the future generation."

### The Hearings

- **Tower** was impressed with basic logic of Irwin's arguments and invited him to address the entire committee
- At hearings, all highly placed monetary experts from Federal Reserve, Treasury, and Congress testified that **removing gold backing would strengthen the dollar, cause gold prices to fall, usher in age of prosperity**

### Irwin's Testimony

- Asserted that removal of gold backing from U.S. currency would cause **gold prices to soar**
- More importantly, warned that **currency devoid of any intrinsic value would lead to massive inflation and unsustainable government debt**
- **This minority opinion was completely ignored, and gold backing was removed**

### Irwin's Predictions Vindicated

- Contrary to everything economists predicted, availability of additional reserves **failed to stop outflows of gold**
- Finally, in **1971 President Richard Nixon closed the window**, severing dollar's last link to gold
- Global economic system became completely based on worthless money
- Over next decade, United States experienced **nastiest outbreak of inflation in our history**
- **Gold headed towards $800 per ounce**

### The Birth of "The Fish Story"

- In 1972, Irwin set out to write first major attack on how Keynesian economics was putting US on unsustainable course
- Book: ***The Biggest Con: How the Government Is Fleecing You*** — enjoyed wide-spread critical acclaim and decent sales
- Among anecdotes: **story about three men on an island who fished with their hands**
- Story had genesis as simple time killer on family car trips — Irwin entertained his two young sons with basic economics lessons through funny stories
- This one became known as **"The Fish Story"**

### Evolution to Illustrated Book

- Allegory served as centerpiece of a chapter in *The Biggest Con*
- Eight years later, after readers commented on how much they loved the story, Irwin developed entire illustrated book around it
- ***How an Economy Grows and Why It Doesn't*** first published 1979
- Went on to achieve quasi-cult status among devotees of Austrian economics

### The 2010 Update

- Thirty years later, as US economy headed off a cliff and government repeated/redoubled mistakes of the past, Peter and Andrew Schiff decided to revise and update "The Fish Story" for new generation
- "Certainly, there has never been a greater need for a dose of economic clarity, and **the story is the best tool we know of to give people a better understanding of what makes our economy tick**."

### Scope and Purpose

- 2010 version "is in many ways more ambitious than the one Irwin drafted 30 years ago"
- Scope is wider, attempt to incorporate historical sequence is deeper
- "In fact, the story would best be described as **a riff on the original**"
- Hope book can appeal to people who go numb when economists drone on about concepts that seem to have nothing to do with reality
- Intend to show that **Keynesian model (governments can spend without consequence in belief that worthless money can be economic lubricant) is false and dangerous**

### The Stakes

- **Bad news**: when you take off rose-colored glasses all economists have forgotten they're wearing, you can see nation is confronting serious problems currently being made worse, not better
- **Good news**: if we allow ourselves clarity, then we can at least attempt to solve the problems
- While subject matter is deadly serious, approached project with humor that is absolutely vital in times of stress — "just as Irwin would have wanted it"

## Chapter 1: An Idea Is Born (continued)

### The Subsistence Economy

- Island has three inhabitants: **Able**, **Baker**, and **Charlie**
- Abundant fish population, each fish feeds one person for one day
- Primitive fishing technology: catching fish by hand in the water
- Each islander catches exactly one fish per day — just enough to survive
- Daily cycle: wake, fish, eat, sleep — no room for anything else
- Economy characterized by: **no savings**, **no credit**, **no investment**
- Everything produced is immediately consumed — nothing left to lend

### Able's Innovation: The Fish Catcher (Net)

- Able wants more from life: better clothing, shelter, leisure time, and "to direct feature films"
- Realizes improvement requires catching more than one fish per day
- Current constraint: all time devoted to hand-fishing for survival
- Able conceives the **fish catcher** (net) — a device to expand human reach and reduce fish escape ability

> "I have been inspired to create a device that will unlock oceans of fishing possibilities. When I'm finished, I'll spend less time fishing, and I'll never go hungry again." — Able

### The Cost of Innovation: Underconsumption and Risk

**Reality Check box** defines key economic principles:

**Underconsumption**: To build the net, Able must forgo fishing one day — sacrificing the fish (income) he would have otherwise caught and eaten. He defers consumption today to potentially consume more in the future.

**Risktaking**: Able has no guarantee the net will work or catch enough fish to compensate for his sacrifice. If it fails, he'll have "a bunch of string and an empty stomach" with no compensation from Baker and Charlie, who warned him of his folly.

**Capital**: Equipment built not for its own sake, but for producing something else that is wanted. Able doesn't want the net itself — he wants the fish. The net is valuable because it can get him more fish.

- Able completes the net through **self-sacrifice** (forgoing one day's consumption)
- That night: Baker and Charlie sleep with full stomachs; Able endures hunger pangs but hopes for a "fish-filled future"

### Testing the Net

- Baker and Charlie mock Able's invention initially
- Able learns to use the net and quickly catches his second fish of the day
- What normally took all three islanders all day (three fish), Able now accomplishes in half a day (two fish)
- **Productivity increase**: Able has doubled his output

> "Laugh it up, boys, but let's see who's laughing when I'm up to my armpits in fish guts." — Able

### The Economic Impact of Increased Productivity

**Reality Check box**:

> "By doubling his **productivity** Able is now able to produce more than he needs to consume. From gains in productivity all other economic benefits flow."

- Before the net: island had no savings
- Able's willingness to take a chance and go hungry led to the island's **first piece of capital equipment**
- Capital produced **savings** (spare fish production)
- This spare production is "the lifeblood of a healthy economy"

**Take Away Section**: Why only humans have economies

- For other species, economics = day-to-day survival amid scarce food, harsh elements, predators, disease, and rare innovation
- Humans different because of: **big brains** and **dexterous hands**
- These two traits together enable humans to build **tools and machines** that magnify our ability to extract more from the environment

**Thomas Woods** thought experiment cited:

> What kind of economy would we have if all machines and tools disappeared? Cars, tractors, iron smelters, shovels, wheelbarrows, saws, hammers, spears — everything made WITH OUR BARE HANDS?

- Without tools: life would be rough, no large game, limited harvest capacity, no factories, perhaps no scissors or nails
- Despite intelligence, we'd be "no better off, economically at least, than chimps and orangutans"
- **Tools create the possibility of an economy** — they magnify labor efficacy
- The more we can make, the more we can consume, the more prosperous we become

**Definition of economy**: "the effort to maximize the availability of limited resources (and just about every resource is limited) to meet as many human demands as possible."

- Tools, capital, and innovation are the keys to this equation
- **What makes economies grow**: finding better ways of producing more stuff that humans want
- This principle doesn't change regardless of economy size

## Chapter 2: Sharing the Wealth

### The Request to Share Capital

- Able now has extra time from increased productivity
- Baker and Charlie ask Able to share the innovative fish catcher
- Charlie: "Since you use that thingy only every other day, how about you let me use it the day you're out doing other stuff?"
- Baker: "C'mon, share the wealth, buddy"

### Able's Refusal and Concerns

- Able remembers his self-sacrifice, their scorn, and the risk he took
- Concerns: "What if they break my net? What if they just don't give it back?"
- Refuses to simply give the net away: "Sorry, guys, no can do. I made my own net, so can you."
- Charlie apprehensive about building his own — lacks experience and fears hunger during construction

### The Loan Proposal

- Charlie: "I get the shakes. I might starve to death before I make a decent net!"
- Baker proposes: "Lend us some of your surplus fish to eat while we make our own nets. That way we won't starve as we build, and we'll repay every fish we borrow from the extra fish we catch!"

### Able's Skepticism and Risk Assessment

- Able skeptical they might "lie on the beach and take the day off"
- Even if they build nets, the nets may not work
- Either way, they might never repay him — he'd lose his savings for nothing
- Baker and Charlie realize they're asking Able to **take a risk for no personal benefit**
- The lure of extra fish motivates them to devise an incentive

### The Birth of Interest

- They "crunched the numbers" and devised a **financial idea**
- Baker's offer: "For every fish you lend us, we'll pay you back **two**. That's a 100 percent profit."

> "Now **that interests** me!" — Able (no apparent irony)

- Able calculates: "If I lend them **two** fish I'll get back **four**. I'll be two fish richer without doing any work. I'll be a fish tycoon!"

### The Morality Question and Economic Reality

- Some might see Able as crossing a line — "making money off the backs of others' labor"
- Cartoon image shows Able as wealthy robber baron oppressing workers tied to railroad tracks
- **But**: even if Able intends only to enrich himself, "his greed, for lack of a better word, would provide a benefit that would have otherwise been unavailable"

### Able's Options Without Making the Loan

Able has four alternatives:

1. **Hold fish for future use** — most secure option, guaranteed no losses, but savings wouldn't grow
2. **Consume his savings** — immediate indulgence
3. **Build his own net rental company** — consume one reserve fish per day for two days to build two more nets (and rent them out)
4. **Lend the fish at interest** — chosen option

## Chapter 2 (continued): Sharing the Wealth

### Able's Options for Using Savings

- Able faces five possible uses for his two-fish savings:
  1. **Save** what he has saved (preserve capital)
  2. **Consume** what he has saved (immediate gratification)
  3. **Lend** what he has saved (become a creditor)
  4. **Invest** what he has saved (deploy capital productively)
  5. **Combine** the other four options

- Able's decision based on his own **risk appetite and reward preferences**
- Whatever he chooses, he benefits the island economy **without imposing burdens** on neighbors
- Able decides to **make the loan** to Baker and Charlie

### The Net Rental Business

- Able could rent nets to Baker and Charlie for half a fish each day
- Nets rental at 0.5 fish/day would give Able 1 fish/day income **without fishing himself**
- Baker and Charlie would catch 2 fish/day with rented nets, pay 0.5 fish rent, and still net 1.5 fish each — **50% more than before**
- But Able notices a flaw: Baker and Charlie might rent for 2 days, then use savings to build their own nets, leaving Able "only two fish ahead"

### The Lending Option: 100% Interest

- Able could **lend two fish at 100% interest** — getting four fish back if repaid
- Risk: Baker and Charlie might default and "stiff him"
- This becomes Able's chosen approach

### Reality Check: The Five Uses of Savings

> "Able (and society) can do only five things with savings: save, consume, lend, invest, or try a combination."

- Whatever Able does benefits the island economy without burdening neighbors
- His decision reflects personal risk/reward preferences

### The Loan's Impact on Island Productivity

- Thanks to Able's loan, **Baker and Charlie now have nets they didn't own before**
- Island's **collective fishing capacity doubles** from 3 fish/day to 6 fish/day
- Economy has doubled in size; future looks brighter

### Demand vs. Production: A Core Confusion

- Growth didn't happen because the three were "unsatisfied with their limited lifestyle"
- **Demand** (hunger/desire) was always present but **not sufficient** to achieve growth
- Humans always want more — more time, fun, choices — which requires more capital

> "The economy didn't grow because they consumed more. **They consumed more because the economy grew.**"

- This is a simple concept, but "amazing what modern economists can do with a simple concept"

### The Keynesian Error: Demand-Side Thinking

- **Most economists think demand can be increased by giving people more money to spend**
- But that doesn't change **real demand** — only nominal spending on already-produced items
- **Only by increasing supply** can people actually get more of what they demand

### Able's Profit as Compensation for Risk

- Some might see Able as exploiting needy neighbors by profiting without working
- But **Able's profit is compensation for the risks he takes**
- His ability to profit **doesn't hinder the advancement of his peers**

### The Mutual Benefit of Lending

- Baker and Charlie can build nets **without under-consuming** (going hungry)
- If they succeed, they improve their futures without hunger; the rest is "gravy… or more accurately, fish oil"
- If they fail and can't repay, **Able takes the loss**

> "Essentially, the lender can benefit only if the borrower benefits."

### The Envy Scenario: Forced Charity

- What if Baker and Charlie grew **envious** of Able's wealth and **demanded** a portion of his savings?
- If Able gave away fish as charity, asking nothing in return, what would Baker and Charlie do with it?
- Free from repayment, they'd likely **increase leisure time** rather than productive capacity
- Charity improves Able's popularity but **doesn't provide the economic boost that a business loan would**

> "While there is nothing inherently wrong with leisure (in fact, it is the goal of most human activity), Baker and Charlie's vacation would not increase the island's productive capacity by a herring."

- **Production** drives possibilities: more fish = more possibilities to eat more, do other activities, or do nothing at all

### Reality Check: Wealth and Greed

- What if Able is greedy and just wants to get richer?
- If the only way to grow savings (without working) is to **make them available to others**, why would he hoard?
- Otherwise wealth stays the same or shrinks as he consumes it
- The "best thing about private capitalism" is that it **forces those motivated by personal gain to raise the living standards of others**

### Take Away: Wealth is Relative

- **Wealth is always a relative term**
- In a primitive society, even the richest can't match the material well-being of the poor in an industrialized economy
- Medieval kings lacked basic amenities modern people take for granted: central heating, indoor plumbing, fresh vegetables in winter
- Baker and Charlie's two-fish-per-day diet seems luxurious to them, but from our perspective "hardly seems enviable"

### The Labor Theory of Value (Debunked)

- Degrees of wealth have always seemed unfair to some
- Central unease: belief that **the rich take wealth from others, creating the poor**
- **"Labor theory of value"**: profit created by paying workers less than they're worth
- In this view, entrepreneurs like Able can get rich **only by making others poor**

### Why This Idea is Wrong

- "This idea has everything to do with moral posturing, and nothing to do with reality"
- The rich get that way (initially) by **offering something of value to others**
- Able offers loans to those with inadequate savings; if he profits, it's because **the service he provides is valuable to others**

### Coercion vs. Voluntary Exchange

- If Able were a bully who could **steal half his neighbors' catch daily**, then his wealth would derive from their poverty
- These coercive actions **wouldn't increase the island's overall productive capacity** — just redistribute it
- More likely, **overall capacity would fall** as the oppressed cut back on work when fruits of labor are stolen

### Historical Examples of Coercion

- Large-scale coercion dominates history: **slavery, serfdom, peasantry**
- Workers respond to force when self-interest is denied, but **respond far better if they are beneficiaries of their labor**

### Economic Freedom and Productive Expansion

- **Examples of large-scale economic freedom are rare** in global history
- But when self-interest is allowed to flourish, **productive capacity expands quickly**

### Credit as Economic Freedom in Action

> "The use of credit is the perfect example of how economic freedom works to everyone's benefit."

- As long as lenders and borrowers are **free to strike their own terms**, collective results will be a success
- However, when the **market for loans is distorted by outside forces**, disaster usually ensues

## Chapter 3: The Many Uses of Credit

### Business Loans: The Best Use of Saved Capital

- Able decided to loan Baker and Charlie fish so they could build nets
- **Business loans expand production** — the best use of saved capital
- Lending money (or fish) to start a business is **no guarantee the venture will be successful**
- Borrower may not fully execute the initial plan

### Risk of Failed Ventures

- If Charlie and Baker failed to produce successful nets, Able's loan would be lost
- Loans to businesses that don't succeed **waste society's store of savings and diminish productive capacity**
- Lender may have trouble getting back principal, let alone interest
- **But business plans that work make up for the ones that don't**

### Other Types of Loans: Consumption and Emergency

- Business loans aren't the only option
- Able could also make **consumption loans** (e.g., vacation) or **emergency loans**

---

## Consumption Loans

### The Vacation Loan Request

- Suppose Baker and Charlie asked Able for a loan to **take a vacation** instead of building nets
- They promise to fish harder after resting and repay with interest

### Reality Check: Government Distortion of Credit Markets

- When **outside forces** (like government) encourage or demand that savers make loans for reasons unrelated to actual likelihood of repayment, **higher degrees of loss are inevitable**
- Such distortions **waste society's savings**

### Government Loan Incentives

- Governments try to "do something good" by influencing how savings are lent
- They pass laws making **some types of loans more appealing than others**
- But **government has no savings** — only individuals do
- If government incentives lead to loans that fail, **the loss falls to individuals who sacrificially under-consumed to create savings**

### The Disincentive Effect

- Able would be **much less inclined to lend** if forced to make risky loans (like fish hypnosis schemes)
- He may **not work as hard or sacrifice as much to save** if forced into bad lending

### The Vacation Loan Problem

- Baker and Charlie can't repay the vacation loan with interest **without expanding productive capacity**
- After vacation, they still catch only 1 fish/day
- To repay Able, they'd need to **cut consumption below 1 fish/day** in the future — living standards would drop

### Able's Wise Rejection

- Able should **deny the loan**
- The transaction would put savings at unnecessary risk
- Capital unavailable for more productive loans
- While he'll earn their scorn, he'll **prevent future hardship**
- **Loans to consumers that don't improve productive capacity are a burden to both lenders and borrowers**

## Emergency Loans

### The Pokalani Pox Outbreak

- A week later, Baker and Charlie are struck by **Pokalani Pox**, preventing them from fishing for a week
- Able's rejection of the "vacation" loan turns out to be **extremely fortunate**

### Hardship Loans vs. Consumption Loans

- Able is now in position to make a **hardship (emergency) consumer loan** from accumulated savings
- Ensures Baker and Charlie can eat and live to work another day
- Risk of nonrepayment is high, but **risk of not making the loan is higher**
- Unlike the consumption loan, **Baker and Charlie can perish if the emergency loan is not made**
- If they died, **the island would lose productive capacity**

### Why Emergency Loans Are Justified

- Emergency loan wouldn't have been possible if Able had already **given away savings through unproductive consumer loans**
- **Savings can mean the difference between life and death of society**

## Reality Check: Can Able Expand Credit?

### The Myth of Expanding Credit by Fiat

- Politicians and bankers often discuss the need to **"expand credit"** by increasing money available to lend
- But can this be done on command?
- In the fishing island, **how can Able lend more fish than he has saved?**
- **The island's total supply of credit is limited by its total supply of saved fish**

### Take Away: Credit Cannot Be Expanded Beyond Savings

- Government influences how savings are allocated via **loan guarantees, tax credits, and penalties**
- Individuals and businesses may be **more willing to apply for certain loans**; banks may be **more willing to grant them**
- More resources directed toward favored activities (home building, college, solar panels)

### The Central Planner Fallacy

- Central impulse: **government planners have a better idea of what's good for society than savers themselves**
- But **no evidence this is true**
- History littered with **grandiose schemes from government think tanks that simply didn't deliver**

### Separation of Cause and Effect

- Imposing a government layer between savers and borrowers **separates cause and effect of lending**
- Leads to **inefficient allocation of savings**

### Private Lending Discipline: Natural Selection for Business

- **Private lenders influenced only by financial results**, not political symbolism
- Businesses with successful models and strong owner track records **repay loans at higher rates**
- These business plans **attract willing lenders**
- Like **Darwin's natural selection**, this lending discipline **produces hardier species** — healthier companies and stronger economy

### When Financial Performance Becomes Secondary

- When financial performance becomes secondary, **loans to individuals or enterprises that don't succeed** in creating innovation or expanding capacity **weaken the economy by wasting savings**

### The Hidden Fact: Real Credit Limited by Savings

- Creation of constantly expanding money supply and government's "limitless ability to take on debt" have **hidden the fact that real credit is limited by a finite supply of savings**

### The Modern Misunderstanding

- People now assume all that's needed for a functioning credit market is **willing borrowers**
- But like any resource, **savings must be accumulated before it can be lent out**

## Chapter 4: Economic Expansion

### Productivity Gains Enable Economic Diversification

- Three islanders (Able, Baker, Charlie) now catching 2 fish/day with nets, shifted from 1 fish/day subsistence
- Decided to save portion of catch rather than consume all productivity gains
- Every couple days, consumed only one fish each—building savings through underconsumption
- Freed time enabled pursuit of other productive and leisure activities:
  - **Able**: designed better palm-leaf clothing
  - **Baker**: expanded diet/culinary skills, gathered coconuts
  - **Charlie**: built island's first hut

### The Mega Fish Catcher

- **Baker** proposed going "industrial"—building much larger capital equipment
- Designed elaborate **underwater fish trap** ("gizmo"):
  - Huge trap with one-way doors
  - Fish check in, don't check out
  - Would catch fish continuously day/night with minimal labor
  - Could eliminate fishing labor entirely if successful

### Capital Formation Through Joint Venture

- Baker couldn't build mega trap alone—insufficient savings, materials, skill
- Proposed **joint venture**: pool savings, underconsume together, dedicate full week to construction
- Risk assessment: no guarantee of success; could fail like Able's first net
- This time risking 20+ fish (pooled savings), not just one
- **Demand for more fish overpowered fear of losing savings**—they moved forward

### Success and Compound Growth

- Built island's first mega fish catcher successfully
- Delivered 20 fish/week on average with minimal effort—"no fuss, barely any muss"
- Only minor repairs/maintenance needed; nearly fully automated
- Rapid savings accumulation → built **second mega fish catcher**

### Interest Rates

**Max's optimal position**: Personal well-being intertwined with bank success—ideal to determine best interest rates

**Lending side risk-based pricing**:
- Lowest rates to most secure borrowers (highest repayment ability)
- Higher rates to riskier borrowers to compensate for added risk

**Deposit side incentive structure**:
- **Longer deposits = higher interest** (reduces bank's fish shortage risk)
- Shorter-term deposits = lower rates

> "Longer-held deposits lowered the bank's risk of a fish shortage. Accordingly, higher interest payments were offered to those willing to leave their fish on deposit for a while."

**Market-determined rates**: Interest rate system fluctuated according to market conditions largely beyond Goodbank's control

### Interest Rate Dynamics

**High savings environment**:
- Vault filled with fish
- Bank willing to drop loan rates
- Losses easier to bear in relative terms
- Healthy economy provides fertile environment for new businesses
- Lower rates charged to borrowers → lower payments to depositors → discourages savings

**Low savings environment**:
- Few fish in vault
- Loan defaults become critical with thin reserves
- Max charges higher rates to borrowers (compensates for increased risk)
- Offers higher rates to depositors to encourage more savings
- **Higher rates discourage borrowing and slow business growth**
- But higher rates also encourage savings
- Eventually coffers build up again and rates drop

**Time preference signal**: Lower savings rate indicates preference for immediate consumption
- Result: long-term capital investments (providing future goods) discouraged

### The Cyclical Mechanism

**Three regulatory forces**:
1. Desire to maximize returns on bank's deposits
2. Fear of losing capital on risky ventures
3. Individual time preferences for consumption

> "This cyclical interest rate mechanism—firmly regulated by the desire to maximize the returns on the bank's deposits, the fear of losing capital on risky ventures, and individual time preferences for consumption—produced a rate of interest that would stabilize the market."

**Banking encouraged saving**: Safety and convenience of bank made people save more
- Deferred consumption provides financing for capital projects
- Capital projects increase future production and raise living standards

> "Under Mr. Goodbank's wise and conservative care, the island's savings and commerce continue to grow."

## Chapter 6: Take Away Section

### Federal Reserve Control of Interest Rates

- **Federal Reserve** sets base interest rates for nearly 100 years
- Theoretically private bank, but practically an extension of Treasury Department
- Sets "federal funds" rate—doesn't dictate specific loan rates, but moves entire market up/down
- Banks charge public higher rates than what they pay Fed to borrow
- Theory: Fed economists can determine optimal interest rate to keep economy on track
- Can boost struggling economy by lowering rates (encouraging borrowing) or cool overheated economy by raising them

### Two Massive Flaws in Fed System

**Flaw #1: Central Planning vs. Marketplace**
- Assumes small group at Fed makes better decisions than millions of independent marketplace participants
- Fed has "no skin in the game"—doesn't generate savings, won't suffer if loans fail
- Fed doesn't bear consequences of bad decisions; people's savings and bank profits depend on wise stewardship
- Inherently inefficient lending without this direct connection

**Flaw #2: Political, Not Economic, Decision-Making**
- Fed decisions always determined by political rather than economic considerations
- Low rates make economy appear better, reduce mortgage costs, help financial firms profit
- Presidents seeking reelection pressure Fed for lower rates
- Fed policy makers want to be seen as helpful, not as recession-causers
- **Savers have no well-organized interest group**—voices never heard
- Consistent bias toward keeping rates too low rather than too high
- **Result: United States transformed from nation of savers to nation of borrowers**

### False Signals and Boom-Bust Cycles

- Rates too low relative to savings supply send false signals about economy health and investment viability
- Consumption hasn't been truly deferred (as would happen with market-based interest rates)
- Capital investments to satisfy future consumption much less likely to succeed
- Creates **phony booms followed by spectacular busts** (stocks, real estate)

## Take Away: Understanding Inflation

### The Definition Problem

> "One of the reasons that economists have been so successful in obscuring the source of inflation is that they have short-circuited the very definition of the word."

- **Common belief**: Rising prices means inflation; no rising prices means no inflation
- **Actual definition**: "Rising prices are merely the results of inflation! The inflation is the expansion of the money supply."
- **Pre-1990 dictionaries**: Defined inflation purely as money supply expansion
- **Newer editions**: "Hedged their bets"

### Inflation Can Exist Without Price Increases

- **True understanding**: Possible for prices to stay flat or fall while money supply inflates
- **During recession**: People wisely stop spending, demand drops, prices should fall
- **But expanding money supply** can counterbalance this, diminishing currency value
- **Result**: Prices may rise (if printing fast enough), stay flat, or fall less than they would without inflation

### Why Falling Prices Are Necessary in Recessions

- **Prices need to fall** to rebalance the economy
- **Recessions should be deflationary**: Falling prices cushion job losses and low employment
- **Natural process**: When prices fall far enough, people start spending again
  - Unneeded inventories worked off
  - Prices fall to level justified by supply and demand
- **Modern economists' error**: See falling prices as "never-ending abyss toward demand destruction"
  - Forget people eventually start spending when prices drop enough

### How Inflation Prevents Recovery

- **Keeping prices artificially high through inflation** prevents natural rebalancing from happening

### Housing Boom Impact on Economy

- Housing financing, construction, and furnishing became **central dynamo of U.S. economy**

- **Robert Shiller** (*Irrational Exuberance*): 1900-2000 home prices increased 3.4% annually
- Rate just slightly higher than inflation
- Prices tied to ability to pay (function of income and credit availability)
- **1997-2006**: home prices gained 19.4% per year on average
- Incomes barely budged — difference was **cheap, easy credit** from government policy
- Credit couldn't expand forever; when conditions tightened, nothing held prices up

- When housing market crested, easy money stopped flowing
- **Recession was inevitable and necessary** to rebalance economy
- Policymakers mistook the **cure for the disease** — treated contraction as the problem itself
- Bush and Obama administrations both aimed to restore pre-crash spending levels
- Question: where would the money come from with unemployment rising and home prices falling?

### Government Spending Solution

- If people can't spend, government must spend for them
- Government has no money — only what it collects in taxes or borrows/prints
- Creating **massive public debt** ($1.6 trillion per year and counting)
- Currently selling debt on open market, primarily to foreigners

### Two Options for U.S. Government

1. **Default** — tell creditors we can't pay, negotiate settlement
2. **Inflate** — print money to pay off maturing debt

- Both options lead to painful consequences
- Default offers possibility of real reckoning and fresh beginning (actually better alternative)
- **Inflation is worse but more politically expedient**

## Chapter 17: THE FISH HIT THE FAN

### Sinopian Withdrawal from Fish Reserve Notes

- When Sinopians (biggest buyers) reduced Fish Reserve Note purchases, supply overwhelmed demand and prices fell into a death spiral
- Bongobians and Dervishes joined the selling, creating panic with plenty of sellers and no buyers
- Fish Reserve Notes depreciated rapidly — visual metaphor shows notes buying fewer and fewer fish

### The Sinopian King's Decision

- Facing stacks of nearly worthless notes, the king prepared subjects "to bite the bullet," promising short-term pain would lead to long-term gain
- Sinopian economy was pushed into disarray as some businesses closed
- But other businesses "stepped in and used the spare capacity to make things the Sinopians actually needed"

### Sinopia's Economic Recovery

- Despite currency losses, Sinopia thrived because essential ingredients remained: fish catching, production, savings
- Savings previously locked in Fish Reserve Notes were redirected to local factories for domestic consumption
- More domestic production meant stores stocked with goods, increased inventories enabled prices to fall
- Living standards improved as savings stayed in Sinopian banks instead of being exported

### Usonia's Currency Collapse

- In Usonia, the meager domestic catch forced bank technicians to work harder and more creatively
- **Official fish** began shrinking at an alarming rate, fishflation flared out of control
- **Hyper fishflation**: official fish bundled in packages of 50, then 100 — islanders needed 200 fish per day just to stay alive
- Any savings in Fish Reserve Notes became "essentially worthless"

### Usonian Economic Deterioration

- Fewer products from Sinopia left Usonian retailers with diminished inventories
- Skimpier fish chasing fewer products caused **soaring prices**
- Senators attacked retailers for "price gouging," implementing **price controls** on products and services
- Price controls focused only on symptoms, making matters worse — manufacturers and retailers couldn't make profit, so they stopped selling
- **Black market** of illegally high-priced merchandise arose

### Capital Flight Restrictions

- Citizens tried depositing fish in offshore banks to protect savings from "senatorial slicing and dicing"
- Senators made it **illegal to move savings off the island**
- Fear of shrinking fish became so prevalent that no deposits were left in banks — every fish caught was immediately consumed
- Result: "no savings, no credit, and no investment" — back to pre-growth economy conditions

### The Stimulus Debate

- Senators discussed plans for the next stimulus — prior attempts "simply too small"
- Next round "would just have to be bigger" but "no one was quite sure what would be used as a stimulant"
- A Sinopian cargo ship appeared on the horizon, lifting spirits

### Sinopian Buyout of Usonian Assets

- Sinopian agents spread across the island with wheelbarrows of real fish and cartloads of Fish Reserve Notes
- Bought everything, "even the stuff that was nailed down"
- Since Usonians had no real fish anymore, Sinopians "could outbid everybody on everything"

### Asset Liquidation

- Sinopians bought and dismantled the **Water Works**, lighthouses, donkey carts, surfboards, hand nets, used bongos, mega fish catchers
- Even purchased **empty condos** for Sinopian workers to use as vacation huts
- Left behind accumulated Fish Reserve Notes, taking "everything of value with them"
- Usonian stores now stocked only with worthless paper currency

### Senator Ocuda's Realization

- Senators surveyed the devastation, wondering where it went wrong
- Finally realized: "It was all much simpler than what they had thought"
- **Senator Ocuda's honest assessment**: "Does anybody here remember how to make a net? I think it's time we all went fishing."

## TAKE AWAY (Second Section)

### Government Spending and the Debt Trap

- Governments historically get in trouble by spending more than they have
- When gaps become too large, difficult choices arise between raising taxes, cutting spending, defaulting, or inflating

### Tax Increases vs. Spending Cuts

- **Raising taxes** is never popular and hard to push through in democracies
- Higher rates "always discourage productivity and deflate economic vitality"
- There's a limit to how high taxes can go before people stop working or start rioting
- **Cutting spending** is often more difficult than raising taxes
- Those whose benefits are cut express hostility "both at the polls and on the street"
- Politicians make promises to secure elections; voters rarely consider taxpayers' ability to foot the bills

### Default as Political Escape

- Some governments choose **default** to avoid politically unpopular options
- Simply tell creditors they can't pay the full amount of debt obligations
- If debt largely owed to foreigners, the decision is "much easier to make"
- Politically better "to stiff a foreigner than to raise taxes on, or deny benefits to, a country's own citizens"

### Inflation as the Easiest Choice

- Default can be embarrassing as "official acknowledgment of insolvency"
- Many governments opt to **print money** to pay debts, effectively repudiating obligations by inflating them away
- "Since inflation is usually the easiest choice to make, it is often the most likely"
- "But while it may seem easy at first, it ultimately exacts the harshest toll"

### How Inflation Works

- Allows governments to avoid hard choices and dispose of debt "on the sly"
- Nominally pay back all owed, but do so "by diluting their currency"
- Creditors get paid, "but what they get isn't worth much"
- **Inflation transfers wealth** from anyone with savings to anyone with debt in the same currency
- With hyperinflation, savings value gets "completely wiped out" and debt burden removed
- Hard assets rise in nominal value when inflation flares, unlike currency savings

### Historical Hyperinflation Examples

- **France** (1790s), **Confederate States of America** (1860s), **Germany** (1920s), **Hungary** (1940s), **Argentina and Brazil** (1970s and 1980s), **Zimbabwe** (today)
- In all cases, circumstances leading to hyperinflation and economic devastation that followed "were remarkably similar"
- Countries satisfied staggering debt by wiping out currency value, throwing "their own populations into abject poverty"

### America's Vulnerability

- U.S. would be "the largest and most advanced economy to ever experience hyperinflation"
- Current protection: **reserve status of the U.S. dollar** means it remains widely accepted regardless of fundamentals
- But if reserve status is lost, "our currency would be just as vulnerable as those that went down before"
- "We must look at these possibilities and head them off now, before we no longer have the ability to decide for ourselves"

## EPILOGUE

### The Financial Crisis Origins

- The longer leaders pursue policies responsible for the financial crisis, "the greater that eventuality becomes"
- **Keynesian stimulus** ideology born with Keynes, nurtured with Roosevelt, but truly came into its own with **Alan Greenspan, George Bush, Ben Bernanke, and Barack Obama**
- Before 2002: never seen federal deficits of this magnitude (now exceeding $1.5 trillion annually), never experimented so radically with ultra-low interest rates and credit market manipulation

### The 2002-2003 Recession Response

- Following dot-com malinvestments, economy should have had protracted downturn
- George Bush didn't want bad economy to jeopardize reelection, so dialed up "Keynesian remedies of spending and easy money to an extent not seen for generations"
- Result: **2002-2003 recession was one of the shallowest contractions on record**
- But benefit came with heavy long-term cost — U.S. ended recession with **greater imbalances** than before downturn began

### The Housing Bubble

- Instead of real growth, kicked off "even bigger asset bubble (in housing)" that temporarily overcame drag of busted technology bubble
- Rising housing prices created many "benefits" that masqueraded as economic health — "but as we have seen, that vigor was illusory"

### Failure to Learn from 2008

- Six years after 2002-2003, when next crash came in 2008, "we had failed to learn anything from these mistakes"
- Economists and politicians are "getting it dangerously wrong" in diagnosing causes and prescribing cures
- Consensus emerged that "lack of adequate regulations" brought on the crisis
- Government and Federal Reserve roles "have been largely ignored"
- Result: "getting more of what we don't need (spending and restrictive regulations) and less of what we do (savings and free enterprise)"

### Wall Street's Role

- Wall Street leaders were "also irresponsible" — profits during boom years were obscene, should have paid more dearly after crash
- But "bankers were playing the distorted hand dealt them by government"
- Government "irrationally promoted home-buying, discouraged savings, and recklessly encouraged borrowing and lending"

### Government Housing Policies

- **Federal Reserve, Federal Housing Administration, Fannie Mae, and Freddie Mac** (always government entities in disguise) created advantages for home buying/selling and removed disincentives for lending/borrowing
- Result: "credit and real estate bubble that could only grow—until it could grow no more"

### Monetary Policy Distortions

- **Artificially low interest rates** made economy appear healthy, invigorated adjustable-rate mortgage market, gave birth to teaser rates making overpriced homes seem affordable
- **Alan Greenspan** actively encouraged home buyers to partake
- Government agencies guaranteed adjustable-rate mortgages "based solely on the ability of borrowers to afford the teaser rates"
- Without guarantees, "most of these mortgages never would have been funded"

### Removing Fear from Markets

- Free market prices set by supply and demand
- Financial and real estate markets governed by "opposing tension between greed and fear"
- "Government has done all that it can to remove fear from the equation"

### The 2008 Response

- As market forces moved to deflate credit and housing bubbles, government stepped in to re-inflate both
- **Bear Stearns and AIG bailouts**, guarantees for **Goldman Sachs and Bank of America**
- **$700 billion TARP** to purchase mortgage assets "that no one in the private sector would touch"
- Government took over student loan market via **Sallie Mae** bailout
- Detroit automaker bailouts followed
- Banks and businesses that should have failed were propped up
- Capital and labor that should have been freed for productive uses "were instead calcified in unneeded activities"

### The $700 Billion Stimulus

- After housing boom deprived consumers of easy money, government stepped in with massive stimulus
- This spending "which the government has borrowed from future generations, has kept us from the pain of living within our means"

### Resisting the Cure

- By refusing to allow market forces to rein in excess spending, liquidate bad investments, replenish savings, fund capital investment, and help workers transition from service to manufacturing
- Government "has resisted the cure while exacerbating the disease"
- "We have turned just about all forms of debt into government debt, and have blown up another bubble, this time in Treasury bonds"

### The Treasury Bond Bubble

- This bubble "threatens to dwarf all preceding asset bubbles"
- Its eventual bursting will cause consumer prices and interest rates to soar, with "even more devastating effects on the economy than the dot-com and housing bubbles combined"

### The Path Forward

- "There is time to stop the train before it heads off the cliff"
- Need leaders "who have the courage to be honest with voters, and voters who have the strength to accept the hard work of economic renewal"
- "For years we have been living beyond our means, and we must summon the resolve to finally live within them"
- If we allow free market forces to operate unhindered, "we can rebalance our economy and set the stage for a real expansion"

### The Alternative

> "However, if we choose to put our faith in debt, the printing press, and the promise of pain-free government solutions, we will all be fishing without a net."
