## Chapter 15: Fiduciary Media and Their Origin (continued)

### Money Substitutes and the Possibility of Fiduciary Media

A claim on a bank, payable on demand, is a **complete money substitute**
so long as the issuer's solvency is unimpeachable and convertibility on
demand is certain — "ready money," present goods, for all economic
purposes. What makes fiduciary media possible is a property unique to
money: **money is never consumed**, only spent to be passed on. Claims
to money need never actually be redeemed in the way claims to bread
must be. A baker with 1,000 loaves cannot safely issue more than 1,000
bread tickets; a bank issuing money claims can safely issue far more
than its reserve because most claims simply circulate indefinitely.
**Fiduciary media** are claims issued in excess of the money reserve,
possible only because money is not consumed in exchange.

### Deposits as the Origin of Circulation Credit

Deposit banking is the soil from which fiduciary media grew; notes and
current accounts both developed from deposit practice. Mises draws a
sharp distinction the law obscures: a demand deposit is **not** a credit
transaction. Credit is an exchange of a present good for a future good.
A demand depositor has exchanged a present good for another present
good — no time sacrifice. Treating demand deposits as credit distorts
monetary theory from the outset.

Two kinds of bank deposit must be kept separate: **current accounts**
(demand deposits, little or no interest, form the basis of fiduciary
media) and **investment/savings deposits** (interest-bearing, repayable
on notice, genuine time credit). By the law of large numbers the
fraction of the investment pool that must actually be paid out on any
day is small and predictable, and universal banks that pool both kinds
enjoy a reserve advantage over pure savings banks.

The crucial point for monetary theory: **only current-account business
affects the volume of money.** Banks with current-account or note-issue
business can lend from a fund that did not exist before the loans were
granted. A bank that merely re-lends existing deposits never creates
new purchasing power; a bank that issues notes or opens current
accounts creates purchasing power above and beyond existing savings.

### The Granting of Circulation Credit

Mises rejects the view (descended from Fullarton) that when a bank
lends in its own notes it is merely an intermediary passing along
credit from whoever ends up holding them. Fullarton cited pre-branch
Lancashire evidence — nine-tenths of payments in bills, one-tenth in
money or notes — to argue that banknotes were just a handier kind of
bill. Mises insists on the opposite: a person who holds a convertible
note grants no credit; he has given nothing in exchange for a future
good. The note is present money to him.

Notes can be issued two ways: (1) exchanged for money as **money
certificates**, fully backed, no profit possible; (2) issued as loans
as **fiduciary media**, generating Hermann's *Übergewinn* — the
surplus profit from creating new purchasing power. Imagine a country
with 100 million ducats, a bank founded, 50 million in deposits, 50
million in notes issued, 40 million lent out at interest. The interest
is the bank's profit, not the noteholders'. New notes entering the
loan market tend to **lower** the rate of interest — the opposite of
what bills do, since more bills raise the demand for credit. A time
bill and a banknote are therefore fundamentally different instruments.

> "The activity of note issue cannot in any way be described as
> increasing the demand for credit in the same sense as... an increase
> in the number of bills current."

Mises considers it "one of the most remarkable phenomena in the history
of political economy" that the distinction between notes and bills went
unnoticed; the insight was present in the Classical School and the
**Currency School** but destroyed by later Banking School theorists.

Using fiduciary media does not transform indirect exchange into direct
exchange. Paying by check still involves two money-mediated transactions,
and the holder of substitutes is equally exposed to all fluctuations in
money's objective exchange value. Much of the "unsatisfactory nature"
of banking theory arises from imprecise terminology that treats notes,
demand deposits, and bills of exchange as interchangeable "credit
instruments" and blinds theorists to their distinct economic natures.

## Chapter 16: The Evolution of Fiduciary Media

Fiduciary media are claims payable on demand in money, not covered by
a money reserve, accepted in place of money because of their legal and
commercial characteristics. Token coinage (the German thaler under the
gold standard, modern fractional coins) likewise functions as fiduciary
media. The issuer may treat them as a liability or as pure seigniorage
income; the economic effect is the same. Fiduciary media have two
historical roots — the **deposit and giro banks**, which produced notes
and current accounts, and the **state minting prerogative**, which
produced convertible Treasury notes and token coin. The two sources
converge as states increasingly act like banks.

The current account is older; the banknote is a development from it.
Economically the two are identical, differing only in legal form.
Banknotes outstripped current accounts for medium and small payments
because they were easier to transfer; current accounts regained ground
for large transactions in the second half of the 19th century as
checks and clearing handled large sums more efficiently. Some of the
displacement of notes by deposits was artificial: the **Currency
Principle** led regulators to see danger only in over-issue of notes,
so deposits were encouraged while notes were restricted.

### Fiduciary Media and the Clearing System

Exchanges made with money can be settled without money moving if claims
are offset against counterclaims. Without credit, offsetting requires
simultaneous matching; **credit** treats temporally separated
transactions as simultaneous for settlement. International and
interlocal bill dealing extends this to enormous scale.

> "Money which is not present performs an economic function; it has
> its effect solely by reason of the possibility of its being *able*
> to be present."

Clearing reduces the demand for money in the **broader sense** (physical
transfers avoided) without affecting money's function as medium of
exchange; fiduciary media reduce the demand in the **narrower sense**
(substitutes take the place of money in circulation). Banking literature
routinely conflates the two, a persistent source of obscurity.

Bills of exchange, despite circulating quasi-money-like in 18th and
19th century commercial centers, are **not** fiduciary media. Each
holder must assess the credit of prior endorsers; endorsing is not
final payment; if dishonored, liability revives. Bills lack the
unlimited augmentability of true fiduciary media. The **clearinghouse**,
a 19th-century development, is the key institution systematically
settling claims by offsetting, with only balances paid in money.

### Domestic and International Circulation

In pre-war Germany and England actual gold coin was used only for
payments of 20–100 marks or £1–£5; smaller and larger payments went
through token coin, notes, or deposits. Gold coin persisted only
because of legislative intervention — in England the battle over the
**one-pound note** ended with the sovereign coin winning, setting the
Continental pattern against small notes for decades.

Silver-standard countries, chiefly India, were forced toward the world
gold standard after the monometallism controversy ended, but importing
enough gold would have convulsed European money markets and dumping
silver would have damaged India's competitive position in Asia.
**Ricardo's** 1816 pamphlet supplied the template: a gold standard
without a gold currency. **Lindsay** (1879) and **Probyn** (1897)
developed competing plans; both were initially rejected by the 1898
Indian Commission, but events vindicated the theorists. In India, the
Straits Settlements, the Philippines, and other Asian states, internal
payments were settled in silver rupees (partly relics, partly newly
minted for state profit), and a government-managed **conversion fund**
exchanged silver for gold at a fixed rate. In substance the silver
rupee was **nothing but a metallic note** for whose conversion the
state was responsible. The arrangement is the **gold-exchange
standard**, differing only in degree from the European gold standard.

Clearing operates across national boundaries — the motive was always
strongest in inter-regional trade, where moving money was costly and
risky. Bills, checks, and cable transfers built an interlocal clearing
system through market arbitrage. But **fiduciary media remain
nationally limited**: no internationally accepted money substitute
exists, and residual international balances must be settled in actual
money (gold). True international fiduciary media would require a
**world bank** with the right to issue notes as loans and open credit
accounts. Obstacles are severe: currency variety, political reluctance
to submit citizen accounts to a supranational body exposed to wartime
seizure, and irreconcilable disagreement among inflationary and
deflationary factions about how much credit to issue.

## Chapter 17: Fiduciary Media and the Demand for Money

Clearing and fiduciary media together counteract the tendency for the
exchange value of money to rise as the money economy expands. Without
them, rising demand for money would drive up the value of gold,
diverting capital and labor into precious-metal production at social
cost.

> Adam Smith (summarized): "The issue of notes creates, as it were, a
> path through the air and makes it possible to turn a large part of
> the roads into fields and meadows and in this way considerably to
> increase the annual yield of land and labor."

Both **Adam Smith** and **Ricardo** saw the chief advantage of notes
as reducing the cost of the monetary apparatus: circulating gold is a
road that grows nothing; paper is a road through the air. Ricardo
recommended expelling precious metal from domestic circulation
entirely. By the early 20th century this consensus was so settled that
opposition had "sunk into silence." Where fiat or credit money is
used the resource-saving argument ceases, and the case for fiduciary
media then rests purely on avoiding increases in the value of money —
still decisive.

Demand for money is governed by population, division of labor, and
extension of the exchange economy in the long run, by the business
cycle in the medium run, and by concentrated payment dates (paydays,
month-ends, quarter-ends, agricultural autumns) in the short run.

A common claim says the clearing system gives money "elasticity" to
adjust automatically to demand. Mises separates effects: clearing can
only cancel claims that actually exist between parties on a given date.
Multilateral clearinghouse netting extends this, but a second
insurmountable limit remains — complete cancellation would require
everyone to spend every receipt immediately, holding no reserves.
Because people always hold some reserve there is a **rigid minimum**
below which clearing cannot push money demand. The clearing system's
development is independent of the money supply/demand ratio; apparent
correlations are policy-driven, not automatic.

### Refuting the Banking School

The **Banking School** (Tooke, Fullarton, Wilson) held that banks of
issue cannot increase or decrease note circulation at will; quantity
is determined by the community's demand for payment media; banks are
passive; any surplus reflexes back. Mises considers this their fatal
error. When a bank discounts a bill it exchanges a present good for a
future good — and creates the present good practically out of nothing.
The quantity of future goods offered on the loan market is **not
fixed**; banks can expand demand simply by setting their rate below
the **natural rate of interest** (Wicksell's term: the rate that would
prevail if real capital were lent without monetary intermediation).
Loan demand vanishes only when the bank rate exceeds the natural rate.
**Fullarton's reflux argument** overlooks that debtors repay old loans
by taking out new ones.

The **commodity-bill theory** — that bills backed by real goods give
notes an "organic connection" to economic life, the logic behind the
**Reichsbank's** backing of notes on commodity bills — is equally
confused. A bill is a promissory note in special legal form; there is
no economic difference between a bill-backed claim and any other
short claim of equal maturity and creditworthiness. The volume of
commodity bills presented for discount depends entirely on bank credit
policy: ease conditions and more transactions occur on credit, more
bills are drawn; tighten and the reverse. A cotton dealer drawing a
three-month bill on a spinner, who redeems it by drawing on a weaver
— whether these transactions happen at all depends chiefly on the
bank discount rate.

> "The quantity of fiduciary media in circulation has no natural
> limits."

Any limitation requires deliberate banking policy. A single bank in
competition cannot pursue a fully independent discount policy —
competitors drain its reserves — so competitive banks in practice
fluctuate circulation roughly with demand, producing the appearance
of automatic elasticity. The Banking School correctly observed the
phenomenon and completely misunderstood its cause.

The **German Bank Act of March 14, 1875** required notes beyond gold
cover to be backed by commodity bills of at most three months, bearing
at least two solvent names. The provision did not link issue to demand
organically; its real value lay in **restricting** credit expansion.
By the time the Act passed, commodity bills were already declining and
the Reichsbank found it increasingly hard to find qualifying paper.

Borrowers demand **capital**, not money; they seek capital in money
form because only money-command gives access to real goods. Banks
satisfy this by issuing money substitutes, but only provisionally —
banks cannot create real capital, and the cost is borne by those
harmed by the resulting change in money's exchange value. The
mercantile distinction between "demand for money" (short-term) and
"demand for capital" (long-term) is spurious; all short-term credit
demand is ultimately capital demand. The **German Bank Act Amendment
of June 1, 1909** extended the tax-free note quota from 550 to 750
million marks around quarter-days, codifying decades of Reichsbank
practice of absorbing settlement-day demand through expanded
fiduciary media rather than higher rates.

## Chapter 18: The Redemption of Fiduciary Media

Money substitutes are liquid claims against issuers whose solvency is
beyond doubt, and in theory their value equals the money they
represent. But solvency is never literally beyond doubt — banks whose
solvency was unquestioned the day before have collapsed overnight —
so rational holders assign a small discount. For small-denomination
substitutes (token coins) such doubts do not practically arise.

A **conversion fund** cannot protect against a panic; its purpose is
not to redeem media that return through lost confidence. Confidence
in fiduciary media is either universal or nonexistent. A lone skeptic
is a comic figure; if **everybody** presents notes simultaneously,
any bank must suspend payments.

> "Any bank that issues fiduciary media is forced to suspend payments
> if *everybody* begins to present notes for redemption or to withdraw
> deposits. Any such bank is powerless against a panic; no system and
> no policy can help it then."

This is not a flaw in banking technique but the nature of fiduciary
media — the bank promises immediate payment of a sum it can never
command in full. Historical failures have been blamed on imprudent
credit, tied-up capital, or state loans, but even perfect short-term
investment would not have saved them.

Since before Ricardo, it has been recognized that no issuer can
protect itself against panic consequences. Three positions have been
taken — prohibit uncovered notes, prohibit clearing except with full
metallic cover, or combine both — but none was adopted, because
fiduciary media vastly increased the efficiency of money, absorbed
demand-growth from expanding commerce, and reduced the cost of the
monetary apparatus. Prohibition would not kill banking; ordinary
commodity credit would survive. Fiduciary media persist because of
their effect on the objective exchange value of money.

### The Redemption Fund

A bank can circulate no more money substitutes than its customers need
for business **among themselves**; the rest returns. The exception —
a critical warning — is a single monopoly bank or all banks acting in
concert on uniform principles. Most countries have a two-tier
structure: a privileged **central bank** with a monopoly on note
issue, relying on its own reserve, and second-order rival banks that
hold assets acceptable to the central bank.

The central bank must cover only the portion of money demand that
cannot be satisfied by fiduciary media — which equals the demand for
money in **foreign trade**. The foreign-exchange clearing mechanism
already cancels most cross-border transfers: "only exceptionally
nowadays that two ships meet at sea, one taking gold from London to
New York and the other bringing gold back." International flows are
governed mainly by newly mined metal distributed worldwide, with
**London** often as middleman. A small redemption fund still remains
necessary so holders can convert for foreign payments without a
discount emerging.

### Liquidity, Short-Term Cover, and Foreign Bills

Mises distinguishes **solvency** (assets eventually sufficient to
satisfy creditors) from **liquidity** (ability to meet every liability
on time). A credit-issuing bank cannot achieve genuine liquidity — by
their nature, fiduciary media require that a large proportion stay
in circulation and that claims never be enforced simultaneously. In
a panic, a bill with thirty days to run is no more useful than a
thirty-year mortgage — what matters in a crisis is **negotiability**,
not term. Formal liquidity through demand-callable lending merely
shifts the problem to borrowers, who cannot instantly repay.

Why, then, do banks prefer short-term loans? A minor reason: easier
to evaluate. The **major reason**: restricting credit to short-term
loans **limits the total quantity of fiduciary media that can be
issued**. Not by enabling redemption — by capping expansion. The rule
emerged from centuries of banking experience, was always
misunderstood, and is valuable precisely because of what it restrains.

The soundness of cover matters only secondarily. Capacity for
circulation depends on confidence in **prompt redemption**, not on
backing assets. Fiduciary media can circulate with zero cover (token
coins); full asset backing cannot support circulation if prompt
redemption is in doubt. Proposals to back notes with mortgages are
therefore mistaken: if redemption is uncertain the media trade at a
discount, cease to be money substitutes, and become **credit money**
with an independent valuation.

Since the redemption fund's real purpose is foreign trade, it may
include foreign money substitutes, bills, checks, and short-notice
deposits with foreign banks. The **Hamburg Giro Bank** held part of
its reserve in bills on London, and other issuers followed later in
the 19th century. But if **all** countries hold reserves in foreign
claims to gold rather than gold itself, gold ceases to serve any
monetary function — its value as money vanishes entirely. This is
the fundamental critique of the **gold-exchange standard**.

## Chapter 19: Money, Credit, and Interest

Chapter 19 asks whether monetary changes affect production goods
differently from consumption goods, because only then could they move
the rate of interest — which reflects the margin by which capital-good
values fall short of expected product values, rooted in the natural
discount of future against present goods. The sharp form of the
question: can banks depress the loan rate by issuing fiduciary media,
pushing it to the floor set only by their operating costs? This is
the old question of the **"gratuitous nature of bank credit."**

What lay opinion calls plentifulness or scarcity of money is really
plentifulness or scarcity of **capital**. Entrepreneurs demand money
only because money-command gives access to real capital goods. **Hume**
and **Adam Smith** established the consensus that money quantity does
not determine interest, yet writers continually argue the opposite.

### Currency vs. Banking School, Revisited

The central 19th-century question was whether banks can grant credit
beyond available capital. The **Banking School** (Tooke, Fullarton,
followers) held that banks cannot expand note issue beyond the needs
of business and that once circulation is saturated no bank can lend
except from its own capital — but its fatal flaw was forgetting that
convertible notes can remain permanently in circulation through
repeated loan prolongation, and that demand for credit is not fixed
(it expands when interest falls). The **Currency School** (Overstone,
Torrens) held that banks **can** arbitrarily extend note issue and
asked how equilibrium is restored, building a complete theory of
money's value and credit's effect on prices and interest.

> "The works of the much abused Currency School contain far more in
> the way of useful ideas and fruitful thoughts than is usually
> assumed, especially in Germany."

Mises defends the School against its German detractors, who know it
only through **Tooke**, **Newmarch's** *History of Prices*, and
**J.S. Mill's** *Principles*.

### Money Stock, Distribution, and Transitional Interest

Money-quantity changes affect interest only **indirectly**, through
displacements in the social distribution of income and wealth. If
additional money were distributed uniformly between consumption and
production goods there would be no effect on interest at all. Higher
incomes save more and discount the future less, so a richer
distribution means more saving, a larger subsistence fund, and a
lower natural rate; a more concentrated distribution downward produces
the opposite. Direction depends on who gains and who loses.

During the transition while price changes spread unevenly,
entrepreneurs are affected first: with falling money value
(inflation) they gain (inputs at old prices, output at rising prices,
profits up, bid up interest rates); with rising money value
(deflation) they lose and rates fall. These movements last only as
long as the price-level adjustment continues.

### Wicksell, Restoration, and the Gratuitous Credit Question

**Knut Wicksell** clearly states the problem by distinguishing the
**natural rate of interest** (what would prevail if real capital were
lent in natura) from the **money rate** (charged on money loans).
Banks can push the money rate below the natural rate through
fiduciary expansion; it must eventually return. Mises finds Wicksell's
restoration mechanism flawed — he argues that rising prices deplete
bank reserves as the public demands more cash, but this contradicts
his own assumption of a pure fiduciary system with unrestricted
issue. His secondary limit (gold flows to industrial uses) works only
for commodity money, and even there it is distant.

Can banks reduce the loan rate to their working costs? They **can**
— competition forces others to follow — but doing so sets in motion
forces that restore the rate. The naive position, that credit can
be granted nearly free because interest is just compensation for
temporary relinquishment of money, Mises calls insurpassable naivety
because it denies the existence of interest altogether. The orthodox
Banking School position simply denies the problem exists, on its
untenable premise.

The quantity of fiduciary media is limited by discount requests, but
discount requests are not independent of bank policy. By lowering
rates banks **create** more credit demand, which they then meet. A
single bank cannot do this alone — competitors drain its reserves —
but **all credit-issuing banks acting in concert** face no effective
limit. A single monopoly bank for an isolated community, with full
public confidence, could expand without bound; if artificial
restrictions were removed and all banks coordinated, "the complete
cessation of money use would only be a matter of time."

### Artificially Low Rates and the Period of Production

The natural rate of interest is set by the productivity of the
marginal extension of the production period: interest must exceed the
return on the last justifiable lengthening and fall short of the
return on the first unjustifiable one. The equilibrium **period of
production** is exactly long enough that the entire subsistence fund
is needed and sufficient to pay workers throughout.

When the loan rate is pushed below the natural rate, entrepreneurs
are enabled and obliged to enter longer roundabout processes. Longer
processes yield absolutely greater returns but relatively smaller
ones; at the natural rate the extra roundaboutness does not pay, but
below it appears to. These longer processes are only feasible if the
**subsistence fund** has grown to feed workers throughout. Fiduciary
expansion does not increase the fund — it redistributes purchasing
power. The bank rate corresponds to a production period longer than
the actual subsistence fund can sustain, and a time must come when
subsistence goods are exhausted before the capital goods in longer
processes have been converted to consumption goods. Worse, the fall
in the interest rate itself **weakens the motive for saving**,
slowing capital accumulation and making the crunch more severe.

### The Self-Reversing Boom and Crisis

When banks lend below the natural rate, production-goods prices rise
while consumption-goods prices rise only moderately (via wages),
temporarily reinforcing downward pressure on the loan rate. But a
countermovement sets in: consumption-goods prices rise, production
goods fall, and the loan rate rebounds. A second force: fiduciary
expansion depreciates money, and during depreciation the loan rate
must include a premium over what it would be with stable money. Banks
may try to offset these counterforces by cutting rates further and
issuing still more fiduciary media, but this accelerates depreciation
and strengthens the very forces they are fighting. Pushed to the
limit, unchecked fiduciary expansion leads to panic-collapse.

> "Such an avalanche of fiduciary media, when its cessation cannot be
> foreseen, must lead to a fall in the objective exchange value of the
> money-and-credit unit to the paniclike course of which there can be
> no bounds."

Banks are eventually forced to stop underbidding the natural rate.
Price ratios approximately return, but precise restoration is
impossible — the expansion has **redistributed property**, and crisis
phenomena mark the **loss of capital sunk in excessively long
roundabout processes**. An enterprise that yielded a 4% net profit
when rates fell from 4.5% to 3.5% is no longer profitable when rates
return; whether it shuts down or continues at lower profit, value is
destroyed. Goods that could have satisfied more important wants were
used for less important ones.

Mises's banking theory, like the Currency School's, ultimately
generates a theory of the business cycle. The Currency School did not
carry its analysis far enough; it only asked what happens when one
country expands more than others, producing the doctrine of the
"**external drain**" that explained English crises up to mid-century.
Historically, banks never go quite as far as they theoretically could
— they stop early from uneasiness or legal restriction, so crises
break out before they need to. There is partial truth in the claim
that credit restriction triggers crises: banks could **postpone**
collapse by continuing to expand, never prevent it.

> "The catastrophe occurs, and its consequences are the worse and the
> reaction against the bull tendency of the market the stronger, the
> longer the period during which the rate of interest on loans has
> been below the natural rate."

## Chapter 20: Problems of Credit Policy

Since the Currency School, governments have officially tried to
restrain over-issue of fiduciary media, but this has been continually
undermined by the desire to keep interest rates low: "cheap money"
and "reasonable prices" have been the competing aims. From the early
20th century the cheap-money aim grew steadily stronger; during WWI
it became the dominant objective.

### Peel's Act

**Peel's Bank Act of 1844** still sets the standard for credit policy.
Economic literature attacked its fixed-quota system as theoretically
mistaken, but legislature after legislature adopted its approach. The
Currency School's weakness was failing to recognize the essential
similarity between **banknotes and bank deposits**; Banking School
critics exploited this, but the correct cure would have been to apply
the same rules to deposits, not to abandon restriction. Mises
considers the deposit oversight a minor error relative to Banking
School confusions, and understandable given the underdeveloped English
deposit system of the time.

The Act contained an unintentional safety valve: its failure to cover
deposits allowed banking to expand with growing business. Its
fundamental flaw was fixing the quota of unbacked notes for all time,
blocking any future use of fiduciary media to satisfy demand growth.
The result: **Peel's Act did not achieve its aim.** Fiduciary media
suppressed as banknotes simply re-emerged as **deposits**; the deposit
system developed at the expense of the note system. In crisis after
crisis, confidence in deposit-based banks collapsed while confidence
in banknotes held — in England the Bank of England expanded its own
notes to fill the gap; in the United States **clearinghouse
certificates** served the same role, and the **Aldrich-Vreeland Act**
had partial success. No note-restriction regime has ever proved
insurmountable; fiduciary media suppressed in one form reappear in
another. The real obstacle to unlimited expansion is not legislative
restriction but the absence of a world bank or uniform procedure
among all credit-issuing banks.

### Discount Policy and the Gold-Premium Devices

Discount policy has a clear purpose. Every credit-issuing bank must
fix its loan rate in conformity with others because if it charges
less, payments to other banks' customers balloon and solvency is
imperiled. Raising the discount rate safeguards the ability to pay —
not by defending a reserve, but by preventing artificial
over-expansion. Banks would need discount policy **even without legal
reserve requirements**. The domestic-versus-international debate (as
in the **Reichsbank** controversy) is largely meaningless: capital is
mobile, forming a homogeneous world capital market, and the natural
rate of interest is determined internationally. A rise in German
rates following US events is no more remarkable than a rise in corn
prices after a foreign harvest. Capital-exporting nations resist
foreign investment to keep domestic rates low; capital-importers
encourage it. **Creditor interests consistently lose to debtor
interests** in political conflicts.

The **Bank of France's gold-premium policy** is the best-known scheme
for holding discount rates low by making gold export more expensive.
France's silver five-franc piece was still legal tender, and the
Bank could redeem notes in gold or silver. For "legitimate" trade
(corn or cotton imports) it released gold willingly; for
interest-rate arbitrage it withheld gold or charged a premium of
4–8% on 3,437 francs per kg. The unspoken aim was to protect **French
rentes** from discount-rate shocks — rentes were the chief investment
of the classes supporting the Third Republic. Mises's verdict: the
Bank could not override economic law. France's low rates came from
being the world's capital-richest nation whose people lacked
enterprise. The premium postponed adjustments marginally, but cut
both ways — it also discouraged short-term inflows. French
short-term rates were therefore more **stable** than London or
Berlin, never as low, never as high. If the premium were pushed to
actually prevent export it would amount to cessation of cash
payments, and fiduciary media would become **credit money** with
independent depreciating value. Many premium advocates were covert
inflationists; the Bank capped the premium at eight per mill.

Pure-gold-standard countries used analogous methods. The **Bank of
England** until 1889 voluntarily redeemed notes in gold ingots at
77s. 10½d. per ounce. Both the Bank of England and the **Reichsbank**
issued export gold as worn coins of inferior value (English
sovereigns 2–3% below newly minted, Reichsbank twenty-mark pieces
averaging 7.943g vs. 7.965g). The Bank of France's attempt to
distinguish "legitimate" commodity trade from "illegitimate"
arbitrage could never be operationalized — countless intermediate
cases exist, and even if gold were given freely to importers the
exchange rate would still rise by the arbitrageur's premium,
defeating the Bank's purpose. The **Austro-Hungarian Bank** was
accused of refusing bills to arbitrageurs, which would equally
restrict gold inflows — damaging to a debtor nation.

Pre-war Germany's **Reichsbank** and the *Kaufmannschaft* of Berlin
(circular of May 2, 1907) pushed to shift payments to checks and
clearing so as to concentrate gold in reserves and lower discount
rates. Mises: a **fundamental theoretical error**. The rate of
interest does not depend on the quantity of the money stock or on
the amount of fiduciary media. Replacing uncovered notes with
uncovered deposits is expansion in another form, not a lower rate.
Tooke and Fullarton correctly noted no fundamental difference between
notes and deposits; their successors refused to draw the conclusion.
Superficial observers commit *post hoc ergo propter hoc* by blaming
central-bank rate hikes for tightening that the rate hikes merely
track. As long as capital is mobile, **no country's credit-issuing
banks can follow an independent credit policy**.

## Part Four: Monetary Reconstruction

## Chapter 21: The Principle of Sound Money

**Sound money** was a product of classical political economy and the
18th-century liberal program, carried through the 19th century by the
most influential political parties of Europe and America. In the
liberal scheme the market economy is the only workable system —
consumers direct producers through prices, entrepreneurs are subject
to **consumer sovereignty**, and the state exists for defense, which
creates the central political problem of preventing rulers from
turning their weapons on the citizenry. Sound money belongs in the
same category as **constitutional guarantees** and **bills of rights**
— a defense of individual liberty against tyrannical encroachment.
Historical impetus came from the American Continental currency, the
French Revolutionary assignats, and the British restriction period.

**Sound money in practice means a metallic standard**: standard coins
of legally specified metal content, with token coins and paper
redeemable on demand. The "battle of the standards" was settled by
the end of the 19th century as gold became the near-universal
consensus; dissenters were dismissed as cranks. Mises faults
sound-money defenders for dismissing opponents rather than refuting
them. Scientific support was shaky because the new **marginal utility
/ subjectivist** value theory, having replaced classical value theory,
left monetary questions to routinists for nearly half a century.

### Virtues and Shortcomings of the Gold Standard

The gold standard's supreme virtue is that it makes the **purchasing
power of money independent of government and political parties**,
preventing rulers from financing unauthorized expenditures through
fiat creation. It is therefore an indispensable part of constitutional
government. The standard has been attacked from both directions in
turn: in the 1850s, after California and Australia discoveries,
**Michel Chevalier** (*Probable Depreciation of Gold*) attacked it as
inflationary; later, inflationists reversed and called it deflationary,
claiming to offset the contraction of "insufficient" gold. In reality
there has been a secular tendency toward falling purchasing power.

Inflation is popular because of a systematic misapprehension: every
group believes it will benefit from higher prices on what it sells
while its purchases stay stable. But under capitalism **debtors** are
typically well-to-do owners of real estate, firms, and stocks, while
**creditors** are people of modest means — bondholders, savings
account holders, insurance policyholders. The common man who supports
anti-creditor measures ignores that he himself is a creditor.

> "For the naive mind there is something miraculous in the issuance
> of fiat money. A magic word spoken by the government creates out of
> nothing a thing which can be exchanged against any merchandise a
> man would like to get. How pale is the art of sorcerers, witches,
> and conjurors when compared with that of the government's Treasury
> Department!"

Inflation depends on public ignorance. Early stage: the housewife
thinks "prices are too high; I'll wait." Critical stage: "I'll buy
today because it will be more expensive later." Terminal stage: "I'll
buy any object rather than hold these scraps of paper one minute
longer" — the hyperinflationary crack-up. Inflation is self-defeating
once the public sees through it. **Index numbers**, once widely used,
force governments to frame it as emergency-only. Intentional
inflation is dishonest; it works only when government deceives the
public. The gold standard did not "collapse" — governments **abolished**
it, deploying police, courts, prisons (in some countries executioners)
to break solemn pledges and defy constitutions.

> "To refer to this condition as a rule of an alleged game is no more
> reasonable than to declare that the preservation of Paul's life
> depends on compliance with the rules of the Paul's-life game because
> Paul must die if somebody stabs him to death."

The core anti-gold argument is the **expansionist fallacy**: that
interest is an artifact of artificial money scarcity, and that freeing
the supply would drive rates toward zero. Mises: interest is
**originary time preference**, an irreducible feature of human
valuation — no human ever valued an apple available in a year equally
to one available now. The "economic isolationist" version — that
gold forces a central bank to match foreign rates, and leaving gold
would permit independent low-rate policy — always appeared in
**debtor** countries (Germany, Austria in the 1870s–80s) benefiting
from capital inflows, and never in creditors like England or the
Netherlands. Credit expansion can manufacture an initial boom but
inevitably ends in slump.

### The Full-Employment Doctrine and the Emergency Argument

The "grocer's philosophy" holds that business is bad because customers
lack money and the cure is more money. This was exploded by **Adam
Smith** and **Jean-Baptiste Say**, revived by **Keynes** as the
full-employment doctrine, and is now official policy outside the
Soviet bloc. Keynes produced no tenable argument against **Say's
Law**. Unemployment is a **wage-rate phenomenon**: if a worker cannot
sell his labor at his asking price he must lower it or stay
unemployed. If government or unions enforce wages above market-clearing
levels, the resulting unemployment is institutional. The only
effective path to higher real wages is **more per-capita capital
invested**.

The doctrine's fatal evasion is that it refuses to discuss how wage
rates are actually set. Its procedure — raise money until full
employment is reached, and call it policy rather than inflation —
works only by covertly cutting real wages. Inflation is wage
reduction by deception. In the 1930s some countries briefly saw
nominal wages lag behind prices, reducing real wages and unemployment,
but this was a lag, not a cure.

> "When in 1936 Lord Keynes declared that a movement of employers to
> revise money-wage bargains downward would be much more strongly
> resisted than a gradual and 'automatic' lowering of real wages as
> a result of rising prices, he had already been outdated and refuted
> by the march of events."

By 1936 unions had begun indexing wage demands; the Keynesian argument
was obsolete at the moment of its proclamation.

All economic arguments for inflation have been refuted, but a political
one remains: inflation is self-defeating, but some emergencies are
worse, and inflation may be the smaller evil when national freedom is
at stake. Mises's counter: inflation adds nothing to a nation's actual
power of resistance, neither material nor moral. Military equipment
must be provided by restricting consumption, intensifying production,
and drawing on accumulated capital — all achievable through taxation
if the citizenry genuinely supports the cause. The real situation
emergency-inflationists have in mind is one in which government is
in irreconcilable antagonism with majority opinion. Inflation becomes
a way to circumvent the democratic process — to deceive voters
rather than persuade them. "The elected executive no longer deems
himself the people's mandatory; he turns into a **führer**." When
majorities will vote only for taxes on "the rich," inflation taxes
everyone through price rises while politicians deny responsibility.

> "Inflation is the fiscal complement of statism and arbitrary
> government. It is a cog in the complex of policies and institutions
> which gradually lead toward totalitarianism."

"This heroic spirit cannot be bought by inflation." A nation unwilling
to make material sacrifices cannot win no matter how much fiat is
printed.

## Chapter 22: Contemporary Currency Systems

Before WWI, every variety of gold standard (classical and
gold-exchange) shared one feature: **gold parity fixed by duly
promulgated law** and understood as permanent. In the classical gold
standard, gold coins circulated privately alongside banknotes and
fractional coin; in the **gold-exchange standard** there was no
domestic gold circulation but the central bank dealt within the gold
margin.

Post-WWI came the **flexible standard**, originating from the
gold-exchange standard: domestic parity is not fixed by law but set
by a government agency, changeable without notice and almost always
used to **devalue**; unconditional redemption is gone; **pegging**
substitutes for redemption; operations are conducted by a central
bank or a special **foreign-exchange equalization account**.

If government restrains new issuance and the public believes
inflation will eventually stop, an inflationary system can persist for
years — the **freely vacillating currency**, in which the holder has
no claim against any agency and the pieces are **money proper**, not
substitutes. The outstanding example is the U.S. dollar, the "New
Deal dollar." If Treasury advisers had had their way, "the dollar
would have long since gone the way of the German mark of 1923"; only
protests from a few economists restrained them.

An **illusive standard** is a decreed fictitious parity the market
does not honor; government forbids transactions at any other rate and
makes market trading a crime. To enforce the fiction the state
**expropriates all foreign exchange** from citizens at the official
rate. Seizing foreign exchange below market rate is an **export duty**
that reduces exports; selling it below market rate **subsidizes
imports** and swells demand. The "**dollar shortage**" is a nonsense
concept: scarcity is essential to any good, and what observers call a
shortage merely means that at the fictitious parity demand exceeds
supply — the inevitable result of any price ceiling. If 1 dollar =
100 rurs on the free market and government fixes the rate at 50,
exports collapse and import demand surges; no balance-of-payments
intervention can cure this. Foreign-exchange control is primarily a
device for expropriating foreign investments, destroying the
international capital market — "one of the most important factors
in the decline of Western civilization."

## Chapter 23: The Return to Sound Money

There is near-universal agreement that monetary affairs are
unsatisfactory and near-total confusion about the remedy. The
**Federal Reserve System** and government-controlled central banks
were the instruments of monetary demolition, yet reform proposals
assign governments still more power and envision "superprivileged
superbanks." Even the futility of the **International Monetary Fund**
does not dissuade dreamers of a world bank issuing cheap credit. The
root cause is that money is a market phenomenon while the dominant
ideology condemns the market economy; where there is no market, there
is no money.

Inflation is structurally necessary for anti-capitalist politics. It
finances deficit spending and voter-bribing; rising prices provide the
pretext for price controls; illusory inflation profits are taxed as
"excess profits" and capital is confiscated; social discontent
spreads, providing ground for socialist propaganda. Governments praise
rising "national income" (measured in depreciating currency) and then
blame capitalism and "big business" for the resulting scarcity. Sound
money reform is impossible without rejecting the ideological framework
of the **New Deal** and **Fair Deal**.

### The Integral Gold Standard

> "Sound money still means today what it meant in the nineteenth
> century: the gold standard."

A genuine return to gold requires that government spend only what it
collects in taxes (no backdoor inflation through bank borrowing);
**100 percent reserve** against all new checkbook money; and **no
emergency exceptions** — any exemption will always be invoked.
Inflation provides neither weapons nor capital goods; it only lets
rulers evade blame. The core pedagogical goal is to destroy the
superstition that governments and banks create wealth "out of
nothing"; the shortsighted observer sees what government spends with
new money but not the real goods diverted from other uses.

Mainstream sound-money reformers are insufficient: they want to stop
fiscal inflation but still permit credit expansion for business, which
is the **British Banking School** error that produced 19th-century
collapses. Today the motive is not government borrowing but
**full-employment policy** — credit expansion to keep wages above
market-clearing levels. Turning points: England 1931 and the U.S. 1933
adopted what **Keynes** theorized in his *General Theory* (1936);
**Blum's** 1936 Matignon agreements forced the Bank of France to
lend freely for union-dictated wages.

> "Inflation is the true opium of the people and it is administered
> to them by anticapitalist governments and parties."

### Currency Reform in Ruritania

**Ruritania** was on gold; government issued paper rurs 1:1 with gold
rurs, printed more than it redeemed, and by **Gresham's Law** gold
coins disappeared from circulation. The stabilization mechanism is
purely technical: (1) stop any further increase in domestic currency
supply; (2) wait for purchasing-power parity to stabilize; (3) when
the market rate stabilizes and the rur begins to appreciate slightly,
fix that market rate as the new **legal parity**; (4) establish
unconditional convertibility at that rate.

The reform agency is narrow-purpose — buying and selling gold or
foreign exchange at legal parity, nothing more — and is funded by an
initial interest-free, never-to-be-recalled loan. It may issue new
rurs only when 100% covered by gold or foreign exchange bought from
the public. It deals only with the public, not with government or
central bank, and has no monopoly on gold dealing. Once the measures
are accomplished, Ruritania is on a gold-exchange or dollar-exchange
standard; government is stripped of the power to spend beyond tax
revenues, and the balance-of-payments problem dissolves automatically.

Ruritania's real obstacle is political: nationalized railroads,
telegraphs, mines, and plants run at deficits covered by inflation,
and cutting inflation means cutting the subsidy. Mises answers that
Ruritania's real poverty comes from nationalization and
interventionism, not from insufficient inflation.

**Capital flight** is invoked as an objection and refuted. If
investments become unsafe, owners want to sell and repatriate but can
only sell if buyers exist. If all potential buyers share the same
fear, there is no sale at any price. When buyers do exist, the
buyer's purchase of rurs and subsequent domestic spending adjusts the
price structure to enable exports that generate the needed foreign
exchange.

> "If people want to consume what other people have produced, they
> must pay for it by giving the sellers something they themselves
> have produced or by rendering them some services."

The balance of payments always balances — between Ruritania and
Laputania as between New York and Iowa.

### The United States' Return to a Sound Currency

Return to gold is politically taboo in Washington and on Wall Street
and is denounced by professorial and journalistic apologists for
inflation. Mises concedes openly that the gold standard is
incompatible with **deficit spending** — that is its point. Claims
that return is technically impossible are simply false.

Concrete steps: (1) unconditional halt to all further inflation —
no new dollar bills of any name or legal form, and no bank may
expand deposits except by receiving cash; (2) **100 percent reserve**
on all new deposits, existing deposits exempt; (3) repeal of all
restrictions on gold trading — free market in gold for all persons,
worldwide; (4) government and Fed stay out of the gold market during
transition to prevent sabotage; (5) no new silver certificates; (6)
expected market price about **$36–$38 per ounce** (above the 1934
Gold Reserve Act's $35); when stable, this becomes the new legal
parity; (7) a **Conversion Agency**, funded with roughly $1 billion
in gold bullion (interest-free, never recalled), sells gold at parity
and issues new dollars only against 100% gold reserve; (8) the
Treasury sells its gold to the Agency at parity against existing
legal-tender paper, retiring old paper and deposits proportionally;
(9) the **Federal Reserve System** is kept out — it has been "abused
by the most reckless inflationary policy"; (10) government finance
and the monetary medium **must become two entirely separate things**.

Mises insists on the **classical orthodox gold standard**, not the
gold-exchange standard. Gold must be in everyone's cash holdings.
Withdraw $5, $10, and possibly $20 bills and replace them with gold
coins so ordinary citizens receive gold in paychecks and spend it in
stores. **Gresham's Law works as an early warning system**: when bad
paper drives out gold coins, citizens notice immediately.

> "The classical or orthodox gold standard alone is a truly effective
> check on the power of the government to inflate the currency.
> Without such a check all other constitutional safeguards can be
> rendered vain."

The **gold-exchange standard's fatal defect** is that inflation can
proceed undetected: only specialists notice, and laymen "do not
realize it is not commodities that are becoming dearer but their
tender that is becoming cheaper."

### The Controversy over the New Gold Parity

Two positions recur whenever an inflated currency returns to sound
money: **restorers** want to return to the old pre-inflation parity
($20.67/oz, the McKinley dollar, or Roosevelt's 1934 $35/oz);
**stabilizers** accept a new parity consonant with current market
value. Restorer arguments fail. Restorers see money primarily as a
standard of deferred payments, but pre-1933 contracts are mostly
settled and current holders of old bonds are not the original
creditors. The Roosevelt $35 rate was never a legal parity — merely
the price at which government bought gold while private citizens were
forbidden to hold it; "honest" restorers invoke a rate they condemn
as dishonest.

The deeper error is neglect of inflation's general redistributive
effects. Inflation never raises all prices equally or simultaneously.
Its **winners** are sellers whose output prices have risen while
input costs still reflect old money values; its **losers** are buyers
paying new higher prices while their selling prices lag. The
Roosevelt-Truman inflation hurt many Americans beyond creditors, and
a deflation back to $35 parity would not heal their wounds but would
"merely open new sores." Deflation is psychologically more unpopular
than inflation, and restorer policy would generate an inflationary
backlash that would destroy any sound-money reform.

The historical warning comes from Britain. After the **Napoleonic
Wars** Britain returned to prewar parity — a calamitous deflation
that stirred social unrest and supplied **Engels and Marx** with
their anti-capitalist inspiration. After **WWI** Britain again
returned in 1925 to prewar parity; unions refused wage adjustments,
foreign trade collapsed, the government blamed "mysterious
overvaluation" and adopted inflation as the remedy in **1931**.
Keynesian fallacies drew their popular plausibility directly from
the 1925 reform's suffering.

The monetary disorder is ultimately an outcome of **social ideology**.
People lament inflation while supporting policies that require it.
Sound-money reform presupposes a radical change in economic
philosophy; no gold standard is possible while waste, capital
decumulation, and corruption characterize public affairs.

> "Cynics dispose of the advocacy of a restitution of the gold
> standard by calling it utopian. Yet we have only the choice between
> two utopias: the utopia of a market economy, not paralyzed by
> government sabotage on the one hand, and the utopia of totalitarian
> all-round planning on the other hand. The choice of the first
> alternative implies the decision in favor of the gold standard."

## Appendix A: On the Classification of Monetary Theories

### Catallactic and Acatallactic Monetary Doctrine

Money has attracted speculation even by non-economists; merchants and
jurists trace its value to properties of the precious metals;
**canonist jurisprudence** held that money's value was *valor
impositus*, decreed by the state; biological analogies treat money as
blood in the economic body; and some call it "a draft by everybody on
everybody else." All these share one fatal flaw: they cannot be
integrated into any theory of exchange ratios. Mises calls them
**acatallactic**. **Catallactic theories** explain money's value
through the laws of exchange and fit into a general theory of value;
this is the necessary (though not sufficient) condition for a real
monetary theory.

Acatallactic views persist because economics necessarily treats direct
exchange (barter) first and money-mediated exchange later — "the
most neglected section of our science" — leaving monetary theory
to the acatallactists. Currency controversies, meanwhile, attract
"practical men" with no general economic training who start from
acatallactic premises. **Bamberger** and **Soetbeer** were notable
exceptions among contemporary German economists who fought the trend.

### The "State" Theory of Money

**Georg Friedrich Knapp** is the leading state theorist; his book
succeeded in Germany because the etatist school had already expelled
catallactics from economics. State theory holds that money's value
rests on the authority of the highest civil power, not on commercial
estimation. Its fatal weakness is that it can establish only nominal
*Geltung* (validity in legal units), never *bonitas intrinseca*
(purchasing power in commerce); the canonists had already exposed this
contradiction. **Nominalism** was historically useful to princes who
debased coinage; it persisted for fiscal reasons; **Gentz** and
**Adam Müller** wrote in its defense during the Austrian *Bankozettel*
period. The state theory cannot address commodity prices, wages,
balance of trade, exchange rates, bimetallism, or inflationism.
**Bendixen**, Knapp's disciple, revealed the absurdity clearly by
calling Germany's depreciated wartime currency "desirable" because it
let Germany sell foreign securities at favorable rates. The actual
monetary doctrine of etatism is the **balance-of-payments theory**
(distinguishing *Binnenwert* internal value from *Aussenwert* external
value), propounded by **Lexis**, not Knapp.

### Schumpeter's Catallactic Claim Theory

Calling money a "claim" is an analogy and not inherently wrong but not
itself an explanation. The naive version (money as ticket to a
room of fixed size: more tickets, less room per holder) collapses
into the crudest quantity theory. **Schumpeter**, building on
**Wieser**, attempted a genuine catallactic claim theory by comparing
not the quantity of money to total goods but the **sum of money
incomes** to total prices of all consumption goods — a more
sophisticated formulation.

To isolate "money in effective circulation" Schumpeter must exclude
hoards, "sums unemployed but awaiting employment," and reserves
(minimum holdings kept for unexpected demands); and then also
exclude all sums circulating in "income-distributing" markets such
as real-estate, mortgage, and securities. Mises objects that these
exclusions destroy Schumpeter's theoretical foundation. "Sums
unemployed but awaiting employment" is logically incoherent: in
strict theory all money not changing hands at a given moment is
"awaiting employment," yet as part of a reserve it still performs
money's characteristic function.

> "It is inadmissible to speak of 'sums that are unemployed but
> awaiting employment.' In a strict and exact sense — and theory
> must take everything in a strict and exact sense — all money that
> is not changing owners at the very moment under consideration is
> awaiting employment."

After stripping away hoards, reserves, and capital-market circulation,
what remains? Schumpeter arbitrarily splits money stock and money
demand to make a comparison possible. A complete theory must treat
all three spheres — circulation, hoards/reserves, and capital —
because changes in any of them affect the value of money. Schumpeter
did not construct a complete catallactic theory from the claim
analogy; his sound conclusions were borrowed from existing catallactic
doctrine.

### "Metallism"

The term **metallism** originates with Knapp, who defined metallists
as writers who "start from weight and fineness and see in the stamp
nothing but an attestation of these properties" — who "define the
unit of value as a certain quantity of metal." Mises objects that
virtually no serious economist ever held that the unit of value
consists of a quantity of metal in Knapp's naive sense. Knapp lumps
all non-nominalistic theories as "metallism," ignoring the fundamental
divide between catallactic and acatallactic theories and ignoring
every competing catallactic doctrine. He polemicizes only against a
straw-man acatallactic theory, while completely ignoring **Bodin**,
**Law**, **Hume**, **Senior**, **Jevons**, **Menger**, and **Walras**.

Knapp labels **Adam Smith** and **Ricardo** "entirely metallistic,"
claiming their theory teaches that the pound sterling is definable as
a specific weight of metal. Mises calls this "an incomprehensible
error" that flatly contradicts Smith's and Ricardo's actual value
theory. Smith expounded the social advantages of paper money in a
manner "hardly equaled by any writer before or after him." Ricardo in
his 1816 *Proposals for an Economical and Secure Currency* explicitly
recommended eliminating precious-metal money from domestic circulation
entirely — the basis for what became the gold-exchange standard,
first in India, then the Straits Settlements, the Philippines, and
Austria-Hungary.

> "A well-regulated paper currency is so great an improvement in
> commerce that I should greatly regret if prejudice should induce
> us to return to a system of less utility." — Ricardo, *Works*

Knapp's disciples could have avoided their errors (including the
mistaken interpretation of Austro-Hungarian Bank policy in 1900–1911)
by actually reading Smith and Ricardo.

### "Metallism" in Wieser and Philippovich

Knapp's historical errors were unfortunately adopted by serious
economists who engaged with his work charitably. **Friedrich von
Wieser** contrasts metallists, for whom "money has an independent
value, arising from itself, from its substance," with "modern theory,"
for which "its value is derived from that of the objects of exchange,
the commodities." Wieser's own "modern theory" posits two sources for
monetary-material value — industrial use value and exchange value as
a medium of payment — which combine into a common estimate. Mises's
point: what Wieser calls "modern theory" is simply the monetary
theory that follows logically from the subjective/utility theory of
value, and it is more than two hundred years old. **John Law**
expressed it in classical form in 1705 in *Money and Trade*. Wieser's
"modern theory" has nothing to do with Knapp; nominalism ignores the
problem of the value of money altogether.

Knapp named two "metallists" in his preface — **Hermann** and
**Knies** — but both actually held views very close to Wieser's. In
reality the metallist Knapp had in mind was **Knapp himself** — the
pre-theoretical, historicist version who lectured on monetary
"pragmatics" before he wrote *State Theory of Money*. **Bendixen**,
Knapp's leading disciple, openly admitted he had once been a
metallist.

**Eugen von Philippovich** (*Grundriss*, 1916) contrasts nominalistic
theories (Adam Müller, Knapp, Adolf Wagner) with catallactic theories
and cites Mises's own book as representative of the latter. He then
professes allegiance to chartal/nominal theory while interpreting it
in a way that effaces the distinction. He declares simultaneously
both that the monetary unit's essential feature is its nominal
*Geltung* (Knapp's position) and that the monetary unit is really
its power of purchase or payment (the starting point of all
catallactic theories). Identifying the monetary unit with purchasing
power contradicts Knapp outright; nominalism's defining feature is
precisely that it does not speak of purchasing power at all.

### Nominalism and the English Banking Schools

Some writers equate metallism with the Currency Principle and call
chartal theory "a variety of the old banking principle." Mises
disagrees: the Currency School vs. Banking School debate lies in an
entirely different sphere from the nominalism/metallism debate. Knapp
and his disciples never perceived the problems the English schools
were actually addressing. **Bendixen's** doctrine of money creation is
an exaggerated and extremely naive version of the banking principle,
only loosely connected to Knapp's nominalism. That Bendixen's doctrine
could be regarded in Germany for years as genuinely new — when it was
merely a crude restatement of the banking principle — reflects the
low state of German monetary theory under historicist dominance.

## Appendix B: Translator's Note on Technical Terms

The German *Umlaufsmittel* has no standard English equivalent. The
translator **H.E. Batson** rejected "circulating medium" (wrong
associations), "bank money" (too narrow), and "credit instrument"
(inconsistent with Mises's insistence that issuing fiduciary media
is not a credit transaction in the fundamental sense). The adopted
translation is **"fiduciary medium,"** which matches Mises's
definition (money substitutes not covered by money), evokes the Peel
Act of 1844 controversies in a way consonant with Mises's attitude,
and highlights the essential similarity between uncovered bank
deposits and uncovered notes.
