# The Theory of Money and Credit — Part I & II (Compacted)

## Preface to the New Edition (1952)

- Modern monetary crises are not acts of God but **government-made**
  consequences of the **inflationary fallacy** running from **John Law**
  to **Lord Keynes**. Sound money has progressively given way to
  depreciating fiat money everywhere.
- Politicians chase short-run relief while ignoring long-run consequences.
  Credit-expansion booms are necessarily followed by crises.

> "The great inflations of our age are not acts of God. They are
> man-made or, to say it bluntly, government-made."

## Introduction by Lionel Robbins

- Robbins notes money had "the longest history and the most extensive
  literature" but lacked systematic integration with mainstream economics.
  Mises fills the gap by relating money to **pure equilibrium theory**,
  doing for money what **Marshall** did for general value theory.
- Original contributions: **forced saving** via credit expansion;
  disparities between equilibrium/natural and money rates of interest;
  trade cycles as consequences of credit instability; the role of
  uncertainty in cash balances.
- Structure: rigid analysis of money's function, then successive
  approximations — one-money economy, parallel currencies, foreign
  exchange, modern banking.

## Preface to the English Edition (1934)

- Banking and currency questions change constantly; the theoretical
  apparatus does not. Economics' value lies in foreseeing remote
  consequences.
- **Britain's 1926 return to gold at pre-war parity** is the case study:
  prices and wages had already adjusted downward to wartime depreciation,
  so restoring the old parity required a severe fall in prices — making
  entrepreneurs' position "more difficult" and forcing wage cuts the
  public was never warned about. The problem was not gold but the
  **politically-chosen parity level**.
- **1926–29 boom**: economists warned of the collapse to come; the 1929
  crisis was predicted, not a surprise. Governments then responded with
  artificial wage stabilization, bolstering failing firms, preventing
  central banks from raising rates — eliminating the only real recovery
  mechanism.

> "Stabilization of wages must mean increasing unemployment and the
> perpetuation of the disproportion between actual prices and costs
> and between outputs and sales which is the symptom of a crisis."

- **Gold standard is a red herring.** The real question is whether to
  maintain or reduce purchasing power. Index numbers cannot decide it:
  every method produces different results and each result benefits some
  groups and injures others — so it becomes politics, not science.
- The greatest variations in money's value in the past century originated
  from government/central bank policy, not gold supply fluctuations.
  Modern money is a "plaything of politics."

## Preface to the Second German Edition (1924)

- First edition (1912) was published when inflationism dominated war
  preparation intellectually. Mises' critics later obtained power and
  implemented their doctrines — validating his warnings through collapse.
- Claim that economics "broke down" in the war is ignorant of theory;
  it confuses economics with the **historico-empirico-realistic school**
  (empiricists without theory). Theoretical guidance existed — politics
  refused to follow it.
- **Core thesis**: credit expansion can never substitute for real capital
  accumulation. Artificial stimulus via circulation expansion necessarily
  leads to crisis and depression. Recurring crises are the inevitable
  consequence of trying to stimulate activity through additional credit.

> "Money is nothing but a medium of exchange and it completely fulfills
> its function when the exchange of goods and services is carried on
> more easily with its help than would be possible by means of barter.
> Attempts at carrying out economic reforms from the monetary side can
> never amount to anything but an artificial stimulation of economic
> activity by an expansion of the circulation."

- Mises now holds his theory (an elaboration of the **Currency School**)
  is a *sufficient* explanation of crises, not merely a supplement.

## Part One: The Nature of Money

## Chapter 1: The Functions of Money

### General conditions for money

- Money presupposes: division of labor, private property in both
  consumption and production goods, and the absence of a central
  authority controlling production. In a socialist centrally-planned
  economy, or a purely domestic household, money is unnecessary.
- Market economy production is "anarchistic" — owners of means decide,
  responding to others' valuations expressed in the market. Money's
  **core function**: common medium of exchange.

### Origin of money (Menger's hypothesis)

- Barter fails whenever there is no double coincidence of wants. The
  Roman jurist **Paulus** in the Pandects already observed:
  *"Quia non semper nec facile concurrebat, ut, cum tu haberas, quod ego
  desideraram, invicem haberem, quod tu accipere velles."*
- Individuals engage in indirect exchange when what they receive is
  **more marketable** than what they give. A self-reinforcing process
  follows: the most marketable goods become preferred media → their
  marketability rises further → less-marketable media get rejected →
  convergence toward a single universal medium = **money**.
- Historically gold and silver emerged early due to their physical and
  chemical properties. Mankind "wavered undecided between gold and
  silver for thousands of years"; only where communities isolated from
  trade did a single-metal standard arise, and reopening to trade
  re-introduced duality.
- Monetary theory must account for simultaneous use of multiple media
  wherever the conclusions depend on it.

### "Secondary" functions all reduce to the primary function

- Critics inflate money's functions to half-a-dozen. Mises: all are
  derivatives of the single function of common medium of exchange.
- **Standard of deferred payments**: a useful descriptive phrase but NOT
  a separate function; treating it as one causes theorists to fixate on
  debt relations and ignore the rest of monetary value effects.
- **Transmitter of value through time/space** (Menger): hoarding matters
  historically; spatial transmission still matters (e.g., emigrants
  selling property, carrying money, rebuying abroad).
- **Medium of payment**: a juridical category smuggled into economics.
  The lawyer asks "what is money?" only to determine how to discharge a
  debt; the economist cannot start there without prejudicing the theory.

> "If the function of money as an object which facilitates dealings in
> commodities and capital is kept in mind...there remains neither
> necessity nor justification for further discussion of a special
> employment, or even function of money, as a medium of payment."

- Referenced: **Menger** (*Grundsätze*, *Untersuchungen*), **Wicksell**,
  **Nicholson**, **Laughlin**, **Knies** (*Geld und Kredit*),
  **Schumpeter**.

## Chapter 2: On the Measurement of Value

### Subjective use-values are not measurable

- The notion that money "measures" value is **entirely fallacious** under
  modern subjective value theory. Under the older **objective theory**
  measurement was coherent, and money naturally served as its unit.
- Under modern (subjective) theory, value is the significance an
  individual imputes to a unit of a commodity when choosing among
  alternatives. Valuation is **ordinal**: "bread > iron > meat", but
  not by how much. Scales hold only at a moment, for a given stock of
  wants and goods. Exchange requires no measurement — only comparison.

### Mises vs. Böhm-Bawerk, Fisher, Schumpeter

- **Böhm-Bawerk** argued that choosing between one large satisfaction
  and many small ones requires cardinal measurement. **Cuhel** exposed
  the error: "I prefer 8 plums to 1 apple, and 1 apple to 7 plums" does
  NOT imply apple-satisfaction is between 7× and 8× plum-satisfaction.
  Concluding so requires that *n* units' satisfaction = *n* × 1 unit's,
  which violates **Gossen's law of diminishing marginal utility**.
- **Irving Fisher** tried to define a "util" by comparing marginal
  utilities via a third good (oil): the 100th loaf's utility equals some
  quantity of oil; the 150th loaf's utility equals half that oil
  quantity; ergo its utility is half. But this smuggles in the
  assumption that *n* units of oil yield *n* × marginal utility of one
  unit — exactly what needed proving. "Mathematics here merely conceals
  the artificial construction."
- **Schumpeter** used a fixed consumption quantity as the unit.
  Nobody actually makes decisions that way. Both he and Fisher commit
  the same error: assuming we need to measure in order to compare.

> "Subjective use-value is not susceptible of any kind of measurement."

### Total value and the cows-and-horses example

- **Wieser's formula** (items × marginal utility of one item) for total
  stock value is untenable. An individual with 2 cows and 3 horses whose
  value-scale ranks cow, horse, horse, horse, cow would — faced with
  saving *one* item from fire — save the horse. But forced to save
  *all* cows vs *all* horses, he saves the 2 cows over the 3 horses.
  Group valuation reverses individual ranking.
- **Value exists only in a specific act of appraisal by a specific
  subject over a specific complex of goods.** There is no value outside
  the particular process of valuation.

### Money as a price index (not a measure)

- Exchange creates a need to rearrange value scales: position in the
  scale is no longer determined by use-value alone but also by what can
  be obtained in exchange for a good.
- Money becomes a **price index** (Menger's phrase), a common
  denominator letting individuals navigate "the confusing multiplicity
  of exchange ratios." The entrepreneur's and consumer's entire
  calculation rests on valuing commodities in money — it is "an aid the
  human mind is no longer able to dispense with."
- Calling money a "measure of prices" is acceptable if properly
  understood, but it invites misunderstanding. "We do not call stars a
  function of latitude and longitude."

## Chapter 3: The Various Kinds of Money

### Money-substitutes

- A perfectly secure claim on money, immediately redeemable, can be
  transferred in place of actual money. Money's extraordinary
  **fungibility** is what makes this possible — unlike other goods
  (except shares/bonds), a claim on money can pass through indefinite
  indirect exchanges without the debtor ever being called upon.
- **Money-substitutes** = absolutely secure, immediately payable claims
  on money, valued exactly like cash. Where soundness is doubtful or
  maturity deferred, the claim is valued independently → it becomes
  credit money, not a money-substitute.
- The popular conflation of money and money-substitutes is legally
  harmless but scientifically disastrous. Economic theory must trace
  the laws governing the ratio between money and goods and needs
  precise terminology.

> "Claims are not goods; they are means of obtaining disposal over
> goods... They themselves are not valued directly, but indirectly;
> their value is derived from that of the economic goods to which
> they refer."

### Historical token coinage

- **German Coinage Act of 9 July 1873**: silver tokens capped at 20
  marks per head, nickel/copper at 2.5 marks — limits calibrated to
  estimated demand for small change, kept within demand. **Reichsbank**
  obliged to cash tokens for gold; always done without demur.
- **Reich Treasury notes** (max 120 million marks): not legal tender in
  private transactions but always redeemed by the Reichsbank — de facto
  money-substitutes.
- **German thaler** (pre-Oct 1, 1907): legally legal tender but
  economically a claim on gold — a money-substitute. The Reichsbank
  managed supply by refusing to press thalers on customers.
- **Austria-Hungary 1900–14**: legally a paper standard (Austro-Hungarian
  Bank not obliged to redeem), but in practice a **gold-exchange
  standard** — the bank supplied gold for commercial needs. The true
  money of the country was gold; Bank notes were substitutes.

### Commodity, credit, and fiat money

- Monetary theory has been expressed in juristic terminology built by
  statesmen, merchants, and judges — "practically valueless" for
  economic investigation. Confusion between law and economics "nowhere
  so frequent and fraught with mischievous consequences as in monetary
  theory."
- Mises' threefold classification:
  - **Commodity money**: a commercial commodity used as money
    (gold, silver). The commodity itself is the money.
  - **Fiat money**: objects with a special legal qualification. The
    stamp, not the material, constitutes the money. Theoretically
    conceivable; whether historically realized is doubtful.
  - **Credit money**: a claim against a person or institution,
    maturing in the future, used as a general medium of exchange.
    Maturity must be deferred; if payable on demand and absolutely
    secure, it would be a money-substitute instead.
- The **metallic/paper** distinction is scientifically useless: metallic
  money includes tokens and the German thaler 1873–1907; paper money
  includes both fiat/credit money *and* convertible banknotes.

### Commodity money today — against the chartalists

- **Knapp** and **Hammer** (nominalists/chartalists) deny that gold or
  silver ever truly performed monetary functions — money is "units of
  value with a definite significance assigned by law." Their explanation
  for exchange ratios: "the value of money is determined by the state,
  by statute, by the legal system."
- Nominalism cannot explain exchange ratios between money and goods —
  the central problem of monetary theory — because it denies the
  problem exists. It is the "renascence" of the oldest primitive
  theories, popular because it aligns with **inflationism** and
  "cheap-money" politics.
- **Historical evidence against chartalism**: coins were tendered and
  accepted by weight and fineness, not by tale; debasement never made
  lighter coins pass as equal to heavier ones; the **Schöffen** of
  Schweidnitz reset commodity prices to each newly minted pfennig;
  13th-century Vienna's *forma institutionis* regulated prices against
  new coinage (1460–1474); German merchants at Geneva bypassed official
  coinage entirely, using refined gold ingots priced by Paris weights
  (**Markenskudo** = 3.765g refined gold), which became the unit of
  account for bills of exchange. Early **giro banks** cleared on coins
  of specific fineness or on ingots — "bank money was commodity money
  in its most perfect form."
- Pre-1914 Germany: 1 mark = 1/2790 kg refined gold by German law. State
  intervention merely released individuals from the burden of testing
  weight and fineness themselves.
- In large international trade coins are valued by weight, not number.
  Large domestic payments transfer claims ultimately referring to
  central-bank metal stocks. Modern freely-minted coinage is commodity
  money "just as much as that of ancient and medieval nations."

## Chapter 4: Money and the State

### The state's place in the market

- The state is one market participant among many, subject to the same
  laws of price. It can levy taxes by sovereign right but as buyer or
  seller must conform to market conditions.
- Price controls fail not because they were national: they would fail
  in an isolated state too. **Diocletian's edict** *de pretiis rerum
  venalium*, medieval price regulations, and **French Revolutionary**
  maximum-price laws all failed for structural reasons, not enforcement.
  They could succeed only in a fully socialist state.

> "The concept of money as a creature of law and the state is clearly
> untenable. It is not justified by a single phenomenon of the market."

### Legal vs economic definition of money

- Legally, money is the common **medium of payment**, but it only
  became one because it was first the common medium of exchange.
- Courts accept money-substitutes because commerce already treats them
  as money; refusing them would invite fraud (*malitiis non est
  indulgendum*).
- The state can declare anything legal tender, but that alone cannot
  make it money economically. Legal-tender declarations affect only
  *existing* obligations. For future contracts commerce is free to
  choose any medium — during Germany's 1890s bimetallist scare,
  long-term contracts proliferated **gold clauses** to defend against
  inflationist legislation.

### State influence on the monetary system

- Historical progression: early coins (stamp as proof of fineness, weight
  independently verified) → classified coins → **parallel standard**
  (gold and silver circulating side by side) → legally fixed ratios
  (**bimetallism**).
- State influence has grown but the state has NEVER had the power to
  directly create money — only commercial usage can. Influence rests on:
  (1) the state's role as economic agent, (2) control of the mint, and
  (3) the power to alter money-substitutes in circulation.
- **Knapp's State Theory of Money** (1921) and **Helfferich**'s semi-
  statist view (compulsory obligations create demand for state money)
  are rejected on the same ground: declaring an object legally fit to
  discharge liabilities does not determine what commerce will adopt as
  medium of exchange.
- **Bimetallism as case study in state failure**: wherever the state
  legally fixed a ratio between gold and silver, **Gresham's Law**
  operated — the overvalued metal drove out the undervalued metal,
  converting a parallel standard into an alternative (monometallic)
  standard. The state intended a double standard and got monometallism
  it did not choose.

> "It had been demonstrated, in striking fashion, that the state alone
> could not make a commodity into a common medium of exchange... this
> could be done only by the common action of all the individuals
> engaged in business."

- What the state CAN do via mint control is transition from one metal
  to another by supplying new coins and withdrawing old (Germany's
  1871 switch to gold, funded by the Franco-Prussian indemnity;
  Austria-Hungary and Russia followed similar methods). Legal changes
  were "mere accompaniments" to the metal supply change.
- States never *chose* to introduce credit/fiat money — it always arose
  from fiscal emergencies that stripped convertibility from existing
  money-substitutes, *against* the state's will.

## Chapter 5: Money as an Economic Good

### Money is neither production nor consumption good

- Standard division: consumption (first-order) vs production (higher-
  order) goods. Money fits neither. Most economists defaulted to
  calling it a production good since it clearly is not consumption.
- **Roscher**: money is "the chief instrument of every transfer."
  **Knies**: adds a *third* category — media of exchange — alongside
  production and consumption goods. Mises endorses Knies.
- **Böhm-Bawerk** wrongly includes money in social capital by analogy
  with horses, wagons, roads, railways. Mises: this conflates transport
  (changing spatial utility) with exchange (a distinct economic
  category).
- Logical test: removing money does not destroy production — isolated
  households and socialized communities can produce without it. No
  first-order good requires money as a condition of its production.
- **Adam Smith** (approvingly cited): money is "dead stock, which...
  produces nothing."
- **Key asymmetry**: gain/loss of a production or consumption good
  changes aggregate welfare. Gain/loss of money does NOT — the value
  of money adjusts to compensate.

> "An increase in the quantity of money can no more increase the
> welfare of the members of a community, than a diminution of it can
> decrease their welfare."

### Money as private capital but not social capital

- Terminology matters here because money's capital status bears on the
  relation between the **natural rate of interest** and the **money
  rate** (Part III).
- **Böhm-Bawerk** definition of private (acquisitive) capital: "the
  aggregate of products that serve as a means to the acquisition of
  goods." Money belongs here — historically the concept of capital
  *started* with an interest-bearing sum of money.
- Money is acquisitive capital only when exchanged; idle money is not
  capital. The real capital is what the money buys.
- Money is NOT part of **social (productive) capital** — it is not a
  productive good. Mainstream economists who include money in social
  capital contradict themselves when, analyzing interest determinants,
  they correctly identify the quantity of *other goods* (not money) as
  what matters.

## Chapter 6: The Enemies of Money

### Money under socialism

- Socialism at the collective level mirrors the isolated household and
  has no role for money so long as exchange is excluded. But the moment
  any exchange is permitted, indirect exchange — and therefore money —
  logically follows.
- **Labor certificates** (hours worked redeemable for an aliquot share
  of total output) are NOT money. Their value varies mechanically with
  total output (if output drops 50%, each certificate drops 50%); they
  have no independent valuation process and are simply claims on
  specific goods.

### Money cranks

- "Money cranks" accept private property and free exchange but blame
  money itself for social ills — theft, prostitution, bribery,
  materialism. They confuse the instrument with incentive. Schemes stay
  vague and "call a halt at the point where the difficulties of the
  problem are just beginning."
- **Thomas More's *Utopia*** (golden chains for criminals, gold chamber
  pots for citizens) — primitive symbolic vengeance, not economics.
- A more sophisticated variant: **Ernest Solvay's "social comptabilism"**
  and similar proposals for an **elastic credit system automatically
  adapted to the need for currency**. These schemes are taken from the
  **Tooke/Fullarton** banking-school tradition and are consistent with
  its flawed premises — which is why banking theorists cannot refute
  them. Part III will demolish the elasticity doctrine and render
  further discussion of such schemes unnecessary.

## Part Two: The Value of Money

## Chapter 7: The Concept of the Value of Money

### Subjective and objective factors

- Central category in monetary economics: the **objective exchange
  value** of money (popularly, purchasing power).
- Subjective valuation is as fundamental for money as for any good, but
  money has a unique feature: **its subjective use-value and subjective
  exchange value coincide completely**. Bread has utility even if you
  don't sell it; money has NO utility except the ability to obtain
  other goods. Therefore subjective value of money depends on objective
  exchange value.

> "The exchange value of money is the anticipated use-value of the
> things that can be obtained with it." — Wieser, *Der natürliche Wert*

- Money value is a **derived concept**. Explaining it is harder than
  explaining commodity value: for commodities the economist takes
  subjective use-value as given; for money he must trace objective
  determinants back to their subjective origin.

### The required level of purchasing power

- Practical/technical limits exist — a money one ton of which equaled
  a dollar, or one-thousandth of a milligram equaling a dollar, would
  face impossible logistics. But this is a question of what material
  can serve as money, not of value theory. The precious metals' moderate
  scarcity was historically decisive.

### Objective exchange value (Wieser's *Verkehrswert*)

- Objective exchange value is not an intrinsic property of goods; it
  emerges from collective subjective valuations in the market but
  presents itself to each individual as an accomplished fact. This
  appearance of fact is what misled the **Classical School**
  (Smith, Ricardo) into attributing intrinsic exchange value to goods.
- **Price** = the actual quantity of other goods received. **Objective
  exchange value** = the power to command such quantities. Related, not
  identical, but governed by the same laws.

### Value does not reduce to industrial use of the monetary material

- A widely-held error (held even by Menger): money's value depends
  solely on its **industrial** (non-monetary) employment. This fails:
  - Cannot explain **fiat money** (material value far below monetary).
  - Cannot explain **credit money**.
  - It eliminates rather than solves the problem.
- Gold's value today rests on both monetary and industrial demand,
  impossible to cleanly separate. Ingots move from bank vaults to
  goldsmiths and back; worn coins circulate as jewelry.
- **Demonetization evidence**: the sharp fall in silver after the
  1873 wave of demonetizations; the fall in gold's value 1914–18 as
  countries replaced it with banknotes and gold concentrated in gold-
  standard countries. Monetary demand substantially supports value.
- **Nickel example**: when nickel coinage began (~50 years before
  writing), nickel prices rose so sharply that by 1873 the English
  mint director warned that the material cost would exceed face value.
- Monetary theory takes the industrial-use value of the material as
  given. It must explain money's value *insofar as that value depends
  on monetary function*. For credit/fiat money the material is
  essentially irrelevant — a 100-gulden Austro-Hungarian note commanded
  a higher exchange value than a 100-gulden government security, even
  though the security bore interest.

## Chapter 8: Determinants of the Objective Exchange Value (The Regression Theorem)

### The circularity and its resolution

- Price emerges from subjective valuation (Menger, Böhm-Bawerk). Money
  prices are bounded by marginal buyer and marginal seller valuations.
- Money's subjective use-value *is* its subjective exchange value. So to
  value money, an individual must already assume a certain purchasing
  power — a *point d'appui* is needed.
- This presupposed purchasing power is the exchange ratio **ruling at
  the time** in the market. Money prices of today are linked to prices
  of yesterday and tomorrow. But that just postpones the problem: what
  originally determined the value?

### The regression theorem

- **Answer**: the first value of money was the **commodity value** of the
  monetary material at the moment it was first used as a medium of
  exchange. When men began acquiring goods not for consumption but to
  exchange, they valued them at the **objective exchange value the
  market already credited them with** from industrial use.
- An object cannot become money unless it already possesses exchange
  value from some other use. This refutes social-contract theories
  (**Locke** in *Some Considerations*, 1696 is named) and confirms
  **Menger's** hypothesis.
- The same applies to credit and fiat money: both began as money-
  substitutes or convertible coins, and took their starting value from
  the pre-existing exchange value at the moment convertibility was
  stripped away. Once established, money can retain value even after
  its original non-monetary source of value disappears.

### Past prices as indispensable component

- For ordinary goods, price stability reflects stability of subjective
  valuations (needs, tastes, stocks). Yesterday's beer price has **zero**
  causal bearing on today's — Prohibition could wipe it out overnight.
  Exchange ratios on the German stock exchange today have no causal
  connection to ratios under Hermann or Barbarossa.
- For money, past exchange value is an **indispensable element** in
  present determination, not merely a starting point. A historically
  continuous component is transmitted forward.
- Price-inertia metaphors (from mechanics) are acceptable only if they
  refer to stable underlying valuations. **Zwiedineck** is criticized
  for attributing causal power to inertia itself — which means giving
  up any hope of explaining ultimate causes.

### Prior theories of money's value

- **Cost-of-production theory** (Senior, *Three Lectures on the Value
  of Money*, 1840): formally coherent for commodity money but cannot
  handle credit or fiat money.
- **Davanzati's quantity theory** (1588): equates all goods with all
  monetary metal, deducing ratios mechanically. Not supported by facts,
  but Davanzati was the first to ask about the *origin* of the exchange
  ratio, not merely its changes.
- Most quantity theorists (**Menger**, **Jevons**, **Carver**, **Kinley**,
  **Walras**, **Kemmerer**) *assume* an existing value of money and
  only explain variations.
- **Wieser** acknowledged the problem: the older quantity theory only
  tells us the *direction* of movement, not the level. His own theory
  (*objektive innere Tauschwert*, based on the ratio of money income to
  real income) explained variations but provided no complete theory.
  Still, Wieser deserves credit for identifying the **historical
  element** in purchasing power.
- **Wicksell** argued marginal utility is practically irrelevant and
  locates the regulator in the **loan market**: the ratio between the
  money rate of interest (*Darlehnszins*) and the **natural** rate
  (*natürliche Kapitalzins*) — the rate that would prevail if real
  capital were lent without money intermediation. Mises' critique:
  Wicksell shows only that loan-market forces prevent prices from
  rising too high or falling too low — he never determines the actual
  level, and you cannot explain why equilibrium is maintained without
  first explaining what determined it.

> "The laws of the exchange of commodities contain in themselves
> nothing that could determine the absolute level of money prices."
> — Wicksell (rejected by Mises as amounting to a denial of scientific
> investigation in this sphere)

- **Helfferich** (*Das Geld*, 6th ed., 1923) argued circularity is
  insurmountable: marginal utility of money depends on its exchange
  value. Second objection: the utility of a given money supply can be
  raised at will by raising unit value, so marginal analysis fails.
- Mises' rebuttal:
  1. The regression theorem dissolves the circularity — what must be
     presupposed is **yesterday's** exchange value, not today's.
  2. Helfferich errs by evaluating money from the *community's*
     viewpoint rather than the individual's; a society with no exchange
     would have no money and no monetary utility. Theory must start
     from individuals.
  3. Before indirect exchange became customary, goods were valued only
     by direct utility; the additional monetary utility then emerged
     and both became subject to marginal utility.

## Chapter 9: Local Differences in Purchasing Power

### Interlocal price relations

- Money's utility is relatively **free from distance**. Checks, clearing,
  and wire transfer mean gold at the Bank of England can serve
  anywhere. **Law of interlocal price relations**: under free exchange
  and ignoring transit time, the money price of a commodity in any
  place equals its price elsewhere plus or minus the **money cost of
  transport** (including recoinage, insurance, etc.).
- These transport factors combine into the **foreign exchange rate**.

### Alleged local differences in purchasing power

- Popular belief: purchasing power varies across localities. Pre-1914
  travelers to Austria from Germany "hardly doubted" that money was
  worth more in Germany.
- **Mises' critique**: this confuses different goods for same goods.
  Wheat in Russia and wheat in Germany are economically different
  goods — they differ in the **positional factor** of being ready for
  consumption in different places. Taking the absurdity further, by
  the same logic purchasing power would vary building to building,
  room to room, across customs lines.

### Cost of living

- Common belief: "living is cheaper" in some places. But that is NOT
  the same as purchasing power differing. Equal exchange ratios can
  coexist with unequal costs of satisfying wants, because residence in
  a place **awakens wants** unknown elsewhere — social obligations for
  an upper-class Englishman at home; urban proximity to theaters and
  concerts stimulating desires unknown to the provincial; Europeans in
  tropical climates needing health precautions unnecessary in temperate
  zones.
- **Karlsbad example**: a visitor paying premium prices at the spa is
  paying for spatial proximity to the medicinal springs. A local
  laborer forced to live there gets no benefit from that quality, so
  the higher prices are a real reduction in his welfare — hence
  hardship bonuses and wage premiums. Government pays hardship
  bonuses to officials in expensive posts in explicit recognition
  that nominal purchasing power is uniform but subjective cost differs.

> "The purchasing power of money is the same everywhere; only the
> commodities offered are not the same. They differ in a quality that
> is economically significant — the position in space of the place at
> which they are ready for consumption."

- **Wieser's** objection (international money movements are auxiliary
  to commodity movements; labor being nationally bound keeps national
  price levels distinct) smacks of the cost-of-production theory of
  value and is rejected as incompatible with subjective theory.

## Chapter 10: Exchange Ratios Between Different Kinds of Money

### Parallel standard and foreign exchange are the same problem

- An exchange ratio between two moneys requires both be used
  simultaneously as media of exchange by the same agents.
- Two cases — domestic parallel standard and foreign exchange — rest on
  the same principles. Pre-1873 gold was money for the German buyer of
  English goods, silver for the English buyer of German goods. Both
  metals were money for both areas through trade linkage.

### The static/natural exchange ratio

- The exchange ratio between two moneys is determined by their
  respective ratios against other goods. If 1 kg gold exchanges for *m*
  kg of X and 1 kg silver for *m/15.5* kg of X, the gold:silver ratio
  is 15.5. Disturbances are corrected by arbitrage.
- **Cassel** later coined the term **purchasing-power parity** for
  this static ratio (*Money and Foreign Exchange After 1914*).

### International distribution of money — against Mercantilism

- In pure barter no "balances" arise. Money changes the form, not the
  substance: goods are still ultimately exchanged for goods.
- The classical achievement (Hume, Smith, Ricardo): international money
  movements are **not consequences of trade balances but causes** of
  equilibrating adjustments. Metals distribute among individuals and
  nations according to the intensity of monetary demand; no state
  intervention needed. This "dealt the **Mercantilist theory** its
  death blow."

> "In spite of Hume, Smith, and Ricardo, it [Mercantilism] still
> dominates men's minds more than would be expected. Phoenixlike, it
> rises again and again from its own ashes."

- Mises blames the persistence of Mercantilism on obscure notions about
  **fiduciary media** — writers who know the classical arguments only
  secondhand cannot grasp the true rationale for limiting note issue.
- Money does NOT flow toward the highest interest rates or the richest
  nations; it distributes by marginal utility, like any other good.
  A country's trade deficit or surplus that is not dependent on altered
  demand for money can only be transient. A country without fiduciary
  media can never permanently lose its money stock.
- Classical doctrine was limited to present goods, metallic money, and
  ignored services and intertemporal exchange. Credit-money treatment
  was inadequate — never fully remedied.

## Chapter 11: Measuring Variations in the Exchange Value of Money

### History and nature of the problem

- Index numbers attract attention far exceeding their real significance.
  Laymen demand numerical summaries; figures awaken interest where
  abstract laws do not. "Its history becomes a series of dates, its
  economics a collection of statistical data." Arguments against index
  numbers can be drawn from the very writings of their inventors.
- **Two co-soluble measurement problems**:
  1. Numerically demonstrate that variations in money's exchange value
     occurred.
  2. Determine whether those variations are attributable to the monetary
     side or the commodity side of price ratios.
- A stable measuring rod requires a good of unchanging exchange value,
  but that would require all ratios among all goods to be free from
  variation — never the case. The two problems are co-soluble or
  co-insoluble: proving one impossible proves the other impossible.

### Index methods and their fatal flaw

- All index methods try to **average price movements across many
  commodities** so that commodity-side influences cancel out, revealing
  monetary-side influences. This would work only if ratios between
  non-money goods were constant — which they never are.
- All price determinants work through **subjective valuations**, and
  the weight of any factor on those valuations cannot be measured.
  Statistical investigation is "thrown on its own resources at the very
  point where it most needs support."
- Qualified concession: index numbers can serve for **historical and
  statistical description** — just not for monetary theory or policy.

### Wieser's refinement — and why even it fails

- **Wieser** proposed refining the **budgetary method** (earlier
  associated with **Falkner**): track typical incomes and whether the
  same real income requires more or less money. If nominal wages rise
  but real wages stay constant, money's value has fallen; if both rise
  in parallel, only commodity-side forces are at work.
- Mises calls this "the most nearly perfect and most deeply thought
  out of all methods of calculating index numbers."
- **Fatal flaw**: it still requires the impossible assumption that
  ratios between non-money goods are constant. This presupposes inertia
  of institutions, population, wealth distribution, and subjective
  valuations — "where everything is in a state of flux, the supposition
  breaks down completely."
- Wieser himself acknowledged the difficulty: over long periods, income
  types and consumption classes change; new goods appear, old ones
  disappear (20th-century clothing vs 10th-century clothing are
  incomparable). **Marshall's chain method** (comparing only adjacent
  periods) only pushes back the problem.
- All index systems ultimately rest on the fiction of an **"eternal
  human with invariable valuations."** Wieser's is the apex of the
  methodology — and its failure condemns the entire enterprise for
  monetary theory.

### Practical utility

- Index numbers can still perform workaday services for policy. Over
  short periods, errors may be small enough for rough conclusions. But
  beware demanding more than they can deliver.

## Chapter 12: Social Consequences of Variations in Exchange Value

### Exchange of present for future goods

- Variations in money's objective exchange value cause redistribution of
  income and property via two mechanisms: (1) people fail to foresee
  monetary variability; (2) changes do not affect all goods
  simultaneously.
- For millennia, price variations were attributed only to commodity-side
  factors. **Jean Bodin** was the first to scientifically attack this
  assumption. It disappeared from academic literature but lingered in
  popular opinion — and lingers in **legal** opinion most of all: the
  law treats money's value as stable not as legal fiction but as a
  genuine fact in jurists' minds. Legal codes were set before Bodin;
  modern monetary economics has left no trace on civil law.

> Latin maxim from medieval debasement disputes:
> *"Prout quidque contractum est, ita et solvi debet."*
> (A contract must be discharged in the same kind as it was entered.)

- **Redistribution in credit**: rising purchasing power harms debtors
  and helps creditors; falling purchasing power does the opposite.
  These effects would vanish if parties could foresee changes — but
  neither direction nor extent can be reliably foreseen.
- **Foreign exchange as partial awareness**: people allow for
  variability when two kinds of money visibly fluctuate against each
  other, but still assume the reference currency (e.g., gold) is itself
  stable. Gold is never suspected.
- **Interest rate adjustment**: expected depreciation → lenders demand
  higher rates, borrowers willingly pay them. But the adjustment is
  always inadequate because gold's variability is still unrecognized.
- **Tabular standard / "bundle of goods" proposal**: some economists
  suggest settling credit in a bundle of goods rather than a fixed
  nominal sum. Mises rejects it on the same ground as all index-number
  proposals — equal weighting of wheat and pepper, or any fixed parity
  coefficient, becomes more distorting as conditions of production and
  consumption change. **Irving Fisher**'s purchasing-power-stabilization
  proposals share the flaw.
- Post-WWI European inflation: creditor injury was the product of law
  itself — laws making inconvertible banknotes legal tender, canceling
  banks' conversion obligations, forcing loans to government via new
  issue. Remedy: abandon inflationary policy, not *ad hoc* redistribution.

### Accounting distortions

- Commercial accounting treats money like a unit of length. This embeds
  a second assumption — of stable value — on top of the normal
  uncertainty of valuation (which commerce handles by conservative
  asset and liberal liability estimates).
- When money's value falls, bookkeeping shows **apparent profits** by
  setting current revenue against production costs in older, higher-
  value money and writing off assets at old high book values. What is
  called profit is really consumed capital — dissipated by
  entrepreneurs as income, by consumers via price reductions, by
  workers as higher wages, or by the state as taxes. Result: **capital
  consumption disguised as income**.
- **Austrian export capital drain 1920–21**: Dutch traders sold goods
  to Austria and later bought them back much cheaper. Austrian
  merchants had sold below cost without realizing it because
  depreciation made bookkeeping show profits that were actually capital
  liquidation. Mercantilists misread this as an "export premium" until
  recognizing the source was the community's capital being sold off.
- Remedy: conduct bookkeeping in a stable money. But for depreciation
  of gold itself (the world money) no easy remedy exists.

### Consequences when only one money is used — the gold mine illustration

- A change in a single commodity's price affects only producers and
  consumers of that good and may reflect real wealth increases. A
  change in money's exchange value affects **every agent** because
  everyone holds cash.
- If the change were instant and uniform, it would be like changing
  units of measurement. **Real effects arise solely because changes
  are never simultaneous — they propagate outward from a point.**
- **Four-group gold mine model**:
  1. *Mine owners* receive new money first — maximum gain.
  2. *Luxury-goods producers* gain early as miners spend.
  3. *Other producers* gain from increased demand but are already
     paying higher luxury prices; net gain shrinks.
  4. *Agriculturalists* (last reached) pay higher prices for all
     industrial goods before their own prices adjust; by the time they
     do, the price wave has already passed. Their earlier losses are
     permanently uncompensated.

> "It is these losses of the groups that are the last to be reached
> by the variation in the value of money which ultimately constitute
> the source of the profits made by the mine owners and the groups
> most closely connected with them."

- The same logic applies to paper inflation: new notes reach war
  contractors first, spread through luxury producers outward, ending
  with those on fixed incomes. Under deflation the same mechanism
  reverses: those forced to cut prices while still paying old input
  prices lose; those slow to cut gain.
- **Historical winners and losers** under inflation: wage laborers and
  civil servants (fixed money incomes lagging behind goods) have
  historically been the hardest hit; entrepreneurs gained what they
  lost, until unions enabled faster wage adjustment.
- **Core principle**: an increase in the quantity of money does NOT
  increase the stock of consumption goods. Any apparent welfare gain is
  a transfer — specifically from those reached last by the wave to
  those reached first.
- **International dimension**: countries receiving new money (gold
  discoveries in America, Australia, South Africa) bought goods from
  abroad at still-old prices while domestic prices had already risen.
  Europe exported ships, rails, textiles, machines for gold it scarcely
  needed, at prices that did not fully reflect the coming depreciation.

### Variations between two kinds of money

- 19th-century trade growth coincided with diverging standards (credit
  money in some nations, bimetallism elsewhere). The silver/gold ratio,
  stable for centuries, began to swing sharply; WWI amplified the
  problem.
- **Sequential nature** of cross-currency propagation is the sole
  reason it has real effects. **Stock exchange speculation** anticipates
  changes in money values ahead of commodity prices. Exporters from a
  depreciating-currency country gain temporarily, selling at new
  higher prices while still paying old wage and input costs.
- **Fundamental conclusion**: monetary variations only redistribute
  the social stock of goods; they never increase the total.

## Chapter 13: Monetary Policy

### Definition and currency controversy

- Monetary policy is a modern phenomenon. Before modern central banks,
  state involvement meant minting coin honestly or debasing it fiscally
  — neither constituting policy.
- Modern currency policy: using control of the money supply to pursue
  sociopolitical aims by influencing the objective exchange value.
- The 19th-century **gold vs silver** debate conflated two distinct
  motivations: silver miners wanted higher *commodity* prices for
  silver; farmers and debtors wanted *monetary* depreciation (lower
  purchasing power). Public slogans ("dollar of our fathers," "our dear
  old gulden note") hid the real issue of who gains and who loses.

> "It did not seem as if men were disputing about the distribution of
> economic goods; rather it was as if the precious metals were
> contending among themselves and against paper for the lordship of
> the market."

### Instruments

- Principal monetary tool: state control over the kind of money used.
  Under a metallic standard the only policy action is switching metals.
  Under credit/fiat money the state can additionally alter quantity —
  crude and unpredictable but easy and popular for its drastic effects.

### Inflationism

- **Inflationism** = policy aimed at increasing the quantity of money.
- **Naïve inflationism**: believes more money = more wealth; fails to
  understand that increasing quantity reduces purchasing power. Demands
  the state "create" money to free the poor from capitalists — Mises
  calls this frankly ignorant of monetary theory.

> "You statesmen want to build railways, and complain of the low state
> of the exchequer? Well, then, do not beg loans from the capitalists...
> Create money, and help yourselves."

- **Sophisticated inflationism**: understands depreciation but wants it
  to favor debtors, boost exports, discourage imports, stimulate
  production. Each argument fails:
  - **Debtor relief collapses** when depreciation is anticipated —
    creditors demand a premium. Only *unforeseen* depreciation transfers
    wealth. Post-WWI deposits shrank because banks refused to pay
    interest high enough to compensate for purchasing-power losses.
  - **Production stimulation** would help only if capital accumulation
    resulted — but modern capitalism relies on loans (bonds, deposits,
    mortgages) as the primary savings vehicle; depreciation destroys
    the incentive to save and *impairs* capital accumulation.
  - **Export promotion** is temporary: beneficial only during the
    depreciation process. Once prices fully adjust the advantage
    disappears; only permanent progressive depreciation could sustain
    it, which is self-defeating and technically unachievable.

### Inflation as covert taxation and political tool

- A third group concedes inflation is harmful but argues it is the
  *lesser evil* — useful for war, famine, reparations. Inflation is
  used when taxes cannot be raised and loans cannot be placed; it
  conceals the cost of government policy.

> "A government always finds itself obliged to resort to inflationary
> measures when it cannot negotiate loans and dare not levy taxes,
> because it has reason to fear that it will forfeit approval of the
> policy it is following if it reveals too soon the financial and
> general economic consequences."

- **WWI example**: no European belligerent dared impose mass taxation
  to fund the war; even England printed money. Inflation concealed
  capital consumption, generated pseudo-profits taxable as income, and
  redirected public anger onto "speculators" and "profiteers."
- **Post-war Germany and Austria**: printing press used to fund railway
  losses, food subsidies, reparations; **Ruhr** passive resistance was
  entirely financed by note issue.
- Verdict: **inflation is an instrument of antidemocratic, unpopular
  policy** — it lets governments persist whose policies would not
  survive honest public accounting.

### The inevitable collapse

- Stages of runaway depreciation: cash holdings minimized → buyers
  purchase goods they don't need → commerce reckons in gold or foreign
  currency → domestic currency extruded from retail → wages must be
  paid in something else.
- **mP > Mp paradox**: during rapid inflation the *total gold value*
  of money in circulation falls even as quantity rises, because demand
  for money collapses faster than supply grows. Some wrongly took this
  as disproof of the quantity theory; it is just confirmation that
  exchange rates move ahead of internal prices.
- **Historical examples**: US 1781, France 1796. Collapse destroyed
  only the fiat issues; metallic money re-emerged and commerce
  continued.
- **Carl Menger** at the Vienna Currency Commission (1892): even
  distinguished bankers failed to account for purchasing-power changes
  in their balance sheets. **Theodor von Taussig** of the
  Bodenkreditanstalt missed the point entirely in his reply.

### Three conclusions on inflationism

1. All inflationist goals can be achieved *better* through direct
   measures (moratoria, export premiums, import prohibitions) that can
   discriminate between classes and industries. Inflation is
   indiscriminate.
2. The effects of no inflationary policy can be foreseen.
3. Continued inflation must lead to collapse.

- Inflationism's popularity is due entirely to its power to deceive
  public opinion about its consequences.

### Restrictionism / Deflationism

- **Restrictionism**: policy aimed at raising the objective exchange
  value of money (active withdrawal or passive non-expansion).
- Unpopular because: it costs the exchequer positively (inflationism
  generates revenue); it raises creditor claims at debtors' expense
  ("lenders of money have been held in odium at all times and among
  all peoples"); when one country deflates while others inflate, exports
  become harder and imports easier; creditors are politically weak;
  even large industrialists who hold bonds are primarily producers.
- Deflationism usually gains political traction only after inflationary
  collapse, when the question is whether to stabilize at the depreciated
  level or restore the old parity.

### Devaluation vs revaluation

- Argument for restoring old parity: notes are promises to pay metallic
  money; suspension was temporary; devaluation is "national bankruptcy."
- Mises' counter: credit money is **not merely a promise to pay**. Once
  it achieves monetary status it is valued *as money*, not as an
  uncertain claim to future redemption. Its value as medium of exchange
  exceeds its value as a claim. Calling stabilization at a lower level
  "national bankruptcy" is "beside the point."
- **Stabilization vs revaluation**: stabilizing at a depreciated level
  affects all private debts contracted in credit money, enriching those
  holding money at the moment of stabilization at debtors' expense
  (including the state/taxpayers).
- **Deflation does not undo inflation**: the gainers and losers under
  each policy are different people. "You cannot make good an old breach
  of the law by committing a new one." Creditors already harmed cannot
  be compensated by raising money's value; those who bear deflation's
  cost are not those who benefited from inflation.
- **Debt recalculation** (restating debts at pre-inflation parity) was
  tried in undeveloped credit systems, but it covers only still-current
  debts; bearer bonds and settled debts are excluded — a serious
  shortcoming. Increasing purchasing power is not a suitable remedy
  for inflation's creditor losses.
- **Credit-policy argument** for returning to old parity (to restore
  creditworthiness, particularly relevant for England): Mises calls
  this "dubious psychology" — restoration doesn't help creditors who
  already sold their claims, and rewards those who acquired claims
  *after* depreciation.

### Invariability as policy ideal

- Both inflation and deflation prove impracticable. The ideal becomes
  money of **invariable exchange value**, but achieving this requires
  continuous intervention, and:
  - There is no reliable quantitative knowledge of how any measure
    will affect money's value.
  - It is impossible to determine precisely whether variations have
    occurred or from what cause.
  - "Both their goal and the road to it are obscured by a darkness
    that human knowledge will never be able to penetrate."

> "Once the principle is so much as admitted that the state may and
> should influence the value of money, even if it were only to
> guarantee the stability of its value, the danger of mistakes and
> excesses immediately arises again."

- Practical conclusion: the state should **refrain from influencing
  money's value at all**. A metallic-money system is independent of
  deliberate human intervention — this is the "modern monetary ideal."
  Fluctuations from production and industrial demand changes are
  modest; even if they were larger, metallic money would still be
  preferable to state-managed money, which would fluctuate more.

### Limits of monetary policy

- The state is one party among many; it cannot dictate exchange ratios.
  Interventions must operate through subjective valuations; effects
  cannot be foreseen or even measured after the fact. Even metallic
  currency does not eliminate the channel of **fiduciary media**, which
  remains open for Part III.

### Excursus: "inflation" and "deflation" as terms

- Mises deliberately avoids the terms in this book due to dangerous
  ambiguity.
  - Strict theoretical definition: *inflation* = increase in money
    quantity not offset by increased demand → fall in exchange value;
    *deflation* = the reverse. Under this definition, one or the other
    is almost always occurring — which makes the terms theoretically
    unhelpful.
  - Political usage: reserved for *large*, visible changes — a question
    of degree, not principle.
- The two usages are irreconcilable. **Pigou** (*The Economics of
  Welfare*, 1921) wanted to abandon "inflation" as a term entirely.

> "It would be ridiculous pedantry to attempt to provide an
> economist's contribution to the controversy as to whether in this or
> the other country inflation has occurred since 1914 by saying:
> 'Excuse me, there has probably been inflation throughout the whole
> world since 1896, although on a small scale.'"

## Chapter 14: The Monetary Policy of Etatism

### Etatism defined

- **Etatism**: doctrine of state omnipotence — regulation of all affairs
  by authoritative command and prohibition. The etatist ideal is **state
  socialism** (sometimes called Christian socialism). Etatism leaves
  private title in place but converts every enterprise into a de facto
  government office — prices decreed, production quantities regulated,
  no speculation, no "excessive" profit, no loss, no innovation without
  sanction.
- Etatists cannot conceive of a market order. Every divergence from
  command is a "transient anomaly" and culpable transgression.
  Everything is **Macht** (power/force); social life is denied to be
  governed by laws comparable to those of nature.
- **Adolf Wagner** and **Lexis** held proto-etatist views on exchange
  rates; **Knapp** was the first to apply etatist principles explicitly
  to monetary theory. Etatism peaked in the WWI "war economy" — the
  war was its inevitable consequence. It remains the ruling doctrine
  on the European continent.

### National prestige and the exchange rate

- Etatist view: money is the state's creature; its esteem = the
  economic expression of state prestige. "The monetary standard of the
  victors would ultimately be the best money."
- Refuted by two cases:
  1. **American Continental Dollar** during the Revolution: as
     Washington's victories mounted, the rate *fell*; at final victory
     it was worthless.
  2. **French Revolutionary currency**: metal premium rose despite
     military victories until 1796, when value hit zero.
- Neither victory nor national wealth determines currency value.
  When the German mark hit 10 centimes in Zurich, bankers said
  "Germany is poorer than before, but surely not one-twelfth as poor —
  the mark must rise." It did not. When the Polish mark hit 5 centimes,
  "Poland is rich — wood, coal, oil — the rate should be much higher."
  Irrelevant.
- Currency value depends on the **ratio of quantity of money to demand
  for it**, not wealth or military power.

### Price regulation by decree

- Oldest etatist tool: official maximum prices. Premise: high prices are
  caused by "bulls" and "profiteers," not by money supply increases.
  **Austria in WWI** began criminal-law price controls the same day it
  started the printing press for war finance.
- Logical result (*ceteris paribus*): new money creates new purchasing
  power; new buyers compete with existing; if prices are fixed, supply
  cannot clear demand; allocation shifts from price to arbitrary
  factors (arrival order, personal relationships). Sellers prefer
  barter. Once stock sells at fixed prices, storerooms are not refilled
  — producing is not compulsory. **"The market ceases to function."**

> "The level of money prices cannot be fixed without overthrowing the
> system of social division of labor."

- Price regulation is **all-or-nothing**. Partial controls cause
  disturbances the market absorbs; universal enforcement makes private
  property incompatible with the system. The logical endpoint: forced
  production → universal labor obligation → rationing → full state
  direction → de facto abolition of private property.
- WWI confirmed the sequence — rationing first, then compulsory labor,
  then state subordination of production. Private property existed in
  name only. Price controls failed not from insufficient enforcement
  zeal (violators were imprisoned and executed for millennia) but
  because an economy based on division of labor and private property
  **requires free price determination**. The productive apparatus
  survived only because enforcement was incomplete.

### Balance-of-payments theory

- Etatist/popular view: a country with a "debit balance of payments"
  cannot stabilize; depreciation has an organic cause requiring
  structural remedies.
- Mises' refutation via quantity theory and Gresham: with metallic
  money, outflows are self-correcting (efflux → domestic tightening →
  prices fall → imports restricted, exports encouraged → reflow).
  Metals distribute by intensity of monetary demand; no intervention
  needed.
- Balance of payments is mechanical: at any moment it determines
  foreign-exchange supply and demand, but what determines the balance?
  **Prices.** Foreign exchange rates = purchasing-power parity;
  deviations create arbitrage that eliminates them.
- "Necessities must be imported whatever the price" variant: demand
  intensity fully expresses necessity. More imports require more
  exports (goods or securities); without money expansion, import prices
  rise and domestic prices of dispensable goods fall — no general price
  rise. Only continued note circulation allows the same import volumes
  at rising exchange rates.
- **Conclusion**: it is inflation alone that forces up the exchange
  rate — not poverty, indebtedness, or unfavorable production.
- Import restrictions (banning "luxury" imports) merely redirect
  domestic purchasing power to substitutes; supply of foreign exchange
  falls as much as demand; net effect is zero. To genuinely reduce
  demand for foreign exchange, domestic purchasing power must be
  *destroyed* (taxation + non-expenditure) — i.e., deflation.

### Suppression of speculation

- The doctrine that speculators cause currency depreciation is "an
  indispensable instrument of the lowest form of demagogy" and "the
  resource of governments in search of a scapegoat."
- Speculation does NOT determine prices — it accepts prices determined
  in the market and tries to estimate future ones. It *reduces*
  fluctuations. Bulls and bears are always equal in strength; a bear
  maneuver against market sentiment would be immediately absorbed by
  bulls. Speculators lose most when they are wrong — they do not err
  from malice.
- Real non-profit currency manipulation proceeds from **governments
  themselves** trying to maintain rates *above* equilibrium (artificial
  bull maneuvers), which also fail long-run.
- **German Reichsbank, spring 1923**: attempted to support the mark by
  selling foreign bills — failed because only a small fraction of
  expanding note circulation was recovered.
- The only ways to influence currency value: stop the fall (cease
  expanding notes) or raise the value (reduce them). All etatist
  anti-speculation measures (bans on note import/export, state forex
  monopolies, criminal penalties for quoting rates) proved useless
  but remained for political scapegoating.
- **Depreciating-currency speculation** has two phases: experts begin,
  then **camp followers** (outsiders) push rates further than
  professionals alone would; reaction comes when exchange rates have
  run so far ahead of commodity prices that arbitrage reverses the
  flow. Only after commodity prices catch up does the next wave begin.

### The "patriotic duty" to hold depreciating money

- Statist doctrine frames holding foreign exchange or real assets as
  unpatriotic — citizens should absorb inflation losses to spare the
  state.
- Mises rejects this: national capital = sum of individual capital;
  when individuals protect their property from inflation, they also
  preserve national wealth. Surrender merely transfers it to
  inflation profiteers. Germans who held marks patriotically were
  impoverished; those who converted to real assets preserved both
  personal and national productive capacity.
- **Cassel** (*Money and Foreign Exchange After 1914*, 1922): "A
  perfectly clear understanding of the monetary problem brought about
  by the world war can never be attained until officialdom's
  interpretation of affairs has been disproved point by point."
- Absurdist footnote: a Hungarian Soviet leader told Mises in 1919
  that Soviet Hungarian paper money "ought to have the highest exchange
  rate next to Russian money, for through the socialization of private
  property, Hungary has become the richest state in the world" —
  showing how far ideology can distort monetary reasoning.

- **Benjamin Franklin**, *A Modest Inquiry into the Nature and
  Necessity of a Paper Currency* (Philadelphia, 1729) — anonymous
  early inflationist pamphlet; Franklin himself printed New Jersey

- **Yves Guyot**, *Les problèmes de la déflation* (Paris, 1923) — on
  naive inflationary proposals by Henry Ford, Thomas Edison, Senator

- **Hertzka**, *Währung und Handel* (Vienna, 1876).

- **Pigou**, *The Economics of Welfare* (London, 1921).

## Transition into Part Three

- Part Two has established: money originates from commodity use (the
  regression theorem); its objective exchange value is shaped by the
  intersection of monetary and commodity influences; variations cannot
  be measured by index numbers; any attempt by the state to manage
  value ends in failure or worse. The state has no standing in the
  market beyond that of any other participant.
- Part Three turns to the channel through which the state still
