## Part 1: Getting Started with Commodities

### Chapter 1: The Basics of Commodities

**Defining Commodities**

- Commodities = the raw materials humans use to create a livable world: energy, metals, and agricultural products.
- Three asset classes of commodities: (1) Energy, (2) Metals, (3) Agricultural products.
- These are the essential building blocks of the global economy.

**Criteria for inclusion in this book (must meet all three):**
1. **Tradability** — must have a viable investment vehicle: futures contract, company stock, or ETF
2. **Deliverability** — must be physically deliverable (excludes currencies, interest rates, financial futures)
3. **Liquidity** — must have active markets with buyers and sellers

**Historical context sidebar:**
- Humans have fought wars over commodities throughout history.
- Pizarro's conquistadors wiped out the Inca civilization to get gold (1524).
- Columbus's expedition was motivated partly by desire to find trade routes for spices and commodities.
- The South African Boer Wars (late 19th century) were fought over South African precious metals.
- The Persian Gulf War (1991) was essentially about stabilizing global oil markets after Iraq invaded oil-rich Kuwait.

**Investment Vehicles Overview**

**Futures Markets:**
- Individuals, institutions, and governments transact for price-hedging and speculating purposes.
- Example: Airlines buy jet fuel futures to hedge against volatile fuel prices.
- Arbitrage: taking advantage of price discrepancies between different futures markets.
- Exchanges: Chicago Mercantile Exchange (CME) and Intercontinental Exchange (ICE).
- Methods for futures access:
  - Futures commission merchant (FCM) — like a brokerage but for futures
  - Commodity trading advisor (CTA) — trades futures contracts on your behalf
  - Commodity pool operator (CPO) — manages pooled funds across clients
  - Commodity indexes — basket of most liquid commodities (Goldman Sachs, Reuters/Jefferies CRB)
- Regulatory bodies: SEC and Commodity Futures Trading Commission (CFTC).
- Warning: Futures require mastery of leverage, margin, expiration, contracts — not for everyone.

**Equity Markets:**
- Invest in companies that produce, transform, and distribute natural resources.
- Drawback: Must also consider management, debt, profit margins, taxes — not just commodity price.
- Types of commodity companies:
  - **Diversified mining companies** (Anglo-American PLC [AAUK], BHP Billiton [BHP])
  - **Electric utilities** — large dividends, essential service
  - **Integrated energy companies** (ExxonMobil [XOM], Chevron [CVX])
  - Coal-mining companies, oil refiners, platinum miners, specialty agricultural companies

**Master Limited Partnerships (MLPs):**
- Invest in energy infrastructure: oil pipelines, natural gas storage facilities.
- Publicly traded partnership — trades like a corporation but offers tax advantages of a partnership.
- Required to transfer all cash flow back to shareholders.
- Detailed in Chapter 14.

**Managed Funds:**
- ETFs: track everything from crude oil and gold to commodity indexes; trade like stocks.
- Mutual funds: professionally managed funds that invest in commodities.

**Physical Commodity Purchases:**
- Most suitable for precious metals: gold, silver, platinum.
- Can buy coins, bullion, or jewelry.
- Not practical for crude oil, uranium, livestock.

**Commodities Covered in the Book**

*Energy:*
- Crude oil: 40% of global energy consumption; 90 million barrels/day traded (2022)
- Natural gas: ~25% of US energy consumption
- Coal: 20%+ of world energy; 50% of US electricity (historical)
- Uranium/nuclear power: ~20% of US electricity
- Electricity: traded via PJM futures contract
- Solar power: fastest-growing renewable
- Wind power: increasing investor attention
- Ethanol: produced from corn or sugar, fuel additive

*Metals:*
- Gold: store of value, inflation hedge
- Silver: precious metal + industrial uses (electrical wiring)
- Platinum: jewelry, catalytic converters; rarest metal
- Steel: most widely used metal globally
- Aluminum: second most widely used metal; lightweight, durable
- Copper: third most widely used; great conductor
- Palladium: catalytic converters (almost half of production)
- Nickel: stainless steel alloy
- Zinc: galvanization, corrosion resistance

*Agricultural Products:*
- Coffee: second most widely traded physical commodity after crude oil
- Cocoa: global chocolate supply
- Sugar #11 (world) and Sugar #14 (US)
- FCOJ-A (Florida/Brazil) and FCOJ-B (world)
- Corn: multiple uses including fuel ethanol
- Wheat: one of first agricultural products grown by humans
- Soybeans, soybean oil, soybean meal
- Live cattle, feeder cattle
- Lean hogs
- Frozen pork bellies (essentially bacon)

### Chapter 2: The Pros and Cons of Commodities

**Why the 21st Century Is the Century of Commodities**

- Reuters/Jefferies CRB Index nearly doubled between 2001 and 2006.
- Oil, gold, copper, silver hit all-time highs (not inflation-adjusted) during early 2000s.
- 100% increase in commodities between pandemic lows and 2023 writing.
- Author believes we're in a long-term cyclical bull market in commodities.
- Caveat: no investment goes up in a straight line; pullbacks always occur.

**Global Population Explosion:**
- UN estimates world will add ~1 billion people per decade of 21st century.
- Global population will reach ~9 billion by 2050 (7.75 billion as of 2020).
- Rate of adding 1 billion humans: 130 years in 19th century → ~13 years in 21st century.
- More humans = more demand for raw materials (food, housing, heating).

**Urbanization:**
- Early 20th century: <15% of world population in cities.
- By 2005: 50% in cities; expected 60% by 2030.
- Megacities (5 million+ people) are proliferating.
- Urban populations consume far more natural resources than rural populations.
- Industrial metals (copper, steel, aluminum) needed for apartment buildings, schools, hospitals, cars.
- Largest urbanization occurring in Asia; major demand driver for raw materials.

**Industrialization — The BRIC Countries:**
- New wave of industrialization in Brazil, Russia, India, China (BRIC countries).
- These countries are securing supplies of oil, natural gas, copper, aluminum.

*China specifically:*
- GDP grew 9% annually 2000–2006.
- In 2004, China consumed half the world's cement, one-third of steel, one-quarter of copper, one-fifth of aluminum.
- In 2003, China became second-largest consumer of crude oil (behind US).
- In 2010, China surpassed Japan as world's second-largest economy.
- As of 2022, China consumes >12.5 million barrels of oil per day.

*Brazil specifically:*
- Largest country in South America by GDP, landmass, population, and natural resources.
- World leader or dominant position in: coffee, copper, corn, offshore crude oil, eucalyptus, gold, iron ore, livestock, silver, soybeans, sugar, sugarcane.
- Amazon river basin = world's largest river basin drainage system.

**What Makes Commodities Unique**

**Inelasticity:**
- Inelastic goods = demand doesn't change much when price changes (because they're essential).
- Example: If ice cream price rises 25%, you stop buying it. If gas rises 75% (as in 2022), you still fill up.
- Most commodities are inelastic because they're essential to modern life.
- Absolute necessities: heating in winter, food, shelter (all require raw materials).

**Safe Haven:**
- During turmoil, gold and silver act as reliable stores of value.
- After 9/11, gold prices jumped as investors sought safety.
- Recommendation: keep part of portfolio in gold/precious metals as insurance.

**Inflation Hedge:**
- Commodities are one of the only asset classes that benefit from inflation.
- There's a positive correlation between gold and the inflation rate.
- Post-COVID supply chain disruptions and Russia-Ukraine War caused worst inflation since 1970s.
- Investors who are long on commodities can hedge against AND benefit from inflation.

**Long Lead Times:**
- Bringing new oil online can take up to a decade: discover → drill → develop infrastructure → refine → transport.
- Investors must think long term with commodities.
- High-tech or e-commerce analogy: commodities require radically different time horizons.

**Seasonal Patterns:**
- "Sell in May and go away" doesn't apply to commodities.
- Some commodities perform well in summer (e.g., gasoline during heavy driving season).
- Commodities move in different cycles than stocks.

**Business Cycle Dynamics:**
- Commodities are cyclical — performance varies across economic expansions and recessions.
- General rule: commodities do well during late expansions and early recessions.
- As economy slows, key interest rates decrease, which tends to help commodities.
- Stocks and bonds perform worse during recessions.
- Current commodity bull market has at times acted independently of the traditional business cycle.
- Caveat: cycles aren't an exact science; don't use as sole foundation of strategy.

**Pitfalls of Leverage**

- Leverage = magnifying returns through borrowed capital.
- Double-edged sword: profits AND losses are magnified.
- Stock margin requires at least 50% capital in account.
- Commodity futures margin is much lower — often 4% or less.
- Example: Soybeans at Chicago Board of Trade = 4% margin. $400 controls $10,000 in soybeans.
- Futures account balances are calculated at END OF EACH TRADING SESSION (not when position closes).
- Margin call = must deposit additional capital immediately if market moves against you.
- Recommendation: Only use margin if you have capital reserves to cover potential margin calls.

**Real Risks of Commodities**

**Sovereign Government Risk:**
- Post-2008 financial crisis: banks bailed out consumers; governments bailed out banks.
- When governments fail, few institutions can bail them out.
- European countries (Greece, Portugal, Spain, Ireland, Italy, France) faced severe budget cuts, riots in 2010.
- Germany was the main exception as a financially stable European state.
- Lesson: carefully examine countries you're investing in.

**Geopolitical Risk:**
- Natural resources are scattered across the globe; jurisdiction lies with sovereign governments.
- Russia-Ukraine War (began 2022) disrupted oil, gas, AND agricultural products.
- Sanctions prevented Russia from exporting oil → crude oil prices up 30% since war started.
- Russia cut pipeline natural gas exports to Europe → natural gas prices skyrocketed.
- Both Ukraine and Russia are major wheat producers/exporters → wheat prices spiked dramatically.
- Result: contributed significantly to global inflation experienced since pandemic lockdown end.
- How to protect: invest in companies with scale, breadth, experience in international markets (e.g., ExxonMobil).

**Speculative Risk:**
- Commodity futures markets: ~75% commercial users, ~25% speculators (general rule).
- Speculators provide needed liquidity but increase volatility.
- Can drive prices beyond fundamentals ("irrational exuberance" — Alan Greenspan).
- Resource: CFTC's Commitment of Traders report (www.cftc.gov) — details market participants.
- When too much speculative money comes in, it may be time to exit.

**Corporate Governance Risk:**
- Plain fraud: CFTC and regulatory bodies work to protect investors but can't catch everything.
- Example: Refco hid debts and losses in offshore accounts.
- Mitigation: be extremely vigilant; thoroughly research any firm before investing.

**Methods for Managing Risk**

**Due Diligence:**
- Don't buy on hype; research first.
- For commodity companies: check assets/liabilities, management effectiveness, revenue sources, regulatory history, company structure, geographic exposure, business cycle performance.
- Find info in: company annual report (Form 10-K) and quarterly reports (Form 8-K).

**For Managed Funds — ask:**
- Manager's track record and investing style?
- Any disciplinary actions?
- Client references available?
- Registered with appropriate regulatory bodies?
- What fees? (Watch for hidden fees)
- Assets under management?
- After-tax returns?
- Minimum time commitments?
- Early withdrawal penalties?
- Minimum investment requirements?

**For Futures Market — consider:**
- Which exchange?
- Is there an accompanying options contract?
- How liquid is the market?
- Who are main participants?
- Expiration date?
- Open interest?
- Margin requirements?

**Commodity Fundamentals — always ask:**
- Which country has largest reserves?
- Is that country politically stable?
- How much is produced (daily, monthly, quarterly, annually)?
- Who are the largest consumers?
- Primary uses?
- Are there alternatives?
- Do seasonal factors apply?
- Correlation with comparable commodities?
- Historical production and consumption cycles?

**Diversification:**
- Diversify across asset classes (bonds, stocks, commodities) AND within commodities (energy vs. metals).
- Commodities tend to behave differently from stocks and bonds.
- When stocks aren't doing well, commodities may be performing — the key diversification benefit.

**Crude Oil Quality:**

### Chapter 4: Investing in Natural Gas

**Overview:**
- Natural gas = the "queen of commodities" (if crude oil is king).
- ~25% of US energy consumption from natural gas.
- Second only to petroleum for generating energy in the US.
- Nonrenewable fossil fuel found in large deposits (often near crude oil deposits).
- Crude oil = liquid fossil fuel; coal = solid; natural gas = gaseous.
- Common confusion: "natural gas" ≠ gasoline. Gasoline comes from crude oil; natural gas is separate.

**Natural Gas Measurement:**
- Measured in cubic feet (cf). Abbreviation: cf (lowercase).
  - 100 cf = 1 Ccf
  - 1,000 cf = 1 Mcf
  - 1 million cf = 1 Mmcf
  - 1 billion cf = 1 Bcf
  - 1 trillion cf = 1 Tcf
- Also measured by energy content in British thermal units (Btu).
- 1 cf = 1,027 Btu.
- Therms = another term for Btu; may appear on gas bills.
- For investment purposes: typically quantified in cubic feet.

**Industrial Uses (~40% of total consumption):**
- Feedstock for fertilizers
- Food processing
- Glass melting
- Industrial boiler fueling
- Metal smelting
- Waste incineration
- Chemical composition: primarily methane; also butane, ethane, propane.
- PPI (Producer Price Index) = good gauge of industrial sector health/demand for natural gas.

**Residential Uses (~25% of total consumption):**
- ~70% of US households have natural gas ovens.
- >55% of US homes use natural gas for heating.
- Peak demand in winter in Northern Hemisphere → seasonal trading opportunity.
- Natural gas is one of cheapest energy forms per Btu generated.

**Commercial Uses (~15% of total consumption):**
- Schools, hospitals, restaurants, movie theaters, malls, office buildings.
- Demand rises during periods of economic growth.
- Bullish signal for natural gas: strong economic activity.

**Electricity Generation (~25% of natural gas usage):**
- Natural gas produces ~10% of US electricity (figure increasing).
- Long-term trend: more natural gas needed for electricity generation.

**Transportation Uses (~5% of total consumption):**
- Natural gas vehicles (NGVs): ~3 million worldwide (2006 era); grew to >22 million by 2022.
- Compressed natural gas (CNG) for vehicles.
- Dominant NGV countries: Pakistan, Argentina, Brazil, Iran.
- Important: if natural gas captured a slice of transportation market (2/3 of crude oil use), prices could jump dramatically.

**Liquefied Natural Gas (LNG):**
- Natural gas cooled to –260°F to transform to liquid state.
- LNG takes up ~600 times LESS space than gaseous form — allows long-distance transport.
- Transported in specially designed tankers.
- Regasification process before delivery to consumers.
- US pipeline system: 300 million miles connecting Gulf of Mexico production to East Coast consumers.
- 2005: US received only ~1% of natural gas (170 Bcf) via LNG.
- 2010: jumped to 452 Bcf.
- Top LNG exporters to US: Trinidad and Tobago, Egypt, Algeria, Nigeria, Norway, Qatar.

**Qatar Spotlight:**
- Surrounded by Saudi Arabia, UAE, and Iran — strategic location.
- Third-largest natural gas reserves globally (behind Russia and Iran only).
- Population of only ~1 million.
- GDP per capita ~3x higher than US per capita.
- No corporate or personal income taxes.
- Major international LNG exporter.

**Investing in Natural Gas**

*Futures:*
- CME (Chicago Mercantile Exchange) offers natural gas futures and options.
- Ticker symbol: NG.
- Trades in increments of 10,000 metric million Btu (Mmbtu).
- Can trade for up to 72 months after current month.
- Second-most popular energy contract on CME (behind crude oil).
- Warning: Natural gas is notoriously volatile even by commodities standards.

### Chapter 5: Investing in Renewable and Alternative Energy

**Overview of Renewable Energy:**
- ~7% of total energy use ten years ago → ~11% as of writing (2023).
- Renewable energy = 30% of global electricity generation (massive jump from single digits in 2000s).
- Practical definition: sources that are always present, renewable, and don't emit greenhouse gases.
- Big obstacle to widespread adoption: COST. Solar and wind require massive capital expenditure and government subsidies.
- Fossil fuels still account for 84% of world energy consumption in 2022.
- Focus in this chapter: solar, wind, biomass (have tradable, liquid instruments).

**Solar Energy:**
- Currently only ~1% of total renewable energy sources but fastest-growing.
- Governments worldwide announcing massive infrastructure spending on solar.
- India and China: $30 billion in projects announced over 20 years ahead.
- Saudi Arabia (world's largest oil producer!) and UAE both dedicated large solar budgets.
- Morocco's Noor project: ~$10 billion cost, 2,000 MW capacity, ~40% of Morocco's electricity; will connect to EU and UK grids.
- Morocco generating 3,934 MW as of 2022; target 10,000 MW by 2030.

*Two methods of transforming solar energy:*
1. **Solar photovoltaic** — captures sun's energy and transforms to electricity
2. **Solar thermal** — transforms sun's energy into heat (flat-panel collectors on homes/buildings)

*Solar companies:*
- **First Solar, Inc. (Nasdaq: FSLR)** — one of first solar companies to go public; photovoltaic solar panel manufacture; net profit margins >22% (2010); two segments: components and systems installation.
- **Suntech Power Holdings (NYSE: STP)** — world leader in solar photovoltaic; engineering, construction, maintenance services; operations in Germany, US, Australia, South Africa, Japan, South Korea.

**Wind Energy:**
- Dependent on unpredictable wind — remains a small but growing share.
- Few publicly traded pure wind companies.
- General Electric (NYSE: GE) = global leader in wind turbine manufacture and sales.
- Resource: American Clean Power Association (https://cleanpower.org).

**Biomass:**
- Largest component of renewable energy: 53% of total production estimates.
- Produced by processing common wastes through biochemical conversion.
- Feedstock inputs: corn, ethanol, eucalyptus, forestry residues, industrial residues, municipal wastes, palm oil, sugarcane, vegetable oil.
- Versatile: agriculturally rich countries (Brazil with sugarcane waste) or industrial countries (Russia with industrial residues) can both use it.
- Currently no direct public plays — private companies/municipalities run most biomass plants.
- **CPFL Energia (CPFL)** — world leader in biomass generation; listed on BOVESPA and NYSE (ticker: CPL via ADR); derives significant revenues from transforming sugarcane waste into electricity.

**Ethanol sidebar:**
- Alcohol fuel from corn, sugar, wheat, other ag products.
- Brazil = world's largest ethanol fuel producer; ethanol is primary automotive fuel.
- **Pacific Ethanol (Nasdaq: PEIX)** — recommended company for ethanol exposure.

**Coal:**

*Overview:*
- Before 20th century, coal was king of commodities.
- Dominant during Industrial Revolution.
- Historical turning point: 1912 — Winston Churchill (First Lord of the Admiralty) ordered conversion of all British Navy ships from coal to oil → rise of oil era, decline of coal's dominance.
- Still important: coal = 23% of US total fossil fuel consumption (2009); 22% of US electricity generation (2021).

*Coal types (4 categories, ranked by quality):*
1. **Lignite** (brown coal) — least carbon, most sulfur/ash, used for electricity generation.
2. **Sub-bituminous** — more carbon than lignite, lower sulfur/ash, heats water in steam turbines.
3. **Bituminous** — most common US coal, high value, used for electricity + high-quality steel.
4. **Anthracite** — most valuable; highest carbon, least sulfur/ash, most energy per unit; used for residential/commercial heating.

*Offshore Drilling Vessels (types):*

*Properties that make gold valuable:*

- **Quasi-indestructibility**: resists corrosion; oxygen and heat have almost no effect; only cyanide dissolves gold; retains luster for thousands of years

  | 18K | 75% |
  | 14K | 58.3% |
  | 10K | 41.67% |
  | 9K | 37.5% |
- Fineness: purity expressed as a whole number (e.g., 0.9167 = 91.67%).
- White and red gold created by alloying with other metals (not pure gold).

### Chapter 13: Futures and Options

**Overview:**
- Commodities ≠ futures/options; futures/options are the VEHICLE to invest in commodities.
- Futures and options = derivatives (derive value from underlying financial instrument: stock, bond, commodity).

**Structure of Futures Markets:**
- Regulated (via exchanges) + Over-the-counter (OTC, unregulated).
- Only ~20% of futures market activity takes place on exchanges; 80% is OTC.
- OTC = forward contracts between commercial users; generally not suitable for individual investors.
- Futures market = opposite of cash/spot market (transactions on the spot).
- A futures contract: two parties agree to exchange an underlying security at a mutually agreed-upon price at a specific future time.
- Only ~2% of futures contracts result in actual physical delivery.

**Two Types of Futures Traders:**
1. **Commercial producers and consumers (hedgers)** — use markets to stabilize costs or revenues.
2. **Individual traders, investment banks, financial institutions (speculators)** — use markets to generate trading profits.

**Hedging Example:**
- Southwest Airlines (NYSE: LUV): one of most active hedgers; may fix up to 80% of a year's jet fuel consumption at a specific price via OTC futures agreements.
- Benefit: eliminates price volatility; guarantees fixed cost/cash flow for both parties.
- Risk: if prices fall, airline would've been better off buying spot; if prices rise, producer bears that cost.

**Speculators:**
- Provide much-needed liquidity to futures markets.
- "Everyone is a speculator" — buying stocks = speculating on price movement.
- Liquidity benefits: more participants = better prices, ability to find buyers/sellers, reduced (but not eliminated) volatility.

**Key Futures Contract Elements:**
1. **Underlying asset** — the actual commodity or financial instrument being traded (crude oil, platinum, soybeans, etc.)
2. **Underlying quantity (contract size/trading unit)** — predetermined fixed amount (e.g., CME crude oil = 1,000 barrels; pork bellies = 40,000 lbs; ethanol on CBOT = ~29,000 gallons per rail car)
3. **Product grade** — standard quality specification (e.g., CME gasoline = New York Harbor Unleaded Gasoline)
4. **Price quote** — mostly US dollars; some contracts in GBP, JPY; currency risk applies
5. **Price limits** — minimum tick size and maximum (daily limit); when exceeded, trading halts
   - Example: CME ethanol — minimum tick = $29/contract; maximum tick = $8,700/contract
6. **Trading months** — not all contracts trade all 12 months (e.g., frozen pork bellies = Feb, Mar, May, Jul, Aug only)
7. **Delivery location** — designated physical exchange point (e.g., CME WTI = Cushing, Oklahoma)
8. **Last trading day** — absolute latest date to trade that contract
9. **Trading hours** — vary by exchange and contract; electronic trading expanding hours

*Monthly Abbreviation Codes:*
| Month | Code | Month | Code |
|-------|------|-------|------|
| January | F | July | N |
| February | G | August | Q |
| March | H | September | U |
| April | J | October | V |
| May | K | November | X |
| June | M | December | Z |

**Terminology:**
- **Holder (buyer)** = "going long" — expects prices to rise.
- **Writer/underwriter (seller)** = "going short" — expects prices to fall.
- **Front month** = upcoming delivery month (e.g., June is front month in May session).

**Trading Futures on Margin:**
- Futures margin = minimum capital that must be in account as collateral (different from stock margin).
- **Initial margin** = minimum capital to start trading.
- **Maintenance margin** = subsequent capital needed to maintain minimum requirements.
- Capital gains/losses calculated daily at end of trading session (vs. stocks: when position closes).
- If daily loss exceeds account balance, receive margin call → must replenish immediately.
- Example: CME soybeans initial margin = $1,100 controls ~$359,200 worth of soybeans (5,000 bushels × $71.84) = less than 4% margin requirement.
- Warning: margin is double-edged; losses can far exceed initial investment.

**Contango and Backwardation:**

*Contango:* Front month trades HIGHER than current month (or spot market); future delivery months higher than near delivery months.
- Bullish indicator: market expects prices to rise.
- Example: CME crude oil in contango (Dec 2010 $82.68 → Jan 2011 $83.40 → ... → May 2011 $85.00).

*Backwardation:* Contracts for future months DECREASING in value relative to current month.
- Spot price > front month > future delivery months.
- Bearish sign: traders expect long-term price decrease.
- Example: CME copper (Oct 2022 $3.49 → Nov $3.45 → Dec $3.44).

**Options**

*Key difference from futures:*
- Futures: BOTH buyer AND seller have right AND obligation to fulfill contract.
- Options: Buyer has RIGHT (but NOT obligation) to exercise; seller has OBLIGATION to fulfill IF holder exercises.

*Premium:* Price paid for the option. If not exercised, only the premium is lost.

*Terminology:*
- **Premium** — price paid for the option
- **Expiration date** — date at which option expires and becomes invalid
- **Strike price** — predetermined price at which underlying asset is bought/sold
- **At-the-money** — strike price equals market price
- **In-the-money** — call option: market price above strike; put option: market price below strike
- **Out-of-the-money** — call: market price below strike; put: market price above strike
- **Open interest** — total number of open options/futures contracts; measure of market interest

*Options in action (car dealer example):*
- You pay $5,000 (premium) for the right to buy a $100,000 car in 2 weeks.
- Scenario 1: Car becomes limited edition and doubles to $200,000 → you exercise option at $100,000 → profit = $95,000 ($200K - $100K - $5K premium).
- Scenario 2: Car drops to $80,000 due to Bluetooth defect → you let option expire → only loss is $5,000 premium.

**Call Options:**
- Gives right (not obligation) to PURCHASE a security at specific price in the future.
- Buying a call = BULLISH (expect prices to rise).
- Selling a call = BEARISH (expect prices to fall; if they fall, you keep the premium).

**Put Options:**
- Gives right (not obligation) to SELL a security at specific price in the future.
- Buying a put = BEARISH (expect prices to fall; one way of shorting).
- Selling a put = BULLISH (if prices rise, holder won't exercise; you keep premium).

*Summary of option positions and market sentiment:*
- Buy call = Bullish
- Sell call = Bearish
- Buy put = Bearish
- Sell put = Bullish

**American vs. European Options:**
- **American options** — can exercise at ANY TIME during the option's life, up to expiration. Most US options are American. More flexibility.
- **European options** — can ONLY be exercised at expiration. More rigid. May have smaller premium but author recommends American options.

### Chapter 14: Choosing a Professional and Trading Accounts

**Commodity Mutual Funds**

*Overview:*
- Professionally managed funds investing in commodities.
- Follow long-only strategies (buy-and-hold principle).
- Don't use sophisticated strategies (arbitrage, short-selling) — that's hedge funds.
- Invest in commodity-linked derivative instruments (futures, forwards, options on futures).
- Some also invest in commodity companies (energy companies, mining companies).

*Questions before investing in any mutual fund:*
1. What's the investment objective?
2. What securities does the fund invest in?
3. Who manages the fund? (Check FINRA at www.finra.org)
   - Experience, track record, disciplinary actions, registrations
4. What strategy does the fund use?
5. Profile of typical investor (risk tolerance match)?
6. Main risks?
7. Track record (3, 5, and 10-year periods)?
8. **After-tax performance** (important — many post pre-tax results that are misleading)?
9. Fees and expenses?
10. Minimum capital commitment?
11. Different classes of shares?
12. Tax implications?

*Key Mutual Fund Terminology:*
- **Expense ratio** — % of total assets for operational expenses; directly lowers returns.
- **Sales load** — commission paid to brokers selling mutual fund shares; paid by investor.
- **Sales charge (deferred sales charge/back-end charge)** — fee paid when SELLING mutual fund shares.
- **No-load fund** — no sales load charged.
- **Net asset value (NAV)** — total assets minus total liabilities; calculated per share at end of day.

**Two Major Commodity Mutual Funds:**

*PIMCO Commodity Real Return Strategy Fund (PCRAX):*
- Largest commodity-oriented fund: >$30 billion in assets under management.
- Actively managed; broadly mirrors DJCI (Dow Jones Commodity Index).
- Invests in commodity-linked futures, forwards, options AND bonds/fixed income as collateral.
- **Class A shares**: minimum $5,000; front load 5.5%; expense ratio 1.24%.
- **Class B shares**: no front load; deferred sales charge 5%; expense ratio 1.99%.

*Oppenheimer Real Asset Fund (QRAAX):*
- Smaller than PIMCO; tracks Goldman Sachs Commodity Index (24 commodities basket).
- Minimum investment: $1,000 (lower than PIMCO).
- Five share classes (A, B, C, N, Y).
- **Class A**: no deferred sales charge; front load 5.75%; expense ratio 1.32%.

**Master Limited Partnerships (MLPs)**

*Structure:*
- Publicly traded partnerships (PTPs) — trade like stocks on NYSE, NASDAQ, AMEX.
- General partner (GP) operates the MLP; limited partners (LPs) = investors/unit holders.
- Units = shares of the MLP.
- Must derive 90%+ of income from qualifying sources (commodity production/distribution/transformation) to get tax benefits.

*Tax Advantage (no double taxation):*
- Regular corporations: taxed at corporate level AND shareholder level.
- MLPs: only taxed at individual level (pass-through entity).
- Real-world impact: Corporation must generate $2.20 of income to distribute $1 of after-tax profit to investor. MLP only needs to generate $1.54 of income for the same $1 after-tax distribution.
- MLPs receive K-1 tax form (similar to corporate 1099); mailed in February — inform your accountant early.

*Cash Distribution Tier System (typical):*
| Distribution Tier | Dollar Distribution | LP Payout | GP Participation |
|-------------------|---------------------|-----------|------------------|
| Tier 1 | $0.50 | 98% | 2% |
| Tier 2 | $1.00 | 85% | 15% |
| Tier 3 | $1.50 | 75% | 25% |
| Tier 4 | $2.00 | 50% | 50% |

- Incentive distribution rights (IDRs) encourage GP to maximize cash flow.
- Example: Tier 2 ($1 distribution) → LP gets $0.85, GP gets $0.15. At Tier 4 ($2 distribution), both get $1 each — but LP actually gets MORE dollars even though smaller percentage.

*MLP Risks:*
- Management risk: LPs have no say in operations; if unhappy, can only withdraw.
- Environmental risk: pipelines and drilling rigs vulnerable to natural disasters.
- Liquidity risk: MLP market still fairly small; may be hard to find a buyer.
- Terrorism risk: sensitive infrastructure (pipelines, storage) may be targets.

*Practical MLP investing:* Simply purchase units on exchange where it trades (NYSE, NASDAQ, AMEX) — as easy as buying stocks.

*Questions to ask before investing in an MLP:*
- Historical payout? Cash flow? GP's IDR? Operational activities? Assets under management?

**Commodity Trading Advisors (CTAs)**

*What they are:*
- Licensed to trade futures contracts on behalf of individual clients.
- Pass Series 3 exam (administered by NASD).
- Licensed by CFTC; registered with NFA.
- May provide advice OR fully manage your account.

*Key Selection Criteria:*
- **Track record** — check autumngold.com, iasg.com; look at longest historical + 1/3/6 month + 1/3/5 year annualized.
- **Disciplinary actions** — check NFA's BASIC database at nfa.futures.org/basicnet; also FINRA (brokercheck.finra.org).
- **Management fee** — industry average 2%; goes toward operational expenses.
- **Performance fee** — industry benchmark ~20%; ONLY paid when CTA hits performance targets.
- **Miscellaneous fees** — watch for express mail, check/wire fees, night desk charges, maintenance fees.
- **Margin requirements** — if margin account, know the details.
- **Minimum investment** — ranges $1,000–$200,000; author recommends no more than 5–10% of investing capital with any single CTA.

*CTA vs. CPO:*
- CTA: manages SEPARATE accounts for each client (no commingling).
- CPO: POOLS all client funds in one account; trades en masse.

*Finding CTAs:* AutumnGold (www.autumngold.com), Barclays (home.barclays), IASG (www.iasg.com).

**Commodity Pools (CPO)**

*Advantages over individual CTA account:*
1. Greater purchasing power and leverage (pooled funds = much larger account).
2. Structured as limited partnerships: can only lose principal (no margin calls reaching into personal assets).

*How pools work:* If pool uses leverage and gets a margin call, individual investors are NOT responsible for the additional amount. Only risk is your principal.

**Account Types**

*Self-directed account (nondiscretionary individual account):*
- You make all trading decisions.
- Best for: traders comfortable with exchange-traded products.
- Ask about: minimum capital requirements, account maintenance fees, commission scale.

*Managed account:*
- Professional (CTA) makes all trading decisions.
- Best for: investors who don't follow markets daily, don't have time, or prefer professional management.
- Requires signing power of attorney document.
- CTA gets full discretion over account.
- Difference from commodity pool: managed account is SEPARATE; commodity pool COMMINGLES funds.

**Types of Orders:**
| Order Type | Description |
|------------|-------------|
| Fill or Kill (FOK) | Must be filled immediately at specific price; if no match in 3 attempts, canceled |
| Limit (LMT) | Filled only at specified price or better (buy ≤ market; sell ≥ market) |
| Market (MKT) | Filled at current market price; simplest |
| Market if Touched (MIT) | Trigger at specific price; then executed at market; buy MIT below market; sell MIT above market |
| Market on Close (MOC) | Executed at closing price of trading session |
| Stop (STP) | Executed when trading occurs at/through specified price; buy stop above market; sell stop below market |
| Stop Close Only (SCO) | Stop order only executed at close of trading if closing range hits stop price |
| Stop Limit (STL) | Combination of stop + limit; becomes limit order when stop price is reached |

## Part 5: The Part of Tens

### Chapter 15: Ten Market Indicators to Monitor

**1. Consumer Price Index (CPI)**
- Compiled by Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS).
- Statistically weighted average of basket of goods and services purchased by consumers.
- Closest thing to a cost-of-living index; gauges inflationary trends.
- Rising CPI → Fed may raise federal funds rate.
- **Core CPI**: excludes food and energy; comparing CPI vs. Core CPI shows consumer commodity spending.
- Website: www.bls.gov/cpi

**2. EIA Inventory Reports**
- Energy traders watch every Wednesday at 10:30am Eastern time.
- From Energy Information Administration (EIA), statistical branch of DOE.
- Contents: weekly supply estimates, crude oil supply, disposition rates (consumer consumption), production, refinery utilization, stock changes.
- Direct impact on crude oil and other energy product prices.
- Website: www.eia.gov

**3. Federal Funds Rate**
- Most closely watched by investors overall.
- Short-term rate banks charge each other overnight for Fed Reserve balances.
- Set by Federal Open Market Committee (FOMC).
- Fed DECREASES rate: stimulates sluggish economy.
- Fed INCREASES rate: fights overheating economy/inflation (makes borrowing more expensive).
- Key for commodities because interest rate changes affect demand for and prices of raw materials.

**4. Gross Domestic Product (GDP)**
- Measures all goods and services produced by private consumers, government, business sector, trade.
- Per-capita GDP = purchasing power at individual level.
- Higher GDP growth → more spending on crude oil, natural gas, natural resources.
- Author recommends closely watching: US GDP (largest economy) and Chinese GDP (fastest-growing).

**5. London Gold Fix**
- Gold fixed daily in London by five leading members of financial community.
- Precious metals dealers use as global benchmark for gold spot prices.
- Gold acts as measure of inflation and monetary stability.
- Check futures via CME COMEX gold prices.
- Websites: www.cmegroup.com; www.bullionvault.com/gold-guide/gold-fix

**6. Nonfarm Payrolls**
- Compiled by BLS.
- Includes paid-salary employees at businesses (excludes: government, household, nonprofit, agricultural workers).
- Covers ~80% of total US workforce.
- Released monthly on first Friday of month.
- Shows CHANGE in employment levels from previous month.
- Higher nonfarm payrolls → stronger economy → more consumer spending → bullish for commodities (indirect).
- Website: www.bls.gov/ces

**7. Purchasing Managers Index (PMI)**
- Released by Institute of Supply Management (ISM).
- Composite index; good indicator of total manufacturing activity.
- Manufacturing sector = large consumer of commodities (crude oil, natural gas).
- Strong PMI → manufacturers doing well → likely to spend more on commodities.
- Released at 10am Eastern time, first business day of every month.
- Website: www.ismworld.org

**8. Reuters/Jefferies CRB Index**
- Oldest commodity index; most widely followed commodity benchmark.
- Tracks 19 commodities.
- Best measure of commodity markets as a whole.
- Limitation: tracks only futures-traded commodities (misses steel, for example).

**9. U.S. Dollar**
- Most world commodities (crude oil, gold, copper, coffee) priced in US dollars.
- Any shift in dollar indirectly impacts commodity markets.
- Integrated energy companies: operations abroad in local currencies → USD/local currency shifts directly impact profit accounting.
- Dollar strength/weakness = critical for commodity traders.

**10. WTI Crude Oil**
- West Texas Intermediate (WTI) = most widely followed energy complex benchmark.
- High-grade, low-sulfur, light sweet crude produced in West Texas.
- Traded on NYMEX section of CME.
- Global oil price benchmark; widely quoted in financial press and analyst reports.
- Heavy, sour crudes priced relative to WTI.
- Alternative: North Sea Brent (traded on ICE) — also high-quality benchmark.

### Chapter 16: Ten Investment Vehicles for Commodities

**1. Futures Commission Merchant (FCM)**
- Most direct access to commodity futures markets.
- Registered with NFA; monitored by CFTC.
- Can trade futures contracts, options, and derivatives.
- Orders routed electronically or through open outcry.
- Only for those with solid grasp of futures and options.
- Covered in depth in Chapter 14.

**2. Commodity Trading Advisor (CTA)**
- Authorized by CFTC and NFA to trade on behalf of clients.
- Research track record and investment philosophy before selecting.
- Covered in depth in Chapter 14.

**3. Commodity Pool Operator (CPO)**
- Similar to CTA but pools client accounts under one giant account.
- Two advantages: increased purchasing power; can only lose principal (no margin calls reaching personal assets).
- Usually structured as a company.
- Covered in Chapter 14.

**4. Integrated Commodity Companies**
- Equity market exposure to commodities through companies involved in full supply chain.
- Energy: ExxonMobil (NYSE: XOM), Total (NYSE: TOT).
- Mining/metals: Rio Tinto (NYSE: TRP), BHP Billiton (NYSE: BHP).
- Covered in Chapters 3 and 9.

**5. Specialized Commodity Companies**
- Focus on one commodity or one aspect of supply chain.
- Oil tanker operators: transport crude from A to B (Chapter 6).
- Starbucks (Nasdaq: SBUX): sells and markets coffee (Chapter 10).
- Best for targeted exposure to a specific commodity.

**6. Master Limited Partnerships (MLPs)**
- Hybrid: private partnerships that trade on public exchanges.
- Tax advantage: no corporate-level taxation (pass-through entity).
- Mandate: distribute practically all cash flow to shareholders ($3–$4 per unit is not uncommon).
- Covered in Chapter 14.

**7. Exchange-Traded Funds (ETFs)**
- Privately run funds trading on public exchanges like stocks.
- Track single commodities (gold, silver, crude oil) or commodity indexes.
- Growing rapidly; complete listing in Chapter 12.
- DBC: tracks Deutsche Bank Liquid Commodity Index.

**8. Commodity Mutual Funds**
- Professionally managed; invest directly in commodities.
- Biggest: PIMCO Commodity Real Return Fund, Oppenheimer Real Asset Fund.
- Some mirror commodity benchmarks; others invest in commodity-processing companies.
- Covered in Chapter 14.

**9. Commodity Indexes**
- Like stock indexes: track group of securities for benchmarking and investing.
- Five major indexes: offered by Goldman Sachs/S&P, Reuters/Jefferies, Dow Jones, Jim Rogers, Deutsche Bank.
- Can track via futures markets or ETFs.
- Covered in depth in Chapter 12.

**10. Emerging-Market Funds**
- Commodities scattered across globe; specific countries dominate specific commodities.
- South Africa: largest gold reserves; Saudi Arabia: largest oil reserves; Russia: biggest palladium reserves.
- As commodity demand grows, economies of these emerging markets soar.
- Emerging-market funds = play the commodities boom indirectly.
