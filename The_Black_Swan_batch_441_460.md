## Bibliography and References

### Core Theoretical Foundations

This section documents Taleb's extensive research base spanning probability theory, cognitive psychology, behavioral economics, philosophy of science, and complexity theory.

**Philosophy of Science & Epistemology:**
- **Karl Popper** — *The Logic of Scientific Discovery*, *Conjectures and Refutations* — foundational for understanding falsifiability and the limits of prediction. Popper's critique of historicism underpins Taleb's skepticism toward forecasting and narrative-driven explanations
- **Nassim Taleb** — *Fooled by Randomness* — the precursor work establishing how randomness distorts causal inference and why we mistake luck for skill
- **David Hume** — philosophical skepticism about induction; multiple works on the limits of empirical reasoning
- **Michael Polanyi** — *Personal Knowledge* — tacit knowledge and the gap between what we know and what we can articulate

### Behavioral Economics & Decision-Making

**Overconfidence & Forecasting Errors:**
- **Tversky & Kahneman** — cascade of work on heuristics and biases: anchoring, availability bias, conjunction fallacy, belief in small numbers. Their research on judgment under uncertainty is fundamental to why experts fail
- **Paul Slovic** — *The Perception of Risk* — distinguishes between analytical and affective approaches to risk; shows how feeling dominates reasoning
- **Daniel Kahneman** — prospect theory, mental accounting; how people violate rational choice
- **Stuart Oskamp** — overconfidence in case-study judgments; confidence disconnects from accuracy
- **Philip Tetlock** — expert political judgment; tracking expert forecasts and finding they perform worse than simple models

**Confidence & Calibration:**
- **Russo & Schoemaker** — managing overconfidence in business contexts
- **Paese & Sniezek** — confidence in judgment varies with practice, effort, and information quality
- **Wagenaar & Keren** — calibration studies on blackjack dealers and probability assessments; professionals remain miscalibrated

### Randomness, Probability & Statistics

**Foundational Work:**
- **Lévy distributions & power laws** — **Mandelbrot**, **Pareto** — markets exhibit heavy tails, not Gaussian distributions
- **Benoit Mandelbrot** — fractal geometry applied to markets; *The (Mis)Behavior of Markets*
- **Stephen Stigler** — history of statistics and measurement of uncertainty
- **Ian Stewart** — chaos and nonlinear dynamics
- **Didier Sornette** — critical phenomena, earthquakes, and market crashes follow power-law distributions

**Statistical Inference:**
- **Leonard Savage** — foundations of statistics
- **William Sharpe** — Sharpe Ratio for risk-adjusted returns (Taleb criticizes its limitations with fat tails)
- **Robert Shiller** — market volatility unexplained by fundamentals; *Irrational Exuberance*

### Market & Financial Phenomena

**Crashes & Rare Events:**
- **Robert Posner** — *Catastrophe: Risk and Response*
- **Didier Sornette** — *Why Stock Markets Crash* — complex financial systems and dragon-king events
- **Nassim Taleb** — *Fooled by Randomness*, *Dynamic Hedging*, essays on skewness and tail risk
- **Aaron Brown** — *The Poker Face of Wall Street* — trader's perspective on randomness and risk

**Market Behavior & Irrationality:**
- **George Soros** — *The Alchemy of Finance* — reflexivity and feedback loops in markets
- **Galen Strawson** — narrativity critique; against the assumption that all behavior is narrative-driven
- **Robert Shiller** — *Market Volatility*, *Irrational Exuberance* — psychological factors in bubbles

### Key Theme Integration

The bibliography reflects Taleb's multi-disciplinary attack on the problem of black swans. Popper and Hume establish the epistemological limit: you cannot know what you haven't seen. Tversky, Kahneman, and Slovic show why our minds fail under uncertainty. Mandelbrot, Pareto, and Sornette prove that real-world distributions have heavy tails. Sperber and Strawson explain why narrative feels inevitable but misleads. Evolutionary biology (Tinbergen, Plotkin, Haidt) explains why these cognitive errors persist: they were adaptive in small-scale, stable ancestral environments.

The references are not decoration—they are the scaffolding of the black swan thesis.
