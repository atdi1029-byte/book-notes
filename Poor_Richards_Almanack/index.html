The Way to Wealth

In 1758, after twenty-five years of publishing Poor Richard's Almanack, Benjamin Franklin wrote one final preface. He called it "Father Abraham's Speech," though it would become better known as "The Way to Wealth" — the most reprinted piece of writing he ever produced, more widely circulated than anything else he authored, translated into French, printed as a broadside in Britain, and distributed by clergy to poor parishioners as a free tract.

The setup is a framing device of almost theatrical elegance. Franklin narrates stopping at a vendue — an outdoor auction — where a crowd of merchants and tradespeople are complaining bitterly about heavy taxes. They turn to an old man in the crowd, one Father Abraham, and ask him: how shall we ever pay such burdens, and what do you think of the times? Father Abraham stands and delivers a long speech — built entirely from twenty-five years of Poor Richard's proverbs — on industry, frugality, prudence, debt, and time. The crowd listens attentively. They applaud warmly. Then they immediately buy everything at the auction anyway, "just as if it had been a common sermon." Franklin himself resolves only to wear his old coat a little longer.

That irony — wisdom clearly heard and instantly forgotten — is the most honest observation in the entire book. Franklin does not pretend that good advice reforms people. He is clear-eyed about human nature. We may give advice, says Poor Richard, but we cannot give conduct. The crowd's failure is not the failure of the message; it is the failure of the receiver, which Franklin acknowledges is nearly universal, and which he includes himself in. He only delayed buying a coat. Not a transformation. A coat.

The substance of Father Abraham's speech is organized around what Franklin calls the real taxes — not the government's. Idleness taxes us twice as much as any government. Pride taxes us three times as much. Folly four times. Unlike government levies, these internal taxes have no commissioners to petition, no abatements to seek. Only self-discipline reduces them. This framing — vice as a hidden tax on your wealth — is the central intellectual move of the piece. Franklin is not moralizing about virtue for its own sake. He is making a financial argument: your bad habits cost more than your government does.

The three pillars Father Abraham builds on are industry, frugality, and self-oversight. Industry means treating time as capital — lost time is never recovered, and the idle man isn't resting, he is spending. A man who earns ten shillings a day and wastes half of it in idleness has spent five shillings as surely as if he'd thrown them in the street, plus whatever he spent out of pocket. Frugality means saving is as important as earning — the Indies did not make Spain rich because her outgoes exceeded her incomes. Self-oversight means industry and frugality together are still not enough if you don't watch your own affairs: "The eye of a master will do more work than both his hands."

"Dost thou love life, then do not squander time, for that's the stuff life is made of." — Poor Richard

The debt section of Father Abraham's speech is among the sharpest financial arguments Franklin ever made. He frames consumer debt not as a financial inconvenience but as voluntary slavery. When you borrow for luxuries you cannot afford, you hand your creditor legal power over your body — he can imprison you, he can compel your labor. You would rightly call a king tyrannical if he forbade you to dress as you pleased. Yet you accept that exact same restriction from a draper, voluntarily, for six months of credit on clothes you didn't need. The psychological cascade that follows is equally damaging: shame before creditors leads to avoidance, avoidance to excuses, excuses to lies. "The second vice is lying," says Poor Richard, "the first is running in debt."

What makes "The Way to Wealth" more than a lecture is its self-aware structure. Franklin knows the wisdom he is delivering is not his own. He explicitly credits it to "the sense of all ages and nations" — he gathered it, refined it, packaged it. Father Abraham is a fictional character. Poor Richard is a pseudonym. Franklin stands two removes from his own moralizing, which lets him deliver it with none of the preachy authority that would make the reader defensive. The most influential piece of self-help writing in American colonial history was written by a man who presented himself as merely repeating what everyone already knew.

Franklin the Man

Benjamin Franklin was born in Boston in 1706, the fifteenth of seventeen children of a tallow-chandler who made soap and candles. He had two years of formal schooling. At seventeen he fled to Philadelphia with almost no money, arriving on a Sunday morning eating a large puffy roll while walking through the streets. He died in 1790 the most famous American in the world, having served as printer, scientist, civic organizer, diplomat, inventor, philosopher, and revolutionary. Bob Blaisdell, who edited this Dover anthology, calls him "the most interesting American who ever lived," and it is difficult to argue the point.

What the autobiography excerpts reveal, scattered through this anthology, is a man who was genuinely, intensely empirical about himself. He designed a system for tracking his progress toward thirteen virtues — temperance, silence, order, resolution, frugality, industry, sincerity, justice, moderation, cleanliness, tranquility, chastity, and humility. He made a small notebook with a column per virtue and a row per day, and marked failures with a black dot. His goal was to go through the full cycle of virtues repeatedly until each column was clean. He never fully succeeded. He was, he admitted, particularly bad at Order — the virtue that required keeping everything in its designated place. He kept trying anyway, and concluded that even the imperfect attempt left him "a better and a happier man than I otherwise should have been."

The autobiography also shows a Franklin who understood social dynamics with unusual precision. He banned from his vocabulary the words certainly and undoubtedly, replacing them with I conceive, I imagine, and it so appears to me at present. His reason was not modesty for its own sake — it was tactical. He found that tentative language produced "a readier reception and less contradiction" for his ideas. When he was wrong, he suffered less embarrassment. When he was right, he persuaded more easily. He was "never eloquent," he wrote, "never a good speaker" — and yet he generally carried his points, which he credited entirely to this habit of epistemic humility.

"In reality, there is perhaps no one of our natural Passions so hard to subdue as Pride. Disguise it, struggle with it, beat it down, stifle it, mortify it as much as one pleases, it is still alive... for, even if I could conceive that I had compleatly overcome it, I should probably be proud of my Humility." — The Autobiography, Part 2

One of the finest short passages in the anthology is Franklin's anecdote about working in London printing shops in 1724. His roughly fifty co-workers drank strong beer constantly — six pints per man per day, at intervals through the shift. They called Franklin the "Water-American" and were astonished that he was physically stronger than they were. He explained: the grain dissolved in water gives the strength, not the beer itself. A pennyworth of bread gives more strength than a quart of beer. The workers paid four or five shillings a week out of their wages for the beer; Franklin paid nothing. His dry comment on the situation: "And thus these poor devils keep themselves always under."

Franklin the family man is also here. In 1730 he married Deborah Read Rogers by common law — a practical partnership as much as a romantic one. She was, he wrote, "as disposed to industry and frugality as myself." She helped in the shop, folded pamphlets, tended the counter, purchased rags for paper-makers. Their household kept no idle servants. Franklin's breakfast for years was bread and milk, eaten from a twopenny earthen porringer with a pewter spoon. One morning he came down to find a china bowl and silver spoon in its place. Deborah had bought them while he slept. Her justification: her husband deserved as fine a breakfast service as any of their neighbors. Franklin tells this story not with anger but with wry tenderness — "mark how Luxury will enter Families, and make a Progress, in Spite of Principle." That china bowl was the start of their eventual several-hundred-pound collection.

The Almanack's Mission

Poor Richard's Almanack launched in 1732 and ran for twenty-five consecutive years, selling roughly ten thousand copies annually at its peak. After the Bible, it was the second most common book in American homes in the eighteenth century. Scarce a neighborhood in the colonies was without one. Franklin published it under the pseudonym Richard Saunders — a fictional astrologer and almanac-maker who was, in the prefaces, perpetually henpecked by his wife Bridget, perpetually short of money, and perpetually grateful to readers for buying the book that kept the family fed.

An almanac in this era was primarily a practical tool: weather predictions, tide tables, moon phases, holidays, court dates, astronomical data. Every colonial household needed one for navigation, planting, and scheduling. Franklin used the empty spaces on the calendar pages — the white space between Monday and Tuesday, the margins of June — as real estate for moral philosophy. His explicit stated goal, from his autobiography, was to convey instruction to common people who purchased scarce any other books, using industry and frugality as the means to wealth, and wealth as the means to virtue. He was saying, implicitly, that you cannot be a good person on an empty stomach.

The proverbs themselves were almost never original. Franklin said so repeatedly and without embarrassment. He gathered them from Erasmus, from English folk sayings, from classical sources, from European proverb collections. His contribution was curation, compression, and rhythm. "Early to bed and early to rise, makes a man healthy, wealthy, and wise" had existed in various forms in English for centuries before Franklin stamped it with his cadence. What he added was the packaging — the wit, the memorability, the context of a fictional almanac-maker who seemed to be sharing real-life hard-won wisdom, not dispensing received philosophical truth from a pulpit.

The prefaces that open each year's almanac are among the most entertaining pieces in the anthology. They are comic fiction — Richard Saunders complaining about rival almanac-makers, defending his astrological predictions, feuding satirically with the dead, and occasionally giving his fictional wife Bridget a paragraph to express her own opinions. Franklin used them to comment on everything from printer's errors (a Bible edition once printed "Thou shalt commit Adultery" — the Company of Stationers lost its royal charter over it) to the nature of celebrity to the absurdity of taking almanac predictions too seriously. The jokes were the hook; the wisdom was the payload.

Industry & Time

The deepest argument Franklin makes across twenty-five years of almanacs is about time. Not just "use your time wisely" — a piece of advice anyone can safely ignore — but a precise economic claim: idle time has a real cost equal to the wages you would have earned, plus any out-of-pocket expenses you incur while idle. Time is not free. Every hour spent not working, for a man who depends on his labor, costs him money as surely as if he had spent it. "Time is money" — first coined by Franklin in his 1748 "Advice to a Young Tradesman" — was not a cliché when he wrote it. It was a genuinely new frame for thinking about idleness.

The time-related proverbs in Poor Richard are relentless and concrete. The sleeping fox catches no poultry — there will be sleeping enough in the grave. He who rises late must trot all day and barely catches up by nightfall. Laziness travels so slowly that poverty soon overtakes it. Lost time is never found again. These are not gentle suggestions. They are descriptions of mechanism: idleness is not rest, it is acceleration toward poverty, and the mechanism is arithmetic, not metaphor.

Franklin's conception of industry is more nuanced than a simple work-more injunction. He distinguishes between the leisure of earned rest and the laziness of work avoidance — a distinction that matters enormously because the two look identical from the outside. He also insists that wishing for better times is useless: industry need not wish. He that lives upon hope will die fasting. The point is not that optimism is bad — it is that optimism without action is a kind of fraud you commit against yourself. The industrious man does not wait for favorable conditions; he creates them.

"Industry need not wish, and he that lives upon Hope will die fasting." — Poor Richard (1736)

Persistence is a separate theme from sheer busyness, and Franklin treats it distinctly. Little strokes fell great oaks. Constant dropping wears away stones. These proverbs are not about working harder — they are about the compounding effect of regular, steady effort over time. The same logic that produces compound interest in money produces compound achievement in work. The daily increment is invisible; the annual sum is decisive. Franklin understood this in 1740, a century and a half before it became a staple of self-improvement literature.

The "Advice to a Young Tradesman" letter, written around 1748, is the most condensed economic education Franklin ever produced. Beyond "time is money," he articulates that credit is money — money left in your hands past its due date is effectively a loan to you from your creditor, with interest he is giving you free. And money is prolific — five shillings turned becomes six, and six can become seven and three, and so on until a hundred pounds. "He that murders a Crown," Franklin writes, "destroys all it might have produced" — killing a pound kills all its offspring. This is an explicit statement of compound growth theory, expressed in 1748, framed as common sense.

Frugality & Money

Franklin's treatment of frugality goes well beyond "spend less." His argument is that for the great majority of people, the failure to accumulate wealth is not a failure of earning — it is a failure of saving. A man can keep his nose to the grindstone his entire life and die worth nothing if he cannot control his outgoes. The Spain example he returns to repeatedly is his macroeconomic proof: the Spanish Empire controlled the entire wealth of the Americas, extracting silver and gold from colonies for two centuries, and still ended up impoverished. The Indies did not make Spain rich. Her outgoes exceeded her incomes. The richest input in history could not overcome structural overspending.

Small expenses receive particular emphasis because they are the ones people reliably underestimate. "Beware of little expenses; a small leak will sink a great ship." The single purchase that seems trivial — a yard of ribbon, an evening at a tavern, a garment you don't need — does not register as a financial decision. But the accumulation of trivial purchases across a year, tracked in an account book, reveals sums that shock people. Franklin advises in the "Young Tradesman" letter to keep exact accounts of all expenditures: "you will discover how wonderfully small trifling expenses mount up to large sums." The point is not penury. It is visibility.

The concept of artificial wants is one of Franklin's most useful analytical tools. Natural wants — food, shelter, warmth — are finite and manageable. Artificial wants — fashionable clothes, fine crockery, keeping up with neighbors — are infinite and self- perpetuating. For every one person in genuine poverty, Franklin estimates there are a hundred "indigent" — not actually lacking necessities, but made effectively poor by spending on artificial wants that crowd out the savings that would protect them. The distinction between being poor and being indigent is social, not material: the indigent person has enough money but is spending it on things that feel necessary because social comparison makes them seem so.

"Buy what thou hast no need of, and ere long thou shalt sell thy necessaries." — Poor Richard

Franklin's analysis of appearance spending is particularly incisive. Buying one fine thing forces ten more purchases to match it. You buy a fine coat; now your hat looks shabby. You replace the hat; now your shoes look wrong. "'Tis easier to suppress the first desire," says Poor Richard, "than to satisfy all that follow it." This is the psychology of lifestyle inflation stated with precision in the mid-eighteenth century. The compounding trap of status consumption is not a modern problem — it is built into human nature, and Franklin saw it clearly. He also noted the most damning version of the trap: the genteel person who overspends on appearances is eventually forced to borrow from precisely the industrious and frugal tradespeople they were trying to outclass.

"A fat kitchen makes a lean will" is one of his more compressed formulations of the same idea: high living now produces low inheritance later. The money that flows out as entertainment and comfort in youth is exactly the money that will not exist as security or bequest in age. Franklin is not advocating asceticism — he enjoyed good food, good company, and wine. He is arguing for conscious choice rather than unreflective drift. A frugal man can afford occasional extravagance. A spendthrift can afford nothing, because he has spent the surplus that would fund it.

Debt & Pride

No theme in Poor Richard is treated with more urgency than debt. Franklin's argument against consumer debt is not primarily moral — it is structural. Debt changes the power relationship between you and another person in a way you cannot fully control. When you owe money, your creditor can come for it at any moment; his memory is perfect while yours is conveniently fuzzy. "Creditors," Poor Richard observes, "are a superstitious sect, great observers of set days and times." The due date arrives with mathematical certainty. Your ability to pay arrives with no certainty at all.

Franklin frames the worst consumer debt — borrowing for luxuries, for status items, for things you cannot afford — as equivalent to accepting a form of tyranny you would violently resist if a government imposed it. A king who said "you may not dress as you please without my permission" would provoke revolution. Yet anyone who walks into a shop, buys clothes on six months' credit, and thereby gives the shopkeeper legal power over his body — including imprisonment for default — has accepted exactly that same restriction voluntarily. The freedom surrendered to a creditor is identical to the freedom surrendered to a tyrant. The only difference is the mechanism of surrender.

"The borrower is a slave to the lender, and the debtor to the creditor." — Poor Richard

The psychological deterioration that debt produces gets as much attention as the financial. Debt begins in shame — you start avoiding streets where your creditor walks. Shame produces excuses. Excuses slide into dishonesty. "Lying rides upon debt's back." A man who was honest when solvent finds himself lying when pressed by creditors, not because he has become a different person but because debt creates situations where the truth is unbearable. The character corrosion is incremental and nearly invisible until it is substantial. Franklin's "the second vice is lying, the first is running in debt" is not a moral ranking — it is a causal chain.

Pride receives its own extended treatment as a separate financial destroyer. The pride of appearance — dressing above your means, furnishing above your means, entertaining above your means — is doubly destructive because it is invisible to the person doing it. No one believes they are spending on pride. They believe they are spending on necessity, on quality, on the minimum acceptable standard for someone of their position. Franklin is clear: "pride of appearance confers no health, eases no pain, and adds no merit to the person it is supposed to benefit." It is pure expenditure with no return except the briefly maintained illusion of status. And that illusion invites envy rather than respect.

The metaphor he uses most memorably is the comparison of someone poor aping the rich to a frog swelling to equal an ox. The frog can try, and may even briefly deceive passing glances, but the fundamental disparity of capacity makes the effort ridiculous. "Pride that dines on vanity sups on contempt" — the meal that status-seeking promises is the opposite of what it delivers. Franklin saw this as a mechanism, not a moral failure. The person caught in the pride-debt spiral is not wicked; they are trapped in an incentive structure that systematically punishes exactly what they are trying to achieve.

Character & Virtue

Franklin's thirteen-virtue project from the autobiography is the most detailed personal development system produced by any founding-era figure, and it stands out partly because of its honest accounting of failure. He was the only one who knew how bad he was doing. The notebook with its black dots was a private ledger of moral shortfalls, maintained across years of systematic effort. He never achieved a clean week. He got better at some virtues while others deteriorated. Eventually he decided that a "speckled ax" — an ax that has been used, marked, worn, but still cuts well — was a better goal than a polished one that never gets applied. Perfection was not the point. Progress was.

The thirteen virtues, with their one-sentence precepts, were: Temperance — eat not to dullness, drink not to elevation. Silence — speak not but what may benefit others or yourself; avoid trifling conversation. Order — let all your things have their places; let each part of your business have its time. Resolution — resolve to perform what you ought; perform without fail what you resolve. Frugality — make no expense but to do good to others or yourself; waste nothing. Industry — lose no time; be always employed in something useful; cut off all unnecessary actions. Sincerity — use no hurtful deceit; think innocently and justly. Justice — wrong none, by doing injuries or omitting the benefits that are your duty. Moderation — avoid extremes; forbear resenting injuries. Cleanliness — tolerate no uncleanliness in body, clothes, or habitation. Tranquillity — be not disturbed at trifles, or at accidents common or unavoidable. Chastity — rarely use venery but for health or offspring. Humility — imitate Jesus and Socrates. The ordering was deliberate: Temperance first because it produces the clear head needed for all the others; Silence second because Franklin wanted to break his habit of "prattling, punning, and joking"; Humility was added last, at the suggestion of a Quaker friend who told him he was "generally thought proud."

The virtue he treated with the most self-awareness was humility. He placed it last on the list, having added it at the suggestion of a Quaker friend who pointed out that Franklin was widely regarded as proud and insolent. Franklin's method for cultivating humility was the one described above: banning words of certainty from his vocabulary. But he was aware that this was a performance of humility as much as an experience of it, and he was honest enough to say so. His observation that he would probably be proud of his humility if he ever achieved it is one of the most psychologically acute things in the book — not a joke, but a genuine insight about the recursive trap of self-improvement.

"Genius without Education is like Silver in the Mine" — unrefined raw material has no practical value. Franklin believed profoundly in the improvability of human beings through effort and education, but he also believed that natural ability without cultivation was worthless. This was both a personal conviction, shaped by his own entirely self-educated career, and a practical observation: the talented man who relies on talent rather than work loses to the less gifted man who works systematically.

Honesty occupies a special place in Franklin's character writing, partly because he ties it directly to financial circumstances. He notes in the autobiography that wealth enables honesty — "it is more difficult for a man in want to act always honestly." This is not an excuse for dishonesty; it is a systems observation. The person who is financially desperate faces temptations the financially secure person never encounters. Frugality and industry are, in this frame, not merely money strategies — they are character strategies, because economic stability makes virtue easier to maintain.

Social Wisdom

The social proverbs scattered across twenty-five years of almanacs form a coherent picture of how Franklin thought human relationships work. The key is calibration: too much of anything — familiarity, speech, trust, advice — produces the opposite of the desired effect. "Friendship increases by visiting friends, but only by visiting seldom." Daily contact produces familiarity; familiarity produces contempt; contempt ends friendship. The scarcity of contact maintains the value. This is Franklin applying basic economic logic to social bonds.

On speech, his advice is systematic and concrete. The best proverbs on this theme are not "be careful what you say" — they are observations about mechanism. "Speak with contempt of none, from slave to king; the meanest bee hath, and will use, a sting." The danger of contemptuous speech is not that it is immoral; it is that it generates enemies in every direction, and enemies accumulate interest. "Better slip with foot than tongue" — a physical misstep is recoverable; a verbal one is remembered. Strategic opacity is equally valued: "Let all men know thee, but no man know thee thoroughly; men freely ford that see the shallows." Partial concealment protects from manipulation.

The essay "An Apology for Printers" (1731) is Franklin's formal defense of free expression, and it deploys a social observation as its core argument: you cannot produce a single piece of writing that offends nobody, because opinions are as various as faces. Unlike a shoemaker, who can make shoes for everyone, a printer cannot practice his trade without offending someone. His solution is professional detachment and the principle that "when Truth and Error have fair play, the former is always an overmatch for the latter." Free press is not a luxury — it is the mechanism by which correct information eventually prevails over incorrect information in a society that allows both to compete.

"Tart Words make no Friends: a spoonful of honey will catch more flies than a Gallon of Vinegar." — Poor Richard (1744)

Franklin's observations about self-knowledge are among his most universal. "Many complain of their Memory, few of their Judgment" — people attribute failure to forgetting rather than to poor reasoning, because the former is embarrassing in a limited way while the latter is damning. "Good Sense is a Thing all need, few have, and none think they want." The people most in need of better judgment are the ones least likely to recognize the need. This is the epistemic problem that no amount of advice can fully solve — which is why Franklin's closing insight, that "we may give advice but we cannot give conduct," is the honest capstone of everything else.

Wit, Satire & the Leeds Hoax

Before the wisdom and the maxims, there was the comedy. Franklin launched his almanac in 1732 by predicting, in his first preface as Richard Saunders, that rival Philadelphia almanac-maker Titan Leeds would die on October 17, 1733, at 3:29 PM. This was a direct imitation of a famous trick pulled by Jonathan Swift, who had predicted the death of an astrologer named John Partridge in 1708 and refused to acknowledge his survival. Franklin borrowed the bit wholesale, improved the execution, and ran it for four years.

When Leeds failed to die on schedule, and published a subsequent almanac attacking Franklin as "a false Predictor, an Ignorant, a conceited Scribler, a Fool, and a Lyar," Franklin had his response ready: the real Leeds had been too genteel to write anything so vulgar. Therefore the almanac claiming to be by Leeds must be a forgery by someone exploiting his name after his death. The logic was airtight and completely circular. He eulogized Leeds as "a Man of exemplary Sobriety, a most sincere Friend, and an exact Performer of his Word" — all attributes the very-much-alive Leeds was presumably reading — and expressed his deep personal grief at the loss.

The hoax ran for four years, with each annual preface adding a new layer of deadpan elaboration. In the 1735 edition Franklin produced a clinching argument: Leeds's own 1734 almanac, while denying he survived past October 26, thereby admitted he did not survive to October 26 — and since he died before then, he must have died on October 17, exactly as predicted. The logic was impeccable. The conclusion was absurd. Franklin's readers understood that this was not a sincere astrological dispute — it was a demonstration that the same rhetorical tools used to defend astrology could be used to prove anything at all.

"Alice Addertongue" (1732) is a different kind of comedy — a fully developed fictional persona, a thirty-five-year-old woman who has made gossip her life's vocation. Alice runs her scandal operation with ledger-book precision: a daybook for raw items of defamation, a journal for elaborated versions with "probable Circumstances" added, a general ledger for cross-references. She defends herself against the charge that gossip-mongering is a crime by noting that those who accuse women of scandal-spreading are themselves spreading scandal about women — which is either deeply ironic or deeply hypocritical, depending on how you look at it. Franklin gives Alice a sharp economic logic: gossip, like "other Virtues, is in part its own Reward, as it gives us the Satisfaction of making our selves appear better than others."

"The Speech of Miss Polly Baker" (1747) is the most radical thing in the anthology. Polly Baker is a fictional Connecticut woman being prosecuted for the fifth time for having a child outside marriage. Her argument before the court is methodical: she has brought five fine children into the world at the risk of her life, raised them by her own industry, and burthened no township. Can it be a crime to add to the number of the King's subjects in a new country that genuinely needs people? The law punishes her while the man who seduced her — who promised marriage and then abandoned her — is now a magistrate of the county, sitting in judgment. The crowd's first seducer gets promoted; the woman who loved and trusted him gets whipped. Franklin makes no editorial comment. He does not need to.

On Governance & Liberty

The later writings in this anthology — mostly from the 1780s, when Franklin was in his seventies — show a different dimension of the same mind. "Remarks Concerning the Savages of North America" (1784) opens with a sentence that has lost none of its edge: "Savages we call them, because their manners differ from ours, which we think the perfection of civility; they think the same of theirs." This is not the common-sense folk wisdom of Poor Richard. This is Enlightenment relativism applied systematically to the question of civilization.

Franklin describes Native American governance with genuine admiration. Indian society was governed entirely by counsel and persuasion — no prisons, no officers with power to compel, no force. The best orator had the most influence. Women preserved the community's historical memory with such precision that their treaty recollections, checked against written European records over a century, were consistently found exact. Their council decorum — profound silence while the speaker finished, several minutes of silence afterward so he might add anything omitted — was compared favorably to the British House of Commons, where "scarce a day passes without some confusion that makes the Speaker hoarse in calling to order."

"You saw that we, who understand and practise those rules, believed all your stories; why do you refuse to believe ours?" — Susquehanna Indian orator, via Franklin's Writings

The most devastating scene in the essay involves a Swedish minister who preached the Gospel to a group of Susquehanna Indians — the fall, the redemption, the miracles. An Indian orator thanked him politely and offered in return an Indian creation myth. The minister dismissed the Indian story as "mere fable, fiction, and falsehood." The Indian's reply: you saw that we believed all your stories without contradiction; why do you refuse to believe ours? Franklin's point is not that the two myths are equally true — it is that the courtesy and intellectual openness extended by the Indians was not reciprocated by the missionary. Who, exactly, is the more civilized party?

The daylight saving essay (1784), written as a satirical letter to the Journal de Paris, is a masterpiece of mock-earnest calculation. Franklin "discovers" that the sun rises early and gives free light — a discovery he presents as a startling novelty. He then calculates that the hundred thousand families of Paris, burning candles for seven unnecessary hours each night across a summer of 183 nights, are wasting 64 million pounds of wax and tallow annually — roughly 96 million livres tournois. His proposed solution: ring church bells and fire cannons at sunrise to wake the population. The essay is entirely ridiculous, and entirely serious. Franklin was actually arguing that free sunlight was being wasted due to a social convention (fashionable people do not rise before noon). The comedy was the vehicle; the critique of waste was the content.

Franklin's press freedom arguments, developed through the Pennsylvania Gazette from 1731 onward, rest on a consistent principle: "when Truth and Error have fair play, the former is always an overmatch for the latter." This is not naive optimism. It is a functional claim: suppressing speech doesn't eliminate false beliefs, it just prevents them from being examined and refuted. A society that allows all arguments to compete will eventually converge on the correct ones. A society that suppresses arguments deemed incorrect has no mechanism for finding out which arguments those actually are.

The Whistle & Later Essays

The most famous piece Franklin wrote outside the almanac proper is a short letter sent from Paris in 1779 to his French friend Madame Brillon, built around a childhood memory. When Franklin was seven years old, some friends gave him pocket money for a holiday. On the way to the toy shop he fell in love with a whistle another boy was carrying and spent every penny he had on it — paying, as he later discovered, four times what the whistle was worth. His family's ridicule at the overpayment caused him more grief than the whistle had given him pleasure. The lesson stayed with him for seventy years.

The essay catalogs every type of person who pays too much for their whistle. The court favor-seeker sacrifices leisure, liberty, virtue, and friendship for a title. The popularity-monger neglects his own affairs entirely in pursuit of public applause. The miser gives up comfort, friendship, and esteem to accumulate money he never enjoys. The pleasure-seeker trades health and judgment for bodily sensation, "providing pain for yourself, instead of pleasure." The appearance-obsessed contracts debts for fine clothes and carriages and ends in prison. Franklin's framework — false estimates of value cause most human misery — ties together every theme he ever wrote about: the debt trap, the artificial wants, the pride spending, the wasted time. "The Whistle" is the emotional capstone of the entire body of work.

"I conceive that great part of the miseries of mankind are brought upon them by the false estimates they have made of the value of things, and by their giving too much for their whistles." — B. Franklin, letter to Madame Brillon, 1779

Franklin closes the letter with characteristic self-awareness: he admits that even he, with all this wisdom, might be tempted by "the apples of King John" and ruin himself. The maxim is not armor against temptation. It is simply a reminder to check the price.

The Dialogue between Franklin and the Gout (1780) is a different kind of self-examination — a comic one. Written at midnight in Passy, it stages a dramatic conversation in which Madam Gout, personified and wielding painful twinges as punctuation, puts Franklin on trial for sedentary living. Franklin defends himself: he takes as much exercise as he can given his circumstances. Gout demolishes every claim. Franklin reads and writes all morning instead of walking before breakfast. He spends his afternoons playing chess — "the least eligible recreation for a sedentary man," Gout argues, because chess demands rigid mental concentration that retards circulation, unlike walking, which actively pumps blood through the legs. When Franklin pleads that riding in a carriage counts, Gout explains the physiology: a carriage on springs provides almost no motion; walking in winter warms you thoroughly in an hour; a carriage won't warm you all day.

Gout keeps a written list of offenses. Franklin had promised himself walks in the Bois de Boulogne 199 times in writing and skipped them all. At a friend's garden with a hundred-step terrace, he visited twice a week for months and never once descended the stairs for exercise — despite having quoted his own proverb that "a man may take as much exercise in walking a mile up and down stairs as in ten on level ground." He knew the rule. He just didn't apply it. Gout's verdict: "You philosophers are sages in your maxims, and fools in your conduct." Franklin agrees, promises reform, and Gout departs with the skeptical observation that after a few months of good health he will return to his old habits, "your fine promises forgotten like the forms of last year's clouds."

"Our debts and our sins are always greater than we think for." — Poor Richard, quoted by Franklin to the Gout

"The Handsome and Deformed Leg" (c. 1780) offers a deceptively simple framework for understanding why the same circumstances produce happiness in some people and misery in others. The answer, Franklin argues, is purely a matter of where attention fixes. Every situation has conveniences and inconveniences; every face has fine features and defects; every government has good laws and bad ones. Happy people fix attention on what is pleasant and discuss it. Unhappy people see only contraries and talk of nothing else. A philosopher friend of Franklin's had one handsome leg and one crooked, deformed leg. He used the two legs as a social instrument: if a new acquaintance at a first meeting focused on the deformed leg, the philosopher avoided further contact, having identified an unpleasant disposition that would make friendship miserable. Franklin's advice: "Leave off looking at the ugly leg."

The Political Satirist

Franklin's sharpest writing is not the almanac proverbs — it is his political satire, which he used with surgical precision at moments of maximum consequence. "Rules by Which a Great Empire May Be Reduced to a Small One" (1773) is twenty ironic "rules" addressed to ministers managing "extensive dominions" that have become troublesome to govern. It is a comprehensive indictment of British colonial policy, written two years before Lexington and Concord, disguised as helpful administrative advice. Franklin was describing exactly what Britain was already doing, rule by rule.

The rules build from administrative contempt to deliberate provocation. Never incorporate distant provinces into the mother country — deny them the same rights, impose laws they had no voice in making. If they built themselves at their own expense and grew your armies and commerce — forget it, or better, resent it as if they had done you an injury. Appoint governors from among "ruined prodigals, broken gamblers, and stock-jobbers"; use "quarrelsome lawyers" as judges; the ignorant, wrong-headed, and insolent will serve "so much the better." When colonists bring complaints of oppression, punish the petitioners with delay and expense and a final judgment in favor of their oppressors — this discourages future complaints and "encourages your Governors to further acts of injustice." When odious governors have "crammed their coffers" and must flee, reward them with pensions — to encourage the next round.

The rules escalate toward open provocation. When colonists claim constitutional liberties — habeas corpus, trial by jury — annihilate these with commercial regulations, admiralty courts, and a Declaratory Act asserting Parliament's power to bind them "in all cases whatsoever." Send revenue collectors who are "indiscreet, ill-bred, and insolent"; if they provoke beatings, promote them. If told colonial discontent is general, never believe it — redress nothing, grant nothing, hang the agitators, and "the blood of martyrs shall work miracles in favour of your purpose." The final rule — give your general in the provinces unconstitutional powers over all troops and fortresses, following the Roman imperial precedent — ends with the prediction that all the provinces will immediately join him. Franklin's satire is so precise because it is not invention. It is observation.

"A great Empire, like a great Cake, is most easily diminished at the Edges." — Rules for Reducing a Great Empire, 1773

His final published piece, written weeks before his death in April 1790, was his most devastating use of the same technique. "Sidi Mehemet Ibrahim" (March 1790) is a satirical address by a fictional Muslim Algerian official arguing against freeing Christian slaves captured by Algerine pirates. The argument mirrors, point for point, the speeches American slaveholders had just delivered in Congress against Quaker abolition petitions. Without them, who will do the labor in this hot climate? The freed slaves won't return home — hardship there is worse. They won't adopt our religion or manners. They will become beggars or criminals. "Men accustomed to slavery will not work for a livelihood when not compelled." Besides, they are better fed and housed here than they were as free men in their own countries. And the Alcoran explicitly supports the institution — "Masters, treat your slaves with kindness; slaves, serve your masters with cheerfulness and fidelity" — the same scriptural logic American slaveholders used from the Bible.

By placing every argument in the mouth of a Muslim Algerian pirate-state, Franklin made the moral bankruptcy of the position impossible to miss for any reader who had not already chosen blindness. He had signed the abolition petition to Congress as president of the Pennsylvania Abolition Society just weeks earlier. He was eighty-four years old. He died twenty-five days after the piece was published. It was the last thing he chose to write.

Trading & Investing Lessons

Time Costs Are Real Costs

Franklin's formulation that idle time costs a worker its equivalent in wages is the earliest precise statement of opportunity cost in American economic writing. For traders: the cost of being in cash is not zero. Time spent flat or in underperforming positions is not neutral — it is the foregone return of better-deployed capital. Every day you hold a position that isn't working, you are paying the price of not holding something that would work.

Compound Growth Is Arithmetic, Not Magic

"Money can beget Money, and its Offspring can beget more" — Franklin's 1748 statement of compounding — is the core logic of every long-term investment strategy. The practical implication he draws is often ignored: killing a small position early (the "Crown" that "murders all it might have produced") destroys not just current value but the compounded future value of that capital. Cutting winners early is not just a tactical error — it is a compounding error.

Credit Reputation Is an Asset

Franklin's argument that punctual payment gives you access to "all the money your friends can spare" at any time is a precise statement of what modern finance calls liquidity access. A reputation for reliability means capital is available to you in emergencies and opportunities both. For investors: the ability to act quickly when opportunity appears — to have dry powder and willing counterparties — is worth more than any specific position.

Small Leaks Sink Ships

Franklin's emphasis on tracking small expenses as the hidden destroyer of wealth maps directly onto fee drag, spread costs, and management fees in modern portfolios. The individually trivial cost that "won't matter" on a single trade is the cost that compounds into a meaningful return deficit over years. Beware the small leak. It is not the storm that sinks most ships — it is the leak that nobody noticed growing.

Visible Work Signals to Counterparties

Franklin's observation that a creditor who hears your hammer at five in the morning will extend you another six months of credit — while a creditor who sees you at a billiard table will demand payment the next day — is a precise description of how signaling works in financial relationships. Counterparties, lenders, and prime brokers all form models of your operation from observable signals. The signals you send about your discipline and diligence matter regardless of your actual solvency.

Artificial Wants vs. Real Needs

Franklin's distinction between artificial and natural wants maps cleanly onto the behavioral finance concept of lifestyle creep — the expansion of perceived necessities as income grows, which prevents wealth accumulation at every income level. The trader who earns more and spends more has not improved his financial position proportionally. He has changed his lifestyle baseline, making every future period of reduced earnings more painful and every decision to cut risk more costly personally.

Key Takeaways

Time Is Capital

Idle time is not free — it costs the wages you would have earned. Lost time is never found again. The compounding of small, regular effort over long periods produces decisive outcomes that no single burst of activity can replicate. One today is worth two tomorrows.

Frugality Is the Other Half of Earning

Saving is as important as earning. The Indies could not make Spain rich. A fat kitchen makes a lean will. Small expenses accumulate fatally — the small leak sinks the great ship. Keep exact accounts; you will discover how much you are spending on things you do not remember buying.

Debt Surrenders Liberty

Consumer debt for luxuries is voluntary slavery — legal, psychological, and character-corroding. Debt leads to shame, shame to excuses, excuses to lies. The borrower is a slave to the lender. Rather go to bed supperless than rise in debt.

The Real Taxes Are Internal

Idleness taxes you twice as much as government. Pride three times. Folly four times. These taxes have no commissioners and grant no abatements. Only self-discipline reduces them.

Credit Is as Valuable as Cash

A good reputation for punctual payment gives you access to all the money your friends can spare, at any time. The good paymaster is lord of another man's purse. Credit is money — treat it as carefully.

Money Is Prolific

Compound growth is not a modern discovery. Money can beget money, and its offspring can beget more. Killing a Crown today destroys every pound it might have eventually produced. Small capital, briskly turned, produces great advantage.

Wisdom Can Be Transmitted; Conduct Cannot

We may give advice but we cannot give conduct. The crowd at the vendue heard everything Father Abraham said and bought everything anyway. Knowledge of what to do and actually doing it are entirely separate problems — and the second is the hard one.

Contrarian Ideas

Poor People Are Usually Not Poor — They Are Indigent

Franklin's distinction between the genuinely poor and the "indigent" — those made effectively poor by their own spending — was sharp and uncomfortable in the eighteenth century and remains so. The implication is that most poverty in a functioning economy is self-inflicted through artificial want, not externally imposed. For every truly poor person, Franklin estimates a hundred who are poor by choice of spending. This is not a popular argument but it is an important one.

Good Advice Changes Almost Nothing

Franklin's most honest observation is also his most pessimistic: the crowd that heard Father Abraham's complete and compelling speech immediately ignored it. The essay closes not with optimism about human improvability but with Franklin's modest personal resolution to delay one coat purchase. Wisdom broadcast to receptive audiences changes individual behavior at the margins, not in the aggregate. This is a deeply pessimistic conclusion from the man who spent twenty-five years trying to improve people through published proverbs.

The Wise Man Keeps His Shallows Hidden

"Men freely ford that see the shallows" — full transparency about your knowledge and capabilities invites exploitation. Partial opacity is not dishonesty; it is self-defense. Franklin practiced this personally: he was the most famous diplomat of his era, celebrated for his openness and accessibility, while being strategically opaque about his actual positions and intentions.

Imperfect Virtue Is More Valuable Than Theoretical Perfection

The speckled ax cuts better than the polished one that never gets used. Franklin's abandonment of the goal of perfect virtue in favor of progressive improvement is a genuinely contrarian position in an era of religious moralizing. The goal is not to be good; it is to be better than you were. The striving matters more than the achievement.

What Didn't Fit

The Ass Fable

In "An Apology for Printers," Franklin deploys a Fable of the Ass to close his argument that you cannot please every critic. A man and his son take their donkey to market. Every passerby criticizes their arrangement: first the father rides while the son walks (cruel), then both ride (cruel to the ass), then the son rides while the father walks (disrespectful), then neither rides (stupid). Finally the man concludes: it grieves me that we cannot please all these people; let us throw the ass over the next bridge. Franklin's application is direct: attempting to satisfy every objection leads to destroying the very thing you are defending. He will not, he says, burn his press and melt his letters to avoid controversy. The fable is older than Franklin but he deploys it with such precision — at exactly the right moment in exactly the right argument — that it reads as original.

The Perfect Number Correction

In the 1750 almanac preface, Franklin pauses to correct an error from a previous edition: he had stated that the next perfect number after 28 was 120. It is, in fact, 496. He notes this with the dry observation that the printer garbled the vowels, which is responsible for multiple errors in that edition — including changing "made" to "mad" and "warp'd" to "wrapp'd." Franklin's treatment of printing errors throughout the anthology is consistently funny and revealing: he understood the physical, fallible process of eighteenth-century typography with the authority of someone who had set type himself, and he was neither too proud to admit errors nor too careless to leave them standing. The mathematics footnote is a small moment, but it is the mark of a man who took accuracy seriously enough to correct a number that most readers would never check.

Mrs. Bridget Saunders Gets a Voice

In the 1734 almanac poetry section, Franklin gives his fictional wife Bridget Saunders a poem written from her perspective, in response to Poor Richard's December verses from the prior year. The poem is a direct economic critique of male drinking: the husband who haunts taverns, rises at noon, and lets his trade go to ruin while his family starves. "God in his Mercy may do much to save him," Bridget concludes, "but woe to the poor Wife whose lot it is to have him." Franklin gives his fictional wife not only a voice but a sharp analytical one — she is not scolding, she is calculating. The economic consequences of the drinking husband are laid out with the precision of a ledger entry. This is an unusually sympathetic portrait of a working-class woman's financial exposure to her husband's vices, written in 1734.

The Four-Part Formula for Good Writing

In a 1728 paper for his Junto intellectual club, Franklin laid out his criteria for good writing: it should benefit the reader, proceed from known to unknown in just method, use the most expressive words most generally understood, say nothing expressible in one word with two, be as short as possible while remaining clear, and be pleasant to the ear. "Smooth, clear, and short" is his summary standard. This is the operating specification behind every Poor Richard proverb — each one tested against this checklist before publication. The criteria are also a reasonably complete description of Franklin's own prose style, which remains more readable than almost any other eighteenth-century American writing.

Canassetego's Market Analysis

In the Savages essay, a Native American leader named Canassetego — an old friend of Franklin's interpreter Conrad Weiser — provides one of the most cutting pieces of institutional analysis in the anthology. He had attended church services in Albany and noticed that on the day merchants claimed to meet "to learn good things," the price of beaver skins dropped from four shillings to three shillings sixpence. His conclusion: "If they met so often to learn good things, they would certainly have learned some before this time. But they are still ignorant." He concluded the meetings were held primarily to coordinate price-fixing against Indian traders. Franklin reports this without comment. The scene is a perfect illustration of his broader point about civilization: the form of virtue — churchgoing, sabbath-keeping, public piety — is not the same as its substance.

About the Author

Benjamin Franklin (1706–1790) was the fifteenth of seventeen children born to a Boston tallow-chandler. He had two years of formal schooling before being apprenticed to his older brother, a printer, at age twelve. At seventeen he ran away to Philadelphia with almost no money and spent the next decade building a printing business from nothing into one of the most profitable publishing operations in the colonies. He retired from active business at forty-two, having already published the almanac for ten years and established the Pennsylvania Gazette as the most influential newspaper in North America.

The second half of his life, the half that made him famous outside America, was given to science and diplomacy. His kite-and-key experiment with lightning led to the lightning rod and election to the Royal Society of London. His invention of bifocal glasses and the Franklin stove were characteristic — practical problems solved with practical elegance, published without patents because he believed knowledge should be free. As a diplomat he spent two decades in London attempting to preserve the colonial relationship with Britain before concluding, reluctantly, that independence was necessary. He then spent the critical years of the Revolutionary War in Paris, securing the French alliance without which American independence was likely impossible.

At the Constitutional Convention of 1787, aged eighty-one and brought in by chair because he could no longer walk, he gave the convention's closing speech calling for unanimous ratification despite his personal reservations. He spent his last years as an increasingly vocal abolitionist — the man who had once owned slaves became the president of the Pennsylvania Society for Promoting the Abolition of Slavery. He died in Philadelphia in 1790, at eighty-four, attended by twenty thousand mourners. No figure in American history bridged the gap between ordinary trades people and the highest reaches of intellectual and political life more completely or more naturally.

Why This Book Matters

Poor Richard's Almanack is the founding document of American practical philosophy. Before it, moral instruction in the colonies came primarily through sermons — delivered from a position of spiritual authority to a congregation that received it passively. Franklin invented a new delivery mechanism: a cheap annual pamphlet, sold by the almanac-maker himself, framed as the hard-won wisdom of a common tradesman rather than the commands of a divine. The shift from pulpit to page, from God's authority to earned experience, from eternal salvation to financial security — this is the intellectual revolution that Poor Richard enacted, and it produced what historians have called the first distinctly American cultural form.

Its direct legacy runs through every American self-help tradition that followed. The belief that a person of any origin can improve their circumstances through discipline, education, and consistent effort — without needing permission from birth, church, or aristocracy — is the defining American idea, and Franklin expressed it more clearly and memorably than anyone before or since. When you hear "no pain, no gain" (first recorded in Poor Richard), "time is money" (Franklin, 1748), "early to bed and early to rise" (Franklin's version), or "a penny saved is a penny earned" (Franklin's variant), you are hearing the direct echo of twenty-five years of almanac-making. The phrases have outlasted their author by two and a half centuries.

What gives the book its enduring edge is the irony Franklin built into it. The greatest piece of financial self-help literature in American history ends with its own audience ignoring it completely. Father Abraham gives the perfect speech. The crowd applauds. They buy everything at the auction. Franklin resolves to wear his old coat a little longer. The honest conclusion — that wisdom changes almost nothing — is not despair, but it is not naive optimism either. It is the wry, clear-eyed acknowledgment that the distance between knowing what to do and actually doing it is the whole of the human problem, and that no amount of proverbs closes it entirely. Franklin spent twenty-five years trying anyway. That, too, is a lesson.
