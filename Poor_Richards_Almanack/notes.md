## "The Way to Wealth" (1758) — Benjamin Franklin

### Overview & Framing Device

- Full text is a single essay/speech, not a multi-chapter almanac
- **Framing device:** Franklin narrates stopping at a vendue (auction) of merchant goods; a crowd
  complains about heavy taxes; they appeal to an old man named **Father Abraham** for advice
- Father Abraham delivers a long speech drawn entirely from **Poor Richard's Almanack** aphorisms
- Franklin reveals at the end that Father Abraham had "thoroughly studied my almanacs" — the wisdom
  is a curated distillation of 25 years of Poor Richard sayings, not original to the speech
- **Meta-irony:** After the speech, the crowd applauds — then immediately buys extravagantly anyway,
  "just as if it had been a common sermon." Franklin himself resolves to wear his old coat longer.
- Source: *The Works of Benjamin Franklin*, ed. **Jared Sparks**, Vol. 2 (Boston, 1836), pp. 92–103

---

### Theme 1: The Real Tax — Idleness, Pride, and Folly

- Government taxes are heavy, but Franklin argues we are taxed far more by our own vices:
  - **Idleness** taxes us twice as much as government
  - **Pride** taxes us three times as much
  - **Folly** taxes us four times as much
- Unlike government taxes, these internal taxes have no commissioners to grant abatements

> "We are taxed twice as much by our idleness, three times as much by our pride, and four times
> as much by our folly." — Father Abraham (quoting Poor Richard)

> "God helps them that help themselves." — Poor Richard (1733)

---

### Theme 2: Industry & The Value of Time

- **Time is the supreme resource** — wasting it is the "greatest prodigality" because lost time
  is never recovered
- Sloth is not merely unproductive; it actively shortens life by causing disease

> "Sloth, like rust, consumes faster than labor wears, while the used key is always bright."
> — Poor Richard

> "Dost thou love life, then do not squander time, for that's the stuff life is made of."
> — Poor Richard

- The sleeping fox catches no poultry; *there will be sleeping enough in the grave*
- **Industry compounds**: diligence does more with less confusion; *sloth makes all things
  difficult, but industry all easy*
- He that rises late must trot all day and barely catches up; *laziness travels so slowly that
  poverty soon overtakes it*
- Key aphorism linking time and money: *early to bed, and early to rise, makes a man healthy,
  wealthy and wise*

### On Work Ethic and Not Wishing

- Wishing for better times accomplishes nothing; *industry need not wish*
- *He that lives upon hope will die fasting* — action, not optimism, produces results
- Having a trade is an estate; having a calling is an office of profit — but only if actually
  worked: the estate and calling enable tax payment only through diligence
- *At the working man's house hunger looks in, but dares not enter*
- **Industry pays debts; despair encreaseth them**
- *Diligence is the mother of good luck* — luck is not random; it rewards consistent effort
- *One today is worth two tomorrows* — present action beats future intention

> "Have you somewhat to do tomorrow, do it today." — Poor Richard

- Persistence beats talent: *constant dropping wears away stones*; *little strokes fell great oaks*

---

### Theme 3: Personal Oversight — Don't Delegate Your Affairs

- Industry must be paired with **attention to one's own business**; trusting too much to others
  is a leading cause of ruin
- *The eye of a master will do more work than both his hands*
- *Want of care does us more damage than want of knowledge*
- *Not to oversee workmen is to leave them your purse open*
- In worldly affairs, *men are saved not by faith but by the want of it* (i.e., vigilance, not
  passive trust)
- Small neglects cascade into catastrophe — the **horseshoe nail chain**:

> "For want of a nail the shoe was lost; for want of a shoe the horse was lost; and for want
> of a horse the rider was lost." — Poor Richard

- Stability matters: *three removes is as bad as a fire* — frequent relocation destroys wealth
  the way fire does; settled families thrive over transplanted ones
- *Keep the shop, and thy shop will keep thee*

---

### Theme 4: Frugality — Saving Is as Important as Earning

- Industry without frugality is futile: a man can work his whole life and *keep his nose to the
  grindstone* yet die worth nothing if he cannot save
- *A fat kitchen makes a lean will* — high living now means low inheritance later
- **Spain example:** The Indies (colonies) did not make Spain rich because her outgoes exceeded
  her incomes — a macroeconomic illustration of the frugality principle
- *If you would be wealthy, think of saving as well as of getting*
- Small expenses are the hidden killer: *many a little makes a mickle*

> "Beware of little expenses; a small leak will sink a great ship." — Poor Richard

- Vices are expensive multipliers: *what maintains one vice would bring up two children*
- Fools make feasts; wise men eat them — extravagance funds others' wealth, not your own
- **Artificial wants** proliferate: for one poor person there are a hundred "indigent" —
  people not truly poor but made needy by spending beyond their means on luxuries
- Buying cheap things you don't need is still waste: *buy what thou hast no need of, and ere
  long thou shalt sell thy necessaries*
- *Many have been ruined by buying good pennyworths* (bargains that weren't necessary)
- **Keeping up appearances** is self-defeating: buying one fine thing forces ten more purchases
  to match; *'tis easier to suppress the first desire than to satisfy all that follow*

> "A ploughman on his legs is higher than a gentleman on his knees." — Poor Richard

---

### Theme 5: Debt — The Slavery of Borrowing

- Running into debt for luxuries is described as **voluntary enslavement**
- Debt transfers power over your liberty to your creditor: they can imprison you or sell you
  into servitude
- Psychological costs of debt: shame before creditors, fear, dishonesty, eventual lying —
  *the second vice is lying, the first is running in debt*
- *Lying rides upon debt's back* — debt corrupts character progressively
- **Creditors have perfect memories; debtors forget**: creditors are "superstitious observers
  of set days and times" — due dates always arrive faster than expected
- *Those have a short Lent who owe money to be paid at Easter*
- Debt framing as tyranny: people would rightly call a government tyrannical if it forbade them
  to dress as they pleased — yet going into debt for dress voluntarily surrenders that same
  freedom to a creditor

> "The borrower is a slave to the lender, and the debtor to the creditor." — Poor Richard

- Gain is temporary and uncertain; expense is constant and certain
- *'Tis easier to build two chimneys than to keep one in fuel* — earning capacity is harder
  to sustain than expenses are to accumulate
- **Final directive:** *Rather go to bed supperless than rise in debt*

---

### Theme 6: The Limits of Wisdom — Character and Providence

- Franklin closes with a qualification: industry, frugality, and prudence are necessary but
  not sufficient — they "may all be blasted without the blessing of heaven"
- Practical wisdom must be paired with **humility and charity** toward those less fortunate
- *Experience keeps a dear school, but fools will learn in no other* — wise people learn from
  others' mistakes; fools require personal disaster
- *We may give advice, but we cannot give conduct* — knowledge can be transmitted; behavior
  cannot be forced
- *They that won't be counseled can't be helped*

### Notable Structural Point

- The entire essay is a **speech-within-a-frame**: Franklin → narrator → Father Abraham → Poor
  Richard. Franklin distances himself from the moralizing while still delivering it.
- The crowd's failure to act on the speech is the essay's darkest and most honest observation:
  good advice, clearly understood, is routinely ignored. Franklin himself admits he only resolved
  to delay buying a coat — not to transform his life.
- The wisdom Franklin credits to Poor Richard is explicitly described as *gleaned from the sense
  of all ages and nations* — a compilation of universal folk wisdom, not Franklin's invention.

## Introduction (by Bob Blaisdell)

### Franklin's Life & Character
- Born 1706, died 1790 — editor **Bob Blaisdell** calls him
  "the most interesting American who ever lived"
- Boston-born son of a tallow-chandler; two years of schooling,
  fled New England at 17, arrived in Philadelphia nearly penniless
- Roles: printer, writer, businessman, community activist,
  militia-man, scientist, diplomat, philosopher, philanderer,
  social animal
- Regarded in Europe as America's **"representative man"**;
  helped forge "American" as a national identity
- Tried colonial compromise with England in 1750s–60s before
  becoming the Revolution's most eloquent spokesman
- A **skeptic in religion** and true believer in humankind;
  became a prominent abolitionist late in life
- Franklin became a model for "inventive, diligent creative
  geniuses — but convincingly pretended to be a model for
  the rest of us too"

### The Almanack's Reach & Purpose
- **Poor Richard's Almanack** launched 1732, ran ~25 years under
  the pseudonym **Richard Saunders**; sold ~10,000 copies/year
- After the Bible, the **second most popular book** in American
  homes in the 18th century
- Original function: annual guide to weather, tides, moon, stars
- Franklin used the blank calendar spaces to insert **moral
  sentences, prudent maxims, and wise sayings** — the real payload
- Explicit goal: convey instruction to common people who bought
  scarce any other books; **industry and frugality** as means to
  wealth and thereby virtue
- Proverbial wisdom collected from "many Ages and Nations" —
  Franklin freely drew on existing proverbs, didn't invent them all

> "it is hard for an empty Sack to stand upright."
> — Franklin, *Autobiography* Part 3 (on why virtue requires
> economic security)

- The 1757 preface compiled the best sayings as a connected
  discourse — **"The Way to Wealth"** / **"Father Abraham's
  Speech"** — reprinted across all continental newspapers,
  reprinted in Britain as a broadside, translated into French;
  clergy distributed it gratis to poor parishioners
- Franklin credited it with influencing Pennsylvania's growing
  prosperity by discouraging wasteful foreign luxuries

### Editorial Notes (Blaisdell)
- This Dover anthology (2013) selects for **humor, wit, satire,
  and practical common-man advice** — not a full biography
- Sayings included from every year 1733–1758
- Franklin's original spelling, capitalization, italicization,
  and punctuation largely retained
- Prefaces dated to a year actually belong to the *prior* year
  (printed in fall for the following year)

## From his *Autobiography*, "My Almanack" (1732)

- Franklin published almanac in 1732 under name **Richard Saunders**
- Sold ~10,000 copies annually; scarce a neighborhood without one
- Filled calendar spaces with proverbs "chiefly such as inculcated
  **Industry and Frugality**"
- Key insight: wealth enables honesty — "it is more difficult for
  a Man in Want to act always honestly"
- The 1757 compilation into "Father Abraham's Speech" was
  universally approved; copied in all continental newspapers,
  reprinted in Britain on a broadside, translated into French

## From his *Autobiography*, "a reasonable creature" (1723)

- Context: Franklin left Boston 1723; had become a vegetarian
  in 1722 under the influence of his master **Tryon**
- On a sea voyage, smelled fresh-caught cod frying; wavered
  between principle and appetite
- Rationalized eating fish by observing smaller fish inside
  the cod's stomach: "If you eat one another, I don't see why
  we mayn't eat you"

> "So convenient a thing it is to be a *reasonable creature*,
> since it enables one to find or make a reason for everything
> one has a mind to do."
> — *Autobiography*, Part 1

- **Key insight**: Franklin uses this anecdote as self-aware
  satire on rationalization — humans are skilled at constructing
  post-hoc justifications for what they want

## From his *Autobiography*, "strong beer" (c. 1725)

- Context: Franklin worked in London printing shops 1724–1726
- He drank only water; ~50 co-workers drank strong beer
  constantly (6 pints/day per man: before breakfast, at
  breakfast, mid-morning, dinner, afternoon, end of day)
- Workers called Franklin the **"Water-American"** and were
  astonished he was physically stronger than the beer-drinkers
- Franklin argued: beer's strength comes only from the grain
  dissolved in water — a pennyworth of bread gives more
  strength than a quart of beer
- Workers paid 4–5 shillings/week out of wages for beer;
  Franklin paid nothing

> "And thus these poor devils keep themselves always under."
> — *Autobiography*, Part 1

- **Key theme**: frugality and sobriety as competitive advantage;
  self-defeating habits trap the working poor

## A Query, "How shall we judge of the goodness of a writing?" (c. 1728)

- From **"Proposals and Queries for the Consideration of the
  Junto"** — Franklin's intellectual club
- Franklin's criteria for good writing:
  1. **Benefit the reader** — improve virtue or knowledge
  2. **Just method** — proceed from known to unknown,
     distinctly and clearly
  3. **Most expressive words** that are most generally understood
  4. **Economy of words** — nothing expressible in one word
     should take two; no synonyms
  5. **Brevity** — as short as possible consistent with clearness
  6. **Euphony** — words arranged to be agreeable to the ear
- Summary standard: writing should be **smooth, clear, and short**

## Printer's Errors (March 13, 1730)

- Letter to publisher of *The Pennsylvania Gazette*; signed "J.T."
  (Franklin writing as a reader)
- Occasion: a typo printed "died" instead of "dined" regarding
  Governor **Belcher** at Pontack's tavern
- Franklin catalogs famous typographical disasters for comic effect:
  - A Bible edition omitted "e" from "made" → "I am fearfully and
    wonderfully **mad**" (Psalm 139) — a preacher delivered a
    half-hour sermon on spiritual madness from this text
  - The **Company of Stationers** left out "not" from the Seventh
    Commandment → entire edition read *"Thou shalt commit Adultery"*
    — Crown revoked their printing patent
  - A Common Prayer Book printed "hanged" for "changed" in the
    Funeral Service: "We shall all be **hanged**"
  - *The Speech of James Prouse* (written night before execution)
    printed "I **died** a Protestant" instead of "I **die**"
- Moral: those who pass over others' faults in silence fare better
  when they make mistakes themselves; the censorious invite
  universal censure

## From his *Autobiography*, "He that would thrive, Must ask his Wife" (c. 1730)

- Context: Franklin married **Deborah Read Rogers** by common law
  in 1730; she died 1774
- Deborah was as disposed to **industry and frugality** as Franklin
  himself — assisted in business, folded pamphlets, tended shop,
  purchased rags for paper-makers
- Household kept no idle servants; table plain and simple
- Franklin's breakfast for a long time: bread & milk, eaten from
  a **twopenny earthen porringer** with a pewter spoon
- One morning he found a china bowl and silver spoon at breakfast —
  Deborah had bought them without his knowledge for 23 shillings
  — her justification: "her Husband deserved a Silver Spoon &
  China Bowl as well as any of his Neighbors"
- This was the **first Plate & China** in their house; wealth
  subsequently grew to several hundred pounds in value

> "mark how Luxury will enter Families, and make a Progress,
> in Spite of Principle."
> — *Autobiography*, Part 2

- **Key theme**: even the most frugal household is vulnerable to
  the social pressure of keeping up with neighbors; luxury
  creeps in through pride and comparison

## "An Apology for Printers" (June 10, 1731)

### The Printer's Unique Burden

- Franklin wrote this after giving "extraordinary Offence" by printing an
  advertisement with a controversial notation
- Core argument: printers occupy a uniquely impossible social position —
  unlike smiths, shoemakers, or merchants, they cannot do their trade
  without offending someone
- **"So many Men, so many Minds"** — opinions are as various as faces;
  no printer can please everyone
- The merchant can buy and sell with Jews, Turks, heretics, and infidels
  without giving offense; the printer cannot take a single job without
  angering somebody

### Ten Particulars in Defense of Printers

1. Opinions of men are almost infinitely varied
2. Printing's business is chiefly to do with men's opinions
3. Hence the peculiar unhappiness of the trade — nearly impossible to work
   without offending someone
4. Unreasonable to expect every printed thing to please you
5. **Free press principle:** "when Men differ in Opinion, both Sides ought
   equally to have the Advantage of being heard by the Publick; and that
   when Truth and Error have fair Play, the former is always an overmatch
   for the latter"
6. Printers develop professional detachment — they print with "utmost
   Calmness and Indifference," yet readers blame them as much as the author
7. Printers print contradictory things constantly; it's absurd to infer
   they endorse each item
8. If printers only printed what offended nobody, "there would be very
   little printed"
9. If vicious things are printed, it's because the public is "viciously and
   corruptly educated" — *Robin Hood's Songs* sold out in a year; *David's
   Psalms* sat unsold for twice that time
10. Franklin personally refused to print anything promoting vice or injuring
    persons, even at great financial cost — and made enemies for it

> "Poets loose half the Praise they would have got / Were it but known what
> they discreetly blot; / Yet are censur'd for every bad Line found in their
> Works with the utmost Severity."
> — Edmund Waller, quoted by Franklin

### The Ass Fable — You Cannot Please Everyone

- A man and his son take their ass to market; every passerby criticizes how
  they ride or walk — father rides alone (cruel), both ride (cruel to the
  ass), son rides alone (disrespectful), neither rides (stupid)
- The man finally says: "it grieves me much that we cannot please all these
  People; Let us throw the Ass over the next Bridge"
- **Moral:** Attempting to satisfy every critic leads to absurdity; Franklin
  will not "burn my Press and melt my Letters" to avoid controversy
- Conclusion: "I consider the Variety of Humours among Men, and despair of
  pleasing every Body; yet I shall not therefore leave off Printing"
- Published in *The Pennsylvania Gazette*

## "Alice Addertongue" — A Satire on Scandal (September 12, 1732)

### Franklin's Persona: The Professional Gossip

- Written under the fictional persona **Alice Addertongue**, a 35-year-old
  woman who has made gossip her life's work — a biting satire on scandal-
  mongering
- Alice's argument: those who condemn scandal-spreading as a crime are
  themselves guilty of scandal by accusing women of it — a self-defeating
  absurdity
- She freely admits her motivation: as a child she was punished for
  self-praise, so she redirected the impulse into disparaging others —
  "what great Difference can there be, between putting your self up, or
  putting your Neighbour down?"

> "Scandal, like other Virtues, is in part its own Reward, as it gives us
> the Satisfaction of making our selves appear better than others, or others
> no better than ourselves."
> — Alice Addertongue (Franklin)

### The Scandal Trade — Run Like a Business

- Alice runs her gossip operation with ledger-book precision:
  - **Daybook:** every article of defamation entered as a transaction
  - **Journal:** stories embellished with "probable Circumstances"
  - **Ledger:** the whole regularly posted
- Her maxim: "That no Trade can subsist without Returns" — she gives two
  stories for every one received
- Her technique for extracting gossip: praise a woman to her acquaintances
  → jealousy produces a flood of her failings; praise a man to his rivals
  → same result
- During elections: praise each candidate before his opponents — they
  supply the dirt freely

### The Self-Justifying Logic of a Gossip

- Alice reasons that since people conceal their vices, at most one-fifth
  of anyone's faults ever become known; therefore making a scandal "three
  times worse than it is" still understates the truth
- Her conclusion: "the worst that is said of us is only half what might be
  said, if all our Faults were seen"
- **Franklin's real target:** the smug self-righteousness of those who
  spread gossip while believing themselves righteous — Alice's "logic"
  is a reductio ad absurdum of rationalized vice
- Published in *The Pennsylvania Gazette*

## Poor Richard's Preface, 1733 — "My Regard for Mr. Titan Leeds"

### The Birth of Poor Richard

- Franklin admits the almanac is motivated by poverty ("I am excessive
  poor") and a nagging wife who threatened to "burn all my Books and
  Rattling-Traps" if he didn't make them profitable
- The real comic device: Franklin predicts the **death of rival almanac-
  maker Titan Leeds** on Oct. 17, 1733 at 3:29 PM — calculated from
  astrological conjunction of Sun and Mercury
- Leeds had disputed this calculation for 9 years; Franklin claims Leeds
  is "inclinable to agree" at last

### The Leeds Controversy

- **Titan Leeds** responded furiously in his 1734 almanac, calling
  Franklin a "Fool and a Liar" for predicting his death
- Leeds noted the exact minute Franklin predicted (3:22 PM) differed from
  his own survival time — proof, he said, of Franklin's error
- Franklin's satirical trap: Leeds's angry response *proved* he was dead,
  because the real **Titan Leeds** was too gentlemanly to use such language
- This was an explicit imitation of **Jonathan Swift's** "Isaac Bickerstaff"
  hoax against astrologer John Partridge (1708)

### Poor Richard's Sayings, 1733

> "The poor have little, beggars none, the rich too much, *enough* not one."

- "God works wonders now & then; / Behold! a Lawyer, an honest Man!"
- "Beware of the young Doctor & the old Barber."
- "Nothing more like a Fool, than a drunken Man."
- "Great Talkers, little Doers."
- "Hunger never saw bad bread."
- "A good Wife lost is God's gift lost."

## From the Autobiography — Language Learning (c. 1733)

### Franklin's Self-Taught Method

- Franklin mastered French to reading fluency in 1733; then Italian;
  then Spanish — all self-taught
- Key technique for Italian: agreed with a chess-playing acquaintance that
  the winner of each game could impose a grammar task on the loser —
  they "beat one another into" the language
- Discovery: after learning French, Italian, and Spanish, he opened a Latin
  Testament and understood far more than expected — the Romance languages
  had unlocked Latin retroactively

### Critique of Standard Language Pedagogy

- **Conventional wisdom:** start with Latin, then modern derivatives are
  easier
- **Franklin's counterargument:** this is like climbing a staircase from
  the top — possible, but illogical. Better to ascend from the bottom
- Practical problem: most students who begin with Latin quit before
  mastering it, leaving them with nothing useful
- **Franklin's proposal:** begin with French → Italian → Spanish; if a
  student quits at any point, they still have a living, useful language
- From *The Autobiography*, Part 3

## From the Autobiography — "Arriving at Moral Perfection" (c. 1733)

### The Project

- At age 27, Franklin "conceived the bold and arduous Project of arriving
  at moral Perfection" — to live without committing any fault
- Key insight from failure: "the mere speculative Conviction that it was
  our Interest to be compleatly virtuous, was not sufficient to prevent our
  Slipping" — **knowing right from wrong doesn't make you do right**
- Habit and inclination override reason; contrary habits must be broken
  and good ones established through systematic practice

### The Thirteen Virtues

Franklin identified **13 virtues**, each with a one-sentence precept:

1. **Temperance** — Eat not to Dullness. Drink not to Elevation.
2. **Silence** — Speak not but what may benefit others or yourself.
   Avoid trifling conversation.
3. **Order** — Let all your Things have their Places. Let each Part of
   your Business have its Time.
4. **Resolution** — Resolve to perform what you ought. Perform without
   fail what you resolve.
5. **Frugality** — Make no expense but to do good to others or yourself;
   waste nothing.
6. **Industry** — Lose no Time. Be always employ'd in something useful.
   Cut off all unnecessary Actions.
7. **Sincerity** — Use no hurtful Deceit. Think innocently and justly.
8. **Justice** — Wrong none, by doing Injuries, or omitting the Benefits
   that are your Duty.
9. **Moderation** — Avoid Extreams. Forbear resenting Injuries.
10. **Cleanliness** — Tolerate no Uncleanlness in Body, Cloaths or
    Habitation.
11. **Tranquillity** — Be not disturbed at Trifles, or at Accidents
    common or unavoidable.
12. **Chastity** — Rarely use Venery but for Health or Offspring.
13. **Humility** — Imitate Jesus and Socrates.

- **Humility was added last**, at the suggestion of a Quaker friend who
  told Franklin he was "generally thought proud" and overbearing in
  conversation

### The Virtue Tracking System

- Franklin made a small book with one page per virtue; ruled with 7 columns
  (days of week) and 13 rows (virtues); marked black spots for each
  violation
- **Weekly rotation:** focused on one virtue per week, aiming for a clear
  page; 13 virtues = 13 weeks = 4 complete courses per year
- Ordering was deliberate: **Temperance first** (produces clearness of head
  needed for the others); **Silence second** (Franklin wanted to break his
  habit of "Prattling, Punning & Joking"); Order, Resolution, Frugality,
  Industry followed to produce affluence and independence; Sincerity and
  Justice last

### Failure, Rationalization, and the "Speckled Ax"

- **Order** proved the hardest — Franklin had a good memory and had never
  been forced into orderly habits; his faults in it "vex'd me so much"
  he nearly gave up
- The **speckled ax parable:** a man asks a smith to grind his whole ax
  bright; grows tired of turning the wheel; finally accepts the ax as it
  is, saying *"I think I like a speckled Ax best"* — Franklin's metaphor
  for rationalizing unimproved faults
- Self-honesty: "I was by the Endeavor made a better and a happier Man
  than I otherwise should have been, if I had not attempted it" — the
  striving matters even without perfection

### The Humility Lesson — Tentative Language as Social Power

- Franklin forbade himself words implying fixed opinion: *certainly,
  undoubtedly* — replaced with *I conceive, I apprehend, I imagine, it so
  appears to me at present*
- Result: his opinions "procured a readier Reception and less Contradiction"
- He prevailed more easily with others when right, suffered less when wrong
- Credited this habit — more than any eloquence — with his influence in
  public affairs: "I was but a bad Speaker, never eloquent... and yet I
  generally carried my Points"

> "In reality, there is, perhaps, no one of our natural Passions so hard to
> subdue as *Pride*. Disguise it, struggle with it, beat it down, stifle it,
> mortify it as much as one pleases, it is still alive... for, even if I
> could conceive that I had compleatly overcome it, I should probably be
> proud of my Humility."
> — *The Autobiography*, Part 2

## From the Autobiography: "This Modesty in a Sect" (c. 1733)

### The Dunkers and Intellectual Humility

- Franklin contrasts the **Quakers** (who published fixed pacifist principles
  and became embarrassed when war required them to act otherwise) with
  the **Dunkers**, a German Baptist sect he admired for a remarkable
  epistemic stance
- Franklin knew **Michael Welfare**, one of the Dunkers' founders, who
  complained that rival sects calumniated them unfairly
- Franklin suggested they publish their Articles of Belief — Welfare
  explained why they refused:

> "When we were first drawn together as a Society, it had pleased God to
> enlighten our Minds so far, as to see that some Doctrines which we once
> esteemed Truths were Errors, & that others which we had esteemed Errors
> were real Truths. From time to time he has been pleased to afford us
> farther Light, and our Principles have been improving, & our Errors
> diminishing. Now we are not sure that we are arriv'd at the End of this
> Progression… and we fear that if we should once print our Confession of

- **Key analogy:** A man traveling in foggy weather sees fog everywhere

- The Quakers chose to quit public power rather than abandon their

- **Core idea:** Codifying belief freezes learning; the Dunkers were wise

## "A Man of Sense": Socrates–Crito Dialogue (Feb. 11, 1735)

*Published in The Pennsylvania Gazette; submitted to Franklin by a reader
who overheard the conversation.*

### What Makes a Man of Sense?

- Premise: the term **"Man of Sense"** is freely applied to any man being
  praised, but what does it actually require?
- Crito's initial position: a Man of Knowledge = a Man of Sense
- Socrates systematically eliminates candidate knowledge-types:
  - Games, music, dancing — no
  - Languages, logic, rhetoric — no (many know logic but cannot persuade;
    knowing terms of shoemaking ≠ knowing how to make shoes)
  - Mathematics, astronomy, natural philosophy — no (Franklin knew scientists
    who "acted very weakly" in managing their own lives)
- **Conclusion reached by elimination:** only knowledge of one's **true
  Interest** — what is best in all circumstances of human life to arrive
  at **Happiness** — constitutes a Man of Sense

### Vice and Knowledge Are Incompatible

- Key move: can a vicious man know his true interest, and still be vicious?
- Crito initially says yes — many men discourse well about virtue but act
  badly
- Socrates: talking well about virtue is like talking well about
  shoemaking — it proves nothing; you must *do it*
- Vice, by definition, damages the vicious man's happiness — so a vicious
  man demonstrably does NOT know his true interest, he only recites what
  he's "pick'd out of Books or Conversation" without it ever touching his
  heart
- **Resolution:** Vicious men do not qualify as Men of Sense; they are
  "Fool, a Dunce, and a Blockhead" regardless of other learning

### Allowance for Human Fallibility

- Socrates allows: a Man of Sense need not be morally perfect
- Key analogy: the best arithmetician can make an error in a long account —
  but **knowing how to find and fix the error** is what makes him an
  arithmetician; the man who always blunders and can't correct himself
  is not one
- A Man of Sense may slip morally — but he must recognize the failing and
  diligently work to correct it; the habitually vicious man cannot claim
  the title

> "It seems to follow then, that the vicious Man, tho' Master of many
> Sciences, must needs be an ignorant and foolish Man… the Vicious Man
> however learned, cannot be a *Man of Sense*, but is a Fool, a Dunce,
> and a Blockhead."
> — *The Pennsylvania Gazette*

- **Practical implication:** The **Science of Virtue** is "of more worth,
  and of more consequence to his Happiness than all the rest put together"
  — Franklin's hierarchy of knowledge places ethics above all technical
  learning

## "A Man Does Not Act Contrary to His Interest by Marrying" (Mar. 4, 1735)

*A reader's letter replying to anti-marriage verses published in Franklin's
Gazette. The writer claims to be old, having had three wives.*

### Defense of Marriage Against a Cynical "Piece of Advice"

- The anti-marriage verses argued: marriage is bondage, women are tyrants,
  only fools wed, a married man loses liberty and friends
- The letter-writer (Franklin likely ventriloquizing or endorsing) rejects
  each claim:

**On the bachelor dying alone:**
- An old bachelor dying without issue has extinguished the line of life from
  Adam through all generations — "he has wrapt up and hid his Talent in a
  Napkin" — and may justly fear punishment for it

**On marriage as bondage:**
- Marriage is like tending a **"beautiful and fertile Garden"** — the cares
  are the pleasures; "Human Planting" (raising children) is more honorable
  than gardening since the plants are more excellent in nature

**On women as tyrants:**
- The writer notes "there are in the World infinitely more He-Tyrants than
  She-Ones" — the anti-marriage adviser's aversion may stem from his own
  mother being a **Xantippe** to a man who was no Socrates
- A man who commands in "a proper Manner" will find his wife knows how to
  show proper obedience

**On marriage harming financial interest:**
- "What we get, the Women save" — a married man fixes his attention on
  business more steadily; the idleness of men, not the extravagance of
  women, is what ruins families
- A man without a wife cannot leave home without neglecting his business;
  **a wife increases liberty, not reduces it**

- **Franklin's consistent view:** marriage is economically rational,
  morally required, and practically freeing — the cynical bachelor view is
  self-serving rationalization

## Conclusion of "Old Mistresses Apologie" Essay (p. 61)

### Marriage as Social & Economic Asset
- A married man with a trustworthy wife can conduct business with "much more
  Freedom" — she holds down the fort while he operates abroad
- Marriage does NOT cost friends — it adds a wife's entire family network,
  ready to assist the new couple
- A man with wife and children is **more creditworthy**: seen as more settled,
  under greater obligations to behave honestly "for his Family's Sake"
- Franklin (as "A.A.") dismisses the anti-marriage adviser's claim that
  "the best of the Sex are no better than Plagues" — calls it the bitter
  complaint of a failed bachelor projecting his own inadequacy
- Satirical diagnosis: such men, having failed in early romantic attempts,
  convince themselves they are "really Impotent" rather than face the truth,
  and become advisers against marriage like "the Fox who could not use his
  Tail" — urging others that tails are useless
- Source: *The Pennsylvania Gazette*

## Poor Richard's Preface, 1740

### The **Titan Leeds** Ghost Letter — Extended Satire
- In **1733**, Poor Richard predicted the death of rival almanac-maker
  **Titan Leeds** on October 17 at 3:29 PM — Leeds did die, but rivals
  **W.B.** and **A.B.** continued publishing almanacs in his name for years
- By 1739, they finally admitted his death but claimed he pre-supplied
  "Calculations for 7 Years to come"
- Franklin's comic masterstroke: claims he fell asleep and woke to find a
  **letter from the ghost of Titan Leeds** written through his hand
- The ghost's letter: confirms he did die precisely at the hour predicted
  (only 5 min. 53 sec. variance); denies furnishing any posthumous
  calculations; says the rival almanac is "no more mine than 'tis yours"
- Ghost explains how spirits work: they wander freely, enter imaginations,
  give hints waking or sleeping — he entered through Richard's left nostril,
  found the nerves controlling his right hand, and wrote the letter
- Ghost offers three "Signs" to prove it: (1) rival J.J—n will convert to
  Catholicism, (2) friend W.B—t will be sober 9 hours, (3) W.B. and A.B.
  will publish yet another almanac in Leeds' name despite everything
- Franklin closes: "I am convinc'd that the above Letter is genuine. If
  the Reader doubts of it, let him carefully observe the three Signs."
- **Significance**: masterclass in satirical logic — uses rival's own
  absurdity (publishing for a dead man) to mock them while maintaining
  a deadpan persona

## Letter to a Young Friend: "Prefer Old Women to Young Ones" (June 25, 1745)

### Franklin's Case for Marriage (Primary Argument)
- **Marriage as completeness:** Man alone is "an incomplete Animal. He resembles
  the odd Half of a Pair of Scissars." Man and woman united make "the compleat
  human Being."
- Each supplies the other's deficiency: she gains his "Force of Body and Strength
  of Reason"; he gains her "Softness, Sensibility and acute Discernment."
- A prudent wife + industry in profession + her good economy = "a Fortune sufficient"

### The Paradox: Eight Arguments for Older Women
Franklin acknowledges the counsel seems strange, then systematically defends it:

1. **Knowledge & conversation:** Older women have "more Knowledge of the World,"
   minds "better stor'd with Observations" — their conversation is "more improving
   and more lastingly agreeable"
2. **Utility over beauty:** When women "cease to be handsome, they study to be good."
   They supply "the Diminution of Beauty by an Augmentation of Utility" — learn
   "a 1000 Services small and great," especially when you are sick
3. **No risk of children** — "irregularly produc'd may be attended with much
   Inconvenience"
4. **Discretion & reputation:** More experience makes them "more prudent and discreet
   in conducting an Intrigue to prevent Suspicion" — safer for both parties' reputations
5. **Physical equivalence:** Age affects the face and neck first, lower parts last —
   "in the dark all Cats are grey" — pleasure is "at least equal, and frequently
   superior, every Knack being by Practice capable of Improvement"
6. **Less sin:** "debauching a Virgin may be her Ruin, and make her for Life unhappy"
7. **Less guilt:** Making a young girl miserable produces "frequent bitter Reflections;
   none of which can attend the making an old Woman happy"
8. **"They are so grateful!!"**

- Closes: "Thus much for my Paradox. But still I advise you to marry directly."
- Signed: *B. F.* — source: *Writings*

## The Speech of Miss Polly Baker (April 1747)

### Context & Setup
- **Miss Polly Baker** prosecuted for the fifth time for having a "Bastard Child"
  in Connecticut — the speech so moved the court they dispensed with punishment;
  one judge married her the next day
- Franklin authored this as a **fictional rhetorical exercise** (published in
  *Writings*) — a masterpiece of ironic legal argument

### Core Arguments
- **Unjust law, not immoral woman:** She has "brought Five fine Children into the
  World, at the Risque of my Life" — maintained them "by my own Industry, without
  burthening the Township"
- "Can it be a Crime (in the Nature of Things I mean) to add to the Number of
  the King's Subjects, in a new Country that really wants People?"
- Her betrayer — the man who first seduced her with a promise of marriage — is
  now "a Magistrate of this County," while she is whipped. The law punishes her
  misfortune and honors her betrayer.
- **Religious argument:** If she truly sinned, leave punishment to God — she is
  already "excluded from all the Comforts of your Church Communion." Civil and
  divine punishment together are double jeopardy.
- **Natural law:** "how can it be believed, that Heaven is angry at my having
  Children, when, to the little done by me towards it, God has been pleased to
  add his divine Skill... and crown'd it by furnishing them with rational and
  immortal Souls?"
- **Bachelors as greater offenders:** Men who never marry out of "mean Fear of
  the Expence of a Family" leave "unproduced... Hundreds of their Posterity to
  the Thousandth Generation" — this is "little better than Murder." They should
  be compelled to marry or pay "double the Fine of Fornication every Year."
- **Closing claim:** She has fulfilled "the first and great Command of Nature, and
  of Nature's God, Increase and multiply" — deserves "a Statue erected to my
  Memory" instead of a whipping.

## Advice to a Young Tradesman, "Remember that *time* is Money" (c. 1748)

### Franklin's Core Financial Principles
This letter to "Friend A.B." is one of Franklin's most concentrated economic essays.

**The Four Remembers:**

1. ***Time* is Money** — idle time is not free; a man who earns 10 shillings/day
   and idles half a day has spent 5 shillings *plus* any out-of-pocket expense.
   Idleness has a real cost equal to foregone earnings.

2. ***Credit* is Money** — money left in your hands past its due date is a loan
   from the creditor; "he gives me the Interest, or so much as I can make of it
   during that Time." Good credit is effectively capital.

3. **Money is prolific** — "Money can beget Money, and its Offspring can beget more."
   Five shillings turned is six; compounding accelerates. "He that murders a Crown,
   destroys all it might have produc'd, even Scores of Pounds." — killing a
   breeding sow destroys all future offspring.

4. **Six pounds a year is a groat a day** — small daily savings, unnoticed, give
   a man of credit the effective use of £100. "So much in Stock briskly turn'd by
   an industrious Man, produces great Advantage."

### Credit & Reputation Management
- **Punctuality as capital:** "The good Paymaster is Lord of another Man's Purse."
  Pay exactly when promised; this power lets you "raise all the Money his Friends
  can spare" at any time.
- Creditors notice everything: "The Sound of your Hammer at Five in the Morning
  or Nine at Night, heard by a Creditor, makes him easy Six Months longer. But if
  he sees you at a Billiard Table, or hears your Voice in a Tavern... he sends for
  his Money the next Day."
- Finer clothes than the creditor wears "shocks his Pride, and he duns you."
- **Partial repayment is better than none:** Creditors prefer ten voluntary partial
  payments over one forced lump sum — shows you are mindful and honest.
- **Track expenses:** Keep an exact account — you'll "discover how wonderfully
  small trifling Expences mount up to large Sums."

### The Formula
> "The Way to Wealth... depends chiefly on two Words, *industry* and *frugality*;
> i.e., Waste neither *Time* nor *Money*, but make the best Use of both."
> — *An Old Tradesman* (Franklin), c. 1748

## Letter to Peter Collinson: "Whenever We Attempt to Mend the Scheme of Providence" (May 9, 1753)

### On Labor, Welfare, and Human Nature

- **Observation**: English laborers who emigrate to America, where wages are higher, become *less*
  industrious in proportion — their industry diminishes as wages rise
- **Contrast**: German immigrants retain their "habitual Industry and Frugality," accumulate wealth
  and become prosperous despite higher wages
- **Franklin's hypothesis**: England's **Poor Laws** (compelling the rich to maintain the poor)
  create **dependency** that reduces incentive to save for old age
- Notes that Protestant countries in Europe have more industrious poor than Catholic ones — more
  charitable foundations may render people less self-reliant
- Warns against "fighting against the order of God and Nature, which perhaps has appointed Want
  and Misery as proper Punishments for, and Cautions against Idleness and Extravagancy"

### The Unintended Consequences Argument

- **New England blackbird example**: colonists made laws to destroy blackbirds (deemed harmful to
  corn) → blackbirds diminished → worms that blackbirds had eaten proliferated and devoured
  the grass → loss in grass far exceeded savings in corn
- Principle: **"Whenever we attempt to mend the scheme of Providence... we had need be very
  circumspect lest we do more harm than Good"**

### On the "Natural" Human Preference for Ease

- **Transylvanian Tartar anecdote**: asked Franklin why so many nations (Tartars, American Indians,
  Africans) preferred wandering lazy lives over civilized arts. His reply: *"God make man for
  Paradise, he make him for to live lazy; man make God angry, God turn him out of Paradise,
  and bid him work... man want to go to Paradise again, he want to live lazy; so all mankind
  love lazy."*
- Franklin finds this plausible: hope of one day being free from labor + fear of penury are the
  "main-springs of most people's industry"
- **American Indians as evidence**: nearly all their wants supplied by nature with minimal labor;
  even Indians educated in English colleges returned to the wilderness — found civilized life
  too burdensome. One man inherited a good estate, but relinquished it to a younger brother,
  keeping only "a gun and a match-Coat," and returned to the wilderness.
- Whites captured young by Indians, even after ransom, often "become disgusted with our manner
  of life" and escape back to the woods at the first opportunity

### The Six Nations / College Anecdote

- English Commissioners at a treaty offered to educate six Indian youths at their college (free,
  with a foundation for Indians). The **Six Nations** politely declined: previous Indian
  graduates returned "absolutely good for nothing, being neither acquainted with the true methods
  of killing deer, catching Beaver, or surprizing an enemy." Counter-offered: send a dozen
  English children to **Onondago** — the Council would educate them properly and "make men of them"

### On German Immigration to Pennsylvania

- Germans arriving are "generally of the most ignorant Stupid Sort" — credulous when misled,
  suspicious when honest — and English cannot reach them via press or pulpit due to language barrier
- By 1753: of Pennsylvania's 6 printing houses, 2 entirely German, 2 half-German, only 2 entirely
  English; one German newspaper, signs in streets increasingly German-only
- Franklin's concern: Germans "will so out number us, that... our Government will become precarious"
- The French were actively settling Germans in Illinois Country as a potential fifth column
- Franklin's **solution**: not to exclude Germans (who have "exemplary industry and frugality") but
  to distribute them more equally, establish English schools, and stop ship owners from "sweeping
  German Gaols" to fill passenger manifests

### On Britain's Future

- Quotes **Milton's** *Paradise Lost* on nations that descend so low from virtue that justice and
  a "fatal curse" deprive them of outward liberty — their inward liberty already lost
- Expresses hope Britain will act as "affectionate parent" rather than oppressor — warns that a
  "Mortification begun in the Foot may spread upwards to the destruction of the nobler parts"

## "The Way to Wealth" / Father Abraham's Speech
### Poor Richard's Preface, 1758

### Setup & Frame Narrative
- Franklin's most famous piece — later reprinted worldwide as a standalone pamphlet
- Fictional frame: **Richard Saunders** (Poor Richard) overhears an old man
  called **Father Abraham** address a crowd at a merchant goods auction
- The crowd complains about **bad times and heavy taxes**;
  Father Abraham quotes Poor Richard throughout his entire speech
- Ironic twist at the end: the crowd approves Father Abraham's wisdom,
  then immediately buys extravagantly at the auction anyway —
  "just as if it had been a common Sermon"
- Franklin's self-deprecating admission: not a tenth of the wisdom
  was his own — he called it *"Gleanings from the Sense of all Ages and Nations"*

### The Real Taxes: Idleness, Pride, Folly
- Government taxes are heavy, but we are taxed far more by ourselves:
  - *Taxed twice as much by **Idleness***
  - *Three times as much by **Pride***
  - *Four times as much by **Folly***
- These internal taxes cannot be eased by any government abatement

> *God helps them that help themselves.*
> — Poor Richard, 1733

### On Industry
- Wishing and hoping change nothing — action does
- **"Industry need not wish"** — a trade is an estate; a calling is an office
  of profit and honor, but only if worked diligently
- At the working man's house, Hunger looks in but dares not enter;
  the bailiff and constable also stay away

> *Industry pays Debts, while Despair encreaseth them.*

> *Diligence is the Mother of Good-luck.*

- Work while it is today — **"One To-day is worth two To-morrows"**
- The lazy man never earns true leisure; leisure belongs to the diligent
- **"A Life of Leisure and a Life of Laziness are two Things"**
- Trouble springs from idleness; industry gives comfort, plenty, respect

> *Fly Pleasures, and they'll follow you.*

- Key: be not just industrious but **steady, settled, and careful**;
  oversee your own affairs with your own eyes
- **"Three Removes is as bad as a Fire"** — constant relocation destroys wealth
- **"Keep thy Shop, and thy Shop will keep thee"**
- **"The Eye of a Master will do more Work than both his Hands"**
- **"Want of Care does us more Damage than Want of Knowledge"**
- The famous cascade of neglect:

> *For want of a Nail the Shoe was lost; for want of a Shoe the Horse was lost;
> and for want of a Horse the Rider was lost.*

### On Frugality
- Industry alone is not enough — must add **Frugality**
- Spain's example: the Indies did not make Spain rich because
  **outgoings exceeded incomes** — a powerful macro lesson
- *"If you would be wealthy, think of Saving as well as of Getting"*
- Small extravagances compound: *"Many a Little makes a Mickle"*
- **"A small Leak will sink a great Ship"**
- Warning against "cheap" purchases: items you don't need are never cheap

> *Buy what thou hast no Need of, and ere long thou shalt sell thy Necessaries.*

> *Many have been ruined by buying good Pennyworths.*

- **Artificial wants** multiply beyond natural wants; luxury goods
  are not necessaries, barely conveniences — yet people sacrifice food for them
- *"For one poor Person, there are an hundred indigent"* — the genteel
  reduced to poverty by extravagance, forced to borrow from those
  they formerly despised

> *A Ploughman on his Legs is higher than a Gentleman on his Knees.*

### On Pride of Appearance
- Pride of dress is a "Curse" — buying one fine thing demands buying ten more
  to match; *"'Tis easier to suppress the first Desire, than to satisfy
  all that follow it"*
- **"Pride breakfasted with Plenty, dined with Poverty, and supped with Infamy"**
- Pride of appearance: promotes no health, eases no pain,
  increases no merit, creates envy, hastens misfortune

### On Debt — Liberty at Stake
- Running into debt for luxuries = **voluntary enslavement**

> *You give to another Power over your Liberty.*

- Debt leads to shame, fear, deceit, then lying:
  *"The second Vice is Lying, the first is running in Debt"*
- **"Lying rides upon Debt's Back"**
- A creditor has authority to confine you to gaol for life
  or sell you as a servant — yet people accept this tyranny
  for the sake of fine clothes

> *'Tis hard for an empty Bag to stand upright.*

- Creditors have better memories than debtors; the due date arrives
  before you are prepared
- **"The Borrower is a Slave to the Lender, and the Debtor to the Creditor"**
- Franklin's prescription: *Be industrious and free; be frugal and free*

> *Rather go to Bed supperless than rise in Debt.*

### Closing of Father Abraham's Speech
- Doctrine is reason and wisdom, but don't depend entirely on industry
  and prudence alone — all can be blasted without the blessing of Heaven
- Reference to **Job**: suffered, then was prosperous — humility required
- Franklin's concluding irony: *"Experience keeps a dear School, but Fools
  will learn in no other"*

> *They that won't be counselled, can't be helped.*

- Signed by **Richard Saunders**, July 7, 1757
- Franklin's personal resolution after hearing Father Abraham:
  went away determined to wear his old coat a little longer

## Letter to John Baskerville: On Typography Prejudice (c. 1760)

- **John Baskerville**: prominent English printer and typeface designer
  whose typefaces were considered too thin and elegant by some
- A gentleman told Franklin that Baskerville's letters would "blind all
  Readers in the Nation" — strokes too thin, hurting the eye
- Franklin's experiment in **cognitive bias**: he secretly substituted
  a sample of **Caslon's** type (the familiar standard) labeled as
  Baskerville's, then asked the same gentleman to identify the flaws
- The gentleman confidently pointed out all the "painful disproportions"
  in what was actually the very type he had read comfortably his entire life —
  including the type his own book was printed in
- Franklin's lesson: **prejudice is pre-loaded**, not derived from actual
  perception; we find fault where we expect to find it

## Letter to David Hume: Lightning Rods and Maypoles (May 19, 1762)

- Addressed to philosopher **David Hume** — shows Franklin's wide intellectual
  circle in Britain
- Technical discussion of **lightning rod design**: recommends lead for
  underground portions (less rust than iron); conductor should reach
  a well or distant grounding rod to prevent explosion from moisture
  rarefaction in the earth (he had observed water in glass tubes
  exploding with the force of gunpowder when subjected to electrical discharge)
- Explains why trees **shatter into splinters** during lightning strikes:
  rapid rarefaction of sap in longitudinal capillary pores
- On Hume's compliment that Franklin brought "Gold and Wisdom" to Scotland:
  Franklin deflects with an economics observation — value derives from
  scarcity relative to demand; gold was so plentiful in Solomon's time
  it had no more value than street stones
  - Implication: Scotland has such a **"Plenty of Wisdom"** already,
    his own supply would fetch a better price elsewhere

### The Maypole Anecdote
- A town dispute between Church People (pro-Maypole) and Puritans (anti-Maypole)
- Mayor's pragmatic Solomonic ruling:
  *"You that are for having no Maypole shall have no Maypole;
  and you that are for having a Maypole shall have a Maypole.
  Get about your Business and let me hear no more of this Quarrel."*
- Franklin uses this as a model of practical dispute resolution —
  let each party have what they want, stop arguing

## *Pax quaeitur Bello* — "Peace through War" (Jan 26, 1766)

### Context
- Historian **Edmund Morgan** on Franklin's satire:
  *"Only when men in power acted foolishly, adopting policies harmful to
  others, did he lift the restraints on both his passions and his wit."*
- Written for *The Public Advertiser* under the pseudonym **"Pacificus"**
  (ironic: "the peaceful one")
- Political context: the **Stamp Act** controversy — Britain taxing
  American colonies without representation

### The Satirical Proposal (Swift-style)
- Argues sarcastically that Americans will never submit to the Stamp Tax
  voluntarily because they are descendants of English freedom-lovers —
  **Pymms, Hampdens** — nursed in "Old English Principles"
- Proposes a "cheap" and "economical" plan to force compliance:
  1. Raise **2,000 Highlanders** (preferably those who rebelled in 1745,
     Roman Catholics preferred — the more alien the better)
  2. Transport them to Quebec; combine with Canadians
     (natural enemies of the colonists) for a force of 5–6,000
  3. Hire **"Savages"** on the frontier with payments and scalp bounties
  4. March on the colonies without warning
  5. **Burn all colonial capitals to the ground**;
     cut the throats of all inhabitants — men, women, children — and scalp them
  6. Destroy all shipping to prevent smuggling
  7. Anyone who still refuses to buy stamped paper: **500–600 lashes**
     "in a cold frosty Morning"
  8. If depopulation is a concern: repopulate with British convicts
- Signed "Yours, PACIFICUS" — the savage irony unmistakable
- Target: British ministers advocating coercive colonial policy

## The Stamp-Act: "A Red Hot Iron in His Hand" (March 23, 1767)

- Published in *The Pennsylvania Chronicle*
- Argues against a proposed compromise: requiring Americans to pay back-stamps
  for the period between the Act's commencement (Nov 1765) and Repeal (May 1766)
- Franklin's argument: this partial demand is **worse than nothing** —
  it keeps all the animosity alive for a trivial sum,
  destroys the goodwill gained by Repeal, and will be just as impossible
  to enforce as the original Act

### The "Red Hot Iron" Analogy
- A Frenchman on the Pont-Neuf accosts English strangers very politely,
  holding a **red-hot iron**, and asks: "Do me the Favour to let me have
  the Honour of thrusting this hot Iron into your Backside?"
- When the stranger refuses and threatens him, the Frenchman replies:
  *"If you do not chuse it, I do not insist upon it. But at least, you will
  in Justice have the Goodness to pay me something for the heating of my Iron."*
- The analogy is precise: Britain imposes an unlawful tax, Americans resist,
  Britain repeals it — then demands **compensation for the cost of imposing it**

## New Fables (January 2, 1770)

*[Franklin uses animal fables to mock British officials who couldn't see
that the American colonies had grown up and deserved self-representation.]*

### Fable I: The Cows and the Avaricious Farmer
- Farmer milked cows abundantly, then mowed their grass for profit,
  leaving them to find their own food — yet still expected full milk yield
- The cows, offended by his unreasonableness, resolved to **suckle one
  another** instead
- **Allegory:** Britain exploiting colonies while refusing to sustain them,
  then wondering why loyalty dried up

### Fable II: The Eagle and the Cat
- Eagle (King of Birds) mistakes a cat for a rabbit, seizes it, carries
  it aloft intending to prey on it
- Cat claws into eagle's breast; eagle tries to drop her, but cat holds
  on — eagle forced to set her down exactly where he picked her up
- **Allegory:** Britain seizing the colonies will find them clinging back
  and forcing an undignified retreat

### Fable III: The Lion's Whelp and the Mastiff
- Young lion shipped to America as a gift; allowed to roam freely as
  it seemed tame and harmless
- A stately **English Mastiff** (the captain's dog) bullied the lion —
  stole its food, evicted it from its bed
- The voyage was long; the lion grew daily in size and strength until
  it was a match for the mastiff — who then received a stunning blow
  from the lion's paw
- Mastiff regretted he had not "secured its Friendship rather than
  provoked its Enmity"
- **Allegory:** Britain's contemptuous treatment of a growing America
  will end badly for Britain — the colony it once bullied will become
  strong enough to strike back

> "regretting that he had not rather secured its Friendship than provoked its Enmity."
> — *The Public Advertise*

## From the Autobiography: "Living One's Life Over Again" (c. 1771)

- Franklin says if given the choice, he would live his life again —
  only asking the same advantage an author has in a **second edition**:
  to correct the faults of the first
- Even if denied the chance to change things, he'd still accept the
  offer — just to live it again
- Since actual repetition is impossible, the next best thing is
  **Recollection** — and writing it down makes that recollection durable

> "…the Thing most like living one's Life over again, seems to be a
> *Recollection* of that Life; and to make that Recollection as durable
> as possible, the putting it down in Writing."
> — *The Autobiography*, Part 1

## From the Autobiography: "There Are Croakers in Every Country" (c. 1771)

- **"Croakers"** = perpetual pessimists who prophesy ruin regardless of
  evidence
- Example: **Samuel Mickle**, an elderly man of Philadelphia with a
  "wise look and very grave manner of speaking," stopped Franklin at
  his new print shop and told him the venture would fail — Philadelphia
  was a sinking city, people nearly bankrupt, prosperity illusory
- Franklin was left "half melancholy" — had he met Mickle earlier, he
  might never have opened the shop
- Mickle continued his doom-saying for years, **refusing to buy a house
  in Philadelphia** because the city was doomed
- Franklin's payoff: he lived to see Mickle pay **five times as much**
  for a house as he could have bought it for when his croaking began
- **Core lesson:** Pessimism dressed in gravitas is not wisdom — it is
  paralysis that costs people real money and real opportunity

## Letter to Anna Mordaunt Shipley: "What Sort of Husbands Would Be Fittest"
### (August 13, 1771)

*[Written to the wife of Bishop **Jonathan Shipley** of St. Asaph,
recounting a charming carriage conversation with their 11-year-old
daughter **Kitty Shipley**.]*

- Franklin escorts Kitty home from Twyford; they amuse themselves by
  planning ideal husbands for each Shipley sister
- **Georgiana:** a Country Gentleman who loves travel, books, has a
  good estate and sits in Parliament
- **Betsy:** must not be a rough, passionate man — "none of 'em must be
  so; for then they would not be good Husbands" — no clergymen or
  officers (income disappears when they die, leaving nothing for the family)
- **Emily** (Kitty's favorite): must have the Duke — Kitty negotiates
  hard, forces Franklin to cede the Duke to Emily and settle for an
  Earl for Anna-Maria
- **Anna-Maria:** a rich man with a large family to manage — she is
  good at managing, helps Mama, can oversee bills and family business;
  awarded an Earl
- **Kitty herself:** prefers an old General of 70 or 80 — she likes
  old men, they all like her; she'll be "a rich young Widow" at 30
- Franklin's playful counter: take a young officer and let him grow old
  in your hands, so you'll like him "better and better every Year"
- Kitty refuses: he must already be old

> "He must be an old Man of 70 or 80, and take me when I am about 30:
> And then you know I may be a rich young Widow."

- **Franklin's charm on display:** completely delights in children's
  company; writes with warmth and wit about a trifling conversation —
  the letter is itself a demonstration of his social genius

## Letter to Kitty Shipley: "Alas! Poor Mungo!" (September 26, 1772)

*[Kitty's pet squirrel, **Mungo**, has died. Franklin writes a mock-elegy
in a broken, sighing style he calls "monumental."]*

- Franklin invents a formal elegy for the squirrel — not prose, not
  verse, but an interrupted style with sighs baked in
- The squirrel had a good education, had "travell'd far, and seen much
  of the World" — therefore deserves more than a common epitaph
- The moral, addressed to "Subjects, Sons, Squirrels or Daughters":

> "That apparent *Restraint* may be real *Protection*, Yielding Peace,
> Plenty, and Security."

- Mungo left his safe wired cage seeking more freedom — and was killed
  by a dog in the wild
- Franklin contrasts his serious elegy with the crude alternative:
  *"Here Skugg / Lies snug / As a Bug / In a Rug"* — and notes that
  some people of little feeling would think that sufficient
- **Subtext:** **liberty without protection is dangerous** — the same
  theme Franklin was exploring with the colonies

## Rules for Reducing a Great Empire to a Small One (September 11, 1773)

*[Franklin's masterpiece of political satire — 20 ironic "rules" for
British ministers on how to lose the American colonies. Published in
The Public Advertiser.]*

### Core Satirical Premise
- Addressed to ministers managing "extensive Dominions" that have
  become troublesome to govern
- A great Empire, "like a great Cake, is most easily diminished at
  the Edges" — start with the **remotest Provinces**

### The 20 Rules (summary of key ones)

**Rule I:** Focus first on remote provinces; once rid of them, the
next may follow in order

**Rule II:** Never incorporate provinces into the Mother Country —
deny them same rights, same commercial privileges, govern by severer
laws they had no voice in making; this cuts the dough for easy breaking

**Rule III:** If provinces built themselves at their own expense, grew
your armies and commerce and naval power — **forget it all, or resent
it** as if they'd done you injury. If they are Whigs who love liberty,
punish those principles once revolution succeeds — such principles are
"odious and abominable"

**Rule IV:** Always presume colonies are **inclined to revolt** no
matter how peacefully they submit. Quarter troops among them; their
insolence will provoke mobs, and bayonets can suppress them — thus
converting suspicion into reality

**Rule V:** The **Choice of Rulers** matters enormously to colonial
opinion of the King. Therefore: appoint ruined prodigals, broken
gamblers, and stock-jobbers as Governors. Use quarrelsome lawyers as
judges. The ignorant, wrong-headed, and insolent will serve best —
"so much the better"

**Rule VI:** When colonists bring complaints of oppression to the
capital, **punish such Suitors** with long delay, enormous expense,
and a final judgment in favor of the Oppressor — this prevents future
complaints and encourages governors to further acts of injustice

**Rule VII:** When odious governors have "crammed their Coffers" and
must flee for safety, **recall and reward them with Pensions** — this
encourages new governors to follow the same practices

**Rule VIII:** When colonists voluntarily give generous war aid,
**despise their voluntary Grants** and resolve to harass them with
novel taxes. When they petition Parliament for redress — flout their
claims, reject their petitions, refuse even to hear them. "None ever
forgave Contempt"

**Rule IX:** When laying taxes, **never regard the heavy burdens**
colonists already bear — defending frontiers, building roads, churches,
bridges. Forget the monopoly profits your merchants extract from them.
Declare that your power to tax them has no limits, so taking a shilling
implies the right to take all nineteen

**Rule X:** If colonists still claim **constitutional Liberty of Person
and Conscience** — habeas corpus, trial by jury, freedom of religion —
annihilate this comfort with infinite commercial regulations, seizure
of property for any failure, admiralty courts with judges appointed at
your pleasure. Then declare opposition to your edicts is **Treason**,
transportable to the Metropolis for trial. Pass the Declaratory Act:
Parliament has "full Power and Authority to bind the unrepresented
Provinces *in all cases whatsoever*"

**Rule XI:** Send indiscreet, ill-bred, insolent revenue officers to
collect the taxes. Pay them from the extorted revenue; exempt them from
local taxes. If officers are suspected of any sympathy for the people —
discard them; if they provoke beatings — **promote them**

**Rule XII:** Misapply tax revenues — give them as pensions and salaries
to governors who distinguished themselves by enmity to the people

**Rule XIII:** Pay governors directly from the Crown so they no longer
depend on the goodwill of local assemblies — ensuring they need feel no
obligation to treat people kindly or do justice

**Rule XIV:** When provincial Parliaments dare claim rights, harass
them with **repeated Dissolutions** — adjourn them to remote villages,
exercise prerogative to increase discontent and dis-affection

**Rule XV:** Convert brave naval officers into petty customs men who
prey on colonial trade in peacetime worse than enemies did in war.
Let boat crews land on farms, rob orchards, steal pigs and poultry —
and call any colonial resistance **High Treason and Rebellion**; send
fleets and armies, threaten to hang them

**Rule XVI:** If told of colonial discontent, never believe it is
general or that you caused it. Redress no grievance. Grant no request.
Take all information from governors in enmity with the people. If a
few agitators are the problem — **catch and hang them**, and the
"Blood of Martyrs shall work Miracles in favour of your Purpose"

**Rule XVII:** If rival nations rejoice at your colonial disunion and
privately stimulate you to harsher measures while publishing colonial
complaints — don't be alarmed. "You all mean the same Thing"

**Rule XVIII:** If a colony built a fortress at its own expense —
**betray it into your hands**, convert it to a citadel to intimidate
them; seize the arms they contributed to your conquests — "Ingratitude
added to Robbery"

**Rule XIX:** Send armies under pretense of protection, but demolish
frontier forts and march troops into the interior — letting savages
attack the frontiers while troops are protected by colonists. This
confirms you are "no longer fit to govern them"

**Rule XX (Final):** Give the General of your army in the provinces
unconstitutional powers, free from control of even your civil
governors. All fortresses, all troops under his command. Then —
following the Roman imperial precedent — he may set himself up for
himself. And all the Provinces will join him immediately.

> "…you will that Day (if you have not done it sooner) get rid of the
> Trouble of governing them, and all the Plagues attending their Commerce
> and Connection from thenceforth and for ever."
> — *The Public Advertise*

### Why This Piece Matters
- Written **two years before Lexington and Concord** — Franklin is
  describing exactly what Britain was already doing, rule by rule
- The satirical form (ironic instructions) allows Franklin to be
  comprehensive and damning without being legally seditious
- Demonstrates Franklin's unique weapon: he exposes folly not through
  anger but through **devastating precision and wit**

## Conversation of a Company of Ephemerae (c. 1778)

*[A philosophical fable written in France, addressed to **Madame Brillant**
(likely Madame Brillon de Jouy, Franklin's Passy neighbor).]*

- Franklin observes a community of **ephemerae** (mayflies) at the
  Moulin Joly garden — creatures whose entire generations are born and
  die within a single day
- The young ones argue heatedly about two foreign musicians — spending
  their short lives on trivia, "seemingly as regardless of the shortness
  of their life as if they had been sure of living a month"
- Franklin's observation: "Happy people, you live certainly under a
  wise, just, and mild government, since you have no public grievances
  to complain of" — only art disputes
- He then notices an old grey-headed ephemera, soliloquizing alone on
  a separate leaf — and records his speech
- The old ephemera notes that philosophers of his race believed the
  world (the Moulin Joly) could not last more than **eighteen hours** —
  and the sun has clearly been declining toward the ocean...
- **Allegory of mortality and human scale:** just as the ephemera's
  "world" and "history" are compressed into hours, human civilization
  and history look similarly brief from a larger perspective
- **Franklin's deeper theme:** Even an eighteen-hour life well-lived
  has worth — a meditation on aging written when Franklin himself was
  in his 70s in Paris

> "Happy people, thought I, you live certainly under a wise, just, and
> mild government, since you have no public grievances to complain of,
> nor any other subject of contention but the perfections or
> imperfections of foreign music."

<!-- pp. 161-180 -->

## "Ephemera" — Conclusion (p. 161)

- The aged ephemera reflects on living 420 minutes (seven of the sun's
  twelve hours) — a "great age," having seen generations born and die
- Questions the value of his political struggles and philosophical
  studies: laws without morals corrupt quickly; philosophy advances little
- The old fly's consolation: "the reflection of a long life spent in
  meaning well, the sensible conversation of a few good lady ephemerae,
  and now and then a kind smile and a tune from the ever amiable Brilliant"
- Final tone: resigned, warm, gently melancholic — fame means nothing to
  one who no longer exists

> "Alas! art is long, and life is short!"
> — B. Franklin, *Writings*

## Letter to Madame Brillon: "The Whistle" (November 10, 1779)

### The Central Metaphor

- Written from Passy, France to **Madame Brillon** (a close French friend)
- Core argument: most human misery comes from **giving too much for the
  whistle** — overvaluing things and sacrificing too much to obtain them
- Origin: at age 7, Franklin spent all his pocket money (given by friends
  on a holiday) on a whistle he coveted from another boy — paid four times
  its worth; his family's ridicule gave him more grief than the whistle gave
  pleasure
- The lesson stuck: whenever tempted to overspend or over-sacrifice, he
  told himself *"Don't give too much for the whistle"*

### Applications Across Life

Franklin catalogs types who "give too much for the whistle":
- **The court favor-seeker** — sacrifices time, repose, liberty, virtue,
  and friends for a title or appointment
- **The popularity-monger** — neglects his own affairs in political bustle
- **The miser** — gives up comfortable living, doing good, friendship,
  and esteem to accumulate wealth
- **The pleasure-seeker** — sacrifices health and mind for mere bodily
  sensations, "providing pain for yourself, instead of pleasure"
- **The appearance-obsessed** — contracts debts for fine clothes and
  houses, ends in prison
- **The woman in a bad marriage** — "What a pity that she should pay so
  much for a whistle!"

> "I conceive that great part of the miseries of mankind are brought upon
> them by the false estimates they have made of the value of things, and
> by their giving too much for their whistles."
> — B. Franklin, *Writings*

- Self-aware coda: Franklin admits even he, with all this wisdom, might be
  tempted by "the apples of King John" and ruin himself — humility about
  the limits of his own maxims

## Dialogue between Franklin and the Gout (October 22, 1780)

### Form and Setting

- A **dramatic dialogue** in Franklin's voice, written at Passy, midnight
- **Gout** (personified as "Madam Gout") interrogates Franklin about
  his sedentary lifestyle; Franklin defends and deflects; Gout delivers
  painful "corrections" (twinges) between arguments
- Genre: comic self-flagellation; Franklin mocks his own indolence
  through the voice of his ailment

### Gout's Indictment

- Franklin claims he takes "as much exercise as I can" given his sedentary
  state; Gout rejects this entirely
- Gout's case: Franklin reads books and newspapers instead of walking
  before breakfast; sits at his desk all morning; spends afternoons playing
  **chess** (2-3 hours) — "the least eligible recreation for a sedentary
  man" because chess requires rigid mental attention that retards
  circulation, unlike walking which actively pumps blood
- Franklin pleads that riding in a carriage counts as exercise; Gout
  demolishes this: carriage on springs gives negligible motion; walking
  in winter warms you in an hour; horseback requires four hours to match
  that; a carriage won't warm you all day
- Gout's physiology of walking: alternating weight on legs creates
  pressure on foot vessels, repels blood, then allows vessels to refill —
  accelerating circulation, producing ruddy cheeks and health
- Gout cites the **"fair friend at Auteuil"** (likely **Mme. Helvétius**)
  who walks all day and is a model of health and charm — Franklin insists
  on taking his carriage to visit her despite the short distance

### The Charges

- Gout keeps a written list of Franklin's offenses:
  - Promised walks in the Bois de Boulogne 199 times and skipped them all
    (Franklin guessed "perhaps ten times in a year")
  - At M. Brillon's gardens (which have 100 steps to a terrace), Franklin
    visited twice a week and **never once** descended the stairs for exercise
  - Franklin quoted his own maxim: "a man may take as much exercise in
    walking a mile up and down stairs as in ten on level ground" — yet
    never applied it

> Franklin: "I am convinced now of the justness of Poor Richard's remark,
> that 'Our debts and our sins are always greater than we think for.'"
> — *Writings*

> Gout: "You philosophers are sages in your maxims, and fools in your
> conduct." — *Writings*

### Resolution

- Gout claims credit as Franklin's real physician — saved him from palsy,
  dropsy, and apoplexy
- Franklin promises to give up chess, exercise daily, and live temperately
- Gout's skeptical reply: "after a few months of good health, you will
  return to your old habits; your fine promises will be forgotten like
  the forms of last year's clouds"
- Gout departs, promising to return "at a proper time and place"
- Deeper point: **self-knowledge without self-discipline is useless**;
  Franklin knew better and still failed to act on it

## The Handsome and Deformed Leg (c. 1780)

### The Two Sorts of People

- Franklin's essay on the **disposition to see the good or bad** in every
  situation — the root of happiness or misery
- Same circumstances (health, wealth, comfort) produce happiness in some,
  misery in others — purely a matter of where attention is fixed
- Every situation has conveniences and inconveniences; every face has fine
  features and defects; every government has good and bad laws
- **Happy people** fix attention on the pleasant parts; **unhappy people**
  see only the contraries and talk of nothing else

### Consequences of the Fault-Finding Disposition

- The **querulous disposition** is not innate — it originates in imitation
  and grows into habit; it can be cured once its bad effects are recognized
- Social costs: nobody loves fault-finders; they get minimum civility;
  if they seek rank or favor, no one helps them; if they incur disgrace,
  everyone piles on
- Franklin's philosopher friend used a practical test: one of his legs was
  handsome, the other crooked and deformed (by accident); if a stranger
  at first interview focused on the ugly leg, the philosopher avoided
  further acquaintance — a **two-legged instrument** for detecting
  unpleasant dispositions

> "I therefore advise those critical, querulous, discontented, unhappy
> People, that if they wish to be respected and belov'd by others, and
> happy in themselves they should *leave off looking at the ugly Leg*."
> — B. Franklin, *Writings*

## Letter to Richard Price: "Advantages of the Press" (June 13, 1782)

- Ancient orators could only reach citizens within earshot; most people
  couldn't read, so written works had little reach
- **The printing press** changes everything: now one can speak to nations;
  good books and pamphlets have "great and general influence"
- Key insight: the same truths can be "repeatedly enforced by placing them
  in different lights, in newspapers which are everywhere read" — repetition
  from multiple angles builds persuasion
- Famous formulation: "it is not only right to strike while the iron is
  hot, but that it is very practicable **to heat it by continual striking**"
- Franklin sees the press as the great equalizer and amplifier of reason
  in democratic society

## Remarks Concerning the Savages of North America (1784)

### Relativism as the Opening Move

- "Savages we call them, because their manners differ from ours, which we
  think the perfection of civility; they think the same of theirs"
- **Key claim**: examined impartially, no nation is without rules of
  politeness, and none is without some remaining rudeness

### Native American Governance and Society

- Indian men: hunters and warriors when young; **counsellors** when old
- Government entirely by **counsel and persuasion** — no force, no prisons,
  no officers to compel obedience; best speaker has most influence
- Women: farm, cook, nurse children, and **preserve oral historical memory**
  (the living records of the council — their treaty recollections verified
  against European writings for 100+ years, always found exact)
- Council decorum: old men sit first, warriors next, women and children
  behind; speaker rises, all observe profound silence; after finishing,
  five or six minutes of silence allowed so he may add anything omitted;
  interrupting is "highly indecent"
- Contrast with the **British House of Commons**: "scarce a day passes
  without some confusion, that makes the speaker hoarse in calling to order"

### The Missionary / Indian Exchange

- A **Swedish minister** preached the Gospel to **Susquehanna Indians** —
  the fall, Christ's miracles, etc. The Indian orator thanked him politely,
  then offered in return the Indian creation myth: two hunters share venison
  with a spirit-woman who descends from clouds; she rewards them by
  promising plants — maize, kidney-beans, and tobacco sprouted from where
  she touched the ground
- The missionary called the Indian story "mere fable, fiction, and
  falsehood"; the Indian replied: **"You saw that we believed all your
  stories; why do you refuse to believe ours?"**
- Franklin's point: the Indians' courtesy in believing the missionary's
  story exceeded the missionary's own capacity for reciprocal openness

### Hospitality as Virtue

- Indians keep a **"strangers' house"** in every village; arriving travelers
  are fed, given skins to sleep on, pipes brought out — nothing exacted
- **Conrad Weiser** (interpreter, naturalized among the **Six Nations**,
  spoke Mohawk) visited **Canassetego** (old Indian acquaintance) —
  received furs, beans, venison, rum; only after refreshment did
  conversation begin
- **Canassetego's critique of Christian church meetings**: he went to
  Albany, found that on the day merchants claimed to meet "to learn good
  things," prices for beaver skins actually dropped (from 4 shillings to
  3s 6d); concluded the meetings were "only to contrive the cheating of
  Indians in the price of beaver"

> "If they met so often to learn good things, they would certainly have
> learned some before this time. But they are still ignorant."
> — Canassetego, via B. Franklin, *Writings*

- Canassetego's final contrast: any white traveler entering an Indian cabin
  is dried, warmed, fed, given soft furs — for free; but if an Indian enters
  a white man's house at Albany asking for food, the reply is "Where is your
  money? Get out, you Indian dog"

## An Economical Project (April 26, 1784)

### The Satirical "Discovery" of Daylight

- Written as a letter to the *Journal de Paris* — Franklin's famous
  **proto-daylight saving time** essay, written entirely in mock-earnest
  voice
- Setup: at a demonstration of the new **Quinquet and Lange lamp**, a
  debate arose whether the oil it consumed was proportional to the light
  afforded — everyone eager to reduce lighting costs
- Franklin went to bed late (3-4 AM) and was woken at 6 by sunlight
  flooding his room — his servants had forgotten to close the shutters;
  he "discovered" the sun rises early and gives light immediately

### The Calculation

- Assumes **100,000 families in Paris**, each burning ½ pound of *bougies*
  (wax candles) per hour at night
- Between March 20 and September 20: **183 nights**, 7 hours/night of
  unnecessary candle-burning = **1,281 hours × 100,000 families =
  128,100,000 hours** by candlelight
- At ½ pound of wax per hour: **64,050,000 pounds of wax and tallow**
- At 30 sols per pound: **96,075,000 livres tournois** wasted annually
  by sleeping through free sunlight
- Franklin's mock conclusion: "An immense sum that the city of Paris might
  save every year, by the economy of using sunshine instead of candles!"
- He demands no pension, no exclusive privilege — only that Paris wake up
  earlier (the essay continues beyond p. 180)

> "Utility is, in my opinion, the test of value in matters of invention,
> and a discovery which can be applied to no use is good for nothing."
> — B. Franklin, *Writings*

## Daylight Saving (conclusion, p. 181)

- Franklin closes his satirical letter defending his "discovery" that
  the sun gives light as soon as it rises
- Anticipates critics saying ancients already knew this — rebuts:
  even if true, it was "long since forgotten" and unknown to
  Parisians, who profess to love economy yet burn expensive candles
  nightly rather than wake with the sun
- The irrefutable proof: no sensible, economy-minded people would
  endure smoky, expensive candlelight if they knew they could have
  sunlight for free
- Signed: *An Abonne* (a subscriber) — Franklin's satirical persona

## A Petition of the Left Hand (1785)

### Core Conceit
- **Allegory**: The **Left Hand** petitions educators on behalf of
  neglected ambidextrous potential — a comic vehicle for arguing
  equal education for all children
- Right Hand (the sister) received masters for writing, drawing,
  music; Left Hand was beaten for touching a pencil or pen
- Left Hand was used only from necessity, never given equal training

### Practical Argument
- The whole family's subsistence depends on both hands working
- If the Right Hand (subject to gout, rheumatism, cramp) falls ill,
  the family is ruined — Left Hand cannot even write a petition
  for relief, having to employ another's hand to transcribe it
- Plea: parents must distribute care and instruction equally among
  all children; favoritism creates dangerous dependence

> "Condescend, Sirs, to make my parents sensible of the injustice
> of an exclusive tenderness, and of the necessity of distributing
> their care and affection among all their children equally."
> — *The Left Hand*, Writings

## Letter to George Whatley, "But what signifies our Wishing?"
### (May 23, 1785)

- Franklin quotes *The Old Man's Wish* — a song wishing for warm
  house, easy horse, good companions, Sunday pudding, Burgundy —
  each stanza ending: "May I govern my Passions with an absolute
  sway, / Grow wiser and better as my Strength wears away"
- Final verse: face last day with courage; be remembered as one who
  "governed his Passions"
- **Franklin's confession at eighty**: he sang that wishing song a
  thousand times when young — now finds all three "contraries"
  have befallen him: gout, the stone, and passions not yet mastered
- Punchline: like the girl who resolved not to marry a Parson, a
  Presbyterian, or an Irishman — and married an **Irish Presbyterian
  Parson**
- **Core wisdom**: wishing doesn't determine fate; life delivers
  ironies regardless of resolution

## Letter to Jane Mecom, "the bad Spelling…is generally the best"
### (July 4, 1786)

- Franklin consoles his sister **Jane Mecom** about her poor spelling
- Counterintuitive claim: **"bad" spelling often conforms better
  to actual sound of words** than conventional spelling
- Example: a letter arrived reading *"Not finding Brown at hom, I
  delivard your meseg to his yf"* — the gentleman and his lady
  couldn't decode *yf* until their chambermaid Betty solved it
  instantly: "*yf* spells *Wife* — what else can it spell?"
- Betty's observation: "Sir and Madam were very deficient in
  sagacity... sometimes the Betties have the brightest understanding"
- Franklin's point: phonetic spelling is often more logical than
  the conventional *double-you, i, ef, e* which "in reality spells
  *doubleyifey*"
- **Populist epistemology**: common sense beats formal education
  when it comes to natural logic

## From his Autobiography, "Human Felicity" (c. 1789)

> "Human Felicity is produc'd not so much by great Pieces of good
> Fortune that seldom happen, as by little Advantages that occur
> every Day."
> — *The Autobiography*, Part 3

- **Key insight**: **daily small advantages** matter more than
  rare windfalls
- Example: teaching a poor young man to shave himself and keep his
  razor in order contributes more to his happiness than giving him
  1,000 Guineas — money is soon spent, but skill gives daily
  pleasure and avoids constant vexation (waiting for barbers,
  dirty fingers, dull razors)
- **Compound benefit of habits**: a single skill, practiced daily,
  outperforms one-time wealth transfers in total life satisfaction

## An Account of the Highest Court of Judicature in Pennsylvania,
## Viz. the Court of the Press (September 12, 1789)

### Power of This "Court"
- The **press** can receive and promulgate accusations against any
  person — judge, sentence, condemn to infamy — with or without
  inquiry or hearing, *at the court's discretion*
- Serves roughly **1 in 500 citizens**: those who can write well
  enough to print, or who own a press — they gain the privilege
  of accusing and abusing the other 499 at pleasure

### No Due Process
- No grand jury vets the accusation before publication
- Accuser's name need not be revealed
- The accused cannot confront witnesses — "they are kept in the
  dark, as in the **Spanish court of inquisition**"
- No petty jury; proceedings so rapid an honest citizen can be
  "judged and condemned" the same morning he is accused

### Hypocrisy of Press Officers
- If an officer of this "court" receives the slightest check for
  misconduct, he immediately claims his constitutional rights as a
  free citizen — demanding to know his accuser, confront witnesses,
  and have a jury trial

### Foundation of Its Authority
- Founded on **liberty of the press** in the Pennsylvania
  constitution — a liberty "every Pennsylvanian would fight and die
  for, though few have distinct ideas of its nature and extent"
- Franklin's distinction: if liberty of the press means discussing
  **public measures and political opinions** — welcome; if it means
  **affronting, calumniating, and defaming** one another — he would
  trade his "liberty of abusing others for the privilege of not
  being abused myself"
- Any man with pen, ink, paper, and a press "may commissionate
  himself" — no credentials, no oversight
- Natural support: **depravity** of minds not mended by religion
  or education; people who despair of distinction by virtue are
  happy to depress others to their level

> "There is a lust in man no charm can tame, / Of loudly publishing
> his neighbour's shame." — *Dryden*, quoted by Franklin

### Franklin's Satirical Remedy: **Liberty of the Cudgel**
- No checks on press abuse currently exist
- Franklin's proposal: restore the *liberty of the cudgel* — in
  pre-law societies, if one man gave another ill language, the
  offended party could return it with a box on the ear or a
  "good drubbing"; laws now punish the physical response while
  press abuse continues unrestrained
- Practical application: if an impudent writer attacks your
  reputation publicly with his name, go break his head; if he
  hides behind a printer, waylay him at night and drub him; if
  he hires better writers, hire brawnier porters
- For public affronts: "tarring and feathering, and tossing them
  in a blanket"
- True remedy Franklin recommends to legislators: explicit law
  marking the limits of **both** press liberty and physical
  redress — securing both the person from assault **and**
  reputation from defamation

## Letter to John Alleyne, "Early Marriage" (October 30, 1789)

### Franklin's Case FOR Early Marriage
- Young people's **tempers and habits are not yet stiff**;
  they adapt to each other more easily — fewer occasions of disgust
- If youth lacks prudence, parents and elder friends supply advice
- Early marriage **forms youth to regular and useful life** sooner;
  prevents "accidents or connections" that might injure constitution
  or reputation
- When nature has rendered bodies fit for marriage, "the presumption
  is in nature's favour"

### Late Marriage's Disadvantages
- Less chance parents survive to see children educated
- Spanish proverb: **"Late children are early orphans"**
- American pattern: marry in morning of life → children educated
  and settled by noon → peaceful afternoon and evening of leisure

### American Demographics Argument
- Early marriage + mothers nursing their own children =
  **swift population growth** unparalleled in Europe
- Celibacy diminishes a man's value: "An odd volume of a set of
  books bears not the value of its proportion to the set: what
  think you of the odd half of a pair of scissors?"

### Advice to the Newlywed
- Treat your wife always with respect — it procures respect from
  her and from all observers
- Never use a slighting expression, even in jest — "slights in
  jest, after frequent bandyings, are apt to end in angry earnest"
- Formula: studious → learned; industrious and frugal → rich;
  sober and temperate → healthy; virtuous → happy

## Sidi Mehemet Ibrahim on Algerine Piracy and the Slave Trade
### (March 23, 1790) — Franklin's Last Work

### Satirical Frame
- **Franklin's last published piece** — a devastating satire
- Fictional persona: **Sidi Mehemet Ibrahim**, a Muslim Algerian
  official, argues against freeing Christian slaves captured by
  Algerine pirates — **mirroring exactly** the arguments American
  slaveholders used against abolition
- Source: Franklin's response to pro-slavery speeches in Congress;
  the *Erika* (fictional Algerian religious sect) = American
  Quaker abolitionists

### The Pro-Slavery Arguments (Satirically Assigned to Algiers)
- If we free slaves, who will cultivate our lands in this hot
  climate? Who will perform common labor?
- Algiers has 50,000 slaves; without fresh supply, the number will
  "gradually annihilate" — land values will collapse, rents fall
  by half, government revenue destroyed
- Who will indemnify masters for the loss? The treasury can't;
  the *Erika* can't
- If freed, slaves won't return home (hardship there is worse);
  won't adopt our religion or manners; we won't intermarry with
  them — so they'd become beggars or criminals
- "Men accustomed to slavery will not work for a livelihood when
  not compelled"
- Slaves here are better off: fed, housed, clothed better than
  laborers in their own countries; not forced to fight in wars;
  brought to the light of true religion
- The *Alcoran* explicitly supports slavery: "Masters, treat your
  slaves with kindness"; "Slaves, serve your masters with
  cheerfulness and fidelity" — same scriptural logic as the Bible
- Manumission would "create universal discontent, and provoke
  insurrections, to the endangering of government"

### Franklin's Satirical Point
- Every argument used by American slave-owners to block abolition
  was identical to arguments a Muslim pirate-state would use
- **Mirror technique**: by placing the arguments in an alien
  context (Algiers/Islam), the moral bankruptcy becomes obvious
- Franklin had recently signed an abolition petition to Congress
  as president of the **Pennsylvania Abolition Society**
- Written just weeks before his death (April 17, 1790) — his
  final act of political satire
