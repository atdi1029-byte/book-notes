# Poor Richard's Almanack
**Benjamin Franklin** · 1732–1758 (Dover anthology, 2013) · 198 pages

---

## What It Is

Poor Richard's Almanack is not one book — it is twenty-five years
of annual pamphlets, essays, prefaces, and proverbs that Benjamin
Franklin published under the pen name **Richard Saunders** from
1732 to 1758. This Dover anthology, edited by Bob Blaisdell,
selects the best of that output: the satirical prefaces, the
memorable sayings, the poems, and the essays that made Franklin
the most-read author in colonial America after the Bible.

The centerpiece is **"The Way to Wealth"** (1758), Franklin's
famous valedictory preface in which a fictional old man named
Father Abraham recites twenty-five years of Poor Richard's best
proverbs to a crowd complaining about taxes. The crowd applauds —
then immediately buys extravagantly at the auction anyway. That
irony is the whole book in miniature.

## The Three Pillars

Franklin's practical philosophy rests on three legs:

**Industry** — time is the supreme resource, and wasting it
is the greatest prodigality. Poverty overtakes the idle man
because laziness travels slowly. The sleeping fox catches no
poultry. One today is worth two tomorrows.

**Frugality** — earning without saving is futile. Spain had
the entire wealth of the Indies and still went broke because
her outgoes exceeded her incomes. Small expenses are the
hidden killer: a small leak will sink a great ship. Artificial
wants — silks, fashions, appearances — are traps that make
hundreds "indigent" who are not truly poor.

**Prudence** — industry and frugality alone aren't enough;
you must oversee your own affairs. The eye of a master does
more work than both his hands. Trusting too much to others
is a leading cause of ruin.

## Debt as Slavery

Franklin's treatment of debt is the sharpest financial
argument in the book. Debt surrenders liberty. Your creditor
can imprison you or sell your labor. The psychological toll
compounds: shame before creditors leads to excuses, then
lies — "the second vice is lying, the first is running in
debt." He frames excessive consumer borrowing as tyranny
voluntarily accepted: the same people who would protest a
king forbidding them to dress as they liked will hand that
exact power to a shopkeeper for six months' credit.

## More Than Proverbs

The almanac is often reduced to its aphorisms, but the best
material in this anthology is the narrative surrounding them:

- The **Leeds hoax** — Franklin predicted rival almanac-maker
  Titan Leeds would die on October 17, 1733. Leeds did not
  die. Franklin spent four years insisting he had, mocking
  Leeds's increasingly furious denials with deadpan logic.

- **"Alice Addertongue"** — a satirical essay on gossip,
  written from the perspective of a professional scandal-
  monger who runs her rumor operation like a double-entry
  ledger.

- **"The Speech of Miss Polly Baker"** — a fictional legal
  defense of a woman prosecuted five times for bearing
  children outside marriage. Her seducer is now a magistrate.
  Franklin's argument: the law punishes the wrong person.

- **"Advice to a Young Tradesman"** — the famous "time is
  money" essay, which also coined "credit is money" and
  articulated the theory of compound growth.

- **"Remarks Concerning the Savages of North America"** —
  Franklin's defense of Native American governance as in some
  ways superior to European institutions, including a scene
  where a Native American points out that Christian merchants
  seem to go to church primarily to coordinate price-fixing.

## The Man Behind the Mask

Franklin published Poor Richard under a pseudonym deliberately —
it gave him distance from the moralizing. He wrote as a printer,
a tradesman, a common man. But he was also the man who argued
for free press, mocked religious hypocrisy, championed education
for women, and at the end of his life became a prominent
abolitionist. The almanac's folk-wisdom exterior contains a
radical Enlightenment interior.

The autobiography excerpts woven through the anthology reveal
his method: he tracked his progress toward thirteen virtues in
a notebook, gave himself a column per virtue per day, and
noted failures with a dot. He admitted he never achieved
perfection. He also admitted he probably would have been proud
of his humility if he had.

## Key Themes

1. **Time is capital** — spend it as carefully as money
2. **Debt is slavery** — psychological and legal both
3. **Small expenses compound** — track everything
4. **Wisdom is gleaned, not invented** — Franklin credited
   "the sense of all ages and nations" for most of the sayings
5. **Human nature is resistant to advice** — the crowd
   at the auction heard everything and changed nothing
6. **Frugality requires no genius** — just consistency
7. **Credit reputation is as valuable as cash**
8. **Virtue and wealth reinforce each other** — hard to be
   honest when poor; wealth enables integrity

## Why It Matters

Poor Richard's Almanack was the first American self-help book,
the first bestselling personal finance guide, and one of the
earliest examples of viral content — reprinted in every
colonial newspaper, translated into French, distributed by
clergy as a tract for the poor. Its core argument — that
ordinary people can improve their circumstances through
discipline and self-oversight — was genuinely radical in an
era of hereditary class distinctions. Franklin was saying:
it's not your birth, it's your habits.
