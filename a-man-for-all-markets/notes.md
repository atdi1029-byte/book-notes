# A Man for All Markets — First Half (Compacted Notes)

## Preface

### Learning to Think

- Thorp's journey spans science, gambling, and securities markets
- Born during **Great Depression** (1930s), family struggled financially
- Key resource: **learned how to think** despite lack of connections
- Thinks using models — simplified versions of reality for prediction
- Discovered simple devices follow basic rules through experimental testing
- Inspired by **crystal radio** sets — invisible rules he could discover

> "The notion that things I couldn't even see followed rules I could discover just by thinking—and that I could use what I discovered to change the world—inspired me from an early age."

### Four Key Habits

- **Test claims yourself** rather than accepting conventional wisdom
- **Invent new experiments** to test theories
- **Make realistic plans and persist** until success
- **Be consistently rational** across all aspects of life
- Value of **withholding judgment** until decision based on evidence

## Foreword (by Nassim Nicholas Taleb)

### Thorp's Significance

- **Ed Thorp**: first modern mathematician to *successfully* use quantitative methods for risk-taking
- First mathematician to achieve financial success doing it
- Predecessors (**Girolamo Cardano**, **Abraham de Moivre**) loved chance but had "their love largely unrequited"

### Thorp's Method

- Cuts to the chase by identifying clear **edge** (puts odds in favor long-term)
- Example: calculated roulette wheel momentum, estimated ~40% edge per bet
- Built first wearable computer with **Claude Shannon** (father of information theory)
- Hard part: **capturing the edge** — converting to dollars without betting too much or too little
- Refined by **John Kelly**: originator of **Kelly Criterion** (formula for bet sizing)

### Simplicity vs. Academic Sophistication

- Academic work judged by colleagues often rewards complication, not substance
- As practitioner, *"you want the mountain to give birth to the simplest possible strategy"*
- Thorp's blackjack: **Simple rules** — keep tally starting at zero, add/subtract for cards, bet incrementally

### The Black-Scholes Connection

- Discovered what's now **Black-Scholes option formula** before Black and Scholes
- Called it **Bachelier-Thorp** — derivation "too simple—nobody at the time realized it could be potent"

### Money Management and Survival

- **Warren Buffett**: "In order to succeed you must first survive"
- Need to avoid ruin at all costs
- **Kelly-Thorp method**: needs only ratio of expected profit to worst-case return, dynamically adjusted

### Two Competing Methods

- **Economists' method**: tend to blow up routinely
  - Example: **Long-Term Capital Management** blew up 1998
- **Information theorists' method** (Thorp): practiced by traders and scientist-traders
  - Examples: **Ray Dalio**, **Paul Tudor Jones**, **Renaissance Technologies**, **Goldman Sachs**

### Personal Wisdom

- Many successful speculators get involved in large structures after first break
- Not Thorp — after separation from partners, didn't start new mega-fund
- Less stressful to be independent
- Most stressful job: running math department at UC Irvine
- Man in control of his life — looked younger in 2016 than in 2005

## Roulette Physics and the Martingale Fallacy

### Betting Systems Don't Work

- Mathematics proved all betting systems must fail under general assumptions
- No method of varying bet sizes can overcome casino's advantage
- **Martingale system** (doubling after losses) fails — either run out of money or casino won't allow it

### Physical Prediction of Roulette

- Thorp argued roulette could be beaten despite mathematical proof
- High school physics idea: friction gradually slows orbiting ball, gravity pulls toward center
- Equations could forecast ball position during this process
- Random irregularities (**noise**) limit prediction but not enough to ruin it
- Didn't know **Henri Poincaré** had "proven" physical prediction impossible

## Chapter 4: Las Vegas

### Blackjack History and Rules

- Same as Spanish game **twenty-one**, referred to as early as 1601 in story by **Cervantes**
- Mid-eighteenth century: French called it **vingt-et-un**
- Twentieth century: bonuses for special combinations
- **Ace of Spades plus black Jack** = "blackjack"
- Dealer gives two cards to everyone including himself (first faceup, second facedown)
- Goal: draw cards to get close to 21 without going over
- **Aces** count as 1 or 11; **Ten-value cards** all count as 10
- Dealer must draw until 17, then stand
- Dealer's edge: player must risk busting first

### Player Advantages Over Dealer

- Dealer has fixed rules; player has choices
- Can **split pairs** into two new hands
- Can **double down**: turn first two cards faceup, double bet, get exactly one more card
- Free to draw or stand on any total of 21 or less

### The Seven-Card 21

### Why Gamblers Keep Losing

- Despite math proving no betting system could overcome casino advantage, people kept losing tens of billions
- For most casino games, mathematicians proved no system of varying bets could blunt casino's edge

### Games Where Edge Can Be Computed

### The 550 Strategy Table Problem

- Leading to gigantic library of thirty-three million strategy tables

### Simplifying the Problem

- Predicted strategies and player's edge depended mainly on **fraction** of each type of card remaining

## Counting Systems and the Ten-Count Strategy

### Refining Card Counting Methods

- Removing four 5s from deck was optimal — changed casino edge 0.21% to huge player edge 3.29%
- **Ultimate strategy** assigned point values proportional to effect on game
- Aces −9, 2s +5, down to Tens −7
- Too complex for mental calculation
- Best practical compromise: small cards (2-6) = +1, neutral cards (7-9) = 0, large cards (10-A) = −1
- Became foundation for nearly all modern blackjack counting systems

### The Five-Count System

- Simple winning system based on tracking 5s in deck
- Bet small when 5s remain, bet big when they're gone
- When only 13 cards left, all 5s gone happens 30% of time
- At these favorable points, player has 3.29% edge

### Testing in Las Vegas (Summer 1960)

- Cross-country family drive from Boston to California
- Stopped in Vegas with $200 bankroll (~$1600 in 2016)
- Used **palm-sized strategy card** — first of its kind
- Started counting "36, 16" (36 non-Tens and 16 Tens in full deck)
- Ratio 36 ÷ 16 = 2.25 was baseline
- When ratio dropped to 2.0, player had ~1% edge
- Bet $2-$10 at favorable counts, $1 otherwise
- Lost $32 over session — within expected variance
- Proved system worked at casino speed without looking at card

## Chapter 15: Rise (continued from first half)

### Risk Management Evolution

**Individual hedge tracking (early 1970s):**
- Of 200 hedges tracked: 80% winners, 10% even, 10% lost
- Losses considerably smaller than gains on average

**Portfolio-wide hedging:**
- Hedged overall risk by neutralizing impact of interest rate shifts across spectrum
- Offset danger from sudden large shifts in market prices and volatility
- 1980s onward: techniques adopted by modern investment banks/hedge funds

### Value at Risk (VaR) and Stress Testing

**VaR methodology:**
- Estimated damage for worst events among most likely 95% of outcomes
- **Defect**: doesn't fully account for worst 5% of "tails" where ruin occurs
- October 19, 1987: S&P 500 fell 23% — leading academic said if market traded every day for 13-billion-year life of universe, chance of this happening even once was negligible

**Stress-testing (modern approach):**
- 2008 example: multibillion-dollar hedge fund tested ten-day windows from 1987 crash, First Gulf War, Hurricane Katrina, 1998 LTCM crisis, tech drop 2000-02, Iraq War
- Showed potential losses of at most $500M on $13B portfolio (max 4% risk)
- Actually lost over 50% by 2009, brought to brink of ruin before recovering in 2012
- **2008 credit collapse different in kind** from past worst cases tested

**PNP's comprehensive approach:**
- Analyzed and incorporated tail risk
- Asked: "What if the market fell 25% in one day?" — more than decade later, it did exactly that, portfolio barely affected
- When moving account to **Goldman Sachs**, asked: "What happens if Goldman Sachs New York destroyed by terrorist nuclear bomb?" Answer: "Duplicate records stored underground in Iron Mountain, Colorado"

> "Computers and formulas can't protect you from being swindled or defrauded."

### Precious Metals Options Fraud (High Inflation Era)

**XYZ Corporation case:**
- Compared XYZ's option prices to "correct" model prices PNP used
- **Discovery**: XYZ offering to sell options at less than half expected payoff
- Counted option sale proceeds as income, did not set aside reserves to pay off options
- Correct reserves should have been more than twice what they were being paid
- **Classic Ponzi**, bound to end badly
- Thorp bought $4,000 worth of six-month options, doubled money in four months and cashed out
- Few months later: offices shuttered, operators gone, fraud investigation

### Market Distortions 1979-1982

**Extreme conditions:**
- Short-term US Treasury bill returns: almost 15% (1981)
- Fixed-rate home mortgages: peaked above 18% per year
- Gold futures arbitrage: buy at $400, sell at $500, store one year, deliver for 25% gain in twelve months
- Some copper stolen from warehouse broker used

**Savings and Loan (S&L) crisis:**
- S&Ls borrowed short-term from depositors, lent long-term for home mortgages at fixed rates
- As short-term rates shot up, S&L costs rose rapidly while revenue from existing mortgages did not
- Interest rate mismatch led to ruin of many S&Ls in 1980s
- Bailout cost to taxpayers: several hundred billion dollars
- **Could have been predicted and prevented** by suitable regulation, but wasn't

## Chapter 15: Black Monday 1987

### Sam Scared vs. Charlie Compounder

- **Sam Scared**: each time it doubles, puts $1 profit in sock instead of reinvesting

- **Charlie Compounder**: lets profit ride
- After ten doublings: Charlie has **$1,024**

### The Power of Small Rates Over Time

- Over sufficiently long time, compound growth at small rate will vastly exceed any rate of arithmetic growth
- **Robert Malthus** (1766–1834): believing population grew geometrically and resources arithmetically, forecast increasingly great misery

### How Fast Do Investments Grow?

- Best simple long-term choice: broad common-stock index fund
- At average past growth rate of ~10% per year, investment doubles in about **7.3 years**
- Historically, inflation offset about 3%, stretching to little over decade
- Taxable investors in index fund pay government another percent or so annually, delaying doubling time to about twelve years

### The Rule of 72

- **"The rule of 72"**: If money grows at percentage R in each period, with all gains reinvested, it will double in **72/R periods**
- Example: money grows at 8% per year — 72 ÷ 8 = **9 years** to double

## Bill Sharpe's Principle

### The Efficient Market Debate

- Met **Bill Sharpe** at UCI in 1968-69
- Sharpe's Principle: Combined holdings of all active investors replicates the index
- Before costs, passive investors get same return as index
- Active investors as a group also match index before costs, but have more risk

### The Cost Disadvantage of Active Management

- **Index funds trade infrequently**: stocks turn over just a few percent per year
- **Active investors trade over 100 percent** of portfolio annually
- Trading costs include commissions and **market impact**
- Active investors pay ~1% extra in trading costs plus 1% for "helpers" (**Warren Buffett's term**)
- Active investors trail index by ~2% before considering taxes

### Tax Impact on Active Investing

- High portfolio turnover creates **short-term capital gains** (taxed higher than long-term)

- Before costs: 10.1% (both passive and active)
- After costs: Passives 9.7%, Actives 7.7%

- **Passive investors earn more than double** after-tax returns

## The Financial Crisis and Its Causes

### Invisible Hand and Its Limits

- **Adam Smith** (1776): In economy of many small buyers/sellers, each trying to increase own profit, collective benefit maximized as though guided by "invisible hand"
- **Limited use**: Most markets not as Smith assumed

### Tragedy of the Commons

- Opposite concept explained by **Garrett Hardin**
- Natural resource anyone can freely use (e.g., catching fish in ocean)
- 18th century: Schools of cod so vast **Benjamin Franklin** was amazed
- After two centuries of overfishing, population collapsed
- Global scale example: pollution—individual humans freely burned fossil fuels, greatly increased greenhouse gases

### Cui Bono and Political Economy

- **Charlie Munger** favorite: "Look for the incentives" (closely related to "Cui bono?" or "Who gains?")

### Politically Connected Rich

- "The politically connected rich are the dominant economic and political power in the United States"
- They buy politicians using campaign contributions, career opportunities, investment profits
- "We saw how they used the government to bail them out of the financial crisis of 2008–09"

### Two Types of Rich

- No objection to some people being richer, even much richer, than others
- Objects to gain of wealth through political connections rather than earning by merit
- **Two types**: Those who use government to tilt playing field in their favor vs. those who don't

### Education Crisis

### Brain Drain

- **Economists**: One factor explained nation's future economic growth and prosperity more than any other: **output of scientists and engineers**
- "To starve education is to eat our seed corn. No tax today, no technology tomorrow"
