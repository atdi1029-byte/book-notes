Introduction: Why "How Much" Is the Only Question That Matters

Most trading books obsess over when to buy and sell. Vince thinks that's the wrong question. The real question—the one that determines whether you get rich or go broke using the exact same signals—is how much of your money you put on each trade. Two traders can use identical strategies, enter and exit at the same prices, and end up in completely different places purely based on position size.

Think of it like driving. Knowing which road to take (your trading signal) matters, but if you're going 120 mph on a winding mountain road, the best directions in the world won't save you. Position sizing is your speed dial.

The Four Things Most Traders Get Wrong

Vince starts by blowing up four common beliefs:

1. "More risk = more reward, in a straight line." Nope. The relationship between how much you risk and how much you make is a curve with a peak. Push past that peak and you actually start losing money, even if your strategy is profitable. Imagine a volume knob— crank it past the sweet spot and the music distorts. Same thing with position sizing.

2. "Stocks are safer than futures." Wrong framing. The vehicle doesn't determine your risk—the percentage of your account you put on the line does. Trading Apple stock at 5x leverage is riskier than trading pork belly futures at conservative sizing. The instrument is irrelevant; only the fraction matters.

3. "Diversification protects me." It helps, but way less than you think. If all your positions drop on the same day (which happens more often than you'd expect), diversification won't save you. Vince argues traders dramatically overestimate how much protection spreading across multiple markets actually provides.

4. "Prices move in orderly ticks." This is the dangerous one. We imagine price going from $100 to $99 to $98 in a smooth walk downward. In reality, price can jump from $100 to $80 with nothing in between. No stop loss at $95 will help you if price gaps right through it. These jumps happen far more often than most traders believe.

Hope for the Best, Prepare for the Worst

Here's Vince's scariest sentence in the entire book: If you play a game with unlimited liability, you will go broke with a probability that approaches certainty as the length of the game approaches infinity.

Translation: if you trade long enough in something where your losses aren't capped (stocks, futures, forex), a catastrophic wipe-out isn't just possible—it's mathematically inevitable given enough time. It's like lightning strikes. In any given year, your odds are tiny. But if you lived a thousand years? You'd get hit.

So what do you do? Three options:

Option A: Only trade instruments with capped losses (like buying options, where the most you lose is the premium you paid).

Option B: Set a profit target in advance and actually quit when you hit it. Walk away from the table while you're ahead.

Option C: Accept the risk but manage your position size so carefully that many small wins accumulate before the inevitable bad day arrives.

Vince insists you write down a contingency plan—literally on paper, in your desk drawer—for what you'll do when disaster hits. Who do you call? What's your legal situation? At what point do you stop trading? He says if you haven't done this exercise, close the book because nothing else in it will help you.

Chapter 1: Finding the Sweet Spot (Empirical Techniques)

The Two Decisions You Make on Every Trade

Every time you put on a trade, you're making two choices whether you realize it or not:

1. Direction: Am I going long (betting it goes up) or short (betting it goes down)?

2. Size: How many shares or contracts am I trading?

Most traders spend 95% of their energy on decision #1 and almost none on decision #2. Vince thinks that's backwards. Your account grows fastest when you trade a specific fraction of your equity on each trade. Not a fixed dollar amount. Not "whatever feels right." A mathematically determined fraction.

What Is "f" and Why Should You Care?

Meet the variable f—the fraction of your account you're risking. Here's how it works in plain terms:

Say you have a $50,000 account. Your worst-case loss on one trade is $5,000. If you're trading 5 contracts, you're risking $25,000 of your $50,000, which means f = 0.5 (50%). If you're trading just 1 contract, you're risking $5,000 of $50,000, so f = 0.1 (10%).

Now here's the magic: there's a specific value of f—the optimal f—where your account grows as fast as mathematically possible. Trade at a lower f and you leave money on the table. Trade at a higher f and you're actually shrinking your account over time, even if your strategy has a positive edge.

The Coin Toss That Explains Everything

Vince uses a beautifully simple example. Imagine a coin-toss game: heads you win $2, tails you lose $1. Obviously a good game—you'd play this all day. But how much should you bet each flip?

The optimal f turns out to be 0.25—bet a quarter of your bankroll each time. After 20 flips, your stake grows to about 10.5 times what you started with. Sounds great.

But what if you get greedy and bet half your bankroll (f = 0.5)? You'd think bigger bets = bigger profits. Wrong. At f = 0.5 you're just breaking even. And if you bet even more than half? You're now losing money in a game where you have a 2-to-1 edge. That's the terrifying power of the f-curve: overbetting turns winners into losers.

And it gets worse. At half the optimal (f = 0.125), your growth is dramatically slower—but the relationship isn't symmetric. Being slightly above optimal is far more destructive than being slightly below. The curve punishes greed much harder than it punishes caution.

Are Your Wins and Losses Random?

Before you can find optimal f, you need to know whether your wins and losses are independent (like coin flips) or dependent (like cards in blackjack).

With a coin flip, the last 10 results have zero effect on flip #11. Each flip is its own universe. But in blackjack with a fresh deck, if four aces have already been dealt, your probability of getting an ace drops from 7.7% to 5.9%. Past events change future odds.

For trading, it's not always obvious which type you have. That's where the runs test comes in. It checks whether your winning and losing streaks are longer (or shorter) than you'd expect from pure randomness. If your system tends to have 5-win streaks followed by 3-loss streaks more often than chance would predict, there's dependency in your results—and you need to account for it.

The test produces a Z-score. If it's above 1.96, you can be 95% confident your streaks aren't random. Above 2.58? 99% confident. Below those thresholds? Your wins and losses are probably independent, and you can treat each trade as its own coin flip.

Rebalance Every Day (Yes, Really)

Once you know your optimal allocation across multiple systems, you need to adjust your positions daily as your equity changes. If you start with $50,000 split across three systems and tomorrow your account drops to $45,000, the number of contracts in each system needs to shrink proportionally.

This feels like a hassle, and it costs transaction fees. But Vince argues the alternative—letting your positions drift away from optimal—costs you more in the long run than any commissions. Re-optimize the entire portfolio every three months, but rebalance position sizes daily.

The Leverage Secret: Negative Correlation

Here's something counterintuitive. If two trading systems are negatively correlated (when one wins, the other tends to lose), the optimal portfolio can allocate more than 100% of your equity across both systems combined. That sounds impossible, but it works because the systems offset each other's losses.

Example: two identical systems, each needing $10,000 in equity per contract when traded alone. If their correlation is -1.00 (perfect opposites), you can trade one contract of each per $5,000—that's 200% total allocation. The negative correlation creates natural hedging that lets you be more aggressive without taking more risk.

Why Steady Beats Spectacular

This is maybe the most important insight in the chapter. Vince compares two systems:

System A: Wins $2 per bet with a 70% win rate. Boring but steady.

System B: Wins $28 per bet with a 10% win rate. Rare huge payoffs.

System B has a much higher average payout per trade. But System A produces higher geometric growth—meaning if you're reinvesting your profits (as every real trader does), System A makes you richer over time. Why? Because System B's huge losses between those rare wins dig a deep hole that takes many trades to climb out of. Variance is the killer. A steady stream of small wins beats an occasional jackpot.

The fundamental equation of trading captures this: reducing the spread of your outcomes (standard deviation) improves your compound growth more than increasing your average win. In practice, this means strategies that cap their downside— like a buy/write (buying stock while selling covered calls)—can outperform pure long positions despite lower peak returns.

Chapter 2: The Realities of Fixed Fractional Trading

How Small Can Your Account Be?

One of the first practical questions: how much money do you actually need to start? Vince gives a simple rule. Take the bigger of these two numbers:

Number 1: Your biggest losing trade divided by your optimal f. If your worst loss is $2,000 and optimal f is 0.4, that's $5,000.

Number 2: Your margin requirement plus your worst historical drawdown. If margin is $2,500 and drawdown was $6,000, that's $8,500.

Whichever is larger is the minimum to trade one contract. In this example, $8,500. Below that, you can't even play the game properly.

The threshold to geometric tells you something slightly different: at what account size should you switch from trading a fixed number of contracts to trading a fixed fraction of your equity? Below the threshold, just trade one contract. Above it, start scaling up proportionally. The trough of this curve occurs right at optimal f—meaning you get to add your second unit at the lowest possible equity level when you're trading at the mathematically ideal fraction.

One Bankroll, Not Many

Should you keep separate accounts for each trading system, or pool everything together? Vince's answer is emphatic: one combined bankroll, always.

He runs the numbers with two identical systems (50% win rate, 2:1 payoff). With separate bankrolls of $50 each, you end up with $42.38 in total profit. Combine them into a single $100 bankroll? If the systems are negatively correlated, profit jumps to $102.73—nearly 2.5x better. The math is unambiguous: pooling money and recapitalizing daily from the combined total always wins.

Treat Every Trade as Infinite

Here's a mental model that sounds weird but works: treat every single trade as if you're going to repeat it an infinite number of times. Why? Because the optimal f for that trade is the fraction that maximizes growth over infinite repetitions, which is exactly what you want for any trade in a long trading career.

This gets interesting when your system has dependency—when winning streaks and losing streaks aren't random. If you win 55% of the time after a win but only 45% after a loss, you should use a different optimal f for each situation. After a win, you might size up (f = 0.325). After a loss, the math might say don't trade at all (negative expectation). Vince recommends treating these as two completely separate systems, each with its own sizing rules.

The Hidden Cost of Juggling

Here's a problem nobody talks about: efficiency loss. When you trade multiple systems at once, you can't recapitalize after every individual trade. If all ten of your systems lose on the same day, each loss compounds on top of the others before you can adjust. It's like taking ten punches before you can put your guard back up.

Adding a new system with low correlation to your existing ones always helps—in theory. But there's a crossover point. Eventually the efficiency loss from juggling one more system outweighs the diversification benefit. More isn't always better.

The Time-vs-Safety Tradeoff

Want to cut your risk in half? Easy: trade at half your optimal f. But here's the price tag: it takes 31% longer to reach your goal. The good news? You also eliminate half the drawdown and three-quarters of the variance. For most humans, that's a trade worth making.

Think of optimal f as a mountain road. Full f gets you there fastest but with terrifying cliff edges. Half f is the highway—slower but you actually arrive alive.

The Problem with Your Biggest Loss

Optimal f depends heavily on your worst historical trade. One bad outlier and your entire sizing formula shifts. Vince sees two problems here:

First, one random disaster shouldn't dictate your entire strategy. He suggests replacing the historical worst loss with a volatility-based estimate—something that adapts to current market conditions rather than being anchored to a single bad day from three years ago.

Second, and this is crucial: your worst loss is still in your future. Whatever the worst trade was in your backtest, there's a concrete probability you'll see something worse. The math demands humility.

Joe at the Gas Pump

Vince tells a great analogy about two cousins. Joe puts $20 of gas in his car every week, no matter the price. His cousin Cecil buys exactly 5 gallons every week regardless of price.

Joe ends up paying below-average prices over his lifetime. When gas is cheap, his $20 buys more gallons. When it's expensive, he buys fewer. He automatically buys more when it's cheap. Cecil pays exactly the average price because he always buys the same quantity.

This is dollar-cost averaging in disguise, and it's the same principle behind fixed-fraction trading. Committing a fixed fraction (not a fixed number of contracts) automatically adjusts your exposure to market conditions.

The Depressing Truth About Drawdowns

The arc sine laws from probability theory reveal something psychologically brutal: your equity highs and lows are most likely to happen at the beginning or end of your trading period, not in the middle. And if you're trading at optimal f, expect to spend 35-55% of your time in the longest drawdown.

Read that again. More than a third of your trading life will be spent watching your account sit below its peak, waiting to recover. This isn't a flaw in the strategy. It's a mathematical law. Position sizing is ultimately a test of psychological endurance.

Chapter 3: Using the Bell Curve (Parametric Optimal f)

The Big Upgrade

Chapters 1 and 2 found optimal f by replaying your actual historical trades. That's the empirical approach—it works, but it has limits. What if you only have 50 trades? What if market conditions have changed? What if you want to test a scenario you've never experienced?

The parametric approach is like upgrading from a bicycle to a car. Instead of replaying trades, you fit a mathematical curve (a probability distribution) to your trade outcomes and use that to calculate optimal f. Three big advantages:

1. It uses true probabilities. If your system wins 60% of the time but the last 20 trades only showed 55%, the parametric method knows the real rate is 60%. The empirical method would incorrectly size you for 55%.

2. It needs less history. You don't need hundreds of trades. You just need enough data to estimate the shape of your distribution (mean and standard deviation).

3. It enables "what if" scenarios. What if volatility doubles? What if your average win shrinks by 20%? Parametric methods can answer these questions. Empirical methods are stuck replaying whatever actually happened.

Distribution Shapes, in English

Every set of trades has a "shape"—a pattern of how outcomes are spread out. Four numbers describe this shape:

Mean: Where's the center? If your average trade makes $330, that's your mean. It tells you the "typical" result.

Standard deviation: How spread out are results around the mean? A small standard deviation means trades cluster tightly around $330. A large one means some trades are at +$5,000 and others at -$4,000.

Skewness: Is the distribution lopsided? Negative skew means a long tail of bad outcomes (many small wins, occasional big losses). Positive skew means occasional big wins with many small losses. Zero skew means symmetric.

Kurtosis: How fat are the tails? High kurtosis (leptokurtic) means extreme outcomes happen more often than a bell curve predicts. Low kurtosis (platykurtic) means extremes are rarer. Most trading distributions have fat tails.

The Bell Curve (Normal Distribution)

The Normal Distribution—the classic bell curve—is the starting point. It was discovered by Gauss (hence "Gaussian distribution") and assumes outcomes cluster symmetrically around the mean, with extreme results getting exponentially rarer.

Why does it show up everywhere? The Central Limit Theorem: if you take enough random samples from any distribution and average them, those averages will form a bell curve. Doesn't matter if the original data is uniform, exponential, or completely weird—the averages converge to Normal. This is why the bell curve appears in everything from height distributions to test scores to trade outcomes.

Vince also introduces the Lognormal Distribution—a modification that's more realistic for prices. Under the Lognormal, a stock dropping from $10 to $5 (losing 50%) is just as likely as rising from $10 to $20 (gaining 100%). That makes sense: percentage moves are symmetric even when dollar moves aren't. A stock can't fall below zero, but it can rise infinitely—the Lognormal captures this asymmetry.

Why Kelly Isn't Enough

You may have heard of the Kelly criterion—the famous formula for optimal bet sizing. Kelly works perfectly when outcomes are binary: you either win X or lose Y, with probability P. A coin flip. A roulette bet.

But real trading isn't binary. Your wins and losses come in all sizes, spread across a continuous distribution. Kelly can't handle that. Applying Kelly to continuous outcomes is like using a screwdriver as a hammer—it sort of works but you'll mess things up.

Vince's parametric method generalizes Kelly to any distribution. You break the continuous bell curve into discrete slices (typically 30+ data points spanning 3 standard deviations on each side of the mean), calculate the probability and profit at each slice, then test different f values to find the one that maximizes geometric growth across all slices. Same idea as Kelly, but it works for the real world.

Chapter 4: When the Bell Curve Doesn't Fit

The Dirty Secret of Trading Data

Here's the problem with Chapter 3: most trading results don't actually follow a bell curve. Real trade data tends to have fatter tails (more extreme wins and losses), more skewness (lopsided shapes), and general weirdness that the Normal Distribution can't capture.

So how do you know if the bell curve fits your data? That's where the K-S test (Kolmogorov-Smirnov test) comes in. It's essentially a "goodness of fit" check. You line up your actual trade results against the bell curve and measure the biggest gap between them. If the gap is small, the bell curve fits. If it's large, you need something better.

Vince's 232-trade example passes the K-S test with a significance level of 99.7%—meaning the bell curve fits those trades beautifully. But that's lucky. Most trading data won't be so cooperative.

Build Your Own Distribution

Since off-the-shelf distributions usually don't fit, Vince does something clever: he creates a custom distribution function with four knobs you can turn to match virtually any bell-shaped pattern:

LOC (Location): Slides the peak left or right. Adjusts where the center of your distribution sits.

SCALE: Makes the curve wider or narrower. Controls how spread out your results are.

SKEW: Tilts the curve to one side. A negative value creates a longer tail on the loss side; positive creates a longer tail on the win side.

KURT (Kurtosis): Controls how pointy the peak is and how fat the tails are. Higher values mean more extreme outcomes.

The fitting process is brute force: you try thousands of combinations of these four knobs, run the K-S test for each combination, and keep the one that matches your actual data best. For the 232-trade example, the best fit comes from LOC = 0.02, SCALE = 2.76, SKEW = 0, KURT = 1.78.

From Custom Fit to Optimal f

Once you have your fitted distribution, finding optimal f works exactly like Chapter 3—same procedure, same logic. The only difference is that the probabilities at each slice come from your custom function instead of the bell curve.

The result for the 232-trade example: optimal f = 0.744, meaning you should risk about 74% of your equity per trade (aggressive!), with a geometric mean of 1.0265 (2.65% growth per trade on average). For a $25,000 account, that translates to about 3 contracts.

The key insight: the shape of your distribution matters enormously. Using the wrong distribution (like assuming a bell curve when your data has fat tails) can give you a dangerously wrong optimal f.

Chapter 5: Juggling Multiple Positions at Once

Options Change Everything

Up to this point, the book has dealt with simple long/short positions. Chapter 5 adds options to the mix, which fundamentally changes the math because options have asymmetric payoffs. A call option can make you unlimited money on the upside but can only lose you the premium on the downside. That asymmetry has to be accounted for when finding optimal f.

Volatility: The Key Input

To price options (and thus find optimal f for option positions), you need to estimate volatility —how wildly the underlying asset moves. Two methods:

Implied volatility: Work backward from the market price of an option. If an option is trading at $5, what volatility would make the model spit out $5? That's the implied vol. It tells you what the market thinks volatility is.

Historical volatility: Look at actual price movements over the last N days. Calculate the standard deviation of daily log returns, then annualize it (multiply by the square root of 252 trading days). This tells you what volatility actually was.

These two numbers often disagree, and the gap between them is itself a tradeable signal for many option traders.

Option Pricing Models (The Cliff Notes)

Vince covers three models. You don't need to memorize them, just understand what they do:

Binomial model: Imagines price can go up or down at each step, branching like a tree. Simple and intuitive.

Black-Scholes: The famous formula. It's the Binomial model taken to its limit (infinite branches). Takes five inputs: stock price, strike price, time to expiration, volatility, and the risk-free rate. Spits out a "fair value" for the option.

Black model: Same as Black-Scholes but adapted for futures options.

All three models assume things that aren't true in reality: constant volatility, no early exercise, smooth price movements. But arbitrageurs enforce the theoretical prices by buying underpriced and selling overpriced options, so the models work well enough as practical tools.

Vince also presents his own model that works with any probability distribution, not just the lognormal that Black-Scholes assumes. The idea is simple: an option's fair value is just the average of what it would be worth at every possible future price, weighted by probability, discounted back to today. You can plug in whatever distribution you believe in—Normal, fat-tailed, skewed, anything.

Finding Optimal f for Options

The procedure for a single long option: pick an exit date, map out every possible underlying price within 3 standard deviations, calculate what the option would be worth at each price, weight by probability, and find the f that maximizes geometric growth.

An important discovery: the optimal exit date often isn't the expiration date. Time premium decays (bad for the option holder), but the range of possible price moves also expands (good). The interplay between these two forces can make it optimal to exit well before expiration.

Causal vs. Correlative Relationships

When you hold multiple positions simultaneously, their relationship matters:

Causal: There's a direct mechanical link. IBM stock and IBM call options are causally connected—if the stock goes up, the call must go up (all else equal). The math combines their HPRs by summing.

Correlative: Statistical co-movement without a mechanical link. Gold and the S&P might move together sometimes, but there's no physical law connecting them. The math multiplies probabilities across a multi-dimensional grid.

Random: Zero correlation. Each position's outcomes are completely independent of the others. This creates the largest computation because you have to consider every combination of outcomes.

Chapter 6: Building the Best Portfolio (Efficient Frontier)

Taking a Step Back

Chapter 6 temporarily puts optimal f aside and asks a simpler question: if you have several possible investments, how should you split your money among them? This is the domain of Modern Portfolio Theory, developed by Harry Markowitz in 1952.

The Core Idea

Imagine plotting every possible portfolio on a chart. The horizontal axis is risk (measured as variance of returns). The vertical axis is expected return. Every portfolio lands somewhere on this chart.

Some portfolios are clearly better than others. If Portfolio A has the same return as Portfolio B but lower risk, A dominates B. The set of portfolios that can't be beaten—where you can't get more return without taking more risk, or less risk without giving up return—forms a curved line called the efficient frontier.

Everything below the frontier is suboptimal. Everything above it is impossible with the available investments.

What You Need to Know About Each Investment

To build the frontier, you need three things for each investment:

1. Expected return. What do you think this investment will make over the next holding period? Typically estimated from historical data via linear regression.

2. Variance (or standard deviation). How wildly do returns bounce around? Higher variance = more risk.

3. Correlations between every pair. How do they move together? This is the secret sauce. Two high-risk investments with low correlation can combine into a lower-risk portfolio than either one alone.

Vince walks through an example with four investments: Toxico (7.5% return, moderate risk), Incubeast Corp. (13%, higher risk), LA Garb (21%, high risk), and a savings account (6.5%, zero risk). The correlations between them determine how much diversification benefit you get. Low correlations = more benefit. High correlations = less.

Critical rule: your return data, variance data, and correlation data must all use the same time frame. If you estimate returns from yearly data, your correlations must also come from yearly data. Mixing daily correlations with monthly returns will give you garbage results.

Chapter 7: The Geometric Frontier (Where Growth Really Lives)

The Arithmetic Lie

Chapter 6 built the efficient frontier using arithmetic average returns. That's what most textbooks teach. But Vince argues it's misleading for anyone who reinvests profits (which is everyone).

Here's why. Say your portfolio returns +50% one year and -50% the next. The arithmetic average is 0% (breakeven). But you're not breakeven. You started with $100, went to $150, then fell to $75. You lost 25%. The arithmetic average lied to you.

The geometric average captures reality. It's always lower than the arithmetic average, and the gap grows with variance. This means high-variance strategies that look great on paper (high arithmetic return) can actually destroy wealth when you compound returns over time.

The conversion formula is beautiful in its simplicity: take the arithmetic average squared, subtract the variance, and take the square root. Variance directly subtracts from your compound growth. This single insight explains why reducing volatility is more valuable than chasing higher returns.

The Geometric Optimal Portfolio

If you redraw the efficient frontier in geometric terms, the optimal portfolio is the point where a 45-degree line from the origin just touches the curve. This is the portfolio that maximizes compound growth. It's not the highest-return portfolio. It's not the lowest-risk portfolio. It's the one with the best ratio of growth to variance.

The paradox: higher geometric growth comes with deeper drawdowns. You can't maximize compounding without accepting gut-wrenching dips. The two are mathematically inseparable.

Breaking the 100% Barrier

Standard portfolio theory constrains your weights to sum to 100%. You put 40% in stocks, 30% in bonds, 30% in cash. Vince asks: what if we remove that constraint?

He adds non-interest-bearing cash (NIC) as a portfolio component. NIC earns nothing and has zero variance. With NIC in the mix, the math allows individual allocations above 100%. In his example, the unconstrained optimal allocates 102.6% to Toxico, 49% to Incubeast, 40% to LA Garb, and a whopping 708% to NIC.

How can you invest more than 100%? Through leverage. The low correlations between components mean you can safely be more aggressive in each position because they hedge each other. NIC absorbs the mathematical slack. In practice, this translates to leveraged positions in futures or margin accounts.

Connecting Back to Optimal f

Here's where it all comes together. Each trading system has its own optimal f from earlier chapters. The portfolio framework gives each system a weighting. To get the actual position size, you divide the per-system optimal f by its portfolio weighting.

Example: Toxico has an optimal f that says trade one contract per $2,500. Its portfolio weighting is 1.026 (102.6%). So the adjusted f-divisor becomes $2,500 / 1.026 = $2,437. Trading at this adjusted level puts you on the geometric optimal portfolio.

The threshold to geometric works the same way— divide the single-system threshold by the portfolio weighting to know when to add the next unit in a portfolio context.

Chapter 8: Surviving in Practice (Risk Management)

The Drawdown Problem

Everything up to this point has been about finding the theoretical optimum. Chapter 8 is about the cold hard truth: trading at full optimal f will gut you emotionally. The drawdowns are enormous—often 50-85% of your equity— and they last for months or years.

Vince makes a provocative point: even boring blue-chip stocks produce devastating drawdowns when traded at geometric optimal. From a drawdown perspective, trading IBM at optimal f is no safer than trading pork bellies. The vehicle is irrelevant; only the fraction of capital matters.

Two Ways to Dial It Down

Since nobody can psychologically withstand full optimal f drawdowns, Vince offers two approaches:

Static fractional f: Just trade at a fixed percentage of optimal. If optimal f says trade 10 contracts, trade 5 (half f) or 3 (30% f). Simple, effective, and permanent. Your growth rate drops but so does your drawdown. Pick a fraction and stick with it.

Dynamic fractional f: This is cleverer. Split your account into two buckets: active and inactive. Only the active bucket determines how many contracts you trade. All gains and losses flow through the active bucket only.

The magic of dynamic f: as your account grows, the active fraction naturally increases, automatically moving you up the growth curve. As your account shrinks, the active fraction naturally decreases, automatically protecting you. It's a built-in accelerator and brake.

Four Ways to Split Active and Inactive

Once you've decided to use fractional f, how do you decide the split? Vince gives four methods:

1. Investor Utility ("Feel Good"): The simplest. Ask yourself: what's the maximum drawdown I can sleep through? 30%? Then put 30% in active equity. If the active portion goes to zero, you've lost 30% of your total—painful but survivable. Vince sarcastically calls this the "here, have some money" method because it's based on feelings, not math.

2. Scenario Planning: More sophisticated. Define three or four possible future scenarios (bull market, sideways, crash), assign probabilities to each, and calculate the optimal starting allocation. If there's a 25% chance of a crash that wipes out 50% of active equity, factor that in.

3. Share Averaging: Periodically pull a fixed percentage from total equity (active + inactive) and move it to a separate account. This creates systematic income while maintaining the dynamic f structure. It's like paying yourself a dividend from your own trading.

4. Portfolio Insurance: The most complex. You mathematically replicate having bought a put option on your entire portfolio. This creates a "floor"—a minimum value your portfolio can never fall below. You continuously adjust your hedge ratio as the portfolio moves, just like an options market maker. It provides protection but requires constant monitoring and adjustment.

Dealing with Margin

Theory says your optimal portfolio might require 200%+ allocation. But your broker requires margin deposits. If you don't have enough margin, you can't hold the optimal portfolio.

Vince's solution: find the constrained tangent portfolio—the best possible portfolio that fits within your margin limits. The surprising result: this constrained portfolio has the same Sharpe ratio as the unconstrained optimal. Margin limits reduce your leverage but don't reduce your efficiency. You're still on the best possible risk/return line; you're just not as far along it.

The Honest Conclusion

Vince ends the book with remarkable candor. He acknowledges that every model he's presented rests on assumptions that are probably wrong:

The Normal Distribution assumption? Violated— real prices have fatter tails. Finite variance? Maybe not—measured variance grows the longer you look. Efficient markets? Doubtful—no model of rational investors fully explains what we observe.

But he argues the models are still the best tools available. They're wrong, but they're useful. Like a map that's slightly inaccurate, they'll still get you closer to your destination than wandering blindly. The key is using them with humility, knowing that the map is not the territory.

Appendices: The Distribution Zoo

A Field Guide to Probability Distributions

The appendices describe the probability distributions referenced throughout the book. Think of them as different templates for how random events can be shaped:

Geometric Distribution: "How many tries until I succeed?" If you're rolling a die waiting for a 5, on average you'll roll 6 times. The Geometric Distribution models this kind of "keep trying until it works" scenario.

Poisson Distribution: "How many rare events happen in a fixed time?" Phone calls to a switchboard per hour, equipment failures per month, typos per page. It's for counting rare, random events. The quirky property: both the mean and variance equal the same single number.

Exponential Distribution: The flip side of Poisson. If calls arrive at a Poisson rate, the time between calls follows an Exponential Distribution. It models waiting times in queuing systems.

Student's t Distribution: Looks like the bell curve but with fatter tails. Crucial for small samples (under 30 data points) where the Normal approximation breaks down. As you add more data, Student's t converges to the Normal.

The Infinite Variance Bombshell

Here's the most unsettling idea in the entire book, buried in the appendices. If you measure the variance of a stock's daily closing prices over one month, then one year, then five years, you'd expect the number to stabilize. It doesn't. It keeps growing.

This suggests that the distribution of price changes has infinite variance—a property of certain distributions (like the Paretian/Student's t family) where the theoretical variance is literally infinite.

Why does this matter? Because every model in this book—optimal f, the efficient frontier, Black-Scholes, Markowitz portfolio theory—assumes finite variance. If variance is actually infinite, all those models are built on a foundation that doesn't exist.

Vince doesn't resolve this. He simply acknowledges it as an open question where "debate is certain to rage for quite some time." But the takeaway for practitioners is clear: don't trust your models too much. They're useful approximations of a reality that may be fundamentally wilder than any mathematics can capture.
