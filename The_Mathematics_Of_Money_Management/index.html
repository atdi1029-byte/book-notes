Introduction: The Worst-Case Scenario

Machines, Not Tools

This book is about machines, not tools. Where his earlier Portfolio Management Formulas laid out the individual mathematical instruments, this sequel assembles them into larger, more powerful systems where the whole exceeds the sum of parts. The mathematics involved—gamma functions, incomplete gamma functions, probability density integrals—reside just beyond the typical trader's comfort zone, but The reader need not calculate them by hand, only understand their role in the architecture.

The book's true prerequisite is not mathematical but psychological. Trading is like a fistfight: the marketplace delivers significant beatings, and success requires discipline to endure pain at "Level 19 out of 20." The analogy to music theory versus instrument performance—this book is about the theory of money management, not a how-to guide for picking winning trades. The reader will find no indicators or entry signals here. What they will find is a rigorous framework for answering the question that matters most: how much to risk on each trade.

Four Misconceptions

Four prevalent misconceptions need demolishing. First, the relationship between potential gain and risk is not a straight line—it is a curve with a distinct peak. Second, your position on the risk spectrum depends not on the vehicle you trade but on how much of your capital you commit. Third, diversification reduces drawdowns far less than most traders believe. Fourth, and most dangerously misunderstood, price does not behave rationally. Price is synthetic, born of value, and capable of gigantic leaps that bypass all intermediate ticks. Being on the wrong side of such a move can wipe out a trader entirely.

The foundation of any effective money management strategy is simple: hope for the best but prepare for the worst. A core axiom puts it bluntly: if you play a game with unlimited liability, you will go broke with a probability approaching certainty as the length of the game approaches infinity. The analogy is being struck by lightning—the probability in any given year is minuscule, but if you live long enough, the improbable becomes inevitable. For traders in unlimited-liability vehicles, three escape routes exist: trade only limited-liability instruments like options, set a profit target and quit when you reach it, or accept that many smaller wins will suffice and manage position size accordingly.

The book demands that readers pause and write out a contingency plan for a catastrophic loss—who to call, what legal protections exist, what the exit strategy looks like. This is not fatalism but objectivity. The sheet of paper goes in the top drawer of your desk, and from this foundation of preparedness, everything else in the book is built.

Chapter 1: The Empirical Techniques

The Variable f

The first chapter condenses the earlier Portfolio Management Formulas to bring all readers to a common foundation. Every time you enter a trade, you make two decisions: whether to go long or short, and upon what quantity. The quantity decision is always a function of account equity, but it is also a function of your worst-case loss, the speed at which you wish to grow the account, and any dependency structure in your trade outcomes.

The variable f is a divisor of the biggest perceived worst-case loss that determines how many contracts to trade. In a $50,000 account with a worst-case loss of $5,000 per contract, an f of 0.5 means trading one contract per $10,000 in equity (five contracts total). The Terminal Wealth Relative (TWR) measures what your stake grows to over a sequence of trades at a given f value. Using a simple coin-toss game paying 2-to-1 on wins with 50% probability, the optimal f is 0.25. At this fraction, your stake grows fastest. At half the optimal (f = 0.125), growth is dramatically slower. Beyond optimal f, growth reverses: at f greater than 0.5 in this example, you are losing money despite a positive expectation game.

The chapter covers basic probability concepts: independent trials (like coin tosses, where each outcome is unaffected by previous results) versus dependent trials (like blackjack with a finite deck, where prior cards drawn change probabilities). For trading systems where dependency cannot be determined by inspection, the runs test which compares the number of consecutive wins and losses against what a random distribution would produce. The Z-score from the runs test tells you whether streaks in your system are statistically significant, with confidence levels mapped to specific Z-score thresholds (1.96 for 95%, 2.58 for 99%).

The geometric mean portfolio strategy calls for daily rebalancing—adjusting contract quantities as equity changes to maintain optimal allocations. The recommendation is to rerun the optimization program every three months, though the optimal portfolio composition tends to remain stable over time. A critical insight emerges when correlations between market systems are negative: the optimal portfolio can allocate more than 100% of equity across components, effectively creating leverage through diversification. When two systems have a correlation of -1.00, each with optimal f at $5,000 per contract, you can trade one contract of each per $5,000 (200% total allocation) rather than the $10,000 required for a single system.

Dispersion and Geometric Growth

The dispersion of outcomes affects geometric growth by comparing two systems. System A pays 2:1 with 70% win rate; System B pays 28:1 with 10% win rate. Despite System B's vastly superior arithmetic expectation, System A produces higher geometric growth at optimal f because its lower variance means faster recovery from drawdowns. The fundamental equation of trading crystallizes this: reducing standard deviation improves geometric growth more than increasing arithmetic mean. The practical implication is that a buy/write strategy with lower variance can outperform a naked long position with higher arithmetic returns.

Chapter 2: Characteristics of Fixed Fractional Trading

Minimum Account Size

For small accounts, the formula for the minimum equity needed to trade one contract: take the larger of (biggest loss divided by negative f) or (margin plus absolute drawdown). A trader with optimal f of 0.4, biggest loss of $2,000, and margin of $2,500 would need at least $8,500 to trade a single contract. The threshold to geometric provides an alternative calculation that determines when to switch from constant-contract to fixed-fractional trading, using the ratio of arithmetic to geometric average trade multiplied by the f-divisor.

A powerful result concerns combined versus separate bankrolls. When trading multiple systems simultaneously, a single combined bankroll dramatically outperforms separate accounts. With two identical systems (50% win rate, 2:1 payoff): with negative correlation and combined banking, profits of $102.73 dwarf the $42.38 from separate bankrolls. The principle extends to any number of systems—fixed fractional trading is always best served from a single combined bank.

Each trade should be treated as if it will be performed an infinite number of times into the future. When dependency exists (say a 55% win probability after wins, 45% after losses), the optimal f changes for each scenario. After a winning trade, f = 0.325; after a losing trade, the mathematical expectation may turn negative, meaning no bet should be placed at all. The solution is to segregate dependent trade streams into separate systems, each with its own optimal f.

Efficiency Loss and the Arc Sine Laws

Efficiency loss arises when trading multiple systems simultaneously because you cannot recapitalize after every single wager. If ten market systems all lose on the same day, the damage compounds because you cannot reduce positions in real time. Adding a new system with correlation less than 1.0 to existing components always helps, but there is a point where adding another system does more harm (through efficiency loss) than good (through diversification). The time required to reach a specified goal scales predictably: trading at half of optimal f takes 31.44% longer to double your stake, but eliminates half the drawdown and three-quarters of the variance.

A persistent problem is that optimal f is too sensitive to the single biggest losing trade. One solution is making the largest loss a function of current market volatility rather than a historical outlier, and warns that the concrete possibility of experiencing a larger loss in the future than any seen in the past must always be acknowledged. The dollar-averaging analogy of Joe Putzvakian—who buys a fixed dollar amount of gasoline weekly regardless of price— illustrates how consistent fractional commitment beats consistent quantity commitment over time. Joe pays below-average prices for life; his cousin Cecil, who buys a fixed number of gallons, pays the average.

The arc sine laws from random walk theory reveal unsettling truths: the maximum or minimum of an equity curve is most likely to occur near the endpoints (beginning or end of trading), not in the middle. Traders at optimal f levels should expect to spend 35% to 55% of their time in the longest drawdown—a psychologically devastating prospect that underscores why position sizing is ultimately a test of endurance.

Chapter 3: Parametric Optimal f on the Normal Distribution

The Parametric Pivot

Moving from empirical to parametric techniques marks the book’s major intellectual pivot. Rather than replaying historical trades to find optimal f, the parametric approach fits a probability distribution to trade outcomes and uses that distribution to calculate the optimal fraction. The advantages are threefold: parametric methods use the true underlying probabilities (not sample frequencies), require less historical data, and enable "what if" scenarios about the future.

Probability Foundations

The basics of probability distributions come first. Discrete distributions use bins to categorize outcomes; continuous distributions represent the limiting case of infinitely thin bins. Descriptive measures include the arithmetic mean (most common but poor for broad-tailed distributions), the median (which divides the distribution into equal halves), and the geometric mean (which cannot be used if any value is zero or negative). The moments of a distribution capture progressively finer detail: the first moment is location (mean), the second is dispersion (variance and standard deviation), the third is skewness (asymmetry—negative skewness means thicker tails on the positive side), and the fourth is kurtosis (peakedness, where leptokurtic distributions have fatter tails than the Normal).

The Normal Distribution, discovered by Gauss and earlier by de Moivre, is the workhorse. Galton’s board—an isosceles triangle with pegs where dropped balls form a bell curve—as physical intuition. The Central Limit Theorem provides the theoretical justification: regardless of how a population is distributed, the distribution of sample means will be approximately Normal, with the approximation improving as sample size increases. This is why the Normal Distribution appears everywhere in nature and finance.

Working with the Normal Distribution requires converting raw data to standard units (Z-scores) via the formula Z = (X - U) / S, where U is the mean and S the standard deviation. The N(Z) function gives the height of the standard Normal curve at any point, and its integral gives cumulative probabilities. Both one-tailed and two-tailed probability formulas for practical calculation.

The Lognormal Distribution modifies the Normal for real-world trading applications. The key difference: under the Lognormal, a stock dropping from $10 to $5 is as probable as rising from $10 to $20, reflecting the fact that percentage changes (not absolute changes) are symmetrically distributed. To use the Lognormal, you convert price data to natural logarithms and then apply Normal Distribution mathematics.

The Kelly criterion is itself a parametric optimal f, but it only works for Bernoulli-distributed outcomes (binary win/loss). A coin-toss example shows why parametric methods are superior: if the true probability of heads is 60% but the last 20 tosses showed only 55% heads, the empirical method suggests risking less than the parametric method. The parametric answer (f = 0.2) is correct because the next toss still has a 60% probability of heads regardless of recent history.

To find optimal f on Normally distributed data, the continuous distribution must be discretized. The process uses two parameters: the number of standard deviations to cover (typically 3, capturing 99.73% of outcomes) and the number of equally spaced data points within that range (minimum of 10 times the number of sigmas, so at least 30 points for 3 sigmas). For his 232-trade example with an average trade of $330.13 and standard deviation of $1,743.23, the procedure constructs a matrix of associated P&L values and their probabilities, then calculates HPR for each test value of f. The optimal f maximizes the geometric mean of these HPRs. The by-products include TWR, geometric average trade (GAT), and the threshold to geometric.

Chapter 4: Parametric Techniques on Other Distributions

The Kolmogorov-Smirnov Test

Most distributions of trade profits and losses do not fit neatly into the Normal or any other well-known distribution. Chapter 4 addresses this reality by introducing the Kolmogorov-Smirnov (K-S) test and a custom distribution function that can model virtually any unimodal shape.

The K-S test is the most popular method for comparing two distributions. It measures the maximum absolute difference between two cumulative density functions. After standardizing trade data (subtracting the mean, dividing by the standard deviation) and sorting in ascending order, you compare the empirical CDF against the theoretical CDF at each point. For 232 trades, a K-S statistic of 0.04 corresponds to a significance level indicating you can be 99.7% certain the theoretical distribution generated the observed data. The statistical significance formula involves an infinite sum of exponential terms, though practical calculation methods make it tractable.

The Characteristic Distribution Function

Since the Normal Distribution is generally not a good model for trade P&Ls, the book introduces a custom characteristic distribution function with four adjustable parameters controlling the first four moments. The base equation is a general bell-shaped function Y = 1/(X^2 + 1), augmented with LOC (location, controlling the peak position), SCALE (affecting the horizontal spread), SKEW (controlling asymmetry from -1 to +1), and KURT (controlling peakedness and tail thickness). This function can replicate the probability densities of many unimodal distributions without assuming any particular distributional form.

Fitting the parameters is an empirical optimization process. You convert raw trade data to standard units, sort it, then systematically test combinations of the four parameters, running a K-S test for each combination to find the best fit. For the 232-trade example, the best-fitting parameters are LOC = 0.02, SCALE = 2.76, SKEW = 0, KURT = 1.78, yielding a K-S statistic of 0.0836 with a significance level of 7.84%. Once the distribution is fitted, finding optimal f follows the same procedure as Chapter 3, but with probabilities calculated from the custom distribution rather than the Normal. The result: optimal f = 0.744, geometric mean = 1.0265, geometric average trade = $174.45. For a $25,000 account, this translates to approximately 3 contracts.

Chapter 5: Multiple Simultaneous Positions

Options and Volatility

This chapter extends the parametric optimal f framework to handle multiple positions simultaneously, including options combined with underlying instruments. The chapter is designed for traders who enter and exit via judgment rather than mechanical systems, using parameter estimates (volatility, distribution shape, current prices) for instruments they have not yet traded.

Volatility estimation is the critical input for option pricing. Implied volatility works backward from market prices to solve for the volatility that makes the model price equal the market price. Historical volatility uses actual price changes: for a 20-day calculation, you take the natural log of each day's close divided by the previous close, compute a 20-day moving average and sample variance, then annualize by multiplying the standard deviation by the square root of 252 (trading days per year). For March 1991 Japanese Yen futures, this yields a historical volatility of about 15%.

Three option pricing models are presented. The Binomial model (Cox-Ross-Rubinstein) assumes price can branch in two directions each period over infinitely many periods before expiration. The Black-Scholes model is the limiting form of the Binomial, taking inputs of underlying price, exercise price, time to expiration, annual volatility, and risk-free rate. The Black model adapts Black-Scholes for futures options. All three assume European exercise (no early exercise), constant volatility, lognormal distribution of price changes, and constant risk-free rates— assumptions that are violated in practice but enforced by arbitrageurs who converge on theoretical fair values.

A novel European options pricing model works with any distributional assumption, not just the Lognormal. The theoretical fair value of an option is defined as the present value (discounted at the risk-free rate) of the arithmetic mathematical expectation of the option at expiration. This means taking all discrete price increments with non-trivial probability, calculating what the option would be worth at each price, weighting by probability, and discounting.

For a single long option, the procedure to find optimal f involves determining an exit date, counting days to expiration, establishing price points within plus or minus three standard deviations of the current price, computing theoretical option prices and probabilities at each point, then calculating HPRs across a range of f values. The geometric mean of these HPRs, maximized over f, gives the optimal fraction. An important insight: optimal f varies substantially for different underlying prices, and the optimal exit date may not be the mandated expiration date. Time premium decays while the window of possible price moves expands—the interplay between these two functions can create positive mathematical expectation for being long an option even when the raw arithmetic suggests otherwise.

Causal vs. Correlative Relationships

Multiple simultaneous positions fall into two categories. Causal relationships have a factual, mechanical connection—IBM call options and IBM stock move in lockstep because one derives from the other. Correlative relationships show statistical co-movement without a mechanical link. For causally related positions, HPRs are calculated by summing the coefficients of each leg (debit legs use one sign convention, credit legs the reverse) weighted by their probabilities. For randomly related positions (correlation coefficient of zero), the probabilities of each leg are multiplied together rather than summed, creating a multi-dimensional matrix of outcomes.

Chapter 6: Correlative Relationships & the Efficient Frontier

The E-V Framework

This chapter temporarily sets aside optimal f to derive the efficient frontier from first principles. The problem in the simplest possible context: a portfolio of stocks in a cash account, fully paid for with no margin. The goal is to find portfolios with the lowest expected risk for a given level of expected gain—the foundation of Markowitz's Modern Portfolio Theory (E-V theory: Expected return minus Variance of return).

Using four hypothetical investments—Toxico (7.5% return, 10% variance), Incubeast Corp. (13% return, 25% variance), LA Garb (21% return, 40% variance), and a savings account (6.5% return, 0% variance)— The complete parametric information needed. Returns and variances must be measured over the same holding period (yearly data requires yearly correlations). Risk is defined as variance in expected returns, though true risk is "far broader, and its definition is quite elusive."

The correlation coefficient grid captures how returns co-move between each pair of investments. From correlations and standard deviations, covariances are calculated using COV(a,b) = R(a,b) * S(a) * S(b). The expected return of the entire portfolio is the weighted sum of individual returns, while portfolio variance expands into a double summation involving all pairwise covariances. Four equivalent formulations of the variance equation, each useful in different computational contexts.

The E-V investor performs linear regression on historical returns to estimate expected future returns, then calculates variance from the residuals. The efficient frontier is the set of portfolios that cannot be improved in one dimension (return or risk) without worsening the other. Points below the frontier are suboptimal; points above it are unattainable with the given securities.

Chapter 7: The Geometric Efficient Frontier

Arithmetic vs. Geometric

The central innovation is converting the efficient frontier from arithmetic to geometric terms. When traders reinvest their profits (as nearly all do), the arithmetic average HPR is misleading—it is the geometric HPR that determines actual wealth accumulation. The conversion formula is GHPR = (AHPR^2 - V)^(1/2), where V is the variance. This single equation captures why high-variance strategies can have attractive arithmetic means yet produce poor geometric growth: variance directly subtracts from geometric performance.

The geometric TWR (Time to Wealth Ratio) is simply GHPR raised to the N-th power, where N is the number of trades. The geometric optimal portfolio is found where a 45-degree line from the origin touches the AHPR efficient frontier—visually, this is the point that maximizes compound growth. A key trade-off emerges: higher GHPR means greater drawdown risk. This is not a defect but a structural feature of geometric optimization.

The frontier extends into unconstrained territory by adding non-interest-bearing cash (NIC) as a portfolio component with an HPR of exactly 1.0 and zero standard deviation. With NIC, the constraint that portfolio weights sum to 1.0 is lifted, and optimal allocations can exceed 100% for individual components. In the Toxico/Incubeast/LA Garb example, the unconstrained portfolio allocates 102.6% to Toxico, 49.0% to Incubeast, 40.2% to LA Garb, and 708.1% to NIC. This is not a computational artifact but a reflection of leverage: negative correlations between components allow total allocation to exceed unity while maintaining optimal geometric growth.

Equalized f Values

Optimal f integrates with the portfolio framework through equalized f values. Rather than using raw optimal f for each component, you divide by the portfolio weighting to get adjusted f values. For example, Toxico at $2,500 per contract with a weighting of 1.026 yields an adjusted f-divisor of $2,436.69. Trading at these adjusted values places you on the geometric optimal portfolio. The threshold to geometric for portfolios follows the same logic: divide the single-system threshold by the portfolio weighting to determine when adding another unit is justified.

Chapter 8: Risk Management

Blue Chips and Pork Bellies

Having established the mathematics of geometric optimality, this chapter confronts the practical reality: tremendous drawdowns are expected at full optimal f. The only guard is diluting the geometric optimal portfolio with a risk-free asset. A provocative point emerges: even blue-chip stocks, when traded at unconstrained geometric optimal, produce drawdowns as severe as pork bellies—from a drawdown perspective, the vehicle does not matter, only the fraction of capital committed.

Two fractional f strategies address the drawdown problem. Static fractional f trades the portfolio at a fixed fraction of the unconstrained optimal, reducing dispersion below the geometric optimal. Dynamic fractional f splits equity into active and inactive portions: only the active portion determines contract quantities, and equity changes are reflected exclusively in the active portion. As the account grows, the portfolio effectively moves along the Capital Market Line, with dispersion remaining constant as a fraction of optimal f. Dynamic fractional f produces faster growth than static because the active fraction automatically adjusts as equity changes.

Four Reallocation Methods

Four asset allocation (reallocation) methods give traders flexibility in managing the active/inactive split. The investor utility method— sarcastically dubbed "feel good" or "here, have some money"—allocates a percentage equal to the maximum tolerable drawdown. If you can stomach a 30% drawdown, you allocate 30% to active equity. Scenario planning evaluates multiple future scenarios with assigned probabilities and determines optimal initial allocation accordingly. Share averaging periodically pulls a percentage from total equity (active plus inactive), withdrawing systematically over time. Portfolio insurance (dynamic hedging) replicates buying a put option on the portfolio by continuously adjusting the hedge ratio using H = f * A / E, where A is the active portion and E is total equity.

The margin constraint brings theory into contact with brokerage reality. Positive arithmetic expectation tends toward positive correlation between equity curves (they all rise together), which is problematic for daily management. The solution is detrending equity curves via least-squares regression before computing correlations, then using constrained optimization to find the geometric optimal portfolio that fits within margin limits. The Sharpe ratio of the constrained tangent portfolio equals that of the unconstrained geometric optimal—margin constraints change the level of leverage but not the efficiency of the portfolio.

Honest Limitations

The closing thoughts are remarkably candid about the limitations of the models presented. The E-V framework assumes finite variance, but real-world price distributions may be Paretian with infinite variance—the further back you measure, the larger the observed variance grows. The efficient market hypothesis is flawed because it assumes rational investors, which no model can guarantee. Yet these models remain the best starting points available, better than their alternatives, and adequate for practical money management. Portfolio models, he concludes, are "but one facet of money management, but they are a facet where debate is certain to rage for quite some time."

Appendices: Probability Distributions

Discrete and Continuous Distributions

The appendices serve as a reference for the probability distributions used throughout the book. The Geometric Distribution models independent Bernoulli trials until the first success—tossing a die until a 5 appears, for instance, has an expected six tosses. The Poisson Distribution handles rare events occurring randomly over time (telephone calls to a switchboard, equipment failures, radioactive decay), with the distinctive property that both its mean and variance equal the single parameter L. As L approaches infinity, the Poisson approaches the Normal.

The Exponential Distribution is the continuous complement to the Poisson, modeling interarrival times in queuing systems. If calls arrive according to a Poisson process with rate L, the time between calls follows an Exponential with parameter 1/L. The Chi-Square Distribution and Student's t Distribution are the workhorses of hypothesis testing. Student's t is crucial when sample sizes fall below 30, where the Normal approximation breaks down; its shape resembles the Normal but with thicker tails and a lower peak, converging to the Normal as degrees of freedom approach infinity via the Central Limit Theorem.

The Infinite Variance Problem

The most provocative concept in the appendices is infinite variance. If you measure the variance of daily closing prices for a stock over progressively longer periods—one month, one year, five years— the variance grows rather than stabilizing. This suggests the distribution of log price changes has infinite variance, sometimes modeled by the Student's t Distribution. This observation challenges the foundational assumption of finite variance that underlies Markowitz portfolio theory, Black-Scholes option pricing, and virtually every quantitative model in finance. This tension is not resolved but presented honestly as a frontier of ongoing debate.

Key Takeaways

Quantity trumps timing

How much you trade matters more than when you trade. Two traders using identical entry and exit signals will produce vastly different results based solely on position sizing. The f-curve demonstrates that there is a single optimal fraction of equity to risk, and deviations in either direction reduce geometric growth.

The f-curve is asymmetric

Trading below optimal f costs you growth; trading above optimal f costs you everything. Overleveraging turns a positive-expectation game into a losing one. At f greater than the optimal in a fair coin toss paying 2:1, you actually lose money despite favorable odds.

Geometric beats arithmetic

When reinvesting profits, the geometric mean—not the arithmetic mean—determines wealth accumulation. A system with lower arithmetic expectation but lower variance can outperform a system with higher expectation but higher variance. GHPR = (AHPR^2 - V)^(1/2) makes this explicit.

Parametric over empirical

Fitting a probability distribution to trade outcomes and calculating optimal f from the distribution is superior to replaying historical trades. Parametric methods use true probabilities (not sample frequencies), require less data, and enable scenario analysis.

Worst case is your anchor

Every money management strategy must start from the worst-case scenario and build upward. The biggest losing trade determines the f-divisor, and the concrete possibility of experiencing a worse loss in the future must never be dismissed.

One combined bankroll

When trading multiple systems, a single combined bankroll dramatically outperforms separate accounts, especially when systems are negatively correlated. Recapitalize the combined bank daily.

Diversification has limits

Adding correlated systems creates efficiency loss from simultaneous drawdowns. There is a crossover point where adding another system does more harm (through efficiency loss) than good (through diversification).

Infinite variance is real

Real-world price distributions may have infinite variance, meaning measured variance grows without bound as the observation period lengthens. This challenges the finite-variance assumption underlying virtually all quantitative finance models.

Drawdowns are structural

Arc sine laws predict that equity peaks and troughs are most likely near the endpoints of a trading period. Traders at optimal f should expect 35-55% of time spent in the longest drawdown. This is a mathematical property, not a trading failure.

Fractional f buys survivability

Trading at half of optimal f takes 31% longer to reach your goal but eliminates half the drawdown and three-quarters of the variance. Dynamic fractional f (split equity) provides automatic adjustment as the account grows, offering a practical middle ground between theoretical optimality and real-world survival.

Contrarian Ideas

Blue chips are as dangerous as pork bellies

From a drawdown perspective, trading blue-chip stocks at geometric optimal is just as risky as trading commodity futures. The vehicle does not determine risk—the fraction of capital committed does. This contradicts the common advice that "safe" stocks require less risk management.

Diversification is overrated

Diversification reduces drawdowns "to a far lesser degree than most traders realize." Efficiency loss from simultaneous positions can offset diversification benefits, especially when correlations are positive and you cannot recapitalize between trades.

Price is not rational

Price does not move from one tick to the next in an orderly fashion. It can make "gigantic leaps," bypassing all intermediate prices. This is not an anomaly but a structural feature of synthetic, value-driven price formation.

The Kelly criterion is a special case

Kelly only gives the correct optimal f for Bernoulli-distributed outcomes. For the continuous distributions that describe real trading, Kelly is inadequate and potentially dangerous if applied without modification.

You should plan for retirement from trading

The advice is to set a profit target and quit when you reach it. In unlimited-liability vehicles, the probability of catastrophic loss approaches certainty over infinite time. The only rational response is to define an exit point in advance.

Models are wrong but useful

E-V theory, the Normal distribution assumption, and finite-variance models are all flawed. Yet they remain better than any alternative and serve as adequate starting points for practical money management.

The Mathematics of Money Management: Risk Analysis Techniques for Traders by Ralph Vince
Published 1992 by John Wiley & Sons · 212 pages
A dense, technical treatise on position sizing, optimal f, and portfolio construction that bridges probability theory, option pricing, and modern portfolio theory into a unified framework for determining how much capital to risk on each trade.

About the Author

Ralph Vince is a self-taught mathematician and money management theorist who came to prominence in the trading world through his work on position sizing and portfolio optimization. Without formal academic credentials in mathematics or finance, he developed his theories from direct trading experience and independent study, becoming one of the few practitioners to bridge the gap between abstract probability theory and the daily reality of futures and options trading.

His first book, Portfolio Management Formulas (1990), introduced the concept of optimal f to the trading community and established him as the leading voice on mathematical position sizing. The Mathematics of Money Management (1992) expanded that foundation into a complete system incorporating parametric statistics, custom distribution fitting, and geometric portfolio optimization. He later published The New Money Management and The Handbook of Portfolio Mathematics, continuing to refine and extend his framework over two decades.

What distinguishes Vince from academic portfolio theorists is his insistence on worst-case thinking and his candor about the limitations of his own models. He writes as a trader who has endured drawdowns, not as a professor describing them from a distance. His work occupies a unique niche: too mathematical for most traders, too practical for most academics, and indispensable for the small community that takes position sizing seriously.

Why This Book Matters

Before The Mathematics of Money Management, the trading literature treated position sizing as an afterthought—a few paragraphs at the end of a book about indicators and entry signals. This book inverted that hierarchy, arguing that how much you trade matters more than when you trade. That insight, backed by rigorous mathematics, changed how a generation of professional traders thought about risk.

The book's integration of probability theory, option pricing, and Modern Portfolio Theory into a single position-sizing framework had no precedent in 1992 and has had few successors since. The concept of optimal f—that there exists a single mathematically correct fraction of capital to risk on each trade, and that deviations in either direction cost you— remains one of the most powerful and counterintuitive ideas in quantitative trading.

Perhaps most importantly, the book is honest about what it cannot do. In an era when quantitative finance was becoming increasingly confident in its models, Vince devoted his final pages to the possibility that price distributions have infinite variance—a finding that would undermine virtually every model in the book. That intellectual honesty, combined with mathematical rigor and practical orientation, gives the work a durability that more polished but less candid treatments lack.
