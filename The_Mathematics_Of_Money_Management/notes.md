### Some Prevalent Misconceptions

**Misconceptions addressed in the text:**

1. **Potential gain to potential risk is straight-line function** — Not true; more nuanced, depends on spectrum of risk
2. **Position on risk spectrum depends on vehicle type** — More complex relationship
3. **Diversification reduces drawdowns** — Can do this, but only to a far lesser degree than most traders realize
4. **Price behaves rationally** — Not true

> "The last of these misconceptions, that price behaves in a rational manner, is probably the least understood of all, considering how devastating its effects can be."

**"Rational manner"** = When trade occurs at certain price, it proceeds in orderly fashion to next tick (up or down), making a new price from one price to next tick.

**Reality**: Price does NOT behave this way. Price is **synthetic because of value**, therefore does not act rationally. Price can make very large leaps at times when proceeding from one price to next, completely bypassing all prices in between. Price capable of **gigantic leaps**, far more frequently than most traders believe.

> "To be on the wrong side of such a move can be a devastating experience, completely wiping out a trader."

Foundation of any **effective gaming (or money management)** strategy is to *hope for the best but prepare for the worst*.

### Worst-Case Scenarios and Strategy

**The "hope for the best" part is easy**. Preparing for worst is difficult, something most traders never do.

Preparing for worst = preparing for trading at something that has **zero or infinitesimally small probabilities of occurrence**. Easy when we consider benign worst-case scenarios; something we must do is **identify the worst**, then prepare from that starting point in money management strategy.

> "You will see as you proceed through this text that we always build a strategy from a worst-case scenario. We always start with a worst case and incorporate it into a mathematical technique to take advantage of situations that include the realization of the worst case."

**Core axiom**: *If you play a game with unlimited liability, you will go broke with a probability that approaches certainty as the length of the game approaches infinity.*

- Not a pleasant prospect, but can be understood by analogy: being struck by lightning
- If you trade vehicle with unlimited liability (most vehicles are unlimited), you will eventually experience loss of such magnitude as to **lose everything you have**
- Probabilities of being struck by lightning are extremely small for next fifty years, but probability exists
- If you live long enough, eventually this extremely small probability becomes realization
- Likewise, probability of experiencing cataclysmic loss of position may be extremely small today or over next ten years, yet if you trade long enough, **eventually this probability becomes realization**

**Three possible courses of action:**

1. Trade only vehicles where liability is limited (such as long options)
2. Not trade for infinitely long period of time (most traders will die before hit by lightning)
3. Probability of enormous winning trade exists; don't have to have gigantic winning trade—**smaller wins will suffice**

Many smaller wins will suffice. If you aren't going to trade in **limited liability vehicles** and aren't going to die:

> "Make up your mind that you aren't going to quit trading vehicles altogether and when your account equity reaches some prespecified goal. If and when you achieve that goal, get out and don't ever come back."

**We've been discussing worst-case scenarios and how to avoid or reduce their probabilities**. However, this has not truly prepared us for their occurrence; you must prepare for the worst.

Consider today: you had that cataclysmic loss. Your account has been hit. The leverage ratio you were using wants to know about that big fat debit in your account. You weren't the one who chose today; the fates did.

**Exercise to build contingency plans:**
1. Take some time and try to imagine how you are going to feel in such situation

4. Make it as definitive as possible
5. Do it all *if* happens; *know* what to do without having to think about these matters

> "Are there arrangements you can make now to protect yourself before this possible cataclysmic loss? Are you sure you wouldn't rather be trading a vehicle with limited liability? If you're going to trade a vehicle with unlimited liability, at what point on the upside will you stop? Write down what that level of profit is."

Don't just read this and forget; **plow through the book, close the book and think about these things for awhile**. This is the point from which we will build.

**Point has not been to get you thinking in fatalistic way**—that would be counterproductive. Point has been to get you to **think about worst-case scenario objectively** and make contingency plans in case such worst-case scenario occurs.

> "Now, take that sheet of paper with your contingency plans (and with the amount at which point you will quit trading unlimited liability vehicles altogether written on it) and put it in the top drawer of your desk. ## Chapter 1: The Empirical Techniques

*This chapter is a condensation of Portfolio Management Formulas. Purpose: bring readers unfamiliar with empirical techniques to same level of understanding as those who are.*

### Deciding on Quantity

Whenever you enter trade, you have made **two decisions**:
1. Whether to enter long or short
2. **Upon the quantity to trade in**

**Quantity decision is always a function of account equity**. If you have $10,000 account, don't think you would be leaning toward 1 contract per 100 gold contracts. Likewise, if you have $10 million account, don't think you'd be leaning toward 1 contract per $10,000.

**Verifiable fundamental fact**: Account will grow fastest when we trade a **fraction of the account** on each and every trade.

However, quantity decision is not simply function of equity in account; it is also function of other things:
- Function of **"worst-case loss"** you can sustain
- Function of **speed** with which we wish to make account grow
- Function of **dependency** (or lack thereof) of dependent variables

> "Yet we do have control over the quantity we have on. Since one does not dominate the other, both considerations are important, focusing on putting on the right quantity."

### The f Curve and Terminal Wealth Relative (TWR)

**On any given trade, you have a perceived worst-case loss**. You may not even be conscious of this, but whenever you enter trade you have some subconscious feeling about level of equity in account that shapes decision about how many contracts to trade.

**There is a divisor of this biggest perceived worst-case loss** between $0 and account equity in question, determining how many contracts to trade.

**Example**: $50,000 account
- Worst case: lose $5,000 per contract
- If you have 5 contracts, divisor is .5
- 50,000/(5,000/.5) = 5

> "In other words, you have on 5 contracts for a $50,000 account, so you have 1 contract for every $10,000 in equity."

**In worst case: lose $5,000 per contract, thus divisor is .5**
- If had only 1 contract, divisor would be .1

**We call this divisor by its variable name f**

TWR calculation example for 20 sequences of coin flips (+2 wins, -1 losses):
- 50% chance of winning $2 versus 50% chance of losing $1 on every play
- Optimal bet: 10.53 times your original stake, or ~95% profit
- At f = .25 (half of optimal): 10.55 at TWR, profit only $4.66
- At f = .05 (only 15% away from optimal): merely $.08 clipshod

> "At f = .1, you would be making 1 bet for every $2.50 in your stake. At f = .4, you would be making 1 bet for every $.46. At f of .46, clearly it does not pay to bet more, as it brings you no greater outcome. You make the same amount as if you had bet a quarter of that amount."

**Key insight**: At f greater than .5 you are only breaking even. That means you win only half your bets, you bet for every $2 in your stake. **At f greater than .5 you are losing in this game**, and it is simply a matter of time before you are completely tapped out.

### Basic Concepts

**Probability statement** = number between 0 and 1 specifying how probable an outcome is (0 being no probability whatsoever, 1 being question in question certain to occur).

**Independent trials process (sampling with replacement)** = sequence of outcomes where probability statement is constant from one event to next.

**Example**: Coin toss is independent trials process. Each toss has 50/50 probability regardless of outcome of prior toss. Even if last 5 flips were heads, probability of this flip being heads is unaffected and remains .5.

**Dependent trials processes (sampling without replacement)** = outcome of prior events *does* affect probability statement.

**Example**: Blackjack is dependent trials process in a game with 1 or 2 decks. If new deck is shuffled and 4 removed to removing card and probability of drawing ace was 4/52 or .0769230769. After 4 aces are drawn and replaced, probability of drawing ace on next draw is 3/51 or .0588235294.

> "Try to think of the difference between independent and dependent trials processes as simply whether the probability statement is fixed (independent) or is a variable (dependent trials) from one event to the next based on prior outcomes."

### The Runs Test

When sampling without replacement from deck of cards, we can determine by inspection that there is dependency. For certain events (such as profit and loss stream of a system's trades), where dependency cannot be determined upon inspection, we have **the runs test**.

**The runs test** will tell us if our system of trades has (or had) more consecutive wins and losses than a random distribution.

The runs test is essentially obtaining the **Z scores** for win and loss streaks of a system of trades. A Z score of 2.00 is 2.00 standard deviations away from mean of distribution.

**Z score** = simply measure of how far away data is from mean of Normal Probability Distribution.

- Z score of 1.00: that line is 1.00 standard deviation from mean
- Incidentally, this is perfectly normal

Z score is also known as **confidence limit** or **degree of certainty**. Area under curve of Normal Probability Function at 1 standard deviation from mean equals 68% of total area under curve.

**Confidence limits table:**

| Confidence Limit (%) | Z Score |
|---------------------|---------|
| 90.73 | 3.00 |
| 95 | 2.58 |
| 98 | 2.33 |
| 99 | 2.12 |
| 96 | 2.05 |
| 95.45 | 2.00 |
| 95 | 1.96 |
| 90 | 1.64 |

With minimum of 30 closed trades we can compute our Z scores. What we are trying to answer: What are win (loss) streaks of system we are testing in line with what we could expect?

**Runs test equation**:
(1.01) Z = (N*(R-.5)-X)/(X*(X-N))/(N-1))^(1/2)

where:
- N = Total number of trades in sequence
- R = Total number of runs in sequence
- X = 2*W*L
- W = Total number of winning trades in sequence
- L = Total number of losing trades in sequence

## Chapter 1 (continued): The Geometric Mean Portfolio Strategy

### Daily Procedures for Using Optimal Portfolios

- **Rebalancing positions daily** — Adjust contract quantities as account equity changes, recalculating to new total equity each day
- Example: 3 market systems with optimal f values of 10%, 50%, 40%. Starting equity $50,000 yields 1 contract for A, 10 for B, 10 for C
- Next day equity drops to $45,000 → recalculate: 1 contract for A ($5,000), 9 for B ($22,500), 9 for C ($18,000)
- **The portfolio is temporarily out of balance** — adjusting daily costs transaction costs vs. benefit of staying optimal
- Generally better to adjust positions on day-by-day basis "rather than constantly" for longer-term systems

> "This matter of implementing the correct daily positions is not such a problem. Recall that in finding the optimal portfolio we used the daily HPRs as input. We should therefore adjust our position size daily (if we could adjust each position at the price it closed at yesterday)."

- **Time period for portfolio rebalancing** — no definitive answer; more back data improves future resemblance
- Using 5 years of data (1983) on stock indices showed portfolios being "a part of the optimal portfolio" — precious metals did poorly for most 1980-1981 systems
- **Rerun optimization program every 3 months** — even then, high likelihood of arriving at same/similar optimal portfolio composition

### Allocations Greater Than 100%

- **Negative correlation enables leverage** — when correlations < 1.00, optimal portfolio can allocate >100% total
- Example: Markets A and B identical except negative correlation (r = -1.00). Each has optimal f of 50% at $5,000 per contract
- Trading 1 contract for A per $10,000 equity yields optimal growth, but with perfect negative correlation can trade **1 contract for each per $5,000** (200% total allocation)
- **Optimal portfolio can exceed 100%** when correlation coefficient is low enough

> "When there is negative correlation, however, it can be shown that the optimal geometric mean portfolio is arrived at by trading 1 contract for an amount less than $10,000 in equity for market system A and market system B. In order to arrive at a negative correlation, you can have the sum of percentage allocations to the market systems exceed 100%."

- **Accommodating optimal f in dollars** — divide optimal f by number of market systems to get per-system allocation
- Must also use **cash as another market system** for non-interest-bearing cash (HPR of 1.0)
- If optimal portfolio includes 60% to A, 60% to B, and 0% to cash → divide by factor >1 to normalize

### How the Dispersion of Outcomes Affects Geometric Growth

- **Mathematical expectation vs. geometric mean** — why 70% 1:1 system beats 10% 28:1 system despite lower arithmetic average
- Examined via coin toss: System A has 2:1 payoff, 70% win rate (f is .4); System B has 4:1 payoff, 70% win rate (f is 4)
- **Geometric means at optimal f levels**: System A = 1.044176755, System B = 1.08576529
- System A makes 8.577629% per bet average when reinvesting at optimal f; System B makes 4.4% per bet average
- **Dispersion matters more than arithmetic mean** — System B's 16.5× greater worst-case drawdown (2.0 vs. 9+ trades) means recovering requires far more trades

> "What are we really after is to realize about this isn't so much the effect is in what it can do to improve your geometric mean. The technique for finding the optimal portfolio by looking at the geometric mean alone, without regard to the frequencies, is tantamount to saying: Thus, the geometric mean is this statistical measure of how beneficial a portfolio is."

- **Goal: maximize the square root of the estimated HPR squared minus variance** — TWR = (1+R)^N
- Estimated TWR equation: (1.17) TWR = (1+R)^N, where R = arithmetic mean per period, N = number of periods
- **Diversification lets you get more** if done properly (positive mathematical expectation maintained)

### The Fundamental Equation of Trading

- **Core insight**: reducing standard deviation more than reducing arithmetic average HPR improves geometric mean
- If A is arithmetic average HPR and A > 1, then as N approaches infinity, result can be no greater than 1
- **If A < 1 (negative expectation), no optimal f** — simply don't trade
- Provided A > 1: **increasing N increases expected profits**, coefficient multiplied by square root

> "The other important point to note about the fundamental trading equation is that if you reduce your arithmetic average HPR, you are better off. It stands to reason, therefore, that cutting profits short, if possible, benefits you."

- Example: buy/write strategy (buying underlying OPX index, selling puts) had lower transaction costs than being long both underlying and long put option, **despite lower estimated TWR** (2.02 vs. 3.24) — options clearly superior as fundamental equation testifies

## Chapter 2: Characteristics of Fixed Fractional Trading and Salutary Techniques

### Optimal f for Small Traders Just Starting Out

- **Formula for smallest account to trade 1 contract**:
  - (2.01) A = MAX {(Biggest Loss/-f), (Margin+ABS(Drawdown))}
  - Where A = dollar amount to first contract, f = optimal f (0 to 1), Margin = initial speculative margin, Drawdown = historic maximum drawdown, MAX{} = maximum of bracketed values, ABS() = absolute value

- **Subtracting from equity each day** — trader utilizes remainder to divide by (Biggest Loss/-f), answer rounded to integer = contracts to trade
- Example: optimal f is .4, biggest loss -$2,000, margin $2,500 → A = MAX{($7,500), ($2,500+$6,000)} = $8,500 for first contract
- Account equity $22,500 → subtract $8,500 = $14,000 → $14,000/$7,500 = 1.867 → trade 2 contracts

### Threshold to Geometric

- **Threshold to geometric** = another calculation for small accounts, related to by-products of optimal f
- Uses geometric mean and standard deviation to determine **"tells us at what point we are assuming we are starting out constant-contract trading"**
- Formula: (2.02) T = ∆∆T/G∆T*Biggest Loss/-f, where T = threshold to geometric, ∆∆T = arithmetic average trade, G∆T = geometric average trade, f = optimal f
- Example: 2-to-1 coin toss with 50% win → optimal f = .25, threshold = $8.24
- **Figure 2-1 shows threshold to geometric for 2:1 coin loss** — trough at optimal f means since threshold to geometric is at optimal f, going to 2 units at lowest level of equity

> "Notice that the trough of the threshold to the geometric curve occurs at the optimal f. This means that since the threshold to the geometric is at the optimal f, when you go to 2 units at the lowest level of equity, optimally, when incorporating the threshold to the geometric at the optimal f."

- **Valid only if you do not trim back units** after downside when switching to geometric mode
- Can use similar approach to determine switching from 2 cars to 3 cars, 100 cars starting out to 101, etc. — however **only valid if you do not trim back units**

### One Combined Bankroll Versus Separate Bankrolls

- **When trading >1 market system simultaneously, generally better using only one combined bankroll** to figure contract sizes rather than separate bankrolls for each
- **Recapitalize subaccounts daily** as equity fluctuates
- Example: two systems A and B, both 50% win, 2:1 payoff, optimal f = .25. Starting equity $50 each
- First run: positive correlation, each system's bankroll separate, $50 each → both win → profit $21.19140
- Combined bank starting at 100 units betting $1 for every $8 combined, bet $1 for every $4 → combined profit = $42.38 (vs. two separate banks totaling $42.38)
- **Now negative correlation between same two systems** from separate bankrolls: Total net profit $42.38
- Combined bank: starting 100 units, betting $1 for every $4 → combined bank profit $102.73 vs. two banks $42.38

> "With the combined bank, the results are dramatically improved. When using fixed fractional trading you are best off operating from a single combined bank."

### Threat Each Play as if Infinitely Repeated

- **Maximize current event as though performed infinite number of times** into future
- Determine optimal f which maximizes play as though performed infinite number of times in future
- Example: dependency scenario where last play affects next play (55% win after win, 45% after loss)
- Kelly formula (1.10): f = ((2 +1)*P -1)/(2), where P = probability of win
- With dependency: if last play was win, next has 55% win → f = (3*.55-1)/2 = .325
- After losing play: f = ((2+ 1)*.45-1)/2 = (3*.45-1)/2 = .35/2 = -.175

> "Notice in this example that betting after both the wins and the losses still has a positive mathematical expectation individually. What if, after the loss, there were a profit on a win, but not a win per se, the mathematical expectation is negative, hence there is no optimal f and as a result you wouldn't take this play."

- **Segregate trades if dependency present** — treat as separate market systems
- Same principle applies to betting systems where outcomes are maximized **if each play is considered to be performed an infinite number of times**

### Efficiency Loss in Simultaneous Wagering or Portfolio Trading

- **2:1 coin-toss game** example: System A and B both 50% chance, 2:1 payoff, optimal f is 1 unit for every 4.347826 in equity
- **When starting with bank of 100 units** and finish with bank of 156.86 units:
  - System A alone: optimal f is 1 bet per every 4.347826 → TWR after 4 plays = 1.2656 or geometric mean 1.06066
  - System B alone (identical): 1.2656^(1/4) = 1.06066
- **Combining market systems with correlation coefficient > -1.00**: rate of growth increases, but **when combining market systems, your rate of geometric growth can never be any less than with the single-bet case, no matter of how high the correlations are**

> "Thus, we can state that when combining market systems, your rate of geometric growth can never be any less than with the single-bet case, no matter of how high the correlations are, provided that the market system being added has a positive mathematical expectation."

- **Efficiency loss** = caused by inability to recapitalize after every single wager (vs. single-bet case)
- If 10 market systems, all day every day, they all suffer simultaneously → could be terminal to account since can't trim back each now that each lost
- **Adding each new market system has correlation coefficient less than 1.0** to other components = gain
- Point at which adding another system does more harm than good depends on correlation and positive mathematical expectation magnitude

### Time Required to Reach a Specified Goal and the Trouble with Fractional f

- **Expected number of trades to reach specific goal** (not same as time elapsed):
  - (2.09) N = ln(Goal)/ln(Geometric Mean), where N = expected number of trades, Goal = ratio at optimal f (e.g., 2:1 to double stake), ln() = natural logarithm
- Example: at optimal f geometric mean = 1.06066, at half f = 1.04582499 → calculate expected number of trades required to double stake
- Full f: N = ln(2)/ln(1.06066) = .6931471/.05881314 = 11.76993 plays (trades) to double
- Half f: N = ln(2)/ln(1.04582499) = .6931471/.04480602 = 15.46996 trades to double
- **Trading half f takes 31.44% longer** to reach goal, eliminates drawdown by half and variance by three-quarters

> "The smaller the fraction of optimal f that you use, the smoother your equity curve, and hence the more time it will take to be exposed to be in the worst-case drawdown."

- **Half f account doubles at 16 trades** to multiple of 1.7124805781; full f account grows to 1.71424827457 in same 16 trades
- **Nothing you sacrifice with trading at fractional vs. full f is time required** to reach specific goal — completely correct, we can gain same goal as with full optimal f with less drawdown

### Comparing Trading Systems

- **Two-dimensional measure of system performance** — must be measured on geometric mean and value for f itself (not just which system has higher geometric mean at optimal f)
- **Higher geometric mean at optimal f = better system**; also **lower optimal f = better system** (two-axis plot)
- Single one-dimensional measure: **higher geometric mean/optimal f is better** (geometric mean ÷ optimal f)

> "The higher the geometric mean at the optimal f, the better the system. Also, the lower the optimal f, the better the system."

### Too Much Sensitivity to the Biggest Loss

- **Optimal f too dependent on biggest losing trade** — rather than many general trades, shouldn't be so much function of single bad trade in past
- Algorithms exist to make largest loss a function of current volatility in market (absolute value of daily net change over last few weeks, or average absolute value)
- **Concrete possibility of experiencing loss larger in future than what largest loss seen** in past is in past
- **Largest loss will be X^-Y** — not deterministic relationship; it simply means usually is *somewhere near* X^-Y

> "If we could determine in advance what the largest possible loss would be going into today, we could then have a much better handle on our money management. Here again is a case where we must consider the worst-case scenario and build from there."

- Problem: we do not know exactly what largest loss risk-of-ruin algorithm going into today
- **Using largest loss in trading strategy is so useful** — knowing floor (absolute maximum loss) is extremely handy for money management, particularly for any trade in advance

### The Arc Sine Laws and Random Walks

- **Arc sine laws assume you know in advance** amount you can win = amount you can lose, and ties always = constant mathematical expectation
- **First arc sine law**: percentage of time on either side of origin (starting point) as percentage of time spent on one side of arithmetic mathematical expectation line
- Example: toss coin N times, probability (Prob) of K of events in positive territory is (2.13) Prob~1/(Pi*K^.5*(N-K)^.5)
- **If tossing coin 10 times (N = 10)**: probabilities of maximum (or minimum) point at any point K given by Equation (2.13); Pi = 3.141592654

- **Second arc sine law**: maximum or minimum most likely to occur near endpoints of equity curve, least likely in center
- Trading at optimal f levels, whether one or many market systems, **35% to 55% of time spent in longest drawdown** (longest time between equity peak and time equity peak equaled or exceeded)

## Chapter 3: Parametric Optimal f on the Normal Distribution

### The Basics of Probability Distributions

- **Distribution of finishing positions** for horse starting at racetrack, recording winner in pole position
- Using fewer bins reduces information content but necessary in real life; **continuous distribution** often necessary
- **Discrete distribution** = discussed (first in discussion) vs. **continuous distribution** = series of infinitely thin bins (limiting case)
- Although binning does reduce information, in real life often necessary to lose information content while keeping profile of distribution same

### Descriptive Measures of Distributions

- **Arithmetic mean** (most common): (3.01) A = (∑[i = 1,N] Xi)/N, where A = arithmetic mean, Xi = ith data point, N = total number of data points
- Arithmetic mean tends to poor measure when distribution has very broad tails
- **Median** divides probability distribution into two halves (area under curve of one half = area under curve of other half); frequently used as superior measure of central tendency than arithmetic mean
- **Mode** = peak of distribution curve; sometimes is more than one mode
- **Geometric mean**: (3.02) G = (∏[i = 1,N](Xi)^(1/N), where G = geometric mean, Xi = ith data point, N = total number of data points
- Cannot be used if any variate-values is zero or negative
- **Arithmetic mathematical expectation** = arithmetic average outcome of each play (constant 1-unit basis)
- **Geometric average outcome of each play** = on constant 1-unit basis when bet size is not fixed

### Moments of a Distribution

- **Range** = difference between largest and smallest values
- **10-90 percentile range** = difference between 90th and 10th percentile points
- **Semi-interquartile range (quartile deviation)** = one half distance between first and third quartiles (25th and 75th per-centiles)
- **Half-width** = similar to 10-90 percentile range, divided by 2
- **Mean absolute deviation (mean deviation)**: (3.06) M = 1/N ∑[i = 1,N] ABS (Xi-A), where M = mean absolute deviation, N = total number of data points, Xi = ith data point, A = arithmetic average
- Population vs. sample mean absolute deviation: replace 1/N with 1/(N-1) for sample version

- **Variance**: (3.07) V = 1/N ∑[i = 1,N] ((Xi-A)^2), where V = variance, N = total number of data points, Xi = ith data point, A = arithmetic average
- **Standard deviation** = square root of variance; in Normal Distribution **standard deviation is simply the square root of the variance**

- **Third moment (skewness)**: describes extent of asymmetry about distributions mean
- Negative skewness = tails thicker on positive side, vice versa; perfectly symmetrical distribution has skewness of 0
- **Fourth moment (kurtosis)**: peakedness or flatness of distribution relative to Normal Distribution
- **Platykurtic** (kurtosis < Normal peak), **Leptokurtic** (kurtosis > Normal peak); when peak resembles Normal, kurtosis equals zero (called type of peak on a distribution **mesokurtic**)

### The Normal Distribution

- **Normal Distribution (Gaussian distribution, de Moivre's distribution)**: discovered R-Karl Friedrich Gauss (1777-1855), century earlier and far more obscurely Abraham de Moivre (1667-1754)
- Considered to be most useful distribution in mathematics; accurately models many phenomena
- **Galton's board** (isosceles triangle): vertically mounted board with pegs, balls dropped forming Normal Distribution as they exit

### The Central Limit Theorem

> "Thus, we can state that if N random samples are drawn from a population, then the sums (or averages of samples of a given size), will be approximately Normally distributed, regardless of the distribution of the population from which the samples were drawn from. The Normal Distribution improves as N (the number of samples) increases."

- **Uniform distribution**: all elements equally likely (numbers 1 to 100); take sample of five numbers, average them
- Keep sampling, sample means are Normally distributed even though population is uniformly distributed
- True **regardless of how population is distributed** — if population happens to be Normally distributed, distribution of sample means will be exactly (not approximately) Normal

### Working with the Normal Distribution

- **Finding percentage of area under curve at given point**: use formula for curve itself or convert to standard units, use standard normal curve
- **Standard normal deviate (Z-score)**: (3.16) Z = (X-U)/S, where Z = (X-U)/S, U = mean of data, S = standard deviation, X = observed data point
- Equation (3.16) gives **number of standard units** data point corresponds to
- Example: data point at value of -9, mean is 11, standard deviation is 20 → Z = (-9 - 11)/20 = -1 (one standard unit to left of mean, i.e., one standard deviation below mean)

### Normal Probabilities

- **Convert raw data to standard units**, use N(Z) function: (3.15a) N(Z) = .398942*EXP(-(Z^2/2))
- Height of standard Normal curve is .39894; function N(Z) also expressed as EXP(-(Z^2/2))/((8*ATN(1))^(1/2))
- **Integral to N(Z) function** to find areas under curve (probabilities): integral from -∞ to N(Z) = 1-N(Z) (1-tailed probabilities)
- (3.15a) N'(Z) = .398942*EXP(-(Z^2/2)) where Y = (1-231/6419*ABS(Z))
- **2-tailed probabilities**: (3.22) 2-tailed probability = 1-((1-N(ABS(Z)))*2)

## Chapter 3: The Normal Distribution and Other Distributions (continued)

### The Lognormal Distribution

- **Lognormal vs Normal**: Many real-world trading applications require modification of the Normal Distribution → **Lognormal Distribution**
- Normal Distribution works with percentage changes rather than absolute price changes
- Key difference: As price drops toward zero, it becomes progressively harder to get lower under Lognormal
- Example: Stock at $10/share dropping to $5 is as probable under Lognormal as a drop from $10 to $5
- Under Normal Distribution, $10 to $5 drop would be about as probable as $10 to $2.50 drop
- **Practical application**: When discussing price moves, use Normal on percentage changes; when discussing absolute changes, use Lognormal
- To use Lognormal: convert data to natural logarithms, then apply Normal Distribution mathematics
- For monthly closing prices ($10, $5, $10, $10, $20): calculate **HPRs** (Holding Period Returns) from month to month
- Convert HPRs to natural logs to study distribution under Normal
- Natural log notation: log(X) vs ln(X) - this text uses log for natural logarithms

### The Parametric Optimal f

- **Kelly formula** example of parametric optimal f: function of two parameters (percentage of winning bets and payoff ratio)
- Kelly only gives correct optimal f when outcomes have **Bernoulli distribution**
- When outcomes don't have Bernoulli distribution (Normally distributed outcomes), Kelly won't give correct optimal f
- **Parametric techniques far more powerful than empirical counterparts**
- Can derive optimal f on Normally distributed data using the Kelly formula or empirical technique from *Portfolio Management Formulas*
- Suppose: coin toss system, long run 60% tosses heads, 40% tails, payoff 1:1
- Kelly formula: bet 2/10 of stake on next bet
- Last 20 tosses: 11 heads, 9 tails - empirical result suggests risking .2 returned by parametric technique
- **Critical question**: Which is correct - .2 from parametric or .1 from empirical for last 20 tosses?
- Correct answer: **.2** - next toss still has 60% probability of heads, not 55%
- **Second advantage**: Empirical technique *requires* past history of outcomes
- Parametric requires arithmetic mean, population standard deviation, threshold to geometric - can be estimated
- **Third advantage**: Parametric technique allows "What if" scenarios about the future
- **Biggest drawback**: Parametric techniques require extensive past data history to arrive at empirical optimal f closest
- Example cited: 50 tosses assumed, but with 1,000 tosses empirical optimal f closer to theoretical

> "The second advantage is that the empirical technique requires a past history of outcomes whereas the parametric techniques do not."

### Finding Optimal f on the Normal Distribution

- Technique more powerful than Kelly formula because it allows **full spectrum of outcomes** (not just Bernoulli)
- **Normally distributed outcomes** described by 2 parameters: arithmetic mean and population standard deviation
- Kelly formulas require 2 parameters: payoff ratio and probability of winning
- To find optimal f on Normally distributed data: Use **parametric technique**
- Normal Distribution is continuous, must make discrete for calculations
- **Two-step discretization process**:
  1. Determine number of sigmas either side of mean (range) and number of equally spaced data points within range
  2. Determine how many equally spaced data points to divide range between extremes
- Example: 99.73% of data falls between ±3 sigmas, so use 3 sigmas as bounding parameter
- For equally spaced data points: bare minimum 10 times number of sigmas (if 3 sigmas, minimum 30 data points)
- Using 100 equally spaced data points means dividing by 30-1 or 29 to get spacing of 6/29 ≈ .2068965517
- Must determine: arithmetic mean, population standard deviation, shrink, stretch
- **Input parameters** (What if parameters): Allow performing "What if" scenarios on the future

### The Mechanics of the Procedure

- Demonstrated on 232 trades currently in points
- Convert to dollar amounts (arbitrary value chosen: $1,000/point)
- Average trade of .330129 becomes .330129*$1000 = $330.13
- Population standard deviation 1.743232 multiplied by $1,000/point = $1,743.23
- **Construct matrix**: determine range in sigmas (example uses 3 sigmas)
- Range: -3 sigmas to +3 sigmas from mean
- Determine arithmetic mean for input: from 232 trades = $330.13
- Determine population standard deviation: $1,743.23
- Choose **Shrink = 1** (multiplier on average trade to see effect when average trade changes)
- Choose **Stretch = 1** (always positive, affects dispersion/standard deviation)
- **Associated P&L column** calculated using: D = (U*Shrink)+(S*E*Stretch) where U=arithmetic mean, S=pop std dev, E=standard unit value
- Example for -3 standard units: D = (330.129*1)+(1743.23*-3*1) = -4899.567
- **Associated Probability column**: Use N(Z) function without "If Z<0" provision for each standard value
- **HPR calculation**: HPR = (1+(L/W(-f)))^P where L=associated P&L, W=worst-case P&L, f=test value, P=probability
- **By-products**: Calculate TWR, geometric mean (GAT)
- GAT formula: (G(f)-1)*W(-f) where G(f)=geometric mean for given f value, W=worst-case P&L
- **Threshold to geometric**: E/Q where E=account equity, Q=optimal f result

### The Kolmogorov-Smirnov (K-S) Test

- **Most popular method** for comparing two distributions
- Best test for our purposes: comparing **unbinned distributions that are function of single independent variable**
- All cumulative density functions have minimum value 0 and maximum value 1
- K-S test measures variable D: **maximum absolute value of difference between two cumulative density functions**
- To perform K-S test: standardize N objects (trades) by subtracting mean and dividing by population standard deviation, sort in ascending order
- For each trade: cumulative density function (CDF) = 1/N
- Example calculation for 100 trades, K-S statistic = .04
- Statistical significance formula: SIG = Σ[j = 1, ∞] (-1)^(j-1) × (j^2) × 4.2^2 × EXP(-2j^2) × (2^N × (1/2)^D/2)
- For 100 trades with K-S statistic .04: can be 99.7% certain actual distribution generated by theoretical distribution
- **Very good significance level** for 232 trades example

### Creating Our Own Characteristic Distribution Function

- Normal Distribution **generally not very good model** for distribution of trade P&Ls
- Must create function to model distribution ourselves
- Distribution of log price changes generally assumed **stable Paretian** variety
- Distribution of trade P&Ls regarded as **transformation** of price distribution
- What we study is **not** the stable Paretian - it models other unimodal distributional functions and replicates their probability densities
- **General bell-shaped equation**: Y = 1/(X^2+1)
- Add variables to create **characteristic function** with control over first four moments of distribution:
  - **LOC** (Location) = first moment, affects where peak is positioned
  - **SCALE** (Scale) = second moment, moves horizontal axis up/down, enlarges/shrinks distribution
  - **SKEW** (Skewness) = third moment, controls symmetry about location
  - **KURT** (Kurtosis) = fourth moment, controls peak sharpness and tail thickness
- Final equation: Y = (1/(ABS((X-LOC)*SCALE)^KURT+1))^C where C = exponent for skewness
- **Four parameters are unidimensional** - meaning only in relative context, relevant only to this distribution
- Parameter ranges:
  - LOC: -infinity < LOC < +infinity
  - SCALE > 0
  - -1 ≤ SKEW ≤ +1
  - KURT > 0

### Fitting the Parameters of the Distribution

- Convert raw trade data to standard units (subtract mean, divide by population standard deviation)
- Sort in ascending order
- Objective: find values for LOC, SCALE, SKEW, KURT that best fit our actual trades
- **K-S test** determines "best fit" by empirically examining different parameter value combinations
- Run every combination for SCALE from .3 to .5 by -.1, LOC and SKEW at 0
- Perform K-S test for each combination
- Best-fitting result for 232 trades: LOC = .02, SCALE = 2.76, SKEW = 0, KURT = 1.78
- K-S statistic .0835529, significance level 7.8384%
- Shows distribution function with **roughness** (points of inflection) matching actual data

### Using the Parameters to Find Optimal f

- Once best-fitting parameters found, can find optimal f on this distribution
- Same procedure as used for Normal Distribution
- Only difference: probabilities for each standard value calculated per procedure for Equations (4.06) and (4.12)
- Must follow procedure: calculate N(X), keep running sum, find N(X) resultant, add running sums, divide by sum total
- **Associated probabilities** column calculated differently than Normal Distribution
- Example for 232 trades binned -3 to +3 sigmas with 100 equally spaced data points:
  - Optimal f = .744 (using step increment of .01)
  - Geometric mean 1.0265
  - Corresponding GAT: $174.45
- **TWR has no real meaning as by-product** - interim value needed to obtain geometric mean
- Can convert optimal f to contracts: K = E/Q where K=number of contracts, E=account equity, Q=worst-case P&L divided by optimal f
- Example: $25,000 account → 3.796 contracts → round down to 3 contracts

### Estimating Volatility

Two methods for determining volatility for option pricing models:

**1. Implied Volatility**
- Uses market price of option to solve backward for the volatility value
- Volatility that yields theoretical price equal to market price
- Determined by iteration (solving for volatility that makes model price = market price)

**2. Historical Volatility**
- Determined from actual price changes in underlying instrument
- More commonly used, calculated from annualized data
- Example: calculating 20-day annualized historical volatility for March 1991 Japanese Yen
  - Step 1: Divide today's close by previous day's close
  - Step 2: Take natural log of result from Step 1
  - Step 3: After 21 days of data, calculate 20-day moving average
  - Step 4: Calculate sample variance (subtract moving average from each log, square differences, sum, divide by 19)
  - Result: 20-day sample variance for last 20 days

> For 901226 (December 26, 1990 in Gregorian format), using 252 trading days per year:
> - 20-day sample variance = .00009
> - Square root = .00948683
> - Multiply by √252 (15.87450787) = .1505988048
> - **Historical volatility = 15.06%** (input for Black-Scholes)

### Option Pricing Models

**Binomial Option Pricing Model**
- Sometimes called **Cox-Ross-Rubinstein model** after developers
- Assumes infinite number of periods before option expiration
- Price can branch into 2 directions each period
- Black-Scholes is limiting form of Binomial model
- Fair value of call: C = U*EXP(-R*T)*N(H)-E*EXP(-R*T)*N(H-V*T^(1/2))
- Fair value of put: P = -U*EXP(-R*T)*N(-H)+E*EXP(-R*T)*N(-V*T^(1/2)-H)

**Black-Scholes Stock Option Pricing Model**
- Formulas for call and put options with variables:
  - C = fair value of call
  - P = fair value of put
  - U = price of underlying
  - E = exercise price
  - T = decimal fraction of year left to expiration
  - V = annual volatility (percent)
  - R = risk-free rate
  - ln() = natural logarithm
  - N() = cumulative Normal density function

**Black Futures Option Pricing Model**
- Adapted for futures (stock-type settlement, no dividends)
- Deltas differ:
  - Call Delta = EXP(-R*T)*N(H)
  - Put Delta = -EXP(-R*T)*N(-H)

### Key Assumptions of Option Pricing Models

First assumption (European style):
- **Option cannot be exercised until exercise date**
- European style vs. American style (can exercise anytime)
- Underperformance expectation means European options worth less than American

Other key assumptions:
- Underlying instrument volatility will **remain constant** throughout option life
- Volatility will **not change** as underlying price changes
- Distribution of volatility changes is **lognormal**
- Risk-free interest rate remains constant throughout option life
- Short-term rates are higher than long-term rates (resulting in fair price undervaluation by models)

> "All of these assumptions made by the pricing models aside, the theoretical fair prices returned by the models are monitored by professionals in the marketplace."

- When arbitrage opportunities exist, arbitrageurs converge to claim theoretical price
- **Fair price prediction becomes accurate** when using expiration inputs (T, underlying price, strike, etc.)

### A European Options Pricing Model for All Distributions

Vince's own pricing model devoid of distributional assumptions regarding price changes:

**"Theoretically fair" definition**: discount to present worth at risk-free rate of **arithmetic mathematical expectation of option at expiration**

**Mathematical expectation** (arithmetic mean):
- (1.03) Mathematical expectation = ∑[i = 1,N] (p*a_i)
  - p = probability of winning/losing ith trial
  - a = amount won/lost on ith trial
  - N = number of possible outcomes

**In nutshell**: option pricing model takes all discrete price increments with probability > 0.001 at expiration, determines arithmetic mathematical expectation

(5.10) C = ∑(p*a_i)*EXP(-R*T)/ ∑ p_i

- User can derive theoretically fair value using any distributional assumptions
- Can use **Normal, Paretian, Student's t, Poisson**, or any custom distribution
- Still need to amend model to express arithmetic expectation as present value:

(5.11) C = (∑ (p*a_i)*EXP(-R*T))/ ∑ p_i

**Present worth of arithmetic mathematical expectation** = underlying at expiration minus strike, assuming directional bias (i.e., **current price** of underlying equivalent to current price, not expected value at expiration)

### The Single Long Option and Optimal f

For single outright purchase of call option (not taking full history, but all possible outcomes):
- Weight each outcome by probability of occurrence
- Derive probability-weighted HPR for each value from mandated exit til expiration
- Look at full spectrum of option price outcomes (mandated exit date + expiration)
- Find value producing **highest geometric mean** = optimal value

In almost all cases, optimal f for options differs substantially for different underlying prices:
- Four basic inputs affect theoretical value: (a) time remaining, (b) underlying price, (c) strike, (d) volatility
- Of these, time remaining and underlying price change, volatility/strike rarely change to extent of underlying price/time
- **Must look at theoretical price returned by model for different underlying prices and different times**

(5.14) HPR(T,U) = (1+P*(Z(T,U-Y)/S-1))*P(T,U)

where:
- HPR(T,U) = HPR for given test value for T and U
- f = tested value for f
- S = current price of option
- Z(T,U-Y) = theoretical option price if underlying at price U-Y with time T remaining
- P(T,U) = 1-tailed probability of underlying being at price U by time T remaining
- Y = difference between arithmetic mathematical expectation of underlying at time T and current price

**Finding optimal f procedure**:
- Step 1: Determine exit date (definite or expiration)
- Step 2: Count days (day 1 = first day), convert to decimal fraction of year
- Step 3: Determine points within ±3 std. devs. of current price
- Step 4: Convert to discrete values using K-S test
- Step 5: Calculate Z(T, U-Y)s and P(T, U)s for outcomes, then HPRs
- Step 6: After completing Step 5, calculate probability-weighted HPRs for all price increments
- Step 7: Find optimal f by parabolic interpolation or search algorithm

**Geometric mean HPR formula**:
(5.18a) G(f,T) = {∏[U = -3SD,+3SD](HPR(T,U))^(1/∑[U = -3SD,+3SD]P(T,U))}

**Optimizing optimal exit date**: run procedure for each day between now and expiration, find date with highest geometric mean

**As you work with f-day options**:
- Day 1: most positive mathematical expectation, least window
- Each passing day: expectation gets lower, window of X std. devs. expands (slower rate)
- Greatest mathematical expectations occur early, then decay gradually
- Wider windows increase variance, downside is fixed with option but upside is not

> "The rate of change between the two functions, time premium decay and the expanding window of X standard deviations, may create positive mathematical expectation for being long a, given option."

## Chapter 6: Correlative Relationships and the Derivation of the Efficient Frontier

### Definition of the Problem

**Efficient frontier of portfolios**: for given stocks, find those with **lowest expected risk for given level of expected gain** (levels determined by particular aversion to risk)

|------------|----------------|---------------------------|

**Expressing returns**: can express expected returns, HPR's by adding 1 to them

- Investor must examine past **returns and variances** over specified holding periods
- **Variance** is statistical variance of percentage returns
- User of E-V approach often performs **linear regression** to determine return (expected return) in next holding period, then variance calculated from past data points

### The Parametric Information

Need two parameters: **linear correlation coefficients** and **covariances** of returns

**Linear correlation coefficients**:
- Arrived at empirically (estimation) or by combination of two
- If using yearly data to determine expected returns and variance (yearly basis), then use yearly data
- If using daily data, then use daily data
- **Very important**: use data points at **same time frame** that you use to determine expected returns and variance

**Correlation coefficients of returns** (gains in stock price **not** underlying price of stocks):
- If you are using year-by-year returns, use yearly data for correlation coefficients
- If you are using daily returns, use daily data

**Covariance** between any two securities:
(6.01) COVa,b = Ra,b*Sa*Sb

where:
- COVa,b = covariance between ath security and bth one
- Ra,b = linear correlation coefficient between a and b
- Sa = standard deviation of ath security
- Sb = standard deviation of bth security

### Solving the Problem: Basic Parametric Information Compiled

Given covariance and standard deviations, can calculate linear correlation coefficient:
(6.02) Ra,b = COVa,b/(Sa*Sb)

**Next parameters defining E in E-V theory** (expected return of entire portfolio):
(6.05) ∑[i = 1,N]Xi*Ui = E

where:
- E = expected return of portfolio
- N = number of securities comprising portfolio
- Xi = percentage weighting of ith security
- Ui = expected return of ith security

**V portion of E-V theory** (variance in expected returns):
(6.06a) V = ∑[i = 1,N]∑[j = 1,N] Xi*Xj*COVi,j
(6.06b) V = ∑[i = 1,N]∑[j = 1,N] Xi*Xj*Ri,j*Si*Sj
(6.06c) V = (∑[i = 1,N]Xi^2*Si^2) + 2*(∑[i = 1,N]∑[j = i+1,N]Xi*Xj*COVi,j)
(6.06d) V = (∑[i = 1,N]Xi^2*Si^2/2)+2*∑[i = 1,N]∑[j = i+1,N]Xi*Xj*Ri,j*Si*Sj

All four forms of Equation (6.06) are equivalent

## Chapter 7: The Geometric Efficient Frontier (continued)

### Converting Arithmetic to Geometric HPR

- **Problem with arithmetic average HPR**: When reinvesting profits, must convert to geometric HPR for the vertical axis of efficient frontier
- **Conversion formula**: GHPR = (AHPR^2·V)^(1/2)
  - GHPR = geometric average HPR
  - AHPR = arithmetic average HPR
  - V = variance coordinate (standard deviation squared)

### Geometric TWR (Time to Wealth Ratio)

- **GTWR formula**: GTWR = GHPR^N
  - Shows vertical axis corresponding to given GHPR after N trades
  - Converts geometric average HPR to TWR
- **Arithmetic TWR formula**: ATWR = 1+N*(AHPR-1)
  - Shows what happens when not reinvesting

> "The geometrical optimal portfolio is one where a line drawn from (0,0), with slope 1, intersects the AHPR efficient frontier. That is, **it rows what you can expect on a one-trade basis.**"

### How Many Trades to Geometric Optimum?

- **Critical equation**: N = ln(.031)/ln(1.01542) = .035294/.015023 = 1.995075
  - Determines trades needed for geometric optimal portfolio to match a specific TWR goal
  - Uses natural logarithm function
- **ATWR needs to reflect two trades elapsed**: GTWR approaches ATWR at constant rate, but ATWR accelerates
- **Geometric and arithmetic TWRs**: Can be expressed as GHPR^N = (AHPR-1)*N + 1

### CML Lines for AHPR and GHPR

- **Both have CML lines**: Calculated from same risk-free rate
- **CML for GHPR equation**: CMLG = (CML*A^2·VT^P)/(1/2)
  - CMLG = E coordinate (vertical) to CML line for GHPR at given V coordinate
  - CMLA = E coordinate (vertical) to CML line for AHPR at given V coordinate

### Geometric Optimal Portfolio Conditions

- **RFR = GHPROPT-1**: When geometric average HPR equals risk-free rate, geometric optimal and tangent to CML are same
- **Higher drawdowns with higher GHPR**: Geometric optimal portfolio maximizes geometric growth but increases drawdown risk
  - Paradox: Want high geometric mean, but higher mean = greater drawdown percentage
  - Solution: View as obtaining highest geometric mean, not lowest drawdown

### Asset Allocation Philosophy

### Fractional f Strategies

#### Static Fractional f

- **Definition**: Trade portfolio at fractional f on unconstrained AHPR efficient frontier
  - Dispersion less than geometric optimal
- **Practice**: Combine portfolios by splitting equity into subaccounts
  - Initial fractional level > 0 (must be between 0 and 1)

#### Dynamic Fractional f

- **Split-equity technique**: Active equity in one subaccount, inactive in another
  - Only active equity determines contracts traded
  - Equity changes reflected in active portion only
- **Lower fraction = faster account growth**: As account equity increases, portfolio moves right on CML line
  - Dispersion remains constant as fraction of optimal f

### Four Asset Allocation Methods

1. **Investor utility method** (aka **feel good** or **Here, have some money**)
   - Willing to see X% drawdown, initially allocate X% to active equity
   - Complete retracement of active equity possible

2. **Professional management scenarios**
   - Dollar amounts invested matter (futures trade minimums)
   - Risk-free asset: Treasury Bills or long-term government/corporate bonds

3. **Scenario planning method**
   - Determine initial active equity amounts and reallocation probabilities
   - Regular intervals with probability assessments
   - **Optimal f of .11** example: 3 scenarios (50% drawdown, 25% no gain, 25% +300%)

4. **Portfolio insurance / dynamic hedging**
   - Buy put option on portfolio (floor value)
   - Typical strategy follows equity market with floor
   - **Noninvasive methods**: Using futures/options for delta adjustment

### Reallocation Techniques

#### Share Averaging Method

- **Pull out periodic percentage** of total equity (active + inactive)
- **Example**: Pull out X% monthly from total equity
- **Must benefit from dynamic fractional f**: Time required to terminate if equity stagnates
  - Formula: N = ln(INACTIVE)/ln(1-P)

#### Constant Dollar Withdrawals

- **Withdraw constant percentage** of total account equity each month
- **Bypass middle account**: Withdraw constant dollar from trading to buffer demand deposit
- **Buffer account**: Trader makes regular withdrawals of constant dollar

### Portfolio Insurance

- **Definition**: Akin to buying put option on portfolio
- **Floor value**: Worst portfolio can be worth (e.g., 90)
- **Dynamic hedging**: Continuously adjust portfolio to appropriate delta
  - Use put option pricing model to determine correlation coefficients
  - **Hedge ratio formula**: H = f*A/E
    - H = hedge ratio
    - f = optimal f (0 to 1)
    - A = active portion of funds
    - E = total equity

- **Problem**: Delta changes significantly for deep out-of-money options
  - Suggests >100% of account as active equity (impossible)
  - Upper limit: Use upside fraction of f as constraint

- **Linear correlation coefficient**: Positive arithmetic expectation tends toward positive correlation
  - Equity curves rise together (problematic for up-and-down days)

## Appendix B: Probability Distributions (continued)

### The Geometric Distribution
- **Discrete distribution** for independent Bernoulli trials measuring number
  of trials before first success/failure
- Probability density function: N(X) = Q^(X-1) × P
  - P = probability of success for a given trial
  - Q = probability of failure for a given trial
- Used to model: tossing die until specific number appears, waiting for first
  call to switchboard
- Example: tossing die until 5 appears—if we toss 6 times, mean is 1/.1667 = 6
  - Thus on average we expect to toss the die 6 times to get a 5
  - Repeating and recording how many tosses it takes yields the Geometric
    Distribution
- **Key relationship to Binomial**: As N (sample size) approaches infinity,
  Hypergeometric approaches Binomial as a limit
- Population size of 100-1000 is generally sufficient to approximate
  Hypergeometric with Binomial

### The Poisson Distribution
- Important **discrete distribution** used for modeling rare events occurring
  randomly over time
- Used for: incoming telephone calls, breakdowns, defects, equipment failures,
  typing errors, bacterial colonies, radioactive decay
- Events can occur at points in time (discrete), along a line (one dimension),
  area (two dimensions), or any N-dimensional continuum
- Arrival of events (X's) along a timeline modeled as: ●——●——●●●—●——●●●—
- Originally developed to model incoming telephone calls to a switchboard
- **Main difference from Binomial**: not appropriate for events that can occur
  more than once within a given time frame
  - Example: automobile accident over next 6 months—Binomial uses two distinct
    cases (occurs with probability P or doesn't with 1-P)
  - Poisson can account for multiple accidents in the time period
- Probability density function: N(X) = (L^X × EXP(-L))/X!
  - L = parameter of the distribution
- Properties:
  - Mean = 1/B (B.10)
  - Variance = L (B.18)
  - In Poisson, both mean and variance equal L
  - Therefore in example case: mean is 4 calls, variance is 4 calls, standard
    deviation is 2 calls (square root of 4)
- Figures B-9 and B-10 show Poisson with parameter values of .5 and 4.5
- When parameter L is small, distribution is reversed; when large, not
  reversed
- **Limiting relationship**: as L approaches infinity, Poisson approaches
  Binomial; as Binomial approaches infinity, it approaches Normal
- Very interesting relationship between Exponential and Poisson distributions
  - As N (sample size) becomes larger, differences between Hypergeometric and
    Binomial closer to each other
  - Hypergeometric approaches Binomial as a limit when N approaches infinity

### The Exponential Distribution
- Related to Poisson—continuous distribution with an ugly name sometimes
  called **Exponential Distribution**, sometimes **Negative Exponential
  Distribution**
- Used to model interarrival times in queuing systems (times on equipment
  before sudden failures, manufacturing defects, radioactive decay, etc.)
- Very interesting relationship between Exponential and Poisson distributions
- Arrival of calls to queuing system follows Poisson Distribution with arrival
  rate L
- The interarrival distribution (time between arrivals) is Exponential with
  parameter 1/L
- Probability density function N(X) for Exponential: N(X) = A × EXP(-A^X)
  - A = single parametric input, equal to 1/L in Poisson Distribution (must be
    greater than L in Poisson, must be greater than 0)
  - EXP() = exponential function
- Integral of (B.21): N(X), cumulative density function for Exponential is
  given as:
  - (B.22) N(X) = 1-EXP(-A^X)
  - A = single parametric input, equal to 1/L in Poisson Distribution (must be
    greater than 0)
  - EXP() = exponential function
- Figures B-14 and B-15 show Exponential Distribution functions

### Infinite Variance Concept
- If there are three or more degrees of freedom, variance of the distribution
  equals: (B.26) Variance = V/(V-2) for V>2
  - V = degrees of freedom
- Suppose two independent random variables

  - S = sample standard deviation
  - N = size of sample
  - U = population mean

- We measure variance of daily closing prices for the next year and record

- Farther we go into more degrees of freedom, variance approaches infinity
