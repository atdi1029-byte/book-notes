# Anatomy of the Bear: Lessons from Wall Street's Four Great Bottoms

**Author:** Russell Napier
**Pages:** 439
**Category:** Analytical Finance / Market History

## Summary

Russell Napier analyzes the four occasions in the 20th century when US equities traded at more than a 70% discount to the replacement value of corporate assets: August 1921, July 1932, June 1949, and August 1982. Drawing on approximately 70,000 Wall Street Journal articles from the months surrounding each bottom, Napier identifies a remarkably consistent set of signals that preceded every great buying opportunity.

The book's central finding is that bear market bottoms share common features across vastly different monetary regimes (gold standard, Bretton Woods, free float) and economic conditions (deflation, stagflation, wartime controls). The q ratio falling below 0.3x, commodity price stabilization (especially copper), government bonds leading corporate bonds leading equities, good news being ignored by the market, final declines on low volume, and Dow Theory confirmation all appeared at every bottom.

Napier demolishes several popular myths: that bottoms require capitulation selling (all four occurred on low volume), that you should wait for bad news to end (good news was abundant at every bottom -- investors simply ignored it), that Fed easing reliably signals the bottom (it preceded it by months with significant interim losses), and that fiscal deficits prevent recovery (deficits expanded at every bottom while bull markets raged).

The book is especially valuable for its parallel structure -- each of the four parts covers the same topics (road to the bottom, course of the Dow, market structure, stock market, bond market, sentiment, price stability, liquidity, Dow Theory) -- allowing direct cross-comparison. The Conclusions distill tactical and strategic rules, including the "Einstein's questions" checklist for identifying the next great bottom.

## Key Themes

- **Valuation as destiny:** The q ratio below 0.3x has been the single most reliable indicator of generational buying opportunities
- **Price disturbance as catalyst:** A material shock to the general price level (inflation then deflation, or severe deflation alone) is required to push equities to extreme undervaluation
- **Bond-equity sequencing:** Government bonds bottom first, then corporate bonds, then equities -- a consistent pattern across all four episodes
- **Good news ignored:** Bear markets end not when bad news peaks, but when good news fails to move prices -- the market's inability to decline further on positive developments signals exhaustion
- **Time over crash:** Excluding the 1929-32 outlier, equities take approximately 14 years to travel from peak to trough valuation -- bear markets are slow grinds, not sudden crashes
- **Auto sector as canary:** Recovery in automobile sales preceded all four equity bottoms
- **Copper as leading indicator:** Copper price stabilization preceded or coincided with every equity bottom
