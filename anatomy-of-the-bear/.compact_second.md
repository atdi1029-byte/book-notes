## Part II (Continued): 1932 Bond Market

### Government Bond Collapse and Stabilization

- Long-term govt bond yield: 3.70% (Sept 1929) → 3.15% (June 1931) →
  spiked to 4.26% (January 1932) — even as wholesale prices fell 31%
- Sterling's departure from gold standard (Sept 1931) was the trigger:
  foreign central banks liquidated US dollar assets; domestic depositors
  hoarded cash over deposits → banks forced to liquidate government bonds
- NY Fed raised rediscount rate 1.5% → 3.5% on 16 October 1931 to
  defend gold standard exchange rate — further pressure on bonds
- **Government bond stabilisation** (early 1932): **Reconstruction Finance
  Corporation (RFC)** established January 1932 (borrowing power $1.5B,
  expanded to $3.3B by July Emergency Relief and Construction Act) —
  lent to banks, reducing need to liquidate bonds; Fed bond-buying
  from April 1932

### The Corporate Bond Collapse

- Corporate bond investors initially expected normal recession; **Baa-rated
  bond yields actually declined** from 6.12% (Sept 1929) to 5.65%
  (Sept 1930)
- First banking crisis (3Q 1930) ended complacency; by Dec 1930 Baa
  yields exceeded 6.7%
- Second banking crisis + internationalisation (1931): Baa yields jumped
  from 6.72% (April 1931) to 10.42% (December 1931)
- Corporate bond bottom: **June 1932** — Baa yield peaked at 11.52%,
  one-third higher than the highest yield ever previously recorded
- AAA corporate bonds: peak yield 5.41% June 1932 (highest since Dec 1921)
- Corporate bond market bottomed ~5 weeks **before** the equity bottom
  in July 1932

### What Turned the Corporate Bond Market

- **RFC** provision of credit to distressed banks and railroads alleviated
  forced selling
- **American Securities Investment Corporation** (ASI) — pool formed by
  major US commercial banks to buy bonds collectively; market surged on
  mere *announcement* before any buying occurred
- **Lausanne Agreement** directly boosted corporate bonds — German bonds
  jumped 10 points; commodity prices posted 3 consecutive weeks of gains
  in mid-July; by August advance "sustained for more than two months"
- Garner Plan ($1B bond issuance for public works) worried bond investors
  but never became law; **fears proved misplaced** — deficits ballooned
  without bond sell-off; bond bull market 1932–1949
- British war loan conversion: £2B 5% war loan converted to 3.5% perpetual;
  4% consols rose 8 points to £110 — just days before US equity bottom;
  likely redirected capital to US securities

### Key Sequencing at the 1932 Bottom

- **Government bonds bottomed:** January 1932
- **Corporate bonds bottomed:** early June 1932
- **Equity market bottomed:** 8 July 1932
- **Commodity prices stabilising:** visible by late May 1932
- Commodity stabilisation led corporate bonds by ~2 weeks and equities
  by ~6 weeks — deflationary process ending was the foundational signal

### Roosevelt and the 1932 Bottom

> *'But getting back to politics, boys, this spring is only going to be a
> preview of the presidential election in 1932. Then we'll have Democrats
> all the way from the White House to the street cleaners on every block.'*
> — James T. Farrell, *Judgement Day*

- **Counterintuitive**: WSJ reported Roosevelt as likely President was
  *depressing*, not supporting, equity prices — markets preferred a
  compromise "business-friendly" candidate
- Roosevelt campaign sent contradictory signals: called for "bold
  experimentation" (Oglethorpe commencement, 23 May), yet promised to
  cut government spending 25% and balance the budget (Pittsburgh, 19 Oct)
- **Guaranty Trust Co.** told clients Roosevelt's election would have
  little material impact on financial markets
- **Napier's conclusion**: Roosevelt's nomination was coincidental with,
  not causal of, the July 1932 bottom; first setback in the new bull
  began *with* Roosevelt's November election and lasted until his March
  inauguration — markets feared gold standard commitment
- Despite abandoning gold, criminalising gold hoarding, and devaluing
  the dollar from $20.67 to $35/oz (Jan 1934), the bull market raged on

> *There had never been a bear market like the bear market of 1929-32.
> The market declined 89%, far surpassing the worst previous declines of
> 45% in the panics of 1857 and 1907. As in 1921, equity prices did not
> stop declining until they reached a 70% discount to replacement value.*

## Part III: June 1949

> *When we isolate the four most profitable times to invest in stocks,
> June 1949 comes second on our list after July 1932, but well ahead
> of 1942.*

- June 1949 ranks #2 for long-term returns (40-year horizon benefits
  from 1982-89 bull)
- April 1942 was also a rare bear bottom (q below 0.3x) but subsequent
  returns from 1949 significantly exceeded those from 1942

### The Road to June 1949

- World utterly transformed: WWII killed 55 million; US military grew
  from 250,000 to 12+ million; 300,000 Americans did not return
- **Equity market a backwater**: daily NYSE turnover often below $500,000;
  speculative enthusiasm focused on property, not stocks
- On 13 June 1949 — the day DJIA hit its postwar low — NY Times headline
  was about a heat wave ("1,500,000 Cool off at City's Beaches")

### The Course of the Dow — 1932-37

- July 1932 to March 1937: DJIA rose from 41.22 to 194.15, a **370% gain**
  with GDP deflator rising only 11%
- Dividend yield at July 1932 bottom: **10%**; dividends grew 60% to 1937
- Net national product grew **12% per annum** (real) from trough 1933 to
  peak 1937 — "Such economic growth rates have not been recorded before
  or since"
- S&P Composite earnings rose **176%** from 1932 to 1937; despite this,
  1937 earnings still below 1929/1928/1926/1925/1917/1916 levels
- Powerful combination: **surging earnings + surging dividends + falling
  interest rates** (excess capacity kept inflation low despite rapid growth)

### Volume Collapse — Evidence of Investor Disengagement

- From 1929 record trading day to 1942 low: NYSE volume fell **99%**
- **Turnover ratio**: 119% (1929) → 30% (1937) → 9% (1942) → 13% (1949)
- Bear market disinterest in 1949 higher than even worst Depression years
  of 1921 (59%) and 1932 (32%)
- NYSE market cap in 1949 **exceeded** the 1929 level — almost no one
  wanted to participate
- NYSE seat prices: $495,000 (1929) → $68,000 low (1932) → $17,000 (1942)
  — 75% decline from 1932 even as DJIA doubled; recovered 1942-46, then
  slid to $35,000 low in 1949

### The 1937-42 Bear

- By peak of recovery in 1937, **unemployment still at 14.3%**
- Fed began raising **reserve requirements** August 1936; raised margin
  on securities from 25% to 50% (using new **Banking Act** powers)
- Federal deficit/GDP fell from 4.2% (1936) to 2.8% (1937); corporate
  profitability fell on higher labour costs
- Industrial production fell one-third from 1937 peak to 1938 low;
  **S&P earnings declined almost 50%**
- In 12 months from March 1937, **DJIA declined 49%**, back to mid-1933 levels
- From 1938 low, S&P earnings doubled by December 1941 — but DJIA unchanged;
  investors lost faith in return to pre-1929 normalcy
- UK declared war on Germany: **DJIA rose 15%** in first 8 trading days
  of September 1939 (investors recalled US neutrality and WWI profits)
- Hitler's **blitzkrieg** (10 May 1940): **DJIA declined 23%** in 15 days
- **Lend-Lease** (March 1941): US receiving credit not cash; market disliked it
- **Pearl Harbor** (7 Dec 1941): market continued gradual decline, bottoming
  **28 April 1942** — five months later, down another 20%
- April 1942: **q ratio** at 70%+ discount to replacement value — a level
  only seen in 1921 and 1932

### The 1942-46 Bull — Fed Pegs the Yield Curve

- April 1942: Fed fixed **Treasury bill rate at 0.375%** for duration of war;
  de facto capped the entire federal yield curve until March 1951
- Maximum yield on longest-term government bonds: **2.5%**

| Security | Capped Yield |
|---|---|
| Treasury bills | 0.375% |
| 1-yr certificates | 0.875% |
| Short bonds | 2.0% |
| Longer bonds | 2.25% |
| 25-30 yr bonds | 2.50% |

> During this period, the Fed, in the words of its chairman **Marriner
> Eccles**, 'merely executed Treasury decisions.'

- With bonds yielding ~2.5% and equities yielding **11%+ in 1942**,
  the relative case for equities was compelling
- Tax receipts as % of GNP: 7% (1941) → **21% (1945)**; top income tax
  rate hit **90%**
- Excess profits tax reached flat rate of **95% by 1943**
- Result: S&P earnings and dividends basically unchanged 1946 from 1942
- The 1942-46 bull was driven **entirely by valuation expansion**,
  not earnings growth — against a background of negative real bond yields
- Bull market peaked May 1946 — **DJIA still 44% below September 1929
  all-time high**

### The 1946-49 Bear: Inflation vs. Deflation Debate

- Bulls expected pent-up consumer demand + return of normal corporate tax rates
- Bears remembered post-WWI Armistice: soaring inflation → sharpest annual
  deflation in US history + vicious bear market
- In 1946-49 bear, **S&P earnings increased nearly 200%** — yet equities fell
- **Sewell Avery** (Montgomery Ward): studied commodity price chart back to
  19th century; concluded postwar depression was foregone conclusion;
  loaded up on government bonds instead of investing in his business —
  deflation never came, bet proved disastrous

> "Prices rose and fell recurrently… The pattern had repeated itself as
> recently as 1919-1920… To Avery, a postwar depression was therefore a
> foregone conclusion. 'Who am I to argue with history?' he asked."
> — James Grant, *Money of the Mind*

### Why This Time Was Different

- **Employment Act of 1946** enshrined "maximum employment" as government mandate
- **Bretton Woods** (July 1944) explicitly designed to make deflation less likely
- Government sector now 15% of GDP vs. 3% in late 1920s
- Non-agricultural union membership: 33% of workforce by 1949 (up from
  13% in 1932) — wage pressure built into the system
- Wholesale price index rose **32% in 1946** alone when price controls lifted
- Post-WWII deflation: only **7.9%** (Aug 1948–June 1950) vs. **45%**
  post-WWI — dramatically muted

### Cold War Overlay

- **Churchill**, Missouri, March 1946: "an iron curtain has descended"
- **Truman Doctrine** (March 1947); **Marshall Plan**; Berlin Blockade
  May 1948 → Allied airlift; **NATO** formed April 1949
- Soviet atomic bomb detonated **29 August 1949** — "hot war" fears
- Soviet failure to act militarily over NATO eased fears; cold war arms race
  better for equities — echoes of US rearmament boom 1915-16

> "For over three years fears of what is now occurring in the business
> world have dampened market sentiment... There should be room for
> plenty of cheer in the realisation that: There isn't going to be
> a war…" — broker **Sam Smith**, Bache & Co., *WSJ*, 12 July 1949

### Structure of the Market in 1949

- NYSE market cap (end May 1949): **$64 billion** — one-third below Sept 1929
  but four times the 1932 bottom
- 1,457 listed equity securities; 1,043 companies; average market cap **$58 million**
- Investment management industry decimated: only **150 investment management
  companies** registered with SEC; total AUM = **$2.7 billion** — less than
  5% of US equity market
- Daily trading: ~900 of 1,500 listed issues; monthly volume just **$526
  million** (vs. $1,946 million at January 1946 peak)
- Market trading **half-days on Saturday** — liquidity extremely thin

### NYSE Sector Composition: 1932 vs. 1949

- **Oil** rose from 10.9% → 16.0% of market cap (1st by 1949)
- **Chemicals** rose 7.8% → 9.0% (2nd)
- **Utilities** fell 15.5% → 6.7%; **Railroads** 8.7% → 5.1%
- **Retail** and **Autos** grew significantly

### Sector Returns: June 1932 – June 1949 (Best/Worst)

**Best performers (total return):**
- Beer: +3,993% (repeal of Prohibition Dec 1933)
- Games: +2,606%
- Wholesale: +2,396%
- Autos: +2,201%
- Services: +2,199%

**Worst performers:**
- Utilities: +280%; Tobacco: +398%; Coal: +489%; Comms: +527%

> "The last shall be first and the first last." — Matthew's Gospel
> (Napier's summary of sector rotation from 1929-32 leaders to 1932-49
> laggards)

- 1946-49 bear (DJIA -24%): utilities again defensive (-9.7%);
  transport -33.4%; oil sole large-sector gainer (+5.8%)

### The Bond Market in 1949

- NYSE-listed bonds: $128B market value in 1949 vs. $32B in 1932
- But number of NYSE bond issues fell from 1,600 (1932) to 912 (1949) —
  lowest since 1905
- Corporate bond market shrinking: 1932-1945, corporate bond retirements
  exceeded new issues; net retirements $42.8B
- **Government bonds dominated**: federal debt grew from $19.5B (1932) to
  $168.6B (1949 face value) — federal debt market alone ~3x the equity market
- NYSE bond trading bottomed 1949: $3M/day average; less than $1B traded
  for entire year — under 1% of $128B outstanding

> "For every $1,000 Government bond sale recorded on the Exchange there
> were $1,000,000 par value unrecorded transactions made by a very few
> banking houses known as Government bond dealers." — C.F. Childs

- Foreign bond market collapsed: 202 foreign bond issues on NYSE at Sept
  1929; only 46 by June 1949 DJIA bottom; countries below half par: Chile,
  Colombia, Costa Rica, Greece, Italy, Mexico, Peru, Serbia, Yugoslavia

> **Benjamin Graham** dismissed foreign bonds as having 'a bad investment
> history since 1914'… "Yet every few years, market conditions are
> sufficiently favourable to permit the sale of some new foreign issues
> at a price about par." — *The Intelligent Investor*

### Valuation at the 1949 Bottom

- DJIA at June 1949: back to February 1926 level — 57% below the 1929
  all-time high, nearly 20 years of zero capital progress
- **CAPE** fell to **5.8x earnings by June 1949** — even lower than 1932's 9.4x
- **Q ratio**: 0.43x (end 1932) → 0.36x (end 1949)
- Economy expanded materially (real GDP per capita +62%, 1926-49) while
  equities went nowhere
- Government's share of economy: expenditure rose from 3% to 15% of GDP
- **"Normalised" earnings in 1949 were only 33% higher than normalised
  1932 earnings** — deeply disappointing vs. nominal GDP growth
- Nearly 2/3 of all profit growth in 1932-49 occurred in just 1945-49
  (driven by repeal of **excess profits tax**); investors doubted sustainability

### Good News and the Bear (WSJ Headlines, Spring/Summer 1949)

> *"As in 1921 and 1932, the market's failure to respond to good news
> was more indicative of the end of the bear market than any proliferation
> of bad news."*

- 21 Apr: Q1 earnings better than expected
- 22 Apr: International Harvester farm equipment sales +30% YoY
- 28 Apr: U.S. Steel, Du Pont, General Motors Q1 reports show improvements
- 2 May: 197 firms Q1 earnings $732M — 21% above 1948 (WSJ survey)
- 12 May: Treasury Secretary Snyder — postwar adjustments "practically completed"
- 2 July: Unemployment jumped to 7-year high of 3,778,000 — but increase
  entirely in under-25 age group; non-farm adult employment actually rose
- **DJIA bottomed June 1949; NBER recession end date: October 1949** —
  equity market led the economic recovery by 4 months
- 1 August: NAPA purchasing survey — order bookings 30% reporting increases
  vs 28% decreases (first such signal since October 1948)
- 6 August: Fed cuts reserve requirements of member banks — freeing
  **$1,800 million** for investment or lending

### Auto Industry as Bear Bottom Indicator

- US auto sales: 2.1M (1946) → **5.1M (1949)** vs. 3.8M pre-war (1941)
- Average car age rose from 5.33 years (1941) to 8.73 years (1948);
  13 million cars 10+ years old vs. 5 million pre-war → secular demand
- Across all four bear market bottoms, **auto sector recovery preceded**
  the broader market bottom — products with low diffusion rates (rising
  from low ownership base) are first to benefit

### Price Stability and the Bear (1949)

> *"In 1949, as in 1921 and 1932, a return of general price stability
> coincided with the end of the bear market in equities."*

- Wholesale price index peaked August 1948, fell 7.5% by June 1949 DJIA
  bottom — only marginal further decline followed
- Post-WWII deflation: only **7.9%** decline vs. **45%** post-WWI
- Inventory liquidation had depressed prices; with stocks at rock bottom,
  deflation abated — key confirming feature
- April 1949: rents and food rising; June: wheat and oil prices improved;
  July: price rises in lead, rayon, poultry, zinc, copper

### Liquidity and the Bear (1949)

- Critical distortion: Fed's wartime obligation to **support government bond
  prices** made it a provider of unlimited high-powered money on autopilot
- When reserve requirements raised (Feb 1948) to slow inflation, banks sold
  government securities → Fed forced to buy to support prices → accidentally
  **expanded credit** while trying to tighten it; policy tool self-defeating
- Dec 1948–Oct 1949: only a **25% contraction** in Fed credit outstanding —
  then stopped; far smaller than post-WWI (69%) or 1928-31 (50%)
- **Fed balance sheet gave NO good buying signal** at the 1949 bottom

> *"Though the balance sheet of the Fed did not give a good indication of
> when to buy equities, changes in its policy stance did provide timely
> signals."*

- **Timeline of easing signals**:
  - March 1949: first easing of consumer credit controls (imposed Sept 1948)
  - May 1949: reserve requirement reductions began
  - 29 June 1949: Fed confirms it would stop preventing bond price rises
- Investors buying on first easing signal (March 1949) saw DJIA fall further
  10% before bottoming 13 June — still far better than waiting for balance sheet
- **Commercial bank loans** bottomed June 1949 (coinciding with DJIA bottom);
  grew 3.2% by November — better real-time indicator than Fed balance sheet
- **Broad money (M2)**: first improvement only Q2 1950 — useless for timing

| Episode | First policy easing signal | DJIA bottom | Balance sheet bottom |
|---------|---------------------------|-------------|----------------------|
| 1921 | Discount rate cut (May) | Aug 22 | Jul 1922 |
| 1949 | Consumer credit easing (Mar) | Jun 13 | Oct 1949 |
| 1932 | Rate cuts from Nov 1929 | Jul 1932 | — |

### The Bulls and the Bear (1949 Sentiment)

- **Myth of universal pessimism**: plenty of bullish voices at the bottom
  (as in 1921 and 1932) — waiting for universal pessimism means missing the turn
- 13 April: Broad Street Investing Corp — fund yield above 5% only in
  1932, 1911, 1942 (all 'highly auspicious buying occasions'); current yield 5.25%
- 19 Apr — Paine Webber's Harry D Comer: 20 common stocks yielding 8-12%,
  each earning 50%+ more than dividend payments, 20+ years uninterrupted dividends
- 25 Apr — Arthur Weisenberger: stocks never lower relative to dividends
  "except at the critical points of WWI and II and in the depths of our worst
  depressions, in 1932 and 1873"
- 2 May: Wall Street's largest houses lending "more stock than at any time
  since 1929" — massive **short interest** as a cushion against further decline
- 19 May: **Short interest** reaches 1,628,551 shares — largest in 16 years
  (since Feb 27, 1933); ratio to daily volume = 213 days of trading
- 6 June: L.O. Hooper compiles list of **225 stocks selling below working capital**
- **13 June: DJIA bottoms at 161.6**
- 14 June: Final decline on **low volume** — consistent with 1921 and 1932
- 15 June: Bear market since May 29, 1946 lasted **3 years** — "longer than
  any other, exceeding even September 1929 to July 1932"
- 29 June — Keystone Co. of Boston: DJIA 30 stocks historically sell at ~15x
  earnings; bull peaks ~25x; bear troughs ~10x; currently **6-7x** trailing
  12-month earnings
- 21 July: Short interest = **1,644,313** shares; ratio to daily turnover
  = **2.40 to 1** — highest since May 1938

> "Traders should not overlook that the market is resting on a potential keg
> of dynamite... the latent power of the extremely large short interest is
> tremendous. Very few of the amateur shorts will succeed."
> — Herbert G. King, 25 July 1949

- 5 Aug — Ralph Rotnem (Harris, Upham): stocks yield **4%, more than bonds** —
  widest spread in 20 years — extreme relative value

### Analytical Summary: What the 1949 Bottom Reveals

- **Value was early but not wrong**: value investors beating the drum from
  early 1947 — yet DJIA still fell a further 10% over 18 months before bottoming
- By June 1949, S&P PE was **~60% below** long-term average — driven not by
  large price declines but by explosion in earnings
- **Q ratio** had been below geometric mean since **1939** — market traded
  below fair value for a full decade before the bottom

> *"As early as 1939, the q ratio of the market was below its geometric mean...
> In seeking the bottom it is important to determine equities are below fair
> value but also important to look at other factors if one is to avoid investing
> too early and watching cheap equities become very cheap."*

- Market's failure to respond to bad news = key leading indicator
- **Bear market ends on low volume** — final decline NOT accompanied by
  climactic selling; rebound begins on low volume; short interest does NOT
  capitulate immediately
- Rising prices with rising volumes after first rebound confirms the move
- Dow Theory correctly identified the bottom within a few days in
  **1921, 1932, and 1949** — three for three

### Bonds and the Bear (1946-1949)

- US long government bonds reached yield low of **2.03%** April 1946;
  declined requiring massive Fed support
- Fed's government bond holdings rose **129%** in 1948 alone to defend
  the yield peg; govt bond rally not until November 1948 (yields at 2.18%)
- **Moody's AAA corporate bonds**: yield low 2.46% April 1946; rose to 2.90%
  January 1948; rallied to 2.57% by end of 1949
- **Moody's Baa**: low 2.94% April 1946; rose to 3.56% January 1948
- Corporate bond prices bottomed **January 1948** — 10 months before
  government bond rally and **16 months before equity market bottom**
- In 1921 and 1932: government bonds → corporate bonds → equities;
  in 1949, corporates rallied *before* government bonds due to Fed yield-peg
  distortion — likely investors switched from pegged govt rates to higher-
  yielding corporate paper

> *"It seems more likely the 1921 and 1932 sequence of stabilisation of
> government bonds followed by corporate bonds followed by equities is more
> likely to be the normal sequence of events."*

- As in 1921 and 1932, equity prices stopped falling in 1949 when they
  reached a **~70% discount to replacement value of corporate assets**
- All three episodes (1921, 1932, 1949) occurred under a **fixed-exchange-
  rate regime** — Napier raises the question of whether these signals would
  work in a **free-float** environment (1982 test still pending at chapter's end)

## Part IV: August 1982

> *The old textile plants given over to discount clothing outlets… acres of
> dead railroad track and car shops and stockpiled wheels and empty boxcars
> stick in the heart of the city like a great rusting dagger.*
> — John Updike, *Rabbit is Rich*

- By August 1982, the **S&P Composite Index** in real terms was back where
  it had first been in **August 1906** — a 76-year round trip
- Key question: did systemic inflation (not war-driven, cyclical inflation)
  alter the nature of bear markets and their ultimate denouement?

### Why 1974 Ranks Fifth (Not Fourth) Among Great Bear Bottoms

**Q Ratio at Five Bear Market Bottoms:**

| Year | Q Ratio Year-End | Q Ratio at Market Bottom |
|------|-----------------|--------------------------|
| 1921 | .35 | .28 |
| 1932 | .43 | .30 |
| 1949 | .36 | .29 |
| 1974 | .36 | .35 |
| 1982 | .38 | .27 |

**Real S&P Returns from Market Bottoms:**

| Year | 5 Years | 10 Years | 15 Years |
|------|---------|----------|----------|
| 1921 | +106% | +152% | +211% |
| 1932 | +209% | +46% | +92% |
| 1949 | +84% | +237% | +343% |
| 1974 | +9% | +21% | +114% |
| 1982 | +143% | +163% | +414% |

- **Cyclically adjusted PE** in December 1974: 11.2x — well above 7.4x
  (August 1921) and 4.7x (July 1932); above 9.9x (August 1982)
- 1974's good 15-year return was largely earned *after* the 1982 bottom
- Real S&P price index in 1982 was **13% below** December 1974 level
  (per Robert Shiller)

### The Course of the Dow: 1949-68

- **Bull market**: June 1949 to December 1968 — S&P Composite +662% nominal,
  +413% real; 8 corrections >10%; **Kennedy break** of 1962 the biggest at 27%
- Kennedy break: JFK branded anti-business for blocking steel price hikes;
  Cuban Missile Crisis (Oct 22-28, 1962) barely moved the market
- **Institutional shift**: pension fund assets grew 11-fold 1952–68; mutual
  fund assets 13-fold; institutional equity holdings: 9.5% of NYSE market
  cap (1952) → 21.3% (1968)
- **Yield gap reversal** — structural driver: since 1792, dividend yields on
  equities always exceeded bond yields; institutional shift out of bonds into
  equities pushed S&P dividend yield *below* long-term government bond yields
  in **July 1957** — permanently; by Dec 1968 peak: S&P yield = 2.88%,
  long-term government bonds = 5.65%
- Root cause: acceptance that postwar infrastructure had made **inflation
  structurally permanent**, eliminating the advantage of bonds' fixed payments
- Valuation expansion drove the bull: q ratio 0.49x → 1.06x; PE 5.8x → 18.5x;
  cyclically adjusted PE 11.7x → 25.1x; earnings grew +150% vs. S&P +662%
- **LBJ's "Great Society"** (January 1965): fiscal largesse rivalling FDR's
  New Deal; combined with Vietnam spending, spooked bond market
- Fed held rates steady (discount rate 4.0% in Oct 1967 = same as Sept 1959)
  even as inflation rose 1.23% to 3.56% — first hike: 50bp November 1967
- Market peaked soon after Nixon's November 1968 election victory →
  14-year bear market began

### The Course of the Dow: 1968-82

- **S&P Composite declined 63% in real terms** December 1968 to July 1982
- Bear market really began in 1969 as inflation moved above **3%**
- 1970s inflation averaged **7.1%** — beyond threshold for positive real equity returns
- Nixon suspended dollar-gold redemption **15 August 1971**; dollar devalued
  Dec 1971 ($35→$38/oz), then again 1973 ($42); by March 1973 free float confirmed
- In real terms, S&P hit its low in **July 1982**, 13% below Dec 1974 low —
  back to levels first surpassed in **June 1905**
- **Arthur Burns** became Fed chairman January 1970; Nixon on swearing him in:

> *I respect his independence. However, I hope that independently he will
> conclude that my views are the ones that should be followed.*

### The Nifty Fifty Disaster

- By 1972, investors concentrated in "one-decision" growth stocks —
  the **"Nifty Fifty"**; investors paid average **PE of 42x** in 1972
- These 50 stocks suffered average **62% price decline** in 1973-74
- Counterintuitive: for long-term holders (Nov 2001 horizon), Nifty Fifty
  delivered annual return of 11.62% vs. S&P 12.14% — nearly market returns

### Earnings Failure and Inflation's Equity Swindle

- Nominal EPS increased just **143%** vs. nominal GDP up **246%** —
  a 12% **real decline** in earnings
- Both poor earnings growth AND valuation compression (q ratio 1.06x → 0.38x)
- **Warren Buffett**, Fortune, May 1977:

> *In the decade ending in 1965, manufacturing companies had an average
> annual pre-tax margin on sales of 8.6%. In the decade ending in 1975,
> the average margin was 8%. Margins were down despite a very considerable
> increase in the inflation rate.*

### Burns, Nixon, and the Inflation Spiral

- **OPEC oil shock**: oil price $3.12/barrel (Oct 1973) → $11.63 (Dec 1973)
- Annual inflation surged from 2.9% (Aug 1972) to **12.5% by December 1974**
- Sharp recession (Nov 1973–Mar 1975) **failed** to bring inflation under control
- April 1973: term **"stagflation"** first appears in *Wall Street Journal*;
  coined November 17, 1965 by Conservative MP **Ian Macleod** in House of Commons
- Second oil shock: Iranian revolution January 1979, oil price +150%
- 1978: President Carter appointed **G. William Miller** (CEO/lawyer) as Fed chairman
- Gold exceeded **$300/oz** by August 1979 (vs. $35 under Bretton Woods)
- **Paul Volcker** appointed Fed chairman **6 August 1979**; raised rates
  (4-3 FOMC vote); convened **secret FOMC meeting October 6** — new policy:
  target M-1 aggregate growth; Fed funds rate range set **11.5-15.5%**

> *When Volcker took office, inflation was almost 12%. When he left, it
> was just 4%.*

### The 1980 Credit Shock and Its Aftermath

- Carter imposed **credit controls** March 1980; Q2 1980 GDP declined at
  **fastest rate ever recorded**; Fed Funds fell 20% → 8% in three months
- By end of 1980, Fed funds pushed to new high again; **May 1981** fight
  resumed — Fed funds back above **20% by July**
- Bond market did not believe inflation was beaten until **September 1981** —
  start of a 20+ year bond rally
- **Donald Regan** (Treasury Secretary) recalled Volcker saying he'd ease
  if Congress moved on deficits — standoff kept real rates high

### Financial Crisis Catalyzes the 1982 Bottom

- May 1982: **Drysdale Government Securities** went bust
- Early July 1982: **Penn Square Bank** (Oklahoma) failed — had originated
  $1B+ in loans sold to Continental Illinois
- August 1982: **Mexico** publicly bust; US commercial banks faced massive
  write-downs
- **Walter Wriston** (Citibank) at IMF annual meeting, Toronto, August 1982:

> *We had 150-odd finance ministers, 50-odd central bankers, 1,000
> journalists, 1,000 commercial bankers, a large supply of whiskey…
> 'the end of the world is coming'. It was the Titanic. We were just
> rearranging the deck chairs.*

- FOMC on June 30 — aware Mexico would default — changed direction
  despite M-1 growth
- Fed funds rate: above **14% early July → below 9% by early September**
- This was a **de facto abandonment** of money-supply-growth targeting
- Equity market finally bottomed in **August 1982** with public announcement
  of Mexican crisis and the Fed's pivot

### Structure of the Market in 1982

- NYSE market cap: **$1,143bn (36% of GDP)** vs. $64bn (23% of GDP) in 1949
- Public ownership via intermediaries: **4% of adults (1952) → 28% (1985)**
- Average holding period just over **two years** vs. five years 1949-1981
- Listed companies: 1,043 (1949) → 1,526 (1982); average market cap
  grew from $58m (1949) to **$639m (1982)**
- **Oil** remained #1 sector at every bear-market bottom (1932, 1949, 1982)
- Finance rose from **16th largest sector (1949) to 2nd largest (1982)**

**Sector total returns (1949-1982) — Best:**
- Business Equipment: **80.2x**; Health: 62.7x; Meals: 55.1x

**Worst:** Coal: 6.1x; Chemicals: 16.9x; Steel: 17.9x

**Sectors producing positive real total returns in 1968-82 bear:**
- Tobacco: **+420%** (also best in deflationary 1929-32 bear)
- Telecoms: +194%; Oil: +185%; Health: +180%; Coal: +180%
- Only **5 of 30** industrial sectors produced positive real returns
- S&P Composite up 82% nominally but CPI up **174%** — a real loss

**Bond market scale by 1982:**
- Total US fixed interest: $303bn (end 1952) → **$2,246bn (2Q 1982)**
- 30-year 2½% bond (constant maturity): declined from 101 (1946) to
  17 (1981) — an **83% loss** (Homer & Sylla)
- **Moody's Baa** corporate bond yield reached 17% by February 1982 —
  vs. peaks of 8.6% (1921) and 11.6% (1932)

### Valuations at the 1982 Bottom

- By August 1982, DJIA back to April 1964 levels; real terms: back to
  April 1928
- **q ratio** year-end 1982 = 0.38x; mid-year low probably ~**0.27x**
- **Cyclically adjusted PE**: 9.9x vs. 1881-1982 average of 15.8x
- Q ratio below geometric mean for **almost nine years** before the bottom
- Historic PE below **10x since March 1977** — market looked cheap for
  years before the bottom; took **14 years** to travel from peak valuation
  to trough — consistent with 1921 (~16 years) and 1949 (~13 years);
  1932 was the outlier at 3 years (required 89% DJIA decline)

| Bottom | q ratio (years) | CAPE (years) |
|--------|----------------|--------------|
| 1921   | 16             | 20           |
| 1932   | 3              | 3            |
| 1949   | 13             | 13           |
| 1982   | 14             | 17           |

**Key lesson**: barring a crash of 1929-32 magnitude, expect ~14 years
for equities to travel from extreme overvaluation to extreme undervaluation.

### Good News and the Bear (Summer 1982)

- 14 June: Business inventories cut at fastest real rate since WWII;
  consumer expectations improving; new car sales +5.9% YoY; 10% tax cut
- 16 June: Auto sales pace ~8.3M units; **Paul Volcker** says consumer
  spending "tend to accompany a levelling out of a recession"
- 17 June: Housing starts up 22% in May, above 1M-unit annual rate
  for first time in 10 months
- 22 June: Economy expanding at 0.6% annual rate — first growth since
  Q3 1981; **David Stockman** says "We are past the trough"
- 1 July: **Alan Greenspan** says recovery signs visible "in a matter of weeks"
- 2 August: Q2 price deflator 5% — half the 1981 level of 9.4%
- 6 August: **Blue Chip Economic Indicators** consensus: 3.3% real GNP growth
  in next 12 months — all 40+ forecasters expected at least some growth
- **Actual outcome**: Q2 1982 real GDP posted 2.2%; economy grew 3.2% in
  following 12 months — consensus forecast of 3.3% was almost exactly right

> *"Once again, as in 1921, 1932 and 1949, the bottom of a bear market is
> not marked by the absence of good news, but rather by an increasing supply
> of good news ignored by the market."*

### Auto Sector as Lead Indicator (1982)

- Monthly low for new car registrations: January 1982; by June, down
  only 4% YoY (vs. much steeper earlier declines)
- In 1982, car prices rose 1.9% but US prime lending rate fell from
  >20% to <16% — declining effective financing cost triggered demand recovery
- **New England recovery** preceded all four equity bottoms by just weeks —
  WSJ (19 July 1982) reported Northeast's perennially depressed states
  coming through "with flying colors" driven by tech/telecom boom

### Price Stability and the Bear (1982)

- **CRB index** fell >30% from November 1980 peak to October 1982 low
- Metals component (CRB Spot Metals) bottomed in **June 1982** —
  two months *before* the equity bottom in August
- **Copper** as leading indicator: bottomed August 1921, June 1949, July
  1932, and led the equity bottom in 1982 as well

> *"In all four of the great bear market bottoms analysed in this book,
> the end of the decline in commodity prices marks the end of the great
> bear market in equities."*

> *"On every occasion, the rebound in copper prices preceded or coincided
> with the rebound in the equity market."*

- CRB index rose **24%** in ten months after October 1982; DJIA rose
  >50% in under a year
- Monetary system change (gold standard → flexible rates) did NOT
  invalidate the commodity price signal

**Actionable framework**: look for (1) cheap valuations, (2) rising copper
prices, (3) low inventory positions, (4) evidence of actual (not forecast)
rising auto demand — when all present, "history suggests one is very close
to the bottom of the equity market."

### Liquidity and the Bear (1982)

- Non-borrowed reserves grew only **1.8%** in 21 months from July 1980
  to April 1982
- **May 1982 turning point**: seasonally-adjusted non-borrowed reserves
  grew 2.4% month-on-month; in next eight months expanded **10.6%**
- Signal hard to read in real time; NOW accounts distorted M-1 data
- **FOMC on 1 July** effectively abandoned money-supply targets (no
  announcement made)
- Volcker's 21 July Senate testimony was the first coded signal:

> *'Growth somewhat above the targeted ranges would be tolerated for a time
> in circumstances in which it appeared that precautionary liquidity
> motivations, during a period of economic turbulence, were leading to a
> stronger-than-anticipated demand for money.'*
> — Volcker, Senate Banking Committee, 21 July 1982

- Real catalyst for policy change: **Mexico's bankruptcy was imminent**;
  bond dealer **Lombard Wall** was in trouble; Volcker feared impact on
  US banking system of sovereign debt defaults
- Developing-country debt soared from **$100B (1973)** to ~$540B; expected
  to reach $640B — $327B owed to Western commercial banks
- Countries in trouble: Poland, Mexico, Argentina, East Germany, Brazil,
  Nigeria, Chile, Zaire, Yugoslavia, Bolivia, Venezuela, Peru, Tanzania,
  Sudan, Indonesia, Romania
- Long-term govt bond yields fell from ~14% to 10.5% (Aug-Dec 1982)

**Discount rate cuts as reliable signal:**
- May 1921 first cut → August 1921 bottom (20% intervening loss)
- March 1949 credit controls eased → July 1949 bottom (~10% loss)
- October 1981 first discount rate cut → August 1982 bottom (<10% loss)
- Exception: November 1929 first cut led to catastrophic losses

> **The rule**: Reductions in Fed interest rates in Fed-induced recessions
> (1921, 1949, 1982) are a good time to buy cheap equities. 1929 was NOT
> a Fed-induced recession — it was the consequence of German reparations,
> Allied debt repayments, and inept gold-standard reconstruction.

**Practical rule**: await a further **10% market decline** after the first
discount rate cut before buying — history shows minimal further downside
and very significant upside thereafter.

### The Bulls and the Bears (1982 Sentiment)

- 14 June: Eric Miller (DLJ) — "most stocks have seen their lows"
- 18 June: **James Dines**, the "ultimate gold bug," reversed 20 years of
  bullion advocacy, told subscribers to sell gold — "end of the gold bug era"
- 2 July: Howard Stein (Dreyfus Fund, $1.6B) — trimming utilities, moving
  cash back into equities, sees "50% gain" potential
- 14 July: Merrill Lynch, Goldman Sachs, Bear Stearns, E.F. Hutton all had
  lengthy buy lists — yet their top strategists all thought investors would
  be **better off in bonds**
- 30 July: Leon Cooperman (Goldman Sachs) — "entry into a classical bull
  market must be validated by further and sustained decline in interest rates"
- **12 August: DJIA bottoms**
- 13 August: Minshall (Capital Advisors Tulsa) — "a selling climax will be
  required to end the bear market" [day AFTER the bottom]
- 18 August: **Henry Kaufman** (Salomon Brothers) reverses long-standing
  pessimism — expects "substantial drops in interest rates," long-term
  Treasuries could fall to 9% from 12.5%
- 18 August: One-day DJIA **record rally** — largely attributed to Kaufman's
  reversal; Goldman Sachs tells clients to raise equity allocation to
  55% from 35%
- 19 August: Heavy institutional buying → NYSE one-day **volume record**;
  small investors mainly on the sidelines, sceptical
- 18 August: Alfred Goldman (A.G. Edwards) — "classic ingredient of a real
  bottom is capitulation by institutions, high disgust… they've been
  complacent, confident. It's unusual to get a bull market with so little pain"
- 29 September: Cooperman (Goldman) — "early stages of a bull market; average
  postwar bull market lasts 30 months, trough-to-peak advance of 66%"

**No Capitulation — A Cross-Bottom Pattern:**
- The 1982 bottom, like 1921, 1932, and 1949, **lacked a final high-volume
  sell-off**
- Capitulation may be more a feature of **trading-range markets** than true
  secular bottoms

> *"The signature of a true bear market bottom when equities are cheap:
> market trading sideways or drifting downward on low volume, subdued
> reaction to both good and bad news, then initial recovery on either
> limited or (as in 1982) large volume — confirmed by higher volume at
> higher price levels thereafter."*

- 1982 unique: initial rebound on **very high volumes** (opposite to
  prior episodes where rebound came on low volume)
- Peak short positions: 103.6M shares (14 May) declined to 96.4M by
  mid-August — then when market rallied, **short positions spiked to NEW
  RECORD of 120.5M on 15 September** — confirming the move was a permanent
  reversal

### Bonds and the Bear (1982)

- **Bond bear market in its fifth decade ended October 1981**
- Long-term Treasury yield: 2.03% (April 1946) → **15.1%** (first week
  of October 1981) — a 35-year bear market in bonds
- Real yield on govt bonds rose from 4.4% (September 1981) to **7.4%**
  (July 1982) — bond investors still sceptical despite nominal yield decline
- Government bond market technically bottomed September 1981, but major
  rally did not begin until **July 1982** — when Fed abandoned money supply
  growth targeting
- **Corporate bond sequence confirmed (1982)**:
  - Long-term US Treasury bottomed first week of October 1981
  - **Moody's Baa** yield peaked **mid-February 1982** (17.3%)
  - Baa yield premium over Treasuries peaked November 1982 — *after*
    major bull market in both corporate and government bonds had begun
- Pattern confirmed: **government bonds → corporate bonds → equities**
  (same as 1921 and 1932; 1949 distorted by Fed yield-cap)
- 1982 fiscal deficit: 3.9% of GDP (highest since WWII); reached 5.9%
  by 1985 (surpassing Roosevelt's peacetime peaks) — yet rates fell and
  economy recovered. Senate projected $103.9B deficit for FY1983; actual
  deficit was **$208 billion** — parallel to 1932: fiscal deterioration
  didn't prevent the bull market

> "Suffice to say, the indicators that signalled the bottom of the bear
> market in 1921, 1932 and 1949 worked again in 1982. This is particularly
> encouraging for investors seeking patterns." — Napier

## Conclusions: Strategic

> *Exit, pursued by a bear.*
> — William Shakespeare, *The Winter's Tale*

- Napier calls the indicator set **"Einstein's questions"** — the right
  questions to ask, requiring better-than-average answers to profit

### Valuation at Bear-Market Bottoms

- **q ratio** fell below **0.3x** at all four bear-market bottoms — the most
  reliable single valuation indicator
- **Cyclically adjusted PE** range at bottoms: 4.7x (1932) to 11.7x (1949);
  wide enough to be less useful as a precise signal; inflation-adjusted
  earnings still gives wide range: 5.2x to 9.1x
- **Equities become cheap slowly** — average nine years from peak q to trough
  (14 years excluding the 1929-32 outlier)
- US equity market reached highest-ever valuations in **March 2000**;
  history suggests slow multi-year grind to undervaluation follows
- Real GDP expanded ~52% and nominal GDP ~285% on average across the three
  long bear markets (ex-1929-32) — bear markets occur during expansion,
  not depression (except 1929-32)
- Inflation-adjusted earnings growth ranged from **-67% to +28%** across
  the four bottoms; nominal earnings -67% to +119% — enormous variance

### Price Disturbance as Catalyst

- A **material disturbance to the general price level** is required to push
  equities to cheap levels in all four cases:
  - 1921, 1949, 1982: high inflation followed by deflation (commodity-only
    in 1982)
  - 1932: no initial inflation, but severe deflation
- Price disturbance creates uncertainty about future earnings and the
  opportunity cost of holding bonds — compresses equity valuations
- Without such a disturbance, a bear market may NOT produce "buy of a
  generation" valuations

### Commodity Prices as Leading Signal

- All four bear-market bottoms occurred during **economic recession**
- **Return of price stability** (especially commodity prices) signals the bottom
- **Copper** specifically has been the most accurate commodity signal
- To assess whether price stability is sustainable: look for low inventories,
  rising demand at lower prices, producers selling below cost

### Government Bond Behavior

- Government bond sell-off accompanies at least part of every bear market
- Exception nuance: 1929-32 saw bonds *rally* September 1929 to June 1931
  before the sell-off began (lasting until January 1932)
- Even in the two deflationary bears (1921, 1932) there was some government
  bond sell-off

## Conclusions: Tactical

### Lead Times from Bond Bottom to Equity Bottom

| Year | Govt Bond Lead | DJIA Decline During Lag |
|------|----------------|------------------------|
| 1921 | 14 months      | 23%                    |
| 1932 | 7 months       | 46%                    |
| 1949 | 9 months       | 14%                    |
| 1982 | 11 months      | 6%                     |

- **A recovery in government bond prices precedes equity recovery** in all cases
- Corporate bond recovery leads equities by: 2 months (1921), 1 month (1932),
  5 months (1982); 15-17 months (1949, distorted by post-war controls)

### Fed Rate Cuts

- In the three long bear markets (ex-1929-32), **Fed rate cuts preceded
  equity bottoms** by ~3 months (1921, 1949) and 11 months (1982)
- DJIA decline during each lag was less than 20% in all three cases
- 1929-32 exception: Fed cut November 1929 while bear was still in infancy

### Additional Tactical Signals

- **Auto sector recovery** precedes equity market recovery
- **Bear-market bottoms characterised by good economic news being ignored** —
  bulls bang the drum but prices fall anyway
- **Worsening fiscal position** will be blamed for preventing recovery;
  these warnings will be wrong
- **Corporate earnings declines continue well past the equity bottom** —
  do not wait for earnings recovery to buy; earnings nadir came roughly
  **4-7 months AFTER** equity bottoms (average ~6 months)
- Bottom preceded by **market declining on high volume, rising on low
  volume**; final slump on very low volume; confirmation = rising volume
  at higher prices after first rebound
- **Short interest reaches peak at the bottom** and rises further in the
  first weeks of the new bull market
- **Dow Theory** works to signal a buy for equities (correct at all 4 bottoms)

> "Just as the possession of fur does not, of itself, permit the
> identification of an animal as a bear, the possession of any one of the
> features above should not be considered as constituting positive
> identification of its financial equivalent. Our list is the financial
> equivalent of Einstein's questions." — Napier

## Conclusions: Then and Now (2005 Assessment)

- Written in 2005; Napier applies the framework to the bear market begun 2000
- **q ratio** at end of 1999: all-time high of **2.9x** (geometric mean);
  cyclically adjusted PE was **170% above** its 1881-2005 average
- **From June 2005 levels**: cyclically adjusted PE would need to fall 40%
  to reach long-term average; 60-84% to reach prior bear-bottom levels
- **q ratio** in June 2005 was still **44% above fair value**; to match
  prior bear bottoms, would need to fall another 67% from there
- No general price disturbance had yet occurred (as of 2005)
- No meaningful bond market sell-off; no recession; no Fed rate cuts
- **Napier's conclusion**: DJIA likely to decline **at least 60%, possibly
  80%+** before the bear ends
- Predicted bear market end: "sometime after 2009, though probably nearer
  to 2014" — written in 2005, remarkably prescient about the GFC arc

> "If, as Milton Friedman asserts, inflation 'is everywhere and at all times
> a monetary phenomenon', the next general price disturbance is likely to be
> inflationary, given the current institutional framework." — Napier
