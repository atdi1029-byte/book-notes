Glossary

Valuation Metrics

q ratio: Market value of equities divided by the replacement cost of corporate assets. At fair value, q equals its geometric mean (roughly 1.0x). Below 0.3x signals a generational buying opportunity -- this threshold was breached at all four great bottoms.

Cyclically adjusted PE (CAPE): Price divided by the average of the prior ten years of inflation-adjusted earnings. Smooths out cyclical swings. Ranged from 4.7x (1932) to 11.7x (1949) at the four great bottoms.

Hindsight Value Method: Averaging returns across 40 discrete holding periods (1 to 40 years) from a given starting point. Used by Smithers & Wright to identify the four best entry points in market history.

Market Mechanics

Dow Theory: Framework originated by Charles H. Dow requiring both industrial and railroad averages to confirm any primary market movement. Successfully called all four bear bottoms.

Real bills doctrine: The principle that money creation through rediscounting short-term commercial paper is inherently self-limiting and non-inflationary. Guided early Federal Reserve policy.

Reserve ratio: The proportion of gold and legal tender held by the Fed against its notes and deposits. When this ratio approached the statutory minimum (40% against notes, 35% against deposits), the Fed was forced to tighten regardless of economic conditions.

Deposit-currency ratio: The ratio of bank deposits to currency in circulation. Its collapse from 11.5 (October 1930) to 4.4 (March 1933) reflected the catastrophic loss of public confidence in the banking system.

Call money rate: The interest rate charged on demand loans to brokers collateralized by securities. Call money at 30% in November 1919 and 20% briefly in March 1929 signaled peak speculation.

Institutions & Instruments

Reconstruction Finance Corporation (RFC): Created January 1932 with $1.5 billion in borrowing power (expanded to $3.3 billion by July). Lent to distressed banks and railroads, stabilizing the government bond market and reducing forced liquidation of corporate bonds.

Liberty Bonds: US government war bonds issued 1917-18, subscribed by 18 million Americans. Approximately 80% of original holders sold out during the 1920-21 bond bear market.

Nifty Fifty: Roughly fifty large-cap growth stocks that institutional investors concentrated holdings in during 1971-72, paying an average PE of 42x. Suffered an average 62% decline in 1973-74.

Economic Phenomena

Price disturbance: A material shock to the general price level -- either deflation following inflation (1921, 1949, 1982) or severe standalone deflation (1932). Required to push equities to extreme undervaluation in every historical case.

Crowding out: When government borrowing absorbs savings that would otherwise flow into private investment, pushing up the cost of capital and depressing equity and bond prices.

Sterilization: Central bank actions to neutralize the monetary effects of gold inflows, preventing them from expanding the domestic money supply. The Fed sterilized gold inflows throughout the 1920s, restraining the natural easing mechanism.

Introduction: The Financial Bear

In the twentieth century, real annual returns on US equities were negative in 35 of 100 years. In eight of those years, the negative return exceeded 20%. Nine calendar years produced negative ten-year total real returns. These are not tail risks or black swans -- they are the recurring rhythm of equity markets. Jeremy Siegel showed that a 17-year holding period has never produced a negative real return since 1802, but the average NYSE holding period in the first half of 2005 was just twelve months, and the 1900-2002 average was eighteen months. The gap between the theory of patient capital and the practice of actual investors is enormous, and it is in that gap that bear markets do their damage.

Napier set out to understand what the bottom of a bear market actually looks like -- not in retrospect, but in real time, through the eyes of investors reading the Wall Street Journal every morning. He analyzed approximately 70,000 WSJ articles written in the two months either side of four great bear market bottoms, drawn from the ProQuest archive of every Journal issue since 1889. The result of sixteen months of archival research was a remarkable discovery: the four bottoms share a consistent set of features despite occurring under radically different monetary regimes, political systems, and economic conditions.

"Shoot the bastard." -- David Hempleman-Adams, polar explorer, on what to do when confronted by a bear. Guns, unfortunately, offer no protection from the financial variety.

The four bottoms were selected using the Hindsight Value Method developed by Andrew Smithers and Stephen Wright in Valuing Wall Street (2000), which averages returns across 40 discrete holding periods. The three best year-end entry points were 1920, 1932, and 1948, corresponding to intra-year bottoms in August 1921, July 1932, and June 1949. Napier added 1982 as the fourth -- a choice confirmed by subsequent research. These are the only four occasions when equities traded at more than a 70% discount to replacement value of corporate assets, as measured by the q ratio.

Each bottom coincided with a broader societal transformation. 1921 marked the birth of consumer society. 1932 marked the birth of big government. 1949 marked the birth of the military-industrial complex. 1982 marked the rebirth of free markets. Napier illustrates each era through literary proxies: Nick Carraway quit Wall Street in 1922, heading west just as the greatest bull market began. Studs Lonigan invested his nest egg in 1931 at the worst possible moment and died before the July 1932 bottom. Robert Holton stayed on Wall Street in the late 1940s and rode one of the longest bulls in history. And Harry "Rabbit" Angstrom bought gold krugerrands near their all-time high, the embodiment of inflationary late-cycle thinking.

The academic case against financial history gained force after Henry Markowitz published his Portfolio Selection paper in 1952, launching the efficient market hypothesis and the mathematization of investing. But Wall Street's subsequent track record -- 1987 portfolio insurance, Orange County derivatives, LTCM in 1998, the dot-com bust -- provided a steady supply of counterevidence. When Daniel Kahneman and Vernon Smith won the 2002 Nobel Prize for integrating psychology into economic decision-making, the rehabilitation of historical and behavioral approaches was underway. Napier's book is a contribution to that project: financial history as the empirical study of how humans actually make decisions under uncertainty.

Part I: August 1921

By August 1921, the Dow Jones Industrial Average stood at 63.9 -- a level it had first reached on January 27, 1899. Twenty-two years of zero price progress despite an economy that had grown nominal GDP by 383%, real GDP by 88%, steel production by 320%, and telephones from one million to 13.3 million. Alfred Cowles confirmed in 1938 that his broader index told the same story: the August 1921 market sat at the same level as June 1881. Forty years of zero capital gains. The q ratio had fallen to approximately 0.28x -- equities at more than a 70% discount to the replacement value of the assets behind them. By September 1929, just eight years later, the Dow would stand at 381, nearly a 100% premium to replacement value. The great bull market of the 1920s was born from this extreme cheapness.

The road to 1921 ran through the Federal Reserve's first major policy cycle. Created in 1914 after eighty years without a central bank -- the gap stretching back to Andrew Jackson's veto of the Second Bank in 1832 -- the Fed's original mandate was modest: an elastic currency, a means of rediscounting commercial paper, and more effective banking supervision. The system comprised a Federal Reserve Board and twelve Reserve Banks, each capable of setting its own discount rate, with the New York Fed emerging as the de facto policy driver. From the Fed's creation to June 1920, the money stock more than doubled. During wartime, Fed credit rose from zero to approximately $3 billion by end-1919, some 4% of GDP. The mechanism was straightforward: banks rediscounted loans they had made to citizens buying Liberty Bonds, and the Fed accommodated these rediscounts to keep the government's borrowing costs manageable.

"The Federal Reserve System is the antidote for money contraction and credit shortage." -- Andrew Mellon, Secretary of the Treasury, Saturday Evening Post, 1928

The postwar speculative boom ran through 1919, fueled by the Fed's reluctance to raise rates while government floating debt remained to be funded. Then the statutory limit intervened. The Fed was required to hold 40% gold reserves against notes and 35% legal tender against net deposits. As the reserve ratio fell from 48.1% in December 1918 to 42.7% by January 1920, approaching the statutory floor, rate increases became mechanical rather than discretionary. The discount rate rose to 6.0% by February 1920 -- and wholesale prices, which had surged 147% from June 1914 to May 1920, began their descent. Nine major economies suffered their worst annual deflation of the twentieth century simultaneously in 1921. Unskilled labor wages fell from 60 cents to 25 cents per hour. Farm products dropped 87.7% from their peak. The contraction was savage but structurally necessary: under the gold standard, deflation restored competitiveness, improved external accounts, attracted gold inflows, and eventually eased liquidity. Price stabilization was the practical signal that this painful adjustment was complete.

The corporate earnings picture was deeply misleading. The 1916 wartime profit boom had been so exceptional that nominal S&P profits did not exceed the 1916 level until 1949. In real terms, 1916 earnings were not surpassed until December 1955. By January 1992, real earnings were barely 4.7% above the 1916 level. An investor using depressed 1921 earnings would have seen a PE of 24.5x and concluded the market was expensive. Using peak 1916 earnings yielded 4.6x. The truth, visible only in retrospect through normalized 1922-26 earnings, was a PE of 7x. The cyclically adjusted PE had declined from 25x in 1901 to roughly 5x. Many companies traded below working capital. Copper mines sold for less than the cost of equipping them. The bulls who bought at the 1921 bottom focused on value and technical factors -- not earnings, which had a further 37% to fall before bottoming in December.

"The capitalisation of scores of concerns today is selling below working capital. In the case of many companies the shares are selling for less than the value of the plants." -- Anonymous banker, Wall Street Journal, September 19, 1921

What actually happened at the bottom defied several popular assumptions. The Wall Street Journal in the summer of 1921 was not saturated with doom. It was full of bullish forecasts from businessmen, Fed officials, and politicians -- all accurately predicting recovery. Harry F. Sinclair declared "we have hit the bottom" on June 27. Secretary Hoover said business had "turned the corner" on July 14. Benjamin Strong, Governor of the New York Fed, announced on August 9 that "we have entered the cycles of recovery." The DJIA bottomed on August 24. An investor waiting for the absence of good news before buying would have missed the entire Roaring Twenties. The key insight was not what the news said but how the market responded: prices refused to fall further despite every reason to continue declining.

The sentiment chronicles from the WSJ reveal a textbook pattern of exhaustion. By July 18, it was the "dullest market in three years," with "absolutely no public participation." Broker loans had contracted from $1.25 billion to $500 million. Only 900,000 shares of US Steel were held by Wall Street versus 2.5 million a few years earlier -- massive distribution from weak hands to strong was complete. On August 10, one broker reported: "I have clients who are straining at the leash to buy stocks, and I am holding them by main force." By August 20, the WSJ noted this was "a period of investment accumulation," with buyers "picking up stocks for a pull of a year or two realizing they are cheap." The bottom came four days later -- on low volume, not the high-volume capitulation that modern investors expect.

Jesse Livermore, who had made more than $3 million in a single day shorting the 1907 crash, switched from bear to bull almost exactly at the 1921 bottom. His thesis was elemental: depleted inventories would halt price declines and spark buying. William Peter Hamilton, using Dow Theory, accurately called the turn. Robert Rhea would later codify the approach that Hamilton practiced intuitively: both the industrial and railroad averages must confirm any primary movement. The short interest peaked just as the DJIA bottomed -- stocks lent at a premium reached their maximum, signaling that bearish ammunition was exhausted. And the volume pattern reversed: rising volume accompanied advances, while declines occurred on shrinking turnover. These signals -- value, price stability, volume patterns, short interest, and Dow Theory confirmation -- would repeat at every subsequent bottom.

The bond market told its own story, and it told it first. Liberty Bond prices bottomed decisively in May 1920, rallied for thirteen months, and never retested their lows. Corporate bonds bottomed in the final week of June 1921. The DJIA did not bottom until August 24. The sequencing -- government bonds first, then corporate bonds, then equities -- established a pattern that would hold across all four episodes. Within the corporate sector, the recovery was led by high-yielding, second-grade railroad paper, not by the safest credits. The largest gains accrued to those who took the most risk at the moment of maximum fear.

Part II: July 1932

From 63.9 in August 1921, the DJIA rose to 381.2 on September 3, 1929 -- a 500% gain -- then plunged 89% to 41.22 on July 8, 1932. Napier opens Part II with a contrarian warning: the 1929-32 crash may be more the exception than the rule among bear markets. Over-studying it has misled generations of investors into expecting every downturn to resemble the Great Depression. The other three great bottoms were slow grinds over 13 to 16 years, not sudden collapses. The 1932 bottom, while sharing the same valuation and signal characteristics as 1921, 1949, and 1982, arrived through an entirely different mechanism -- a catastrophic, cascading financial crisis rather than a gradual repricing.

The 1920s bull market started slower than most people remember. The DJIA was still below 100 as late as July 1924 and below its November 1919 high as late as April 1925. The first phase, from 1921 to 1924, was simply a return to pre-recession normalcy -- an 87% gain with an average dividend yield of 6.4%. The speculative explosion began in 1925, catalyzed by Calvin Coolidge's election and Andrew Mellon's tax cuts. Two structural forces drove the genuine bull beneath the speculation. First, electrification: only 8% of US dwellings were wired by 1907, rising to 37.8% by 1921 and 67.9% by 1929. The US produced more electric power in 1929 than the rest of the world combined. Real GDP per man-hour rose 19% from 1921 to 1929. Second, consumer credit: still socially stigmatized before WWI, installment buying became respectable after General Motors Acceptance Corporation opened in 1919. By 1927, 85% of furniture, 80% of phonographs, and 75% of washing machines were sold on installment plans.

The Fed's dilemma was structural. Holding 43.2% of the world's gold reserves by 1925, the US naturally dictated easy policy. But the Fed believed it needed to curb speculation, creating a dual objective that would prove fatal. Rate hikes from 3.5% in February 1928 to 6.0% by August 1929 were too small to stop speculation but large enough to slow the economy. Critically, the source of speculative lending was not the banking system but non-bank corporations and foreign capital flowing into the call money market. By October 1929, non-bank broker loans stood at $6.64 billion versus New York banks' $1.1 billion. The Fed was squeezing the wrong end of the pipe.

"Equities are not assets but the 'small sliver of hope between assets and liabilities' -- wiped out by deflation." -- Merryn Somerset Webb, Foreword

The crash itself -- a 48% decline from September to November 1929 -- was not historically exceptional. The 1907 bear fell 44%, the 1919-21 bear fell 47%. By April 1930, the market had recovered 52% of its decline and sat just 23% below its peak. What made 1929-32 unique was the cascade that followed: three banking crises, each triggering the next. The first, in November-December 1930, saw $550 million in deposits tied up in 608 bank suspensions, including the Bank of the United States -- the largest failure to that date, with 400,000 depositors. The deposit-currency ratio, which had been at an all-time high in October 1930, began a collapse from 11.5 to 4.4 by March 1933. Under fractional reserve banking, every dollar withdrawn as cash forced banks to reduce deposits by fourteen dollars.

The second crisis arrived from Europe. Creditanstalt, Austria's largest bank, failed in May 1931, triggering contagion to Hungary and then Germany, where more than half of bank deposits were owned by foreigners. When Britain devalued sterling in September 1931, eight countries immediately followed. US gold stocks, which had risen 15% from September 1929 to August 1931, then declined 15% in just two months, erasing all gains. The Fed responded by raising the discount rate from 1.5% to 3.5% in October 1931 to defend the gold standard -- forcing banks to dump even government bonds and producing the paradox of government bond prices falling during deflation. The Reconstruction Finance Corporation was created in January 1932 and the Glass-Steagall Act was passed, but the Fed's belated reflationary pivot in April 1932 convinced foreign investors the US might abandon gold, accelerating capital flight. The DJIA ultimately bottomed at 41.22 on July 8, 1932 -- 89% below its peak.

The structure of the market had shifted dramatically. The real 1920s bull market leaders were chemicals (+369%) and electrical equipment (+349%), not the popularly remembered autos and radios. Tobacco was the only true safe haven in 1929-32, declining just 38% while the DJIA fell 89%. Even seemingly defensive sectors failed: utilities fell 82%, food fell 72%. The Cowles Foundation found that dividends fell 48% from 1929 to 1932. By 1932, 85% of bread consumed in America was baked at home.

At the July 1932 bottom, the cyclically adjusted PE stood roughly 70% below its 1881-1932 average. The q ratio had probably fallen below 0.3x -- the same extreme cheapness as August 1921. Real S&P earnings were below those reported in October 1873. Among 21 DJIA stocks, Bethlehem Steel traded at 9% of book value, US Steel at 13%, and American Smelting at 15%. The WSJ called the bottom-day by coincidence: on July 8, 1932, it quoted the ninety-three-year-old John D. Rockefeller.

"In the 93 years of my life, depressions have come and gone. Prosperity has always returned, and will again." -- John D. Rockefeller, July 8, 1932 -- the day the DJIA bottomed

The behavioral signals at the 1932 bottom mirrored 1921 with uncanny precision. The market stopped responding negatively to bad news. A selective advance preceded the average's bottom: 67 stocks were already trading 50% or more above their 1932 lows on the day the DJIA bottomed. AT&T weakness signaled small investor capitulation -- the most widely held stock finally being thrown overboard. The volume pattern reversed: from March to June, volume rose on declines and dried up on recoveries; from June onward, volume surged on advances and shrank on dips. And the final decline came on low volume, not the dramatic capitulation selling that conventional wisdom demands.

The bond sequencing held perfectly. Government bonds bottomed in January 1932. Corporate bonds bottomed in early June 1932, with Baa yields peaking at 11.52% -- one-third higher than any previously recorded yield. Equities bottomed on July 8. The RFC's provision of credit to distressed institutions, the formation of the American Securities Investment Corporation by major banks, and the Lausanne Agreement abolishing German reparations all contributed to breaking the international financial logjam. Commodity price stabilization led corporate bonds by roughly two weeks and equities by six weeks. Robert Rhea, using Dow Theory, called the bottom on July 21, 1932 -- two for two for the theory.

The fiscal obsession of 1932 proved to be the bear market's great red herring. The WSJ's insistence that a balanced budget was a precondition for equity recovery was the most erroneous investment judgment of the year. The budget deficit was only 0.60% of GDP in 1931, then ballooned to 4.6% in 1932, 4.61% in 1933, and 5.50% in 1934 -- while one of history's greatest bull markets raged. The real culprit was the gold drain: US monetary gold stock contracted 12% from January to July 1932, driven by trade deterioration and capital flight prompted by the Fed's own reflationary signals. The outflow stopped precisely when the Fed ended its purchase program and Congress adjourned -- both coinciding with the market bottom.

Part III: June 1949

June 1949 ranks second on the all-time list for long-term equity returns, behind only July 1932. Yet the world that produced this extraordinary buying opportunity was almost unrecognizable from the depths of 1932. The economy had been transformed by war, regulation, and the permanent expansion of government. On June 13, 1949 -- the day the DJIA hit its postwar low of 161.6 -- the New York Times front page was about a heat wave: "1,500,000 Cool off at City's Beaches." The equity market had become such a backwater that its bottom did not merit a headline.

The journey from 1932 to 1949 encompassed one of the most extraordinary bull markets and one of the most dispiriting bears in history. From July 1932 to March 1937, the DJIA rose 370%, powered by real national product growing at 12% per annum -- rates never recorded before or since. S&P earnings rose 176%. But even at the 1937 peak, earnings remained below 1929, 1928, 1926, 1925, 1917, and 1916 levels. Then the Fed raised reserve requirements in August 1936 and margins from 25% to 50%, while the federal deficit shrank. Industrial production fell by a third, earnings declined nearly 50%, and the DJIA dropped 49% in twelve months, back to mid-1933 levels. From the 1938 low, S&P earnings doubled by December 1941 -- but the DJIA went nowhere. Investors had lost faith in any return to pre-1929 normalcy.

The war years saw the Fed effectively cease to exist as an independent institution. Treasury bill rates were fixed at 0.375%, and the entire federal yield curve was pegged, with maximum long-term government bond yields capped at 2.5%. As Chairman Marriner Eccles put it, the Fed "merely executed Treasury decisions." With bonds yielding 2.5% and equities yielding 11% or more in 1942, the relative case for stocks was compelling -- but the excess profits tax reached a flat rate of 95% by 1943, ensuring that neither S&P earnings nor dividends grew meaningfully from 1942 to 1946. The entire 1942-46 bull market was driven by valuation expansion, not earnings growth. And it peaked with the DJIA still 44% below its September 1929 all-time high.

"Prices rose and fell recurrently... The pattern had repeated itself as recently as 1919-1920. To Avery, a postwar depression was therefore a foregone conclusion. 'Who am I to argue with history?' he asked." -- James Grant, Money of the Mind, on Sewell Avery of Montgomery Ward

The 1946-49 bear was unlike anything before it. S&P earnings increased nearly 200% during the decline -- yet equities fell. The trauma of post-WWI deflation dominated investor thinking. Sewell Avery of Montgomery Ward studied commodity price charts stretching back to the nineteenth century and concluded that postwar depression was inevitable. He loaded up on government bonds instead of investing in his business. But this time was structurally different: the Employment Act of 1946 enshrined maximum employment as a government mandate. Bretton Woods was designed to make deflation less likely. Government spending was now 15% of GDP versus 3% in the 1920s. Non-agricultural union membership had reached 33% of the workforce. Post-WWII deflation measured only 7.9% versus 45% after WWI. The deflationary gale that Avery expected came as a breeze.

The Cold War provided a background of geopolitical tension that simultaneously depressed and supported equity prices. Churchill's Iron Curtain speech in March 1946, the Truman Doctrine, the Marshall Plan, the Berlin Blockade, the formation of NATO in April 1949, and the Soviet atomic bomb test in August 1949 all contributed to an atmosphere of existential anxiety. But the Soviet failure to act militarily over NATO's creation eased fears. Broker Sam Smith of Bache & Co. captured the shift on July 12, 1949: "There should be room for plenty of cheer in the realisation that: There isn't going to be a war." Cold War arms spending, it turned out, was better for equities than the hot war that investors feared -- echoing the US rearmament boom of 1915-16.

The investment management industry had been devastated. Only 150 investment management companies were registered with the SEC, managing a total of $2.7 billion -- less than 5% of the US equity market. NYSE turnover had collapsed: the ratio of trading volume to market capitalization fell from 119% in 1929 to 32% in 1932 to 9% in 1942, and recovered only to 13% by 1949. NYSE seats, which had sold for $495,000 in 1929, hit $17,000 in 1942 -- a 75% decline from the 1932 low even as the DJIA doubled. The market traded half-days on Saturdays. Beer stocks had returned 3,993% since the 1932 low on the back of Prohibition's repeal; the broad market had barely moved.

Valuations at the June 1949 bottom were in some respects even more extreme than 1932. The cyclically adjusted PE fell to 5.8x -- lower than 1932's 9.4x. The q ratio stood at 0.36x. The DJIA had returned to its February 1926 level, 57% below the 1929 high, representing nearly twenty years of zero capital progress while real GDP per capita grew 62%. L.O. Hooper compiled a list of 225 stocks selling below working capital. Keystone Company of Boston noted that the DJIA historically traded at 15x earnings, with bear troughs around 10x; the current reading was 6-7x. Equities yielded 4% -- more than bonds -- the widest spread in twenty years.

"Traders should not overlook that the market is resting on a potential keg of dynamite... the latent power of the extremely large short interest is tremendous. Very few of the amateur shorts will succeed." -- Herbert G. King, July 25, 1949

The signal pattern held. Good news was abundant and ignored: Q1 1949 earnings came in better than expected, International Harvester farm equipment sales were up 30% year-over-year, and Treasury Secretary Snyder declared postwar adjustments "practically completed." The auto industry was booming -- US auto sales reached 5.1 million in 1949 versus 3.8 million pre-war, with the average car age at 8.73 years and 13 million cars over ten years old. Short interest reached its highest level in sixteen years. The final decline came on low volume. And Dow Theory called the bottom correctly -- three for three.

The bond sequencing was distorted by the Fed's yield curve peg but still pointed in the right direction. Corporate bonds bottomed in January 1948 -- sixteen months before equities -- as investors shifted from pegged government rates to higher-yielding corporate paper. Government bonds, artificially supported by the Fed, rallied later. The normal sequence (government bonds first, then corporates, then equities) was disrupted by policy, but the underlying principle -- fixed income leads equity -- remained intact.

Part IV: August 1982

By August 1982, the S&P Composite in real terms had returned to a level first reached in August 1906 -- a seventy-six-year round trip. The question that made 1982 different from the prior three bottoms was whether systemic inflation, as opposed to war-driven or cyclical inflation, would alter the nature of bear markets. The 1968-82 bear had unfolded under an entirely novel monetary regime: free-floating exchange rates, fiat currency, and persistent inflation averaging 7.1% annually. Napier's central finding is that it did not. The same signals that worked in 1921, 1932, and 1949 worked again in 1982.

The bull market from June 1949 to December 1968 had delivered a nominal return of 662% on the S&P Composite (413% real), punctuated by eight corrections exceeding 10%. The most dramatic, the Kennedy break of 1962, saw a 27% decline after JFK blocked steel price hikes. The structural story was institutional: pension fund assets grew 11-fold from 1952 to 1968, mutual fund assets 13-fold. Institutional equity holdings rose from 9.5% to 21.3% of NYSE market capitalization. This institutional shift produced one of the most important structural changes in market history: the yield gap reversal of July 1957, when the S&P dividend yield fell permanently below the long-term government bond yield for the first time since 1792. The underlying logic was that postwar institutions had made inflation structurally permanent, eliminating the advantage of bonds' fixed payments.

The 1968-82 bear was slow and grinding. The S&P declined 63% in real terms. Nominal earnings per share grew 143%, but nominal GDP grew 246% -- a 12% real decline in corporate earnings. Both poor earnings growth and valuation compression (q ratio from 1.06x to 0.38x) contributed. The Nifty Fifty disaster of 1973-74 epitomized the era: investors who had paid an average PE of 42x in 1972 suffered average declines of 62%. The OPEC oil shock of October 1973 drove the crude price from $3.12 to $11.63 per barrel in three months. Annual inflation surged from 2.9% in August 1972 to 12.5% by December 1974. The word "stagflation" appeared in the WSJ for the first time in April 1973, though it had been coined by Conservative MP Ian Macleod in 1965.

"In the decade ending in 1965, manufacturing companies had an average annual pre-tax margin on sales of 8.6%. In the decade ending in 1975, the average margin was 8%. Margins were down despite a very considerable increase in the inflation rate." -- Warren Buffett, Fortune, May 1977

Paul Volcker's appointment as Fed chairman on August 6, 1979 changed everything -- though not immediately. He convened a secret FOMC meeting on October 6 and announced a new policy: targeting M-1 aggregate growth rather than interest rates, with a Fed funds rate range of 11.5% to 15.5%. President Carter imposed credit controls in March 1980, producing the fastest quarterly GDP decline ever recorded. The Fed funds rate swung from 20% to 8% and back above 20% by mid-1981. The bond market did not believe inflation was truly beaten until September 1981, which marked the beginning of a twenty-year-plus bond rally.

The actual catalyst for the 1982 equity bottom was a sovereign debt crisis. Drysdale Government Securities went bust in May 1982. Penn Square Bank failed in early July, having originated more than $1 billion in loans sold to Continental Illinois. Mexico was publicly bankrupt by August. The FOMC, aware that Mexico would default, had already changed direction on June 30 despite M-1 growth running above target. The Fed funds rate fell from above 14% in early July to below 9% by early September -- a de facto abandonment of money-supply targeting. Developing country debt had soared from $100 billion in 1973 to an expected $640 billion, with $327 billion owed to Western commercial banks. Countries in trouble stretched from Poland to Mexico to Argentina to Brazil to Zaire. Walter Wriston of Citibank captured the atmosphere at the IMF annual meeting in Toronto: "We had 150-odd finance ministers, 50-odd central bankers, 1,000 journalists... 'the end of the world is coming.' It was the Titanic."

The signal checklist confirmed again. Valuations were extreme: the q ratio had probably fallen to 0.27x, the lowest of all four bottoms. The cyclically adjusted PE stood at 9.9x. The DJIA was back to its April 1964 level -- in real terms, back to April 1928. Copper bottomed two months before equities. The CRB Spot Metals index bottomed in June 1982. New car registrations hit their monthly low in January 1982 and were recovering by June. New England recovery preceded the equity bottom by weeks, as it had at all four bottoms. Good news was ignored: auto sales improving, housing starts up 22%, Volcker himself saying consumer spending was "levelling out" -- all dismissed by a market that had forgotten how to be bullish.

On August 12, the DJIA bottomed. On August 18, Henry Kaufman of Salomon Brothers reversed his long-standing pessimism, predicting "substantial drops in interest rates." The DJIA staged a record one-day rally. Goldman Sachs told clients to raise equity allocation from 35% to 55%. A volume record was set on August 19. But the 1982 bottom was unique in one respect: the initial rebound came on very high volume, the opposite of the low-volume rebounds in 1921, 1932, and 1949. Short positions did not capitulate. In fact, they spiked to a new record of 120.5 million shares on September 15, confirming that the move was a permanent reversal rather than a bear-market rally. The bond bear market -- thirty-five years old, having taken long-term Treasury yields from 2.03% in April 1946 to 15.1% in October 1981 -- had ended the previous autumn. Government bonds led, then corporate bonds (Baa yields peaking at 17.3% in February 1982), then equities. The sequence held for the fourth time.

Conclusions: Einstein's Questions

Napier calls his framework "Einstein's questions" -- the right questions to ask, requiring better-than-average answers to profit. The q ratio fell below 0.3x at all four bear market bottoms and proved the single most reliable valuation indicator. The cyclically adjusted PE ranged more widely (4.7x to 11.7x), making it less useful as a precise trigger. Equities become cheap slowly: the average journey from peak q to trough took nine years, or fourteen excluding the 1929-32 outlier. Three of the four bear markets occurred during periods of economic expansion, not depression. Real GDP grew roughly 52% on average across the three long bears. Inflation-adjusted earnings growth ranged from -67% to +28% -- the variance is enormous, and waiting for earnings clarity means missing the bottom by months.

A material disturbance to the general price level is required to push equities to extreme cheapness. In 1921, 1949, and 1982, high inflation was followed by deflation (commodity-only in 1982). In 1932, severe standalone deflation did the work. Without such a price disturbance, a bear market may not produce "buy of a generation" valuations. When the disturbance arrives, commodity price stabilization -- especially in copper -- signals the turn. In all four cases, the end of the decline in commodity prices marked the end of the great bear market in equities. Rising demand at lower prices, low inventory positions, and producers selling below cost are the confirming signals.

The tactical rules are remarkably consistent. Government bond recoveries precede equity recoveries by 7 to 14 months, with the DJIA declining a further 6% to 46% during the lag. Corporate bond recoveries lead equities by 1 to 5 months. Fed rate cuts in Fed-induced recessions (1921, 1949, 1982) preceded equity bottoms by roughly 3 to 11 months, with less than 20% further downside. The exception was 1929, which was not a Fed-induced recession but the consequence of reparations, Allied debts, and botched gold-standard reconstruction. The practical rule: wait for a further 10% market decline after the first discount rate cut before buying -- history shows minimal further downside and very significant upside thereafter.

"Just as the possession of fur does not, of itself, permit the identification of an animal as a bear, the possession of any one of the features above should not be considered as constituting positive identification of its financial equivalent." -- Russell Napier

Additional tactical signals include: auto sector recovery preceding equity recovery; bear-market bottoms characterized by good economic news being ignored rather than by bad news dominating; worsening fiscal positions being blamed for preventing recovery (these warnings were wrong every time); corporate earnings continuing to decline well past the equity bottom (by 4 to 7 months on average); the market declining on high volume and rising on low volume before the final low, followed by a reversal after the first rebound; short interest peaking at or near the bottom and rising further in the first weeks of the new bull market; and Dow Theory correctly signaling a buy at all four bottoms.

Applying his framework in 2005, Napier noted that the q ratio at end-1999 stood at an all-time high of 2.9x, with the cyclically adjusted PE 170% above its 1881-2005 average. From June 2005 levels, equities would need to decline 60-84% to reach prior bear-bottom valuations. No general price disturbance had yet occurred. No meaningful bond sell-off, no recession, and no Fed rate cuts had materialized. His prediction: the DJIA would likely decline at least 60%, possibly 80% or more, with the bear ending "sometime after 2009, though probably nearer to 2014." The Global Financial Crisis would arrive three years later.

Trading & Investing Lessons

The Bond-Equity Sequencing Trade

At all four bottoms, government bonds bottomed first, corporate bonds followed, and equities came last. The lead time ranged from 7 to 14 months for government bonds and 1 to 5 months for corporates. An investor monitoring corporate bond yields for a peak and reversal had a reliable 1-5 month head start on the equity bottom. In practical terms: when Baa yields stop rising and begin to compress, start building equity positions.

Copper as a Leading Indicator

Copper price stabilization preceded or coincided with every equity bottom. This is the simplest cross-asset signal in the book: when copper stops falling and begins to recover, the commodity deflation that has been crushing equity valuations is ending. Watch copper alongside auto sales and inventory data for confirmation.

The Fed Rate Cut Rule

In three of four bear markets (excluding the 1929-32 anomaly), the first Fed rate cut preceded the equity bottom by 3 to 11 months, with less than 20% further downside. The practical application: after the first rate cut in a Fed-induced recession, expect a further 10% decline as the maximum drawdown before the bottom. This rule specifically does not apply to crises caused by structural breakdowns rather than deliberate tightening.

Volume Pattern as Confirmation

During the final phase of every bear market, volume rose on declines and dried up on recoveries. The reversal of this pattern -- rising volume on advances, shrinking volume on dips -- was the most reliable real-time confirmation that the bottom had been reached. Do not look for high-volume capitulation selling; the actual bottom comes on low volume, in exhaustion rather than panic.

Earnings Are a Lagging Indicator

Corporate earnings bottomed 4-7 months after equities at every great bottom. Investors waiting for earnings improvement before buying were typically paying double the bottom price. In 1921, the DJIA bottomed in August while earnings had a further 37% to fall. In 1932, earnings did not trough until December -- five months after the equity bottom. Value and technical signals must be trusted over earnings data.

Fiscal Deficit Warnings Are Noise

At every bottom, commentators warned that fiscal deterioration would prevent recovery. Deficits expanded substantially during each recovery without preventing massive bull markets. In 1932, the deficit went from 0.6% to 5.5% of GDP while equities soared. In 1982, the deficit reached 5.9% -- surpassing FDR's peacetime peaks -- with no negative impact. Fiscal austerity arguments at bear bottoms have a 100% failure rate.

Key Takeaways

The q Ratio Below 0.3x Is the Signal

The replacement value of corporate assets provides a more reliable anchor for equity valuation than earnings, which are cyclical and manipulable. When the q ratio falls below 0.3x -- equities at a 70%+ discount to replacement value -- you are in the presence of a generational buying opportunity. This threshold was breached at every great bottom and no other time.

Bear Markets Are Slow, Not Fast

Excluding the 1929-32 outlier, equities took approximately 14 years to travel from peak to trough valuation. The 1921 bottom came 16 years after the q ratio peaked. The 1949 bottom came 13 years after the prior peak. The 1982 bottom came 14 years after 1968. Investors who expect bear markets to look like 1929-32 will miss the slow grinding reality of the typical case.

A Price Shock Is Required

No great bear bottom occurred without a material disturbance to the general price level. Either inflation followed by deflation (1921, 1949, 1982) or severe standalone deflation (1932) was needed to create the uncertainty about future earnings and bond yields that compresses equity valuations to extreme levels.

Good News at the Bottom Is Normal

Every great bottom was accompanied by abundant good economic news that the market ignored. The popular belief that bear markets end amid universal pessimism and terrible headlines is contradicted by all four cases. The signal is not the character of the news but the market's inability to respond to it -- either positively or negatively.

Bonds Lead Equities -- Always

Government bonds bottomed 7-14 months before equities. Corporate bonds bottomed 1-5 months before equities. This sequence held under the gold standard, Bretton Woods, and free-floating exchange rates. The only distortion occurred when the Fed was pegging the yield curve (1949), and even then corporate bonds led equities by over a year.

The Bottom Comes on Low Volume

High-volume capitulation selling is a feature of corrections within bull markets, not of true secular bear-market bottoms. All four great bottoms saw final declines on low volume. The rebound confirmation came when volume rose at higher prices -- not at the bottom itself.

Dow Theory Works

The requirement that both industrial and transportation averages confirm any primary movement correctly identified the bottom at all four great bear markets. A simple framework from 1899 outperformed every quantitative model and macroeconomic forecast across four radically different environments.

Contrarian Ideas

1929-32 Is the Exception, Not the Rule

Conventional wisdom treats the 1929 crash as the template for bear markets. Napier argues the opposite: it is the only one of four bottoms reached through a sudden catastrophic decline rather than a slow multi-year grind. The other three took 13-16 years. Investors who prepare for 1929 may miss the 1921, 1949, or 1982 pattern unfolding beneath their feet.

Fed Easing Is Not a Buy Signal

Conventional wisdom says "don't fight the Fed" and treats rate cuts as bullish. Napier shows that in 1921, buying on the first rate cut (May) meant enduring a further 20% DJIA decline before August bottom. In 1932, buying on the open-market program (April) meant losing a third of your capital by July. In 1982, the first discount rate cut (October 1981) preceded the bottom by eleven months. Fed easing is a necessary but far from sufficient condition.

Capitulation Selling Is a Myth

The widely held belief that bear markets end with a dramatic, high-volume wash-out is directly contradicted by all four great bottoms. The final decline was characterized by exhaustion, apathy, and dwindling volume -- the absence of sellers rather than their climactic surrender.

Fiscal Deficits Don't Prevent Recovery

At every bottom, fiscal hawks insisted that growing deficits would crowd out private capital and prevent recovery. The prediction failed every time. The 1930s saw deficits explode alongside history's greatest bull market. The early 1980s saw the largest peacetime deficits ever recorded alongside a massive equity rally. Deficit concerns at bear bottoms are a reliable contrary indicator.

Short Sellers Don't Capitulate at the Bottom

In 1932, short positions increased from the July 8 bottom through July 27, even as the DJIA rose 29%. In 1982, shorts hit a new record of 120.5 million shares on September 15 -- more than a month after the August 12 bottom. The bears' refusal to cover quickly was actually a positive signal for the sustainability of the rally.

Frameworks & Proposals

The Bear Bottom Checklist

Napier distills his findings into a composite checklist that appeared at all four bottoms. No single indicator is sufficient -- the full set must be assessed together:

  1. q ratio below 0.3x -- equities at 70%+ discount to replacement value
  2. Commodity price stabilization -- especially copper recovery
  3. Government bond recovery preceding equities by 7-14 months
  4. Corporate bond recovery preceding equities by 1-5 months
  5. Fed rate cuts in Fed-induced recessions (3-11 month lead)
  6. Good news ignored by the market for weeks or months
  7. Final decline on low volume with volume rising on first rebound
  8. Short interest at peak and still rising after the initial rally
  9. Auto sector recovery visible before the broad market turns
  10. Dow Theory confirmation -- both averages agreeing on reversal

The 10% Rule

After the first Fed discount rate cut in a Fed-induced recession, wait for a further 10% decline in the DJIA before initiating equity purchases. Historically, the maximum additional downside from the first rate cut was 20% (1921), and the average was roughly 10-15%. This provides a simple, rules-based entry discipline that avoids both premature buying and paralysis.

The Bond Sequencing Framework

Monitor three fixed-income signals in sequence: (1) government bond yields peak and begin declining; (2) corporate bond spreads (Baa over Treasuries) peak and begin compressing; (3) equity purchases are warranted. In 1921, government bonds led by 14 months. In 1932, by 7 months. In 1982, by 11 months. The corporate-to-equity lag is shorter (1-5 months) but more reliable as a timing signal because it is less subject to policy distortions.

The Price Disturbance Filter

Before assuming a bear market will produce generational cheapness, verify that a material price disturbance has occurred or is occurring. If inflation has not been followed by deflation (or severe standalone deflation has not appeared), the bear market may produce merely fair-value prices rather than extreme undervaluation. The 1974 bottom -- q ratio at 0.35x, not below 0.3x -- illustrates this: a genuine bear bottom but not a great one, with five-year real returns of only +9% versus +84% to +209% from the four great bottoms.

What Didn't Fit

Germany 1921: The Inflationary Alternative

While the US deflated its way to the 1921 bottom, Germany chose the opposite path. Gold-to-note coverage fell from 54% in 1914 to 1.5% by 1921; Austria's fell to 0.01%. The Frankfurter Zeitung stock index rose 40% from March to August 1921, and the Berlin Stock Exchange suspended trading in September 1921 amid the frenzy. What appeared to be positive policy for equities in the short term led to catastrophe: the German Wholesale Price Index went from 152 in 1918 to 1,338 in 1921 to 23,927 in 1922 to 11.634 trillion in 1923. Peak monthly inflation hit 3 million percent. The lesson: the inflationary escape from deflation can produce short-term equity gains but ultimately destroys capital almost completely.

The Sugar-to-Bank Pipeline

Sugar prices crashed from 26 cents per pound in 1920 to 5.5 cents in 1921. National City Bank, having lent heavily to Cuban sugar producers, seized collateral on defaulted loans and inadvertently became one of Cuba's largest sugar producers. The bank was left running plantations it had never intended to own -- an early example of how commodity busts create bizarre credit consequences far from the trading floor.

The Curb Market Goes Indoors

Until June 28, 1921, the New York Curb Exchange operated literally on the sidewalk at Broad Street. Traders stood outdoors in all weather, conducting business in companies not large enough for the NYSE. This was where you could buy British American Tobacco, Gillette, Philip Morris, and RCA. The move indoors in 1921 coincided almost exactly with the bear market bottom -- the formalization of what had been an informal market, paralleling the broader institutionalization of American finance.

Imperial Russian Bonds at 20

Among the foreign sovereign bonds trading on the New York Curb in 1921 were Imperial Russian Government bonds, priced at roughly 20 cents on the dollar. The Bolshevik government had repudiated all Tsarist debt. These bonds would trade on various exchanges for decades as collectibles and speculative instruments, never paying another coupon -- a permanent reminder that sovereign credit risk is not theoretical.

The Nifty Fifty's Surprising Vindication

The Nifty Fifty suffered an average 62% price decline in 1973-74 after investors paid a 42x average PE. But for long-term holders measuring through November 2001, these fifty stocks delivered an annual return of 11.62% versus 12.14% for the S&P -- nearly identical to the market. Overpaying for genuine quality was less destructive than overpaying for speculative junk, though neither was as good as buying anything at the 1982 bottom when the entire market was cheap.

The 1942 Bottom Nobody Noticed

The DJIA bottomed on April 28, 1942 -- five months after Pearl Harbor, in the darkest days of the war. The q ratio was at a 70%+ discount to replacement value, matching 1921 and 1932. NYSE seat prices had fallen to $17,000 -- a 75% decline from the 1932 low despite the DJIA having doubled from that level. Trading volume had collapsed 99% from the 1929 record day. The public had comprehensively abandoned equities. Napier treats 1942 as an important data point but not a primary case study because the yield curve peg and wartime controls make it less generalizable than the other four.

Anatomy of the Bear: Lessons from Wall Street's Four Great Bottoms
Russell Napier · 2005 (4th edition 2016) · 439 pages
A meticulous study of the four occasions when US equities reached extreme undervaluation, built from 70,000 Wall Street Journal articles and identifying a consistent set of signals that appeared at every great bear market bottom across four radically different monetary and economic regimes.

About the Author

Russell Napier has spent more than twenty-five years in financial markets, specializing in global macro strategy and financial history. He holds law degrees from Queen's University Belfast and Magdalene College, Cambridge, and is a Fellow of the CFA Society and Honorary Professor at Heriot-Watt University. He is the author of the Solid Ground investment report and co-founder of the ERIC investment research portal.

Napier founded and directs the Practical History of Financial Markets course at Edinburgh Business School, which draws investment professionals from around the world to study the patterns that repeat across centuries of market history. In 2014, he established the Library of Mistakes, a financial history library in Edinburgh dedicated to the proposition that the best way to avoid investment errors is to study them systematically.

His work combines the rigor of academic research with the practical sensibility of a working investment strategist. The sixteen months of archival research that produced Anatomy of the Bear -- analyzing approximately 70,000 Wall Street Journal articles from the ProQuest database -- reflects his conviction that financial history is not an academic exercise but the empirical foundation of investment decision-making.

Why This Book Matters

Anatomy of the Bear occupies a unique position in investment literature. Most books about bear markets focus on a single episode -- the 1929 crash, the 2008 financial crisis -- and draw lessons specific to that context. Napier's achievement is the systematic comparison of four bottoms across radically different monetary regimes (gold standard, Bretton Woods, free float), political systems (Harding's laissez-faire, Roosevelt's New Deal, Truman's managed economy, Reagan's deregulation), and economic conditions (postwar deflation, depression, Cold War anxiety, stagflation). The discovery that the same signals appeared at every bottom transforms what could be historical anecdote into actionable analytical framework.

The book's methodology -- primary-source analysis of 70,000 Wall Street Journal articles rather than retrospective data fitting -- gives its conclusions a credibility that purely quantitative studies lack. Napier shows what investors were actually reading, thinking, and debating in real time at each bottom. The result is not a model that works in backtests but a pattern recognition framework grounded in the psychology of how markets actually process information at extremes. The Financial Times called it "a cult classic in the investment community," and institutional investors have treated "Einstein's questions" as a practical checklist for two decades.

For anyone who trades, invests, or manages risk in equity markets, the book's most important contribution is negative: it demolishes the myths that mislead investors at precisely the moment when getting it right matters most. Capitulation selling is a myth. Fed easing is not a buy signal. Fiscal deficits don't prevent recovery. Earnings are a lagging indicator. Good news at the bottom is normal. The actual signals -- q ratio, bond sequencing, copper, volume patterns, Dow Theory -- are simple, repeatable, and historically robust. Whether the next great bottom comes in two years or twenty, the investor who has internalized Napier's framework will recognize it when others are still waiting for conditions that never arrive.
