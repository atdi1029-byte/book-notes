## Preface: Track Record & Structural Headwinds

### Previous Forecasts
- **2005 edition:** Predicted DJIA would fall **60% minimum** before bear ended
- **Actual:** DJIA peaked October 2007; fell 54% to March 2009
- March 2009 was only 40% below November 2005 level (not 60%)
- Valuations reached fair value, NOT the deep discounts of historic great bottoms
- **Conclusion:** March 2009 was NOT the bear market bottom for the 2000 crash

### What Triggered 2007–2009
- Chinese banking reforms (2005) shifted growth model → boosted global inflation
- US import prices from China rose **7%** (March 2007–July 2008)
- US inflation reached **5.6% by July 2008**
- 10-year Treasury yield rose 4.5% to 5.3%; Fed Funds rate climbed 4.0% to 5.25%
- First rate cut September 2007; business cycle peaked December 2007
- By September 2008, **deflation** appeared; DJIA collapsed

### Post-2009 Structural Forces

**Demographics:** Baby-boomers (51–69) highly leveraged; peak debt at ages
45–54 (87% in debt), falls to 66% at 65–74. Retirees paying down debt =
consumption falls, inflation struggles.

**China:** Growth model relied on undervalued yuan (policy since 1994) + cheap
labor. Chinese wages surged ~**200% since 2008**. US current account deficit
shrunk from 5.9% GDP (2006) to 2.4%. China faces slower growth or devaluation.

**2015 China diagnosis:** GFC 2008-09 prompted another massive credit expansion
→ still more productive capacity → depressed global prices. China trapped:
currency linked to rising USD, competitiveness eroding, capital flight.

**Devaluation scenario:** Most likely outcome: RMB devaluation to ease policy.
Precedent: China's **1994 devaluation** flooded system with cheap goods.
New devaluation would severely test developed-world CBs' ability to generate
growth vs. massive **deflationary force**.

**Treasury crowding-out:** Foreign CBs' share of US Treasury market rose from
**12% to 38% (peak 1Q 2009)**. When that obligation falls back on private
savers: **crowding out** → lower share/bond prices → higher cost of capital.
Combined effect: lower US consumption + China capital outflow = deflation risk.

**Valuation warning (2015):** Cyclically adjusted PE and q ratio both indicate
poor long-term returns. Even 10-year holder unlikely to exceed **2% real annual
return** vs. historical 5–6%.

**Post-deflation scenario:** After deflation forces equities very cheap, expect
reflationary government response — student debt forgiveness, "QE for the
people," credit controls, exchange controls. Would generate higher nominal GDP
with inflation. If equities are cheap enough when this arrives → new bull market.

> "One of the key lessons of this book is that equities can discount almost
> anything when they are cheap enough."
> — Russell Napier, November 2015

## Introduction: The Financial Bear — A Field Guide

### Scope
- 84 million US equity shareholders; bear markets can "destroy or seriously
  damage" their financial futures
- **Prof. Jeremy Siegel**: hold 17 years, never lose money in real terms since
  1802. But average NYSE holding period (H1 2005) = **12 months**;
  1900–2002 average = 18 months
- 20th century: real annual return on US equities was **negative in 35 of
  100 years**; in 8 of those, negative return exceeded **20%**
- **9 years** of the past century produced negative 10-year total real returns
- The four bottoms studied are the **only occasions** equities traded at more
  than a **70% discount to replacement value** of assets

### Why Financial History
- **Henry Markowitz** (1952): *Portfolio Selection* in *Journal of Finance* →
  academic assault on value of history → **efficient market hypothesis**
- New Wall Street failures: 1987 portfolio insurance, Orange County/Gibson
  derivatives, **LTCM** 1998, dot-com bust
- **Daniel Kahneman** and **Vernon I. Smith** — 2002 Nobel Prize for integrating
  psychology into decision-making under uncertainty
- Financial history = empirical study of human decision-making = guide to future

### Methodology
- Analyzed **~70,000 WSJ articles** written in two months either side of four
  great bear market bottoms; primary research from ProQuest archive (every
  WSJ since 1889); ~16 months of back issues
- Result: remarkable **similarity across all four bottoms** → replicable signals

### Selecting the Four Bottoms

**Hindsight Value Method** (Smithers & Wright, *Valuing Wall Street*, 2000):
average of 40 discrete return periods (1- to 40-year horizons).
- Best three years: **1920, 1932, 1948** (year-end); intra-year: **August 1921,
  July 1932, June 1949**
- Fourth period, **1982**, added by Napier — top four confirmed 23 years later

**q ratio:** stock-market valuation / replacement value of assets.
- "Fair value" = q at its geometric mean
- Four periods studied = only times equities traded at more than **70% discount
  to replacement value**
- Cyclically adjusted PE also useful; Smithers/Wright found **q ratio** more
  accurate for predicting superior future returns

### Literary Proxies
- **Nick Carraway** (Fitzgerald): quit Wall Street 1922, heading west just as
  the greatest US bull market began
- **Studs Lonigan** (Farrell): invested nest egg in 1931 at worst moment; dead
  before the July 1932 bottom
- **Robert Holton** (Vidal): stayed on Wall Street late 1940s — benefited from
  one of the longest bull markets in history
- **Harry 'Rabbit' Angstrom** (Updike): bought gold/krugerrands near gold's
  all-time high

Four bottoms = four societal transitions: 1921 = birth of consumer society;
1932 = birth of big government; 1949 = birth of military-industrial complex;
1982 = rebirth of free markets.

> "Shoot the bastard."
> — David Hempleman-Adams (polar explorer) on what to do when confronted by
> a bear; guns offer no protection from the financial bear.

## PART I: AUGUST 1921

> *"'Can't repeat the past?' he cried incredulously. 'Why of course you can!'"*
> — F. Scott Fitzgerald, *The Great Gatsby*

By August 1921, the DJIA had returned to its level of **22 years prior**; blue
chip railroad shares back to 1881 prices. Equities trading at a **70% discount
to replacement value**. By September 1929, equities at nearly a **100% premium**
to replacement value; DJIA had risen almost fivefold.

---

### The Road to August 1921: Course of the Dow, 1896–1921

**Market Structure:**
- 1921: two indexes tracked — **Dow Jones 20 Industrial** and **20 Railroad**
- 1896: industrials = 48% of NYSE volume; railroads = 52%
- DJIA first published May 1896; originally 12 components; expanded to 20
  in October 1916, to 30 in October 1928

**1896–1914 arc:**
- JP Morgan amalgamated bankrupt railroads after 1893-95 panic; mergers surged
  from 69 (1897) to **1,200+ in 1899**; railroad bull market dominated
- **William McKinley** assassination (1901) → **Theodore Roosevelt** trust-busting
- By 1911, industrials exceeded railroad activity for first time

**WWI transformation:**
- NYSE closed 31 July 1914 (DJIA at 71.42); reopened 12 December 1914;
  industrials had fallen **32%**, railroads rose slightly to 90.21
- Feared gold outflow reversed — funds **poured INTO the US**; by war's end,
  industrials = **80%+ of NYSE volume**; most railroads nationalized
- Railroads nationalized **noon, 28 December 1917** — equity converted to
  de facto bonds (fixed dividend based on pre-nationalization earnings)

**The 1916 Profit Boom — A Stunning Fact:**
- "War brides" — companies with huge European war orders — surged
- 1916 corporate earnings so exceptional: **nominal profits did not exceed
  the 1916 level until 1949**
- In **real terms**, 1916 earnings not exceeded until December 1955
- As late as January 1992, S&P real earnings barely 4.7% above 1916 level
- By the 1919 bull market peak, railroad turnover = only **13.8%** of total
  trading volume

### Living with the Fed — The 1921 Context

**Why the Fed was created:**
- **1895 crisis**: only loan from **JP Morgan** and **Rothschilds** prevented
  exhaustion of US gold reserves
- **1907 panic**: JP Morgan again brokered rescue
- Both triggered by public shift from deposits to cash → liquidity collapses
- **Andrew Jackson** had vetoed the Second Bank of the US in 1832 — 80-year
  gap without central bank

**Structure:**
- **Federal Reserve Board** + 12 **Federal Reserve Banks**; Reserve Banks
  could set own discount rates; NY Fed became de facto policy driver
- Mandate: "elastic currency, means of rediscounting commercial paper, more
  effective supervision of banking"

**Elastic currency mechanics:**
- Money created via: gold receipt, rediscounting commercial paper, discounting
  foreign trade acceptances, open market purchases of government securities
- **Real bills doctrine**: money creation via rediscounting — apparent conflict
  with gold standard; Friedman/Schwartz argued "more apparent than real"

**The wartime abuse (1914–1920):**
- From Fed creation (Nov 1914) to June 1920, money stock **more than doubled**
- Pre-US war entry: Fed minor role — money growth driven by **gold inflows**
  from belligerent nations; US: deficit of **$3.7B in 1914** → surplus of
  similar size by **1919**
- War entry (April 1917): Fed money = 21% of high-powered money → **59% by
  November 1918** via rediscounting war-bond-purchase loans
- Fed credit: $0 (Nov 1914) → **~$3 billion by end 1919** (~4% of GDP)

**Postwar bubble setup (1919):**
- Expected postwar contraction ran course by **March 1919**
- Fed kept rates low to fund floating debt and prop up government bond prices
- Result: **speculative bull market** in industrials and commodities through 1919
- Investors playing by old rules (postwar deflation) missed the 1919 bull market

**The statutory limit that burst the bubble:**
- Fed required: **40% gold reserves against notes**; **35% legal tender vs.
  net deposits**; internal min **40% total reserves against all liabilities**
- Reserve ratio fell from **48.1% (Dec 1918)** to **42.7% (Jan 1920)** —
  approaching the limit
- Call money rates hit **15% (June 1919)**, rising to **30% (November)**
- Fed raised discount rate to **4.75%** (Nov/Dec 1919), then **6.0% (Jan/Feb
  1920)** — this tightening, forced by the reserve ratio floor, was the
  proximate trigger for the 1919-21 bear market

**Price level uncertainty:**
- Wholesale prices rose **147%** from June 1914 to May 1920 — largest since
  Civil War; how far must they fall to restore US competitiveness?
- French franc and mark: down **60%+** vs. dollar (Jan 1919–Jan 1921);
  Sterling: down **almost 30%** — all with high domestic inflation
- GDP (Balke & Gordon NBER): 1914–1919 nominal GDP +123%; real GDP +25%;
  real growth of **1916 alone** accounts for **70% of all real growth** in
  the five-year period
- Gold stock grew from $1,526m (1914) to $2,873m (1918), then fell postwar

**Germany as alternative scenario:**
- Gold-to-note coverage: Germany fell from 54% (1914) to **1.5% (1921)**;
  Austria to **0.01%**; contrast: US rising from 18% to 93%
- Germany had 29% wholesale price inflation (1921) vs. 24% deflation in
  France, 26% UK, 11% US decline
- German economy avoided recession; Frankfurter Zeitung average +40%
  March–August 1921; **Berlin Stock Exchange suspended trading September 1921**

> "Early in July paper marks began to show such a tendency toward depreciation
> that the investing classes in Germany took alarm and there was a mad rush to
> invest their paper in industrial and other securities before currency dropped
> further." — *Wall Street Journal*, 9 September 1921

- German Wholesale Price Index: 152 (1918) → 1,338 (1921) → 23,927 (1922) →
  **11,634,000,000,000 (1923)**; peak monthly inflation 3 million%
- Key lesson: what appeared positive policy vs. deflation in 1921 led to
  **almost complete loss of capital** for equity investors

### Structure of the Market in 1921

**NYSE:** 382 companies, 586 total issues (185 preferred); common stocks
considered **speculative** — institutional investors focused on bonds.

**Key sectors:**
- **Railroads**: NYSE since 1830; key names: Atchison Topeka & Santa Fe,
  Baltimore & Ohio, Canadian Pacific, Union Pacific, NY Central
- **Steel**: dominated by **US Steel** (JP Morgan via Carnegie Steel); capacity
  utilization fell to **20% by 1921**
- **Automobiles**: Ford ~half of US autos but didn't list until 1956; **General
  Motors**, Studebaker, Pierce Arrow, Willys-Overland available
- **Oils**: 13 former **Standard Oil** trust fragments; independent sector
  focused on Mexico (Mexican Petroleum, Sinclair Oil)
- **Copper**: price collapsed to 12¢/lb (1921) vs. 15.5¢ pre-war avg; only 8
  of leading US copper mines still producing
- **Sugar**: crashed from 26¢/lb (1920) to 5.5¢ (1921); **National City Bank**
  became one of Cuba's largest sugar producers via seized collateral on bad loans
- **Curb Market**: until 28 June 1921, literally on sidewalk at Broad Street —
  home to British American Tobacco, Gillette, Philip Morris, **RCA**

**Railroad index decline only half that of the DJIA** in the bear market —
guaranteed dividends provided downside cushion.

### The Bond Market in 1921

**Long bear since 1899:**
- Federal debt-to-GDP averaged just **4.2%** (1900-16); legislation required
  banks to hold govt bonds as banknote security → 80% of $965m debt pledged
- **Corporate bond market** was the prime investment medium — nearly **20x**
  larger than entire US govt bond market pre-war
- Average yield of prime corporate bonds rose ~**3.2% (1900)** to **~5.2%
  (1921)**; Jan 1917–May 1920: corporate bond prices fell **23.6%** — more
  than half the total 1899-1920 decline in just 3 years

**WWI transforms bonds:**
- Federal debt rose from **2.7% of GDP (1916)** to **32.9% (1921)** — largest
  debt load in US history (edging out Civil War peak of 31.0%)
- Federal debt: **$24.3 billion** by 1920, surpassing the $18.0 billion
  corporate bond market
- **Liberty Bond** issues (1917-18): ~18 million subscribers out of 100 million
  population; 71% of Federal debt in 1921; first yielded 3.5%, fourth 4.25%
- In the 1920-21 bond bear, **~80% of original Liberty Bond holders sold out**

**Bond market structure 1921:**
- Average daily NYSE bond turnover: ~$10M vs. $36M for stocks; Federal debt
  consistently >50% of bond trading
- Total corporate bonds outstanding Dec 1920: 6,000+ issues, 10x NYSE-listed
  stocks — Railroads: 1,700 issues/$9.6bn; Utilities: 3,795/$6.1bn;
  Industrials: 868/$2.4bn
- Foreign sovereign bonds on NYSE: Argentina, Belgium, Brazil, Canada, Chile,
  Cuba, Denmark, France, Japan, Mexico, Norway, Sweden, UK, Uruguay — sign of
  US rivaling UK as global financial center
- **Imperial Russian Government bonds** trading at ~20 on curb market

### At the Bottom with the Bear — Summer 1921

**Undervaluation and the 40-year stagnation:**
- **DJIA on 24 August 1921: 63.9** — a level first reached 27 January 1899;
  zero price progress in two decades despite massive economy:
  nominal GDP +383%, real GDP +88%, real GDP per capita +33%, steel
  production +320%, railroad track +57%, autos $4m → $1.6bn,
  telephones 1m → 13.3m, exports +563%
- **Alfred Cowles** (1938): his broader index confirms the DJIA story — August
  1921 market at similar level to June 1881; **40 years of zero price progress**
- Listed sector earnings grew only **130% from 1881** vs. nominal GDP growth
  of 732% and real GDP growth of 435%
- S&P earnings per share: 1880 peak 123; 1894 trough 40; 1916 peak 383;
  1921 trough 73
- Valuation ambiguity: using depressed 1921 earnings → PE = 24.5x; using peak
  1916 earnings → PE = 4.6x; using normalized 1922-26 average → PE was **7x**

**Valuation at the bottom:**
- Cyclically-adjusted PE declined from ~25x (1901) to ~5x (1921)
- Cumulative retained earnings per share of listed industrials averaged **$48**
  from 1915–1920 = same as the average price of an industrial share in 1921
- Many companies selling below **working capital**; copper mines below cost
  to equip them

> "…the capitalisation of scores of concerns today is selling below working
> capital. In the case of many companies the shares are selling for less than
> the value of the plants… The shares of a large number of industrial
> corporations are selling at one-third their respective intrinsic values."
> — Anonymous banker, WSJ, 19 September 1921

**Q ratio:** peaked at **1.12x (December 1905)**; at year-end 1921: q = **0.35x**;
given significant rally from August to December 1921, the August bottom
implied q ≈ **0.28x** — equities at >70% discount to replacement value.

### Good News and the Bear (1921)

**The "wait for bad news" fallacy:**
- **This advice was wrong for 1921**: the WSJ in summer 1921 was full of
  bullish forecasts from businessmen, Fed officials, and politicians —
  all accurately predicting the end of the bear
- An investor waiting for absence of good news would have missed the entire
  **Roaring Twenties**
- The DJIA bottomed **24 August 1921**; NBER dates economic trough as July 1921
  — improvement in economy coincided with equity improvement

**WSJ Bullish Signals Chronology (June–October 1921):**
- **25 June**: Steel makers taking more cheerful view
- **27 June**: Fed Reserve Bulletin declares business has begun to improve
- **27 June**: DR Crissinger (Comptroller) predicts 5% commercial paper rates soon
- **27 June**: Harry F. Sinclair: "We have hit the bottom"
- **14 July**: Secretary Hoover declares business "turned the corner"
- **18 July**: Ford, Studebaker, Dodge running nearly full; motor sales improving
- **1 August**: US Steel quarterly earnings $10M better than expected
- **1 August** (Judge Gary, US Steel): 'The biggest business this country has
  ever witnessed' is coming
- **9 August**: **Benjamin Strong** (Governor, NY Fed): "We have entered the
  cycles of recovery. One of the best symptoms of recovery is the easier
  rates for money."
- **24 August: DJIA bottoms**
- **22 September**: Secretary of Labor Davis — "the greatest era of prosperity
  the United States has ever known near at hand"
- **21 October**: VP Coolidge — "a definite turning of the tide in the business
  and industrial activities of the country"

**Key insight:** Extreme undervaluation periods are precisely when investors
should NOT wait for absence of good news.

### Price Stability & the Bear (1921)

**The mechanism:**
Under gold standard, the contraction mechanism: deflation reduced domestic
prices → improved competitiveness → improved external accounts → gold flowed
back into US → easing liquidity → economic recovery. **Price stabilisation**
was the practical signal this had completed its work.

**Scale of 1921 deflation:**
- **Nine major economies** suffered their worst annual deflation of the 20th
  century simultaneously in 1921: Australia, Canada, France, Netherlands,
  South Africa, Sweden, Switzerland, UK, US
- Unskilled labour: 60¢/hour (peak) → 25¢/hour (summer 1921)
- Price declines from 1920 peak to 1921 lows:
  - Farm products: **-87.7%** (still 18% above 1913)
  - Metals and metal products: **-78.9%** (still 20% above 1913)
  - Food: **-72.2%** (still 52% above 1913)
  - All commodities: **-69.8%** (still 52% above 1913)
- Most prices did NOT have to deflate all the way to 1913 levels

**Demand response confirmed the bottom:**
- Automobile industry saw demand improving from early summer in response to
  price cuts; phonograph companies cut prices by a third → high-priced model
  sales stimulated
- Coal operators refusing to sell forward (believed prices would rise); iron
  prices advancing first in Chicago then spreading nationally
- Even investors who anticipated postwar deflation couldn't fully protect:
  one broker bought $15,000 of diamonds in 1918 → pawnshops only lent 10%
  of purchase price; forced to sell for $6,000

### Liquidity and the Bear (1921)

**Gold accumulation:**
By 1921, the US held **one-third of the world's monetary gold supply**.
Many believed forecasting the money/credit cycle turn was the key to
predicting economic and equity recovery.

**Credit lags:**
- Prices in textiles began declining Feb/March 1920; general price level
  didn't fall until 3 months later
- **Credit volume peaked October 1920** — 5 months after general price peak
- By summer 1921: Department of Labor index 46% below maximum, commodity
  index 58% below maximum, but national bank loans only **14% below maximum**
- Discount rate rose from **4% (November 1919)** to **7% (June 1920)** — a
  level not surpassed until 1973

**The reserve ratio as key indicator:**
- Fed law: 40% gold against notes; 35% cash against net deposits
- Monitoring the reserve ratio was how investors assessed the Fed's capacity
  and willingness to ease money
- First rate cut came in May 1921 when ratio had risen to **56.4%** (highest
  since August 1918)
- That cut came **three months before the equity market bottomed** — DJIA
  fell another 20% in the interim

**Liquidity signals were misleading:**
- **Market interest rates** also misleading: call money fell from 10-14% (June
  1920) to 5% (August 1921) — yet stock market continued to fall
- **Nominal M2** showed no improvement until mid-1922 — well after equity bottom
- Real M2 showed improvement April–May 1921 — but signaled buy before final
  20%+ DJIA decline
- Credit growth continued to fall until mid-1922 while the economy expanded
  7% real and DJIA rose 36%
- **Conclusion:** watching credit growth numbers was useless for finding the
  1921 bottom

**Why banking system survived 1920-21 (unlike 1929-33):**
- Banks entered deflation in a **highly liquid position**
- **Chase National Bank** balance sheet (30 June 1920): cash/Fed Reserve 24%
  of assets; only 12% demand loans
- Public faith in banking held → deposits increased; long experience with
  deflationary periods under gold standard had trained banks to maintain
  liquid balance sheets

### Key Analytical Conclusions: What Actually Worked (1921)

**1. Sentiment extremes as contrarian signals:**
- "Bear tips are now quite the thing, and anyone who even intimates that a
  stock may go up is looked on with suspicion. Such a condition usually marks
  the end of a bear market"
- Edward L. Doheny of Mexican Petroleum had newsletter writer **WC Moore**
  indicted for criminal libel over a bearish newsletter

**2. Short interest as technical signal:**
- When supply of stocks for lending decreases and borrowing cost rises →
  shorts running out of ammunition
- Very bottom occurred when number of stocks lent at a **premium peaked** —
  peak shorting activity = impending wane of bear pressure

**3. Ownership concentration: US Steel / AT&T as bellwethers:**
- US Steel broker holdings: 58% (1916 peak) → 23% (June 1921)
- In 1919-21, **AT&T** became co-equal bellwether with US Steel
- Peak holder count in both coincided with nadir of public participation —
  once public was maximally conservative, any shift would increase equity

**4. Volume at the bottom (contrarian):**
- 1921 bottom attended by **low volume**, NOT high-volume capitulation
- Rising volume after first DJIA rebound suggested the price rise was
  sustainable; modern "high-volume capitulation" adage did not apply

**5. Key players:**
- **Jesse Livermore**: made >$3M in one day shorting 1907 crash; switched
  from bear to bull **almost exactly at the 1921 bottom**; his bull thesis:
  depleted inventories would halt price declines and spark buying
- **William Peter Hamilton** (WSJ editor): using **Dow Theory** accurately
  called the 1921 bottom

**6. The earnings trap:**
- **Bulls of 1921 focused on value and technical factors — not earnings**
- Market bottomed August 1921; reported earnings had a **further 37% to fall**
  before bottoming December 1921

### Dow Theory

- First expounded by **Charles H. Dow** in WSJ 'Review and Outlook' from
  April 1899; first codified by **S.A. Nelson** in *The ABC of Stock
  Speculation* (1903)
- **William Peter Hamilton** joined WSJ 1899; wrote *The Stock Market
  Barometer* (1922); three simultaneous movements: **primary** (≥1 year),
  **secondary** (sharp counter-trend moves), and daily fluctuation
- Key practical rule: both averages (industrials + rails) must **corroborate
  each other** — no primary movement where they disagree

> 'One of the best tested of them is that the two averages corroborate each
> other, and that there is never a primary movement, rarely a secondary movement,
> where they do not agree.' — **William Peter Hamilton**

### Bonds and the Bear (1921)

**Timeline:**
- Bear market in bonds had been underway since **1899**
- **Dow Jones index of 40 corporation bonds** hit its lowest reading: **57.29 in
  May 1920**, re-bottomed at 57.75 in June 1921 — then launched a
  **25-year bull market in bonds** that ran until 1946
- **Liberty Bond** market: bottomed decisively May 1920; did not retest lows;
  rallied 13 months May 1920–June 1921, then made equal gains again in just
  5 months June–October 1921

**Sequencing of recovery:**
- **Government bonds led**; **corporate bonds followed** ~3 weeks later
- **Liberty Bond prices** bottomed: first week of June 1921
- **Corporate bond index** bottomed: final week of June 1921
- **DJIA** did not bottom until **24 August 1921** — bonds clearly led stocks
- Within corporates, **industrial bonds were last** — companies had cut
  dividends, while railroad/utility outlook was more stable
- Recovery led by **high-yielding paper and second-grade rails** — biggest
  gains in the riskiest credits

**Investor sentiment at bond bottom:**
- No "crescendo of pessimism" — instead, clamour of good news being ignored
- WSJ (27 June): commercial banks becoming major buyers of government bonds as
  demand for credit "for expansion purposes has almost ceased"
- Call money ~5%; corporate bonds available at 8% yields — obvious carry trade
  underutilized due to fear of continued deflation
- Price stabilization reduced deflation risk, encouraging borrowing short at
  ~5% to buy higher-yielding bonds

**High-grade rail bonds** yielded ~5.5% at the August 1921 recovery point;
lower-grade rails averaged ~8.6% (Erie conv 4s: 9.8%; St L & San Fran Inc 6s:
10.3%).

**1921 Bear Market Bottom Checklist:**
> *US equities traded below fair value from 1917 to 1926. How was an investor
> to know that 1921 was the opportune moment? There was a combination of
> signals: improving demand at lower prices for selected goods (particularly
> autos); commodity price stabilisation; improving economic news being ignored
> by the market; rising volumes on a strong stock market; falling volumes on
> a weak stock market; a rising short interest; a final fall in equity prices
> on low volumes; reductions in Fed-controlled interest rates; a rally in the
> government bond market; a rally in the corporate bond market; positive signals
> from the Dow Theory. This is the checklist of features one needs to focus on
> if one seeks the bottom of the bear market.*

## PART II: JULY 1932

> 'It wasn't anywheres near as frightful as this one.' — **James T. Farrell**,
> *Judgement Day*

- DJIA: 63.9 (24 August 1921) → **381.2 (3 September 1929)** (+500%)
- Then plunged **89%** to **41.22 on 8 July 1932**
- Napier's contrarian warning: **1929–32 may be more the exception than the
  rule** among bear markets — over-studying it may have misled generations

### The Road to July 1932

**The 1920s bull market — slower start than remembered:**
- DJIA still below 100 as late as **July 1924**; still below November 1919
  high as late as **April 1925**
- 1921–24 = return to pre-recession profit and valuation ("normalcy"); rose
  87% from 1921 low with avg. dividend yield of **6.4% by April 1925**
- NYSE seat prices flat 1921–24 (~$77k–$101k range); surged to **$625k by 1929**
- NYSE volume: 260M shares (1922) → **1.12 billion (1929)** — speculative
  explosion only truly began in 1925

**Political catalyst:**
- **Calvin Coolidge** victory November 1924 seen as catalyst; Republicans
  reluctant antitrust enforcers
- **Andrew Mellon** (Treasury Secretary, one of America's richest men):
  tax cuts, cut government spending
- Coolidge inaugural (4 March 1925): "The collection of any taxes, which are
  not absolutely required… is only a species of legalised larceny."

**Two structural drivers of the 1920s bull:**

**1. Electrification:**
- Only 8% of US dwellings wired by 1907; **37.8%** by 1921; **67.9%** by 1929
- Factory machinery powered by electricity: **32% (1919) → 49% (1929)**
- Commercial electrical equipment sales in 1929: **146% higher** than 1921
- US produced more electric power in 1929 than **rest of world combined**
- Real GDP per man-hour rose **19%** from 1921–1929; capital 23% more
  productive in 1929 vs. 1921
- **Deflationary surprise**: most US prices in 1929 *below* 1921 levels
- 5 million fewer horses on US farms in 1929, freeing 1/3 of cropland from
  fodder to food; household appliance sales +189%; radio sales nearly **30-fold**
- Cowles Commission: S&P Composite earnings per share rose **455%** from 1921–1929

**2. Consumer credit expansion (instalment credit):**
- Still socially stigmatized pre-WWI; **General Motors Acceptance Corporation**
  opened 1919 — made instalment credit respectable
- Household car purchases on instalment: **4.9% (1919) → 15.2% (1929)**
- By 1927: 15% of all consumer durables ($6B) on instalment; 85%+ of
  furniture, 80% phonographs, 75% washing machines sold this way
- Key insight: instalment credit expansion happened **within overall monetary
  moderation** — a new channel where none previously existed

**Fed policy complications (1920s):**
- Large gold inflows naturally dictated easy policy; Fed believed it had to
  **curb speculation** associated with easier money
- **Dual objectives** (curb speculation + ensure gold flows to partners) would
  complicate policy responses in 1927-29

**Gold concentration and the interwar gold standard:**
- By 1925, US held **43.2% of world gold reserves**; 48 countries had
  returned to gold by 1929
- **France** deliberately undervalued its currency, accumulating gold;
  **Britain** returned at a rate that overvalued sterling
- From 1923–1929, Fed **sterilized gold inflows** to dampen monetary growth

**Fed credit growth was NOT excessive in the 1920s:**
- Total bank loans grew **43.5%** from 1921–1929; nominal GDP grew 42% — credit
  broadly in line
- Money stock at the peak (August 1929) was actually **lower than 16 months
  earlier** — the Fed had been tightening since 1928

**The speculative lending problem:**
- Source of speculative loans was **not the banking system** — it was
  non-banks, corporations, and foreign capital flowing into the call market
- NYSE-listed stocks and bonds rose from $27B (end 1924) to $89B (October 1929)
- By October 1929, "Others" (non-bank) broker loans: **$6.64B** vs. NY banks'
  $1.1B — direct pressure on member banks was largely irrelevant
- NY Fed raised rates for first time in **February 1928**
- NY Fed discount rate: **3.5% (Feb 1928) → 6.0% (Aug 1929)**
- Call-money rates: **4.24% (Jan 1928) → 9.23% (July 1929)**; March 1929
  brief panic at **20%** — then fell back

**Rate hikes that killed the economy:**
- Hikes too small to stop speculation but large enough to slow the economy
  by July 1929 — curbing speculation by curbing overall economic activity

### Course of the Dow 1929–32

**End of the bull market:**
- Industrial/factory production peaked **June 1929**; NBER reference peak:
  **August 1929**
- Global slowdown already underway: Australia/Netherlands slowed 1927;
  Germany/Brazil slumped 1928; Argentina/Canada/Poland H1 1929
- US ran current account **surplus** throughout the 1920s — holding 43% of
  world gold reserves while not running a deficit restricted global growth
- US private capital outflows fell **42% in 1929** from 1927–28 average,
  starving world of liquidity

**Triggers of the crash:**
- **DJIA peaked 3 September 1929**; fell 32% by 28 October
- **26 September 1929**: Bank of England raised discount rate 5.5% → 6.5%
  to defend against capital drain to "orgy of speculation 3,000 miles away"
  (Chancellor **Philip Snowden**) — changed US/UK rate differential
- **30 September**: Collapse of **Clarence Hatry**'s UK business empire —
  forging scrip to raise finance; banks pulled credit, stocks free-fell
- **11 October**: Massachusetts DPU refused Boston Edison a 4:1 stock split —
  "no one… on the basis of its earnings, would find it to his advantage to buy"
- The magnitude of initial crash was not exceptional: 1907 bear -44%;
  1919-21 -47%; Sept–Nov 1929 -48%
- By 17 April 1930, market had recovered **52% of the decline** — just 23%
  below Sept 1929 high; not abnormal

**What makes 1929-32 unique — the four phases:**
> DJIA phases of the 1929–32 bear (Figure 41):
- The Crash (Sept–Nov 1929): -48% (cumulative -48%)
- 1st Rally (Nov 1929–Apr 1930): +48% (cumulative -23%)
- Summer Decline (Apr–Sept 1930): -17% (cumulative -36%)
- **First Banking Crisis** (Sept–Dec 1930): -36% (cumulative -59%)
- 2nd Rally (Dec 1930–Feb 1931): +23% (cumulative -49%)
- **Second Banking Crisis** (Feb–Oct 1931): -56% (cumulative -77%)
- Potential Stability (Oct 1931–Mar 1932): +3% (cumulative -76%)
- **The Gold Drain** (Mar–Jul 1932): -54% (cumulative -89%)

**Public participation and investment trusts:**
- Pre-WWI: ~500,000 Americans owned securities; by 1929: **up to 20 million**
- 1920: at most 40 investment trusts; 1927-1930: **700 trusts created**;
  1929 alone: 265 new trusts with $3B subscribed; peak market cap: **$8 billion**
- Dubious practices: illiquid stock, price manipulation, extreme leverage

**The first banking crisis (Oct–Dec 1930):**
- From mid-1929 to Oct 1930, economy contracted only modestly: GNP -1.8%,
  CPI -0.9% in 12 months from June 1929
- Nov–Dec 1930 alone: **$550 million** in deposits tied up in 608 bank
  suspensions; Key failure: **Bank of the United States** — largest US bank
  failure to that point, 400,000 depositors
- Before Oct 1930, the **deposit-currency ratio** was at an all-time high
- When deposits began to flee, fractional reserve meant banks had to reduce
  deposits by **$14 for every $1** needed as cash
- DJIA declined 36% from mid-September to mid-December 1930, crashing
  through the November 1929 low

**Deposit-currency ratio collapse:**
- 11.5 (Oct 1930) → 4.4 (March 1933) — levels not seen since 19th century
- Total bank deposits: $59.8B (June 1930) → $49.5B (Dec 1931) — back to
  1924 levels

**Second banking crisis and European contagion (1931):**
- **May 1931**: Austria's **Creditanstalt** failed; exchange controls imposed;
  contagion to Hungary then Germany (Creditanstalt had controlling interest
  in Hungary's biggest bank)
- More than half of German bank deposits owned by non-Germans; full-scale
  German banking crisis July 1931
- Domestic bank suspensions: Aug 1931–Jan 1932 — **1,860 banks** with
  $1,449M in deposits — equalling the **entire 1921–29 period** in 6 months

**The gold drain and the Fed's failure:**
> "… no longer any fear on the part of the banks or the business community
> that some sudden and temporary business crisis may develop and precipitate
> a financial panic… The Federal Reserve System is the antidote for money
> contraction and credit shortage."
> — **Andrew Mellon**, Secretary of the Treasury, Saturday Evening Post, 1928

- The Fed offered no such antidote — it did NOT expand credit to member banks
- **September 1931**: UK devalued sterling; eight countries immediately followed
- US gold stocks had risen **15%** (Sept 1929 to Aug 1931) then declined **15%**
  (Aug–Oct 1931), erasing all gains
- Fed raised discount rate: **1.5% → 3.5% in October 1931** — forced banks to
  dump even government bonds, which for first time saw prices decline

**The final blow — 1932 gold drain:**
- October 1931: **National Credit Corporation** launched; February 1932:
  **Reconstruction Finance Corporation** created; **Glass-Steagall Act** passed
  allowing government bonds as collateral for Fed notes
- March 1932 DJIA marginally above October 1931 — investors who bought then
  lost **54%** before July 1932 bottom; US gold reserves fell a further 12%
  Mar–Jul 1932
- **George Harrison** (FRBNY governor) admitted open-market program was
  prompted by fear of **Congressional interference**, not genuine policy
- The Fed's belated reflationary pivot convinced foreign investors the US might
  abandon gold standard — they liquidated dollar deposits, accelerating the drain
- DJIA bottomed July 1932 at a level **89% below** its September 1929 peak

- 1,587 bond issues; $52,193m par value; market value $36,856m
- Composition by market value: US government **40.5%** ($14,929m) — just
  11 issues; Foreign government **30.5%** ($11,242m); Railroads **13.4%**

- Fidelity Fund: 65% bonds, 28.6% stocks, 6.4% cash as of 30 June 1932

| Sector     | Sep 1929 | Jun 1932 |
|------------|----------|----------|
| Government | 3.70%    | 3.76%    |
| Industrial | 4.10%    | 6.80%    |
| Railroad   | 5.38%    | 9.14%    |
| Utility    | 5.16%    | 7.21%    |

- **BAA corporate bonds**: yield rose from ~6% (1929) to nearly **12% at 1932

**The paradox — government bonds fell in deflation:**
- Long-term govt yield: 3.70% (Sept 1929) → 3.15% (June 1931, normal
  safe-haven) → spiked to **4.26% (January 1932)** over a period when
  wholesale prices fell 31%
- Trigger: sterling's departure from gold (Sept 1931) → foreign CBs liquidated
  US dollar assets → gold stock declined → banks also hoarded cash
- **Government bond stabilization** in early 1932 likely due to **RFC**
  (January 1932, borrowing power $1.5B expanded to $3.3B) + Fed bond-buying

**Foreign government bonds — a surprise:**
- US became the world's primary capital exporter in the 1920s; Jan 1926:
  116 foreign government issues ($3,024m); by Sep 1929: **202 issues

- **1929 prices vs. 1932 lows:** Bolivia: 84½ → **2**; Brazil: 88⅛ → **14**;
  Colombia: 77⅞ → **13½**; German external 7s: 105¼ → **41¼**;
  French external 7s: **109 → 109** (held perfectly — undervalued currency);
  UK 5½s: **102 → 90** (resilient despite leaving gold)

**Pattern of corporate bond prices:**
- Sept 1929–Sept 1930: rally — industrial yields fell 33 bps as recession
  appeared normal; Sept 1930 onward: first banking crisis → dramatic decline
- **Key implication:** 11-month corporate bond rally after the 1929 crash
  suggests the stock market crash itself may not have caused the Depression —
  the real break came with the banking crisis one year later

**Corporate bond bottom:** Baa yields jumped from 6.72% (April 1931) to
**10.42% (December 1931)**; corporate bond bottom: **June 1932** — Baa yield
peaked at **11.52%**, one-third higher than the highest yield ever previously
recorded (data available from 1919). Corporate bonds bottomed ~**5 weeks
before** the equity market bottom.

### At the Bottom with the Bear — Summer 1932

**What made 1932 different:**
- In 1921, stocks became cheap through a **slow process** — prices drifted
  sideways while economy and earnings grew
- In 1932, the market crashed from extreme overvaluation to extreme
  undervaluation in under three years
- DJIA peaked September 1929 at **381.2** — 220% above the previous bull
  market high of November 1919; 500% above August 1921 low
- **1929-32 is the exception** among the four bear bottoms — the other three
  feature slow, grinding devaluation; 1932 features a sudden crash

**Earnings collapse:**
- DJIA fell 89%; S&P earnings fell **68%** from Sept 1929 to July 1932
- Real S&P earnings in July 1932 were below those reported in **October 1873**
- GDP (nominal) returned to ~1917 level; real GDP to ~1922 level

**Valuations at the July 1932 bottom:**
- Cyclically adjusted PE: **~70% below** its 1881-1932 average
- q ratio: probably fallen below **0.3x** — equities at more than a 70%
  discount to replacement value; same extreme cheapness as August 1921
- Despite earnings at 1880 levels, market capitalized them at only **10.2x**
  — a 26% discount to the 1871-1932 average monthly PE

**PE comparison:**
- **Cyclically adjusted PE** (S&P Composite): rose from **7.4x (Aug 1921)** to
  **31.6x (September 1929)**; long-run avg 1881–1929 = **15.3x**; previous
  peak was **26.5x (June 1901)**

**Price-to-book at the bottom:**
- *WSJ* (19 May 1932): 21 DJIA stocks at simple average **0.66x price-to-book**
  vs. 0.82x at 1921 low; DJIA fell further **22%** before July 8 low → final
  P/B ~**0.52x**
- Selected data (% of book value, 1932 low):
  - Bethlehem Steel: **9%** | US Steel: **13%** | American Smelting: **15%**
  - Mack Trucks: **19%** | Westinghouse: **25%** | International Harvester: **26%**
  - GE: **100%** (at book) | GM: **60%**

**The Roosevelt uncertainty:**
- By May 1932, **FDR** (before his nomination) stated capital must accept
  smaller returns relative to labor
- This made "normalized earnings" genuinely unknowable — the 68% decline
  could represent a new regime, not just a cycle

> "Bear markets, where three-year price declines make overvalued equities
> cheap, are the exception and not the rule."

### Good News and the Bear (Summer 1932)

- By July 1932: 34 months of economic decline, two banking crises, 89%
  market decline, wholesale prices -40%, industrial production halved
- NBER bottom of recession: **March 1933** — the equity market bottomed
  **9 months before** the economy
- DJIA never revisited its July 8, 1932 low even after a third banking crisis

**WSJ positive reports (May–September 1932):**
- **9 May**: Auto sales up 36% March-to-April in seven states
- **21 May**: Employment up in 8 of 16 major industrial groups
- **11 June**: Chevrolet sales curve rising
- **21 June**: Fisher's wholesale price index records first gains across cotton,
  sugar, corn, wheat, cattle, hogs, tin, gasoline
- **8 July: DJIA bottoms**
- **9 July**: World commodity markets respond to Lausanne reparations agreement
- **11 July**: Bank suspensions drop to 24 from 41 the prior week

> "As in 1921, the investor's ability to **ignore good news**, rather than the
> predominance of bad news, characterised the end of the bear market in 1932."

**Pattern similarities between 1921 and 1932 bottoms:**
- Both: **motor industry** in vanguard of recovery; New England showed early
  improvement; pickup in demand appeared first in higher-quality, higher-priced
  goods; commodity price stability was the most reliable leading indicator

### Price Stability and the Bear (1932)

- Signs of **price stability** coincided with the bottom in both 1921 and 1932
- 1932 wholesale price improvement was **not sustained** unlike 1921 — economic
  growth stalled around November 1932; by March 1933, activity back below July
  1932 levels; prices fell through July 1932 lows
- **BUT**: equity prices at 1933 lows remained **20% above** July 1932 levels —
  stocks led commodities; sustained commodity advance finally began February
  1933 coinciding with most dramatic phase of the 1932-1937 bull market

**Commodity price carnage:**
- **Procter & Gamble** cut Ivory soap to its **1879 launch price**; sugar at
  1895 levels; copper, lead, zinc at 50-year lows; cotton near **1848 levels**

> "In the 93 years of my life, depressions have come and gone. Prosperity has
> always returned, and will again."
> — **John D. Rockefeller**, 8 July 1932 (the day the DJIA bottomed)

- Rockefeller also bullish on 30 October 1929: "my son and I have for some
  days been purchasing sound common stocks" (WSJ)

### Liquidity and the Bear (1932)

**Why liquidity was so hard to read:**
- Those waiting for Fed credit outstanding to stabilize as a buy signal were
  **still waiting in mid-1924** — three years after the 1921 bottom
- This explains the near-absence of positive liquidity commentary in the *WSJ*
  in summer 1932

**The internal Fed battle (1929–1932):**
- **FRBNY** vs. **Federal Reserve Board** longstanding conflict
- NY Fed bought **$160M of government securities** in October 1929 despite only
  $25M explicit Board authorization
- **Governor Benjamin Strong** died 1928 — denting NY's influence
- Board held upper hand; Fed credit outstanding showed no increase until late
  summer 1931
- From August–October 1931, total bills/securities held by Fed rose from $930M
  to **$2,062M** — but equity market didn't respond (sterling devalued
  simultaneously)

**April 1932 open-market program:**
- Widely attributed to **Congressional pressure**, not genuine policy conversion
- Fed credit expansion essentially ceased when **Congress adjourned on 16 July**

**Federal Reserve Bills & Securities Discounted (peaks/troughs):**
| Period | Amount |
|---|---|
| Peak October 1920 | $3,358M |
| Trough July 1924 | $827M |
| Peak December 1928 | $1,766M |
| Trough July 1931 | $906M |
| Peak October 1931 | $2,062M |
| Trough March 1932 | $1,635M |
| Highest March 1932–March 1933 | $2,407M |

**Why the injection didn't work:**
- Fed's purchases merely **offset gold outflows** — no net injection
- Commercial bank loans continued declining until **April 1933** — transmission
  failed entirely
- Key lesson: **betting on Fed easing as the equity buy signal was extremely
  dangerous** — buying August 1931 (first open-market program) exposed to
  further 69% DJIA decline; buying April 1932 (second program) lost one-third
  by July 1932
- Broad money growth didn't show improvement until Q1 1933

### The Bulls and the Bear — WSJ Chronicle, May–August 1932

**Key thesis:** Both in 1921 and 1932, investors who watched for **returning
price stability** as the market turning point were proven correct. Liquidity
analysis was fraught with problems in both bottoms.

**May 1932:**
- **9 May**: Sharpest upswing in months on Hoover budget-balance plea
- **12 May**: Volume rising on advances, declining on declines — early

- **28 May**: GE cuts quarterly dividend 60%; AT&T hits new low; market

- **2 June**: DJIA down ~60% since March 8; "the very depth of the decline

- **6 June**: Each recovery so far had been "swiftly followed by a reaction"

- **16 June**: Volume increases on advances, subsides on declines — classic

- **17 June**: "Without an advance in bonds, permanent improvement in stocks

- **21 June**: Daily volume hits **400,000 shares** — lowest since June 1924

- **25 June**: More than 50% of stocks trading **under $10/share**; 226 of
  422 issues at $10 or less; not one common at $100 or better
- **28 June**: DJIA breaks May low — Dow Theory confirms bear market continuation

**July 1932 — The bottom:**
- **2 July**: WSJ recommends quality stocks: AT&T, Consolidated Gas, Pacific

- **12 July**: 67 stocks trading **50%+ above their 1932 lows** on the day

- **25 July**: Bond advance starting to feed through to stocks via margin

- **26 July**: Volume dries up on dips, surges on advances — **direct reversal**

- **30 July**: "Trend line from September 1929 finally broken — conclusive

**August 1932 — Confirmation:**
- **16 August**: London led the summer rally (broker circular 26 July: American

- Security loans declined $112M in July to lowest level on record — total
  security loans down $8.518B from peak of $13.205B on 30 September 1929

### Key Behavioral Signals at the 1932 Bottom

**Signal 1 — Market stops responding negatively to bad news:** A subtle shift
from a market that had reacted to every bad headline.

**Signal 2 — Selective advance before the average bottoms:** 67 issues trading
50%+ above 1932 lows on the day the average bottomed; common trait: benefited
from higher commodity prices.

**Signal 3 — AT&T weakness as capitulation:** Most widely-held stock; major
one-day declines indicated small investors **finally throwing in the towel**.

**Signal 4 — "Safe" sector collapse:** Food stocks fell 23.2% in May; tobacco
fell 21.3% — vs. average monthly bear-market declines of 3.4% and 1.3%.
**Caveat:** food fell 27.9% and tobacco 21.3% in September 1930 — those who
bought on that "capitulation" lost half their money by July 1932. Only useful
**in conjunction with other indicators**.

**Signal 5 — Volume pattern reversal (most reliable):** From March to June
1932: volume rose on declines, dried up on recoveries. From June 1932 onward:
volume rose on advances, dried up on dips — **same constructive pattern noted
at the 1921 bottom**.

**Short sellers and capitulation:**
- In 1932, short positions **continued to increase** from the bottom (8 July)
  through 27 July — DJIA rose 29% in that span, meaning shorts endured severe
  pain before capitulating
- Bears not covering quickly was actually a **positive sign** for sustainability
- **Reality in both 1921 and 1932:** final decline on *low* volume; high volume
  only developed *after* the initial rise began
- Pattern: **volume rises after the bottom, not at the bottom**

**The earnings trap (1932):**
- S&P earnings bottomed **December 1932** — not visible until Q2 1933
- DJIA bottomed **8 July 1932** at 41.22; investors waiting for earnings clarity
  were buying in early summer 1933 at ~**2x the bottom**
- Listed companies (S&P data) never reported aggregate losses; but *all* US
  corporations (Harold Borger data): aggregate corporate profits went from
  +$1,696M (3Q 1929) to **-$677M (3Q 1932)**

**Shareholder count:**
- Among 346 monitored companies (R.G. Dun & Co.), stockholder count rose
  **42% from May 1930 to May 1932**
- US Steel shareholder count almost doubled 1929-32 — flight to safety
- Signal misfired in early 1931 (premature buy); at the *actual* bottom,
  stocks moved **back from public to professionals** — concentration into
  bigger hands marked the bull market's return

### The Fiscal Red Herring and What Actually Caused the Final Plunge

**The WSJ's obsession with fiscal policy was wrong:**
- Budget deficit was only **0.60% of GDP in 1931** — small by historical
  standards; deficit then ballooned to **4.6% (1932), 4.61% (1933), 5.50%
  (1934)** — while one of history's greatest bull markets was underway
- "Calling for a balanced budget as a precondition for equity recovery was
  the most erroneous investment judgement of 1932"

**The real culprit — gold drain:**
- US monetary gold stock contracted **12% (Jan–July 1932)** to $3.65 billion
- Two factors: (1) Trade deterioration — UK imported only 396 US autos
  (first four months 1932) vs. 5,188 in all of 1928; (2) Fed's April 1932
  open-market program interpreted as precursor to abandoning gold standard
  → capital flight
- Gold outflow stopped when Fed ended its purchase program AND Congress
  adjourned — both happened simultaneously with the bottom

**The Lausanne Agreement — the actual catalyst:**
- Germany's reparation history: defaulted 1921 → Ruhr occupation → hyperinflation
  → **Dawes Plan** (1924) rescheduled → **Young Plan** (1929) reduced → Hoover
  moratorium (summer 1931)
- At Lausanne: parties agreed to abolish Germany's reparation obligations in
  return for bonds — a de facto **90% reduction** in Germany's financial burden
- **Market impact:** news seen as breaking the international financial logjam;
  commodity markets rallied; German bonds jumped **10 points** in days

**Adolf Hitler defeated** by Paul von Hindenburg in April 1932 presidential
election — markets welcomed this; Nazi party failed to increase its vote in
31 July Reichstag elections.

- **Government bonds bottomed:** January 1932

## Part II (Continued): 1932 Bond Market

### Government Bond Collapse and Stabilization

- Long-term govt bond yield: 3.70% (Sept 1929) → 3.15% (June 1931) →
  spiked to 4.26% (January 1932) — even as wholesale prices fell 31%
- Sterling's departure from gold standard (Sept 1931) was the trigger:
  foreign central banks liquidated US dollar assets; domestic depositors
  hoarded cash over deposits → banks forced to liquidate government bonds
- NY Fed raised rediscount rate 1.5% → 3.5% on 16 October 1931 to
  defend gold standard exchange rate — further pressure on bonds
- **Government bond stabilisation** (early 1932): **Reconstruction Finance
  Corporation (RFC)** established January 1932 (borrowing power $1.5B,
  expanded to $3.3B by July Emergency Relief and Construction Act) —
  lent to banks, reducing need to liquidate bonds; Fed bond-buying
  from April 1932

### The Corporate Bond Collapse

- Corporate bond investors initially expected normal recession; **Baa-rated
  bond yields actually declined** from 6.12% (Sept 1929) to 5.65%
  (Sept 1930)
- First banking crisis (3Q 1930) ended complacency; by Dec 1930 Baa
  yields exceeded 6.7%
- Second banking crisis + internationalisation (1931): Baa yields jumped
  from 6.72% (April 1931) to 10.42% (December 1931)
- Corporate bond bottom: **June 1932** — Baa yield peaked at 11.52%,
  one-third higher than the highest yield ever previously recorded
- AAA corporate bonds: peak yield 5.41% June 1932 (highest since Dec 1921)
- Corporate bond market bottomed ~5 weeks **before** the equity bottom
  in July 1932

### What Turned the Corporate Bond Market

- **RFC** provision of credit to distressed banks and railroads alleviated
  forced selling
- **American Securities Investment Corporation** (ASI) — pool formed by
  major US commercial banks to buy bonds collectively; market surged on
  mere *announcement* before any buying occurred
- **Lausanne Agreement** directly boosted corporate bonds — German bonds
  jumped 10 points; commodity prices posted 3 consecutive weeks of gains
  in mid-July; by August advance "sustained for more than two months"
- Garner Plan ($1B bond issuance for public works) worried bond investors
  but never became law; **fears proved misplaced** — deficits ballooned
  without bond sell-off; bond bull market 1932–1949
- British war loan conversion: £2B 5% war loan converted to 3.5% perpetual;
  4% consols rose 8 points to £110 — just days before US equity bottom;
  likely redirected capital to US securities

- **Government bonds bottomed:** January 1932
- **Corporate bonds bottomed:** early June 1932
- **Equity market bottomed:** 8 July 1932
- **Commodity prices stabilising:** visible by late May 1932

### Roosevelt and the 1932 Bottom

> *'But getting back to politics, boys, this spring is only going to be a
> preview of the presidential election in 1932. Then we'll have Democrats
> all the way from the White House to the street cleaners on every block.'*
> — James T. Farrell, *Judgement Day*

- **Counterintuitive**: WSJ reported Roosevelt as likely President was

## Part III: June 1949

> *When we isolate the four most profitable times to invest in stocks,
> June 1949 comes second on our list after July 1932, but well ahead
> of 1942.*

- June 1949 ranks #2 for long-term returns (40-year horizon benefits
  from 1982-89 bull)
- April 1942 was also a rare bear bottom (q below 0.3x) but subsequent
  returns from 1949 significantly exceeded those from 1942

### The Road to June 1949

- World utterly transformed: WWII killed 55 million; US military grew
  from 250,000 to 12+ million; 300,000 Americans did not return
- **Equity market a backwater**: daily NYSE turnover often below $500,000;
  speculative enthusiasm focused on property, not stocks
- On 13 June 1949 — the day DJIA hit its postwar low — NY Times headline
  was about a heat wave ("1,500,000 Cool off at City's Beaches")

### The Course of the Dow — 1932-37

- July 1932 to March 1937: DJIA rose from 41.22 to 194.15, a **370% gain**
  with GDP deflator rising only 11%
- Dividend yield at July 1932 bottom: **10%**; dividends grew 60% to 1937
- Net national product grew **12% per annum** (real) from trough 1933 to
  peak 1937 — "Such economic growth rates have not been recorded before
  or since"
- S&P Composite earnings rose **176%** from 1932 to 1937; despite this,
  1937 earnings still below 1929/1928/1926/1925/1917/1916 levels
- Powerful combination: **surging earnings + surging dividends + falling
  interest rates** (excess capacity kept inflation low despite rapid growth)

### Volume Collapse — Evidence of Investor Disengagement

- From 1929 record trading day to 1942 low: NYSE volume fell **99%**
- **Turnover ratio**: 119% (1929) → 30% (1937) → 9% (1942) → 13% (1949)
- Bear market disinterest in 1949 higher than even worst Depression years
  of 1921 (59%) and 1932 (32%)
- NYSE market cap in 1949 **exceeded** the 1929 level — almost no one
  wanted to participate
- NYSE seat prices: $495,000 (1929) → $68,000 low (1932) → $17,000 (1942)
  — 75% decline from 1932 even as DJIA doubled; recovered 1942-46, then
  slid to $35,000 low in 1949

### The 1937-42 Bear

- By peak of recovery in 1937, **unemployment still at 14.3%**
- Fed began raising **reserve requirements** August 1936; raised margin
  on securities from 25% to 50% (using new **Banking Act** powers)
- Federal deficit/GDP fell from 4.2% (1936) to 2.8% (1937); corporate
  profitability fell on higher labour costs
- Industrial production fell one-third from 1937 peak to 1938 low;
  **S&P earnings declined almost 50%**
- In 12 months from March 1937, **DJIA declined 49%**, back to mid-1933 levels
- From 1938 low, S&P earnings doubled by December 1941 — but DJIA unchanged;
  investors lost faith in return to pre-1929 normalcy
- UK declared war on Germany: **DJIA rose 15%** in first 8 trading days
  of September 1939 (investors recalled US neutrality and WWI profits)
- Hitler's **blitzkrieg** (10 May 1940): **DJIA declined 23%** in 15 days
- **Lend-Lease** (March 1941): US receiving credit not cash; market disliked it
- **Pearl Harbor** (7 Dec 1941): market continued gradual decline, bottoming
  **28 April 1942** — five months later, down another 20%
- April 1942: **q ratio** at 70%+ discount to replacement value — a level
  only seen in 1921 and 1932

### The 1942-46 Bull — Fed Pegs the Yield Curve

- April 1942: Fed fixed **Treasury bill rate at 0.375%** for duration of war;
  de facto capped the entire federal yield curve until March 1951
- Maximum yield on longest-term government bonds: **2.5%**

| Security | Capped Yield |
|---|---|
| Treasury bills | 0.375% |
| 1-yr certificates | 0.875% |
| Short bonds | 2.0% |
| Longer bonds | 2.25% |
| 25-30 yr bonds | 2.50% |

> During this period, the Fed, in the words of its chairman **Marriner
> Eccles**, 'merely executed Treasury decisions.'

- With bonds yielding ~2.5% and equities yielding **11%+ in 1942**,
  the relative case for equities was compelling
- Tax receipts as % of GNP: 7% (1941) → **21% (1945)**; top income tax
  rate hit **90%**
- Excess profits tax reached flat rate of **95% by 1943**
- Result: S&P earnings and dividends basically unchanged 1946 from 1942
- The 1942-46 bull was driven **entirely by valuation expansion**,
  not earnings growth — against a background of negative real bond yields
- Bull market peaked May 1946 — **DJIA still 44% below September 1929
  all-time high**

### The 1946-49 Bear: Inflation vs. Deflation Debate

- Bulls expected pent-up consumer demand + return of normal corporate tax rates
- Bears remembered post-WWI Armistice: soaring inflation → sharpest annual
  deflation in US history + vicious bear market
- In 1946-49 bear, **S&P earnings increased nearly 200%** — yet equities fell
- **Sewell Avery** (Montgomery Ward): studied commodity price chart back to
  19th century; concluded postwar depression was foregone conclusion;
  loaded up on government bonds instead of investing in his business —
  deflation never came, bet proved disastrous

> "Prices rose and fell recurrently… The pattern had repeated itself as
> recently as 1919-1920… To Avery, a postwar depression was therefore a
> foregone conclusion. 'Who am I to argue with history?' he asked."
> — James Grant, *Money of the Mind*

### Why This Time Was Different

- **Employment Act of 1946** enshrined "maximum employment" as government mandate
- **Bretton Woods** (July 1944) explicitly designed to make deflation less likely
- Government sector now 15% of GDP vs. 3% in late 1920s
- Non-agricultural union membership: 33% of workforce by 1949 (up from
  13% in 1932) — wage pressure built into the system
- Wholesale price index rose **32% in 1946** alone when price controls lifted
- Post-WWII deflation: only **7.9%** (Aug 1948–June 1950) vs. **45%**
  post-WWI — dramatically muted

### Cold War Overlay

- **Churchill**, Missouri, March 1946: "an iron curtain has descended"
- **Truman Doctrine** (March 1947); **Marshall Plan**; Berlin Blockade
  May 1948 → Allied airlift; **NATO** formed April 1949
- Soviet atomic bomb detonated **29 August 1949** — "hot war" fears
- Soviet failure to act militarily over NATO eased fears; cold war arms race
  better for equities — echoes of US rearmament boom 1915-16

> "For over three years fears of what is now occurring in the business
> world have dampened market sentiment... There should be room for
> plenty of cheer in the realisation that: There isn't going to be
> a war…" — broker **Sam Smith**, Bache & Co., *WSJ*, 12 July 1949

### Structure of the Market in 1949

- NYSE market cap (end May 1949): **$64 billion** — one-third below Sept 1929
  but four times the 1932 bottom
- 1,457 listed equity securities; 1,043 companies; average market cap **$58 million**
- Investment management industry decimated: only **150 investment management
  companies** registered with SEC; total AUM = **$2.7 billion** — less than
  5% of US equity market
- Daily trading: ~900 of 1,500 listed issues; monthly volume just **$526
  million** (vs. $1,946 million at January 1946 peak)
- Market trading **half-days on Saturday** — liquidity extremely thin

### NYSE Sector Composition: 1932 vs. 1949

- **Oil** rose from 10.9% → 16.0% of market cap (1st by 1949)
- **Chemicals** rose 7.8% → 9.0% (2nd)
- **Utilities** fell 15.5% → 6.7%; **Railroads** 8.7% → 5.1%
- **Retail** and **Autos** grew significantly

### Sector Returns: June 1932 – June 1949 (Best/Worst)

**Best performers (total return):**
- Beer: +3,993% (repeal of Prohibition Dec 1933)
- Games: +2,606%
- Wholesale: +2,396%
- Autos: +2,201%
- Services: +2,199%

**Worst performers:**
- Utilities: +280%; Tobacco: +398%; Coal: +489%; Comms: +527%

> "The last shall be first and the first last." — Matthew's Gospel

- 1946-49 bear (DJIA -24%): utilities again defensive (-9.7%);
  transport -33.4%; oil sole large-sector gainer (+5.8%)

### The Bond Market in 1949

- NYSE-listed bonds: $128B market value in 1949 vs. $32B in 1932
- But number of NYSE bond issues fell from 1,600 (1932) to 912 (1949) —
  lowest since 1905
- Corporate bond market shrinking: 1932-1945, corporate bond retirements
  exceeded new issues; net retirements $42.8B
- **Government bonds dominated**: federal debt grew from $19.5B (1932) to
  $168.6B (1949 face value) — federal debt market alone ~3x the equity market
- NYSE bond trading bottomed 1949: $3M/day average; less than $1B traded
  for entire year — under 1% of $128B outstanding

> "For every $1,000 Government bond sale recorded on the Exchange there
> were $1,000,000 par value unrecorded transactions made by a very few
> banking houses known as Government bond dealers." — C.F. Childs

- Foreign bond market collapsed: 202 foreign bond issues on NYSE at Sept
  1929; only 46 by June 1949 DJIA bottom; countries below half par: Chile,
  Colombia, Costa Rica, Greece, Italy, Mexico, Peru, Serbia, Yugoslavia

> **Benjamin Graham** dismissed foreign bonds as having 'a bad investment
> history since 1914'… "Yet every few years, market conditions are
> sufficiently favourable to permit the sale of some new foreign issues
> at a price about par." — *The Intelligent Investor*

### Valuation at the 1949 Bottom

- DJIA at June 1949: back to February 1926 level — 57% below the 1929
  all-time high, nearly 20 years of zero capital progress
- **CAPE** fell to **5.8x earnings by June 1949** — even lower than 1932's 9.4x
- **Q ratio**: 0.43x (end 1932) → 0.36x (end 1949)
- Economy expanded materially (real GDP per capita +62%, 1926-49) while
  equities went nowhere
- Government's share of economy: expenditure rose from 3% to 15% of GDP
- **"Normalised" earnings in 1949 were only 33% higher than normalised
  1932 earnings** — deeply disappointing vs. nominal GDP growth
- Nearly 2/3 of all profit growth in 1932-49 occurred in just 1945-49
  (driven by repeal of **excess profits tax**); investors doubted sustainability

### Good News and the Bear (WSJ Headlines, Spring/Summer 1949)

> *"As in 1921 and 1932, the market's failure to respond to good news
> was more indicative of the end of the bear market than any proliferation
> of bad news."*

- 21 Apr: Q1 earnings better than expected
- 22 Apr: International Harvester farm equipment sales +30% YoY
- 28 Apr: U.S. Steel, Du Pont, General Motors Q1 reports show improvements
- 2 May: 197 firms Q1 earnings $732M — 21% above 1948 (WSJ survey)
- 12 May: Treasury Secretary Snyder — postwar adjustments "practically completed"
- 2 July: Unemployment jumped to 7-year high of 3,778,000 — but increase
  entirely in under-25 age group; non-farm adult employment actually rose
- **DJIA bottomed June 1949; NBER recession end date: October 1949** —
  equity market led the economic recovery by 4 months
- 1 August: NAPA purchasing survey — order bookings 30% reporting increases
  vs 28% decreases (first such signal since October 1948)
- 6 August: Fed cuts reserve requirements of member banks — freeing
  **$1,800 million** for investment or lending

### Auto Industry as Bear Bottom Indicator

- US auto sales: 2.1M (1946) → **5.1M (1949)** vs. 3.8M pre-war (1941)
- Average car age rose from 5.33 years (1941) to 8.73 years (1948);
  13 million cars 10+ years old vs. 5 million pre-war → secular demand
- Across all four bear market bottoms, **auto sector recovery preceded**
  the broader market bottom — products with low diffusion rates (rising
  from low ownership base) are first to benefit

### Price Stability and the Bear (1949)

> *"In 1949, as in 1921 and 1932, a return of general price stability
> coincided with the end of the bear market in equities."*

- Wholesale price index peaked August 1948, fell 7.5% by June 1949 DJIA
  bottom — only marginal further decline followed
- Post-WWII deflation: only **7.9%** decline vs. **45%** post-WWI
- Inventory liquidation had depressed prices; with stocks at rock bottom,
  deflation abated — key confirming feature
- April 1949: rents and food rising; June: wheat and oil prices improved;
  July: price rises in lead, rayon, poultry, zinc, copper

### Liquidity and the Bear (1949)

- Critical distortion: Fed's wartime obligation to **support government bond
  prices** made it a provider of unlimited high-powered money on autopilot
- When reserve requirements raised (Feb 1948) to slow inflation, banks sold
  government securities → Fed forced to buy to support prices → accidentally
  **expanded credit** while trying to tighten it; policy tool self-defeating
- Dec 1948–Oct 1949: only a **25% contraction** in Fed credit outstanding —
  then stopped; far smaller than post-WWI (69%) or 1928-31 (50%)
- **Fed balance sheet gave NO good buying signal** at the 1949 bottom

> *"Though the balance sheet of the Fed did not give a good indication of
> when to buy equities, changes in its policy stance did provide timely
> signals."*

- **Timeline of easing signals**:
  - March 1949: first easing of consumer credit controls (imposed Sept 1948)
  - May 1949: reserve requirement reductions began
  - 29 June 1949: Fed confirms it would stop preventing bond price rises
- Investors buying on first easing signal (March 1949) saw DJIA fall further
  10% before bottoming 13 June — still far better than waiting for balance sheet
- **Commercial bank loans** bottomed June 1949 (coinciding with DJIA bottom);
  grew 3.2% by November — better real-time indicator than Fed balance sheet
- **Broad money (M2)**: first improvement only Q2 1950 — useless for timing

| Episode | First policy easing signal | DJIA bottom | Balance sheet bottom |
|---------|---------------------------|-------------|----------------------|
| 1921 | Discount rate cut (May) | Aug 22 | Jul 1922 |
| 1949 | Consumer credit easing (Mar) | Jun 13 | Oct 1949 |
| 1932 | Rate cuts from Nov 1929 | Jul 1932 | — |

### The Bulls and the Bear (1949 Sentiment)

- **Myth of universal pessimism**: plenty of bullish voices at the bottom
  (as in 1921 and 1932) — waiting for universal pessimism means missing the turn
- 13 April: Broad Street Investing Corp — fund yield above 5% only in
  1932, 1911, 1942 (all 'highly auspicious buying occasions'); current yield 5.25%
- 19 Apr — Paine Webber's Harry D Comer: 20 common stocks yielding 8-12%,
  each earning 50%+ more than dividend payments, 20+ years uninterrupted dividends
- 25 Apr — Arthur Weisenberger: stocks never lower relative to dividends

- 2 May: Wall Street's largest houses lending "more stock than at any time
  since 1929" — massive **short interest** as a cushion against further decline
- 19 May: **Short interest** reaches 1,628,551 shares — largest in 16 years

- 6 June: L.O. Hooper compiles list of **225 stocks selling below working capital**

> "Traders should not overlook that the market is resting on a potential keg
> of dynamite... the latent power of the extremely large short interest is
> tremendous. Very few of the amateur shorts will succeed."
> — Herbert G. King, 25 July 1949

- 5 Aug — Ralph Rotnem (Harris, Upham): stocks yield **4%, more than bonds** —

### Analytical Summary: What the 1949 Bottom Reveals

- **Value was early but not wrong**: value investors beating the drum from
  early 1947 — yet DJIA still fell a further 10% over 18 months before bottoming
- By June 1949, S&P PE was **~60% below** long-term average — driven not by
  large price declines but by explosion in earnings
- **Q ratio** had been below geometric mean since **1939** — market traded
  below fair value for a full decade before the bottom

> *"As early as 1939, the q ratio of the market was below its geometric mean...
> In seeking the bottom it is important to determine equities are below fair
> value but also important to look at other factors if one is to avoid investing
> too early and watching cheap equities become very cheap."*

- Market's failure to respond to bad news = key leading indicator
- **Bear market ends on low volume** — final decline NOT accompanied by
  climactic selling; rebound begins on low volume; short interest does NOT
  capitulate immediately
- Rising prices with rising volumes after first rebound confirms the move
- Dow Theory correctly identified the bottom within a few days in
  **1921, 1932, and 1949** — three for three

### Bonds and the Bear (1946-1949)

- US long government bonds reached yield low of **2.03%** April 1946;
  declined requiring massive Fed support
- Fed's government bond holdings rose **129%** in 1948 alone to defend
  the yield peg; govt bond rally not until November 1948 (yields at 2.18%)
- **Moody's AAA corporate bonds**: yield low 2.46% April 1946; rose to 2.90%
  January 1948; rallied to 2.57% by end of 1949
- **Moody's Baa**: low 2.94% April 1946; rose to 3.56% January 1948
- Corporate bond prices bottomed **January 1948** — 10 months before
  government bond rally and **16 months before equity market bottom**
- In 1921 and 1932: government bonds → corporate bonds → equities;
  in 1949, corporates rallied *before* government bonds due to Fed yield-peg
  distortion — likely investors switched from pegged govt rates to higher-
  yielding corporate paper

> *"It seems more likely the 1921 and 1932 sequence of stabilisation of
> government bonds followed by corporate bonds followed by equities is more
> likely to be the normal sequence of events."*

- As in 1921 and 1932, equity prices stopped falling in 1949 when they
  reached a **~70% discount to replacement value of corporate assets**
- All three episodes (1921, 1932, 1949) occurred under a **fixed-exchange-
  rate regime** — Napier raises the question of whether these signals would
  work in a **free-float** environment (1982 test still pending at chapter's end)

## Part IV: August 1982

- By August 1982, the **S&P Composite Index** in real terms was back where

- Key question: did systemic inflation (not war-driven, cyclical inflation)

### The Course of the Dow: 1949-68

- **Bull market**: June 1949 to December 1968 — S&P Composite +662% nominal,
  +413% real; 8 corrections >10%; **Kennedy break** of 1962 the biggest at 27%
- Kennedy break: JFK branded anti-business for blocking steel price hikes;
  Cuban Missile Crisis (Oct 22-28, 1962) barely moved the market
- **Institutional shift**: pension fund assets grew 11-fold 1952–68; mutual
  fund assets 13-fold; institutional equity holdings: 9.5% of NYSE market
  cap (1952) → 21.3% (1968)
- **Yield gap reversal** — structural driver: since 1792, dividend yields on
  equities always exceeded bond yields; institutional shift out of bonds into
  equities pushed S&P dividend yield *below* long-term government bond yields
  in **July 1957** — permanently; by Dec 1968 peak: S&P yield = 2.88%,
  long-term government bonds = 5.65%
- Root cause: acceptance that postwar infrastructure had made **inflation
  structurally permanent**, eliminating the advantage of bonds' fixed payments
- Valuation expansion drove the bull: q ratio 0.49x → 1.06x; PE 5.8x → 18.5x;
  cyclically adjusted PE 11.7x → 25.1x; earnings grew +150% vs. S&P +662%
- **LBJ's "Great Society"** (January 1965): fiscal largesse rivalling FDR's
  New Deal; combined with Vietnam spending, spooked bond market
- Fed held rates steady (discount rate 4.0% in Oct 1967 = same as Sept 1959)
  even as inflation rose 1.23% to 3.56% — first hike: 50bp November 1967
- Market peaked soon after Nixon's November 1968 election victory →
  14-year bear market began

### The Course of the Dow: 1968-82

- **S&P Composite declined 63% in real terms** December 1968 to July 1982
- Bear market really began in 1969 as inflation moved above **3%**
- 1970s inflation averaged **7.1%** — beyond threshold for positive real equity returns
- Nixon suspended dollar-gold redemption **15 August 1971**; dollar devalued
  Dec 1971 ($35→$38/oz), then again 1973 ($42); by March 1973 free float confirmed
- In real terms, S&P hit its low in **July 1982**, 13% below Dec 1974 low —
  back to levels first surpassed in **June 1905**
- **Arthur Burns** became Fed chairman January 1970; Nixon on swearing him in:

> *I respect his independence. However, I hope that independently he will
> conclude that my views are the ones that should be followed.*

### The Nifty Fifty Disaster

- By 1972, investors concentrated in "one-decision" growth stocks —
  the **"Nifty Fifty"**; investors paid average **PE of 42x** in 1972
- These 50 stocks suffered average **62% price decline** in 1973-74
- Counterintuitive: for long-term holders (Nov 2001 horizon), Nifty Fifty
  delivered annual return of 11.62% vs. S&P 12.14% — nearly market returns

### Earnings Failure and Inflation's Equity Swindle

- Nominal EPS increased just **143%** vs. nominal GDP up **246%** —
  a 12% **real decline** in earnings
- Both poor earnings growth AND valuation compression (q ratio 1.06x → 0.38x)
- **Warren Buffett**, Fortune, May 1977:

> *In the decade ending in 1965, manufacturing companies had an average
> annual pre-tax margin on sales of 8.6%. In the decade ending in 1975,
> the average margin was 8%. Margins were down despite a very considerable
> increase in the inflation rate.*

### Burns, Nixon, and the Inflation Spiral

- **OPEC oil shock**: oil price $3.12/barrel (Oct 1973) → $11.63 (Dec 1973)
- Annual inflation surged from 2.9% (Aug 1972) to **12.5% by December 1974**
- Sharp recession (Nov 1973–Mar 1975) **failed** to bring inflation under control
- April 1973: term **"stagflation"** first appears in *Wall Street Journal*;
  coined November 17, 1965 by Conservative MP **Ian Macleod** in House of Commons
- Second oil shock: Iranian revolution January 1979, oil price +150%
- 1978: President Carter appointed **G. William Miller** (CEO/lawyer) as Fed chairman
- Gold exceeded **$300/oz** by August 1979 (vs. $35 under Bretton Woods)
- **Paul Volcker** appointed Fed chairman **6 August 1979**; raised rates
  (4-3 FOMC vote); convened **secret FOMC meeting October 6** — new policy:
  target M-1 aggregate growth; Fed funds rate range set **11.5-15.5%**

> *When Volcker took office, inflation was almost 12%. When he left, it
> was just 4%.*

### The 1980 Credit Shock and Its Aftermath

- Carter imposed **credit controls** March 1980; Q2 1980 GDP declined at
  **fastest rate ever recorded**; Fed Funds fell 20% → 8% in three months
- By end of 1980, Fed funds pushed to new high again; **May 1981** fight
  resumed — Fed funds back above **20% by July**
- Bond market did not believe inflation was beaten until **September 1981** —
  start of a 20+ year bond rally
- **Donald Regan** (Treasury Secretary) recalled Volcker saying he'd ease
  if Congress moved on deficits — standoff kept real rates high

### Financial Crisis Catalyzes the 1982 Bottom

- May 1982: **Drysdale Government Securities** went bust
- Early July 1982: **Penn Square Bank** (Oklahoma) failed — had originated
  $1B+ in loans sold to Continental Illinois
- August 1982: **Mexico** publicly bust; US commercial banks faced massive
  write-downs
- **Walter Wriston** (Citibank) at IMF annual meeting, Toronto, August 1982:

> *We had 150-odd finance ministers, 50-odd central bankers, 1,000
> journalists, 1,000 commercial bankers, a large supply of whiskey…
> 'the end of the world is coming'. It was the Titanic. We were just
> rearranging the deck chairs.*

- FOMC on June 30 — aware Mexico would default — changed direction
  despite M-1 growth
- Fed funds rate: above **14% early July → below 9% by early September**
- This was a **de facto abandonment** of money-supply-growth targeting
- Equity market finally bottomed in **August 1982** with public announcement
  of Mexican crisis and the Fed's pivot

### Structure of the Market in 1982

- NYSE market cap: **$1,143bn (36% of GDP)** vs. $64bn (23% of GDP) in 1949
- Public ownership via intermediaries: **4% of adults (1952) → 28% (1985)**
- Average holding period just over **two years** vs. five years 1949-1981
- Listed companies: 1,043 (1949) → 1,526 (1982); average market cap
  grew from $58m (1949) to **$639m (1982)**
- **Oil** remained #1 sector at every bear-market bottom (1932, 1949, 1982)
- Finance rose from **16th largest sector (1949) to 2nd largest (1982)**

**Sector total returns (1949-1982) — Best:**
- Business Equipment: **80.2x**; Health: 62.7x; Meals: 55.1x

**Worst:** Coal: 6.1x; Chemicals: 16.9x; Steel: 17.9x

**Sectors producing positive real total returns in 1968-82 bear:**
- Tobacco: **+420%** (also best in deflationary 1929-32 bear)
- Telecoms: +194%; Oil: +185%; Health: +180%; Coal: +180%
- Only **5 of 30** industrial sectors produced positive real returns
- S&P Composite up 82% nominally but CPI up **174%** — a real loss

**Bond market scale by 1982:**
- Total US fixed interest: $303bn (end 1952) → **$2,246bn (2Q 1982)**
- 30-year 2½% bond (constant maturity): declined from 101 (1946) to
  17 (1981) — an **83% loss** (Homer & Sylla)
- **Moody's Baa** corporate bond yield reached 17% by February 1982 —
  vs. peaks of 8.6% (1921) and 11.6% (1932)

### Valuations at the 1982 Bottom

- By August 1982, DJIA back to April 1964 levels; real terms: back to
  April 1928
- **q ratio** year-end 1982 = 0.38x; mid-year low probably ~**0.27x**
- **Cyclically adjusted PE**: 9.9x vs. 1881-1982 average of 15.8x
- Q ratio below geometric mean for **almost nine years** before the bottom
- Historic PE below **10x since March 1977** — market looked cheap for
  years before the bottom; took **14 years** to travel from peak valuation
  to trough — consistent with 1921 (~16 years) and 1949 (~13 years);
  1932 was the outlier at 3 years (required 89% DJIA decline)

| Bottom | q ratio (years) | CAPE (years) |
|--------|----------------|--------------|
| 1921   | 16             | 20           |
| 1932   | 3              | 3            |
| 1949   | 13             | 13           |
| 1982   | 14             | 17           |

**Key lesson**: barring a crash of 1929-32 magnitude, expect ~14 years
for equities to travel from extreme overvaluation to extreme undervaluation.

### Good News and the Bear (Summer 1982)

- 14 June: Business inventories cut at fastest real rate since WWII;
  consumer expectations improving; new car sales +5.9% YoY; 10% tax cut
- 16 June: Auto sales pace ~8.3M units; **Paul Volcker** says consumer
  spending "tend to accompany a levelling out of a recession"
- 17 June: Housing starts up 22% in May, above 1M-unit annual rate
  for first time in 10 months
- 22 June: Economy expanding at 0.6% annual rate — first growth since
  Q3 1981; **David Stockman** says "We are past the trough"
- 1 July: **Alan Greenspan** says recovery signs visible "in a matter of weeks"
- 2 August: Q2 price deflator 5% — half the 1981 level of 9.4%
- 6 August: **Blue Chip Economic Indicators** consensus: 3.3% real GNP growth
  in next 12 months — all 40+ forecasters expected at least some growth
- **Actual outcome**: Q2 1982 real GDP posted 2.2%; economy grew 3.2% in
  following 12 months — consensus forecast of 3.3% was almost exactly right

> *"Once again, as in 1921, 1932 and 1949, the bottom of a bear market is
> not marked by the absence of good news, but rather by an increasing supply
> of good news ignored by the market."*

### Auto Sector as Lead Indicator (1982)

- Monthly low for new car registrations: January 1982; by June, down
  only 4% YoY (vs. much steeper earlier declines)
- In 1982, car prices rose 1.9% but US prime lending rate fell from
  >20% to <16% — declining effective financing cost triggered demand recovery
- **New England recovery** preceded all four equity bottoms by just weeks —
  WSJ (19 July 1982) reported Northeast's perennially depressed states
  coming through "with flying colors" driven by tech/telecom boom

### Price Stability and the Bear (1982)

- **CRB index** fell >30% from November 1980 peak to October 1982 low
- Metals component (CRB Spot Metals) bottomed in **June 1982** —
  two months *before* the equity bottom in August
- **Copper** as leading indicator: bottomed August 1921, June 1949, July
  1932, and led the equity bottom in 1982 as well

> *"In all four of the great bear market bottoms analysed in this book,
> the end of the decline in commodity prices marks the end of the great
> bear market in equities."*

> *"On every occasion, the rebound in copper prices preceded or coincided
> with the rebound in the equity market."*

- CRB index rose **24%** in ten months after October 1982; DJIA rose
  >50% in under a year
- Monetary system change (gold standard → flexible rates) did NOT
  invalidate the commodity price signal

**Actionable framework**: look for (1) cheap valuations, (2) rising copper
prices, (3) low inventory positions, (4) evidence of actual (not forecast)
rising auto demand — when all present, "history suggests one is very close
to the bottom of the equity market."

### Liquidity and the Bear (1982)

- Non-borrowed reserves grew only **1.8%** in 21 months from July 1980
  to April 1982
- **May 1982 turning point**: seasonally-adjusted non-borrowed reserves
  grew 2.4% month-on-month; in next eight months expanded **10.6%**
- Signal hard to read in real time; NOW accounts distorted M-1 data
- **FOMC on 1 July** effectively abandoned money-supply targets (no
  announcement made)
- Volcker's 21 July Senate testimony was the first coded signal:

> *'Growth somewhat above the targeted ranges would be tolerated for a time
> in circumstances in which it appeared that precautionary liquidity
> motivations, during a period of economic turbulence, were leading to a
> stronger-than-anticipated demand for money.'*
> — Volcker, Senate Banking Committee, 21 July 1982

- Real catalyst for policy change: **Mexico's bankruptcy was imminent**;
  bond dealer **Lombard Wall** was in trouble; Volcker feared impact on
  US banking system of sovereign debt defaults
- Developing-country debt soared from **$100B (1973)** to ~$540B; expected
  to reach $640B — $327B owed to Western commercial banks
- Countries in trouble: Poland, Mexico, Argentina, East Germany, Brazil,
  Nigeria, Chile, Zaire, Yugoslavia, Bolivia, Venezuela, Peru, Tanzania,
  Sudan, Indonesia, Romania
- Long-term govt bond yields fell from ~14% to 10.5% (Aug-Dec 1982)

**Discount rate cuts as reliable signal:**
- May 1921 first cut → August 1921 bottom (20% intervening loss)
- March 1949 credit controls eased → July 1949 bottom (~10% loss)
- October 1981 first discount rate cut → August 1982 bottom (<10% loss)
- Exception: November 1929 first cut led to catastrophic losses

> **The rule**: Reductions in Fed interest rates in Fed-induced recessions
> (1921, 1949, 1982) are a good time to buy cheap equities. 1929 was NOT
> a Fed-induced recession — it was the consequence of German reparations,
> Allied debt repayments, and inept gold-standard reconstruction.

**Practical rule**: await a further **10% market decline** after the first
discount rate cut before buying — history shows minimal further downside
and very significant upside thereafter.

### The Bulls and the Bears (1982 Sentiment)

- 14 June: Eric Miller (DLJ) — "most stocks have seen their lows"
- 18 June: **James Dines**, the "ultimate gold bug," reversed 20 years of
  bullion advocacy, told subscribers to sell gold — "end of the gold bug era"
- 2 July: Howard Stein (Dreyfus Fund, $1.6B) — trimming utilities, moving
  cash back into equities, sees "50% gain" potential
- 14 July: Merrill Lynch, Goldman Sachs, Bear Stearns, E.F. Hutton all had
  lengthy buy lists — yet their top strategists all thought investors would
  be **better off in bonds**
- 30 July: Leon Cooperman (Goldman Sachs) — "entry into a classical bull
  market must be validated by further and sustained decline in interest rates"
- **12 August: DJIA bottoms**
- 13 August: Minshall (Capital Advisors Tulsa) — "a selling climax will be
  required to end the bear market" [day AFTER the bottom]
- 18 August: **Henry Kaufman** (Salomon Brothers) reverses long-standing
  pessimism — expects "substantial drops in interest rates," long-term
  Treasuries could fall to 9% from 12.5%
- 18 August: One-day DJIA **record rally** — largely attributed to Kaufman's
  reversal; Goldman Sachs tells clients to raise equity allocation to
  55% from 35%
- 19 August: Heavy institutional buying → NYSE one-day **volume record**;
  small investors mainly on the sidelines, sceptical
- 18 August: Alfred Goldman (A.G. Edwards) — "classic ingredient of a real
  bottom is capitulation by institutions, high disgust… they've been
  complacent, confident. It's unusual to get a bull market with so little pain"
- 29 September: Cooperman (Goldman) — "early stages of a bull market; average
  postwar bull market lasts 30 months, trough-to-peak advance of 66%"

**No Capitulation — A Cross-Bottom Pattern:**
- The 1982 bottom, like 1921, 1932, and 1949, **lacked a final high-volume
  sell-off**
- Capitulation may be more a feature of **trading-range markets** than true
  secular bottoms

> *"The signature of a true bear market bottom when equities are cheap:
> market trading sideways or drifting downward on low volume, subdued
> reaction to both good and bad news, then initial recovery on either
> limited or (as in 1982) large volume — confirmed by higher volume at
> higher price levels thereafter."*

- 1982 unique: initial rebound on **very high volumes** (opposite to
  prior episodes where rebound came on low volume)
- Peak short positions: 103.6M shares (14 May) declined to 96.4M by
  mid-August — then when market rallied, **short positions spiked to NEW
  RECORD of 120.5M on 15 September** — confirming the move was a permanent
  reversal

### Bonds and the Bear (1982)

- **Bond bear market in its fifth decade ended October 1981**
- Long-term Treasury yield: 2.03% (April 1946) → **15.1%** (first week
  of October 1981) — a 35-year bear market in bonds
- Real yield on govt bonds rose from 4.4% (September 1981) to **7.4%**
  (July 1982) — bond investors still sceptical despite nominal yield decline
- Government bond market technically bottomed September 1981, but major
  rally did not begin until **July 1982** — when Fed abandoned money supply
  growth targeting
- **Corporate bond sequence confirmed (1982)**:
  - Long-term US Treasury bottomed first week of October 1981
  - **Moody's Baa** yield peaked **mid-February 1982** (17.3%)
  - Baa yield premium over Treasuries peaked November 1982 — *after*
    major bull market in both corporate and government bonds had begun
- Pattern confirmed: **government bonds → corporate bonds → equities**
  (same as 1921 and 1932; 1949 distorted by Fed yield-cap)
- 1982 fiscal deficit: 3.9% of GDP (highest since WWII); reached 5.9%
  by 1985 (surpassing Roosevelt's peacetime peaks) — yet rates fell and
  economy recovered. Senate projected $103.9B deficit for FY1983; actual
  deficit was **$208 billion** — parallel to 1932: fiscal deterioration
  didn't prevent the bull market

> "Suffice to say, the indicators that signalled the bottom of the bear
> market in 1921, 1932 and 1949 worked again in 1982. This is particularly
> encouraging for investors seeking patterns." — Napier

## Conclusions: Strategic

> *Exit, pursued by a bear.*
> — William Shakespeare, *The Winter's Tale*

- Napier calls the indicator set **"Einstein's questions"** — the right
  questions to ask, requiring better-than-average answers to profit

### Valuation at Bear-Market Bottoms

- **q ratio** fell below **0.3x** at all four bear-market bottoms — the most
  reliable single valuation indicator
- **Cyclically adjusted PE** range at bottoms: 4.7x (1932) to 11.7x (1949);
  wide enough to be less useful as a precise signal; inflation-adjusted
  earnings still gives wide range: 5.2x to 9.1x
- **Equities become cheap slowly** — average nine years from peak q to trough
  (14 years excluding the 1929-32 outlier)
- US equity market reached highest-ever valuations in **March 2000**;
  history suggests slow multi-year grind to undervaluation follows
- Real GDP expanded ~52% and nominal GDP ~285% on average across the three
  long bear markets (ex-1929-32) — bear markets occur during expansion,
  not depression (except 1929-32)
- Inflation-adjusted earnings growth ranged from **-67% to +28%** across
  the four bottoms; nominal earnings -67% to +119% — enormous variance

### Price Disturbance as Catalyst

- A **material disturbance to the general price level** is required to push
  equities to cheap levels in all four cases:
  - 1921, 1949, 1982: high inflation followed by deflation (commodity-only
    in 1982)
  - 1932: no initial inflation, but severe deflation
- Price disturbance creates uncertainty about future earnings and the
  opportunity cost of holding bonds — compresses equity valuations
- Without such a disturbance, a bear market may NOT produce "buy of a
  generation" valuations

### Commodity Prices as Leading Signal

- All four bear-market bottoms occurred during **economic recession**
- **Return of price stability** (especially commodity prices) signals the bottom
- **Copper** specifically has been the most accurate commodity signal
- To assess whether price stability is sustainable: look for low inventories,
  rising demand at lower prices, producers selling below cost

### Government Bond Behavior

- Government bond sell-off accompanies at least part of every bear market
- Exception nuance: 1929-32 saw bonds *rally* September 1929 to June 1931
  before the sell-off began (lasting until January 1932)
- Even in the two deflationary bears (1921, 1932) there was some government
  bond sell-off

## Conclusions: Tactical

### Lead Times from Bond Bottom to Equity Bottom

| Year | Govt Bond Lead | DJIA Decline During Lag |
|------|----------------|------------------------|
| 1921 | 14 months      | 23%                    |
| 1932 | 7 months       | 46%                    |
| 1949 | 9 months       | 14%                    |
| 1982 | 11 months      | 6%                     |

- **A recovery in government bond prices precedes equity recovery** in all cases
- Corporate bond recovery leads equities by: 2 months (1921), 1 month (1932),
  5 months (1982); 15-17 months (1949, distorted by post-war controls)

### Fed Rate Cuts

- In the three long bear markets (ex-1929-32), **Fed rate cuts preceded
  equity bottoms** by ~3 months (1921, 1949) and 11 months (1982)
- DJIA decline during each lag was less than 20% in all three cases
- 1929-32 exception: Fed cut November 1929 while bear was still in infancy

### Additional Tactical Signals

- **Auto sector recovery** precedes equity market recovery
- **Bear-market bottoms characterised by good economic news being ignored** —
  bulls bang the drum but prices fall anyway
- **Worsening fiscal position** will be blamed for preventing recovery;
  these warnings will be wrong
- **Corporate earnings declines continue well past the equity bottom** —
  do not wait for earnings recovery to buy; earnings nadir came roughly
  **4-7 months AFTER** equity bottoms (average ~6 months)
- Bottom preceded by **market declining on high volume, rising on low
  volume**; final slump on very low volume; confirmation = rising volume
  at higher prices after first rebound
- **Short interest reaches peak at the bottom** and rises further in the
  first weeks of the new bull market
- **Dow Theory** works to signal a buy for equities (correct at all 4 bottoms)

> "Just as the possession of fur does not, of itself, permit the
> identification of an animal as a bear, the possession of any one of the
> features above should not be considered as constituting positive
> identification of its financial equivalent. Our list is the financial
> equivalent of Einstein's questions." — Napier

## Conclusions: Then and Now (2005 Assessment)

- Written in 2005; Napier applies the framework to the bear market begun 2000
- **q ratio** at end of 1999: all-time high of **2.9x** (geometric mean);
  cyclically adjusted PE was **170% above** its 1881-2005 average
- **From June 2005 levels**: cyclically adjusted PE would need to fall 40%
  to reach long-term average; 60-84% to reach prior bear-bottom levels
- **q ratio** in June 2005 was still **44% above fair value**; to match
  prior bear bottoms, would need to fall another 67% from there
- No general price disturbance had yet occurred (as of 2005)
- No meaningful bond market sell-off; no recession; no Fed rate cuts
- **Napier's conclusion**: DJIA likely to decline **at least 60%, possibly
  80%+** before the bear ends
- Predicted bear market end: "sometime after 2009, though probably nearer
  to 2014" — written in 2005, remarkably prescient about the GFC arc

> "If, as Milton Friedman asserts, inflation 'is everywhere and at all times
> a monetary phenomenon', the next general price disturbance is likely to be
> inflationary, given the current institutional framework." — Napier
