## Pages 15-20: Chapter 1 — In the Beginning

- Opening quote: "If one advances in the direction of his dreams, one will meet with success unexpected in common hours." — Henry David Thoreau
- John Nordstrom, founder of the department store, said: "I was not certain what I wanted to do. I started looking around for some small business to get into. Mr. Wallin the shoemaker suggested that we join a partnership and open a shoe store."
- Key insight: The question is not whether entrepreneurship is right for you, but whether YOU are right for entrepreneurship
- Entrepreneurship is described as: nerve-wracking, liberating, difficult, challenging, time-consuming, overwhelming, fun, joyous, productive, uncertain — all in the same day
- Must be able to handle: stress, freedom, lack of structure, uncertainty, and the opportunity that comes with starting your own business
- "If you are not temperamentally cut out for it, it will be a tough road. There is no shame in this."

### Entrepreneurship IQ Quiz (20 questions, scored)
- Tests key traits: self-starter ability, risk tolerance, leadership, handling irregular income, willingness to fire people, work 60+ hours/week, self-confidence, living with uncertainty, persistence, creativity, competitiveness, willpower/self-discipline, individualism, ability to live without structure, business skills, flexibility, industry experience, multi-tasking across business functions, hustle, handling pressure
- **Scoring**: 80-100 = have both temperament and skills to become an entrepreneur; 60-79 = not a natural entrepreneur but may become one over time; Below 60 = should consider something besides self-employment
- Each question has 3 options worth 5, 3, or 1 point(s)
- Quiz reveals areas of strength and weakness — important to understand where you need improvement

## Pages 21-24: Chapter 1 (cont.) — Risk Tolerance & The Next Step

- **Risk Tolerance** is the single most important trait for entrepreneurs — "of all the necessary traits, the one that you must have in abundance is a tolerance for risk, because starting your own small business is a risk"
- Great small businesses work to REDUCE their risks as much as possible — they cover every angle so that the risks they do take are prudent, calculated ones
- "Take a prudent, calculated, intelligent risk with a high likelihood of payoff"
- Traits of the prototypical successful entrepreneur: driven, hard-working, creative, energetic, resourceful, confident, flexible

### Education
- Most people go into business because they love something — the baker wants a bakery, the chiropractor wants a practice
- The problem: they know their specialty but not the BUSINESS side — marketing, advertising, sales, income taxes, hiring/firing
- As a small business owner, you will be president, accountant, marketing wizard, and salesperson all in one
- Recommended reading: *Home Business Magazine*, *Entrepreneur*, *Inc.*
- Resources: SBA.gov, NASE.org, NFIB.com, Microsoft.com/smallbusiness, MrAllBiz.com

### Experience (two forms)
1. **Work in a similar business first** — if you want to open an antiques store, work at one first. Your entrepreneurial dream can wait six months. "Working in a business like the one you want to create will teach you things that no books can impart"
2. **Talk to existing owners** — go to a NEARBY TOWN (not your own, where they'd see you as competition), find similar businesses, take owners to lunch. Ask: What do they like most/least? How much did it cost to start? How much can you expect to make? Where do they advertise? What would they do differently?
- This "informal MBA" reduces risk by giving thorough understanding of what you're getting into

## Pages 25-33: Chapter 2 — Choosing the Right Business

- Opening quote from John D. Rockefeller III: "The road to happiness lies in two simple principles: Find what it is that interests you and that you can do well, and when you find it, put your whole soul into it"
- Two types of entrepreneurs: (1) in love with a specific business idea, (2) in love with the idea of being their own boss

### "If You Do What You Love, Will the Money Really Follow?"
- Doing what you love is the FIRST prerequisite but just a first step — not sufficient alone
- Richard Branson started Virgin Music because he loved it, Bill Gates started Microsoft because he loved computers
- Barbara Winter, *Making a Living Without a Job* (1993): "passion leads to purpose"
- Your first assignment: decide which passions you love enough to build a business around
- Example: love of plants/gardening → nursery, flower shop, lawn-care, organic vegetables, farm, winery
- Tip: "Open the Yellow Pages and look under your category" to see what businesses others have created

### A Word of Caution
- Good ideas are not hard to come by, but they ARE hard to implement
- Thomas Edison: "1 percent inspiration and 99 percent perspiration"
- "Too many new entrepreneurs fall in love with their idea and become convinced that it is the greatest thing since sliced bread. You Do not make this mistake."
- Must be objective — ask around, get feedback, crunch numbers
- Being realistic about market demand is critical — if no one will pay for your Flemish architecture consulting, you don't have a business
- Must be able to sell at a price high enough to profit but low enough that people will buy
- Before jumping in, research: How much does this type of business make? How quickly?

### First Mover's Advantage
- Amazon.com, Yahoo!, Post-its, Pampers were all first — "Being first gives you something called 'the first mover's advantage'"
- Being first = chance to shape the marketplace, enormous profit potential
- BUT: requires a lot of money, drive, risk tolerance, and capital
- For new entrepreneurs: "Now is probably the time to learn, not lead" — pick ideas that others have successfully implemented
- Advantage of following: plenty of books, resources (SCORE — Service Corps of Retired Executives), trade magazines, associations available

### In Love with Entrepreneurship (Jeff Bezos example)
- Bezos didn't start Amazon because he loved books — he saw Internet usage growing 2,300% per year in early 1990s
- He analyzed the marketplace and concluded book sales were the best way to tap the Internet's commercial power
- Bezos is the "prototypical second category entrepreneur"

### Case Study: Roy and Bertrand Sosa (NetSpend)
- Emigrated from Mexico in 1986, couldn't get checking accounts or credit cards as new immigrants
- Saw huge untapped market: financial services for recent immigrants with poor credit histories
- Used prepaid phone cards as model, created prepaid debit cards
- Got SBA's Small Business Investment Companies (SBIC) funding — SBICs guarantee VC investments, inducing VCs to invest in riskier projects
- In five years, went from 0 to 400,000 accounts
- Their three rules: (1) Figure what pain or need the customer has that you can solve, (2) Create strong win-win partnerships, (3) Start with baby steps but think big

### 6-Step Process for Choosing a Business
1. **Research** — analyze marketplace and opportunities, look at similar businesses (How hard? How much money? How much to start?)
2. **Product or Service?** — Service businesses are less expensive to start (no inventory). Product businesses mark up prices and profit on the difference. Choose based on temperament, skills, goals
3. **Analyze Your Skills and Experience** — tap your existing skills even if changing industries; don't choose a business that doesn't use your strengths
4. **Consider Your Options** — start from scratch, buy existing, franchise, home-based. Learn pros/cons of each
5. **Narrow Your Choices** — go out and talk to people who own and run the businesses you're considering
6. **Start Your Engines** — do homework, find a niche that fills a market need

### Business Selection Do's and Don'ts
- DO be patient — selection takes time and knowledge of industry/marketplace/competition
- DO look for opportunity — Wayne Gretzky: "Go to where the puck is going, not where it is"
- DON'T pick something too challenging — you'll be challenged enough
- DON'T pick a business that can't compete — superstores like Costco, Sam's Club, Target, Wal-Mart will beat you on price. Find a niche where you have an advantage

## Pages 35-40: Chapter 3 — Buying an Existing Business

- Opening quote from Winston Churchill: "Some regard private enterprise as if it were a predatory tiger to be shot. Others look upon it as a cow that they can milk. Only a handful see it for what it really is — the strong horse that pulls the whole cart."

### Advantages of Buying Existing
1. Not starting from scratch — business already exists
2. Goodwill already established — favorable community reputation
3. Quicker — everything already in place to hit the ground running
4. Main benefit: REDUCES RISK — you can look at the books, see past performance, project future returns
- "The great entrepreneurs are not gamblers; indeed, they seek to reduce the risk as much as possible"

### Finding Businesses for Sale (4 sources)
1. **Classifieds** — local paper "Business Opportunities" section
2. **Trade magazines** — almost every industry has one with businesses-for-sale sections
3. **Online** — Businessesforsale.com, Bizquest.com, Bizbuysell.com, Mergernetwork.com, Businessbroker.net
4. **Business brokers** — not cheap but have access to unlisted businesses, serve as sounding board

### Evaluating a Business Broker
- Average age of business brokers is 55 — you need experience
- Check if certified by International Business Brokers Association (IBBA) — Certified Business Intermediary (CBI)
- Will broker value the business for you? Negotiate? A good broker should be a financial advisor

### How Much Can You Expect to Make?
- Benchmark: if you can earn ~10% passively in a mutual fund, an active business investment should cover: operating expenses + your salary + loan payments + annual return on capital
- Key due diligence questions:
  - **Why is business being sold?** Owner retiring = good sign. Owner dumping problems = bad sign. Owner can't legally lie (fraudulent = void contract). Get referrals, trade references, talk to neighboring businesses
  - **Competition** — who, where, how does current owner deal with them, what would be your competitive advantages?
  - **Nontransferable intangibles** — some businesses succeed because of the owner's contacts, skills, or relationships. Can it run as well without them?
  - **Pending changes** — is the neighborhood stable? Government or competitor plans for the area?
  - **What needs to be changed** — facilities, plumbing, electricity? Make offers contingent on inspection (like buying a house)
  - **Profitability** — dig into books with your accountant, get audited financials going back at least two years

### Business Valuation 101 — Three Key Questions
1. **What does the business own?** — Assets: trucks, equipment, contracts, IP, goodwill. Sellers overvalue goodwill, buyers undervalue it. Realistically analyze the value of the business name in the community
2. **How much does the business earn?** — A business making $100K/year profit is worth far more than one netting $35K

## Pages 40-42: Chapter 3 (cont.) — Business Valuation & Closing

### Three Business Valuation Methods
1. **Price Building** — list every asset and assign a dollar value. Example: Bill's Machinery Rentals: Real estate $125K + Equipment $40K + Inventory $25K + Goodwill $10K = $200K total
2. **Return on Investment (ROI)** — look at business profit per year as percentage of purchase price. Example: $100K net profit / $200K sale price = 50% ROI. Since few investments offer 50% ROI, a higher asking price is justified
3. **Multiplier** — multiply earnings by an industry-specific factor. A factor of 3 on $100K earnings = $300K asking price. The battle is agreeing on the right factor
- Valuation resources: bizcomps.com, bvmarketdata.com
- Hiring a business broker makes sense given this complexity

### Getting Ready to Close
- Tour the actual premises — peek into nooks and crannies
- Know both positives and negatives, especially get problem questions answered
- "No business will be perfect; your job is to decide whether the benefits outweigh the burdens"
- Final analysis must include lawyer and accountant, even if you have a broker
- Speak with customers and suppliers where possible

### Smart Contract Provisions
- **Link sales price to customer retention** — especially for service businesses where clients may be loyal to the seller, not the business. Tie purchase price to number of customers who stay
- **Have old owner stay for a while** — helps with transition and customer retention. Pay consulting fee — usually worth it

## Pages 43-51: Chapter 4 — Understanding Your Market

- Laura Ashley quote: "We don't want to push our ideas onto customers; we simply want to make what they want."
- "This may be the most important chapter in the whole book" — everything hinges on accurate understanding of your market
- Market research is critical BEFORE quitting your job — "A hunch simply will not do. You need hard facts."
- "The great entrepreneurs endeavor to reduce their risk as much as possible"

### Is Your Idea Feasible?
- Problem: entrepreneurs fall in love with their idea and skip feasibility analysis
- Two possible outcomes of research: discover there IS a need, or there isn't. If not, all is not lost — the idea may just need tweaking
- **Case study: Dave** — computer programmer wanted a cafe, researched one he liked, concluded the lease was too short and drive-by traffic insufficient. Started over, found a better cafe, bought it, and made a good living. Research saved him from a bad investment

### The Three Cs
1. **Your Company** — products/services, size, retail/wholesale, location, positioning
2. **Your Customers** — B2B or B2C? Demographics (age, income, gender, blue/white collar)? What do they need? Why buy from YOU? How can you reach them? What do they read/watch?
3. **Your Competitors** — strengths, weaknesses, what they're doing right/wrong. Can you capitalize on their weaknesses? Can you undersell them?

### Princeton Creative Research Checklist for Evaluating Business Ideas
- Is there a real need for it?
- Have you pinpointed exact problems the idea solves?
- Is it original, new concept, or new combination?
- Are projected returns adequate? Risk factors acceptable?
- Long-range benefits anticipated?
- Checked for faults or limitations?
- How simple or complex is execution?
- Could you offer alternative variations?
- Natural sales appeal? Market ready? Timing factor?
- Competition doing anything in this area?
- User resistance or difficulties considered?
- Does the idea fill a REAL need or must it be created through promotion?
- How soon could it be operational?

### Where to Find Market Research
- **Trade Associations** — every industry has one; ask for survey data, research reports, startup resource kits
- **Trade Shows** — meet hundreds of like-minded people already doing what you want; benefits include saving time, identifying customers, experienced feedback, cost estimates, potential pitfalls, reality check on optimistic plans
- **Trade Magazines** — industry trends, mistakes to avoid, potential costs
- **Web Sites**: Entrepreneur.com/FormNet (free forms), census.gov (demographics), USChamber.com, TSNN.com (trade show database), SBA.gov/sbdc (Small Business Development Centers), SCORE.org, inside.com (*American Demographics*), hoovers.com (business/industry data), marketresearch.com (50,000+ articles from 350+ publishers)

### Interviews and Experiential Research
1. **Interview potential customers** — find people who'd buy your product/service. Ask what they like/dislike about current vendor, what would cause them to switch (price, location, service). Tip: park outside a competitor and ask people for 5 minutes
2. **Study the competition** — become their customer, shop their store, use their service. Analyze strengths, weaknesses, profit potential

### Other Research Methods
- **Hire MBA students** — Small Business Institute program at 250 MBA programs nationwide assigns grad students to market research projects for nominal fees or free. Contact Small Business Advancement National Center at (501) 450-5300
- **Libraries** — librarians are research experts; *The Internet-Plus Directory of Express Library Services* lists 500 libraries with low-cost research services
- **Online Focus Groups** — find a listserv discussion group for your industry, subscribe, ask questions. Listserv list at tile.net/lists/
- **Telemarketing/Phone Research** — costs about 1/3 less than personal interviews. Tips: tell interviewee importance + short duration (5-10 min), avoid pauses, keep questions short and interesting, consistent answer options. 250+ interviews = good sample
- **Direct Mail** — inexpensive with bulk mail but response rates usually <5%. Improve response: nice cover letter explaining purpose, keep questionnaire to 2 pages, address to a person (not Occupant), hand-address letters, include self-addressed stamped envelope

## Pages 53-57: Chapter 5 — Calculating Your Startup Costs

- Bruce Barton quote: "Nothing splendid has ever been achieved except by those who dared believe that something inside them was superior to circumstance."
- Startup costs = educated assumptions based on previous research

### 4-Step Startup Cost Calculation

**Step 1: Startup Expenses** (12 categories)
1. Legal structure creation (sole proprietorship, partnership, LLC, corporation)
2. Accountant
3. Build-out, decorating, remodeling
4. Licenses and permits
5. Stationery and logos
6. Marketing and sales materials
7. First month's rent and security deposit
8. Insurance
9. Telephone and utility deposits
10. Signs
11. Internet and web site
12. Other

**Step 2: Purchasing Assets** (7 categories)
1. Real estate
2. Furniture and fixtures
3. Equipment and machinery
4. Trucks and autos
5. Inventory
6. Supplies
7. Other

**Step 3: Ongoing Monthly Expenses** (16 categories)
- Rent, utilities, payroll, owner's draw, supplies, insurance, transportation, shipping, legal/accounting, advertising/marketing, inventory, production/distribution, taxes, debt repayment, working capital, other
- **Key formula**: Multiply monthly total by 6 → this is how much you need to run the business for 6 months
- Add Steps 1 + 2 + (Step 3 x 6) = total needed to open doors and stay in business 6 months
- **"Ideally, having six months' working capital in the bank before you start is a minimum"**
- Be conservative — "Cash is the lifeblood of any business, but especially a new business"
- "Don't blow it. Don't spend too much. Buy used. Buy off eBay. Hoard your precious, precious capital."

### Example: Perry's Pizza Parlor
- Step 1 (startup expenses): $22,000
- Step 2 (assets): $15,000
- Step 3 (monthly expenses): $10,000
- Monthly x 6 = $60,000
- **Total needed: $97,000** (ideally ~$100K in the bank)

**Step 4: Calculate Monthly Sales**
- Use the monthly expense figure as break-even threshold
- Perry's Pizza must gross at least $10,000/month ($333/day) to break even
- At $30/table average, must serve at least 11 tables per day to break even
- "It is far more difficult and expensive to create a new customer than to keep an existing one"
- Reasonable to assume at least 6 months before making consistent profit

## Pages 60-67: Chapter 6 — Write a Winning Business Plan

### Why You Need a Business Plan
- Business plan = flight plan/road map for your business
- Forces you to think through projected costs, sales, competition, risk vs. reward
- Benefits: avoids pie-in-the-sky projections, allows investors to analyze the business, identifies market and competition, deepens your own understanding
- "Smart businesses create, use, and revise business plans as necessary"
- Two major downsides of NOT having one: (1) your enterprise is a gamble — may succeed or fail, (2) you will never attract an investor without one
- Whether it's a bank, the SBA, an angel investor, Uncle Larry, or a VC firm — a business plan is a prerequisite for funding

### Business Plan Elements (in order)
1. **Title Page** — business name, logo, owner's name, address, phone, email, website
2. **Executive Summary** — "probably the single most important part of your business plan." It's what investors key in on first. Crisp 3-4 page introduction covering: what the business is, target market, what's different, why good timing, why it's a unique opportunity, amount of money being sought. **Tip: write it LAST**, after thinking through the entire plan
3. **Table of Contents**
4. **Business Description** — products/services, market niche, what makes you different. Define target market accurately. Distinguish between *feasible market* (total potential customers) and *market share* (your realistic portion). Include legal form (sole proprietorship, partnership, LLC, corporation). "Write it in your voice with your passion. Experts can smell a prepackaged, ghost-written business plan a mile away."
5. **Management** — banks and lenders take the management team's background and experience very seriously. List team members, backgrounds, responsibilities. If solo, document your own skills and abilities
6. **Industry Description** — insert all background research from trade associations, magazines, web sites, interviews. Discuss macroeconomic trends and relevant economic indicators. Software like Palo Alto Business Plan Pro can help, but use your own language
7. **Competition** — how long in business, location, average annual sales. How will you beat them (better location, convenience, prices, hours, quality, service)? Analyze what they do right/wrong, unmet customer needs, how you'll lure customers away
8. **Marketing Strategy** — positioning (upscale, budget, etc.), pricing strategy, promotion plans, advertising/marketing proposals, existing contacts or contracts
9. **Sales Forecast** — AVOID unrealistic numbers. Sophisticated investors see through phony numbers. Inflated numbers can also lead to unrealistic expectations and running out of money. Be conservative. Include: monthly forecast for year 1 (dollars + units), annual forecast for years 2-4, assumptions underlying the forecast. Base on average monthly sales of similar-sized businesses in similar markets
10. **Financial Analysis** — most difficult part. Must include:
    - **Income and Expense Statement** — projection of income/expenses for year 1 and following 2 years, plus monthly cash flow forecast
    - **Profit and Loss Statement (P&L)** — summary of projected transactions over time. Example (Cafe Coffee): Projected income $187,900, Total expenses $143,650, **Net profit $44,250**. Expense breakdown: COGS $76,300, Labor $33,700, Bank fees $250, Equipment $4,900, Insurance $2,800, Marketing $6,200, Postage $1,200, Phone $2,400, Printing $1,900, Supplies $7,200, Taxes $6,800
    - **Balance Sheet** — snapshot of assets and liabilities at a given date
    - **Cash Flow Statement** — when and where cash comes from and goes. Forces realistic bottom-line analysis of whether you can handle your debts
    - Include analysis of loan proceeds sought, loan amount and term, your personal financial situation and personal contribution
11. **Exit Strategy** — proposed exit (sale of business or eventual retirement)
12. **Appendix** — substantiation documents, references, bank name, lawyer/accountant names, personal net worth statement, letters of intent, insurance coverage details

- "Writing is re-writing. Your business plan is no different, so write it and re-write it."
- "Either you will get funded, or, at a minimum, you will have learned a great deal about how to make your business fly. Either way, you win."

## Pages 69-77: Chapter 7 — Structuring Your Business

- Shakespeare quote: "The first thing we do, let's kill all the lawyers."
- Four options: sole proprietorship, partnership, LLC, or corporation
- Decision should be made with your lawyer and accountant

### Sole Proprietorship
- Cheapest and easiest — ~$100 to start: name the business, get a license, publish fictitious business name statement, open checking account
- SBA stat: roughly 17 million of the 23 million small businesses in the US are sole proprietorships
- **Major problem**: legally, you and the business are one and the same. You are personally on the hook for everything
- Example: if your pizza delivery boy kills a pedestrian while drunk, your insurance won't cover it, and YOUR personal assets (cars, home, retirement) can be tapped for damages
- "Starting a business as a sole proprietor is probably not a good idea, legally speaking"
- Also means working alone — no partners to bounce ideas off

### General Partnerships
- Even MORE precarious than sole proprietorship — not only are partners individually liable, but EITHER partner can get the whole partnership into debt
- "Best friends who become partners do not always stay best friends"
- Benefits: someone to share ideas, workload, and financial commitments
- Must get a partnership agreement drafted by attorney — spells out contributions, responsibilities, and what happens if partnership ends
- Tip: "Start out working on a project or two together first. See if your styles are complementary"

### Limited Partnerships
- One general partner (runs the show, unlimited liability) + limited partners (passive investors, limited liability)
- Limited partner's liability = limited to their financial contribution
- No income tax at entity level — income/losses flow through to individual tax returns
- Popular structure for real estate and stock investment groups

### Corporations and LLCs
- Main reason to incorporate: legally shield personal assets from business debts
- Creditors limited to assets of the corporation only, may not collect directly from shareholders

### Pros and Cons of Incorporating
**Pros**: corporate shield from personal liability; perpetual existence (unlike sole proprietorships/partnerships which end on death/retirement); owners can share profit without staying to run the business; "Inc." behind name adds credibility; tax advantages including pension and profit-sharing options
**Cons**: not inexpensive to create; shareholders have little say in day-to-day operations; greater government regulation and scrutiny; complex tax code requires yearly lawyer/accountant assistance

### S vs. C Corporations
- **C Corp**: taxed TWICE — once on profits, again when distributed to shareholders (double taxation). Large, publicly traded businesses. Advantage: freely sellable shares, good for going-public exit strategy. C corps can deduct 100% of health insurance costs
- **S Corp**: no corporate tax — profits/losses flow through to shareholders' personal returns, taxed only once. Max 75 shareholders, no preferred stock allowed. Sole owner of S corp doesn't pay FICA/Medicare/self-employment tax (~15%) on first $75,000 earned
- **Professional Corporation**: for licensed professionals only (lawyers, doctors, dentists, accountants, psychologists). Only that professional can be sole shareholder. Does NOT shield from malpractice

### Limited Liability Companies (LLCs)
- Hybrid combining best of corporations (liability protection) and sole proprietorships/partnerships (flow-through taxation, informality)
- "The LLC has become the business form of choice for many a small business"
- LLC owners called "members" — can use capital or property to buy into the LLC
- Can distribute profits unequally if desired
- Two types: "member managed" (all owners run the business) and "manager managed" (owners hire managers, members vote on company issues)
- State-specific laws; file "articles of organization" or "certificate of formation" — roughly $75 in most states
- After filing, draft an "operating agreement" detailing contributions, ownership percentages, management structure, distribution handling
- Easy to create, inexpensive, can be formed with only one member (unlike corporations requiring officers + board)
- Recommended books: *Legal Guide to Starting and Running a Small Business* by Fred Steingold; *Your Limited Liability Company: An Operating Manual* by Anthony Mancuso (Nolo Press)

## Pages 80-90: Chapter 8 — The Money Hunt (cont.)

### The Usual Suspects (Self-Funding)
- Vast majority of small businesses start using funds from the entrepreneur themselves
- Even outside investors want to see you sharing financial risk — "risking some money, then, is part of the job description"
- Sources: savings, inheritance advances, IRAs or 401(k) (many owners cash these out despite penalties), stock portfolio
- W.H. Murray quote: "The moment one definitely commits oneself, the Providence moves too... Whatever you can, or dream you can, begin it. Boldness has genius, power and magic in it."

### Home Equity — Avoid If Possible
- Home equity loans are secured by your house — if business fails, you could lose your home
- "Smart entrepreneurs do their best to reduce their risk as much as possible. Risking your home may be too much to ask."

### Credit Cards
- According to one study, almost half of all businesses use credit cards to help with startup
- **The Credit Card Trap**: $7,000 at 17% interest, paying 2% minimum = more than 40 YEARS to pay off and nearly $14,000 total paid (double the original)
- Credit card debt is "one of the most common debt issues that small businesspeople face, and it can be a major factor leading to small business failure"

### Credit Card Smarts
- **Pay more than minimum** — keep paying the original minimum ($142 in the example) even as the required minimum decreases; cuts payoff from 40 years to ~5 years
- **Balance transfer dance** — transfer high-interest balances to cards with lower rates (~4.9% introductory/teaser rates)
- **Grace period trick**: time purchases so you get nearly 2 months interest-free (e.g., charge on the 2nd of the month when payment is due on the 25th)
- "Credit cards, while useful and convenient, can kill even the best small businesses. Use them if you must to fund your startup, but do so with a plan to pay them off as soon as possible."

### Friends and Family Plan
- Next most common funding method after self-funding
- Business plan is essential to show potential investors the plan makes sense
- Advantage: friends/family tend to lend at very low or zero interest
- **Case Study: Trivial Pursuit** — Scott Haney and Chris Abbot were two unemployed journalists. They drafted a business plan, then pestered 32 friends, relatives, and former colleagues into investing, raising about $60,000 for their new board game
- Warning: "if the business does not fly, owing money to friends and relatives for a failed business venture is not a pleasant experience"

### Banks and Credit Unions
- Loaning to an untried brand new startup is the MOST RISKY investment a bank can make
- New businesses have no track record or collateral beyond personal guarantees
- Problem with personal guarantee: it negates the purpose of incorporating to limit personal liability
- SBA to the rescue: the government guarantees certain loans to small businesses, reducing risk for lenders

### SBA Loan Programs
- **History**: Reconstruction Finance Corporation (RFC, Hoover era) → expanded under FDR → Smaller War Plants Corporation (SWPC, 1942) → Commerce Department → SBA created 1952 under Eisenhower
- **7(a) Loan Guaranty** — the "granddaddy" bread-and-butter program. Flexible: startup, working capital, equipment, furniture, real estate. Loan terms 10-25 years
- **7(m) Microloan Program** — up to $35,000 for working capital, furniture, supplies, inventory, fixtures, equipment (NOT real estate). Made through nonprofit organizations, not traditional lenders
- **504 Certified Development Company (CDC) Loan** — long-term, fixed-rate loans for real estate/machinery, modernization, expansion. Typical structure: small business contributes 10%, private lender ~50%, CDC covers 40%. CDC share can go up to $1.3 million
- **Loan Prequalification Program** — SBA reviews loan packages up to $250,000 BEFORE you take them to a lender, offers suggestions and corrections to strengthen your application

### Angel Investors
- Individuals who have made a lot of money and want to invest in startups, usually in their own industry
- Found through networking: lawyers, accountants, stockbrokers, real estate agents, bankers, customers, sales reps, industry colleagues
- Online resources: vfinance.com, capital-connection.com

### Three Steps to Landing an Angel
1. **Prepare Your Elevator Pitch** — 30-second proposal that is "intriguing, intelligent, short, and powerful"
2. **Do Your Homework** — come prepared with flawless business plan AND background knowledge about the investor (how they made money, what else they've funded). "Pique their interest, show potential, and create rapport"
3. **Follow Up** — get references from the angel for other deals, call references, make sure the angel is legitimate and easy to work with
- "Many angels like being mentors and may be willing to use their own contacts to help you find additional funding or other assistance"

### Venture Capital
- VC firms = groups of people (banking firms, professional investors, etc.) with even more money than angels
- Most VCs interested in businesses needing at least $250,000
- A VC firm may receive 1,000+ proposals/year, vast majority rejected
- They look for: uniqueness, growth potential, strong management team
- "Unless yours is a small business with huge potential, you would be best advised looking for funding elsewhere"
- SBA-associated VC firms called SBICs (Small Business Investment Companies) are more accessible — they fund many small businesses that wouldn't otherwise attract VC

### Dealing with Investors — Structuring the Deal
- **Sole Proprietorships/Partnerships**: investment = loan (repaid at agreed rate) OR investor buys a percentage of the business and gets a share of profits
- **Corporations**: two methods:
  1. **Debt securities** (sale of debt) — investment treated as a loan. Advantage: repayments are interest = tax deductible (unlike dividends). Debt holders have legal priority over equity holders if things go wrong
  2. **Sale of equity** (stock) — investor gets actual ownership interest, right to participate in earnings, vote on board. Most common corporate financing. FTC may have oversight duties. How much stock to give = depends on business size, investment amount, authority desired — discuss with lawyer
- **LLCs**: sell membership shares (not stock). May change structure from "member managed" to "manager managed" — in manager-managed LLC, only named managers make decisions; investor would be like a shareholder without board voting rights (LLCs don't have boards)

## Pages 93-98: Chapter 9 — Location, Location, Location

- Wayne Calloway quote: "Nothing focuses the mind better than the constant sight of a competitor who wants to wipe you off the map."

### Not All Locations Are Created Equal
- Not every business needs a great location — depends on business type, brand, foot traffic needs, budget
- Businesses that go TO customers (house cleaning, pool care) don't need prime locations
- Wholesale businesses, warehouses, factories also don't need great locations
- Out-of-the-way locations in redevelopment areas can offer tax breaks
- First consideration: how important traffic will be. A convenience store needs high traffic; a chiropractor does not
- Author's dad: "always liked locating his stores across the street from major malls. He figured he would get the benefit of the mall's advertising and pull without having to pay mall rent"

### Location Checklist
- **Population** — enough people in the area? What happened to similar businesses?
- **Traffic** — drive-by traffic, public transportation, major thoroughfare, center of activity?
- **Competition** — too many competitors nearby?
- **Visibility** — visible from highway, not set back or easily missed?
- **Signs** — can you legally/lease-wise erect a noticeable sign?
- **Facilities** — parking, bathrooms, outdoor lighting, landscaping?
- **Landlord** — responsive and easy to work with? Get references
- **History** — avoid locations with bad reputations (hard to overcome preconceptions)
- **Rent** — important but should NOT be sole deciding factor; cheap + bad location = path to failure
- **Zoning** — make sure zoned for your business type
- Also consider: employee/supplier access, shipping/receiving area, environmental issues, Internet wiring, room to expand

### Negotiating the Lease
- "Remember everything is negotiable"
- You are a valuable commodity to the landlord — finding qualified commercial tenants is not easy
- Research: similar space rental rates, vacancy rates in the area, how long the space has been empty
- Have your lawyer review the lease — it was drafted by the landlord's lawyer for the landlord's benefit
- "Any oral promises made by the landlord must be made part of the written lease or they will be unenforceable"

### Important Lease Issues
- **Square footage** — have an architect measure; landlords include closets, bathrooms, halls in square footage that isn't actual "office space"
- **Rent** — negotiate a few months' free rent on long-term leases
- **Length** — long enough to establish the business, short enough to exit if needed. A year or two with renewal option is good for starters
- **Assignments and subleases** — negotiate the right to assign or sublet. Assignment = new tenant takes full responsibility; sublet = you're still on the hook
- **Gross or net lease** — gross lease = landlord pays insurance, taxes, utilities, etc. Net lease = you pay. Clarify exactly who pays for what (heating, AC, security, cleaning, parking, repairs)
- **Triple net lease** — renter pays rent PLUS all operating costs (security, maintenance, taxes, insurance)
- **Escalation charges** — restrict escalation clauses (landlord passing on increased property taxes, business insurance). You do NOT have to agree to share these costs
- **Signs** — broach signage early, before signing. Can be a deal breaker
- **Utilities** — if buying from landlord, ensure right to audit utility bills
- **Renovations** — landlord may contribute to renovations (increases building value + locks you into longer lease)
- **Restrictive covenant** — prevent landlord from renting to competitors nearby (e.g., in a mall)
- "Try to cultivate a good working relationship with your landlord. That will go further toward working out problems than a dozen letters from your lawyer."

1. **Change in thought** — business world recognizes smaller = quicker, more innovative, more entrepreneurial, better at adapting. "Little is the new big"
2. **Information/tech revolution** — computers, printers, cell phones, PDAs, faxes, great software let any small business look big
3. **Growing market** — big businesses now see and cater to small business market (Staples, small business software, Kinko's)

### Creating Your Brand
- "A brand is the essence of what makes your business unique. It combines your name, logo, and purpose into an identifiable whole"
- "Without a brand, you may find that instead of being all things to all people, you are nothing to no one"
- Nike swoosh + "Just Do It" = the gold standard of branding
- Your name, logo, slogan, location, and pricing structure ALL depend on the brand you're trying to create

### Elements of Your Brand
**Your Name** — two approaches:
1. Quirky/unrelated name (Xerox, Amazon, Kodak) — requires massive marketing budget to establish recognition. Not ideal for small businesses
2. Benefit-descriptive name (Jiffy Lube, Baja Fresh, Quickee Mart) — almost always preferable for small businesses. Creates immediate awareness

"Maria's Discount Italy" won't cut it for upscale Italian furniture, but "Maria's Casa d'Italia" might. (2) The perceived benefit — best name combines image + perceived benefit: NetFlix, Discount Warehouse, Speedy Linguine

- Hire someone: $100 (student) to $10,000 (professional). Graphic arts students can deliver surprisingly good work
- Free logo generators online (e.g., cooltext.com)

**Your Slogan** — incorporated with logo to reinforce brand. Examples: IBM = "Think"; Carpet World = "Elegance Underfoot"; BMW = "The Ultimate Driving Machine"
- Name + logo + catchphrase = "the holy trinity" of branding, all equally essential

**Colors and Fonts** — pick colors and fonts that reinforce the brand. A computer consultant using a computer-looking font makes sense; Old English font would not
- Font, color, name, logo, slogan then used consistently across ALL materials: stationery, business cards, envelopes, mailing labels, signs, invoices, receipts

### Branding Secrets
**Do What You Do Best Again and Again** — brand = a promise: "If you buy this product, you know what you will be getting." Volvos are safe, Nordstrom's has great service. Consistency is key
**Offer Superior Customer Service** — "all your hard work creating that cool brand will be a waste of time and money if it isn't reinforced by happy customers"
**Be a Mench** — Yiddish for "a good person." Practice mench ethics: care for customers, employees, vendors. Pay invoices on time. Do more than asked. Help in the community. "Flash may bring people in the door once, but caring for them gets people to stick with you for the long haul"
- **Cautionary tale: Pets.com** — burned through millions on branding (famous sock puppet on *People* magazine and *Good Morning America*), but "creating an identifiable brand is not the same as creating a valuable business." They focused far more on branding than on the actual business
- Two keys to brand building: (1) develop a specific identity, (2) communicate that identity consistently

## Pages 107-114: Chapter 11 — Products and Inventory

- Walter Landor quote: "Products are made in the factory, but brands are created in the mind."

### It's All in the Buying
- **Case study: Jon's antique shop** — works only 6 hours/day, 4 days/week, makes six-figure income. Store is always busy, full of fascinating knickknacks from dealers, antique shows, garage sales, classified ads, estate sales
- Jon's secret: "It's all in the buying." He won't buy anything, no matter how cool, unless he can get it for the right price. If he pays too much, it takes up valuable shelf space that could be used for products that sell

- **Trade Shows** — best place to find products and suppliers. See trends, meet suppliers, learn lingo, get crash course in industry. Find shows at tsnn.com
- **Sales reps** — manufacturers/wholesalers/distributors have traveling reps. Some represent one company, others represent multiple similar product lines. "By some estimates, there are more independent sales reps than any other type of home based businesses"
- **Trade Magazines** — look at the ads, call companies whose products you like, get a rep sent out
- **Trade Associations** — may have lists of overseas suppliers with lower-cost products
- **Online** — Thomas Register (thomasregister.com) for finding manufacturers
- **Go to the Source** — contact the company directly, ask for the sales department, explain your business, request to meet with the local rep

### Meeting the Reps
- New businesses face challenge of being taken seriously — reps are busy and may want to see letters of credit or references
- "Finding vendors is a matter of relationships as much as anything else"
- Start small if big manufacturers are difficult — build trade credit and references with smaller/local companies first, then expand
- **Net 30** = paying for items 30 days after invoice receipt
- Get samples of products — sometimes free, sometimes not. Verify quality and style match your needs
- Find products that: (1) you can afford, (2) customers will want, (3) build your brand

### Inventory Control
- Stock enough variety for different types of customers — "cars come in all shapes, sizes, and price ranges for a reason"
- Don't stock only what YOU like — you'll miss customers unlike you
- How much to order: shelves must look full + enough to meet normal demand + ~10% extra as cushion for late deliveries
- **Reorder timing**: track supplier delivery times. If you sell 5/week and delivery takes 2 weeks, reorder when you still have 3 weeks' worth
- **Case study: New York Pizza** — Ed got great press coverage, was inundated with customers on a big Friday night, but ran out of dough by 9:30 and cheese by 10:00. "Excited new customers were turned away at the door" — golden opportunity missed because he hadn't bought enough inventory
- Too much inventory = cash flow problem. Excess ties up cash, takes up space (especially bad for perishable/seasonal items)
- Solution for excess: have a SALE. "Sale" is one of the two most powerful words in business ("free" is the other). Sales bring people in who may buy other non-sale items too

### The 80-20 Rule (Applied to Inventory)
- 80% of sales come from 20% of customers. Know who those customers are and treat them right
- 80% of sales come from 20% of products — those 20% are the lifeblood, accounting for most of your cash flow. Always keep those items stocked
- Prioritize inventory spending on that crucial 20%

### Tracking Inventory
- Manual system: each item tagged, tag removed on sale, used tags itemized, checked against physical inventory
- Computerized: accounting programs like QuickBooks have inventory tracking built in
- Point-of-sale (POS) systems create up-to-the-minute inventory records from the point of purchase
- Industry-specific systems from trade associations — more expensive but better fit for your specific business
- Modern software provides sophisticated reports: sales analysis, monthly charts, price comparisons, A/R tracking, sales tax calculations

## Pages 115-119: Chapter 12 — Successful Pricing Strategies

- "If you really put a small value upon yourself, rest assured that the world will not raise your price." — Anonymous
- Price is an important aspect of your brand — "you may need to tinker a bit before you find that perfect price"

### The Psychology of Price
- BMW strategy (fewer products, higher price, wealthier audience) vs. VW strategy (high volume, lower profit per sale) — both are valid
- Even-numbered prices ($200) perceived as higher quality than odd-numbered ($179.99)
- If selling high-end items → even-numbered prices. If competing on low cost → use $.99 pricing
- Peculiar numbers work too: merchandise sold better in lots of 3 for $5.31 ($1.77 each) than at $1.69 each. $3.33 also catches the eye

### 5-Step Process for Determining Optimum Price
1. **Figure out your minimum price** — cost to buy/manufacture + proportional overhead (rent, labor, shipping, insurance) = minimum price. Below this = out of business. Above this = profit
2. **What is the focus of your brand?** — gourmet boutique (charge more, sell less) vs. produce warehouse (charge less, sell more). Or something in the middle
3. **Analyze your competitors** — know what they charge, whether your products are superior/inferior/similar, who's doing the best and whether price is the reason. Offer something better — price, service, location
4. **Set a price** — based on minimum cost, competitor pricing, and desired brand image
5. **Test, test, test** — first price is not your last price. Test different prices in different stores/ads. "Even small business owners who have been in business for a long time should occasionally test new prices for old products"

### Pricing Psychology Deep Dive
- "Product pricing is part art, part skill, and all perception." A customer pays a price if they perceive the value. Ralph Lauren Polo shirts cost ~$100, similar shirt without the polo pony costs $25 — perceived value
- "You need to find that magic equilibrium point where customers perceive value and you perceive dollars"
- **Don't be afraid to raise prices** — inflation is real. What cost you X five years ago costs more today. "The worst thing is that you lose some sales and have to drop your prices again. The best thing is that extra revenue begins to come in the door"

### Gas War! (Why Competing on Price Alone Is Bad)
- Gas war analogy: stations across the street from each other keep undercutting, people flock for cheap gas — but all the work building a brand is reduced to just selling stuff for less
- "Unless you can really compete on price, and want to, don't make the mistake of thinking that you can simply charge what your competitors charge (or less) and all will be fine"
- McDonald's is cheapest because they can afford to be — that IS their business plan. Starbucks is NOT the cheapest coffee but IS the most popular — customers have reasons beyond price
- **Why competing on price is bad**:
  1. You won't make enough money — especially for service businesses. You won't bill 40 hours/week (you also need to run the business). Must charge enough to cover monthly nut AND earn a profit
  2. You will attract only price-sensitive customers — if that's your market, fine. If not, you're in a gas war
  3. You will NOT get good customers — being cheapest drives away quality customers who care about more than price
- Charge extra for rush orders or emergency services

### Negotiating Prices
- Being competitive ≠ offering the lowest price. A fair, reasonable price is different from the lowest price
- "The best advice, then, is to be judicious, avoid negotiating if possible, but do so if you must. It is probably better to get less and secure the sale than to be rigid and get nothing"
- **How to get customers to pay more**: offer a flat fee (peace of mind), offer a free consultation (builds rapport), start small to build confidence then charge more, guarantee your work (builds confidence → higher fees)

### The Loss Leader Strategy
- Pricing strategy that attracts customers by offering sharp discounts on specific items at or near a loss
- Goal: get customers in the door, then sell them other non-discounted items that compensate for the loss
- Example: bookstore advertises a bestseller cheap → customers come in, find companion books, CDs, etc. "The loss leads to a bigger sale"
- Uses: moving unwanted merchandise, attracting new customers (e.g., discount teen novels to attract younger shoppers with disposable income), building a "low-cost leader" brand, creating repeat customers
- Two precautions: (1) make sure lost profit IS offset by other sales — if people only buy the loss leader and nothing else, it's just a loss, not a loss leader. (2) You MUST actually have the item at the advertised price — not having it is fraud and is illegal
- "Use it to build your customer base and your reputation"

## Pages 141-148: Chapter 15 — Accounting Ease

- David Letterman quote: "There's no business like show business, but there are several businesses like accounting."
- "Business accounting is a way to keep score" — helps you know how well you're doing
- Two parts to your business: doing things you love, and doing things you must. Accounting falls in the latter category

### Accounting Basics
- **Accounting** = tracking income and expenses, then using that data to examine financial status
- **General ledger** = the place where ALL financial transactions are kept. Made up of four categories: assets, liabilities, income, expenses
- All entries are double entries — debits on the left, credits on the right. For every debit, there should be an equal and offsetting credit
- Double-entry accounting dates back to the 15th century — first written description from Benedetto Cotrugli (1458), included in Luca Pacioli's *Summa de Arithmetica* (Venice, 1494)
- Each area feeding the general ledger has its own **subledger** — daily sales and payments go into accounts payable/receivable subledgers
- **Tip**: Hire TWO people for accounting — one person writing checks and reconciling the account "is asking for trouble" (embezzlement risk)
- Financial reports (P&L, etc.) are snapshots of fiscal health — income ledger shows revenue, but profit only becomes clear when compared to expense ledger

### Budgeting
- Running a business without a budget = "driving a car with a bag over your head"
- Reframe "budget" as "plan" — "A good plan is a guide, not a noose"
- A budget has two categories: **projected income** and **expenses**
- For income: conservatively estimate based on last year's data or business plan projections. Be realistic — "if you paint too rosy a picture, then you can easily get in over your head"
- For expenses: list everything — advertising, auto, insurance, lease, taxes, phone, utilities, inventory, equipment, payroll

### Cash Flow
- "Cash flow is your business oxygen. Without it, your business will suffocate and die"
- Three ways to control cash flow:
  1. **Live by the rule**: "Without your business oxygen, your business will suffocate and die"
  2. **Create cash flow projections** — always know what your cash balance will be in 6 months. Think ahead
  3. **Keep the pipeline open** — a client today may not pay for months. "You have to keep creating clients and doing work today to keep the cash flow spigot open." Rule: if you need money in 6 months, create new business within the next 3 months, bill it, get paid within 6 months
- Cash flow crunch = usually poor planning. Know your business cycle (Starbucks: coffee up in winter, down in summer → introduce cooler drinks)
- If in a cash crunch: (1) collect receivables aggressively, (2) get a short-term loan

### Collecting Tardy Accounts Receivable
- Assign someone to contact all AR over 30 days old; get specific payment date; call again if not received. AR over 60 days = real problem — prioritize
- Institute 10% surcharge policy on all AR over 30 days (give 30 days notice)
- Inform customers all outstanding balances must be current before any new product ships
- Hire a lawyer or collection agency if necessary
- Last resort: sell the debt to a collection agency (they buy bad debt at sharp discounts daily)

### Hiring an Accountant or Bookkeeper
- Many entrepreneurs are great innovators but eyes glaze over at balance sheets
- **Accountant duties**: track what business owes/is owed, create financial statements, reconcile bank statements. Beyond that: handle taxes (save thousands through proper planning), do payroll (often outsourced), handle audits, deal with IRS, offer business/financial planning, succession planning, business valuation
- **Finding one**: referrals are best — friends, business associates, banker, attorney, other entrepreneurs
- Interview questions: experience with small businesses (especially your field)? Timely reports (at least monthly)? Who actually services the account? Services beyond reporting? Business consulting? Cost?
- Fiducial (fiducial.com) = leader in small business tax/financial/accounting consulting
- Independent accountants = personalized service; Big Five firms = prestige, helps raise capital/establish credit
- **Cutting costs**: keep great records (organized receipts, legible up-to-date ledger, automated if possible), handle small stuff yourself, use a bookkeeper instead of an accountant for basic bookkeeping

## Pages 149-158: Chapter 16 — Making a Profit

- Woody Allen quote: "It is no secret that organized crime in America takes in over forty billion dollars a year. This is quite a profitable sum, especially when one considers that the Mafia spends very little for office supplies."

### Computing Your Profitability — Four Subconcepts
1. **Gross Profit** = revenue from sale minus Cost of Goods Sold (COGS). COGS = actual cost to make/buy the product (raw materials, labor, directly associated costs; for resale = wholesale cost)
2. **Net Profit** = gross profit minus taxes and interest. Also called *earnings* or *net income*
3. **Profit Margin** = Gross Profit / Sales (as percentage). Example: child care center grossing $8,000/month with $5,000 overhead → $3,000 gross profit → $3,000/$8,000 = 37% profit margin. "Any business that makes 37 percent profit margin is doing something right"
4. **Markup** = (Sales Price - Cost to Produce) / Cost to Produce. In the child care example: ($8,000 - $5,000) / $5,000 = 60% markup

### Pricing (Revisited from Profitability Angle)
- Two schools: (1) price low to capture market share (VW strategy), (2) price higher for higher per-unit profit (Mercedes strategy)
- "A big part of how people perceive your business is based on what you charge. Two lawyers may do the exact same sort of work, but the one who charges $350 an hour will be perceived as better than the one who charges $150"

### 5-Step Pricing Analysis
1. Determine your break-even point
2. Identify your customers and brand (middle class or wealthy? Upscale or bargain?)
3. What is the competition doing? Can you beat them?
4. Don't set your price too low — healthy gross profit margin is the best source of cash for growth
5. Test, test, and test some more — trial and error, tinker

### Three Ways to Increase Profit
1. **Sell more** — add new profit centers, replicate what works, read Part IV (Growing Your Business)
2. **Increase prices** — many owners are afraid to raise prices, but often fears are ungrounded. If price is your primary gauge, factors like quality, personal service, convenience, speed get left out. "McDonald's and Wal-Mart emphasize low prices because that is their business model." Test increase on a few customers first; if they don't balk, roll it out
3. **Reduce overhead** — the tried-and-true method, but the risk is cutting the very thing that brings in business. "Firing people is usually not the answer"

### Case Study: Jack Stack and Open Book Management (OBM)
- Jack Stack runs Springfield Remanufacturing Corporation (SRC). Began in debt, struggling
- Within five years, created a new way of doing business worth millions
- He taught employees how the finances and overhead worked (e.g., how reducing waste helps), then gave them a stake through profit sharing
- Employees became "part-owners" — motivated like small business owners
- SRC reduced overhead dramatically and increased profits correspondingly
- Called "open book management" (OBM) — "fairly sacrilegious" to most owners who keep profits/losses secret
- Recommended book: *A Stake in the Outcome: Building a Culture of Ownership for the Long-Term Success of Your Business* by Jack Stack (Currency, 2003)

### Practical Ways to Reduce Overhead
- **Cut back on available supplies** — stop excess waste (pens, FedExes without paying)
- **Rent out space** — sublease unused workspace to another small business
- **Give incentives** — employees on the front lines may see waste you don't. Reward cost-saving suggestions
- **Buy in bulk** — Sam's Club, Costco. Costco executive membership (~$100/year) offers low-cost insurance, telephone service, lines of credit
- **Buy used** — furniture, equipment
- **Use USPS** — Priority mail (2-3 days, ~$4) instead of overnight ($15). Think ahead
- **Rethink phone service** — many carriers, no need to pay retail
- **Review insurance** — coverage from years ago may be outdated/overpriced. Get new quotes. Non-smoker and good driver discounts
- **Use videoconferencing** — reduce travel costs
- **Request proposals from new vendors** — existing vendors may have gotten complacent. Shop around
- **Read Part VIII** (Business on a Shoestring) — many more money-saving ideas

### Paying Yourself
- First figure out what the business needs — its needs come first. "If you bite the hand that feeds you, it will bite you back"
- Three considerations:
  1. **Business structure determines how you pay yourself** — only C corp owners are legally "employees" who can go on regular payroll. Sole proprietors/partners draw from profits. LLCs with multiple owners can distribute profits only if doing so doesn't impair solvency. WARNING: paying yourself too much in a C corp can trigger IRS audit (salaries <$500K generally not challenged)
  2. **Avoid paying yourself as the money comes in** — good month = take extra, bad month = take nothing. This violates budget discipline and doesn't account for emergencies. Set a consistent amount your business can live with
  3. **Consider tax consequences** — C corp = business pays payroll taxes on your salary. Sole proprietors/partners = business profits flow through to personal income tax, PLUS self-employment tax (~15%)

## Pages 159-160: Chapter 17 — Getting and Extending Credit (beginning)

- Benjamin Franklin quote: "There are three great friends: An old wife, an old dog, and ready money."
- "The world runs on credit" — need to know how to get it and when to give it
- Whether obtaining a loan or extending credit to a customer, understanding credit is a must

### Business Loans — The Four Cs of Credit

1. **Capacity** — Can you repay? Banks want sufficient cash flow to service the loan. They ask for financial statements to prove it. Banks prefer businesses that have been around a while with a track record. New startups with little history fail this test — reason to consider SBA-guaranteed loans
2. **Collateral** — Can you secure the loan? Collateral greatly increases chances. If you lack collateral, you need a cosigner who has it
   - **Collateral valuation table (Bank vs SBA)**:
     - House: Bank = market value × .75 minus mortgage; SBA = market value × .80 minus mortgage
     - Car: nothing for both
     - Truck/heavy equipment: depreciated value × .50 for both
     - Office equipment: nothing for both
     - Furniture/fixtures: depreciated value × .50 for both
     - Inventory perishables: nothing for both
     - Jewelry: nothing for both
     - Receivables: Bank = under 90 days × .75; SBA = under 90 days × .50
     - Stocks & bonds: 50-90% for both
     - Mutual funds, IRA: nothing for both
     - CD: 100% for both
3. **Character** — History of repaying on time? You'll likely sign a personal guarantee, so personal credit history matters. Get credit reports from all three bureaus (Equifax, Experian, TransUnion) before applying
   - Check for errors — credit reports are rife with factual errors. One billion pieces of credit reported monthly, lots of incorrect info
   - Fair Credit Reporting Act mandates wrong info must be removed once challenged. Even legitimately late accounts must be deleted if ANY part of the account info is incorrect (name misspelled, wrong account number). This is how people "clean up their credit"
   - Items older than 7 years (except bankruptcy) should no longer appear
   - Get business credit history from Dun & Bradstreet (1-866-472-7362)
4. **Capital** — How much do you need and why? Come with financial statements, business plan, exact amount, how it will be used, when it will be repaid

### Two Additional Factors — The Two Es
1. **Equity** — Banks want to see equity built up in your business through retained earnings or cash injection from owner. Most banks want debt no more than 4× your equity. "Debt to equity ratio" — your balance sheet determines this
2. **Experience** — Your experience in the business and in the area the loan is going toward

### Business Loan Qualification Checklist (SBA)
- Must answer YES to all 10: good credit history, taxes up to date, business can repay, business has equity, not too much debt, owner money invested, collateral available, willing to personally guarantee, solid management team, have a business plan
- If any answer is No, you'll have a hard time getting a conventional bank loan

### SBA Loans
- SBA doesn't make loans directly — it guarantees loans made by selected banks, expanding the pool of businesses that can qualify
- **SBA Loan Types**:
  - 7(a): up to $2 million, 7 years — basic loan program
  - SBA Express: up to $2 million, 7 years — answers in 36 hours
  - Microloan: up to $35,000, 6 years — often used for small startups
  - Disaster Recovery: varies
  - CDC/504: $1 million, 5-10 years — long-term investment for fixed-asset purchases
  - Low Doc: up to $150,000, 5-10 years — answers within 36 hours
  - CapLines: up to $2 million, up to 5 years — short-term and cyclical working capital
  - Community Express: up to $250,000, 7 years — for low and moderate income areas
  - Export Express: up to $250,000, 5-25 years — for exporting companies
  - International Trade: up to $1.25 million, up to 25 years

### Equity Financing
- Alternative to debt financing — sell part of the business in exchange for capital
- Startup stage: often comes from family, friends, business associates, industry colleagues
- Professional equity funding: venture capitalists (VCs) and angel investors
- VCs are typically groups of wealthy individuals or financial institutions, specialize in industries they know well (e.g., Silicon Valley VCs = former computer executives)
- VCs look for companies 3-5 years old with solid plan and sound management team. They review hundreds of pitches to invest in the cream of the crop
- Angels are individuals wanting to invest in growth-opportunity businesses

### Extending Credit to Customers
- Net 30 or Net 60 common (Net 30 should be your maximum)
- Need: accounts receivable plan, monthly invoicing, tracking who paid and who hasn't
- Limit your risk — investigate customer creditworthiness before extending credit
- KEY RULE: "If a customer can't or won't pay when he or she wants to hire you — when they need you most — they probably won't pay later"
- Be picky about whom you work with — prevents problems later
- **Credit application should include**: business name, owner name, legal structure, address, phone, taxpayer ID, years in business, 3 trade references, 2 bank references, credit amount requested, signed authorization
- Filter applications through a credit reporting bureau (Equifax or TransUnion)

### Michael Gerber's 6 Reasons Businesses Fail (from The E-Myth)
1. Lack of management systems
2. Lack of vision
3. Poor financial planning
4. Not understanding your market
5. Competition
6. Underfunding

### Credit Cards and Checks
- Accepting credit cards = proven to increase sales (impulse buys, paying without cash)
- Costs: discount rate 1.5-3% per transaction, equipment costs (lease or purchase), monthly merchant account fees
- Set up merchant account through your bank or independent credit card processing companies (more expensive but more flexible)
- **Check tips**: Don't accept new checks (no name imprinted, numbered 1-500 = new accounts most likely to bounce). Wait for check to clear before giving refunds

## Pages 157-164: Chapter 18 — Insurance

- Ambrose Bierce quote: "Insurance: An ingenious modern game of chance in which the player is permitted to enjoy the comfortable conviction that he is beating the man who keeps the table."
- 9/11 made business owners re-examine insurance policies and needs

### Types of Insurance
1. **Health** — Employees rank it among most important benefits. Covered extensively in Chapter 27
2. **Business Owner** — "catch-all" coverage, basic policy for fire and other mishaps plus some liability
3. **Property/Casualty** — Protects against physical damage or loss of business assets (fire, theft, explosion, vandalism). Consider insuring: buildings, equipment, inventory, machinery, vehicles, computers/printers/phones, furniture, money/securities, important papers, signs/outdoor property, intangible property like goodwill. Rates vary widely — shop around
4. **Liability (CGL)** — Comprehensive general liability. Two functions: (1) pays claims if someone injured due to your/employee negligence, (2) pays attorney costs if sued. Almost a must in today's litigious society
5. **Worker's Compensation** — Required in every state except Texas. Small businesses with few employees (typically <5) may not be required. Covers medical bills and lost wages for injured employees, and protects owner against claims by injured employee's family. Rates depend on how long in business, state, number of claims. Reduce premiums by having good safety record, following OSHA guidelines. Some states have risk-sharing pools as insurance of last resort
6. **Errors and Omissions (E&O)** — For service businesses. Protection if you neglect to do something causing customer damage (e.g., physician malpractice)
7. **Business Interruption (BII)** — Covers loss of income when operations disrupted by fire, flood, disaster. KEY: only covers income loss directly related to loss of physical property. Service businesses may not need it; factories definitely do
8. **Key Man** — Insures against death of key employee (CEO, VP Sales, etc.). If top management is thin and one or two people keep the ship afloat, this is critical. Buy as much as you can afford — estimate what it would cost if the key person vanished
9. **Auto** — Must if you make deliveries or provide company cars. Don't just buy state minimum — a $25K policy won't help if your driver kills someone. For established businesses, $1 million in liability is right. Increase deductible to keep premiums down
10. **Life** — Creates financial stability for family. Banks often require business owner to have life insurance before lending. Term vs whole life: term is like renting (cheaper, no equity), whole is like buying a house (build equity). Recommendation: term is probably better — savings go back into business

### Tax Note on Insurance
- Premiums are tax deductible as business expense, BUT life and Key Man premiums are NOT deductible if the business is the beneficiary

### Buying Insurance — Cost Reduction Tips
- **Agent vs Broker**: Agent works for one company; broker represents many insurers and can shop your needs around. Broker can save you money
- **Take highest deductible you can reasonably afford** — but it has to be an amount you can actually absorb
- **Flexible payment plans** — monthly, quarterly, or yearly. Find a good broker to guide you

### Four Parts of Any Insurance Policy
1. Declarations page — names policyholder, explains what's insured, lists maximum payout
2. Insurance agreement — responsibilities of both sides
3. Conditions — exactly what's covered and under what circumstances
4. Exclusions — what's NOT covered. Read carefully — worst thing is discovering your loss type was excluded in fine print. Property loss policies typically exclude theft by employee

### Making a Claim
- **Before a claim**: Keep excellent records in safe, secure spot (preferably off site). Keep backups of important files and computer data readily accessible. Video your premises and property yearly. Store records in secure location
- **After a loss — 4 steps**:
  1. Report the incident immediately — some policies can deny coverage if notice is late
  2. Protect property from further damage — policies may require this
  3. Provide documentation — receipts, videotape
  4. Communicate and cooperate — make it easy for the adjuster

## Pages 165-168: Chapter 19 — Get a Handle on Taxes (beginning)

- Dave Barry quote about IRS: "The folks at the IRS are regular people just like you, except that they can destroy your life"
- Tax amount depends on: legal form of business, income, expenses, accountant quality, and your own tax knowledge

### Deductions
- "Ordinary and necessary" business expenses deductible from federal income taxes
- **Entertainment**: Limit is 50% of legitimate entertainment expenses. Almost any business-related entertainment counts: golf, dinners, client events, concerts, boat days. Keep ALL receipts, prove business relation, write on receipt whom you were with
- **Staff parties/picnics**: 100% deductible
- **Travel**: 100% deductible for business. Family joining on business trip = their expenses NOT deductible. LOOPHOLE: staying extra night or two for discounted airfare = extra hotel and meals ARE deductible
- **Automobile**: Two methods:
  - Standard mileage: 36 cents/mile plus business tolls and parking
  - Actual expense: total auto expenses (gas, repairs, depreciation) × business use percentage. Example: $10K total, 60% business use = $6K deductible. Keep a log of business vs personal driving
- **Business losses**: Deducted against personal income. If business loss exceeds personal income, extra loss carries forward to future years
- **Loans and credit cards**: Interest on business loans, purchases, advances is deductible
- **Professional associations**: Deductible. Also deductible: up to $25 per business gift, bank charges, magazines/books, theft losses, commissions, web development, parking, tolls, seminars, bus fare
- **Charity**: Sole proprietors/partnerships/LLCs/S corps = pass through to owner's personal return. C corps claim deduction themselves
- **Taxes**: Sales tax on business purchases, fuel/excise taxes, property tax, local assessments, employment taxes — all deductible. Self-employment tax paid by individuals is NOT deductible, nor is federal income tax
- **KEY**: Small businesses lose audits because of poor records. Keep ALL receipts, cancelled checks, credit card statements. Good records = much better chance of surviving an audit

### Other Tax Basics
- Employees must fill out W-4 (federal) and I-9 (Immigration and Naturalization Service) when hired
- **Payroll taxes you must deduct and match**:
  - Social Security (FICA) — withhold and match
  - Medicare — withhold and match
  - Unemployment tax — withhold, match, and pay
  - State income taxes — withhold
- Strongly advised to hire a payroll service (ADP, Paychex) because payroll withholding is complicated

### Sales Taxes
- Five states with no sales tax: Alaska (parts), Delaware, Montana, New Hampshire, Oregon
- If you sell a product, you owe state tax. Amount varies by state; some states tax services too
- Register with state tax department, track taxable vs nontaxable sales, include on state tax return
- **Two exceptions**: (1) Resellers with a resale license (wholesalers, retailers) don't owe sales tax. (2) No sales tax on sales to tax-exempt organizations (schools, churches)

### Tax Deadlines Beyond April 15
- Corporations: file within 2.5 months after end of fiscal year
- Quarterly estimated taxes: April 15, June 15, September 15, January 15
- Sales taxes: quarterly or monthly depending on state
- Employee taxes: weekly, monthly, or quarterly depending on number of employees
- **Quarterlies required** if you expect to pay at least $500 in taxes for the year. Must pay either 90% of what you'll owe or 100% of previous year's tax

### Property Taxes
- If you own real estate, you owe property taxes. If you lease, your lease may require you to pay them
- "Base year taxes" — in some leases, owner pays amount owed before lease was signed, lessee pays any increases
- Commercial property tax rates significantly higher than residential

### Tax Tips

**Set Up a Retirement Account**
- Self-employed can divert pretax dollars into retirement accounts to reduce taxable income
- **Keogh Plans**: Contribute up to $41,000/year into tax-free account. Fairly complicated, need financial advisor. Benefits: contributions deducted from gross income, taxes deferred until withdrawal, interest also tax-deferred, more liberal contribution amounts than IRAs
- **Solo 401(k)**: High contribution limits, up to $41,000/year. Amount you can contribute rises as you get older
- **SEP IRAs**: Contribute and deduct up to 20% of income into tax-deferred retirement account. Simple to set up (few minutes at bank/brokerage), no professional help needed, no annual government reports. Beat regular IRAs because of larger contributions
- NOTE: If you set up SEP, solo 401(k), or Keogh, you must offer it to employees too. Consult employee benefits pro first
- **Bonus**: Tax credit of up to $500 for first three years of creating a business retirement plan (if <100 employees)
- Roth IRA contributions are NOT tax deductible — does little for business tax purposes

**Lease Your Property to Your Business**
- If you personally own property your business uses, lease it to the business. Lease expense is tax-deductible for the business, income from lease is not subject to Social Security tax, and you can take depreciation allowance on the property

**Use the Tax Laws — Depreciation**
- Jobs and Growth Tax Relief Reconciliation Act of 2003: can now depreciate and deduct 100% of cost of almost all new and used assets in the year you buy them. Previously had to depreciate over 5-7 years. Deduction cap raised from $25,000 to $100,000

**Reexamine Your Business Structure**
- Growing S corp may want to become C corp to access group-term life insurance and health-plan options
- Profitable sole proprietor may want LLC (liability protection) or S corp (reduce self-employment tax)

**Pay Your Quarterlies** — on time to avoid penalties and interest

**Delay Receivables / Accelerate Expenses**
- End of tax year: delay collecting accounts receivable until after January 1 to reduce current year's taxable income
- Accelerate expenses into current year: corporate charitable contributions, 60% of health insurance premiums (self-employed), year-end employee bonuses, any other tax-deductible expenses

**Deducting Your Home Office**
- Two requirements: (1) space used "exclusively and regularly" for business, (2) must be either principal place of business or where you meet customers
- Can deduct portion of mortgage, insurance, utilities, etc.

## Pages 173-174: Chapter 19 — Surviving an Audit

- Small businesses are audited more than the general public
- **Get help**: Hire CPA to prepare and attend the meeting. If you can't afford one, prepare yourself — review the return, gather all documentation

## Pages 175-178: Chapter 20 — Legal Ease I

- Mark Twain: "It is easier to stay out than to get out"
- Litigation explosion — businesses are prime targets ("deep pockets"). Need to arm yourself with knowledge
- Author is a lawyer but caveat: no book can replace a lawyer who knows your specific situation

### Contracts
- Contracts are "like your personal set of laws" — as long as you bargain in good faith and subject is legal, almost anything goes
- Three requirements for a legally enforceable contract:

1. **Offer** — Must be clear and unambiguous. "I will buy 200 pounds of flour at $8/pound" = valid offer. "I think I'd like to buy some flour" = NOT an offer. Offers remain open unless accepted but can be revoked anytime before acceptance. Once accepted, cannot be revoked
2. **Acceptance** — Must be clear and unambiguous. "I accept" = yes. "That sounds good, let me call you back" = NOT acceptance. Counteroffer = rejection of original offer plus new offer. Between merchants, minor non-material changes under UCC are deemed accepted. Acceptance can also occur via actual performance (delivering the goods)
3. **Consideration** — "Quid pro quo" / "this for that." Both sides must give up something. "I'll wash your car" with nothing in return = no contract even if you say "okay" — no consideration. Need offer + acceptance + bargained-for exchange
   - **Exception — detrimental reliance**: If someone makes a promise, you rely on it to your detriment, and it was reasonably foreseeable, you can enforce the promise even without a formal contract

### Other Contract Rules
- **Get every contract in writing** — only some contracts legally require writing (land sales, purchases over $500, employment contracts over 1 year), but memories fade and people remember wrong. Get it in writing always
- **Standard/form contracts can be modified** — if you don't like a clause, negotiate to change or delete it
- **Be specific** — ambiguity breeds litigation
- **First to breach usually loses** — legitimate excuses exist (mistake, fraud, impossibility) but generally the first breacher loses
- Nolo.com for DIY contracts and legal resources

### Negligence and Liability
- Being found negligent can be one of the worst things — damages can be astronomical (breach of contract = only the contract value; negligence = hundreds of thousands or millions for pain and suffering)
- **Standard of care**: "You must act as a reasonably prudent person would under the same or similar circumstances." Failure = "breach of duty" = you pay
- Enact standards, policies, and procedures for employees — proves you're a reasonably prudent business owner, harder for plaintiffs to prove negligence
- Buy liability insurance — premiums go up with claims but it beats paying damages out of pocket
- **Famous case: Palsgraf v. Long Island Railroad** — illustrates that negligence must be foreseeable. Railroad employees negligently helped a man with a package (fireworks), which exploded and knocked over scales injuring Mrs. Palsgraf at the far end of the platform. Court ruled: employees were negligent toward the man, but NOT in relation to Mrs. Palsgraf (too remote)
- Key to avoiding negligence: maintain safe workplace, set standards, enforce them

### Product Liability
- If you manufacture, wholesale, distribute, or sell retail products, you face "product liability law"
- If your product injures a consumer, everyone in the supply chain between you and the buyer may be legally affected
- **Three legal theories**:
  1. **Negligence** — injured party must show seller/manufacturer breached standard of care (e.g., car wheel not bolted properly at plant)
  2. **Breach of implied warranty** — if you make express or implied warranty about what product will do, and it fails to live up, you may be liable. Many states limit this to economic loss only. TIP: Avoid making representations in advertising/marketing that could be used against you
  3. **Strict liability** — THE SCARY ONE. Injured party only needs to prove: (1) product was defective when sold, and (2) defect caused injury. No need to prove you breached a standard — just that product was defective. "Your prudence and care is no defense." Example: Firestone tire explosions
- **Minimize product liability risk**: Don't ship/sell defective products, inspect what you can, warn where possible (hence all those warning labels), be careful what you say/promise in promotions, use disclaimers, specify useful life of product — harm after expiration can't be attributed to you

## Pages 181-184: Chapter 20 — Intellectual Property

### Five Areas of Intellectual Property
1. **Trademarks** — Word, phrase, design, or symbol identifying your business and distinguishing from competition. "Service mark" = same for services. Examples: Dr. Pepper, Portland Trailblazers. Register at www.USPTO.gov (U.S. Patent and Trademark Office)

2. **Copyright** — Protects original, physical expression of a creative idea. Must be unique, tangible, and a "work of authorship." Includes musical scores, articles, choreography, photographs, movies, sculptures. Lasts life of author + 75 years. Creator owns copyright unless it's work-for-hire (employer owns). No registration needed — copyright exists as soon as you create the thing. But affix "© All rights reserved" to put world on notice. Can register with U.S. Copyright Office (optional)

3. **Patents** — Protect new and useful "process, machine, manufacture, or composition thereof." Utility patents: machines/industrial processes, last 20 years. Design patents: manufactured item designs, last 14 years. Plant designs: new plant varieties, last 17 years. Not inexpensive — almost always need a lawyer. If you've invented something unique, definitely patent it
   - R. Buckminster Fuller credited his fame to his patent lawyer — without the airtight patent on the geodesic dome, "no one would have ever heard of me"

4. **Trade Secrets** — Defined by Uniform Trade Secrets Act as "information that has independent economic value by virtue of remaining secret." Example: KFC's "17 herbs and spices." Must make efforts to keep secret (doesn't mean never share — just act appropriately to retain secrecy). Use NDAs (nondisclosure agreements) when sharing with third parties

5. **Goodwill** — Positive reputation and valuable customer list developed over time. A business asset usually relevant to exit strategy (selling the business). Protect it

## Pages 185-187: Chapter 21 — Legal Ease II

- Confucius: "I can try a lawsuit as well as other men, but the most important thing is to prevent lawsuits"
- Lawsuits are "a civilized form of war" — expensive, time-consuming, exhausting, frustrating. Both sides usually lose. Goal is to avoid them

### Employment Discrimination and Lawsuits

**At-Will Employment**
- Almost all employees are "at-will" — can be fired at any time, for almost any reason
- "Just cause" employees CANNOT be fired without just cause — usually have employment contract or promise of continued employment
- WARNING: Employee handbook promising job security could be inferred as promise of continued employment by a court — making employees "just cause" rather than at-will
- **Smart move**: Have all employees sign a document acknowledging at-will status. Protects you if they later claim wrongful termination

**Protected Classes — Three Exceptions to At-Will**
1. Cannot fire for exercising a statutory right (e.g., filing worker's comp claim)
2. Cannot fire for exercising a legal duty (e.g., jury service)
3. Cannot fire based on color, gender, religion, age, ethnic background, disability, armed forces membership, past debts, or bankruptcy — these are "protected classes"

- Most discrimination cases = "disparate treatment" — claim employer treated them unfavorably compared to others due to protected class membership
- Cases filed under Title VII of 1964 Civil Rights Act
- **Penalties if found guilty**: back pay, reinstatement, attorney's fees, money for emotional distress, even double damages as punishment
- **NEVER ask about protected class status in interviews** — age, religion, etc. Only ask about competence and qualifications for the job

### Preventing Discrimination Claims
- Have employee handbook explaining: expectations, at-will status, discipline system, nondiscrimination policy, and that claims will be investigated promptly
- **Document, document, document** — regular written performance reviews. When you have problem employees, warn them IN WRITING. Have them sign the warning. Have them sign second warnings. More documentation = less chance a discrimination claim sticks

## Pages 188-192: Chapter 21 — Legal Ease II (continued)

### Sexual Harassment
- Most prominent case: Jones v. Clinton — Paula Jones alleged then-Governor Bill Clinton invited her to hotel room and groped her. Judge ruled even if it happened, Jones suffered no damages (never denied promotion, never treated unfairly at work)
- **Two types of sexual harassment**:
  1. **Quid pro quo** — "this for that." Victim forced to have sex in exchange for job benefit. Example: promising secretary a raise in exchange for sexual favor
  2. **Hostile work environment** — employee must prove supervisor or co-worker made unwelcome advances or physical conduct pervasive enough to poison the work environment and create an abusive workplace. Same-sex harassment claims also apply in many jurisdictions
- **Must-do**: Adopt antisexual harassment policy in workplace AND employee handbook. Make it known to all employees. Written policy = both the right thing to do and good business. Investigate claims promptly, discipline offenders

### Unfair Trade Practices

**Consumer Protection Laws**
- Advertising must be truthful — no deceptive or fraudulent advertising
- FTC (Federal Trade Commission) investigates false advertising claims, can issue cease and desist or sue
- **Case example**: Car dealer advertised car for "1,000 bananas." Customer came with 1,000 bananas, dealer refused to sell. Consumer sued and won $1,000 compensatory damages PLUS $100,000 punitive damages for misrepresentation
- Avoid phony price comparisons — if normal price is $200 but you advertise "$100 off" implying original was $250, you've broken the law
- Be careful with "free" — must genuinely mean free. "Free Printer!" with asterisk requiring $1,000 purchase won't fly in most places. New York law requires conditions on "free" offers to be disclosed near the word "free" in type at least half as large
- Bottom line: avoid deceptive advertising, treat consumers honestly

**Protecting Against Ex-Employee Competition**
- Employees learn confidential information that could be useful if they go to a competitor or start their own business
- **Three documents to have employees sign when hired**:
  1. **Confidentiality agreement** — spells out what you consider trade secrets (customer lists, wholesale costs, etc.). Employee agrees to keep it confidential. Both parties keep a signed copy
  2. **Assignment of inventions** — anything employee invents while at work belongs to the employer. Have them sign acknowledging this. Inventions on their own time = different
  3. **Noncompete agreement** — trickiest of the three. Courts are reluctant to infringe on right to work, but understand sometimes competing would be unfair. Must be limited in TIME (usually 2 years max) and GEOGRAPHY (specific region). California: noncompetes frowned upon and only enforceable in limited circumstances. Most other places: if "reasonable" in length and limited to specific geographic area, usually acceptable
- **At exit interview**: remind departing employees they signed these agreements. Prevention > litigation

### Finding a Good Attorney
- Best way: referral from satisfied client. A satisfied client tells you more than any commercial
- **Questions to ask**:
  - Did the lawyer get good results? (case settled, contract beneficial, taxes reduced)
  - Was the attorney accessible? Call should be returned within 24 hours
  - Were fees reasonable? Cheaper is probably NOT better — you get what you pay for
  - Who does the work? Big firms use underpaid associates for much of the work. Associates are usually bright but young with little experience. Know who's doing the heavy lifting
- Schedule meeting (should NOT be billed for meet-and-greet — if they bill, bad sign). Spend a few hours, get a feel for personality, ask about background, get referrals
- Other options: Yellow Pages (look under Business Law), local bar association (lawyers listed by specialty, nonpartisan recommendations)

## Pages 195-198: Chapter 22 — Successful Advertising Strategies

- Ted Turner: "Early to bed, early to rise, work like hell, and advertise"
- "Not advertising is like being alone in a dark room with the door closed. You know you are there, but no one else does"
- Many small business owners assume their great idea, location, or sign will bring people in — WRONG. Advertising is what brings new customers. Marketing, networking, and word of mouth are important, but advertising has potentially the biggest payoff. "Advertising turns the light on"

### The Five-Step Process for Creating a Winning Ad Campaign
1. Think ahead — purpose, size, and budget for campaign
2. Decide what media works best for your business, brand, and budget
3. Create an ad that builds on that brand
4. Test the ad
5. Once convinced it works, roll it out

### Brainstorming — Two Types of Campaigns
1. **Branding campaign** — publicize your name and corporate identity so people remember you when they need what you offer. Subtler, builds awareness over time. Example: "Divorce for Fathers" ad in newspaper — not an immediate call to action, just building awareness so when someone needs a divorce lawyer, they remember the name. Branding is a marathon — not done in a week, pays dividends over time
   - Even dotcom flops like WebVan and Pets.com got the branding part right — we still remember their names
2. **Sales campaign** — intended to generate sales NOW. Shorter, more intense. Multiple media sources reinforce same message. Example: sidewalk sale, new product arrival, free drawing. This is a sprint — periodic, maybe once a month

### Budget
- **Cost method**: SBA suggests 2% of gross sales for yearly advertising. Others say closer to 5%. Example: $500K gross × 5% = $25,000/year = ~$2,000/month. Your income determines the budget
- **Task method**: Look at how many customers/how much product you need to sell to hit sales goals, then calculate advertising spend based on past return. Example: $15K spent last year generated $300K in sales; to grow 10%, spend $17,500

### Choosing the Right Medium
- Most important factor: zero in on your target audience. Know exactly who they are — what they watch, read, listen to. Your research and business plan should tell you this

**Newspapers**
- Almost every home gets one; can target audience by section (sports, business, home & garden)
- Advantages: details, prices, phone numbers that are hard to get across on TV/radio. Pick ad size, section, day. Even small ad with BIG HEADLINE can work
- Reference your website in every ad — "sitting goldmine" for more info
- Downsides: read once and discarded, small ads compete with big ones and news, no guarantee target audience sees it
- Sold by column and inch. Example: 2 columns × 4 inches = 8-inch ad. At $50/column inch = $400. Discounts for running multiple days
- Sunday = most read but most expensive and most competitive
- Position matters: request page 3 of your desired section (first page with ads, what people see first). Worth paying extra for this
- Request outside position for coupon ads (easier to cut out)

**Television**
- Most powerful advertising medium but cost-prohibitive for most small businesses
- Requires substantial time and money commitment, months to pay off, must have regional appeal
- **Good TV ad**: highly visual, GRAB ATTENTION quickly, repeat phone number and business name several times. Don't make it more interesting than your product. Don't be afraid to copy a good commercial format
- **GRP (Gross Ratings Point)** = 1% of TV households in area. 2 million homes = 1 GRP = 20,000 households. Need at least 150 GRPs/month
- Cost per GRP varies by market: New York $2,500, smaller markets $100
- **TV cost-saving tips**: Advertise off-peak (not 8-11 PM prime time), use cable (cheaper, better demographic targeting), use a media-buying service (they buy millions of dollars worth, have more pull negotiating rates — charge ~7.5% but save more than that)

**Radio**
- Can target specific demographics by station choice (rock, talk, oldies, Christian)
- **Cost per minute by market** (top tier / secondary / tertiary):
  - New York: $1,500 / $1,000 / $350
  - Los Angeles: $1,500 / $1,000 / $350
  - Chicago: $1,200 / $800 / $250
  - Major markets (SF, Dallas, DC, Seattle, Atlanta): $1,000 / $750 / $250
  - Mid markets (Boston, Denver, Philadelphia): $850 / $600 / $175
  - Smaller markets (Houston, Minneapolis, Phoenix): $750 / $250-500 / $150
  - Small markets (Baltimore, Portland): $500 / $350 / $100
- People need to hear an ad SIX TIMES before it sinks in — repetition is key, and repetition costs money
- Verify station claims — get verifiable info on audience size, rating points, demographics

**Internet**
- Smarter to buy search engine placement than banner ads/popups (no one reads those). Google boxed ads next to results = great investment

**Magazines**
- Best at targeting your specific audience (so many magazines exist). Not inexpensive but magazines stick around the house for months, read by many people — more bang for the buck

**Yellow Pages**
- In homes and businesses for a year. People open it ready to buy
- Downsides: expensive (quarter-page ~$500/month), people increasingly search online instead
- Not every business benefits — service businesses (plumbers, locksmiths) definitely should. Beware: Yellow Pages shoppers often call on many ads looking for best price — if you don't compete on price, may not be best choice
- **Tips**: Ask for discount (first-timers can get up to 40% off), create memorable ad (color, picture, white space), consider your category (several sections may apply — find the largest/most-read one)
- Recommended book: *Yellow Pages Advertising: How to Get the Greatest Return on Your Investment* by Jeffrey Price

## Pages 205-207: Chapter 22 — Creating a Winning Ad (AIDA Method)

### AIDA Formula: Attention, Interest, Desire, Action

**Attention**
- FIRST THING: Grab their attention. Without it, nothing else matters
- Use a powerful, benefit-laden headline. Examples:
  - "Amazing New Technique Relieves Arthritis!"
  - "Free Pamphlet Makes Your Computer as Easy to Use as a Telephone!"
  - "Save 50% on Office Supplies—Today Only!"
  - "The Lazy Man's Guide to Riches!"
- Intrigue them enough to want to know more

**Interest and Desire**
- Body of ad must describe benefits in simple, interesting terms. Clearly explain how product/service fills a need. If you solve a problem and your ad easily explains that — bingo!

**Action**
- Ask for the order. Give incentives to buy NOW. Make it easy — coupon, 800 number, email, online order form, fax order line
- **Example AIDA ad**: "Bankruptcy? Free Seminar! Free Advice!" (Attention — captures someone in debt, uses "Free" = most powerful word in advertising). Body describes the free seminar with attorney (Interest/Desire). "Seating Is Limited. Call (212) 555-9000" (Action — scarcity creates urgency, easy phone number)

### Test the Ad
- Before sinking a lot of money in: test it first. Run smaller version in print, run on less expensive station/show, run regionally in local paper
- Use different tracking codes for different media sources ("Mention code 1122 for 10% off") to identify which source works best
- **Coca-Cola / New Coke cautionary tale**: Coke was challenged by Pepsi, secretly experimented with new formulations, found one that beat Pepsi in taste tests. Rolled out New Coke triumphantly — disaster. Problem: they never test-MARKETED New Coke (never put batches in stores in, say, Des Moines). Testing is key to avoiding expensive belly flops

### Roll It Out
- Once you have a winning ad, it's your meal ticket. Run it as often as you can afford, avoiding oversaturation
- **Don LePre strategy**: Find a product/service to sell, create and test a small inexpensive classified ad, and when it pulls, roll that exact ad out to hundreds of newspapers across the country
- If you plan on advertising a lot, consider hiring an ad agency — they can help create ads and buy time/space at rates you can't get yourself

### Fees — Never Pay the Rate Card
- "The first rule of media is Never Pay the Rate Card." The rate card is the quoted price from their book
- Sometimes inventory is tight and you must pay it, but not always. If buying a lot of time/space, or they need advertisers, you can negotiate down
- If you can't negotiate down the rate card, ask for extras: free response cards, better position, free color, ads in special editions

## Pages 209-214: Chapter 23 — Marketing Muscle

- J.G. Gallimore: "Image creates desire"
- Marketing, advertising, and branding are "interwoven threads that make up the tapestry that is your business image." Together = most powerful weapons for getting people to notice and buy. If threads are disparate (advertising says one thing, marketing does another, neither reinforces brand), the tapestry is ugly. If they communicate a unified message = clear identity people can recall
- Advertising brings in new clients; marketing also keeps them around. Marketing builds your brand, and an identifiable brand gets people to remember your business

### Marketing Overview
- Peter Drucker: "Marketing is what your business is as seen through the eyes of your customers"
- Law office must look stately (serious issues), so needs embossed letterhead, prestigious address, proficient receptionist. All = marketing
- Marketing = the process of getting customers to perceive value in what you offer
- Many different methods — impossible to cover all. Chapter 35 (Marketing on a Shoestring) covers more

### Marketing Plans
- Same five-step process as advertising: (1) Think ahead on purpose/size/budget, (2) Decide what media works best, (3) Create materials that build on brand, (4) Test if applicable, (5) Roll it out
- According to *Wall Street Journal*, only 14% of small businesses have a written business plan

### Marketing Tools — Think Big
- Most small businesses use only 1-3 marketing methods. THINK BIG — there are many ways to market
- Smart entrepreneurs have multiple ways to bring in business — if one income stream slows, others keep the money pumping
- **"Multiple profit centers"** — term coined by Barbara Winter in *Making a Living Without a Job*. Amazon started selling books, now sells everything. When book sales are down, CD sales may be up. Multiple marketing methods create multiple profit centers

### The Elevator Pitch
- Quick, interesting, powerful answer to "What do you do?" — one of the simplest yet most powerful marketing tools
- You only have one chance to make a good first impression. You want a pitch that sparks "Tell me more"
- **Anecdote**: Author was talking to another speaker who described his business in a long, bland paragraph. Author asked himself "whom would you hire?" — the dynamic pitch-giver, not the rambler
- **Questions to create a great elevator pitch**:
  1. **What problem are you trying to solve?** Every great business solves a problem
  2. **Can you keep it simple?** Use plain English, be intriguing, have conviction. Example: Silicon Valley entrepreneur changed from "We utilize the latest 20-50 key exchange using Duffle transponders" to simply "We protect communications"
  3. **Why would people want to know more?** Instead of "I am a graphic artist," try "I help people get more business by illustrating their dreams"
  4. **Does it accelerate your heart rate?** A great pitch is a passionate pitch
- **Example elevator pitch**: "Hi, I'm Mara Sydney, president of Jillian Publishing. We publish books, newsletters, and video programs designed to help architects, drafters, and urban planners become more successful. Our best-selling title, *How to Become the Recognized Expert in Your Field*, was named Specialty Book of the Year by *Architecture* magazine."

### Networking
- Best prospects often come from networking — chamber socials, groups like LeTip International, social situations
- Business is about relationships — "not what you know, but whom you know, and networking ensures that you will know more people"
- According to *The Millionaire Next Door*, most self-made millionaires network all the time, everywhere: car wash, golf course, business conferences. They never know which contact may lead to a big deal
- **Two tips for networking**:
  1. Use your elevator pitch — prepackaged snappy opener leads to meaningful conversation
  2. Pretend you are the host — rather than sitting back waiting, flip it around and act gregarious, self-assured, positive

### Form a Strategic Partnership
- Of 23 million small businesses in the US, 17 million have NO employees. Of those with employees, vast majority have 10 or less. We often need help that large corporations have built in (marketing, accounting departments)
- Team up with businesses that share your customers but aren't competitors. Combine and leverage distribution, infrastructure, and knowledge for "very little extra money"
- Examples: veterinarian and pet store cross-promote. HP and Microsoft team up to sell computers + software
- **Case study — All Systems Go! (Radames Pera)**: Owner is the child actor who played "grasshopper" in *Kung Fu*. Started his home theater installation business in 1988 in L.A. by forming relationships with stereo store salespeople, letting them know he was available to install the surround-sound systems they were starting to sell. Business grew almost exclusively by word of mouth. Celebrity clients: Johnny Depp, Nicholas Cage, Sharon Stone, Phil Knight, Charlize Theron, Ben Stiller
  - When he moved to Portland, he recreated the business by partnering with local stereo/electronics retailers. "The business took off, right away"
  - When Portland market saturated, he pivoted — marketed to builders and architects instead. Strategic partnerships allowed him to recreate his business in a new location
- **Making strategic partnerships work — 4 questions**:
  1. Is the other business congruent with yours? Same goals? Agree on purpose and expectations
  2. What are the downsides? Brainstorm both positive and negative
  3. Is the deal a win-win? How will disagreements be resolved?
  4. What is the exit strategy? Define objectives so you know when it's working and when it's not

### Direct Marketing
- One of the oldest, most tried-and-true marketing methods
- Use direct mail letters, flyers, coupons, reply envelopes to sell to qualified prospects, increase awareness, create new customers
- Gallup survey of 251 businesses: top choice for educating consumers, creating sales, and introducing products = direct marketing
- **Three-step process**:
  1. Find a letter/offer/flyer/coupon that works — test, test, test. Start small, tinker, analyze. Example: restaurant sends free wine offer to 1/3 of homes, 10% off coupon to another 1/3, free child's dinner to last 1/3. Tabulate results, then send winning offer every 4 months
  2. Acquire a usable list — can be self-generated (existing customers) or purchased from list brokers (Yellow Pages under Direct Marketing). Be very specific: "homeowners in surrounding five counties" or "men aged 25-34." An up-to-date list of 500 is far more valuable than an unqualified list of 5,000. Build list by asking customers to fill out preferred customer cards
  3. Get people to read and respond — tips:
     - **Hand address the envelopes** — hand-addressed much more likely to be opened than computer-generated
     - **Effective offer answers "What's in it for me?"** — offer something they want (save time or money)
     - **GRAB THEIR ATTENTION QUICKLY**
     - **Conversational tone, deliver credibility** — testimonials and guarantees. Include expiration to create urgency
     - **Be patient** — often takes several letters. Good direct mail response rate = about 5%. If you mail 1,000 at $500 cost and get 50 new customers, what's that worth?
     - **Always include a P.S.** — postscripts are almost always read. Great place to reiterate offer or call to action

### Have a Sale
- One of most powerful marketing tools — reinforces your name with old customers, attracts new ones, gets rid of unwanted inventory, creates buzz
- Sales are loss leaders — mark down enough to be worthwhile but not so much you lose money
- **Markdown guidelines by age**: <4 months = max 30% off; <8 months = max 50% off; 8+ months = up to 75% off. If something's been sitting 8 months, move it — it's taking up space for product that can turn over
- **Before the sale**: Mark everything clearly with reduced price, put merchandise where it can be seen and touched, organize by size and category, first week is always busiest — have plenty of inventory
- **During the sale**: Keep displays full (customers won't buy if they think best deals are gone), watch slow movers and mark down more if needed, create buzz with big signs, balloons, outdoor SALE signs
- **Case example**: Carpet World in L.A. had huge sign in middle of store: "Our Word of Mouth Advertising Starts With You!"

### Brochures
- For the right business, can be the most important marketing method. Chance to put best foot forward
- **How to create a brochure**:
  1. **Brainstorm** — meet with team, get input on what customers need to know, what sells them, what needs you fulfill
  2. **Decide purpose** — sales tool? Trade show handout? Point of sale?
  3. **Consider competition** — look at competitor brochures for ideas, strive to beat them
  4. **Get software** — creating brochures in-house is much cheaper now with graphic arts software
  5. **Create mockup** — don't cram every fact in. Less is more. Use color, bullet points, white space, pictures to spark interest
  6. **Print it** — Kinko's or commercial printer. Professional results no longer require huge budgets

### Publicity
- Newspaper/magazine article or radio/TV segment about your business can do more than almost anything else to market and build your business
- How small businesses become big: at a critical growth moment, the business invests in public relations and gets publicity. That publicity becomes additional business, then more publicity = buzz is born
- Article/story = independent verification of your great business — you can't buy that kind of credibility
- People who see the story will patronize your business. You can link it to your website, tape/copy it, use it in marketing materials forever. A quote from the article can become an advertising headline
- Publicity is cost effective — compare cost of front page Business section placement vs. equivalent advertising. Even hiring a PR firm or doing it yourself is a bargain
- **Krispy Kreme marketing lesson**: (1) Excellent product from 1937 French chef recipe locked in fireproof vault. (2) Clever, memorable name — represents crispy outside and soft creamy middle. (3) Unique experience — watching doughnuts being made on conveyor belt. (4) Manipulate supply and demand — when entering new market, give local press plenty of warning, give out free samples, build anticipation for months before single store opens. Lesson: create unique product, memorable name, market creatively, generate publicity, create buzz

### How to Get Publicity
- Give the editor/producer a NEWS HOOK — they're in the news business, not promotions. Couch your desires as news
- **Two ways**: (1) Hire a public relations firm — they have expertise and contacts. Word of mouth is best for finding one; compare results not just fees. (2) Do it yourself — draft and send a press release
- **Press Release Tips**:
  - Send to editor BY NAME (who covers your industry) and also to the reporter BY NAME
  - Use a catchy headline
  - Make it newsworthy — "Dog Bites Man" is not newsworthy; "Man Bites Dog" is. Be unique, creative
  - Do not sell — inform
  - Keep it short and sweet — no more than 500 words
  - One page explaining who, what, where, when, and how of your newsworthy story
  - Intent: capture editor's attention, make them want to know more, assign a reporter to interview you

### Getting Traffic
- Most small businesses find the Internet frustrating — spent money on a site but it's basically an e-billboard that does little to drive sales. Problem: people don't know you're out there

### Getting Listed on Search Engines and Directories
- Search ENGINE = tool compiling computerized results. Search DIRECTORY = inquiry result created by humans listed by category. Yahoo! homepage = directory; Yahoo! search tool = engine
- **Two methods**: (1) Do it yourself — submit URL to different search engines, follow up. Submission doesn't guarantee high ranking, just that the engine knows your site exists. (2) Use automated service — submitit.com submits to up to 400 engines and periodically resubmits. Also submissionpro.com

### Submission Tips
- What really drives traffic = your ranking and being listed in directories. Being on page 3 of results doesn't help; top 10 ranking does
- Create a list of 25+ words describing your site, including 3 key words people would use to find you. Example: athletic shoe store in Boston → "athletic shoes Boston"
- **Paid submissions** — wisest course is to submit to Yahoo! first. $300/year flat fee for commercial listings. You WILL get listed and linked, which drastically increases chances of being found by Google. Google's web crawler automatically searches for links — more sites linking to yours = better. A Yahoo! listing and link starts this process
- If you can't afford Yahoo!, work on getting reciprocal links with other sites. Google may then find you anyway
- **LookSmart** — also worth paying for (listings distributed to MSN Search). Free submission via Zeal.com (owned by LookSmart, volunteer editors)

### Crawlers
- Google is the most important search engine — uses web crawlers to find and rank sites. Crawlers look for links — the more linking to your site, the better
- Submit to Inktomi (provides MSN Search results, <$50) and AllTheWeb (powers Lycos), Teoma (powers Ask Jeeves), and Alta Vista
- Crawler-based engines have three elements: (1) "Spider" visits websites monthly to read pages, (2) info goes into master index/catalogue, (3) "search engine software" sifts through index to match queries and rank results

### Higher Rankings
- Different sites use different algorithms analyzing words on homepage and links to/from site
- Two things: optimize your site for search engines, and spend some money

**Get Your Site Ready**
- Search engines rank on three things: (1) keywords at/near top of homepage, (2) frequency of keywords elsewhere, (3) links to and from site
- Don't spam by writing "athletic shoes Boston" 100 times — that will get you a LOW ranking. Link analysis plays a large role
- **Pick keywords carefully** — the words people will use to find your site. Each page needs keywords reflecting its content
- **Keyword placement** — put in HTML "meta tags" and "title tags" at top of each page code. Title tag = what people see when they bookmark your page. Work with webmaster to ensure keywords are in meta/title tags. Page titles should use keywords you want associated with that page
- **Have specific pages** — create pages with HTML content and keyword placement specific enough to catch a spider's eye
- **Get linked up** — other sites linking to you is one of the best ways to get better rankings. Expect the process (submission to listing) to take several weeks
  - TIP: Search for your keywords, visit top-ranked sites, email asking about link exchanges

**Buying Good Rankings**
- Can't buy a high ranking, but can buy exceptional placement. Google shows boxed ads to the right; Lycos shows paid results above actual results
- Pay per click (~25 cents per person who sees your ad and clicks) or flat monthly fee
- Great way to end up on the first page of results even without a top organic ranking

## Pages 231-235: Chapter 24 — Getting People to Buy / E-commerce

### Getting People to Buy
- Jeff Bezos (Amazon founder) says the key to online success is "offering the customer something that cannot be replicated in the physical world"
- Amazon started as "The World's Biggest Bookstore" — could list literally more than a million books, something no physical store could do. Offered something unique, driving people to the site
- **Case study — Speakermatch.com (Bryan Caplovitz)**: Started as a physical speaker's bureau matching speakers with meeting planners. Created a website. Had Big Idea based on Bezos' dictum — "you have to offer something unique to succeed online." Realized speakers' frustration (finding gigs is time-consuming) and planners' frustration (finding the right speaker is time-consuming) could be solved online. Built Speakermatch.com = "the Monster.com for the speaking industry." Meeting planners list events/needs for free; speakers pay monthly fee to browse and pursue leads
- **Two pearls of wisdom from Caplovitz**:
  1. Smartest thing: create an opt-in subscription newsletter. Visitors opting in = prequalifying themselves as interested in your services. They've given you their email = powerful marketing tool. Newsletter should provide valuable content but also pitch new products/services
  2. Dumbest thing: buying an opt-in list from a broker. "It's a waste of money"
- Lesson: offer something unique, not easily duplicated in the physical world, that serves a market need

### Drop Shipping
- "Drop shipping" = arrangement between you and a wholesaler/distributor where you virtually stock your online store with their products and take orders. You inform the wholesaler of sales, they ship the merchandise under your label. You're a middleman (though no one knows that)
- **Example**: Athletic shoe site carries "The Best Tube Sock Ever" — package of 10 for $44 + $6 S&H. Customer buys, you email the tube sock manufacturer (often automatically), they ship under your label, bill you $35. PayPal deposits $50 in your account. You make $15 per order, all you did was take the order
- **Benefits**: No labor for shipping/handling, can stock name brands, easily add/delete items, no inventory to buy
- **Finding drop shippers**: Ask the manufacturer first. If they don't drop ship, find a distributor (business with inventory that distributes). Thomas Register (thomasregister.com) = comprehensive resource for finding manufacturers
- **When evaluating wholesaler/distributor**: Compare wholesale vs retail prices, shipping charges, billing terms, return/refund policies
- Avoid companies that want to CHARGE YOU to sell their products. Choose quality products that reinforce your image and afford healthy profit margin

### eBay
- If you have products to sell and don't need drop shipping, eBay is one of the best, easiest, least expensive ways to create another profit center
- People sell ~12 million items on eBay every day, generating ~$40 million in sales
- Most popular items: collectibles, books, movies, music, furniture, cars, sports memorabilia. But almost anything can sell — "from a pig farm to a rubber ball"
- Old dictum: buy low, sell high. If you can buy at a cheap price, reselling for a healthy profit is not that difficult on eBay
- **eBay Strategies**:
  - Set a proper, hidden "reserve price" (minimum your auction will accept) to ensure profit on every sale
  - Focus on customer service — answer questions promptly, stand behind your product, ship promptly
  - **Use photos** — people want to see what they're buying
  - **Write a great headline** — headlines grab attention
  - **Be thorough** — full product descriptions make a big difference
  - **Accurately estimate shipping costs** — overcharging or undercharging both hurt you

### Email Marketing

### Chapter 25: Exodus — Exit Strategies

**Succession Planning**
- Succession planning = process of identifying and grooming replacements for the CEO/president and possibly key managers
- Should be part of any small business plan — you owe it to employees, vendors, shareholders, investors who depend on you
- **Cautionary tale — Marty (flooring chain owner)**: Created what he thought was a foolproof succession plan. Died at 50. Will gave right-hand man 25% and the job of running it; remaining 75% split equally among 3 children (two still minors). Oldest son wanted to run the business but the will gave that to the employee. Employee resented giving 75% of profits to Marty's kids. Eventually children sold their shares to the employee. Marty's master plan and dream "prematurely died" too
- **Three questions for a succession plan**:
  1. What is your vision for the future of the business in your absence?
  2. Who can best implement that vision?
  3. What plan ensures the vision is carried out?
- **Key decisions**: Do you want the business to continue? Would a partner's death end the partnership? (Legally, death of a partner ends the partnership unless otherwise agreed.) In an LLC or corporation, death does NOT ipso facto end the business
- **Memorialization**: Put the plan in TWO places: (1) the operating agreement for the business, (2) your will or living trust (if ownership shares are being transferred)
- **Choosing a successor**: Look for skills, personality, leadership ability, intelligence — NOT a clone of yourself. Consider giving candidates a self-assessment test. If it's your son/daughter, discover whether they actually WANT to become the boss
- **Jack Welch**: In 1991, GE Chairman said the thing that preoccupied his mind most was succession planning — this was NINE YEARS before he stepped down
- Put your succession plan in writing to avoid ambiguity

**Selling Your Business**
- Business sales are complicated, time-consuming transactions — can take a year or more
- **Line Up Your Ducks**:
  - **Increase curb appeal**: Paint what needs painting, fix what's fixable, clean up, get the place ready to show (like selling a home)
  - **Get a Business Valuation**: Many owners overestimate their business value, especially goodwill. Pay for a professional valuation early so you know what to expect. Value based on: profitability, goodwill, assets, liabilities
  - **Get Your House in Order**: Buyers want to reduce risk — they look at your books. All records (P&L statements, tax returns, contracts, permits, leases) must be in order
  - **Boost Your Profits**: People buy profitable businesses. Increase sales and profits before selling
  - **Speak with Your Advisors**: Attorney and accountant may know potential buyers and should review the deal

**Hiring a Business Broker**
- Two options: sell yourself or hire a broker
- **If selling yourself**, advertise in: classifieds (Business Opportunities section), trade magazines (backs often have "business for sale" sections), online (search "business for sale")
- **Why hire a broker**: Like selling a home without a real estate agent — you lose access to expertise, contacts, listing services. Brokers have listing services and associates to find buyers. They separate serious buyers from "looky-loos" and usually garner a better price
- Brokers are paid a percentage of the sales price
- **What to look for in a broker**: (1) Experience in business valuation, sales, financing, negotiations; (2) Certification — look for Certified Business Intermediary (CBI) from International Business Brokers Association (IBBA); (3) Services provided — should value the business, negotiate the deal, and serve as overall financial advisor

**Pricing Your Business — Four Key Questions**:
1. **What does the business own?** Assets include trucks, equipment, contracts, intellectual property, goodwill (community reputation)
2. **What does the business owe?** Liabilities offset value — less is more
3. **What is the business's profitability?** Bigger profit = bigger price
4. **What about intangibles?** Great location, favorable lease, valuable employees all add value

## Pages 256-275: Chapter 25 (cont.) — Business Valuation & Bankruptcy / Chapter 26 — Hiring and Firing / Chapter 27 — Employee Benefits

### Business Valuation Methods (Three Ways to Price)
1. **Price Building**: Looks at hard numbers — assets, profits, accounts receivable, goodwill, leases. Example: Real estate $400K + Equipment $100K + Inventory $50K + Goodwill $50K = $600K subtotal, less $100K liabilities = **$500K total**
2. **Return on Investment (ROI)**: Looks at profit per year to determine buyer's return. Example: $200K net profit on $500K sale price = 40% ROI. Since a good passive investment returns ~10%, a 40% active return may be high — suggesting a higher price is warranted
3. **Multiplier**: Most common method. Look at earnings and multiply by a factor that varies by industry. A factor of 3 on $200K earnings = $600K asking price. "The battle is what that factor should be"
- Referenced book: *How to Sell Your Business* by C.D. Peterson

### Bankruptcy
- Author spent many years as a bankruptcy lawyer
- Key insight: "Most of the law is about retribution, except bankruptcy." Bankruptcy is about FORGIVENESS — "it forgives your debts, forgives your mistakes, and gives you a chance for a 'fresh start'" (phrase comes straight from the bankruptcy code)
- **Two pieces of good news**: (1) Forgiveness is rare in law — bankruptcy offers a genuine fresh start; (2) As soon as you file, the court issues an "automatic stay" — a federal court order sent to ALL creditors instructing them to leave you alone. No more calls, threatening letters, or lawsuits (all halted regardless of how far along)

**Four Types of Bankruptcy** (each "Chapter" is a chapter in the bankruptcy code):
1. **Chapter 7** ("straight bankruptcy" / "liquidation"): 95% of all bankruptcies. Quick (4 months for individuals), inexpensive. Almost all debts "discharged" (wiped out). BUT for a business, Chapter 7 shuts you down — operations cease, trustee sells assets for creditors' benefit. Most business and personal debts discharged if sole proprietorship or partnership
2. **Chapter 11**: Business reorganization. When you hear a company "emerged from bankruptcy" = Chapter 11. Existing management continues to run the business but bankruptcy court approves significant decisions. Creditors appointed to a committee to develop a reorganization plan. Company may pay 10 cents on the dollar or 100 cents — it varies. Takes several years
3. **Chapter 12**: For farmers specifically
4. **Chapter 13**: Mini Chapter 11 for smaller companies. Reorganization plan for sole proprietorships and small businesses that want to keep doors open

## PART V: Dealing with People

### Chapter 26: Hiring and Firing

**Employee vs. Independent Contractor**
- Tempting to hire people and call them independent contractors because obligations are minimal:
  - Rarely need to provide worker's comp insurance
  - Don't need to match unemployment insurance
  - Most importantly: don't pay any portion of their Social Security or Medicare taxes
- **Two key distinctions**: (1) Employees follow directions; independent contractors decide HOW, WHEN, and WHERE they work. You can approve/disapprove the project but cannot control the process. (2) Employees are exclusive to one company; independent contractors must offer services to the public at large and often work for several businesses simultaneously
- **WARNING**: If you call someone an independent contractor but they're really an employee, consequences are severe — back taxes, interest, penalties to the federal government, money owed to employee for lost benefits, trouble with state labor department. "Taking this shortcut is a mistake to avoid"

**Hiring Employees — Three Steps**:
1. **Preparation**: Write a clear job description (duties, hours, responsibilities). Determine required schooling, experience, people skills. Create an employment application
2. **Finding Qualified Applicants**: Beyond classifieds and Monster.com, consider: temp agencies (can "test-drive" before committing), seniors (excellent work ethic), state employment agencies (may prescreen for you), colleges (inexpensive, smart part-timers)
3. **Interview**: Discover responsibility, why they left last job, ability to take directions, if ever fired, why they want THIS job, qualifications. Don't overlook personality and compatibility. Cannot discriminate based on race, gender, color, national origin, religion, finances, or disabilities — stick to job-related questions

**Coachability** — an often-overlooked hiring factor:
- "Coachability is the ability of an employee to take directions and make changes, to listen and adjust, to think and respond"
- Analogy: Running a small business is like running a sports franchise — good teammates make everyone better, bad teammates hog the ball. Uncoachable employees can ruin your team
- Look for employees who listen, take constructive criticism, try new things, are adaptable and positive

**The New Employee — Agreements to Sign**:
- Noncompete agreement
- Nondisclosure agreement (NDA) for trade secrets
- Assignment of inventions / "work for hire" agreement (for creative work)
- **Employment contract** (optional but recommended): Must clearly state "nothing in this agreement is intended to guarantee employment or alter the fact that [name] is an at-will employee." Should cover: compensation (base salary, bonus/commission benchmarks), detailed job description, benefits, stock options (if any), arbitration clause, immigration status verification
- **Employee handbook**: Covers workplace safety, antidiscrimination, complaint procedures, discipline, sick leave, vacation policies. Should reiterate at-will status
- **FMLA**: If 50+ employees, must comply with Family and Medical Leave Act — 12 weeks unpaid leave per 12-month period for birth/adoption, family health issues, or personal serious health issue

**Learning from New Employees**:
- New employees see things entrenched employees miss
- After a few months, give them a survey: describe your job, most important things you do, best/worst parts, what you'd change, does the job match hiring expectations, suggestions for hiring process improvements

**Firing — "It Just Isn't Working Out"**:
- **Avoid probationary periods**: Implies different rules apply before and after — you want ONE set of rules from day one to year ten
- **Don't fire immediately** when things go wrong — document problems first. Create a paper trail: written letters explaining what they're doing wrong, what to fix, have them sign it
- **7-point pre-termination checklist**:
  1. Is the problem the employee or your business procedures?
  2. Have you documented transgressions in writing?
  3. If you have a grievance/complaint procedure, was it followed?
  4. Have similar employees been treated similarly? (Disparate treatment = litigation)
  5. If employee is in a protected class (handicapped, minority, older), is the termination reason valid and documented?
  6. Is the employee being fired in retaliation for exercising a legal right?
  7. Does the employee have a contract (written or implied) for continued employment?
- **Timing**: Best midweek in the morning; late Friday afternoon also works. Do it in their office or a conference room, gently, briefly, with dignity. Have security nearby if concerned about violence
- **At-will vs. just cause**: At-will employees can be fired for any legal reason. Just cause employees (with long-term contracts, like tenured teachers) can only be terminated for just cause (illegal behavior, excessive absenteeism, insubordination, sexual harassment, serious incompetence). Firing a just cause employee requires investigation, notice, and hearings
- **After termination**: Change locks/access codes if needed. Write a disengagement letter covering housecleaning matters
- Cannot fire for: discrimination, retaliation, or if they have a long-term contract

### Chapter 27: Employee Benefits Demystified

**Required Benefits** (by law):
- Pay at least minimum wage
- Provide worker's compensation insurance
- Withhold and match FICA taxes
- Pay unemployment taxes
- Max 40 hours/week or pay overtime (unless exempt employees)
- Time off for military service, jury duty, voting

**NOT Required** (but important for attracting talent):
- Bonuses, health insurance, paid vacations, sick leave, retirement plans, stock options, life insurance, holidays

**Bonuses — Two Structures**:
1. **Individual benchmarks**: Sliding-scale bonus as employee hits each goal. Works best for sales staff
2. **Company-wide goals**: Communicate goals to all employees (sales targets, reduced overhead, less shrinkage). Pool of bonus money fills as goals are hit; divided equally at year-end. Advantages: teaches employees about business, gives everyone a stake, creates teamwork, makes employees feel invested
- **Shrinkage** = reduction in inventory from accounting error, employee theft, shoplifting, administrative error, vendor fraud

**Stock Options and Ownership**:
- Great, successful small businesses often give employees ownership stakes — makes them more motivated, dedicated, conscientious
- Three types of stock ownership plans:
  1. **Stock option plan**: Employee gets option to buy company stock at specified price within a certain time. ~10 million employees in business hold stock options
  2. **ESOP (Employee Stock Ownership Plan)**: Like a 401(k) but funds invested in employer's stock. Company contributes cash to buy its own stock, shared among employees. Significant tax benefits. ~8 million employees invest in ESOPs
  3. **ESPP (Employee Stock Purchase Plan)**: Employees buy stock at a discount (usually ~15%), can sell for profit or hold
- National Center for Employee Ownership: 510-208-1300, NCEO.org

**Retirement Plans**:
- **401(k)**: Most popular employer-sponsored plan. Tax-deductible contributions for employers, tax-deferred for employees. Participation optional. Must decide whether to match employee contributions (expensive but tax deductible). More investment options = more expensive administration
- 401(k) administration costs at least $1,000/year (reports filed with IRS, Dept. of Labor, plan participants)
- To set up any tax-deferred retirement plan, speak with a financial planner or accountant

**Health Insurance**:
- Employees consistently rank health insurance as the most important employer-provided benefit
- Small businesses face frightening rate increases with no ability to absorb them like large businesses can
- **First step**: Shop around. Use ehealthinsurance.com to compare plans. Insurance broker can shop many providers; agent works exclusively with one company
- **Health plan options**:
  - **Traditional Health Insurance**: Most flexible, most expensive. Employees pick any doctor/specialist. Must meet deductible first, cap on out-of-pocket expenses
  - **HMO (Health Maintenance Organization)**: Directs employees to providers within system. Primary care physician is gateway. Small co-pays, deductibles usually not required
  - **PPO (Preferred Provider Organization)**: See any provider, but higher co-pays for out-of-network doctors
  - **POS (Point of Service)**: Like HMO with small co-pay, encouraged to stay in-network. Going out-of-network requires meeting deductible plus paying percentage of fee
  - **HSA (Health Savings Account)**: Like an IRA for medical care. Two parts: (1) health insurance for expensive hospital bills, (2) tax-free contributions to interest-bearing investment account. Withdrawals tax-free for medical care; if not used, accumulates with tax-free interest until retirement
  - **Health Purchasing Alliance**: Join alliance of many health plans, jointly administered. All competing for your dollars = better prices and options
- Check if your business qualifies for discounted healthcare through Chambers of Commerce or trade associations
- **Cost reduction alternatives**: Cut dental/vision coverage (unpleasant but keeps core coverage), increase employee co-pays (higher co-pay = lower premium), increase prescription co-pays

**Holidays, Sick Leave, and Vacation**:
- Per Fair Labor Standards Act, you are NOT required to give paid time off for holidays, sick days, or vacation — these are benefits, not rights
- **Traditional method**: Set number of sick days, vacation days, holidays per year. New employees typically get ~16 days/year total, split between vacation and sick days. Most employees expect a paid week or two off per year
- Standard paid holidays: New Year's, Memorial Day, July 4th, Labor Day, Thanksgiving, Christmas Eve/night. Religious holidays = allow time off without pay or use vacation days. Most small businesses allow ~4 days paid bereavement
- **Innovative option — Pooled time off**: Instead of separate categories, pool ALL time off into a bank of hours. Example: instead of 11 holidays + 5 vacation days, give 100 hours/year to use however they want. Benefits: promotes honesty, respects employees as adults, employees responsible for their own choices. Some employers let employees who bank 75+ hours cash out extra at current pay rate
- **Flex-holiday plan**: Create list of 25+ major holidays (everything from Christmas to St. Patrick's Day to Kwanzaa), employees select any 11. Respects employees as adults

**Intangible Benefits**:
- Family-friendly policies (leave for family commitments, welcome children at work, job-sharing for new moms)
- Employees appreciate innovative policies as much as expensive benefits packages — and they cost much less

## Pages 276-295: Chapter 28 — Training and Motivating Your Staff

### Training
- Key insight: Spending time working IN your business (day-to-day minutiae) vs. ON your business (big picture). Training and motivating employees ensures your plans and vision are carried out even when you're busy
- **Case study — Nancy Clark (dance apparel stores)**: Owns two retail locations, can't be at both places at once. Spends up to a month training new employees before letting them work independently. Training during regular store hours covers everything from opening/closing procedures to merchandise knowledge to operating the cash register. Trains "from the ground up" so employees can handle anything. "Thorough training creates knowledgeable staff, and that in turn creates a well-run business"

**Day-One Training Should Cover**:
- Philosophy: Your way of doing business
- Brand: What your business is about, what you want the company "personality" to be
- Policies: Overtime, vacation, sexual harassment, workplace safety
- Operations: Alarm, lights, point-of-sale system, computer, restocking shelves, handling complaints
- Expectations: What you expect of them

**Sales Training** (most important and hardest area):
- Attitude: Should staff be friendly, chatty, professional, reserved, aggressive, or nearly invisible?
- Products: Product line knowledge, how it ties into business goals, how it fits customer needs
- Money: How to handle cash, credit cards, checks
- Operations: Returns, refunds, etc.
- Best tip: Have new employees shadow your best salesperson for a few days
- **Case study — Long & Levit (law firm)**: New lawyers got essentials training plus one hour daily in basic civil litigation workshop. Taught them not just how to be good employees but how to be better lawyers — accelerated their contribution to the firm

**Ongoing Training**:
- *Inc.* magazine: "Make 'train to retain' your company mantra"
- Benefits of ongoing training: stronger workforce, loyalty, stronger "bench" for internal promotions, reduces layoffs (additional training may solve problems vs. hiring from scratch)
- **KPMG example**: "Our training doesn't stop between the major events of an individual's career." Programs include technical updates, industry conferences, enabling skills training
- Popular ongoing training: computer skills, financial management, technical training, career path training — none need cost a fortune
- **California Chamber of Commerce survey** of 100 most successful small businesses: Asked "the real key to business success is..." Options were hard work/perseverance, fine products/service, advertising, knowing business fundamentals, and employees. Most popular answer: **Employees**

**Training Managers — Four Traits**:
1. **Listen**: Ernest Hemingway: "Most people never listen." If managers listen to employees, employees will listen to managers. Respect breeds respect
2. **Ask**: No one likes being told what to do. Ask for help instead of barking orders — especially as employees get older
3. **Be reasonable**: Better results with honey than vinegar
4. **Prioritize**: Great leaders set priorities and enlist people to accomplish them
- Referenced book: *The One Minute Manager* by Ken Blanchard & Stuart Johnson — one-page goals for each employee (top 20% of tasks), one minute behavior-based praise, one minute behavior-based reprimands

**Training Techniques** (beyond boring lectures):
- **Shadowing**: Send people into action with trained employees
- **Videos**: People recall what they see — teach selling, opening/closing, spotting theft, motivation
- **Role-playing**: Staff take turns being the thief, reluctant customer, irritated patron
- **The Internet**: Online training is booming, convenient, and low-cost
- **Review**: Staff contribute botched sales calls or success stories as examples

### Motivation

**Money Motivators**:
- The possibility of making more money transforms an employee into an entrepreneur — "If I implement that plan, we could increase sales by 10 percent!"
- Link pay to performance (commissioned salespeople). Link bonuses to desired outcomes (director of operations gets bonus for reducing overhead by 10%, manager gets 10% of increased revenue)
- **Key rule**: Reward must be linked to an outcome the employee can CONTROL. Don't reward operations director for increased sales (they can't affect that) — reward for what they CAN affect (overhead)
- **Contests**: Build excitement. Successful contests use realistic/achievable goals, limited time period, desirable prizes, link rewards to performance, uncomplicated rules

**Motivation on a Shoestring** (without money):
- **Gallup Organization findings**: Employees are unmotivated when they don't know what's expected, feel stagnant, don't feel appreciated, jobs become boring/routine, bosses are clueless, employer seems to care more about money than people
- First step: ENGAGE them. Learn what excites them workwise. Individualized approach is the opposite of what most managers do (notice problems and fix them — motivates no one)
- **Show appreciation**: Pat on the back, call from the president, special parking spot, night out, increased territory. FedEx inscribes names of employees' special children on the nose of new planes
- **Recognize publicly**: Minnesota Dept. of Natural Resources survey — 68% of employees said being appreciated was important to job satisfaction. Blanchard Training (Escondido, CA) reprints customer/manager praise in company newsletter
- **Ask for input**: Listening to employee ideas makes them feel like part of a team. Grumman Corporation (NY) gives gift certificates for implemented suggestions. Fel-Pro (Skokie, IL) has $1,000 yearly drawing for all suggestion program participants
- **Offer freebies**: Afternoon off, gift certificate to Nordstrom's, tickets to a game. H.B. Fuller Company (St. Paul) gives paid birthday off. Mary Kay Cosmetics gives birthday girl a lunch voucher for two
- **Make it a special place to work**: Massage therapist every other week for 15-minute back massages, in-house yoga class, yearly picnic with families, rafting trip. These cost little but are appreciated enormously
- **Michael LeBoeuf's Top Ten Rewards** (from *The Greatest Management Principle in the World*): (1) money, (2) recognition, (3) time off, (4) ownership shares, (5) favorite work, (6) promotion, (7) freedom, (8) personal growth, (9) fun, (10) prizes

### Creating an Exceptional Culture
- Training and motivation only take you so far — ultimately, the CULTURE of your business matters most
- Negative culture = employees feel unappreciated, unharmonious workplaces, incompetent bosses
- Positive culture = sense of direction/mission, teamwork, cooperation, fun. Reflects your goals and ideals. Gives work meaning, gives employees direction, boosts profits

**Mission Statement**:
- Must be real and believed in (not corporate-retreat wallpaper)
- Tells you, employees, and customers what the business is really about and where it's headed
- Questions to create one: personal values, qualities/characteristics, resources, niche, grand vision ("think BIG!"), purpose, personal qualities to infuse, how to serve clients/family/employees/investors, markets, customers, commitment level
- Draft 50-400 words. "Make it large, bold, and fantastic, something you believe in with all of your heart"
- Source: Burt Dubin, Speaking Success System

**Six Steps to Create a Valuable Business Culture**:
1. **Have clear goals and values and LIVE by them** — get the entire team to buy in daily
2. **Communicate** — quarterly state-of-business reports, one-on-one career meetings. Let employees hear what's going on, know where things are headed, offer feedback and suggestions
3. **Make employees feel part of a team** — go to games together, help a charity. McCormick & Company (Baltimore): employees encouraged to work one extra Saturday/year for charity of choice, donate their pay, company matches dollar for dollar. 90% of employees participate
4. **Reward your staff** — profit sharing or gift certificates, big or small
5. **Demand excellence** — demand the best of yourself and others. Have fun. Do your best
6. **Have fun** — "The whole point of leaving the corporate world and striking out on your own is to create a business that you love." Workplaces where employees are encouraged to do their work AND have fun are more productive. Referenced book: *Fish! A Remarkable Way to Boost Morale and Improve Results* by Stephen C. Lundin
- Beware negative influences: bored, berated, unchallenged, unhappy employees can sabotage your business — they need to "get on board or get off the bus"

## Pages 296-315: Chapter 29 — Extraordinary Sales and Exemplary Customer Service / PART VI: Franchises / Chapter 30 — Selecting the Right Franchise

### Chapter 29: Extraordinary Sales and Exemplary Customer Service

**Sales Secrets**
- Zig Zigler: "Every sale has five basic obstacles: No need, no money, no hurry, no desire, no trust"
- **Brian Tracy anecdote**: World's best salesperson, first sales job selling soap door-to-door to earn YMCA camp money. Heard rejection after rejection until he slightly rephrased his pitch — instead of "Would you like to buy a box of soap?" he said he was selling soap but "it was only for beautiful women." Getting to camp was a breeze after that. Smallest sales secret can make the biggest difference

**Rapport**:
- Once established, makes sales almost effortless — removes the trust obstacle
- Build rapport the old-fashioned way: be your best self, find things in common, be friendly and helpful. "People rarely buy from someone they do not like and, conversely, will go out of their way to buy from someone they do"
- One successful saleswoman: "Your job is to create rapport, inform them about the product, avoid pressuring them, let them make an intelligent decision, and if they leave before buying, make sure they have everything they need (your phone number, e-mail address, brochures) so they can easily make the purchase later on"
- Focus on CUSTOMER NEEDS. They're there for a reason — they have needs/wants they think you can fill. If you focus on fulfilling those needs, the sale becomes exponentially easier
- Tom Hopkins: "The human body has two ears and one mouth. To be good at persuading or selling, you must learn to use those natural devices in proportion. Listen twice as much as you talk"
- **Question types**: Yes/no questions add nothing. Price/technical questions don't help either. Open-ended questions about needs and motivations DO help. Follow-up questions requiring more explanation help even more

**Go the Extra Mile**:
- It costs much more to create a new customer than keep an old one — foster relationships with current clientele
- Study trends in their industry, know a bit about their competitors — impresses customers and keeps them around
- **Pickle jar analogy**: Getting the first pickle out takes work, but once it's out, the rest come easily. Same with sales — first sale is hardest, subsequent sales with that customer are much easier
- Story of sports agent who flew cross-country just for a brief meet-and-greet with a potential client. The sports star was so impressed by the personal service that he signed up
- It can take up to SIX interactions to close a sale
- Follow up with thank-you notes; check if the product is working out

**Cold Calls**:
- Begin with the right attitude — competitive spirit required
- Smile before calling (puts you in the right frame of mind)
- It's a numbers game — smile and dial
- If they balk: "All I need is 10 minutes of your time. I wouldn't waste my time coming all the way out to see you for 10 minutes if I didn't think it would really benefit us both"
- Be specific: Don't say "Can we meet next week?" Say "How is next Thursday at 11:00 for you?"

**Golf Lessons** (sales paradox):
- The harder you try in golf, the worse you do. Same in sales — customers can sense when a sale is your priority and back off. Stop trying so hard. Focus on helping them solve their problem, not on making the sale

**Solve Problems**:
- "Benefits, not features, is Sales 101." When customers buy a drill, they don't want a drill — they want a HOLE. Focus on the benefit (the hole), not the feature (aluminum casing)
- Once customers see your product solves their problem, next question is "Why your product and not someone else's?" Have several reasons ready — less expensive, faster, more convenient, higher quality

**Sales Secrets of the Car Salesperson** (four-step process):
1. **Build trust/rapport**: Nice, friendly. Ask seemingly innocent questions that reveal: whether you're ready to buy, how much you'll spend, how much you know
2. **Get you excited**: Walk-around focusing on benefits she's concluded matter to you (safety, color, roominess). Invites you to sit in the car — "taking mental ownership" (real estate equivalent: "falling under the ether")
3. **Get you emotionally invested**: Test drive. Excitement makes you apt to make an emotional rather than logical decision. "A $20,000 decision based upon a 10-minute test drive is illogical. Yet it happens every day"
4. **Create urgency**: "The boss wants this car off the lot," "Someone is coming by this afternoon to look at it again." Incentive to buy NOW
- The "talking to the manager" step: Usually just discussing strategy to raise your offer. Manager becomes the authority figure/closer
- Lesson: Add a "closer" to your own sales process

**The Sales Presentation** (five-fold process):
1. Create rapport
2. Introduce the topic or product
3. Ask questions to see what they need
4. Highlight benefits
5. Ask for the sale
- Preparation is key: organize, prepare selling points, consider possible objections
- Build rapport in first few minutes by asking audience questions they can easily answer
- "There is always a solution" should be your motto
- **Two closing techniques**: (1) One-time incentive: "If you want to do this today, I will throw in an extra hour of my time for free." (2) 100% money back guarantee

**Sales Meetings**: Have an agenda, don't ramble. Start by congratulating/thanking. Avoid chastising. Keep upbeat. Include at least one positive war story. Ask for opinions and customer feedback. Consider bringing in a customer to explain why they buy from you. Keep short — more than an hour and they fade out

### Customer Service
- Most small businesses give lip service to customer service but few truly incorporate it
- **The repeat customer math**: Coffeehouse customer who comes once and spends $5 = $5/year. Customer who comes 3x/week spending $10/week = $520/year. The repeat customer is your most valuable customer and costs little to keep
- Michael LeBoeuf (*How to Win Customers and Keep Them for Life*): "Fancy sales pitches, high-powered marketing strategies, and clever advertising can be very important attention getters... But keeping customers for any period of time depends on how well you reward them"
- Make customer service part of business culture — teach it from day one, reinforce daily
- **Hire a mystery shopper** to see how employees treat customers when you're not around
- **Reward customer service stars**: Restaurant with lobby plaque listing waiters who learned "100 customers' names." Lens Crafters gives bonuses for outstanding service
- **Poll customers**: Hand out surveys about what they like/dislike. Not just about saying yes or handling complaints — it's about making your store the best it can be
- **Just say yes**: When possible, do what the customer asks. Saying yes keeps them around

**Five Commandments of Customer Disservice** (what NOT to do):
1. **Don't put phone calls ahead of real customers**: In-person customers are almost always more important. Teach employees to prioritize face-to-face customers
2. **Don't become a Nopey**: Like Gumby's pal Nopey who said "No!" to everything — employees who reflexively say no anger customers and turn them into ex-customers
3. **Don't be a strict constructionist**: Sometimes the smartest choice is to bend policy and make an exception. Customer will remember it. **Outback Steakhouse example**: Waiters/waitresses can fix problems (free drink, comp a meal) WITHOUT going to their manager. "Bending policy to make customers happy is the norm, not the exception, at Outback"
4. **Don't have bad manners**: Customers shouldn't be thanking you — you should be thanking THEM. Common courtesy goes far
5. **Welcome complaints**: Feedback (positive or negative) is one of the most valuable things you can get. **SBA survey**: 95% of dissatisfied customers would do business again if their problems were solved quickly and satisfactorily. When you get a complaint, ask how they'd prefer it resolved and try to do that. If employee was involved, retrace the problem and retrain. If not at fault, apologize, offer something to placate, explain how to avoid it in the future

## PART VI: Franchises

### Chapter 30: Selecting the Right Franchise

- Ray Kroc (founder of first McDonald's franchise): "In business for yourself, not by yourself"
- Starting from scratch = baking bread from scratch (trial and error, bad batches). Buying a franchise = getting a good recipe right from the start. Trial and error already handled by franchisor — greatly reduces risk
- **Terminology**: Franchisor = company offering its name/system. Franchisee = buyer. Franchise = the business bought

**Pros of Franchising**:
- Buying into a (hopefully) proven system — success methods systematized and teachable. "If you do what they did, you will get the results they got"
- International Franchise Association: www.Franchise.org
- **Critical advice** (repeated throughout chapter): Talk to current and former franchisees. See how they like the system. Discover how much they actually make (vs. franchisor claims). Find out if they'd buy in again knowing what they know now
- Plenty of help — Ray Kroc: "in business for yourself, but not by yourself." Franchisor trains you, other franchisees to learn from, advertising/marketing assistance, benefit of national ad campaigns

**Cons of Franchising**:
1. **Expensive**: You're buying the name, logo, goodwill, expertise, system, and training. Name-brand franchises especially pricey
2. **Less independence**: You agree to run business the franchisor's way, even if you disagree. The ice cream franchisee who doesn't want to give away free cones one summer evening must comply if franchisor says so
3. **Unequal relationship**: Contracts put franchisor in the power position. Many lawsuits from franchisees who feel franchisor overreaches. But there are also plenty of good franchisors

**Researching the Franchise**:
- Start globally, narrow locally. Get *Franchise Handbook* (quarterly, lists most franchises with fees). Visit FranchiseHandbook.com
- Attend franchise expos (only a few of thousands of franchisors will be there, but good face time)
- **Questions for trade show franchisors**: Total investment required? Financing offered? Training/support? Advertising? Can I speak with current franchisees?
- Narrow to industries you like best with most potential. Match skills, interests, and financial capacity. Is there a market in your area?
- Contact franchisors, visit websites, BUT don't rely on their materials alone — find franchisees and talk to them. "*The most important thing you can do in this process is to talk to current and former franchisees*"

**What to Look For in a Franchisor**:
1. **Plenty of support**: Best systems see franchisees as partners. Steve Reinemund (Pizza Hut): "Franchisees are only as successful as the parent company and the parent company is only as successful as the franchisees." Support includes: site selection, lease negotiation, fee financing, extra help during difficulties. **Get all promises of support in writing**
   - Typical support: local/national advertising, field representative feedback, updated operating manuals, continuous training, communication (newsletters, intranets, emails)
2. **Advertising is key**: Superior franchise system helps you get new customers through its ad campaign
3. **Franchisor is flexible**: Rigidity is bad — creativity and independence are part of being an entrepreneur even within a franchise. The Big Mac was invented by a McDonald's franchisee (Jim Delligatti, 1968). Avoid the rigid franchisor
4. **Franchisor keeps up**: Invests in R&D, tests new products and ideas, stays modern as tastes/values change
5. **Solid reputation**: Check with past/current franchisees, do online search, check library for articles, contact Federal Trade Commission for complaints
6. **Fairly large**: Large number of franchises = proven model that works. Smaller/newer franchises are more of a gamble
7. **Franchisee-friendly**: Long-term investment, so pick someone you can work with

- Set up face-to-face meetings with select franchisors. Get a feel for compatibility — don't invest money in a franchisor whose values differ from yours

**The UFOC (Uniform Franchise Offering Circular)**:
- By law, must be presented at least 10 business days before signing any contract or making any payment. Must receive all contracts at least 5 days before signing
- Contains 23 standard items. Key sections:
  - **#2**: Experience/background of directors, officers, managers — "Know thy franchisor!"
  - **#3**: Litigation background. Beware excessive lawsuits, especially from franchisees against the franchisor, or regulatory actions
  - **#4**: Bankruptcies of the business or people in #2
  - **#5, 6, 7, 10**: Money commitment — franchise fees, ongoing royalties, total investment estimate. #10 covers financing availability
  - **#11**: Critical — franchisor's obligations to you (training, etc.)
  - **#19**: Not required by law, but if present, discusses potential profits
  - **#20**: Number of franchisees who entered/left the system recently, plus names, addresses, phone numbers of current and past franchisees. Makes the UFOC very valuable for research
  - **#21**: Three years of audited financial statements — review with your CPA

**Franchise Fees — Four Costs**:
1. **Franchise fee**: What you pay to buy into the system (name, logo, training). Range: $2,500 (small/unknown) to $50,000+ (well-known)
2. **Ongoing royalty**: 3-6% of gross sales per month to franchisor
3. **Advertising fee**: Monthly or quarterly contribution to the advertising pool
4. **Build-out cost** (usually the largest): Building the store per franchisor mandates. Could be minimal or $500,000+. May include: real estate (land, building, security/utility deposits), design fees (architect), construction/remodeling/landscaping, equipment/fixtures (tables, chairs, phones, computers, registers), decor (signs, lights, interior design), inventory, insurance (worker's comp, property, liability), labor costs (staff initial training)
- **Cost examples**: McDonald's: $455,000-$768,500. Subway: $97,000-$222,800. ServiceMaster: $10,200-$52,000

### Chapter 31: Franchise Success Secrets

**Fred Berni Survey** (Dynamic Performance Systems Inc.):
- Surveyed hundreds of franchisees across many industries over several years, 600+ questions covering attitudes, personality traits, skills
- **Key finding**: "Attitudes, not personality, are the best predictors of franchisee performance. Beliefs drive results." Personality and skill-set were important but paled vs. attitudes and values. "Preexisting skill-sets had no predictive value" — because every franchisor trains candidates in the necessary skills

**Five Attitudes/Values of Successful Franchisees** (in order of importance):
1. **Involve their employees in the business**: "The single most important factor in determining franchise success." Successful franchisees see employees as valuable assets (not burdens), manage participatively (not dictatorially), treat employees with decency and respect. This leads to: less turnover, fewer hiring expenses, happier employees, better customer service, more loyal customers, increased profits
2. **Are optimistic**: Positive outlook is a major factor. Valuing employees → happier employees → happier customers → optimistic franchisors spread positivity throughout the business
3. **Are moderately independent**: Surprisingly, not full independence — but franchisees who have ideas, try them out, think for themselves, while still following the system. Balance between following the system and being entrepreneurial is key
4. **Are adept at sales and marketing**: Don't just wait for business or franchisor campaigns. Learn new marketing tricks, network, schmooze, sell, pitch products. Best franchisees take off the "manager hat" — they're out in the store talking to customers, making sales, showing "selling attitude" that employees emulate
5. **Are sociable**: Business is a social game. Being extroverted and gregarious directly affects the bottom line. "Fake it 'til you make it" — staff takes cues from you
- The dictatorial, resentful, restless, or negative franchisee has the least chance of success

**Follow the System**:
- Becoming a franchisor and creating a successful operating system is no small feat. "By the time you bought your franchise, the hood on that system had been up many times, and the engine that is the system had been tinkered with, analyzed, fine-tuned, turbocharged, and perfected"
- "If you do what they did, you should get what they got"

**Be a Jack-of-All-Trades**:
- **Clay Werts (3 Baskin Robbins franchises, 20+ years)**: "If you are a jack-of-all-trades and a master of none, you are most likely to be a success." Running a franchise requires many skills: promotion, management, financial, etc.
- **Cautionary tale**: Couple who owned ice cream store near Werts — husband was king of promotions, wife was queen of decorations. Those were the only two things they did well. They were especially bad at cash management — didn't save summer money for the inevitable winter downturn. Went out of business within a year
- **Werts on location**: "You can stay in business with a great location even if you are not the best businessperson. But if you have a bad location, no matter how good a businessperson you are, your possibilities are inherently limited"
- Werts' final advice: "Think big. If you are an optimist, are willing to take a risk, and work your tail off, anyone can make it"

**Advertising and Marketing**:
- Superior franchisees are proactive about finding new customers — "If it's to be, it's up to me"
- Too often franchisees fail to advertise sufficiently or waste ads on unfocused broad audiences. Target your SPECIFIC audience
- Famous ad executive: Best ad he ever saw read "Farm fresh eggs available today!" — conveyed feeling ("farm fresh"), product ("eggs"), and call to action ("fresh today only, supposedly")
- Know your target market: who they are, what they watch/read, what they buy and why. Then pick the right vehicle to reach them

**Location, Location, Location**:
- McDonald's, Pizza Huts, Taco Bells, Olive Gardens lined up like dominoes on the same street. Food courts packed with competitors side by side. Do they cannibalize each other? Usually NO
- Being close together creates a DESTINATION, building synergy. But to succeed in a cluster, your business has to be very good — competition will eat you alive otherwise
- "Whether your franchise lends itself to a cluster model or not, for the retail franchisee, location makes a big difference"

**Two Types**:

2. Started at home to save money during startup, with intent to move out when economically feasible — earmarks capital for things other than rent and commuting

Disciplined enough to avoid fridge/TV/Internet? 6. Business model works at home? 7. Minimal distractions? 8. Will customers take you seriously? 9. Excited about it? 10. Family support?

### Taking the Plunge — Setting Up the Home Office
- You NEED a separate room — cordoning off space in the living room won't work. Psychologically reinforces you're at work, and required for the home office tax deduction (room must be devoted solely to business)
- Pick a room with: plenty of space, enough electrical outlets, a view, off the beaten path in the house, private
- **Home office equipment needs**: Extra electrical outlets at desk level (consider separate circuit breaker), at least two phone lines (one for phone, one for fax, plus regular home line), DSL or cable internet (consider wireless ~$100)

**Insurance for Home-Based Businesses**:
- Common mistake: thinking homeowner's insurance covers your home business. Some policies specifically EXCLUDE businesses. Review your policy
- Worse mistake: not telling your carrier about the business. If they discover it (computer equipment, inventory, business software are telltale signs), they can legitimately deny business-related claims
- Get a home-based business endorsement that specifically covers your business

### Chapter 33: Successful Home-Based Business Strategies

**Balancing Act — Ground Rules**:
- Direct correlation between success and ability to strike the proper work-home balance
- **Create a schedule and keep to it**: Deviating is the exception, not the rule. Signals to yourself and the world you're really at work
- **Dress appropriately**: Don't make every day casual Friday. Dressing professionally (not in a suit, but not schlubby either) reinforces that your business is real
- **Keep your office separate**: If it doubles as playroom or laundry room, work-home balance will be out of whack
- Others will eventually respect your boundaries if you set them. People who don't work at home "quietly resent those who do" and assume you have lazy afternoons. Ground rules are the antidote. Examples: closed door = do not disturb, 10:00-11:00 AM is phone call time, entrepreneurial friends know they can visit Wednesday/Friday afternoons

**Growth Strategies**:
- **Executive Suites**: Rent by hour, day, week, or month. Fully furnished, secretaries, receptionists, conference rooms, internet, copy machines. Fraction of the cost of a real office. Virtual office option: ~$100-200/month for their address on your stationery, mail receiving, phone answering/forwarding, and a few conference room hours. Regus Business Centers: 400+ locations worldwide
- **Rent the Space You Need**: Continue running business from home while renting space for growth activities. Example: Barsahrin Travis, yoga teacher — held sessions in her living room until classes got too popular, then rented a nearby studio while managing the business from home. You can work from home AND rent space — not either/or
- **Get Help**: Hiring people (employees or independent contractors) is a sign your home business has reached a mature stage. Frees you to concentrate on important matters — stop working IN your business and start working ON it
- **Go Virtual — Virtual Assistants (VAs)**: Independent contractors working from their own home/office. Handle administrative duties: collections, marketing, travel planning, research, accounting, website management, newsletter prep, scheduling. Typically less than $50/hour, pay only for time used. No employment costs/taxes/office space needed. Resources: AssistU (www.assistu.com), International Virtual Assistants Association (www.ivaa.org)

**Adding Profit Centers**:
- Common trap: Business reaches a plateau (making $X/month consistently), entrepreneur rests on their laurels. Stopped being creative and active after finding what works
- Secret to longevity: Continue plotting, planning, implementing new strategies. Add more products, advertise somewhere new, create another niche
- **Best small businesses have multiple profit centers** — many different ways of attracting business. When one slows, others continue
- **Richard Branson / Virgin example**: Started Virgin Records by bartering for space above a shoe shop in London. Record store begat recording studio, begat talent agency, begat airline, cola, phone company, etc. All now called The Virgin Group
- Example franchise profit center: Carlson Travel's SeaMaster Cruise franchise, $5,500-$16,445 to buy in (www.CarlsonTravel.com)

**Getting Paid What You're Worth — Four Steps**:
1. **Decide on a reasonable amount**: Research competitor pricing. Like Goldilocks — not too hot, not too cold, just right (unless your offering is qualitatively different). Consider flat fees: clients love them (know exactly what they'll pay), you have incentive to work faster, no time tracking needed
2. **Give clients reasonable notice**: Advance warning so it's not a shock. Be businesslike, don't apologize. Confidence is key — raising rates is a fact of business. "My fees are still reasonable, and I have not raised them in years. I have to keep up with my overhead"
3. **Test (optional)**: If nervous, try it on a few clients first. If it works, roll out across the board. If not, retreat
4. **Handle resistance**: Give resisters extra TLC. Explain how much you do for them, how much is gratis. Point out switching costs (new provider = time and training). If they still balk, offer a perk — maybe 60 more days at the old price

## PART VIII: Business on a Shoestring

### Chapter 34: The Shoestring Entrepreneur

- Winston Churchill: "I am an optimist. It does not seem too much use being anything else"
- Most startups begin without as much money as owners would have liked — shoestring entrepreneurship puts you in good company
- Three requirements for a tight-budget business: the right attitude, OPM (other people's money), and frugality

**Five Rules of the Road**:
1. **Fortunes have been made on minuscule beginnings**: Lillian Vernon started a multimillion-dollar mail order business with $2,000 from her kitchen table. Peter Hodgson borrowed $137 to buy the goo he'd rename Silly Putty. Arnold Goldstein started his first retail discount store ($100K merchandise) with only $2,600 of his own money. Real estate: 3% FHA loan on a $150K duplex = $4,500 down (97% of the money came from others)
2. **Understand good debt vs. bad debt**: Not all debt is bad. Credit card bills you can't pay = bad debt. Debt that helps you get ahead (start a business, buy a home, finance college) = good debt. Most millionaires started out in debt — but it was good debt
3. **Serve the market**: Your shoestring idea better be a darned good one — you don't often get second chances. Tapping friends and family only works once. Invest only in your best, most commercial idea
4. **Creativity required**: Improvising, making do, borrowing from Peter to pay Paul. Hire students, buy software and learn to design your own website, ask for free help. Must be highly energetic and very creative
5. **You gotta believe**: Northwestern University survey of successful shoestring entrepreneurs — most had never owned a business before, had little business education, didn't have enough money, but started anyway. They knew enough to realize they should have been afraid but went for it. "Shoestring entrepreneurship is for the hearty alone"

**Other People's Money (OPM)**:
- Two types of shoestring operations: (1) No borrowing at all ($500-$2,500, grow incrementally as part-time home businesses — margin for error is slim); (2) Using other people's money
- For OPM: Need a solid business plan with facts and figures, not just rosy rhetoric. Must knock on many doors — it's a numbers game
- **Key to winning investors**: Look like a pro. "Talking big without backup facts will make you look foolish. Be a businessperson. Draft a real business plan. Have your elevator pitch ready. Know your market. Know your numbers inside out. Know thy competition. Be able to defend your plan of action. Explain with conviction why your plan is a great opportunity for the investor"
- **Jay's cautionary tale**: Had a great idea (James Bond-style diamond treasure hunt). Wrote the script, asked friends to act, spent money on a nice brochure... then ran out of money. Put a tiny classified ad in the paper — one person called. Lesson: great idea without adequate funding/marketing = failure

**Locating a Partner**:
- **Chester Carlson / Xerox story**: Inventor by nature, worked in a patent office. Tinkered at home at night, figured out how to reproduce documents electronically. Tried selling the invention to GE, RCA, IBM — no success (he was an inventor, not a salesman). Then Joe Wilson (president of Haloid, a small photo company) heard about it, saw a demo, and said "Of course it's got a million miles to go before it will be marketable. But when it does become marketable, we've got to be in the picture!" Wilson and Carlson became partners — Carlson had the invention, Wilson had the money. Haloid pumped $100 million into the invention. They named the machine and the company **Xerox**
- **Finding partners**: Network with lawyers, accountants, business associates, friends, family, colleagues, people from your place of worship. Talk to suppliers/distributors. Talk to retired people in your field (may want back in or become passive investors). Internet search. Classified ads under "Capital Needed" / "Capital Available"
- Partner sites: businesspartners.com, entremate.com, findyouridealbusinesspartner.com
- **Key**: You get the money only if the partner gets what THEY want. "Ask them what they want and give them what they want" — maybe they want a 50-50 role, or maybe just a monthly cut, or silent investment for equity

**Supplier and Distributor Financing**:
- Suppliers/distributors may lend you money to get started because it turns you into a long-term repeat customer
- They'll want to learn about you, visit your business, check references. Need a solid plan showing how helping you helps their bottom line

### More Shoestring Financing Options

**Franchisor Financing**:
- ~33% of all franchisors offer some type of financing (International Franchising Association)
- Options range widely: interest-only loans, no payment for first year, financing everything, financing just the franchise fee. Some finance 100% (50% is more typical)
- Most franchisors have relationships with lenders — loan guarantees, working capital loans
- Leasing companies often have relationships with franchisors for equipment financing

**Other Options**: Venture capital firms, angel investors, SBA guaranteed loans

**Structuring the Deal**:
- Ask for MORE money than you need — if they negotiate down, you still have enough; if they don't, you have a cushion
- Make sure your COMPANY takes out the loan (you did incorporate, right?)
- Get the interest rate as low as possible — everything is negotiable
- Get as much time as possible to repay, with no prepayment penalty

### Grand Opening — Keeping Expenses Minimal

**Rent**: Avoid high-profile/high-rent locations. Think shoestring: smaller space, out-of-the-way location. Start from home if you must — just don't blow your dough on rent

**Business Incubators**: Public or private nonprofit partnerships that promote entrepreneurship. Provide: inexpensive space, free/cheap administrative assistance, legal help, business planning, financial advice. They nurture ("incubate") a business while it spreads its wings
- Benefits: reduced rent, financial/business expertise, shared services, contacts
- National Business Incubation Association: 614-593-4331, www.nbia.org
- Vary by region: Silicon Valley incubators foster tech startups, Wisconsin ones foster dairy/farming

**Equipment**: Buy used — furniture, equipment, fixtures. Yellow Pages, Internet (eBay), newspaper/magazine classifieds. If you can't find used, many manufacturers finance up to 90% of new purchases. Consider leasing fixtures/equipment to preserve capital

**Inventory — Getting Stock with No Money**:
- Find suppliers who will give goods on credit — they get paid when goods sell
- Prepare a powerful package: business plan, stationery, reference letters, lawyer/banker names — anything that gives legitimacy
- Explain how much inventory you need, the terms you propose, how and when you'll pay
- Contact the credit manager or regional sales manager — good chance of success with a good package, decent credit rating, and trade references
- If they stock your shelves for no money down, agree to continue buying for the term of the loan and grant them a "security interest" in the merchandise (they get it back if you default)
- **Important**: Do NOT agree to buy exclusively from one manufacturer — that should be a deal breaker
- May need multiple suppliers lending small amounts each — either way, shelves stocked without investing capital up front

**Buying a Business with No Money Down**:
- Like buying real estate with leverage — small amount of money into a large purchase, asset secures the loan
- Sellers need buyers. If purchase is contingent on seller financing, they'll often do it (want to secure loan with ability to foreclose if you default)
- Optimum: Combine bank and seller financing. Example: $100K furniture store — bank finances half, seller finances half
- If you're still short (e.g., seller finances 40%, bank matches 40%, need $20K more), creative options:
  - **Debt financing**: Assume seller's business debts, reducing your down payment by that amount
  - **Inventory financing**: Owner liquidates $20K of inventory, reducing purchase price
  - **Broker financing**: Ask broker to reduce commission to keep the deal alive
  - **Supplier financing**: Business's suppliers may loan $20K to keep a major account

### Chapter 35: Marketing on a Shoestring

- Earl G. Graves: "Sell to their needs, not yours"
- Small businesses may envy big budgets, but they have advantages: resourceful, nimble, quick. "The only area where small businesses actually envy big businesses is with regard to their budgets"
- A Wall Street Journal ad might cost a Fortune 500 company $100K. Marketing on a shoestring doesn't need to cost a fortune

**Four Shoestring Marketing Rules**:
1. **Commitment**: You'll try many methods, test them, and add winners to your repertoire. A marketing campaign takes 3-6 months to show results. If three methods net $150K/year, what might six methods do?
2. **Consistency**: Send a consistent message repeatedly. Example: electronics store spent big on a one-week TV campaign that got results for ~2 weeks. Then tried a small, inexpensive weekly ad in the Sunday entertainment section — it became his bread and butter, creating sustained profits
3. **Creativity**: Try many different inexpensive ways. Some will be foreign to you — try them anyway. See which generate the best results
4. **Measurement**: Create benchmarks so you can measure results. Compare sales month-over-month, track calls from flyers, etc.

### Major Media Methods (Shoestring Style)

**Co-Op Advertising**: Manufacturer partially or fully pays for retailer's ad, as long as manufacturer's product/name is mentioned. Author's father had a billboard on the San Diego Freeway: "Carpet World... Elegance Underfoot" with "Featuring Ban-Lon Carpets" at the bottom — Ban-Lon paid for most of it. Manufacturers earmark ~$30 billion annually for co-op advertising, yet much goes unused. Ask your suppliers if they offer co-op funds

**Buy Remnant Space**: Near press time, magazines/newspapers may have unsold ad space ("remnant space") available for a song. Also applies to radio and TV. Contact Media Networks (800-225-3457) for remnant space

**Per Inquiry (PI) / Per Order (PO) Deals**: Station runs your commercial for free; you pay them a percentage of resulting orders/inquiries. Typically station takes ~40% of revenue generated. No upfront cost to you

**Overnight Radio**: Drive time (7-9 AM, 4-7 PM) radio costs $250-$1,000/minute. But overnight spots cost a fraction. Kristy Hicks (radio account executive): many large news/talk stations have nighttime/overnight ratings that rival smaller stations' drive time — at a fraction of the cost. Got a client 60-second spots on a major station for $10/minute. Three elements for effective overnight radio: (1) compelling message, (2) frequency/repetition, (3) consistency (like Motel 6's "We'll leave the light on for you")

**Cable Television**: Broadcast TV is prohibitive, but cable late at night is not. Target specific stations your market watches. Overnight cable ad = as little as $25/spot

**Newspapers**: Cut ad size in half (save 50%), double the headline size. Buy premium placement (~15% extra) and still save 35% with an ad that gets noticed. Advertise only in neighborhoods that buy your goods — save 50%+

**Classified Ads**: Reach people who are shopping and ready to buy. Test one ad; if it works, ask for frequency discount when buying bulk. Snappy headline is key. Rate card is always negotiable

**Yellow Pages**: First-time advertisers should get up to 40% newcomer discount. Trick: advertise for a year, drop out for a year, come back and get newcomer discount again. Consider non-phone-company directories or online-only listings

**Create an In-House Ad Agency**: Media sources give discounts to "insiders" — PR agencies, ad agencies. Create your own in-house agency (just need a checking account and stationery in the agency name) and automatically get 15% discount on all ad buys. Takes an afternoon to set up

**Toll-free numbers** (800, 888, etc.) increase response rates by 30-700% and are very affordable

**Publicity**: Free press releases/press kits cost almost nothing but can get you radio, TV, or newspaper coverage worth its weight in gold. Reproduce that story for years

### Print Techniques
- **e-Newsletters**: Demonstrate expertise in a low-key way; when recipients need your services, they think of you
- **Testimonials**: Ask satisfied clients for testimonial letters on their letterhead. Include in marketing materials. Author's speaking contracts require a letter of recommendation within two weeks — sends them to prospective clients
- **Flyers**: 5,000 flyers at 3 cents each = $150. Hire a student to put them on cars. Emphasize benefits
- **Coupons**: Cost almost nothing. Even unredeemed, they build brand. Must offer at least 15% discount. Add coupons to invoices to sell more to existing customers
- **Gang Run**: Printers batch large jobs together. Buy their unused remnant printing space at a fraction of the cost
- **Personal Letters**: George H.W. Bush wrote 10,000+ thank-you letters — credited with helping him get elected. Write actual letters to current customers with special offers, hire students to address by hand

### Actions (Low-Cost Marketing Tactics)
- **Barter**: Trade goods/services directly or through barter exchanges ("barter bucks"). IRTA: almost half a million small businesses use commercial barter exchanges, generating $10+ billion in sales. Benefits: get rid of excess assets, create new customers, save money
- **Contests**: Generate interest and free publicity. Restaurant example: yearly "eat the most Flaming Chicken Wings" contest — generates clientele interest and local newspaper coverage
- **Networking**: Good elevator pitch creates opportunities from humdrum encounters
- **Demonstrations**: Demo your product at malls, trade shows, high-profile locations. Infomercials = 30-minute demonstrations
- **Samples**: Free samples are loss leaders that create sales (car dealers let you take the car home, gourmet grocers set out free food, Costco samples)
- **Seminars**: Service businesses (doctors, lawyers) use seminars to introduce themselves to potential customers, build rapport. Not the cheapest but very lucrative
- **Become the Expert**: Distinguish yourself from competition, offer something competitors can't, charge more, become the first choice. How? Pick something you're passionate about, devote energy to it, share analysis/ideas publicly. Mark Paul (20 years at Ford, Northrop, patents, consulting) self-published *The Entrepreneur's Survival Guide* — it became his calling card
- Other expert strategies: press releases, TV/radio segments, write articles for trade journals, advertise as "specializing in..."
- **Befriend a Concierge**: Hotel/office building concierges offer services to guests — get on their list
- **Offer Free Consultations**: Usually lead to paying clients
- **Be a Good Citizen**: Sponsor youth sports teams, coach, donate services to charity, sponsor a segment on local public broadcasting

### Chapter 36: Small Business Success Secrets I: Money

**Multiple Profit Centers**:
- **Michael Jackson example** (NY Times, Feb 15, 2004): Despite legal problems and a $70M loan coming due, Jackson wasn't worried because he had about half a dozen income sources. Most valuable: Beatles catalog (~250 songs, co-owned with Sony Music Entertainment, valued at $800M-$900M). His own music catalog: $75M-$90M
- Lesson: Diversify income sources just like stock investors diversify portfolios. All businesses have cycles — if you depend on one or two income sources, there WILL be down times. Multiple profit centers mean that when one is down, others are up
- Photographer specializing in weddings? Add portraits and high school graduations. Wholesale manufacturer? Add retail outlets

**Swimming with the Big Fish — A Board of Advisors**:
- Easy to fall into a comfortable rut. To get to the next level: (1) break the routine, (2) associate with people who can help generate more business and revenue
- **Bob Vukovich** (started two successful businesses, judge at Moot Corporation/UT Austin): "The worst thing you can do as an entrepreneur is to think you know everything. When I need advice, which is very often of a general nature rather than technical, it's very important to have a great board to turn to"
- In 1992, Allegheny College received $22 million from Vukovich and his wife — largest gift in the college's ~200-year history
- A board of advisors expands your business IQ, provides contacts (investors, strategic partners, potential customers), serves as a sounding board, and impresses the outside world
- **Five is a good number.** Look for members who: (1) Have experience — business-savvy heavyweights, not your brother-in-law; (2) Have diverse skills — sales, finance, marketing, legal; (3) Are independent — willing to be the devil's advocate and give honest feedback
- Board members compensated with money, stock, or sometimes just the opportunity to be involved

**Contracts — Going After Bigger Customers**:
- Federal government contracts out $240 billion/year to private business. By law, 23% must go to small businesses = ~$60 billion available
- **Hector Barreto** (SBA head): "What small business wants is the same as what big business wants — more business!" Most small businesses had no idea how to find or bid on government contracts. "They have the know-how but not the know who"
- **Business Matchmaking** (SBA + Hewlett-Packard, 2002): www.businessmatchmaking.com. Traveling program matching small businesses with federal/state/local government agencies AND large private corporations that need small business vendors. Participants include: Air Force, State Dept., Dept. of Treasury, Veterans Affairs, General Dynamics, GlaxoSmithKline, HP, Honeywell, Office Depot, Raytheon, Boeing, General Services Administration, Walt Disney World, Xerox
- Barreto: "In one or two days at one of these events, a small business can see more contractors with real contracts than they could in a year on their own. It's better than a trade show because at our events, buyers who are ready to buy are meeting small businesses ready to sell"

**The System — Michael Gerber's E-Myth**:
- To make more money: organize your business so the owner is free to network, market, go after big contracts, enjoy the business
- The paradox: entrepreneurs start businesses for freedom, but end up spending all their time handling issues and putting out fires — creating another "work prison"
- **Michael Gerber's *The E-Myth***: Business has two parts: (1) the "technical" part (baker bakes bread, web designer makes sites) and (2) the business part (advertising, payroll, money). Knowing how to make a great croissant doesn't mean you know how to run a great business. The second part is where most small businesspeople struggle
- **The McDonald's solution**: Ray Kroc created a SYSTEM. Any franchisee who follows it gets the same result — whether in Moscow, Russia or Moscow, Idaho. Gerber suggests creating a similar system: "If you systematize it, a bit or a lot, you better enable your employees to do their job, thereby freeing you to do yours." The test: "if it works without you working it"
- Gerber quote: "Does it work? [The] answer is a resounding 'Yes!' it does work. Every time it is applied. And it will work for you. It works because it requires the full engagement of the people working it. It can't be done half-heartedly. It can't be done frenetically. It can only be done intelligently, systematically, and compassionately."

**Gerber's Seven-Step Process**:
1. **Create your primary aim**: Not for the business — for YOUR life. What do you want your life to look like?
2. **Create your strategic objective**: Clear statement of what the business must do to achieve your primary aim. Vision, customers, revenue, purpose
3. **Create your organizational strategy**: How to organize so you're not handling every little thing? Job descriptions, training, turnkey operation
4. **Create your management strategy**: "The system is the solution." A series of checklists for every part of the business — anyone should be able to look at their checklist and know what to do. Same French fries every time
5. **Create your people strategy**: Prevent employees from feeling like cogs in the machine. Define your business culture and how to implement it
6. **Create your marketing strategy**: Who are your customers and how do they fit your new business model?
7. **Create your systems strategy**: Three types of systems — hard (computers, registers), soft (people), information (inventory control, sales). Integrate all three
- By systematizing, you can become an absentee owner — hire a manager and employees to run a turnkey operation while you do other things

### Chapter 37: Small Business Success Secrets II: Vision

- Goethe: "What you can do, or dream you can, begin it. Boldness has genius, power, and magic in it"
- Collins & Porras (*Built to Last*, HarperBusiness, 2002): researched what makes truly exceptional companies different. Conclusion: more than profits or pleasing products, the best businesses are founded on and driven by **vision**
- Every Fortune 500 company started as a small business — they grew by adhering to their vision

**The Business Is the Thing**:
- You don't need to be a genius or have the Next Big Thing. Sam Walton (Wal-Mart): "I had no vision of the scope of what I would start, but I always had confidence that as long as we did our work well and were good to our customers, there would be no limit." Wal-Mart was about 20 years in the making
- Slow starts are OK: Boeing's first plane was such a failure his company sold furniture for years. Microsoft lost money its first two years. Walt Disney went bankrupt before *Steamboat Willie*
- P.T. Barnum filed bankruptcy before starting his circus. Henry John Heinz filed bankruptcy in 1875, then introduced tomato ketchup the next year. Henry Ford's first two car companies failed — he started Ford Motor Company at age 40
- Collins & Porras: "Never, never, never give up. Be prepared to kill, revise, or evolve an idea, but never give up on the company"
- "It is not your great idea that will sustain your business; it is your great business that will sustain the idea"

**There Is More to Business than Profits**:
- Transform your idealism into core business values. Best small businesses have core values and LIVE by them
- **Sony founding story**: In 1945, Masaru Ibuka started Sony in a bombed-out, deserted department store in Tokyo with $1,600 of personal savings. Initially made forgettable products (sweet bean-paste soup). But created a company code: "A place of work where engineers can feel the joy of technological innovation," "To pursue dynamic activities... for the reconstruction of Japan," "We shall welcome technical difficulties," "We shall eliminate any unfair profit seeking"
- Henry Ford: credited with creating the middle class by introducing the five-day work week and the $5/day wage (roughly double the prevailing wage). *Wall Street Journal* denounced this, saying Ford had "injected spiritual principles into a field where they do not belong"
- David Packard (HP, 1960 lecture): "I want to discuss *why* a company exists in the first place. Many people assume wrongly that a company exists simply to make money... As we investigate this, we inevitably come to the conclusion that a group of people get together and exist as an institution... so that they are able to accomplish something collectively that they could not accomplish separately — they make contributions to society"
- Core values should be simple, guiding principles. Recruit people who buy into the value system — if they won't adopt your way, they should be on the highway
- **Joe Wilson (Xerox founder)**: When he died, found in his wallet was a small blue index card: "To attain serenity through the leadership of a business which brings happiness to its workers, serves its customers, and brings prosperity to its owners"

**Think Big**:
- Most successful small businesses create clear, specific, big goals and organize to achieve them
- **Ruth Handler / Mattel / Barbie**: Decided to create a shapely woman doll (all girls' dolls had been babies). Huge goal, took 8 years to develop new manufacturing processes. Changed her business and created an industry. Big audacious goals get everyone excited and crystallize efforts
- Goals without commitment = basketball without air. Set goals, commit, enroll your team, achieve them, set more

**Experiment**:
- Earl Tupper (Tupperware) created many forgettable products first (knitting needles, egg peelers, compact mirrors, flour sifters) before hitting on his breakthrough
- R.W. Johnson Jr. (Johnson & Johnson): "Failure is our most important product" — failure teaches and prods in the proper direction
- **Teflon discovery (1938)**: DuPont scientist Roy Plunkett stored TFE in a canister with dry ice. Next day, canister was seemingly empty. He discovered TFE had transformed into a silky substance lining the inside — Teflon. DuPont only found industrial uses. It took Frenchman Marc Gregoire to figure out how to stick nonstick Teflon to frying pans — creating Tefal, still the industry standard

**The Competitor Is You**:
- Best small companies don't obsess over competition — they look at their own values and try to top those. Sports team analogy: "We do not care what our opponent is going to do, what their defensive scheme is, or what plays they will run. This is not about them; it is about us. Are we up to it? How well will we carry out our plan? Will we be our best? If we are our best, no one can beat us"
- "Be bold, for boldness has genius, magic, and power in it"

### Appendix: Sample Business Plan — The Daily Perc
- **The Daily Perc (TDP)**: Specialty beverage retailer using drive-thru facilities and mobile cafes
- Two target markets: "Daily Commuter" (traveling to/from work, shopping, driving) and "Captive Consumer" (restricted environment where departure isn't convenient for refreshments)
- Products: specialty coffees, blended teas, custom drinks, soft drinks, fresh-baked pastries, confections. Seasonal: hot apple cider, hot chocolate, frozen coffees
- Strategy: deploy drive-thru facilities and mobile cafes in most logical/accessible locations. Drive-thrus handle two-sided traffic
- Community angle: donating up to 7.5% of revenue to local charities based on customer choices
- Cash business = initial cost significantly less than traditional models

### The Daily Perc Sample Business Plan — Key Financial Details

**Objectives** (3-year plan):
1. 13 Drive-thru locations + 4 fully booked Mobile Cafes by end of year 3
2. Gross Margin of 45% or more
3. Net After-tax Profit above 15% of Sales

**Three-Part Mission**: Product Mission (finest quality beverage, most efficient time), Community Mission (community support through customer involvement), Economic Mission (profitable growth through sound decisions)

**Keys to Success**: (1) Greatest locations — visibility, high traffic, convenient access; (2) Best products — freshest beans, cleanest equipment, premium containers, consistent flavor; (3) Friendliest servers — cheerful, skilled, professional, articulate; (4) Finest reputation — word-of-mouth, charitable giving

**Company Structure**: LLC, owned by Bart and Teresa Fisher. Plan to sell 100 membership units at $4,250 each (minimum 5 units = $21,250 per investor). Fishers maintain 51%+ ownership

**Startup Requirements**: Total startup expenses $173,770 + startup assets $191,900 = total $365,670. Funded by 12 investment partners ($225,270 total) + long-term liabilities ($131,400) + short-term borrowing ($9,000)

**Drive-thru Location Requirements**: Traffic 40,000+ on store side, visible from roadway, easy entry with light if under 30,000 cars, established retail shops in area

**Sales Forecast** (3-year):
- Year 1: 298,402 units, $558,043 revenue
- Year 2: 1,177,400 units, $2,348,900 revenue (320.93% growth)
- Year 3: 2,992,000 units, $6,022,950 revenue (156.42% growth)

**Break-even Analysis**: 22,181 units/month at $41,034/month. Average revenue per unit $1.85, average variable cost $0.64, monthly fixed costs $26,750

**Pro Forma P&L Summary**:
- Year 1: Sales $558K, Gross Margin 39.56%, Net Profit ($28,872) = -5.17%
- Year 2: Sales $2.35M, Gross Margin 39.58%, Net Profit $210,068 = 8.94%
- Year 3: Sales $6.02M, Gross Margin 45.05%, Net Profit $957,922 = 15.90%

**Balance Sheet Year 3**: Total Assets $2.8M, Total Liabilities $1.8M, Net Worth $1.01M

**Personnel Growth**: Year 1: 15 employees ($278K total payroll). Year 2: 29 employees ($973K). Year 3: 81 employees ($2.33M)

**Exit Strategy — Three Scenarios**:
1. **Franchising**: TDP becomes so successful that other communities request operations → franchise opportunity. Risk: franchises costly to develop, can be destroyed by poor franchisees
2. **Rapid expansion (preferred)**: Become "Drive-thru version of Starbucks" — obtain several million through public/private offering, open 20-30 facilities/year across mountain region metro areas. Risk: competitors establishing foothold first
3. **Buyout**: By year 3, growth and community support make TDP attractive acquisition target. Conservative valuation: $7.5M. 250 units at $30,000 each = 705% ROI over 3 years for investors who paid $4,250/unit

**Conclusion**: Management prefers Scenario #2. With $7.5M corporate valuation, could authorize stock split (250 units → 1,250,000) and issue 750,000 additional shares at $3.75 each, raising $2.8M in expansion capital for 3-5 additional cities
