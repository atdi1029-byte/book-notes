Chapter 1: In the Beginning

Strauss opens with a question that flips the usual entrepreneurial pep talk on its head: the issue is not whether entrepreneurship is right for you, but whether you are right for entrepreneurship. Starting a business is nerve-wracking, liberating, difficult, and joyous — sometimes all in the same day. If you cannot handle stress, uncertainty, irregular income, and the absence of structure, the entrepreneurial road will be brutal. There is no shame in recognizing that about yourself.

The chapter includes a 20-question Entrepreneurship IQ Quiz that tests traits like self-discipline, risk tolerance, creativity, willingness to fire people, and comfort with 60-hour weeks. Scoring above 80 means you have both the temperament and skills. Between 60 and 79, you are not a natural entrepreneur but might grow into one. Below 60, you should consider something else entirely. The quiz is not meant to gatekeep — it reveals where your weak spots are so you can shore them up.

Risk tolerance emerges as the single most important entrepreneurial trait. But Strauss immediately undercuts the cowboy mythology: great small businesses work to reduce their risks as much as possible. They cover every angle so the risks they do take are prudent, calculated, and carry a high likelihood of payoff. The gambling entrepreneur is not the hero of this book.

"Take a prudent, calculated, intelligent risk with a high likelihood of payoff." — Steven D. Strauss

Education matters, but not necessarily the formal kind. Most people go into business because they love their craft — the baker wants a bakery, the chiropractor wants a practice. The problem is they know their specialty but not the business side: marketing, accounting, sales, payroll, hiring. As a small business owner you will be president, accountant, marketing wizard, and salesperson all at once.

Strauss recommends two forms of experiential education before launching. First, work in a business similar to the one you want to create, even if only for six months. The things you learn on the floor cannot be found in books. Second, go to a nearby town — not your own, where you would be seen as competition — and take existing owners to lunch. Ask what they like most and least, how much it cost to start, where they advertise, and what they would do differently. This informal MBA reduces risk by giving you a thorough, unvarnished understanding of what you are getting into.

The prototypical successful entrepreneur, Strauss concludes, is driven, hard-working, creative, energetic, resourceful, confident, and flexible. But the trait list matters less than the willingness to learn what you do not know. Resources like the SBA, SCORE (Service Corps of Retired Executives), trade magazines, and sites like Entrepreneur.com exist precisely because no one is born knowing how to run a business.

The chapter closes with John Nordstrom's origin story. The founder of the department store chain did not have a grand vision — he was simply looking for a small business to get into when a shoemaker suggested they open a shoe store together. Great empires sometimes start with a conversation over a workbench.

This opening sets the philosophical tone for the entire book: entrepreneurship is wonderful, but it demands self-awareness, preparation, and a willingness to learn the business side of whatever you love. Romance without realism leads to failure.

Chapter 2: Choosing the Right Business

Strauss identifies two species of entrepreneur. The first is in love with a specific business idea — the person who has always wanted to open a flower shop. The second is in love with the idea of being their own boss and needs to find the right vehicle. Jeff Bezos exemplifies the second type: he did not start Amazon because he loved books. He saw Internet usage growing 2,300 percent per year and concluded that book sales were the best way to ride that wave. Bezos analyzed the marketplace first and fell in love with the opportunity second.

Doing what you love is the first prerequisite, but it is not sufficient on its own. Richard Branson started Virgin Music because he loved music. Bill Gates started Microsoft because he loved computers. But loving something does not guarantee anyone will pay for it. If nobody wants your Flemish architecture consulting service, you do not have a business. You must be objective: ask around, get feedback, crunch numbers, and confirm that the market will pay a price high enough for you to profit.

The first mover's advantage is real — Amazon, Yahoo, Post-its, and Pampers all shaped their markets by arriving first. But being first requires enormous capital, drive, and risk tolerance. For new entrepreneurs, Strauss advises picking ideas that others have successfully implemented. The advantage of following is that resources already exist: books, trade magazines, SCORE mentors, and association data that tell you exactly what to expect.

Roy and Bertrand Sosa illustrate opportunity recognition perfectly. Mexican immigrants who arrived in 1986 and could not get checking accounts or credit cards, they saw a massive untapped market: financial services for recent immigrants with poor credit histories. Using prepaid phone cards as a model, they created NetSpend, a prepaid debit card company that grew from zero to 400,000 accounts in five years. They secured funding through the SBA's Small Business Investment Companies program, which guarantees venture capital investments and entices VCs to back riskier projects.

The Sosas distilled their approach into three rules: figure out what pain or need the customer has that you can solve, create strong win-win partnerships, and start with baby steps but think big. These rules recur throughout the book in different guises.

Strauss lays out a six-step process for choosing a business: research the marketplace, decide between product and service, analyze your skills, consider your options (start from scratch, buy existing, franchise, or home-based), narrow your choices by talking to actual owners, and then start your engines. Service businesses are less expensive to launch because they carry no inventory. Product businesses mark up prices and profit on the difference.

The chapter's most memorable warning comes from Wayne Gretzky: go where the puck is going, not where it is. But do not pick something so challenging that you are overwhelmed — the business itself will challenge you plenty. And do not try to compete with superstores like Costco, Sam's Club, or Wal-Mart on price. Find a niche where you have an advantage they cannot replicate.

"Good ideas are not hard to come by, but they ARE hard to implement." — Steven D. Strauss

Chapter 3: Buying an Existing Business

Buying an existing business is like buying a house with a track record. The main advantage is risk reduction: you can look at the books, see past performance, and project future returns. Goodwill is already established, the customer base exists, and everything is in place to hit the ground running. Strauss quotes Winston Churchill: some see private enterprise as a tiger to be shot, others as a cow to be milked, but only a handful see it for what it really is — the strong horse that pulls the whole cart.

Finding businesses for sale requires casting a wide net. Classifieds, trade magazines, online marketplaces like BizBuySell.com and BizQuest.com, and business brokers all have listings. Brokers are not cheap, but they have access to unlisted opportunities and serve as financial advisors and negotiators. Look for brokers certified as Certified Business Intermediaries (CBI) through the International Business Brokers Association.

Due diligence is where the real work happens. Why is the business being sold? An owner retiring is a good sign. An owner dumping problems is a red flag. The seller cannot legally lie — fraudulent misrepresentation voids the contract — but you still need to verify independently. Talk to suppliers, neighboring businesses, and get trade references. Analyze the competition, check for nontransferable intangibles (businesses that succeed because of the owner's personal relationships may not transfer well), and investigate pending changes to the area.

A reasonable benchmark for returns: if you can earn roughly ten percent passively in a mutual fund, an active business investment should cover operating expenses plus your salary plus loan payments plus an annual return on your invested capital. If the numbers do not clear that bar, the deal is not worth the risk of active ownership.

Three valuation methods dominate. Price building lists every asset and assigns a dollar value. Return on investment looks at annual profit as a percentage of the purchase price. The multiplier method multiplies earnings by an industry-specific factor — the battle is always over what that factor should be. Strauss provides a worked example: a machinery rental business with $125K in real estate, $40K in equipment, $25K in inventory, and $10K in goodwill totals $200K. If it nets $100K per year, the 50 percent ROI suggests the asking price could be higher.

Two smart contract provisions can protect you. First, link the sales price to customer retention, especially in service businesses where clients may be loyal to the previous owner rather than the business itself. Second, have the old owner stay on as a consultant during the transition period. The consulting fee is usually worth every penny.

Before closing, tour the premises thoroughly, peek into every corner, and get your lawyer and accountant involved regardless of whether you have a broker. No business will be perfect. Your job is to decide whether the benefits outweigh the burdens. Speak with customers and suppliers where possible — they know things the seller may not volunteer.

"The great entrepreneurs are not gamblers; indeed, they seek to reduce the risk as much as possible." — Steven D. Strauss

Chapter 4: Understanding Your Market

Strauss calls this the most important chapter in the book. Everything hinges on accurately understanding your market. A hunch will not do — you need hard facts. Research your market before quitting your day job, because the great entrepreneurs endeavor to reduce their risk as much as possible.

The framework is the Three Cs: your company (products, size, positioning), your customers (demographics, needs, buying habits), and your competitors (strengths, weaknesses, what they are doing right and wrong). Getting these wrong means building a business on sand.

A case study named Dave illustrates the value of research. A computer programmer who wanted a cafe, Dave investigated one he liked and concluded the lease was too short and drive-by traffic insufficient. He started over, found a better location, bought that cafe, and made a good living. Research saved him from a bad investment — the boring homework prevented an expensive mistake.

The Princeton Creative Research Checklist offers a systematic way to evaluate any business idea: Is there a real need? Are projected returns adequate? Have you checked for faults or limitations? Could you offer variations? Is the timing right? What is the competition doing? Each question forces you out of the love affair with your idea and into the cold light of market reality.

Research sources are plentiful and often free. Trade associations have survey data and startup kits. Trade shows let you meet hundreds of people already doing what you want to do. The SBA's Small Business Development Centers offer free counseling. SCORE pairs you with retired executives. Census.gov provides demographics. Hoovers.com has business and industry data. The Small Business Institute program at 250 MBA programs assigns graduate students to market research projects for nominal fees.

For primary research, Strauss suggests parking outside a competitor and asking exiting customers for five minutes of their time. What do they like and dislike about the current vendor? What would cause them to switch? Become a customer of your competition — shop their store, use their service, and analyze everything you experience.

Phone surveys cost about a third less than personal interviews. Two hundred and fifty interviews constitute a good sample. Direct mail is inexpensive with bulk rates but response rates typically fall below five percent. To improve response, address letters to a named person rather than "Occupant" and include a self-addressed stamped envelope.

Hiring MBA students through the Small Business Institute program is one of the book's best hidden gems. Contact the Small Business Advancement National Center and you get graduate-level market research for almost nothing. Libraries are another underused resource — librarians are research experts, and many offer low-cost research services through the Internet-Plus Directory of Express Library Services.

Chapter 5: Calculating Startup Costs

Strauss presents a clean four-step method for calculating how much money you actually need to open a business and keep it alive. Step one covers startup expenses across twelve categories: legal structure, accountant, build-out, licenses, stationery, marketing materials, first month's rent and deposit, insurance, utility deposits, signs, internet, and miscellaneous. Step two covers asset purchases: real estate, furniture, equipment, vehicles, inventory, and supplies.

Step three is where most entrepreneurs stumble. You list ongoing monthly expenses — rent, utilities, payroll, your own draw, supplies, insurance, transportation, advertising, inventory, taxes, debt payments, working capital — and multiply the total by six. That six-month cushion is the minimum you need in the bank before opening day. Cash is the lifeblood of any business, especially a new one.

"Don't blow it. Don't spend too much. Buy used. Buy off eBay. Hoard your precious, precious capital." — Steven D. Strauss

The worked example of Perry's Pizza Parlor makes the math concrete. Startup expenses run $22,000, assets cost $15,000, and monthly expenses total $10,000. Multiply monthly by six and you get $60,000. Add it all up: Perry needs roughly $97,000 — ideally $100K in the bank — to open and survive the first six months.

Step four translates expenses into a sales target. Perry must gross at least $10,000 per month to break even. At $30 per table, that means serving at least eleven tables per day. It is far more difficult and expensive to create a new customer than to keep an existing one, so those early customers are gold. Strauss warns that six months of consistent profitability is a reasonable expectation for the ramp-up period.

The underlying philosophy is extreme conservatism with cash. Entrepreneurs who blow through their capital on fancy offices, new equipment, and premium locations often run out of money before the business gains traction. Buy used. Negotiate everything. Preserve capital for the months when revenue has not yet caught up with expenses.

This chapter is one of the most actionable in the entire book. The four-step framework is simple enough to execute on a napkin but rigorous enough to reveal whether a business idea is financially viable before you commit a single dollar.

Chapter 6: Write a Winning Business Plan

Even if you never show it to anyone, writing a business plan forces you to analyze what you are doing, crunch the numbers, dissect the competition, and scrutinize risk versus reward. It is a road map, like a pilot's flight plan from Seattle to Miami. Without one, your enterprise is a gamble. With one, you at least know where you are heading and can course-correct.

Two major consequences of skipping the business plan: your business becomes a coin flip, and you will never attract outside investment. Whether it is a bank, the SBA, an angel investor, or Uncle Larry — a business plan is a prerequisite for funding. No plan, no money.

Strauss walks through twelve elements in order. The executive summary is probably the single most important section. It is what investors read first — and often last. It should be three to four crisp pages covering what the business is, the target market, what makes it different, why the timing is right, and how much money you are seeking. The critical tip: write it last, after you have thought through everything else.

The sales forecast deserves special caution. Avoid unrealistic numbers. Sophisticated investors see through inflated projections instantly, and phony numbers can also lead you to overspend based on phantom revenue. Base your forecast on average monthly sales of similar-sized businesses in similar markets and be conservative.

The financial analysis section is the most difficult. It must include an income and expense statement, a profit and loss statement, a balance sheet, and a cash flow statement. Strauss provides a worked example for Cafe Coffee: projected income of $187,900, total expenses of $143,650, and net profit of $44,250. The P&L breakdown includes cost of goods sold at $76,300, labor at $33,700, marketing at $6,200, and taxes at $6,800.

The business description section should be written in your own voice with your own passion. Experts can smell a prepackaged, ghost-written plan from a mile away. Distinguish between your feasible market (total potential customers) and your realistic market share. The management section matters enormously to lenders — they take the team's background and experience very seriously.

The plan should end with an exit strategy and an appendix containing substantiation documents, personal net worth statements, letters of intent, and insurance details. Writing is rewriting, Strauss reminds you. Either you will get funded, or you will have learned a great deal about how to make your business fly. Either way, you win.

"Writing is re-writing. Your business plan is no different, so write it and re-write it." — Steven D. Strauss

Chapter 7: Structuring Your Business

Four options exist for structuring a business: sole proprietorship, partnership, LLC, or corporation. The decision should involve your lawyer and accountant. Strauss walks through each with unusual candor about the downsides.

The sole proprietorship is the cheapest and easiest to set up — roughly $100 to get started. About 17 million of the 23 million small businesses in America are sole proprietorships. But legally, you and the business are one and the same. If your pizza delivery driver kills a pedestrian while drunk and your insurance will not cover it, your personal assets — cars, home, retirement — can be seized. Starting as a sole proprietor is probably not a good idea, legally speaking.

General partnerships are even more precarious. Either partner can get the entire partnership into debt, and best friends who become partners do not always stay best friends. If you go this route, have an attorney draft a partnership agreement and start by working on a project or two together first to see if your styles are complementary.

The LLC has become the business form of choice for many small businesses. It combines the liability protection of a corporation with the flow-through taxation and informality of a sole proprietorship. LLC owners are called members, profits can be distributed unequally, and it can be formed with only one member. Filing articles of organization costs roughly $75 in most states. After filing, draft an operating agreement detailing contributions, ownership percentages, and management structure.

For those considering incorporation, the S corporation avoids the double taxation problem of C corps. Profits flow through to shareholders' personal returns and are taxed only once. A sole owner of an S corp does not pay the roughly fifteen percent FICA, Medicare, and self-employment tax on the first $75,000 earned — a significant savings. C corps are better suited to larger businesses planning to go public, since shares are freely sellable.

Strauss includes a comparison table covering liability protection, formalities, transferability, tax benefits, creation difficulty, cost, and frequency of small business use across all seven entity types. The LLC scores well on nearly every dimension, which explains its popularity.

Professional corporations exist for licensed professionals like lawyers, doctors, and accountants. Only that professional can be sole shareholder, and the structure does not shield against malpractice claims — a critical distinction that many professionals miss.

The bottom line: talk to your lawyer and accountant, but for most small businesses, the LLC offers the best combination of protection, simplicity, and tax efficiency. Do not default to a sole proprietorship just because it is easy.

Chapter 8: The Money Hunt

Benjamin Franklin said it best: if you would know the value of money, go and try to borrow some. By this point you have the foundation — the right idea, startup costs calculated, business plan drafted, legal structure chosen. Getting money requires having these pieces in place first, because any lender or investor will want to see them. It is highly unlikely you will secure all funding from a single source.

The vast majority of small businesses start using the entrepreneur's own funds. Even outside investors want to see you sharing the financial risk — skin in the game is part of the job description. Sources include savings, inheritance advances, IRAs, 401(k) plans (many owners cash these out despite penalties), and stock portfolios.

Home equity loans get a strong warning. They are secured by your house, which means a failed business could cost you your home. Smart entrepreneurs reduce risk; risking your home may be too much to ask. Credit cards are equally dangerous. A $7,000 balance at 17 percent interest with minimum payments takes more than forty years to pay off and costs nearly $14,000 total — double the original amount.

The SBA does not lend money directly but guarantees loans made by banks and other lenders, reducing the lender's risk and making them more willing to fund small businesses. SBA 7(a) loans up to $2 million are the most common, with terms up to 25 years for real estate. Microloans up to $35,000 are available through nonprofit intermediaries for very small startups.

Angel investors are wealthy individuals who invest in early-stage companies, typically in exchange for equity. They often provide mentorship alongside capital. Venture capital firms invest larger amounts but want significant ownership and a clear exit strategy — usually an IPO or acquisition. Most VCs focus on technology and high-growth businesses, making them irrelevant for the typical small business.

Friends and family are a common funding source but come with relationship risk. Strauss advises treating these arrangements with the same formality as any other investment: written agreements, clear terms, defined repayment schedules. The fastest way to ruin a Thanksgiving dinner is an ambiguous loan to a relative's failed business.

The chapter's philosophical underpinning comes from W.H. Murray: the moment one definitely commits oneself, Providence moves too. Whatever you can do, or dream you can, begin it. Boldness has genius, power, and magic in it.

"It is highly unlikely that you will secure all of your required funding from one source." — Steven D. Strauss

Chapter 9: Location, Location, Location

The right location can make an average business successful; the wrong one can kill an excellent one. Strauss walks through the key variables: foot traffic and drive-by traffic, visibility from the road, proximity to complementary businesses, parking, accessibility, demographics of the surrounding area, and the terms of the lease.

Lease negotiation deserves as much attention as the location itself. Never sign the landlord's standard lease without modifications. Negotiate the rent, escalation clauses, tenant improvement allowances, renewal options, and exit provisions. A five-year lease with a five-year renewal option protects you if the location works. A personal guarantee clause — where you are personally liable even if the business fails — should be resisted or at least limited in duration.

Strauss recommends scouting locations at different times of day and different days of the week. A strip mall that looks bustling on Saturday morning may be dead on Tuesday afternoon. Count the cars. Watch the foot traffic. Talk to neighboring business owners about their experience with the landlord and the area's trajectory.

For retail businesses, being near complementary businesses creates a destination effect. A children's clothing store near a toy store and a family restaurant benefits from shared traffic. But being near a direct competitor can work too — car dealerships cluster for a reason. The key is whether the cluster creates enough aggregate demand to lift all boats.

Home-based businesses can skip this chapter entirely, at least initially. But Strauss warns that many businesses outgrow the home office, and when that happens, the location decision becomes critical. Planning for eventual relocation during the startup phase saves time and money later.

The chapter also covers industrial and office space. Industrial tenants should verify zoning, ceiling height, loading dock access, and utility capacity. Office tenants should consider proximity to clients, public transportation, and the impression the building makes on visitors. The fanciest office in the world is worthless if no one can find it or park nearby.

Strauss closes with a reminder about opportunity cost. Every dollar spent on premium rent is a dollar not spent on marketing, inventory, or payroll. Unless foot traffic is the primary driver of your revenue, a less expensive location with adequate visibility may be the smarter bet.

Location decisions are among the hardest to reverse. A bad hire can be fired, a bad marketing campaign can be stopped, but a bad lease locks you in for years. Take the time to get this one right.

Chapter 10: Branding 101

A brand is not a logo. It is the sum total of what people think and feel when they hear your business name. Coca-Cola evokes refreshment and Americana. Nike evokes athletic performance. Volvo evokes safety. These associations were not accidents — they were built deliberately over decades through consistent messaging, quality, and customer experience.

For a small business, branding starts with deciding what you want to be known for. Are you the cheapest? The most convenient? The highest quality? The most personal? You cannot be all of these, and trying to be creates a muddy brand that stands for nothing. Pick your position and own it completely.

Your business name is the foundation of your brand. It should be memorable, easy to spell and pronounce, and suggestive of what you do. Strauss warns against names that are too clever, too long, or too generic. "John's Services" tells nobody anything. A good name gives potential customers an immediate sense of your business and personality.

Visual identity — logo, colors, fonts, stationery — must be consistent across every touchpoint. Business cards, letterhead, signage, website, and promotional materials should all look like they belong to the same family. Inconsistency signals unprofessionalism and confuses customers about who you are.

Strauss emphasizes that branding is a promise. Every interaction with a customer either reinforces or undermines that promise. A business that brands itself on customer service but puts callers on hold for twenty minutes is breaking its own brand promise. The gap between what you say and what you do is the distance between a strong brand and a meaningless one.

Small businesses have a branding advantage over large corporations: authenticity. Customers know they are dealing with a real person, not a faceless conglomerate. Lean into that advantage. Share your story, your values, your personality. The small business brand is personal in a way that corporate brands can never replicate.

Building brand awareness on a budget means being strategic about where you invest. Consistent messaging in targeted media beats sporadic splashes in expensive outlets. A weekly ad in the local paper's relevant section builds more brand equity than a one-time television spot that nobody remembers a week later.

The chapter's core lesson: your brand is not what you say about yourself. It is what your customers say about you when you are not in the room. Everything you do — pricing, customer service, product quality, even how you answer the phone — shapes that conversation.

Chapter 11: Products and Inventory

Product selection and inventory management can make or break a retail business. Strauss covers the full cycle: sourcing products, managing inventory levels, dealing with suppliers, and knowing when to add or drop product lines.

The relationship with suppliers is a two-way street. You need their products; they need your shelf space. Negotiate terms aggressively — payment windows, return policies, volume discounts, cooperative advertising, and exclusivity arrangements. A supplier who gives you 60-day payment terms instead of 30 effectively lends you money interest-free.

Inventory management comes down to a simple tension: too much inventory ties up cash, too little means lost sales. The just-in-time approach minimizes inventory by ordering frequently in small quantities, but it requires reliable suppliers and accurate demand forecasting. The traditional approach of keeping larger stocks provides a buffer but costs more in storage and tied-up capital.

Shrinkage — inventory loss from theft, damage, administrative errors, and vendor fraud — is a persistent problem. Retail shrinkage runs roughly two percent of sales industry-wide. Employee theft accounts for the largest share, followed by shoplifting. Security cameras, inventory audits, and a culture of accountability reduce shrinkage but never eliminate it entirely.

Strauss recommends regular inventory counts, not just at year-end. Monthly spot checks catch problems early. Point-of-sale systems that track inventory in real time have become affordable even for very small businesses and pay for themselves through better purchasing decisions and reduced shrinkage.

Adding new products should be driven by customer demand, not by what you personally find interesting. Talk to customers, watch what they ask for, study what competitors carry that you do not. Dropping underperformers is equally important — shelf space occupied by slow movers is shelf space not available for products that actually sell.

The chapter also covers the psychology of product display. Eye-level placement sells best. Impulse items belong near the register. Related products grouped together encourage add-on purchases. These merchandising basics cost nothing to implement but can significantly increase average transaction size.

For service businesses, the "product" is the service itself, and inventory management translates to capacity management. How many clients can you serve in a day? What is your utilization rate? Can you add capacity without adding proportional cost? These are the service equivalent of inventory questions, and they matter just as much.

Chapter 12: Successful Pricing

Pricing is part science, part psychology, and part competitive strategy. Strauss outlines several approaches: cost-plus pricing (calculate costs, add a markup), competitive pricing (match or undercut competitors), value pricing (charge what the market will bear based on perceived value), and loss-leader pricing (sell one item below cost to drive traffic for profitable items).

The most common mistake is pricing too low. New entrepreneurs, desperate for sales, slash prices to attract customers and then discover they cannot cover their costs. Low prices also signal low quality — customers assume you get what you pay for. Raising prices later is psychologically harder than starting at the right level.

Keystoning — doubling the wholesale cost to set the retail price — is the simplest markup method and works for many retail businesses. But it is a starting point, not a rule. Some products can bear higher markups (luxury items, specialty goods), while others cannot (commodities, price-sensitive categories).

Psychological pricing techniques include ending prices in .99 (which genuinely increases sales), bundling related products at a slight discount, and offering tiered pricing that nudges customers toward the middle option. The middle option is where most people land, so make sure it is your most profitable offering.

For service businesses, Strauss advocates knowing your effective hourly rate and never dropping below it. Calculate all your costs (overhead, insurance, taxes, desired profit), divide by billable hours, and that is your floor. Competing on price in a service business is a race to the bottom that nobody wins.

Regular price reviews are essential. Costs change, competitors adjust, and market conditions shift. A price that was competitive a year ago may now be leaving money on the table or, conversely, may be too high for current market conditions. Review prices at least annually and adjust deliberately rather than reactively.

Discounting has its place but should be strategic, not habitual. Chronic discounting trains customers to wait for sales and erodes your brand. Use discounts to move excess inventory, reward loyalty, or drive trial of new products — not as a permanent substitute for competitive pricing.

The chapter's core principle: price communicates value. Before setting a number, understand what your product or service is worth to the customer, not just what it costs you to produce. The gap between cost and perceived value is where profit lives.

Chapter 13: Small Business Technology

Technology has leveled the playing field between small and large businesses. A home office with a computer, laser printer, website, and digital phone system can look and operate as professionally as a Fortune 500 company. Strauss covers the essential technology stack for a small business circa 2005: computers, printers, internet connectivity, phone systems, and basic networking.

The most important technology decision is your computer system. Strauss recommends buying more machine than you think you need, because software requirements grow faster than hardware ages. Skimping on computing power costs more in the long run through lost productivity and premature replacement.

Internet connectivity is no longer optional. A business without a web presence is essentially invisible to an entire generation of customers. Even businesses that do not sell online need a website for credibility, information, and customer communication. Broadband (DSL or cable) is a must — dial-up is a productivity killer.

Phone systems have evolved from expensive PBX installations to affordable VoIP and virtual phone services. A professional phone presence — with proper greetings, hold music, and call routing — is one of the cheapest ways to appear larger and more established than you are.

Strauss warns against two technology traps. The first is buying the latest and greatest when adequate technology is available for a fraction of the price. Technology depreciates faster than any other business asset. The second trap is avoiding technology altogether out of intimidation. Basic computer and internet skills are not optional for modern business owners.

Data backup is the technology task most often neglected and most regretted when disaster strikes. Strauss recommends backing up data daily, keeping copies off-site, and testing restores periodically. A business that loses its customer records, financial data, or inventory systems to a hard drive failure or theft faces an existential threat.

The chapter is dated in its specific product recommendations (Palm Pilots, dial-up modems), but the underlying principles remain timeless: invest in technology that makes you more productive, buy more than you think you need, maintain professional appearances, and protect your data as if your business depends on it — because it does.

Security deserves attention too. Firewalls, antivirus software, strong passwords, and employee training on phishing and social engineering are baseline requirements. A security breach can destroy customer trust overnight and expose you to legal liability.

Chapter 14: Small Business Software

The right software can automate tedious tasks, improve accuracy, and free up time for higher-value work. Strauss surveys the essential software categories: accounting (QuickBooks, Peachtree), office productivity (Microsoft Office), customer relationship management, project management, and industry-specific applications.

QuickBooks dominates small business accounting because it is affordable, relatively easy to learn, and handles invoicing, expense tracking, payroll, and basic reporting. Strauss recommends having your accountant set up the chart of accounts and then learning to maintain it yourself for day-to-day operations.

Customer relationship management software tracks interactions with prospects and clients, ensuring no lead falls through the cracks and no follow-up is forgotten. Even a simple contact database with notes and reminders dramatically improves sales effectiveness compared to relying on memory or scattered Post-it notes.

Word processing, spreadsheets, and presentation software are baseline requirements. Most business communication happens through documents and email, and professional-looking output signals competence. Templates for proposals, invoices, and reports save time and ensure consistency.

Strauss advises against over-investing in software. Buy what you need now and upgrade when you outgrow it. Many small businesses purchase expensive enterprise software they never fully utilize, wasting money that could have gone toward marketing or inventory. Start with the basics and add complexity only when the business demands it.

Training on your software is as important as buying it. An untrained user with powerful software is less productive than a trained user with basic tools. Budget time for learning, take advantage of free tutorials, and consider hiring a consultant for initial setup of accounting and CRM systems.

Integration between systems matters as your technology stack grows. Accounting software that talks to your point-of-sale system, which talks to your inventory system, eliminates double entry and reduces errors. When evaluating new software, compatibility with your existing tools should be a primary selection criterion.

The chapter's practical advice: do not let software complexity paralyze you. Start with QuickBooks for accounting, a spreadsheet for budgeting, and a simple CRM for contacts. Add tools as your business grows and your needs become clearer. Technology should serve the business, not the other way around.

Chapter 15: Accounting Ease

Most entrepreneurs hate accounting. Strauss acknowledges this and then explains why you cannot afford to ignore it. Understanding your financial statements is how you know whether your business is actually making money, where the money is going, and whether you can afford that new hire or equipment purchase.

Three financial statements matter most. The income statement (or profit and loss statement) shows revenue minus expenses over a period, revealing whether you are profitable. The balance sheet shows what you own (assets) versus what you owe (liabilities), with the difference being your net worth. The cash flow statement tracks money coming in and going out, which matters because profitable businesses can still run out of cash if the timing of payments does not align.

Cash versus accrual accounting is a fundamental choice. Cash basis records income when received and expenses when paid — simple and intuitive. Accrual basis records income when earned and expenses when incurred, regardless of when money changes hands. Accrual gives a more accurate picture of profitability but is more complex. Most very small businesses use cash basis; the IRS may require accrual for larger businesses with inventory.

Strauss strongly recommends hiring a good accountant — not just for tax preparation but for ongoing financial guidance. A good accountant helps you understand your numbers, identify tax savings, and make informed decisions about growth. The relationship should be advisory, not just clerical.

Bookkeeping — the daily recording of transactions — can be handled in-house with software like QuickBooks. But review your books monthly, not just at tax time. Monthly review catches errors early, reveals spending trends, and keeps you connected to the financial health of your business. Entrepreneurs who only look at their numbers once a year are flying blind.

The chapter covers basic ratio analysis: gross profit margin (revenue minus cost of goods sold, divided by revenue), net profit margin, current ratio (current assets divided by current liabilities), and debt-to-equity ratio. These ratios let you compare your performance to industry benchmarks and track trends over time.

Accounts receivable management is critical for businesses that extend credit. Track aging — how long invoices have been outstanding — and follow up aggressively on anything past 30 days. Money owed to you that you never collect is the same as money you never earned.

The bottom line: you do not need to become an accountant, but you do need to understand your financial statements well enough to make intelligent decisions. Ignorance of your own finances is not a personality quirk — it is a business risk.

Chapter 16: Making a Profit

Revenue is vanity. Profit is sanity. Strauss hammers this distinction throughout the chapter. A business that generates a million dollars in revenue but spends $1.1 million is not successful — it is dying. Profit is what remains after every expense is paid, and it is the only number that truly measures your business's health.

Increasing profit comes from two levers: increasing revenue and decreasing expenses. On the revenue side, strategies include raising prices (if the market allows), upselling existing customers, expanding product lines, entering new markets, and improving conversion rates on existing leads. On the expense side, renegotiate vendor contracts, eliminate waste, reduce inventory carrying costs, and scrutinize every line item.

Cash flow management is where many profitable businesses still fail. Profit on paper means nothing if you cannot make payroll because your biggest client pays 90 days late. Strauss advises billing promptly, offering small discounts for early payment (2/10 net 30 means a two percent discount for paying within ten days), and maintaining a cash reserve for the inevitable slow months.

The tax implications of profitability vary by business structure. C corporations pay corporate taxes on profits, then shareholders pay again when profits are distributed — the infamous double taxation. S corps, LLCs, and sole proprietorships pass profits through to the owner's personal return. Sole proprietors and partners also pay self-employment tax of roughly fifteen percent on top of income tax.

Strauss introduces the concept of break-even analysis: the point at which total revenue equals total costs. Below break-even, you are losing money. Above it, every additional dollar of revenue drops a significant portion to the bottom line (once fixed costs are covered, only variable costs remain). Knowing your break-even point in units and dollars is essential for pricing, staffing, and growth decisions.

Cost control requires ongoing vigilance, not one-time cuts. Monthly expense reviews, competitive bidding on major purchases, and a culture of frugality keep costs in check without sacrificing quality. Strauss warns against cutting costs that directly affect customer experience — saving $500 on cheaper materials is pointless if it costs you $5,000 in lost customers.

The chapter addresses the owner's draw — the money you pay yourself. Many entrepreneurs either underpay themselves (reinvesting everything) or overpay themselves (draining the business). Neither is sustainable. Set a reasonable draw based on market rates for your role and treat it as a fixed expense in your budget.

Profit is not something that happens to you. It is something you engineer through deliberate pricing, disciplined cost management, and relentless attention to cash flow. Hope is not a profit strategy.

Chapter 17: Getting and Extending Credit

The world runs on credit. Whether obtaining a loan or extending credit to customers, understanding credit is a must. Benjamin Franklin opens the chapter with a reminder that there are three great friends: an old wife, an old dog, and ready money.

Lenders evaluate businesses using the Four Cs of Credit: character (your reputation and track record), capacity (your ability to repay based on cash flow), capital (your personal investment in the business), and collateral (assets pledged to secure the loan). Weakness in any one area can sink a loan application.

Building business credit requires establishing trade credit with suppliers, opening a business bank account, getting a business credit card, and paying everything on time. Your personal credit history matters too, especially for new businesses without their own track record. A business credit score and a personal credit score are evaluated separately but both influence lending decisions.

Extending credit to your own customers is a different challenge. It boosts sales but creates accounts receivable risk. Strauss recommends running credit checks on new accounts, setting clear payment terms, sending invoices promptly, and following up on overdue accounts within days, not weeks. A formal collections process — from friendly reminders to demand letters to collection agencies — should be established before you need it.

Accepting credit cards costs you two to five percent per transaction in merchant fees, but the increase in sales typically far exceeds the cost. Customers spend more when paying with plastic, and refusing cards means losing sales to competitors who accept them.

The chapter also covers SBA loan types in detail. The 7(a) program is the most versatile, covering working capital, equipment, and real estate. The 504 program focuses on fixed assets like buildings and major equipment. Microloans serve very small needs up to $35,000. Each program has different eligibility requirements, terms, and application processes.

Equity financing — selling a piece of your business — is an alternative to debt but comes with strings. You give up control proportional to the equity you sell, and investors expect returns that dwarf loan interest rates. Equity makes sense for high-growth businesses that need significant capital and can deliver outsized returns. For lifestyle businesses, debt is usually the better choice.

The credit chapter's lesson: borrow wisely, lend carefully, and treat your creditworthiness as one of your most valuable business assets. It takes years to build and days to destroy.

Chapter 18: Insurance

Insurance is the business expense everybody resents until they need it. Strauss covers ten types of business insurance: general liability, property, business interruption, professional liability (errors and omissions), product liability, workers' compensation, commercial auto, umbrella, key person, and health.

General liability insurance protects against claims of bodily injury, property damage, and personal injury (libel, slander) arising from your business operations. It is the baseline coverage every business needs. Business interruption insurance replaces lost income if a covered event (fire, flood, theft) forces you to shut down temporarily. Many businesses that survive the event itself fail because they cannot survive the revenue gap during recovery.

Workers' compensation is mandatory in most states for businesses with employees. It covers medical expenses and lost wages for employees injured on the job. Premiums vary by industry — a construction company pays far more than an accounting firm. Maintaining a safe workplace and implementing a return-to-work program can reduce premiums significantly.

Shopping for insurance means getting multiple quotes, understanding what each policy covers and excludes, and choosing deductibles strategically. Higher deductibles mean lower premiums, but make sure you can afford the deductible if a claim occurs. An insurance broker who represents multiple carriers can often find better coverage at lower cost than going directly to a single company.

Key person insurance is critical for businesses that depend heavily on one individual. If the founder dies or becomes disabled, the policy provides funds to recruit a replacement, cover lost revenue, and stabilize the business during the transition. Partners should consider cross-purchasing key person policies on each other.

Strauss advises reviewing your insurance coverage annually, especially after significant changes like hiring employees, adding locations, purchasing major equipment, or entering new markets. Coverage that was adequate last year may be dangerously insufficient this year. Insurance is not a set-it-and-forget-it expense.

When filing a claim, document everything immediately: photographs, police reports, witness statements, repair estimates. Notify your carrier promptly — delays can jeopardize coverage. Keep copies of all correspondence and follow up regularly. Insurance companies do not deny claims maliciously as a rule, but they do deny poorly documented ones.

The chapter's warning: do not assume your homeowner's policy covers your home-based business. Some policies specifically exclude business activities. If your carrier discovers undisclosed business operations, they can legitimately deny business-related claims. Get a home-based business endorsement.

Chapter 19: Taxes

Taxes are the price of profitability, but overpaying is not required. Strauss walks through deductions, strategies, and common mistakes that cost small business owners thousands of unnecessary dollars every year.

The home office deduction is one of the most valuable tax breaks available to small business owners. To qualify, the space must be used regularly and exclusively for business. You deduct a proportional share of mortgage or rent, utilities, insurance, and maintenance based on the percentage of your home devoted to the office. The IRS now offers a simplified option of $5 per square foot up to 300 square feet.

Vehicle expenses can be deducted using either the standard mileage rate or actual expenses (gas, repairs, insurance, depreciation) proportional to business use. Keep a mileage log — the IRS requires documentation, and auditors love to challenge vehicle deductions. The standard mileage method is simpler; the actual expense method sometimes yields a larger deduction but requires meticulous record-keeping.

Retirement account contributions reduce taxable income while building long-term wealth. SEP-IRAs allow contributions up to 25 percent of net self-employment income. SIMPLE IRAs work well for businesses with fewer than 100 employees. Solo 401(k) plans allow the highest contribution levels for self-employed individuals with no employees.

Estimated quarterly tax payments are mandatory for most self-employed individuals. Failing to pay quarterly results in penalties at tax time, even if you pay the full amount owed on April 15. Strauss recommends setting aside 25 to 30 percent of every payment you receive in a dedicated tax savings account.

The chapter covers surviving an IRS audit with practical advice: do not panic, hire a tax professional to represent you, bring only the documents requested (never volunteer extra information), be polite but not chatty, and document everything. Most audits are routine and triggered by specific red flags like large deductions relative to income.

Entity structure affects your tax burden significantly. Sole proprietors pay self-employment tax of roughly fifteen percent on all business income, on top of regular income tax. S corp owners can reduce this by paying themselves a reasonable salary (subject to FICA) and taking additional profits as distributions (not subject to self-employment tax). This strategy alone can save thousands annually.

The chapter's bottom line: work with a good tax professional, keep meticulous records, take every deduction you are legally entitled to, and plan your tax strategy proactively rather than reactively. Taxes should not be a surprise in April — they should be a known, managed expense throughout the year.

Chapter 20: Legal Ease I — Contracts and Liability

Every business transaction is, at its core, a contract. Strauss explains the three essential elements: offer, acceptance, and consideration (something of value exchanged). Without all three, you do not have an enforceable contract. Written contracts are always preferable to oral ones, but both can be legally binding — the problem with oral contracts is proving their terms.

Contract negotiation is where many small business owners get taken advantage of. Strauss advises never signing the first draft, reading every clause (including the fine print), and understanding what happens if things go wrong. Termination clauses, dispute resolution mechanisms, limitation of liability, and indemnification provisions are where contracts get dangerous for the unwary.

Negligence and liability are constant risks. A customer who slips in your store, an employee who causes an accident, a product that injures a user — all can generate lawsuits. Three theories of product liability exist: negligence (you were careless), strict liability (the product was defective regardless of care), and breach of warranty (the product did not perform as promised). Understanding which theory applies helps you assess and mitigate your risk.

Intellectual property comes in five forms: trademarks (brand names and logos), copyrights (creative works), patents (inventions), trade secrets (confidential business information), and trade dress (the visual appearance of a product or its packaging). Protecting your intellectual property is worth the legal expense. Failing to protect it can mean watching competitors copy your innovations with impunity.

Strauss emphasizes that contracts should protect relationships, not just enforce rights. A contract that is so one-sided it poisons the business relationship is counterproductive. The best contracts are fair enough that neither party feels exploited and clear enough that neither party can claim confusion about the terms.

For businesses that extend credit, contracts with customers should include payment terms, late fees, interest on overdue balances, and the right to recover collection costs and attorney's fees. These provisions are only enforceable if they are in writing and agreed to before the transaction.

The chapter covers personal guarantees — when a business owner pledges personal assets to secure a business obligation. Landlords and lenders often demand them. Strauss advises limiting personal guarantees in scope and duration wherever possible. A blanket personal guarantee exposes everything you own for the life of the obligation.

Legal problems are always more expensive to fix than to prevent. A few hundred dollars in legal fees during contract negotiation can save tens of thousands in litigation costs later. Have a lawyer you trust, and use them before you sign, not after you are sued.

Chapter 21: Legal Ease II — Employment Law

Employment law is where ignorance is most expensive. Strauss covers discrimination, wrongful termination, sexual harassment, and unfair trade practices — all areas where a single mistake can result in costly litigation.

Employment discrimination is prohibited by federal law on the basis of race, color, religion, sex, national origin, age (over 40), disability, and genetic information. State laws often add additional protected categories. The key for employers: every hiring, firing, promotion, and compensation decision must be based on legitimate, job-related criteria that you can document and defend.

At-will employment means either party can end the relationship at any time for any legal reason. Most employees in the United States are at-will. But the exceptions are critical: you cannot fire someone for discriminatory reasons, in retaliation for exercising a legal right (filing a workers' comp claim, reporting safety violations), or in violation of a written or implied contract.

Sexual harassment comes in two forms: quid pro quo (demanding sexual favors in exchange for job benefits) and hostile work environment (pervasive conduct that creates an intimidating or offensive workplace). Employers are liable for harassment by supervisors and may be liable for harassment by coworkers if they knew or should have known about it and failed to act. A clear anti-harassment policy, regular training, and a complaint procedure are essential protections.

Unfair trade practices — false advertising, deceptive pricing, bait-and-switch tactics — can trigger both government enforcement and private lawsuits. The Federal Trade Commission and state attorneys general actively pursue these cases. Honesty in advertising and pricing is not just ethical — it is a legal requirement.

Finding a good attorney means looking beyond the yellow pages. Ask other business owners for referrals, contact your local bar association's lawyer referral service, and interview several candidates before committing. Your attorney should have experience with small business issues, be responsive, explain things in plain language, and charge fees you can afford.

Strauss recommends keeping an employment file for each employee documenting performance reviews, disciplinary actions, training completed, and any complaints or incidents. This documentation is your primary defense if a terminated employee files a claim. Without it, you are relying on memory against a motivated plaintiff with a lawyer.

The chapter's lesson: employment law is not optional knowledge. Every employee you hire is a potential plaintiff. That is not cynicism — it is the legal reality. Know the rules, follow them, and document everything.

Chapter 22: Advertising

Effective advertising follows a five-step process: identify your target audience, determine what message will resonate with them, choose the right media to reach them, create the ad, and test the results. Skipping any step wastes money. The most common mistake is jumping straight to creating the ad without understanding who you are trying to reach and what they care about.

The AIDA method structures effective ads: Attention (headline that stops the reader), Interest (opening that makes them want to know more), Desire (benefits that make them want the product), and Action (clear instruction on what to do next — call, visit, buy). Every ad should move the audience through this sequence.

Media selection depends on your audience and budget. Newspaper advertising is local, targetable, and relatively affordable. Television is powerful but expensive — cable TV offers more affordable options for targeting specific demographics. Radio reaches commuters and can be very cost-effective, especially overnight and weekend slots. Internet advertising offers the most precise targeting and measurable results.

Yellow Pages remain relevant for service businesses that people look up when they need something urgently — plumbers, locksmiths, restaurants. Magazine advertising reaches niche audiences effectively but has long lead times and higher costs per impression than newspapers.

Testing is essential. Run two versions of an ad with different headlines, offers, or calls to action, and measure which performs better. Use different phone numbers or coupon codes to track which ad generated which response. Without testing, you are guessing at what works, and guessing is expensive.

Advertising fees vary enormously. Newspaper display ads can range from $50 to several thousand dollars depending on the paper and ad size. Television spots range from $200 on local cable to hundreds of thousands on network. Radio ranges from $10 overnight to $1,000 during drive time. Internet ads can start at pennies per click. Understanding the cost per thousand impressions (CPM) across media helps you compare apples to apples.

Strauss warns against one-shot advertising. A single ad rarely produces significant results. Advertising works through repetition — the rule of seven suggests a potential customer needs to see your message seven times before acting. Budget for sustained campaigns, not one-time splashes.

The chapter closes with a reminder: the best ad in the world cannot fix a bad business. Advertising brings people through the door. What happens after they walk in determines whether they come back.

Chapter 23: Marketing Muscle

Marketing is broader than advertising. It encompasses every way you attract and retain customers: networking, partnerships, direct marketing, publicity, and personal selling. Strauss treats marketing as the umbrella under which advertising is just one tool.

The elevator pitch — a 30-second summary of what your business does and why it matters — is the most underrated marketing tool. You deliver it constantly: at networking events, chance encounters, phone calls, and meetings. A crisp, compelling elevator pitch turns every conversation into a potential business opportunity.

Strategic partnerships multiply your reach without multiplying your budget. A wedding photographer partnering with a florist, a caterer, and a venue creates a referral network that benefits everyone. The key is finding partners whose customers overlap with yours but who are not direct competitors.

Direct marketing includes mail, email, and telemarketing. The advantage is precision — you can target exactly the people most likely to buy. The disadvantage is response rates, which typically run below five percent for mail and even lower for cold email. Strauss advises starting with your existing customer list, which is your most responsive audience.

Publicity — getting media coverage — is the most cost-effective marketing strategy when it works. A press release costs almost nothing to produce and distribute, but a resulting newspaper article or television segment delivers credibility that paid advertising cannot match. The key is making your story newsworthy: a new product launch, a community involvement initiative, an unusual business angle, or a human interest story.

Sales brochures should focus on benefits rather than features, include testimonials from satisfied customers, and end with a clear call to action. Professional design and printing cost more but communicate quality. A cheap-looking brochure undermines whatever message it contains.

Networking requires showing up consistently, not just when you need something. Join your local Chamber of Commerce, industry associations, and community organizations. Be genuinely helpful to others without keeping score. The relationships you build during good times are the relationships that save you during hard times.

Strauss's marketing philosophy: try many approaches, measure everything, keep what works, and drop what does not. Marketing is an ongoing experiment, not a one-time campaign. The businesses that grow are the ones that never stop testing new ways to reach customers.

Chapter 24: Your Web Presence

A website is your 24/7 storefront. It works while you sleep, reaches customers you could never visit in person, and costs a fraction of physical advertising. Strauss covers building, optimizing, and monetizing your web presence.

Search engine optimization is the art of making your site visible when people search for what you sell. Keywords in your page titles, headings, and content tell search engines what your site is about. Links from other reputable sites signal authority. Fresh content keeps you relevant. These basics have not changed since Strauss wrote this chapter, even though search algorithms have grown vastly more sophisticated.

Getting people to visit is one challenge; getting them to buy is another. Strauss lays out the principles of online conversion: clear navigation, prominent calls to action, trust signals (testimonials, security badges, contact information), and a checkout process that does not drive customers away. Shopping cart abandonment is the online equivalent of a customer walking out of your store with a full basket.

Drop shipping enables online retailers to stock products they never touch. You list a supplier's products on your site, take orders, and the supplier ships directly to the customer under your label. The example: athletic shoe site sells a package of ten tube socks for $50. Customer orders, you notify the manufacturer who ships and bills you $35. Your profit is $15 per order with zero inventory or shipping to manage.

eBay offers one of the easiest and least expensive ways to create an additional profit center. Roughly twelve million items sell daily, generating about $40 million in transactions. Success on eBay requires good photos, thorough product descriptions, accurate shipping estimates, prompt communication, and consistent customer service. Setting a proper reserve price ensures profit on every sale.

Email marketing is powerful when done right and destructive when done wrong. An opt-in newsletter that provides valuable content builds a relationship with customers who have prequalified themselves as interested in your offerings. Bryan Caplovitz of Speakermatch.com calls creating an opt-in subscription newsletter the smartest thing he ever did — and buying an opt-in list from a broker the dumbest.

Strauss advises offering something genuinely unique online — not just a digital version of your physical store. Amazon succeeded because it could list over a million books, something no physical bookstore could match. The internet rewards businesses that offer something that cannot be easily replicated in the physical world.

Your website should communicate your brand, serve your customers, and generate revenue — in that order. A beautiful site that nobody visits is useless. An ugly site that ranks well and converts is valuable. Prioritize function over form, and invest in what drives measurable results.

Chapter 25: Exodus — Exit Strategies

Every business owner will eventually exit their business, voluntarily or otherwise. Strauss covers succession planning, selling, and bankruptcy — the full spectrum of endings.

Succession planning should be part of any small business plan from the beginning. The cautionary tale of Marty, a flooring chain owner who died at fifty, illustrates what happens without proper planning. His will gave his right-hand man 25 percent ownership and the job of running the business, with the remaining 75 percent split among three children. The oldest son wanted to run the business but could not. The employee resented giving most of the profits to Marty's kids. Eventually the children sold their shares, and Marty's dream died with him.

Three questions drive a succession plan: What is your vision for the business in your absence? Who can best implement that vision? And what plan ensures the vision is carried out? The plan should be memorialized in both the operating agreement and your will or living trust. Jack Welch said succession planning preoccupied his mind more than anything else — nine years before he actually stepped down from GE.

Selling a business can take a year or more. Preparation includes increasing curb appeal (paint, clean, fix), getting a professional valuation, organizing all financial records, and boosting profits before listing. People buy profitable businesses. Hiring a business broker usually yields a better price than selling independently, for the same reasons a real estate agent usually outperforms for-sale-by-owner.

Bankruptcy is not the end — it is a legal fresh start. Strauss, who spent years as a bankruptcy lawyer, explains that most of the law is about retribution, except bankruptcy, which is about forgiveness. The automatic stay is powerful: the moment you file, a federal court order goes to all creditors instructing them to leave you alone. No more calls, threatening letters, or lawsuits.

Chapter 7 bankruptcy (liquidation) accounts for 95 percent of all filings. It is quick, inexpensive, and discharges almost all debts, but for a business it means shutting down. Chapter 11 allows the business to continue operating under court supervision while reorganizing its debts. Chapter 13 is a mini version of Chapter 11 for smaller operations. Chapter 12 is specifically for farmers.

The exit chapter's most important lesson: plan your exit before you need it. Whether you intend to sell, pass the business to family, or simply close the doors when you retire, having a plan prevents the chaotic outcomes that hurt everyone — employees, customers, creditors, and your own legacy.

Every business is a temporary stewardship, even if it lasts decades. How you end it matters as much as how you run it.

Chapter 26: Hiring and Firing

The difference between a good employee and a bad one is the difference between a business that runs smoothly and one that drains your energy and profits. Strauss covers the full employee lifecycle from hiring to termination with practical, legally aware advice.

Before hiring, understand the distinction between employees and independent contractors. The temptation to classify workers as contractors is strong — no workers' comp, no unemployment insurance, no payroll taxes. But misclassification carries severe consequences: back taxes, interest, penalties, and money owed to the worker for lost benefits. Two tests apply: employees follow directions on how, when, and where to work; contractors control their own process. Employees work exclusively for you; contractors offer services to the public.

Hiring follows three steps: preparation (write a clear job description), sourcing (classifieds, temp agencies, college career centers, state employment agencies), and interviewing. During interviews, focus on responsibility, reasons for leaving previous jobs, ability to take direction, and qualifications. Stick to job-related questions — you cannot legally ask about race, gender, religion, finances, or disabilities.

Coachability is an often-overlooked hiring criterion. A coachable employee listens, takes constructive criticism, tries new approaches, and adapts. An uncoachable employee can be technically skilled but destructive to your team. Strauss compares it to a sports franchise: good teammates make everyone better, bad teammates hog the ball.

New employees should sign noncompete agreements, nondisclosure agreements, and work-for-hire agreements where applicable. An employee handbook covering workplace safety, antidiscrimination policies, complaint procedures, and at-will status protections should be provided on day one. After a few months, survey new hires about their experience — they see things that entrenched employees miss.

Firing should never be the first response to problems. Document issues in writing, explain what needs to change, have the employee sign the documentation, and give them a chance to improve. A paper trail is your primary defense if the termination is challenged. Strauss provides a seven-point pre-termination checklist covering whether the problem is the employee or your procedures, whether documentation exists, whether similar employees have been treated similarly, and whether the employee is in a protected class.

Timing matters: fire midweek in the morning or late Friday afternoon. Do it in the employee's office or a conference room, gently, briefly, and with dignity. After termination, change locks and access codes if necessary and send a disengagement letter covering practical matters.

The hiring and firing chapter is fundamentally about risk management. Every hire is a bet; every termination is a potential lawsuit. Careful processes on both ends protect you legally and help you build the team your business deserves.

Chapter 27: Employee Benefits

Legally required benefits include minimum wage, workers' compensation, FICA matching, unemployment taxes, overtime pay beyond 40 hours (for non-exempt employees), and time off for military service, jury duty, and voting. Everything else — bonuses, health insurance, paid vacation, retirement plans, stock options — is voluntary. But voluntary benefits are what attract and retain good employees.

Bonuses can be structured individually or company-wide. Individual benchmarks work best for sales staff: sliding-scale bonuses as each target is hit. Company-wide goals pool bonus money as collective targets are met and divide it equally at year-end. The company-wide approach teaches employees about the business, creates teamwork, and makes everyone feel invested in the outcome.

Stock ownership transforms employees into partners. Three structures exist: stock option plans (right to buy at a specified price), ESOPs (company-funded stock distributed to employees), and ESPPs (employees buy at a discount). About ten million American employees hold stock options, and eight million invest through ESOPs. The motivational effect of ownership is real — people take better care of things they own.

Health insurance is consistently ranked the most important benefit by employees. Small businesses face frightening rate increases without the bargaining power of large corporations. Options include traditional insurance (most flexible, most expensive), HMOs (lowest cost, least flexibility), PPOs (moderate cost and flexibility), and HSAs (tax-free savings paired with high-deductible plans). Checking whether your Chamber of Commerce or trade association offers group rates can yield significant savings.

401(k) plans are the most popular employer-sponsored retirement vehicle. Contributions are tax-deductible for employers and tax-deferred for employees. Administration costs at least $1,000 per year for reports filed with the IRS, Department of Labor, and plan participants. Whether to match employee contributions is a significant decision — matching is expensive but tax-deductible and powerful for recruitment and retention.

Strauss introduces an innovative alternative to traditional vacation and sick day policies: pooled time off. Instead of separate categories, pool all time off into a bank of hours that employees use however they choose. This promotes honesty, respects employees as adults, and simplifies administration. Some employers let employees who bank more than 75 hours cash out the excess at their current pay rate.

A flex-holiday plan creates a list of 25 or more holidays and lets employees select any eleven. This respects religious and cultural diversity without the administrative nightmare of trying to accommodate every observance individually.

Intangible benefits often matter as much as expensive ones. Family-friendly policies, flexible schedules, recognition programs, and a positive work culture cost little but are appreciated enormously. Employees value being treated as adults and recognized for their contributions — sometimes more than they value another percentage point on their health plan copayment.

Chapter 28: Training and Motivation

The distinction between working in your business and working on your business is central to this chapter. Training and motivating employees ensures your plans and vision are executed even when you are not physically present.

Nancy Clark, who owns two dance apparel stores, spends up to a month training new employees before letting them work independently. She trains from the ground up — opening and closing procedures, merchandise knowledge, operating the register, handling complaints. Thorough training creates knowledgeable staff, and knowledgeable staff creates a well-run business.

Day-one training should cover your business philosophy, brand identity, policies (overtime, harassment, safety), operations (alarms, POS systems, restocking), and expectations. Sales training is the most important and hardest area: attitude, product knowledge, money handling, and returns. The best tip is having new employees shadow your best salesperson for a few days.

Ongoing training is an investment, not an expense. Inc. magazine advises making "train to retain" your company mantra. KPMG's training does not stop between major career events — it includes technical updates, industry conferences, and enabling skills development. A California Chamber of Commerce survey of the 100 most successful small businesses asked what the real key to success was. The most popular answer: employees.

Training managers requires teaching four traits: listen (as Hemingway said, most people never do), ask rather than tell (nobody likes being ordered around), be reasonable (honey beats vinegar), and prioritize (great leaders set priorities and enlist people to accomplish them). Ken Blanchard's The One Minute Manager reduces this to one-page goals, one-minute praise, and one-minute reprimands.

Money is the most obvious motivator but not the only one. Link pay to outcomes the employee can control — do not reward an operations director for increased sales (they cannot affect that) but for reduced overhead (they can). Contests build excitement when they use realistic goals, limited timeframes, desirable prizes, and uncomplicated rules.

Non-monetary motivation often matters more. Gallup research shows employees become unmotivated when they do not know what is expected, feel stagnant, do not feel appreciated, or have clueless bosses. FedEx inscribes the names of employees' children on the nose of new planes. H.B. Fuller Company gives a paid birthday off. Mary Kay Cosmetics gives a lunch voucher for two on birthdays. These gestures cost almost nothing but are remembered.

Creating an exceptional culture ties everything together. A clear mission statement, transparent communication, team-building activities, staff rewards, a demand for excellence, and genuine fun transform a workplace from a job into a calling. Strauss references McCormick & Company, where employees work one extra Saturday per year for a charity of their choice, donating their pay while the company matches dollar for dollar. Ninety percent of employees participate.

Chapter 29: Sales and Customer Service

Zig Ziglar identified five obstacles to every sale: no need, no money, no hurry, no desire, and no trust. Rapport removes the trust obstacle, and once trust is established, sales become almost effortless. People rarely buy from someone they do not like and will go out of their way to buy from someone they do.

Tom Hopkins said the human body has two ears and one mouth, and to be good at selling you must use them in proportion. Listen twice as much as you talk. Open-ended questions about needs and motivations help far more than yes-or-no questions or technical inquiries about price and specifications.

The pickle jar analogy captures the dynamics of repeat sales: getting the first pickle out of the jar takes work, but once it is out, the rest come easily. The first sale to a customer is the hardest; subsequent sales are progressively easier because trust has been established. It can take up to six interactions to close a first sale. Follow up with thank-you notes and check whether the product is working out.

Strauss dissects the four-step car salesperson process as a masterclass in practical selling. Step one: build trust with friendly, seemingly innocent questions. Step two: get the customer excited by focusing on benefits that matter to them specifically. Step three: get them emotionally invested through a test drive (or equivalent experience). Step four: create urgency. The "talking to the manager" ritual is usually just a strategy session to raise the customer's offer. The lesson: add a closer to your own sales process.

"When customers buy a drill, they don't want a drill — they want a hole." — Steven D. Strauss

Customer service math makes the case for retention. A coffeehouse customer who visits once and spends $5 is worth $5 per year. A customer who comes three times a week spending $10 is worth $520 per year. The repeat customer is your most valuable asset and costs almost nothing to keep — as long as you reward them with consistent quality and service.

Strauss lists five commandments of customer disservice to avoid. Do not put phone calls ahead of in-person customers. Do not reflexively say no. Do not rigidly enforce policies when bending them would keep a customer. Outback Steakhouse empowers waitstaff to fix problems — free drinks, comped meals — without manager approval. Bending policy to make customers happy is the norm there, not the exception.

Welcome complaints. An SBA survey found that 95 percent of dissatisfied customers would do business again if their problems were solved quickly and satisfactorily. Every complaint is free consulting — the customer is telling you exactly what to fix. Ask how they would prefer the problem resolved and try to do that.

The sales and service chapter's core insight: selling is not about persuasion. It is about understanding what the customer needs and connecting them with a solution. The less you push, the more you sell. The better you serve, the less you need to sell.

Chapter 30: Selecting a Franchise

Ray Kroc captured the franchise appeal perfectly: in business for yourself, but not by yourself. Starting from scratch is baking bread from scratch — trial and error, bad batches. Buying a franchise is getting a proven recipe. The trial and error has already been done by the franchisor, which greatly reduces risk.

The advantages are substantial: a proven system, brand recognition, training, ongoing support, national advertising, and a community of fellow franchisees to learn from. But the disadvantages are real too. Franchises are expensive — name-brand franchises especially so. You sacrifice independence, running the business the franchisor's way even when you disagree. And the relationship is inherently unequal, with the franchise contract heavily favoring the franchisor.

Research starts globally and narrows locally. The Franchise Handbook lists most franchises with their fees. Franchise expos offer face time with franchisors. But the most important research step is talking to current and former franchisees. Strauss repeats this advice multiple times: franchisors will tell you what they want you to hear. Franchisees will tell you the truth.

The Uniform Franchise Offering Circular (UFOC) is your most valuable research tool. By law, it must be presented at least ten business days before any signing or payment. It contains 23 standard items covering management backgrounds, litigation history, bankruptcies, fee structures, franchisor obligations, and — crucially — the names and contact information of current and former franchisees in item #20.

Franchise costs come in four layers: the initial franchise fee ($2,500 for unknowns to $50,000+ for established brands), ongoing royalties (three to six percent of gross sales monthly), advertising fees, and build-out costs (often the largest expense, ranging from minimal to $500,000+). McDonald's total investment ranges from $455,000 to $768,500. Subway runs $97,000 to $222,800. ServiceMaster starts as low as $10,200.

What to look for in a franchisor: plenty of support (site selection, lease negotiation, financing help), strong advertising programs, flexibility that allows entrepreneurial creativity, ongoing R&D, a solid reputation, sufficient size to prove the model works, and a franchisee-friendly culture. Get all promises of support in writing.

The Big Mac was invented by a McDonald's franchisee named Jim Delligatti in 1968 — proof that the best franchise systems leave room for innovation from below. Avoid rigid franchisors who stamp out creativity. Balance between following the system and bringing your own entrepreneurial energy is the sweet spot.

Set up face-to-face meetings with select franchisors to test compatibility. Do not invest money in a franchisor whose values differ from yours. The relationship will last years or decades — mutual respect and shared values matter as much as the financial terms.

Chapter 31: Franchise Success Secrets

Fred Berni of Dynamic Performance Systems surveyed hundreds of franchisees across many industries over several years with more than 600 questions. His key finding: attitudes, not personality, are the best predictors of franchise performance. Preexisting skill sets had no predictive value because every franchisor trains candidates in the necessary skills. What matters is how you think, not what you already know.

The five attitudes of successful franchisees, ranked by importance: they involve their employees in the business (the single most important factor), they are optimistic, they are moderately independent (following the system while thinking for themselves), they are adept at sales and marketing, and they are sociable. The dictatorial, resentful, or negative franchisee has the least chance of success.

Following the system is the franchise paradox. You bought the franchise precisely because someone else already figured out what works. The system's engine has been tinkered with, analyzed, fine-tuned, turbocharged, and perfected over years. If you do what they did, you should get what they got. Entrepreneurs who buy a franchise and then ignore the system are paying for a recipe and then improvising.

Clay Werts, who ran three Baskin Robbins franchises for over twenty years, offers practical wisdom. Be a jack of all trades — promotion, management, finances, operations. A cautionary couple near his store excelled at promotions and decorations but nothing else. They were especially bad at cash management, failing to save summer revenue for the inevitable winter downturn. They went out of business within a year.

Werts on location: you can stay in business with a great location even if you are not the best businessperson. But with a bad location, no amount of skill can compensate. McDonald's, Pizza Huts, Taco Bells, and Olive Gardens cluster together deliberately. Being near competitors creates a destination and builds synergy. But to survive in a cluster, your business must be very good — competition will eat you alive otherwise.

Superior franchisees are proactive about marketing. They do not wait for the franchisor's national campaign to drive customers through the door. They learn new marketing tricks, network, schmooze, and sell. A famous ad executive said the best ad he ever saw read "Farm fresh eggs available today!" — it conveyed feeling, product, and a call to action in six words.

The best franchisees take off the manager hat and get on the floor. They talk to customers, make sales, and demonstrate the selling attitude they want employees to emulate. Business is a social game, and extroversion directly affects the bottom line.

"If you do what they did, you should get what they got." — Steven D. Strauss

Chapter 32: The Home-Based Business

The home-based business boom is real. International Data Corporation estimated 27 million home-based businesses in America by 2005, generating almost $500 billion annually. Within a decade, an estimated one in three households would have someone working from home. The SBA found that nearly 25 percent of home businesses had yearly gross income between $100,000 and $500,000. Technology has leveled the playing field — a home office can look and operate like a major corporation.

Two types of home businesses exist: those started at home with the intention of staying there permanently, and those started at home to save money during startup with plans to move out when feasible. Both are valid. Microsoft, Apple, Disney, Amazon, and Xerox all started in homes or garages.

The advantages are compelling: lower rent, less commuting, tax deductions, flexibility, healthier eating, more family time, and the freedom to work at midnight if inspiration strikes. A Prevention Magazine survey found home workers exercise more, eat better, and even report a better sex life than office commuters.

The disadvantages are equally real. Family members do not always understand that being home means working. Interruptions are constant. Self-discipline cuts both ways — it is easy to slack off (the TV and golf course beckon) and easy to overwork (the office is right down the hall). Isolation replaces the social interaction of a workplace.

Bette Nesmith's story is the chapter's standout example. A single mother and bank secretary in the 1950s, she was a fine artist but a lousy typist. When the bank asked her to paint Christmas scenes on windows, she had an epiphany: why not paint over typos the same way? She started bringing tempera paint to work secretly and eventually experimented at home with different paint formulations until she invented Liquid Paper — a product she built into a multimillion-dollar business from her kitchen table.

Setting up a home office requires a dedicated room used exclusively for business. Cordoning off a corner of the living room does not work psychologically, and it does not qualify for the home office tax deduction. The room needs adequate electrical outlets, at least two phone lines, broadband internet, and ideally a view and privacy from household traffic.

Insurance is a common blind spot. Homeowner's policies may specifically exclude business activities. Worse, if your carrier discovers undisclosed business operations, they can deny claims. Get a home-based business endorsement that specifically covers your professional activities.

A home business quiz helps assess readiness: do you have a private office space, the self-discipline to work with family nearby, the technology infrastructure, the willingness to work alone, and family support? Scoring 35 or above out of 50 suggests you have the temperament for it.

Chapter 33: Home Business Strategies

Success in a home business correlates directly with the ability to strike a proper work-life balance. Create a schedule and keep to it. Dress appropriately — not in a suit, but not schlubby either. Keep the office separate from living space. Set ground rules: closed door means do not disturb, mornings are for phone calls, friends can visit on Wednesday and Friday afternoons. People who do not work at home quietly resent those who do and assume lazy afternoons — boundaries are the antidote.

Executive suites offer a hybrid approach: rent by hour, day, week, or month for furnished office space with secretaries, receptionists, and conference rooms. A virtual office option for $100 to $200 per month gives you a professional address on your stationery, mail receiving, phone answering, and conference room access. Regus Business Centers has over 400 locations worldwide.

Virtual assistants are independent contractors working from their own locations who handle administrative tasks: collections, marketing, travel planning, research, accounting, and website management. Typically less than $50 per hour with no employment costs, taxes, or office space required. You pay only for the time you use, making it an incredibly efficient way to add capacity without the overhead of an employee.

The biggest trap is the plateau. Your business reaches a comfortable income level and you stop being creative. The secret to longevity is continuing to plot, plan, and implement new strategies. Add products, advertise somewhere new, create another niche. The best small businesses have multiple profit centers — when one slows, others continue.

Richard Branson embodies this principle. He started Virgin Records by bartering for space above a shoe shop in London. The record store begat a recording studio, which begat a talent agency, which begat an airline, a cola brand, a phone company, and dozens more. All are now part of The Virgin Group. Every home business has the potential to spawn additional revenue streams.

Raising your rates is a fact of business, not something to apologize for. Strauss provides a four-step process: research competitor pricing, give clients reasonable advance notice, test on a few clients first if nervous, and handle resistance with extra attention and explanations of your value. Confidence is key — if you do not believe your rates are fair, neither will your clients.

Consider flat fees instead of hourly billing. Clients love knowing exactly what they will pay. You have an incentive to work efficiently. And you eliminate the adversarial dynamic of time tracking, where every minute feels like it is on the meter.

The chapter's core message: a home business is still a business. The same principles of marketing, financial management, employee relations, and strategic planning apply. The home part is just the location. The business part is everything else.

Chapter 34: The Shoestring Entrepreneur

Most startups begin without as much money as their owners would have liked. Strauss normalizes this with inspiring examples. Lillian Vernon started a multimillion-dollar mail order business with $2,000 from her kitchen table. Peter Hodgson borrowed $137 to buy the goo he would rename Silly Putty. Arnold Goldstein started a discount store carrying $100,000 in merchandise with only $2,600 of his own money. Fortunes have been made on minuscule beginnings.

Understanding the difference between good debt and bad debt is essential. Credit card bills you cannot pay are bad debt. Debt that helps you get ahead — starting a business, buying a home, financing education — is good debt. Most millionaires started in debt, but it was the productive kind.

The Chester Carlson and Xerox story is the chapter's most powerful example. Carlson was an inventor who figured out how to reproduce documents electronically. He tried selling the technology to GE, RCA, and IBM with no success — he was an inventor, not a salesman. Then Joe Wilson, president of a small photo company called Haloid, saw a demonstration and said he had to be in the picture. Wilson and Carlson became partners. Haloid pumped $100 million into the invention. They named the machine — and the company — Xerox. The lesson: sometimes you need a partner with complementary skills, not just money.

Five rules guide shoestring entrepreneurship: fortunes have been built from almost nothing, understand good debt versus bad, serve the market with your best idea (you rarely get second chances when borrowing from friends), be creative and resourceful, and believe in yourself despite the odds. A Northwestern University survey found most successful shoestring entrepreneurs had no prior business ownership, little formal business education, and insufficient capital — but they started anyway.

Other people's money requires looking like a professional. A solid business plan, a polished elevator pitch, deep market knowledge, and competitive analysis separate the entrepreneurs who get funded from those who get laughed out of the room. The cautionary tale of Jay illustrates the opposite: a great idea (a James Bond-style diamond treasure hunt) that failed because he spent money on brochures instead of marketing.

Suppliers and distributors can be funding sources. They lend you products on credit because it turns you into a long-term customer. Prepare a powerful package — business plan, stationery, references, lawyer and banker names — and contact the credit manager or regional sales manager. Do not agree to buy exclusively from one manufacturer, and do not agree to pay anyone to sell their products.

Buying a business with no money down works like real estate leverage. Sellers need buyers, and if the purchase is contingent on seller financing, they often agree. Combine bank and seller financing, assume the seller's business debts to reduce your down payment, and ask suppliers to loan small amounts to keep a major account.

Business incubators are nonprofit partnerships that nurture startups with inexpensive space, free administrative help, legal advice, and financial guidance. They vary by region — Silicon Valley incubators foster tech, Wisconsin ones foster dairy. The National Business Incubation Association lists available programs. For the shoestring entrepreneur, an incubator can provide the infrastructure of a real office at a fraction of the cost.

Chapter 35: Marketing on a Shoestring

Small businesses may envy big budgets, but they have advantages big companies do not: resourcefulness, nimbleness, and speed. Marketing on a shoestring does not mean marketing poorly. It means marketing creatively. Strauss provides dozens of specific, low-cost tactics.

Four rules govern shoestring marketing: commitment (a campaign takes three to six months to show results), consistency (send the same message repeatedly — an electronics store that tried a big one-week TV campaign got results for two weeks, but a small weekly newspaper ad became its bread and butter), creativity (try many inexpensive methods), and measurement (track what works so you can do more of it).

Co-op advertising is one of the most underused resources available. Manufacturers earmark roughly $30 billion annually for co-op programs, yet much goes unclaimed. The deal: you run an ad featuring the manufacturer's product, and they pay for part or all of it. Strauss's father had a billboard on the San Diego Freeway — "Carpet World... Elegance Underfoot, Featuring Ban-Lon Carpets" — and Ban-Lon paid for most of it.

Remnant space is unsold ad space available near deadline at a fraction of regular rates. Magazines, newspapers, radio, and TV all have it. Per inquiry deals go even further: the station runs your commercial for free and you pay a percentage (typically forty percent) of resulting orders. Zero upfront cost.

Overnight radio is remarkably affordable. Drive time spots cost $250 to $1,000 per minute, but overnight spots can cost as little as $10. Many large news and talk stations have nighttime ratings that rival smaller stations' drive time audiences. Three elements make overnight radio effective: a compelling message, frequency, and consistency.

Creating an in-house ad agency is one of the book's cleverest tips. Media sources give 15 percent discounts to advertising agencies. All you need is a checking account and stationery in the agency name, which takes an afternoon to set up. You automatically save 15 percent on all media buys.

Barter is a legitimate marketing strategy used by almost half a million small businesses through commercial barter exchanges, generating over $10 billion in annual sales. Trade your products or services directly for advertising, supplies, or services you need. Toll-free numbers increase response rates by 30 to 700 percent and cost very little.

Strauss provides two worked budget examples. A travel agency grossing $4,000 per month allocates 7.5 percent ($300) across personal letters, Yellow Pages, newspaper ads, direct mail, PR, and circulars. A law firm grossing $20,000 per month allocates 15 percent ($3,000) across Yellow Pages, newspaper, radio, direct mail, seminars, and website. The key in both cases is consistency and measurement, not the size of the budget.

Chapter 36: Success Secrets: Money

The Michael Jackson example opens this chapter unexpectedly. Despite legal problems and a $70 million loan coming due in 2004, Jackson was not financially worried because he had roughly half a dozen income sources. Most valuable: the Beatles catalog of about 250 songs, co-owned with Sony Music Entertainment and valued at $800 to $900 million. Lesson: diversify income sources just like stock investors diversify portfolios. When one revenue stream dips, others sustain you.

A board of advisors expands your business IQ, provides contacts with investors, strategic partners, and potential customers, serves as a sounding board, and impresses the outside world. Bob Vukovich, who started two successful businesses, insists the worst thing an entrepreneur can do is think they know everything. Five members is a good number. Look for business-savvy experience, diverse skills (sales, finance, marketing, legal), and independence — people willing to play devil's advocate and give honest feedback.

The federal government contracts out $240 billion per year to private business, and by law 23 percent must go to small businesses — roughly $60 billion available. The SBA's Business Matchmaking program connects small businesses with government agencies and large corporations like Boeing, Hewlett- Packard, General Dynamics, and Walt Disney World. In one or two days at these events, a small business can meet more contractors than in a year of cold calling.

Michael Gerber's E-Myth provides the chapter's intellectual framework. The paradox: entrepreneurs start businesses for freedom but end up spending all their time handling issues and putting out fires, creating another work prison. Gerber's solution is to systematize the business so it runs without you. Ray Kroc created a system for McDonald's that works identically whether in Moscow, Russia or Moscow, Idaho.

Gerber's seven-step process: create your primary aim (for your life, not the business), create a strategic objective, create an organizational strategy, create a management strategy (the system is the solution — checklists for every part of the business), create a people strategy, create a marketing strategy, and create a systems strategy integrating hard systems (technology), soft systems (people), and information systems (data).

"If you systematize it, a bit or a lot, you better enable your employees to do their job, thereby freeing you to do yours." — Michael Gerber

The goal is a turnkey operation that can run without you. Same French fries every time, whether you are there or not. By systematizing, you can become an absentee owner, hire a manager and employees to execute, and free yourself to network, market, go after big contracts, or simply enjoy the business you built.

The money chapter's unifying insight: making more money is not about working harder. It is about diversifying revenue, surrounding yourself with smart advisors, pursuing big contracts you did not know existed, and building a system that multiplies your effort rather than consuming it.

Chapter 37: Success Secrets: Vision

Collins and Porras, in Built to Last, researched what makes truly exceptional companies different. Their conclusion: more than profits or pleasing products, the best businesses are founded on and driven by vision. Every Fortune 500 company started as a small business. They grew by adhering to their vision through decades of challenges.

You do not need to be a genius or have the next big thing. Sam Walton had no vision of Wal-Mart's eventual scope but always had confidence that doing good work and treating customers well would remove limits on growth. Wal-Mart was about twenty years in the making. Slow starts are the norm, not the exception. Boeing's first plane was such a failure that the company sold furniture for years. Microsoft lost money its first two years. Walt Disney went bankrupt before Steamboat Willie. Henry Ford's first two car companies failed — he started Ford Motor Company at age 40.

Core values should be transformed from idealism into business practice. Sony's founding story is remarkable: in 1945, Masaru Ibuka started the company in a bombed-out Tokyo department store with $1,600 in personal savings, initially making sweet bean-paste soup. But the company code was visionary: a place where engineers feel the joy of technological innovation, pursuing dynamic activities for Japan's reconstruction, welcoming technical difficulties, and eliminating unfair profit-seeking.

Henry Ford created the middle class by introducing the five-day work week and the $5 per day wage — roughly double the prevailing rate. The Wall Street Journal denounced him for injecting spiritual principles where they did not belong. David Packard went further, arguing that a company exists not simply to make money but to accomplish something collectively that people could not accomplish separately.

Joe Wilson, the Xerox founder, carried a small blue index card in his wallet until he died. It read: "To attain serenity through the leadership of a business which brings happiness to its workers, serves its customers, and brings prosperity to its owners." That card was both his mission statement and his daily compass.

Thinking big is not optional. Ruth Handler decided to create a shapely woman doll when all girls' dolls had been babies. It took eight years to develop the manufacturing processes. The result was Barbie, which changed her business and created an industry. Big audacious goals crystallize effort and get everyone excited.

Experimentation is how vision becomes reality. Earl Tupper made knitting needles, egg peelers, compact mirrors, and flour sifters before landing on Tupperware. R.W. Johnson Jr. of Johnson & Johnson called failure their most important product. The discovery of Teflon at DuPont in 1938 was a pure accident — a scientist stored TFE in a canister with dry ice and found a silky substance lining the inside the next day. But it took a Frenchman named Marc Gregoire to figure out how to stick nonstick Teflon to frying pans, creating Tefal.

The final lesson is paradoxical: the competitor is you. The best small companies do not obsess over competition. They look at their own values and try to surpass them. It is not your great idea that will sustain your business — it is your great business that will sustain the idea. Be bold, for boldness has genius, magic, and power in it.

"Never, never, never give up. Be prepared to kill, revise, or evolve an idea, but never give up on the company." — Collins & Porras

Trading & Investing Lessons

Risk Management Is Position Sizing

Strauss's repeated insistence that great entrepreneurs reduce risk maps directly to position sizing in trading. Never bet so much on a single trade that a loss destroys your portfolio. The six months of working capital Strauss demands before opening a business is the entrepreneurial equivalent of maintaining adequate margin — the cushion that lets you survive drawdowns without being forced out.

Multiple Profit Centers Are Diversification

The Michael Jackson / Beatles catalog example is pure portfolio theory. Diversified income streams reduce overall volatility, just as a diversified portfolio reduces risk. Traders who rely on a single strategy or a single market are as vulnerable as businesses with one revenue source.

Due Diligence Before Entry

Buying an existing business with its three valuation methods (price building, ROI, multiplier) mirrors equity valuation. The same discipline that prevents overpaying for a business prevents overpaying for a stock. Look at the books, verify the numbers, and never let enthusiasm override analysis.

The E-Myth as a Trading System

Gerber's systematization philosophy is exactly what disciplined traders do: create a system (entry rules, exit rules, position sizing), test it, and execute it consistently. Same French fries every time means same trade execution every time. The system removes emotion from decision-making, which is where most entrepreneurs and traders fail.

Customer Retention as Compounding

The coffeehouse math ($5 one-time customer versus $520 repeat customer) is compounding in action. A small edge exploited consistently over time produces extraordinary results. In trading, a slight edge compounded across thousands of trades builds wealth far more reliably than occasional home runs.

Shoestring Capital Is Leverage

Starting a business with other people's money is leverage in its purest form. Arnold Goldstein's $2,600 controlling $100,000 in merchandise is a 38-to-1 leverage ratio. In trading, leverage amplifies both gains and losses. Strauss's emphasis on good debt versus bad debt is the entrepreneurial version of understanding when leverage helps and when it kills.

Key Takeaways

Reduce Risk Systematically

Great entrepreneurs are not gamblers. They research thoroughly, plan meticulously, maintain cash reserves, and take calculated risks with high probability of payoff. Every chapter reinforces this principle.

Learn the Business Side

Knowing your craft is necessary but not sufficient. Marketing, accounting, sales, legal, HR, and financial management are the skills that separate surviving businesses from failed ones.

Six Months of Working Capital

Calculate monthly expenses and multiply by six. That is your minimum cash reserve before opening day. Conservative cash management prevents more business failures than any other single practice.

The LLC Is the Default Choice

For most small businesses, the LLC offers the best combination of liability protection, tax flexibility, and operational simplicity. Do not default to a sole proprietorship just because it is easier to set up.

Employees Are the Key

The California Chamber of Commerce survey of the 100 most successful small businesses found that employees were the number one key to success. Hire well, train thoroughly, motivate genuinely, and create a culture worth belonging to.

Systematize to Scale

Build a system that runs without you. Checklists, processes, and training create a turnkey operation that frees you to work on the business rather than in it.

Multiple Revenue Streams

Every successful business has multiple profit centers. When one slows, others continue. Diversification is not just a stock market concept — it is a business survival strategy.

Contrarian Ideas

Entrepreneurs Should Not Take Big Risks

The popular mythology celebrates risk-taking entrepreneurs who bet everything on a vision. Strauss argues the opposite: the best entrepreneurs obsessively reduce risk. They do homework, build cushions, and only take prudent, calculated gambles. The gambling cowboy is the exception who gets celebrated, not the rule who succeeds.

Following Is Often Smarter Than Leading

First mover advantage is real, but for most new entrepreneurs, picking a proven business model is wiser than trying to create a new market. The resources, data, and mentorship available to followers far outweigh the glory of being first.

Personality Does Not Predict Franchise Success

Fred Berni's research found that preexisting skills and personality traits had no predictive value for franchise performance. Only attitudes — especially valuing employees — mattered. This contradicts the common belief that certain personality types are naturally suited to entrepreneurship.

Home Businesses Are Serious Businesses

Microsoft, Apple, Disney, and Amazon all started at home. The stigma against home-based businesses is outdated. Technology has eliminated most of the disadvantages, and the SBA found nearly a quarter of home businesses gross between $100K and $500K annually.

Your Competitor Is Yourself

The best companies do not obsess over competition. They focus on their own values and try to surpass their own standards. This contradicts the common strategic advice to watch competitors closely and react to their moves.

Proposals & Frameworks

(1) Startup expenses across twelve categories. (2) Asset purchases across seven categories. (3) Monthly expenses multiplied by six for working capital. (4) Monthly sales target derived from break-even analysis.

The Three Cs of Market Research

Your Company (products, positioning, size). Your Customers (demographics, needs, buying habits). Your Competitors (strengths, weaknesses, market gaps).

The Four Cs of Credit

The Entrepreneur IQ Quiz

Twenty questions scored on a 5-3-1 scale covering everything from willingness to fire people to comfort with 60-hour weeks. Scoring 80 to 100 means you are temperamentally suited for entrepreneurship. Between 60 and 79, you might grow into it. Below 60, consider something else. The quiz is not prescriptive but diagnostic — it highlights specific weaknesses you can work on, like risk tolerance or self-discipline, rather than pronouncing a binary verdict on your entrepreneurial potential.

The Daily Perc Sample Business Plan

The appendix contains a complete business plan for a specialty beverage retailer using drive-thru facilities and mobile cafes. Three-year projections show revenue growing from $558K to $6 million, with break-even at 22,181 units per month. The plan includes a community angle (donating 7.5 percent of revenue to charities based on customer choices) and three exit scenarios: franchising, rapid expansion to become the drive-thru version of Starbucks, or buyout at a conservative $7.5 million valuation yielding 705 percent ROI for initial investors.

Brian Tracy's Soap Story

The world's most famous salesperson started selling soap door-to-door to earn YMCA camp money. After rejection after rejection, he changed his pitch from "Would you like to buy a box of soap?" to saying he was selling soap but it was "only for beautiful women." Getting to camp was a breeze after that. The smallest sales adjustment — making the customer feel special rather than targeted — can transform results entirely.

The Sports Agent Who Flew Cross-Country

A sports agent flew across the country just for a brief meet-and-greet with a potential client — no presentation, no hard sell, just a personal introduction. The sports star was so impressed by the personal service and effort that he signed up on the spot. In an era of phone calls and emails, showing up in person communicates commitment in a way no other medium can match. The cost of the flight was trivial compared to the lifetime value of the relationship.

FedEx's Airplane Naming Tradition

FedEx inscribes the names of employees' special-needs children on the nose of new planes. It costs essentially nothing but communicates that the company values its employees and their families in a deeply personal way. This kind of gesture creates loyalty that no bonus check can match. Employees remember how you made them feel long after they forget how much you paid them.

McCormick's Charity Saturday

McCormick and Company in Baltimore encourages employees to work one extra Saturday per year for a charity of their choice, donating their pay while the company matches dollar for dollar. Ninety percent of employees participate voluntarily. The program costs relatively little but creates a culture of generosity and shared purpose that permeates every other day of the work year.

Outback Steakhouse's Empowerment Policy

At Outback Steakhouse, waitstaff can fix customer problems — free drinks, comped meals, extra servings — without going to a manager for approval. This is not the exception; it is the norm. The policy trusts front-line employees to make judgment calls about customer satisfaction, which speeds resolution, delights customers, and makes employees feel valued and trusted. Most restaurants require manager approval for any giveaway, creating delays that frustrate both customers and staff.

The $137 That Became Silly Putty

Peter Hodgson borrowed $137 to buy a batch of silicone goo that a GE engineer had created as a wartime experiment but could not find a practical use for. Hodgson saw its potential as a toy, packaged it in plastic eggs, and named it Silly Putty. It became one of the best-selling toys in history. The entire business was launched with borrowed money that most people would not consider worth the paperwork to lend, proving that the size of the initial investment has almost no correlation with the size of the eventual outcome.
