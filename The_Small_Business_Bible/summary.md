# The Small Business Bible — Summary
## By Steven D. Strauss (2005, John Wiley & Sons, 449 pages)

### Overview
A comprehensive, practical guide covering every aspect of starting, running, and growing a small business. Written by the USA TODAY small business columnist, the book functions as a one-stop reference organized into nine parts spanning 37 chapters plus a sample business plan appendix. The tone is conversational and action-oriented — heavy on real-world examples, case studies, checklists, and specific how-to advice rather than theory.

### Key Themes

**1. Risk Reduction Through Preparation**
The book's most consistent message: great entrepreneurs are NOT gamblers. They reduce risk through thorough research, market analysis, business planning, and financial preparation. Every chapter reinforces doing homework before committing money — from startup cost calculations requiring 6 months' working capital to buying existing businesses with due diligence checklists.

**2. The Business Side Matters More Than the Craft**
Most entrepreneurs know their specialty (baking, law, web design) but not the business side — marketing, accounting, sales, hiring. Strauss treats this gap as the primary reason businesses fail and devotes the majority of the book to these operational disciplines.

**3. Multiple Profit Centers**
Successful businesses diversify income sources. Richard Branson built Virgin from a record shop into an empire. Michael Jackson's financial security came from owning the Beatles catalog, not performing. The message: when one revenue stream dips, others sustain you.

**4. Systematize to Scale (The E-Myth)**
Drawing heavily on Michael Gerber, Strauss argues businesses must be systematized so they can run without the owner. McDonald's works identically in Moscow, Russia and Moscow, Idaho because Ray Kroc built a system, not just a restaurant. Create checklists, processes, and training so employees can execute consistently.

**5. People Are the Business**
California Chamber of Commerce survey of 100 most successful small businesses: the #1 key to success was employees. Training, motivation, culture, and customer service get extensive treatment. The Fred Berni franchise survey found attitudes (especially valuing employees) predict success better than skills or personality.

### Structure

- **Part I (Genesis)**: Entrepreneurship readiness, choosing a business, buying existing businesses, market research, startup costs, business plans, legal structure, funding
- **Part II (Setting Up Shop)**: Location, branding, products/inventory, pricing, technology, software
- **Part III (Money)**: Accounting, profitability, credit, insurance
- **Part IV (Growing)**: Taxes, legal issues, advertising, marketing, web presence, expansion/exit strategies
- **Part V (People)**: Hiring/firing, benefits, training/motivation, sales and customer service
- **Part VI (Franchises)**: Selecting and succeeding with franchises
- **Part VII (Home-Based)**: Starting and scaling home businesses
- **Part VIII (Shoestring)**: Low-budget entrepreneurship and marketing
- **Part IX (Success Secrets)**: Money strategies (multiple profit centers, boards of advisors, government contracts, E-Myth systematization) and vision strategies (core values, thinking big, experimentation)

### Most Actionable Advice
- Calculate startup costs using the 4-step method, multiply monthly expenses by 6 for working capital
- Write the executive summary of your business plan LAST
- LLC is the business structure of choice for most small businesses
- Use co-op advertising (manufacturers pay for ads featuring their products) — $30B available annually, much unclaimed
- Create an in-house ad agency for automatic 15% media discounts
- Hire mystery shoppers to evaluate customer service
- Buy remnant ad space from magazines/newspapers near deadline
- Overnight radio spots can cost as little as $10/minute on major stations
- Government contracts: $60B/year earmarked for small businesses via SBA

### Notable Case Studies
- **Roy & Bertrand Sosa (NetSpend)**: Mexican immigrants who couldn't get bank accounts created prepaid debit cards — 0 to 400,000 accounts in 5 years
- **Chester Carlson & Joe Wilson (Xerox)**: Inventor couldn't sell his idea; partnered with a businessman who invested $100M to create Xerox
- **Bette Nesmith (Liquid Paper)**: Secretary who couldn't type invented correction fluid from her kitchen
- **Lillian Vernon**: Built multimillion-dollar mail order empire from $2,000 at her kitchen table
- **Peter Hodgson (Silly Putty)**: Borrowed $137 to launch a product empire

### Bottom Line
The Small Business Bible is a reference manual, not a cover-to-cover read. Its value is breadth — it covers everything from sole proprietorship vs. LLC to cold-calling techniques to franchise UFOC analysis. The consistent philosophy: do your homework, reduce risk, systematize operations, treat employees well, diversify revenue, and think bigger than your current circumstances allow.
