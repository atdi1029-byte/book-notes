## "The Way to Wealth" (1758) — Benjamin Franklin

### Overview & Framing Device

- Written as a preface to **Poor Richard's Almanack** after 25 years of publication
- Franklin frames the essay via **Father Abraham**, a wise old man at a vendor auction,
  who is asked: "How shall we ever pay such heavy taxes?"
- Father Abraham responds with a compilation of **Poor Richard's** proverbs on industry,
  frugality, and prudence — essentially Franklin quoting himself
- Ironic ending: the crowd applauds the speech, then immediately buys extravagantly
  at the vendue anyway — wisdom heard but not practiced
- Franklin's admission: not a tenth of Father Abraham's wisdom was originally his own —
  he gleaned it from "the sense of all ages and nations"

> *"A word to the wise is enough, and many words won't fill a bushel."*
> — Poor Richard

---

## Part I: Industry & Time

### The Hidden Tax of Idleness

- Government taxes are heavy, but **idleness, pride, and folly** tax us far more:
  - Taxed **twice** by idleness
  - **Three times** by pride
  - **Four times** by folly
- These internal taxes have no commissioners to grant abatements — only self-discipline
  can reduce them

> *"God helps them that help themselves."* — Poor Richard (1733)

### Time Is the Most Precious Resource

- Sloth shortens life by bringing on disease
- Wasting time is the "greatest prodigality" — *lost time is never found again*
- Key proverbs on time:
  - *"Sloth, like rust, consumes faster than labor wears,
    while the used key is always bright"*
  - *"Dost thou love life, then do not squander time,
    for that's the stuff life is made of"*
  - *"The sleeping fox catches no poultry"*
  - *"Early to bed, and early to rise, makes a man healthy, wealthy and wise"*

### Industry as the Path Forward

- Wishing for better times is useless — *"Industry need not wish"*
- A trade is an estate; a calling is an office of profit — but only if worked diligently
- Industry pays debts; despair increases them
- **Diligence is the mother of good luck** — luck is largely earned, not given

> *"At the working man's house hunger looks in, but dares not enter."*
> — Poor Richard

- Key urgency proverbs:
  - *"One today is worth two tomorrows"*
  - *"Have you somewhat to do tomorrow, do it today"*
  - *"Little strokes fell great oaks"*
  - *"Constant dropping wears away stones"*

### Leisure vs. Laziness

- Franklin distinguishes **leisure** (earned rest) from **laziness** (avoidance of work)
- *"A life of leisure and a life of laziness are two things"*
- Trouble springs from idleness; **grievous toil comes from needless ease**
  (those without labor would live by wits alone, but break for want of stock)
- Industry brings comfort, plenty, and respect: *"Fly pleasures, and they'll follow you"*

## Part II: Attention & Self-Reliance

### Oversee Your Own Affairs

- Industry alone is insufficient — must be **steady, settled, careful**
- Trusting too much to others is the ruin of many
- *"The eye of a master will do more work than both his hands"*
- *"Want of care does us more damage than want of knowledge"*
- *"Not to oversee workmen is to leave them your purse open"*
- *"In the affairs of this world men are saved not by faith, but by the want of it"*
- Small neglect compounds: *"For want of a nail the shoe was lost; for want of a shoe
  the horse was lost; for want of a horse the rider was lost"*
- Stability matters: frequent moving ("three removes is as bad as a fire") prevents
  roots from taking hold — settled families thrive

## Part III: Frugality

### Saving Is as Important as Earning

- A man who doesn't know how to save as he gets can *"keep his nose to the grindstone
  all his life and die not worth a groat"*
- **The Indies have not made Spain rich** — her outgoes exceeded her incomes
  (contrarian historical example: resource wealth without frugality = poverty)
- *"If you would be wealthy, think of saving as well as of getting"*
- Vices are expensive: *"What maintains one vice would bring up two children"*
- Small expenses accumulate fatally: *"Many a little makes a mickle"*;
  *"A small leak will sink a great ship"*
- *"Beware of little expenses"* — cheapness at a vendue is illusory;
  buying what you don't need eventually forces you to sell necessities

### The Danger of Artificial Wants

- **Artificial wants** now outnumber natural wants — vanity items (silks, satins,
  fine clothes) are luxuries mistaken for necessities
- For every one poor person, there are a hundred indigent — driven there by extravagance
- The genteel who overspend are eventually forced to borrow from the industrious
  and frugal they once despised
- *"A ploughman on his legs is higher than a gentleman on his knees"*

> *"Buy what thou hast no need of, and ere long thou shalt sell thy necessaries."*
> — Poor Richard

## Part IV: Debt & Pride

### Debt as Slavery

- Running into debt for superfluities is madness — six months' credit at a vendue
  is a trap, not a benefit
- **Debt surrenders liberty**: you become ashamed to see your creditor, make excuses,
  eventually lie — *"the second vice is lying, the first is running in debt"*
- Creditors have legal power to imprison debtors or sell them into servitude —
  equivalent to accepting tyranny you'd protest if imposed by government
- *"The borrower is a slave to the lender, and the debtor to the creditor"*
- Creditors remember better than debtors: *"creditors are a superstitious sect,
  great observers of set days and times"*
- Gain is temporary; expense is constant and certain:
  *"'Tis easier to build two chimneys than to keep one in fuel"*

> *"'Tis hard for an empty bag to stand upright."* — Poor Richard

### Pride as Financial Ruin

- **Pride of appearance** creates envy and hastens misfortune — confers no health,
  eases no pain, adds no merit
- Buying one fine thing forces buying ten more to match it — a cascade of vanity spending
- *"'Tis easier to suppress the first desire than to satisfy all that follow it"*
- The poor aping the rich is as absurd as a frog swelling to equal an ox
- *"Pride that dines on vanity sups on contempt"*;
  *"Pride breakfasted with plenty, dined with poverty, and supped with infamy"*

## Closing Argument & Irony

- Father Abraham's counsel: industry + frugality + self-reliance = ability to pay any tax
  and complain of no hard times
- Final qualifier: do not depend **too much** on industry and frugality alone —
  ask for heaven's blessing and help those who lack it (Job suffered, then prospered)
- Epistemological humility: *"Experience keeps a dear school, but fools will learn
  in no other"* — we may give advice, but we cannot give conduct
- **The crowd's reaction**: approved the doctrine, then immediately bought extravagantly
  — Franklin's wry proof that wisdom without action is worthless
- Franklin himself: resolved to wear his old coat longer rather than buy a new one —
  modest personal application as the lesson's punchline

> *"We may give advice, but we cannot give conduct."* — Poor Richard

---

## Key Themes

- **Three pillars of wealth**: Industry, Frugality, Prudence (self-oversight)
- **Hidden taxes**: idleness/pride/folly cost more than any government levy
- **Time as capital**: squandered time = squandered money; both lost forever
- **Debt = slavery**: legal and psychological — strips freedom, breeds dishonesty
- **Artificial wants**: consumer vanity is a wealth-destroying trap
- **Irony of human nature**: people hear wisdom and do the opposite —
  the essay ends with self-aware pessimism about whether advice changes behavior
