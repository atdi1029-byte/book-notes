# The Middle East: A Brief History of the Last 2,000 Years

**Author:** Bernard Lewis
**Published:** 1995
**Pages:** 433 (content)

## Summary

Bernard Lewis traces two millennia of Middle Eastern history from the
Roman-Persian dual empire through the rise of Islam, the great caliphates,
the Ottoman centuries, and into the turbulent modern era. Unlike most
one-volume histories that either end with Christianity or begin with Islam,
Lewis bridges both -- rescuing the Byzantine and Persian empires from
footnote status and showing how pre-Islamic civilizations shaped everything
that followed.

The book is organized in five parts. Part I uses a brilliant device -- a
man sitting in a Middle Eastern coffee-house -- to map every Western import
surrounding him, from the chair he sits on to the newspaper he reads.
Parts II and III cover the antecedents (Rome, Persia, Judaism,
Christianity) and the rise of Islam through the great caliphates and
gunpowder empires. Part IV delivers cross-sectional analyses of the state,
economy, elites, common people, religion, and culture -- the book's
intellectual core. Part V chronicles the challenge of modernity: military
defeat, economic decline, constitutional experiments, nationalism, world
wars, decolonization, the Arab-Israel conflict, the Iranian revolution,
and oil.

Lewis's central argument is that Islamic civilization at its peak was
arguably the apex of human achievement -- multiracial, polyethnic,
intercontinental, the first religion to make real progress toward
universal civilization. Yet it was "the poor, parochial, monochrome
culture of Christian Europe" that advanced while Islamic civilization
suffered a loss of creativity and power. The book asks why, without
offering a single answer, instead layering explanations across military
technology, institutional rigidity, the absence of an educated farming
class, resistance to printing, and the structural consequences of
autocratic rule.

## Key Themes

- **Continuity across conquests**: bureaucracies, tax systems, and
  administrative practices survived every regime change -- pharaonic
  through Islamic
- **The state-religion relationship**: Islam had no Caesar, no Church
  separate from the state; Muhammad was "his own Constantine"
- **The slave army paradox**: military slaves (Mamluks, Janissaries)
  defeated Crusaders and Mongols but eventually became kingmakers
- **Commerce and decline**: the Middle East went from commercial
  superiority to dependency, not because of the Portuguese voyages
  (a contrarian Lewis claim) but from deeper structural causes
- **Freedom vs. justice**: in Islamic political tradition, the opposite
  of tyranny was justice, not freedom -- a fundamentally different
  framework from the Western one
- **The coffee-house inversion**: by the 18th century, when a Turk
  drank coffee with sugar, both products had been grown in European
  colonies; "only the hot water was of local provenance"

## Style

Analytical history with deep cross-sectional analysis. Lewis writes
with authority and occasional dry wit, drawing on Arabic, Persian, and
Turkish primary sources. The tone is scholarly but accessible, with
vivid anecdotes (the rice-and-the-horse story, the singing commercial,
the Bektashi dervish's antiwar speech) punctuating dense institutional
analysis.
