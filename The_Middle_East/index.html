Glossary

Political & Administrative Terms

Caliph (Khalifa): Successor to the Prophet Muhammad as head of the Muslim community. The title carried both political and religious authority but was never equivalent to a pope -- there was no separate Church to head.

Sultan: Title meaning "power" or "authority," adopted by the Seljuk Turks and later the Ottomans to denote actual governing power, as distinct from the caliph's religious legitimacy.

Wazir (Vizier): Chief minister heading the entire governmental apparatus. An Abbasid innovation adapted from Sasanid Persian practice. The Ottoman grand vizier wielded enormous power.

Divan: Originally a list or register, it came to mean a government department and eventually the imperial council itself. The Ottoman Divan-i Humayun was a formal institution with prescribed membership.

Dhimmi: Protected non-Muslim subject living under Islamic rule. Christians, Jews, and Zoroastrians accepted subordinate status in exchange for security, religious freedom, and communal autonomy.

Millet: Ottoman system of organizing non-Muslim communities as self-governing religious groups under their own leaders and laws. There were four: Muslim, Greek Orthodox, Armenian, and Jewish.

Capitulations: Privileges granted by Ottoman sultans to European states, allowing their citizens to trade without the disabilities imposed on non-Muslim Ottoman subjects. Originally generous; later exploited as Ottoman power declined.

Religious & Legal Terms

Shari'a: The Holy Law of Islam, derived from the Qur'an and the hadith. Lewis calls it "one of the major intellectual achievements of Islam." It governed personal, commercial, and criminal matters.

Ulema: The learned class of Islamic scholars and jurists. Not priests -- Islam has no ordination or sacraments -- but specialists in religious law who wielded enormous moral authority.

Fatwa: A legal opinion issued by a mufti (jurisconsult) on a point of Islamic law. Not binding like a court judgment but highly influential.

Jihad: Literally "striving in the path of God." The overwhelming majority of early authorities discuss it in military terms, though some modern theologians emphasize spiritual interpretation.

Ijtihad: Independent legal reasoning within the Shari'a framework. Sunni jurists declared "the gate of ijtihad closed" around 900 CE; Shi'a jurists never accepted this closure.

Waqf: Pious endowment in perpetuity, typically funding mosques, madrasas, and charitable works. A major source of income for the ulema, independent of state control.

Military Terms

Mamluk: Arabic for "owned" -- a military slave, usually Turkish or Circassian, bought young and trained for war. The system produced both the finest armies and the greatest internal threat to Islamic rulers.

Janissary: Elite Ottoman infantry recruited through the devshirme (levy of Christian boys). Originally celibate and fanatically loyal, they eventually became a hereditary corporation that held sultans hostage.

Devshirme: The Ottoman levy of boys from Christian populations for state and military service. Condemned by Christians but also a vehicle for extraordinary social mobility.

Sipahi: Ottoman feudal cavalryman granted a timar (land fief) in exchange for military service. The backbone of early Ottoman armies, gradually replaced by professional forces.

Cultural & Social Terms

Madrasa: Islamic theological college, the cornerstone of higher education from the 11th century onward. Modeled partly on Ismaili mission schools and systematized by the Seljuk minister Nizam al-Mulk.

Sufi: Islamic mystic. Sufism began as individual spiritual practice and became a mass movement organized in brotherhoods (tariqas). Sufi sheikhs often had more popular influence than the official ulema.

Adab: Secular literary culture -- poetry, history, belles-lettres -- as distinct from religious learning (ilm). The domain of the scribal bureaucratic class.

Introduction: The Coffee-House

Lewis opens with a deceptively simple image: a man sitting in a Middle Eastern coffee-house. He has a chair, a table, coffee, a cigarette, a newspaper, and a television. Every single object surrounding him is a Western import. The chair and table vanished from the region after the Arab conquest -- Arabs came from a land of scarce wood where furnishing meant wool and leather -- and returned only under European influence. Coffee was an Ethiopian bean popularized through Yemen. The cigarette is American tobacco introduced by English merchants. The newspaper is a European invention. Even the coffee-house itself fills a social void created by Islam's prohibition of alcohol -- there were no taverns to gather in.

The key distinction Lewis draws is between internal and external change. In Europe, the objects in a cafe evolved from within the culture. In the Middle East, they arrived from outside, from an alien civilization. This asymmetry defines the entire modern encounter between Islam and the West. Dress tells the story most vividly: clothing in the Middle East functions as an identity marker signaling religion, tribe, and status. Islamic tradition warned that "he who tries to resemble people becomes one of them," making Western dress a matter not of fashion but of allegiance. In Turkish, sapka giymek ("to put on a hat") formerly meant to become a renegade -- as loaded as "to turn one's coat" in English.

We want to wear civilized clothes. -- Kemal Ataturk, for whom civilization meant modern -- that is, Western -- civilization

The sequence of Westernization always followed the same pattern: military first, then palace, then officialdom, then the urban population. Sultan Mahmud II introduced the fez in the 1820s as a reform headgear, and it was reviled as an infidel innovation. A century later, Ataturk abolished the fez and mandated European hats, which were opposed just as fiercely for identical reasons. Headgear was always the last element to change because peaked brims prevented prostration in prayer. Ottoman gravestones were carved with the deceased's specific headgear -- the kadi's cap, the janissary's sleeve cover -- making headgear a marker of identity beyond the grave.

Lewis draws a remarkable historical parallel: in the thirteenth century, Muslim rulers -- even in Egypt, which the Mongols never conquered -- adopted Mongol dress after Mongol military victories. Mongol dress was the dress of military victory. The fashion persisted until 1315, when the Sultan of Egypt ordered officers to shear their Mongol-style locks after Mongol rulers converted to Islam. But no similar restoration occurred with Western military dress. The Western uniform remains to this day. The implication is profound: Western cultural penetration is deeper and more permanent than anything the medieval world produced.

Even states that violently rejected the West kept its political furniture. After Iran's 1979 Islamic Revolution, officials still wore Western coats and trousers -- only the missing necktie marked their refusal. The Islamic Republic maintained a written constitution, a legislative assembly, and elections, all Western forms. Khomeini framed America not as an imperialist but as "the Great Satan" -- a tempter. Satan does not conquer; he seduces. The battle Lewis identifies is between those who hate and fear the seductive power of Western life and those who see it as a new opportunity in a continuing interchange of civilizations.

The press and broadcasting followed a similar trajectory of external introduction and local adoption. The alphabet is a Middle Eastern invention and paper arrived from China in the eighth century, but printing bypassed the region entirely on its way from China to Europe. The first newspaper in the Middle East was a French-language gazette published in Istanbul in 1795. Napoleon published French newspapers in occupied Cairo. Muhammad Ali and Sultan Mahmud II -- rivals who agreed on almost nothing -- both founded local vernacular presses. Radio arrived in Turkey in 1925, only three years after London. The Italian fascist government inaugurated regular Arabic radio from Bari in 1935, triggering a propaganda war. Khomeini's 1979 revolution was "the first electronically operated revolution in world history," its oratory distributed on cassette tapes.

The ruler's portrait on the coffee-house wall is itself a radical innovation -- Islamic tradition prohibits representational images. Sultan Mehmed II had his portrait painted by the Italian Bellini, but his more pious son disposed of the entire collection. Islam's rejection of representational art channeled creative energy into abstract geometric design and Qur'anic calligraphy. Silent films arrived in Egypt from 1897; local production by 1917; the Egyptian film industry is now the third largest in the world after the United States and India.

Music, Lewis notes, is the last frontier. Western art music has made almost no inroads in the Middle East, in striking contrast with Japan and China. Art was the first domain Westernized; music is the last, because music is the final cultural product a newcomer from outside can understand, accept, and perform. The observation is characteristically Lewis: a small cultural fact that illuminates a deep structural truth about the depth and sequence of civilizational encounter.

Chapter 1: Before Christianity

At the start of the Christian era, the Middle East was divided between two great imperial powers. The western half belonged to Rome, which controlled the eastern Mediterranean from the Bosphorus to the Nile. The eastern half belonged to Persia, which its own people called Iran. Between them lay the Fertile Crescent, a contested borderland that would remain a battlefield for six centuries. Egypt, the oldest surviving civilization, had been conquered successively by Persians, Macedonians, and Romans, yet retained a distinct identity defined by Nile geography. Most modern country names in the region are conscious evocations of rediscovered antiquity, not evidence of uninterrupted survival.

The linguistic history is a catalogue of erasure and replacement. Egyptian hieroglyphs gave way to demotic, then Coptic, then Arabic after the Islamic conquest. Even Christian Egyptians eventually adopted Arabic; Coptic survives only in liturgy. The name "Syria" appeared first in Greek -- Herodotus thought it a shortened form of Assyria -- then vanished after the Arab conquest, reappearing under European influence in the nineteenth century. Most dramatically, after the Bar-Kokhba revolt of 135 CE, the Romans renamed Judaea as Palestine after the long-forgotten Philistines and rebuilt Jerusalem as Aelia Capitolina with a Jupiter temple on the Temple site. The erasure of Jewish nomenclature was deliberate and nearly total.

Aramaic became the international language of commerce and diplomacy across the entire Fertile Crescent, Persia, and Egypt, surviving today only in Eastern Church rituals and a few remote villages. Arabic, the last Semitic language to enter the region, was confined to Arabia itself. Even "Iraq" has a complicated history: the Persian imperial capital sat at Ctesiphon near modern Baghdad -- "Baghdad" itself is Persian for "God gave" -- and medieval Arab usage of "Iraq" denoted only the southern half of the country. The name "Persia" strictly refers to the province of Pars (Fars); the official name Iran, imposed on the world in 1935, derives from ancient aryanam, "land of the Aryans."

You only have I known of all the families of the earth: therefore I will punish you for all your iniquities. -- Amos 3:2

The theological foundations run equally deep. Pre- conquest Middle Eastern religion was syncretistic and polytheistic; eastern cults of Isis, Adonis, and Cybele spread to Rome itself. Akhenaton wrote hymns to one god in the fourteenth century BCE, but his was sporadic and local. The Jews were the first to make ethical monotheism permanent. Zoroaster preached one supreme deity locked in cosmic struggle with evil. Cyrus the Mede, after overthrowing Babylon, authorized the Jews' return from captivity and ordered the Temple rebuilt at government expense -- Isaiah treated him as anointed by God.

Between the pre- and post-captivity Hebrew Bible, notable theological shifts appeared that plausibly came from Iranian influence: the cosmic struggle between good and evil, judgment after death, and the idea of a messiah at the end of time. Karl Jaspers called the period from 600 to 300 BCE the axial age -- simultaneous spiritual breakthroughs in China, India, Iran, Israel, and Greece that permanently shaped human consciousness. Ardashir, who restored Zoroastrianism as state religion around 226 CE, may have created the first example in history of a state religion with imposed orthodoxy and a hierarchic priesthood concerned with detecting and repressing heresy. When Islam later arrived, resistance in Iran came not from orthodox Zoroastrian priests -- too tied to the fallen state -- but from Zoroastrian heretics, accustomed to opposition.

Three major Zoroastrian heresies shaped the wider world. Mithraism won followers across the Roman Empire, especially in the military, with temples found as far as England. Manicheism, the creation of Mani (martyred 277 CE), blended Christian and Zoroastrian ideas into a radical dualism. Mazdak, preaching religious communism in early sixth-century Iran, inspired later dissident Shi'ite movements within Islam. Each heresy carried Iranian theological ideas far beyond Iran's borders, seeding the religious diversity that Islam would later encounter and absorb.

Alexander the Great extended Greek cultural influence from Iran to India to Egypt; after his death, his conquests divided into three kingdoms. Only two peoples effectively resisted Hellenistic and Roman cultural domination: the Persians and the Jews, "with very different results." Greeks and Jews shared a remarkable characteristic: they defined the Other by culture and religion rather than by blood, making their barriers crossable. You could become Greek by adopting Greek culture; you could become Jewish by adopting Jewish religion. Rome extended this principle further still, inventing citizenship as a portable, expandable political identity. The accessibility of Greek culture, Jewish religion, and Roman polity all helped prepare the way for Christianity's rise and spread.

The origins of monotheism, the invention of the alphabet, the first experiments in state religion, the concept of universal citizenship -- all emerged from this region before Christianity or Islam existed. Lewis's point is that the Middle East was not a blank canvas on which Islam was painted. It was a palimpsest, layer upon layer of civilization, each partially erased but never fully obliterated. Understanding what came before is essential to understanding what came after.

Chapter 2: Before Islam

Three developments transformed the Middle East between the first and seventh centuries. Christianity rose from a protest against the Roman order to become its official religion -- Constantine captured the empire for Christianity, and Christianity was in turn captured by the empire. Justinian used the full apparatus of Roman power to impose a single state-approved doctrine. The center of gravity shifted east when Constantine founded Constantinople; after 395 CE, the western empire fell while the eastern Byzantine Empire survived a thousand more years. "Byzantine" is itself a modern scholarly coinage -- the Byzantines called themselves Romans. Throughout, Hellenistic culture penetrated the region so deeply that the New Testament was written in Greek and the Old Testament translated into the Septuagint in Alexandria.

The Perso-Byzantine rivalry was the defining political fact of the pre-Islamic Middle East. The two empires fought continuously, broken only by a single Long Peace from 383 to 502 CE. Shapur I captured the Roman emperor Valerian in battle and had it carved in stone on Iranian mountains, showing the Persian shah with his foot on the emperor's neck -- the relief is still visible today. The wars had deep economic roots: control of the silk trade from China and the spice trade from India. Romans paid for both mainly in gold coin because the Mediterranean world had little else to offer in exchange. Persians profited as middlemen on the overland routes, giving both empires powerful reasons to seek alternative paths that bypassed the other's territory.

Byzantine strategy focused on developing alternative silk routes: a northern path through the Eurasian steppe, from Chinese producers to Turkish khans to the Black Sea to Byzantium; or a southern sea route through the Indian Ocean, up the Red Sea to Egypt. Both bypassed Persian territory. The Persians responded by trying to control the southern route through Yemen. This geopolitical chess game over luxury goods shaped borders, alliances, and wars for centuries -- and would only be resolved when Arab conquests unified the entire trading zone under a single political authority.

Are you not those Romans, who have ten tongues and one deceit? It is strange and unnatural for a Turk to lie. -- A Turkish khan to Byzantine envoys, 576 CE

Along the border, both empires maintained client principalities as buffers. The Nabataeans at Petra served Rome, operating a flourishing caravan city. Augustus sent an expedition to conquer Yemen in 25 BCE -- "a dismal failure" -- and Rome never again attempted military penetration into Arabia proper. Palmyra operated the trans-desert route from the Mediterranean to Mesopotamia until Queen Zenobia (Arab name Zaynab) made a bid for independence and was defeated by Emperor Aurelian. The Roman historian Ammianus Marcellinus dismissed the desert Saracens as people "whom we never found desirable either as friends or as enemies."

The collapse of the buffer states left Arabia completely free of external authority by 100 CE. Trajan had briefly occupied Armenia, crossed the Tigris, captured Ctesiphon, and reached the Persian Gulf in 114-117 CE -- a major Jewish revolt in Judaea coincided, likely not by accident. But Roman power could not hold the eastern territories, and Arabia remained beyond reach, surrounded by small principalities living off caravan trade routes through the peninsula to Yemen.

The sixth century brought a dramatic reversal. Renewed Perso-Byzantine conflict transformed Arabia as both empires wooed Arabian peoples, subsidized chiefs, and fought wars by proxy through client kingdoms: Ghassan for Byzantium (whose chief was made a Patrician of the Roman Empire) and Hira for Persia. In Yemen, a king of the Himyarites converted to Judaism around 525 CE, creating the first Jewish monarchy in centuries. Byzantium sent Christian Ethiopia against Jewish Yemen; the Ethiopians crushed southern Arabia but couldn't hold it; Persians moved in. Then in 552, two Nestorian monks smuggled silkworm eggs from China to Byzantium, ending the Chinese silk monopoly and reducing the strategic importance of the entire trade dispute that had shaped the region for centuries.

By the end of the sixth century, both great powers had weakened. Arabians had absorbed military tactics, new commodities, and -- crucially -- writing. They had grown dissatisfied with primitive paganism. Christianity made inroads among borderland Arabs, who were largely Christian by the seventh century. Jews were prominent in Yemen and the Hijaz, thoroughly Arabized. Persian religion won almost no converts -- it was too ethnically specific. The Hanif, seekers who abandoned paganism but rejected all existing faiths, became among the earliest converts when a new revelation arrived. The stage was set for Islam not by a single cause but by a convergence: imperial exhaustion, religious ferment, commercial sophistication, and a population that had acquired the skills of statecraft without submitting to any existing state.

The Ammianus Marcellinus quotation about the desert Saracens -- "whom we never found desirable either as friends or as enemies" -- captures the Roman attitude that would prove catastrophic. For centuries, the great empires treated Arabia as a strategic irrelevance: too harsh to conquer, too poor to covet, too remote to worry about. They subsidized border chiefs and fought proxy wars but never imagined that the interior of the peninsula could produce a force that would destroy both empires within a generation. The oversight was understandable; it was also fatal.

Chapter 3: Origins

Islamic origins are known almost entirely from Muslim scriptures, traditions, and historical memory. There is virtually no contemporary external testimony for decades after the events. Lewis is characteristically honest: "much of the traditional narrative must remain problematic, while the critical history is at best tentative." What is certain is that Muhammad ibn Abdallah, born around 571 CE in the Quraysh tribe of Mecca, received his first revelation on a night in Ramadan when the angel Gabriel appeared to him on Mount Hira.

Recite in the name of thy Lord who created all things, who created man from clots of blood. Recite, for thy Lord is the most generous, who taught by the pen, who taught man what he did not know. -- First verses of the Qur'an, chapter 96

The Hijra of 622 CE -- Muhammad's migration from Mecca to Yathrib, renamed al-Madina -- was the pivotal transformation. In Mecca, Muhammad was a private individual opposing hostile rulers. In Medina, he became a ruler wielding political, military, and religious authority simultaneously. Moses led his people through the wilderness but never entered the promised land; Jesus was crucified. Muhammad conquered his enemies and governed his community. The Umma, which began as a religious community, became a state. It would soon become an empire.

Muhammad died on 8 June 632 CE, the "Seal of the Prophets." His inner circle chose Abu Bakr as the first caliph -- "successor" and "deputy." Within little more than a century, Arab armies had built an empire stretching from the borders of India and China in the east to the Atlantic in the west. The conquest succeeded for several reasons: Arab taxes were lower than what the conquered peoples had been paying; all monotheist religions received legal tolerance without the Byzantine obsession with enforcing doctrinal orthodoxy; and the Arab army possessed unmatched mastery of the desert, which served them as sea power would later serve maritime empires.

The Qur'an was explicit: "There is no compulsion in religion." The war aim was not to impose Islam but to establish Arab political dominion. Lewis insists that "it is the Arabization and Islamization of the peoples of the conquered provinces, rather than the actual military conquest itself, that is the true wonder of the Arab empire." The process unfolded through garrison cities -- Kufa and Basra in Iraq, Fustat in Egypt, Qayrawan in Tunisia -- purpose-built at the edge of desert and farmland, which became engines of cultural transformation.

The conquests unfroze vast accumulated wealth. Tenth-century historian al-Masudi recorded staggering fortunes among the early elite: Caliph Uthman died with 100,000 dinars in cash; al-Zubayr held 50,000 dinars plus 1,000 horses and 1,000 slaves; Talha's Iraqi estates yielded 1,000 dinars per day. Persian governing classes contributed far more to Islamic development than their Byzantine counterparts because Byzantine magnates could withdraw to Constantinople while Persians had nowhere to go. Most consequentially, the Arab conquest ended the Rome-Persia trade route conflict for the first time since Alexander the Great, unifying the entire Middle East in a single commercial system.

But the most dangerous threats came from within. Three of the first four caliphs were assassinated. When Uthman was murdered in 656 by Egyptian mutineers who installed Ali -- the Prophet's cousin and son-in-law -- the resulting schism produced the Shi'a. After Ali's assassination by a Kharijite in 661, Mu'awiya founded the Umayyad dynasty. Succession became hereditary in fact though never in principle. One supporter announced Mu'awiya's heir by pointing first to the candidate, then to his sword: "The Commander of the Faithful is that one! And if he dies, then that one! And if anyone objects, then this one!"

The Battle of Karbala in 680 CE -- where Husayn, grandson of the Prophet, and roughly seventy followers were killed by Umayyad forces -- became the central trauma of Shi'ite consciousness. Lewis observes that the doctrinal differences between Sunni and Shi'a are of minor importance, far less than those dividing rival Christian churches. But the Shi'ite sense of martyrdom and persecution raised a psychological barrier that has never been fully dismantled. The Kharijites represented the opposite pole: extreme tribal egalitarianism where any believer could be caliph and authority was always revocable. Between the Shi'a (divine right of the Prophet's family) and the Kharijites (radical meritocracy), Sunni Islam found the middle path that would eventually command majority allegiance.

Abd al-Malik consolidated Umayyad power by making Arabic the official language of administration and minting Islamic gold coinage inscribed with Qur'anic verses that directly challenged Christian theology: "He is God, one, eternal; he does not beget nor is he begotten." The Byzantine emperor went to war over these coins, correctly reading them as a civilizational declaration. The Dome of the Rock, built in 691-92 on the Temple Mount in Jerusalem, was the first great Islamic religious structure, its inscriptions repeatedly asserting divine unity and rejecting the Trinity. A wall painting at Qusayr Amra, an Umayyad desert lodge, shows the caliph enthroned while six rulers pay homage: Caesar, the last Visigoth king of Spain, the Persian Chosroes, the Negus of Ethiopia, and two others -- not humiliated captives but subordinates acknowledging universal Islamic supremacy.

Chapter 4: The Abbasid Caliphate

On 9 June 747, Abu Muslim, a manumitted Persian slave leading a militant sect, unfurled black flags of revolt in the eastern province of Khurasan. The movement championed the Abbasids, descendants of the Prophet's uncle, and by 750 had destroyed Umayyad power entirely. The common interpretation frames this as a Persian victory over Arabs, but Lewis disagrees. Shi'ism was of Arab origin; the movement served an Arab pretender; Arabic remained the sole language of government. What Arabs lost was not power itself but their exclusive right to its fruits. Under the Umayyads, only full Arabs reached the top. Under the Abbasids, the passport to power was the favor of the ruler, not noble descent.

The new capital, Baghdad -- built by al-Mansur on the Tigris near the ruins of the Persian capital Ctesiphon, reportedly using bricks from Chosroes' palace -- became the center of a cosmopolitan civilization. Iranian cultural influence surged: the Barmecide family, Central Asian Iranians descended from the Buddhist priesthood of Balkh, served as viziers until their dramatic fall under Harun al-Rashid in 803. Sasanid administrative models, court ceremonial, and political texts were translated wholesale into Arabic. A standing army on the Persian model replaced Arab tribal levies.

The Abbasid caliph became an autocrat in the old Middle Eastern style, claiming divine origin for his authority and exercising it through a vast bureaucracy. When al-Ma'mun tried to impose rationalist Mu'tazila theology as state doctrine and persecuted dissenters, the Sunni ulema proved strong enough to resist. His successor al-Mutawakkil was forced to suppress the Mu'tazila to gain popular Sunni support. The lesson was permanent: no subsequent dynasty attempted to dictate doctrine to the religious institution. The attempt at an Erastian Islam failed once and for all.

By God, our booty, which was shared, has become a perquisite of the rich. Our leadership, which was consultative, has become arbitrary. Our succession, which was by the choice of the community, is now by inheritance. -- Sudayf, an early Abbasid supporter, already disillusioned

Structural decline set in quickly. Spain and North Africa became virtually independent. Egypt fell to Turkish praetorian Ahmad ibn Tulun in 868 and was thereafter almost never ruled from Baghdad. A fiscal crisis -- spendthrift court combined with loss of provincial revenues -- forced caliphs to farm out taxes to local governors who became de facto autonomous rulers. Lewis identifies the tragic structural paradox at the heart of Islamic governance: only a stronger state could preserve community cohesion, yet a stronger state necessarily compromised Islamic social ideals. Every rebellion produced more centralized autocracy, pushing the system further from the Islamic ideal and closer to the ancient Middle Eastern model of despotic rule.

By 946, the Shi'ite Buyids of Persia occupied Baghdad itself, keeping the caliph as a useful figurehead. The grant of the title amir al-umara (Commander of Commanders) to the governor of Iraq formally acknowledged that a governing authority existed beside the caliph. The caliphate became purely titular until the Mongol conquest finally abolished it in 1258.

Between the decline of Arab power and the rise of the Turks, an Iranian national revival flourished in unmistakably Islamic form. The key dynasties -- Tahirids, Saffarids, Samanids, Buyids -- each carried Persian culture forward. The Samanids at Bukhara were the most culturally active, making Persian the official language of their court and fostering the birth of new Persian literature in Arabic script -- profoundly Islamic but distinctively Persian, the tenth- and eleventh-century renaissance.

The Shi'a meanwhile split into Twelvers , who followed a moderate path and awaited the return of a hidden twelfth imam, and Ismailis, who inherited extremist doctrines and revolutionary methods. The Ismaili Fatimid caliphate, established in North Africa in 908 and transferred to newly built Cairo in 969, became the first dynasty to openly reject Abbasid authority. Its economic strategy was to divert eastern trade from the Persian Gulf to the Red Sea, strengthening Egypt and weakening Iraq. At its peak under al-Mustansir, the empire controlled all of North Africa, Sicily, Egypt, Syria, and western Arabia. Its religious branch functioned like a party apparatus in a one-party state, with propaganda reaching from Iraq to the borders of India. The Fatimids nearly achieved final victory: in 1056, a pro-Fatimid general briefly seized Baghdad and proclaimed Fatimid sovereignty from Abbasid pulpits before being driven out the following year.

The Abbasid caliphate ended not with a single catastrophic fall but through a slow hemorrhaging of power across three centuries. Spain and North Africa broke away first; then Egypt; then the capital itself fell to Shi'ite Buyids. The Iranian intermezzo produced great literature; the Fatimid counter-caliphate built Cairo and dominated the Mediterranean. What held the Abbasid fiction together was the absence of any alternative legitimate authority -- even rulers who despised the caliph found him useful as a source of legitimacy. It took the Mongol destruction of Baghdad in 1258 to end even the pretense.

Chapter 5: The Coming of the Steppe Peoples

By the eleventh century, the Islamic world showed signs of deep weakness: fragmentation into autonomous sovereignties, powerless caliphs, most of the empire under Shi'ite rulers, economic deterioration, and a shortage of precious metals pushing the economy toward quasi-feudal arrangements. Attacks came from three directions simultaneously. From Europe, Christians reconquered Sicily and Spain while Crusaders arrived in the Near East. From Africa, the Banu Hilal and Banu Sulaym tribes devastated North Africa. But the most permanent transformation came from the east: the steppe peoples, first Turks, then Mongols.

Muslims first imported Turks as military slaves -- Mamluks -- trained from childhood for war. Turkish commanders gradually displaced Arabs and Persians from military command, beginning a Turkish domination that would last a thousand years. The decisive moment came in 960 when the Karakhanids, an entire free Turkish people beyond the Islamic frontier, converted to Islam en masse. Lewis makes a striking observation: Turks surrendered to Islam more completely than Arabs or Persians. There is no Turkish equivalent to Arab memories of pre-Islamic heroism or Persian pride in pre-Islamic glory. Pre-Islamic Turkish civilizations, religions, and literatures were blotted out and forgotten; even the name "Turk" became synonymous with "Muslim."

The Seljuk grandsons of their namesake crushed existing powers and entered Baghdad in 1055. They adopted the title sultan -- the first rulers to inscribe it on coins. By 1079, Seljuks had seized Syria and Palestine from the Fatimids and conquered most of Anatolia from Byzantium, making it a Muslim Turkish land permanently. Seljuk administration relied on Persian bureaucracy, led by the great minister Nizam al-Mulk, who systematized earlier tax-farming abuses into a formal land-based system that amounted to feudalism. He also established the madrasa system of theological colleges across the empire, modeled partly on Ismaili mission schools -- using the enemy's institutional innovation against them.

The Crusaders arrived during Seljuk internal weakness and established Latin principalities. It took the Kurdish officer Saladin to mount an effective counter-campaign. After abolishing the Fatimid caliphate in Egypt, Saladin launched his jihad in 1187, recaptured Jerusalem, and expelled the Crusaders from all but a narrow coastal strip. Lewis notes that Sunni Muslim contemporaries actually considered Saladin's liquidation of the Fatimid Shi'ite caliphate a greater achievement than defeating the Franks -- the Ismaili threat was seen as far more dangerous than any Christian one.

The most radical opposition came from the Assassins, followers of Hasan-i Sabbah, who reformed the Ismaili movement into what he called the "New Preaching." In 1090, Hasan seized the mountain fortress of Alamut in northern Persia and from there waged a campaign of political murder against rulers -- including Nizam al-Mulk himself in 1092. Grand Masters commanded fanatical followers called Assassins in Europe (from Arabic hashish). Their terror was not ended until the Mongol invasions of the thirteenth century. The Assassins were, in Lewis's assessment, the last serious attempt by the Shi'a to overthrow the Sunni establishment by force.

The Sunni revival that accompanied the Seljuk conquests had three goals: overthrow Shi'ite regimes and restore the caliphate (almost completely achieved), reformulate Sunni doctrine, and integrate the religious institution into political life (the most difficult). The key instrument was the madrasa, modeled on Ismaili mission schools in Cairo. Nizam al-Mulk established the first in Baghdad after the Seljuk conquest; Saladin later extended the system to Egypt. Sufism absorbed much of Shi'ism's emotional content while staying within the Sunni fold. The ancient dichotomy of religious and political authority was retained and institutionalized in the parallel sovereignties of caliph and sultan.

The Mamluk sultanate of Egypt, which emerged from the wreckage of Saladin's Ayyubid dynasty, provides one of history's most extraordinary episodes of female rule. Shajar al-Durr ("Tree of Pearls"), the dead sultan's concubine, kept his death secret and issued orders in his name until a successor could arrive. When the Abbasid caliph in Baghdad protested enthroning a woman -- "If there is not a man left among you, tell us and we shall send you one" -- the Mamluks found a male figurehead. The system they built was quasi-feudal: officers received land grants for life but lived in Cairo, interested in revenue rather than the land itself. Unlike European feudalism, there were no chateaux, no strong local authority, no seigneurial rights. The Mamluks' fatal weakness was psychological: cavalry warriors viewed firearms as unchivalrous, and mounted lancers proved hopelessly outclassed by Ottoman musket-armed infantry.

Lewis notes that the Portuguese voyages around Africa struck an additional blow. Qansawh al-Ghawri, the last effective Mamluk sultan, sent an Egyptian fleet to India to counter Portuguese expansion -- the Portuguese defeated it, then systematically destroyed Muslim merchant shipping in the Indian Ocean and Red Sea. The Mamluks' fiscal strength depended on transit customs, and the opening of direct sea lanes to India outflanked Egyptian commerce at the very moment the sultanate was already reeling from Tamerlane's sack of Damascus, plague, locusts, and Bedouin raids. The Mamluk state was doomed before the Ottomans delivered the final blow.

Chapter 6: The Mongol Aftermath

In the spring of 1206, a Mongol prince named Temujin unfurled the white banner with nine horsetails at the sources of the Onon river and took the title Jenghiz Khan. When a caravan of 450 Muslims was pillaged at Utrar and the merchants put to the sword, Jenghiz led his armies across the Jaxartes. By 1220, Bukhara, Samarqand, and all of Transoxania had fallen. His grandson Hulagu crushed even the Assassins in their mountain fortresses. On 20 February 1258, the last Abbasid caliph, al-Musta'sim, was put to death. The House of Abbas, titular head of Sunni Islam for almost five centuries, ceased to reign.

Lewis offers a characteristically contrarian reassessment. The immediate effects were "probably not as great as has sometimes been suggested." The caliphate had already ceased to be effective; the Mongols "did little more than lay the ghost of something that was already dead." Egypt was never conquered. At 'Ayn Jalut in 1260, the Mamluk army under Baybars decisively defeated the Mongols, and they never broke through again. Iran was hard hit but recovered rapidly; after the Mongol khans converted to Islam in 1295, they fostered a cultural flowering that produced poets Sa'di and Hafiz. The Mongol empire's positive contribution was unprecedented: for the first time, a single dynasty united the civilizations of the Middle East and the Far East, opening overland routes to China.

The Jewish convert Rashid al-Din, serving as vizier, compiled a universal history using a research team that included two Chinese scholars, a Buddhist hermit from Kashmir, a Mongol tribal specialist, and a Frankish monk -- anticipating European universal historiography by half a millennium. But the permanent damage was real. Baghdad and Iraq never recovered their central position. Irrigation works collapsed, civil government broke down, and Bedouin moved into the breaches the Mongols had opened. Unlike the Mongols, the Bedouin stayed. Trade routes shifted north and east to Anatolia and Iran, and south to Egypt and the Red Sea, leaving Iraq to centuries of stagnation.

The Battle of Ayn Jalut in September 1260 was one of the decisive battles in history. The Mamluk army under Baybars , a Kipchak Turk, utterly defeated the Mongol forces. Egypt immediately reoccupied all of Syria; the Mongols never broke through again. The diplomatic aftermath was complex: Mongols and Christian Europe negotiated a joint front against Islam that produced nothing; Baybars countered by allying with the Muslim Mongol Berke Khan of the Golden Horde against the Il-Khans. Baybars made himself sultan and installed an Abbasid refugee as shadow caliph in Cairo -- purely ceremonial, "court pensioners" with no power. This shadow caliphate endured until the Ottomans conquered Egypt in 1517.

Tamerlane (Timur Lang), born a Turkicized Mongol who married into the house of Jenghiz Khan, was the last great convulsion of the steppe. He conquered Central Asia and Iran, defeated the Golden Horde, raided India, overran Syria, and humbled the Mamluks. In 1402, he crushed Ottoman sultan Bayezid at Ankara and captured him; Bayezid later committed suicide. Timur died in 1405 while preparing an invasion of China. Lewis's verdict: "a great conqueror, but no empire-builder." His empire dissolved immediately after his death, marking the end of the age of great steppe migrations that had begun in the tenth century.

The post-Mongol world divided into two cultural zones: a northern Persian-speaking civilization stretching from the Iranian plateau through Anatolia and Central Asia to Mughal India, with Persian as the literary and artistic language; and a southern Arabic-speaking civilization centered on Egypt and extending into the Fertile Crescent and Arabia. The Timurid successors in eastern Iran presided over a brilliant cultural flowering at Bukhara, Samarqand, and Herat -- the classical age of Turkish literary culture.

Sufism, propelled by al-Ghazali's compromise between mysticism and orthodoxy, became the new unifying force across both zones. Al-Ghazali had merged Sufi practice with Sunni theology, and the shock of the Mongol conquest drove theologians and common people into each other's arms. Turkish tribes had been first converted by wandering Sufi monks, not orthodox theologians. Both the Ottoman and Safavid dynasties that would dominate the next era had Sufi origins -- a fact that Lewis considers essential to understanding how the post-Mongol Islamic world reconstituted itself around spiritual authority when political authority had been shattered.

The permanent damage to Iraq deserves emphasis. Baghdad and the Tigris-Euphrates valley never recovered their central position. The elaborate irrigation works collapsed; civil government broke down; Bedouin moved into the breaches left by Mongols -- and unlike the Mongols, they stayed. Trade routes shifted north and east to Anatolia and Iran, and south to Egypt and the Red Sea, leaving Iraq to centuries of stagnation and neglect. The political and cultural center of the Islamic world had moved permanently: Cairo for Arabic civilization, Isfahan and later Istanbul for Persian and Turkish civilization. Baghdad would not matter again until oil gave it new relevance in the twentieth century.

Chapter 7: The Gunpowder Empires

Among the principalities that emerged from the post-Mongol chaos, one grew into an empire. The Ottomans, named for their founder Osman, held a strategic position on the border of Byzantine Bithynia, near Constantinople. They took Bursa in 1326, crossed the Dardanelles into Europe in 1354, and won the battles of Maritza and Kosovo that brought the Balkans under their control. On 29 May 1453, janissaries stormed Constantinople, the last emperor died fighting, and the crescent was raised above Hagia Sophia. Mehmed the Conqueror had achieved what Arab armies had attempted and failed for centuries.

The devshirme system -- the levy of boys from Christian populations for Ottoman state and military service -- was inaugurated in the late fourteenth century and regularized from around 1430. Christian energies were harnessed to the Ottoman state, solving the tension between border-warrior army culture and orthodox Islamic governance. The Janissaries, the "new army," became the empire's elite infantry. Lewis makes the provocative observation that the system, for all its cruelty, offered extraordinary social mobility: "the humblest villager could rise to the highest and most powerful offices in the state" -- a path impossible in contemporary aristocratic Christendom.

The new Safavid dynasty in Iran, brought to power by a radical Shi'ite movement, posed an existential religious threat. Millions of potential Shi'ite sympathizers lived inside Ottoman Anatolia; a dangerous Shi'ite revolt erupted in central Anatolia in 1511. The linguistic irony was delicious: in the angry pre-war correspondence, the Ottoman sultan wrote in Persian (the language of cultivated gentlemen) while the Safavid shah wrote in Turkish (the language of his tribal origins). At the Battle of Chaldiran on 23 August 1514, Ottoman janissaries and artillery crushed Iranian forces, but Selim the Grim did not pursue eastward. The long Ottoman-Safavid struggle continued with sectarian massacres on both sides.

Selim then turned south in a lightning campaign of 1516-17 that overthrew the Mamluk sultanate. Egypt, Syria, and western Arabia fell under Ottoman rule. The sultans now controlled Mecca and Medina -- great prestige and great responsibility. Under Suleyman the Magnificent, the empire reached its zenith: the Battle of Mohacs in 1526 shattered the army of Hungary, and Ottoman fleets challenged the Portuguese in the Indian Ocean. The Elizabethan historian Richard Knolles called the Turkish empire "the present Terror of the World." The first siege of Vienna failed in 1529 but inaugurated 150 years of inconclusive struggle with the Habsburgs.

The Safavids survived Chaldiran and adapted. Shah Abbas (1587-1629) built a new infantry and artillery modeled on Ottoman forces. English brothers Anthony and Robert Shirley arrived in 1598 and served in the Persian military for years. Abbas moved his capital to Isfahan -- Isfahan nisf-i jehan, "Isfahan is half the world" -- and recaptured Hormuz from the Portuguese in 1622 with English East India Company assistance. Meanwhile, Russian expansion pressed from the north: Ivan the Great threw off "the Tatar yoke" in 1480; Ivan IV seized the Volga Tatar capital Kazan in 1552 and the Caspian port Astrakhan in 1556. The Russian road to the Black Sea was not yet open, but it was clearly marked.

Lewis offers a characteristically balanced view of the Mamluk sultanate in its final years, calling it "a kind of Arab Byzantium." In Anatolia and Iran, Turks and Persians had taken cultural and political leadership; Egypt and Syria entered a "long silver age" -- preserving the classical heritage but no longer producing major innovation. The Mamluk refusal to adopt firearms sealed their fate: when Selim's cannon-armed forces met Mamluk cavalry, the result was not a battle but a slaughter. Mehmed the Conqueror had deliberately restrained his eastward ambitions, saying that "to seek the destruction of ancient dynasties of the great sultans of the people of Islam is not good practice." His successors felt no such restraint.

The might of our Empire is such that if it were desired to equip the entire fleet with silver anchors, silken rigging, and satin sail, we could do it. -- Grand Vizier Sokollu after the defeat at Lepanto, 1571 -- real decline already underway but hidden behind bravado

The limits of Ottoman power were already visible. The first siege of Vienna failed in 1529. The second, in 1683, ended in total defeat. The Treaty of Sitvatorok in 1606 was the first negotiated between equals, using the sultan's own title for the Habsburg monarch -- previously just "the king of Vienna." Lewis delivers a memorable verdict: the seventeenth century began with a grudging concession of equality and ended with an unequivocal admission of defeat.

Internal decay tracked military decline. Janissaries, originally celibate slaves recruited from Christian captives, were allowed to enroll their sons from 1568 and opened to anyone who could buy in by the 1590s. They degenerated from an elite force into "a kind of armed rabble, of little use against a disciplined enemy." The feudal cavalry system collapsed; a new class of provincial notables -- the a'yan -- interposed between government and peasantry. Yet Lewis offers a contrarian assessment of Ottoman rule in Europe: in the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries, refugees fled west to east, not east to west. Jews expelled from Spain in 1492 found refuge in Ottoman lands. When Ottoman rule ended, subject Christian nations survived with their languages, cultures, and religions intact. Martin Luther warned in 1541 that the poor, oppressed by greedy German princes, might prefer to live under the Turks.

Chapter 8: The State

The Middle East inherited two models of governance. The Byzantine model maintained a distinction between state and church -- imperfect but real. The emperor bore responsibility for both but could not make himself pope. The Persian model was a military despotism sanctified by a Zoroastrian state church, with an aristocratic closed society governed through a professional bureaucracy. The key strategic difference: Persia's capital Ctesiphon sat exposed on the near side of the Iranian plateau and fell in 637; Constantinople, protected behind the Anatolian plateau, survived all Arab assaults and stood for eight more centuries.

Islam created something fundamentally different. Unlike the Germanic tribes who invaded Rome and adopted its institutions, the Arab conquerors abolished the old order and built their own. Christians had been persecuted for three centuries before Constantine, developing the Church as a separate institution to survive. Muhammad had no such need. During his lifetime, Islam was simultaneously a political and religious allegiance; the Prophet's community in Medina was a state. There was no Caesar. God was the head of the state, and Muhammad his Prophet taught and ruled on his behalf.

In Muslim teaching and experience, there was no Caesar. God was the head of the state, and Muhammad his Prophet taught and ruled on his behalf. -- Bernard Lewis

The caliph was not "pope and emperor in one" because the distinction between spiritual and temporal authority did not exist. His task was to uphold the faith, create conditions for subjects to live as good Muslims, and expand the Islamic state until the whole world was opened to "the light of Islam." Islamic sovereignty was never territorial or ethnic -- there were no equivalents to "King of England." Pre-Islamic Arabian tribal culture provided a powerful check on autocracy: the tribal chief held power only by consent of senior tribesmen who could appoint and dismiss him. Arabic malik (king) was one of God's names; applied to a human ruler, it was pejorative. The Qur'an uses it for Pharaoh, the prototype of unjust tyrannical rule.

Pre-Islamic Arabian tribal culture provided a powerful check on autocracy. The tribal chief (shaykh) held power only by freely given consent of senior tribesmen who could appoint and dismiss him -- first among equals, arbitrator in disputes. The caliphate was conceived as a "super sheikhdom" scaled to all united Muslim tribes. The majlis (tribal council) survived into the early caliphate as a forum where the chief dispensed justice and made political decisions. An early Abbasid supporter lamented the decay: "Our booty, which was shared, has become a perquisite of the rich. Our leadership, which was consultative, has become arbitrary. Our succession, which was by the choice of the community, is now by inheritance."

In practice, caliphal power decayed into the pattern Walter Bagehot described for the British monarchy: the caliph became the "dignified" element (authority, legitimacy) while the sultan became the "efficient" element (actual governance). From the tenth century onward, almost all provinces gave only token recognition -- Friday prayer mention and coinage -- to the caliph. The Ottoman conquest of Egypt in 1517 absorbed the caliphal title into sultanic titulature, where it retained little significance until revived in the late eighteenth century.

The Ottomans made the most ambitious attempt in Islamic history to establish the Holy Law as the effective law of the land, developing the divan-i humayun (imperial council) as a formal institution with prescribed membership, fixed meeting times, and regular agenda. Mehmed II was the first sultan to stop presiding in person, withdrawing behind a screen; Suleyman ceased attending even from behind the screen. The early caliphate inherited existing bureaucracies -- Persian in Iraq and Iran, Christian in Syria and Egypt -- keeping them running and remitting proceeds to the new Arab government. Revenue data from Harun al-Rashid's reign shows eastern provinces yielding 400 million dirhams, with in-kind taxes including elephants from Sind, carpets from Armenia, salted fish, and slaves -- the apparatus of a vast empire that struggled to sustain its own coherence against centrifugal forces.

The early caliphate established three Islamic taxes: kharaj (land tax), jizya (poll tax on non-Muslims), and zakat (tithe paid by Muslims). All rulers supplemented these with extra duties deplored by jurists but collected regardless. The wazir, heading the entire governmental apparatus, was an Abbasid innovation adapted from Sasanid practice. The Ottoman grand vizier's annual income under Suleyman reached roughly 2.5 million aspers. An Ottoman budget from 1669-70 shows revenue of 612 million aspers against expenditure of 637 million, of which 398 million went to the armed forces -- the empire was already spending more than it earned, with the military consuming nearly two-thirds of all revenue.

The Meshveret (consultation) was an Ottoman ad hoc council convened for specific crises, becoming very frequent during the late eighteenth-century emergencies. The formal consultative council traced its origins to the Mongol tribal council (kurultay) via the Persian davan-i buzurg. Lewis's strongest claim about the Ottoman state is that it made "what was perhaps the only really serious attempt, in a Muslim state of high material civilization, to establish the Holy Law of Islam as the effective law of the land." The attempt ultimately failed -- the gap between divine law and administrative reality proved too wide -- but no other Islamic state came closer.

Chapter 9: The Economy

Pre-modern Middle Eastern economic history remains severely underexplored, primarily because most medieval states were overthrown by internal upheavals or external invasions that dispersed their archives. Only the Ottoman archives survive, and those are of "daunting difficulty and immensity." Lewis detects a deep contradiction in Muslim attitudes toward government: the state was both divinely ordained and commonly viewed as evil, contaminating, dangerous. A saying attributed to the Prophet warned that "government and paradise cannot be combined."

Agriculture was the foundation, dependent on two types: river-valley irrigation along the Nile, Tigris-Euphrates, and Central Asian rivers, and inferior rainfall agriculture in Syria and Iran. The Islamic conquests created a remarkable agricultural revolution by unifying territory from Europe to China's borders, enabling the westward movement of crops: rice, sorghum, sugar cane, cotton, watermelons, aubergines, artichokes, oranges, and bananas all traveled under Islamic rule. But the simple wooden wheelless plough of antiquity remained in use because rich river-valley soils gave bountiful harvests without innovation. Lewis identifies a structural explanation: the absence of the European monastery, where educated men applied disciplined minds to farming, and the educated farmer. Educated Middle Easterners did not farm; farmers were not educated.

The vocabulary of Western commerce preserves the memory of Middle Eastern trade dominance: muslin from Mosul, damask from Damascus, gauze from qazz, mohair from mukhayyar, taffeta from Persian taftah. Islamic conquests created the first single political-cultural unit from Morocco to the borders of India, with Arabic as a universal commercial language and the annual hajj pilgrimage stimulating long-distance trade. A sophisticated banking system emerged from the intersection of gold coinage in former Byzantine territories and silver coinage in former Sasanid ones. The Qur'an explicitly permits buying and selling but explicitly bans usury (riba) -- one tradition rates a single act of usury worse than thirty-three acts of fornication. The solution was hiyal shar'iyya ("legal devices") -- formal compliance with the letter of the law while organizing credit, investment, and banking in practice.

Nearly all energy came from human and animal muscle. There were no wheeled vehicles -- shortage of wood and metals made them impractical in arid lands. Ibn Battuta in the fourteenth century thought wheeled carts among Turkish steppe peoples remarkable enough to record. Volney in the eighteenth century noted no wagon or cart in all of Syria -- peasants feared government confiscation. Transport relied on pack animals or water: a camel carries 1,200 pounds, travels 200 miles a day, and goes 17 days without water -- far cheaper than roads and carts in arid climate.

The slave trade was vast and long-lasting. Islamic law restricted enslavement to infidels captured in holy war, cutting off local replenishment and creating insatiable demand from beyond the frontiers. Eunuchs were in high demand for palaces; since castration was forbidden by Islamic law, it was performed on the frontiers before slaves entered Islamic territory. Three source regions supplied the trade: Europe (where "slave" derives from "Slav"), the Eurasian steppe (mainly Turks, later Caucasians), and sub-Saharan Africa. Barbary corsairs raided as far as Iceland in 1627 (capturing 242 people) and Baltimore, Ireland in 1631 (107 persons). Yet the medieval Islamic economy was not primarily slave-based; most slaves were domestic rather than productive laborers.

Silk was the most important luxury good, often a royal monopoly, and its importation from the East was a major theme in the political and military history of transit regions. Incense had been "the oil trade of the ancient world," essential in Greco-Roman temples and Christian churches, but Islam has no use for incense in ritual -- after Islamic expansion, its importance collapsed within the Islamic world. Spices, especially pepper from the Malabar coast, replaced incense as the dominant commodity; spice merchants were wealthy and highly respected.

Lewis's most vivid illustration of economic decline is the coffee-and-sugar inversion. Both were originally Middle Eastern products exported to Europe. By the early eighteenth century, the Dutch were growing coffee in Java, the French were exporting West Indian coffee to Turkey, and colonial sugar had captured the Middle Eastern market. By the end of the century, when a Turk or Arab drank coffee with sugar, both had been grown in European colonies. "Only the hot water was of local provenance." The deeper cause was not the Portuguese voyages but structural: exhaustion of mines, the Black Death, domination by military aristocracies indifferent to commerce, and the quiet capture of Mediterranean trade by Italian cities operating with more active commercial methods.

The pig taboo had broader consequences than dietary restriction. Islam, like Judaism, excluded pigs; some historians argue the prohibition set the geographical limits of Islamic expansion. Muslim faith did not take deep root among pig-rearing peoples in Spain, the Balkans, and western China -- all frontier zones where Islamic rule was eventually reversed. Whether cause or correlation, the observation illuminates how deeply agricultural patterns shaped civilizational boundaries. The fundamental division between peasant and nomadic herder, symbolized in the story of Cain and Abel, ran through Middle Eastern economic life: in Western Europe, agriculture and stock raising were integrated; in the Middle East, an immemorial separation and conflict existed between the two -- and God, in the biblical account, had favored the nomad's offering.

Chapter 10: The Elites

Islamic society recognized a fundamental division between the khassa (the privileged elite) and the amma (the undifferentiated mass). In principle, Islam was radically egalitarian -- no superiority by birth, race, or social status; only piety and good deeds confer honor. In practice, wealth and power holders naturally sought to transmit advantages. Until Ottoman times, most regimes were too short-lived to establish entrenched aristocracies, making the Islamic world, paradoxically, more socially mobile than medieval Europe. What defined the khassa was not primarily birth or wealth but occupation and education -- mastery of Arabic, Persian, or Turkish and command of classical literary traditions.

The one context where Islamic law explicitly discusses social ranking is kafa'a (equality in marriage): a father or guardian can annul a marriage that demeans the family. Malik ibn Anas declared "all the people of Islam are equal to one another," but another school, influenced by Persian hierarchic notions, extended kafa'a to pedigree, profession, and financial status. In early periods, Christian and Jewish poets, scientists, and scholars moved in the same circles as Muslim colleagues in Abbasid Baghdad and Fatimid Cairo; later, non-Muslim communities were gradually isolated from this educated elite.

The bureaucracy may have originated in the Middle East itself, arising to organize irrigation in river valley societies. By the fourth millennium BCE, Egyptian pharaohs were draining swamps and conducting external trade, generating a specialized class of clerks and scribes. This bureaucratic continuity survived every regime change: pharaohs, Hellenistic rulers, Romans, Christian Byzantines, Arab Muslims. After the seventh-century Arab conquests, nothing changed in day-to-day government. Egyptian Christian officials continued collecting the same taxes, writing the same documents, dating by the old Christian era -- only the ultimate destination of revenues changed. "No one could read the accounts except the accountants," and old bureaucratic families kept their craft secrets. As late as the thirteenth and fourteenth centuries, pious Muslims complained that Copts were still running Egyptian administration.

Bureaucrats were known as katib (scribe), wearing a distinctive cloak and led by the vizier, whose ceremonial emblem was an inkpot. Recruitment operated through apprenticeship, not public examination -- public exams were a Chinese invention that reached the Islamic world only via the West -- making patronage a political weapon that persists into modern times. Two types of Islamic learned literature emerged from this division: adab (poetry, history, belles-lettres), the domain of the scribal class, and ilm (religious sciences), the domain of the ulema. Poets served as mass media in the absence of print, and even minor potentates maintained a court poet for the projection of a favorable image. Historians were mostly drawn from the bureaucratic or religious classes; in the later Ottoman Empire, the office of Imperial Historiographer was formalized -- the last holder became the first president of the Ottoman Historical Society.

Physicians formed another elite stratum. Islamic medical knowledge far surpassed Europe in the high Middle Ages -- Rhazes wrote the seminal work on smallpox, Avicenna's Canon dominated European medical studies for centuries. Many leading physicians were Christians and Jews, but they formed part of a single scholarly community with Muslim colleagues. By the early modern period, Islamic medicine had fallen badly behind Europe; not until the nineteenth century did reforming rulers begin sending students to European medical schools. The arc from superiority to stagnation to dependency mirrored the broader trajectory of Islamic civilization itself.

God made you secretaries in the most distinguished positions, men of culture and virtue, of knowledge and discernment. By your means the excellences of the Caliphate are well-ordered. -- 8th-century bureaucrat to his colleagues

The ulema formed a parallel elite. Islam has no priesthood in the theological sense -- no ordination, no sacraments, no priestly mediation. The closest parallel is the Jewish rabbi after the destruction of the Temple: teacher and jurist, not priest. In early centuries, pious ulema viewed state salaries as tainted by extortion, and refusing appointment was a mark of integrity. An unofficial separation of powers evolved: the state conceded exclusive competence of the ulema in matters of Holy Law, while the ulema's aloofness gave them immense moral authority. As ulema moved closer to the state in Ottoman times, popular influence passed to Sufi sheikhs and dervish brotherhoods.

The military elite underwent its own revolution. The Turkish slave army system, inaugurated by caliph al-Mu'tasim in the ninth century, was designed to prevent hereditary military aristocracies: slave soldiers had no kinship networks, each generation replaced by a new intake rather than by sons. The system worked brilliantly -- slave armies defeated both the Crusaders and the Mongols. But its failure mode was predictable: loyalty ran to regiment and officers, not sovereign, and slave commanders eventually became monarchs themselves. The Ottoman Janissaries were the last great slave army, surviving into the nineteenth century. As late as 1863, Egypt sent an expeditionary force to Mexico composed largely of black slaves recruited from the Upper Nile.

Islam's favorable view of commerce distinguished it from medieval Christianity. The Qur'an explicitly approves trade; numerous hadith praise the honest merchant. The ninth-century writer al-Jahiz argued that merchants enjoy security and dignity while government servants endure humiliation. But Islamic land tenure was fundamentally different from European feudalism: the Islamic grantee had no seigneurial rights, could not dispense justice, maintain a private army, or grant sub-fiefs. Autocratic rulers preferred all wealth to derive from the state. Modern technology finally resolved this ancient struggle in favor of the autocrat: "even the pettiest of modern dictators has greater control than even the mightiest of Arab caliphs, Persian shahs and Turkish sultans."

Chapter 11: The Commonalty

All three Abrahamic religions in practice limited full equality to those possessing four characteristics: free, adult, male, co-religionist. The subordinate categories -- slave, child, woman, unbeliever -- each had a path of exit except one. The slave could be freed; the unbeliever could convert; the child grew up. The woman was, in Lewis's blunt phrasing, "irredeemably fixed in her inferiority" under traditional religious law. Islam originally improved women's status over pagan Arabian practice, granting property rights, prohibiting infanticide, and limiting polygamy to four wives. But in inheritance and testimony, a woman counted as half a man.

O, people. We have created you from one male and one female. Indeed the noblest among you in the sight of God is the most God-fearing. -- Qur'an 49:13

Between the categories of free and slave stood the freedman; between male and female, the eunuch -- uniquely able to move between male and female space, and therefore invaluable in palace administration. Between true believer and unbeliever stood Christians and Jews, "people of the book." Each intermediate category occupied a defined social position with specific legal rights and restrictions.

Islamic slavery, while pervasive, differed from the plantation model. Islamic law severely restricted enslavement to non-Muslims captured in war -- no enslaving of free Muslims was permitted. The Islamic world was not primarily a slave-based economy like the Greco-Roman world; agriculture relied on free or semi-free peasants. The main uses were domestic (palaces and homes, mainly African slaves) and military (Turkish and Caucasian). The Baqt Treaty with Nubia (651-62 CE) required Nubians to provide 360 slaves per year in exchange for meat and lentils. The most important slave revolt -- East African slaves in Iraq who rebelled from 868 to 883 CE -- defeated several imperial armies and threatened the caliphate itself. Slavery was abolished in colonial empires in the nineteenth century and in independent Middle Eastern states only in the twentieth.

Islam originally improved women's status over pagan Arabian practice, granting property rights, prohibiting female infanticide, and limiting polygamy to four wives. Polygamy was in practice mostly confined to the wealthy and supplemented by concubinage. But in inheritance and testimony, a woman counted as half a man, and she alone among the subordinate categories had no path of exit from her inferior status.

The dhimmi system for non-Muslims was, by the standards of its time, remarkably tolerant. Christians, Jews, and Zoroastrians accepted subordination in exchange for security of life and property, freedom of worship, and extensive communal autonomy. They paid the jizya poll tax, could not testify in Muslim courts, and wore distinguishing dress -- they could ride only donkeys or mules. In practice, restrictions were frequently unenforced, and dhimmis often acquired great wealth and political influence. Violent outbreaks were rare, usually triggered by excessive exercise of power by dhimmi officials. Lewis notes that dhimmis fared better under Sunni rulers than under sectarian ones, and makes a striking claim: "even at the beginning of the nineteenth century a poor man of humble origin had a better chance of attaining to wealth, power and dignity in the Ottoman Empire than in any of the states of Christian Europe, including post-Revolutionary France."

Radical protest movements throughout Islamic history challenged barriers between rich and poor, Arab and non-Arab, white and black. But Lewis observes that none ever questioned the subordination of slave, woman, or unbeliever -- these were accepted as natural categories that no revolutionary ideology sought to overturn.

The relationship between nomad and settled population defined much of social life: strong government kept nomads quiet; weak government unleashed raids on oases, villages, and caravans. When inspired by a new religious teacher, nomads could conquer settled country and establish entirely new dynasties -- the pattern repeated from the original Arab conquests through the Almoravids in North Africa to the Wahhabis in Arabia.

The Jelali brigands who ravaged Ottoman Anatolia in the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries represent the dark side of this dynamic. Recruited from discharged soldiers, landless peasants, and unemployed madrasa graduates, they ravaged the countryside for decades. Their memory survives in Anatolian folk poetry as a voice of the dispossessed -- a reminder that the social order Lewis describes was held together not by consensus but by the state's ability to suppress those who fell through its cracks.

Chapter 12: Religion and Law

Islam became a majority religion gradually across the conquered territories. The fates of minorities varied enormously: Georgia, Armenia, and Ethiopia retained their Christian character; North African Christianity died out entirely; Zoroastrians fared badly and fled to India as Parsees; Jews received a considerable improvement over their treatment under Christian rule. The word "Islam" itself means "surrender" -- giving oneself wholly to God. Muhammad's mission was understood as the final phase of monotheistic revelation: Moses, David, and Jesus each brought a book; Muhammad brought the Qur'an, completing and superseding all prior scripture.

The mosque is not the Muslim equivalent of a church. There is no institutional hierarchy, no sacraments, no ordained priesthood. The mihrab indicates the direction of Mecca; the minbar serves as pulpit for the Friday sermon. There is no altar, no liturgical music; sculpture is rejected as "blasphemy verging on idolatry." Decoration takes the form of abstract geometric designs and calligraphy -- writing the Qur'an is itself an act of worship. The mosque succeeded the Roman forum and Greek agora as center of political life. The weekly khutba sermon named the ruler; omitting a name was a declaration of independence.

The Shari'a -- the Holy Law -- is what Lewis calls "one of the major intellectual achievements of Islam." In principle, only God's law is valid; no human being possesses legislative power. In practice, new law was made constantly but always in disguise. Solutions came from below as custom, from above as regulations, and from jurists as interpretation. One of the first Muslim visitors to leave a written account of England described the House of Commons with astonishment: the English, lacking a divine law, were "reduced to the expedient of making their own laws in accordance with the necessities of time and circumstance." Four Sunni schools of law -- Hanafi, Shafi'i, Maliki, Hanbali -- coexisted in mutual toleration, justified by ijma (consensus): "My community will not agree upon an error."

Heresy is not a category of Muslim law. The nearest equivalent is bid'a (innovation), primarily a social offense: "Every novelty is an innovation, every innovation is an error, every error leads to Hellfire." The real charge was apostasy, which constituted treason against the community and carried death. A sectarian with extreme beliefs remained legally a Muslim. The dying words attributed to al-Ash'ari capture the principle: "I testify that I do not consider any who pray towards Mecca as infidels. All turn their minds in prayer towards the same object. They differ only in expression."

Jihad -- literally "striving in the path of God" -- was discussed by the overwhelming majority of early authorities in military terms. Every manual of Shari'a law has a chapter on it regulating hostilities, conduct of war, and division of booty. The Muslim jihad was perceived as unlimited, to continue until the whole world adopted Islam or submitted. The Christian Crusade was a delayed, limited response -- concerned with defense and reconquest of lost territory. By the ninth century, repeated failures against Constantinople led Muslim rulers to accept a permanent non-Muslim frontier. When Crusaders arrived in the late eleventh century, Muslim reaction was almost nonexistent -- jihad had faded so completely that some Muslim rulers even allied with Crusader princes against other Muslims. Jihad was revived by Saladin and foundered definitively under the walls of Vienna in 1683.

Paradise is in the shadow of swords. -- Islamic tradition

Sufism began as individual mystical experience and became a mass social movement organized in brotherhoods (tariqas). Worship was mystical, passionate, ecstatic, using music, song, and dance to seek union with God. Sufis "remained part of the people" in a way the official ulema never did. Al-Ghazali, the greatest medieval Islamic theologian, resigned his Baghdad professorship in 1095, spent ten years in solitary study, and found truth in Sufism -- bringing mystical teaching into the Sunni mainstream while defending orthodox positions against both Shi'a esoterism and philosophical rationalism.

Jalal al-Din Rumi (1207-1273), born in Balkh and settled in Konya, was the greatest Sufi poet, writing in Persian, Turkish, and Greek. His Mevlevi brotherhood (the "dancing dervishes") was eventually co-opted by the Ottoman state; by 1648 their head officiated at the girding of the sword of Osman ceremony. But in the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries, when the ulema had developed a doctrine of submission to effective authority, it was the dervish orders who led popular resistance against European imperialism.

I am neither Christian nor Jew, neither Magian nor Muslim. I am not from east or west, not from land or sea. My place is placeless, my trace is traceless. -- Jalal al-Din Rumi

Religion in the Islamic world was not merely belief or worship -- it was "the ultimate basis of identity, the primary focus of loyalty, the sole legitimate source of authority." Ethnic nations and territorial states existed but never acquired the political importance they held in Europe. For Muslims, Jews, and Christians under Muslim rule alike, religious community defined who you were, whom you married, what law governed your life, and where your allegiance lay. This explains why secularism -- the separation of religious from political identity -- has been so much harder to achieve in the Middle East than in Europe, where the Church-state distinction, however imperfect, was embedded in institutional memory from the start.

Chapter 13: Culture

The Middle East stands apart from India and China in two features: diversity and discontinuity. China maintained unbroken continuity from antiquity -- same language, script, and religious tradition. India had the cohesive force of Hinduism, Nagari script, and Sanskrit classics. The Middle East experienced successive waves of Hellenization, Romanization, Christianization, and Islamization that wiped out the greater part of ancient written culture. Ancient languages -- Egyptian, Assyrian, Babylonian, Hittite -- were abandoned and recovered only by Western scholars. The region lacks even a collective name comparable to "India" or "China."

Arabic assumed the role of Greek in the Hellenistic world, Latin in Europe, Sanskrit in South Asia. When Persian and Turkish reappeared as literary languages, both were written in Arabic script with extensive Arabic vocabulary. Lexical fossils reveal the layers of cultural exchange: kimiya (chemistry), falsafa (philosophy), shurta (police, from Roman auxiliary cohort), askar (army, from Latin exercitus). Most remarkably, al-sirat al-mustaqim -- the "straight path" of the Qur'an -- derives from Latin strata, meaning the Roman road.

Classical Islamic perception ranked only literature among civilized arts. Musicians were socially inferior -- slaves or low-status persons -- and the absence of standard notation means Islamic classical music survives only through fallible oral memory. Painters acquired respectable court status only in Ottoman Turkey and Safavid Iran. Sculpture was effectively forbidden throughout. When Mehmed the Conqueror had his portrait painted by the Italian Bellini, his more pious son disposed of the entire collection. Muslim rulers did not put faces on coinage. Medieval Islamic interiors reflected nomadic origins: no tables or chairs, but carpets, mattresses, and cushions -- textile arts dominated.

Arabic poetry begins traditionally in the sixth century with the qasida, the desert encampment ode -- a tribal boast that in court poetry became a panegyric for the sovereign. The Udhri school, named for the tribe of Udhra "whose sons die of love," cultivated unrequited passion as a compromise between sensual desire and religious morality. Wine figures prominently in Arabic, Persian, and Turkish poetry despite the prohibition -- Muslims had to visit non-Muslim subjects to drink, and the Christian convent in Arabic poetry acquired the connotation of a tavern. Sufi poetry used Bacchic and erotic imagery to symbolize mystical union with God, paralleling the Song of Songs. Satire (hija) was understood as a real curse, not mere insult -- the word is cognate with Hebrew hegeh (witchcraft). The poet al-Darimi composed a verse about a woman in a black veil to help a merchant sell unsold black veils in Medina -- Lewis calls it "perhaps the earliest known occurrence of the singing commercial."

Firdawsi's tenth-century Shahnama, the long narrative poem of ancient Iran's gods and heroes, holds a status comparable to the Iliad. The maqama, a short piece in rhymed rhythmic prose combining travelogue, dialogue, and social comment, is considered a masterpiece of Arabic literature. Theatre disappeared from the Middle East in the Islamic period, possibly due to its association with pagan rites. Only the Shi'ite ta'ziya, a passion play commemorating Husayn's martyrdom at Karbala, emerged as a dramatic form -- though despite its centrality in modern Shi'a ceremonies, it dates only from the late eighteenth century.

Islamic historiography was strong from the beginning: the Qur'an itself is full of historical lessons. Three types emerged: heroic narrative of the Prophet's campaigns and early conquests, legal-theological preservation of acts and utterances as precedents, and bureaucratic history aimed at civil servants. But non-Muslim history was considered valueless -- "the meaningless gyrations of ancient pagans." After the struggles of the tenth through thirteenth centuries, history was increasingly written by madrasa-trained scholars whose primary expertise was religious science; the madrasa itself never formally included history in its curriculum. This matters because it meant that the intellectual class most engaged in understanding the past was least equipped to learn from non- Islamic precedents -- a blindness that would prove costly when confronting the Western challenge.

Islamic science during the high Middle Ages far surpassed Europe. Ninth-century patrons commissioned Arabic translations of major Greek works in mathematics, astronomy, physics, chemistry, and medicine -- though they did not translate Greek historians ("pagan gibberish") or poets. "Arabic numerals" came from India; trigonometry was largely a Middle Eastern innovation; algebra was entirely so. Omar Khayyam was famous in the East for algebra, known in the West for his quatrains. Ibn Sina (Avicenna) compiled the Canon, which dominated European medical studies for centuries. Paper reached the Islamic world in 751 CE when Arab forces in Central Asia captured Chinese paper makers after a skirmish; caliph Harun al-Rashid ordered paper used in government because writing on paper could not be erased without leaving traces. But printing met fierce resistance: Bayezid II banned it by decree in 1485. Jewish refugees from Spain introduced presses to the Ottoman Empire, printing in Hebrew and European languages, but not in Turkish or Arabic. Arabic printing was officially authorized only in the early eighteenth century, when Ibrahim Muteferrika, a Hungarian convert, printed just seventeen books before his death in 1745 -- the entire Islamic printing output of its first two decades.

Yet it was the poor, parochial, monochrome culture of Christian Europe that advanced from strength to strength, while the Islamic civilization of the Middle East suffered a loss of creativity, of energy, and of power. -- Bernard Lewis

At its peak, Islamic civilization was "in many ways the apex of human achievement" -- truly multiracial, polyethnic, intercontinental, the first religion to make significant progress toward universal civilization. S.D. Goitein called it the "intermediate civilization," intermediate in time between antiquity and modernity, and in space between southern Europe and Southeast Asia. Lewis poses the implicit question that haunts the rest of the book: in the high Middle Ages, it might have seemed that Islamic rather than Latin Christian civilization offered the greater promise of advancing toward modernity. Why did the opposite happen?

Chapter 14: Challenge

The Islamic worldview divided humanity into the Dar al-Islam (House of Islam) and the Dar al-Harb (House of War). Christian Europe was the one recognized rival -- a world faith with its own universal mission, political support, and military power. For roughly a thousand years, Muslims held the upper hand: Crusader losses in Spain and Sicily were more than offset by the Ottoman advance deep into southeastern Europe. Northern European "barbarians" were beneath notice -- "uncouth primitives offering neither threat nor attraction." The immense Islamic contribution to European civilization -- Greek science preserved and transmitted, Indian numerals, Chinese paper, new crops and cultivation techniques -- flowed largely one way.

The turning point came on 12 September 1683, when Turkish armies withdrew from their second failed siege of Vienna. The Treaty of Carlowitz in 1699 was the first time Ottomans signed peace on terms set by victors; they sought mediation from England and Holland to soften the blow. A contemporary Turkish chronicler called it "a calamitous defeat, of such magnitude that there has never been its like since the first appearance of the Ottoman state." The Treaty of Kucuk Kaynarca in 1774 gave Russia territory, free navigation of the Black Sea, and -- through careful misinterpretation of a clause about a Russian church in Istanbul -- a claim to intervene on behalf of all Orthodox Christian subjects of the sultan. The Russian annexation of Crimea in 1783 was the first loss of an old Muslim land to Christian conquerors.

The most important European contribution to the Islamic world was weaponry. Ottomans adopted firearms on a vast scale, gaining decisive advantage over rival Muslim powers that resisted the new technology. Saladin had justified allowing European merchants into reconquered seaports on exactly these grounds: "there is not one of them that does not bring and sell us weapons of war, to their detriment and to our advantage." The Western expansion from the sea followed, framed by the Portuguese as a continuation of the Crusades and Reconquista. The British Embassy in Istanbul was maintained and paid by the Levant Company, a chartered trading company, until the early nineteenth century -- commerce and diplomacy were inseparable.

Bonaparte's Egypt expedition in 1798 taught a double lesson: a small Western force could conquer a Middle Eastern heartland with ease, and only another Western power could expel it. Local Egyptians played a "comparatively minor role" -- the struggle was mainly between French and British forces. Through the nineteenth century, European powers nibbled at the edges: France took Algeria in 1830, Britain occupied Aden in 1839, the Crimean War of 1853-56 brought direct Western contact with Turkish soil. France occupied Tunisia in 1881, Britain occupied Egypt in 1882, Russia invaded northern Iran in 1911, France established a Morocco protectorate in 1912, and Italy annexed Libya the same year. By 1912, virtually the entire Islamic Middle East was either directly controlled or heavily influenced by European powers. Lewis frames it as a pincer movement formed by the expansion of Europe at both ends since the sixteenth century.

Iran's nineteenth-century history was dominated by Anglo-Russian rivalry. Russia pursued military conquest, annexing lands on both sides of the Caspian. Britain, consolidating India, extended power to Iran's southeastern border. Key milestones came in rapid succession: the British opened the first telegraph in Iran in 1864; the Reuter Concession of 1872 granted a British firm exclusive right to develop mineral resources, establish a bank, and build railways -- cancelled under Russian pressure; in 1879, a Cossack Brigade was established, trained and partly officered by Russia; and the British oil concession of 1901 set the pattern for the next century of Western economic penetration.

The loss of Crimea in 1783 was especially devastating -- the first loss of an old Muslim land to Christian conquerors, "a bitter blow to Muslim pride." The Crimean Tatars had maintained allegiance to the Ottoman sultans, and the Black Sea had been a Turkish-Tatar lake with active trade in foodstuffs and East European slaves. Russia's annexation severed this centuries-old relationship and demonstrated that the Islamic world could not merely lose border territories but forfeit entire Muslim-majority regions to permanent Christian rule.

The Crimean War of 1853-56 paradoxically deepened both Western alliance and Western penetration. It was the first war fought with Western allies on Turkish soil, and direct contacts with the West "brought immense changes." The telegraph arrived during the war, and the region became "hooked on a daily fix of news." Ottoman defeats would have been far worse without Western intervention; the empire's survival increasingly depended on the strategic calculations of powers that had their own reasons for keeping it alive. This dependency -- military survival through foreign alliance, economic survival through foreign loans -- defined the last century of Ottoman existence.

The Islamic contribution to European civilization had been immense and largely one-directional: Greek science and philosophy preserved and transmitted when Europe had forgotten them, Indian numerals, Chinese paper, new crops and cultivation techniques. European contributions to Islam were mainly material and technical: clocks, watches, eyeglasses, telescopes, food plants from the Americas -- maize, potato, tomato, tobacco. But the most consequential European export was the idea that the existing order could be changed. For a civilization founded on the principle that God's law was complete and final, this was the most subversive import of all.

Chapter 15: Change

The economic decline was both relative and absolute. Contributing factors cascaded: rising military costs, price inflation from New World silver, transoceanic routes bypassing Middle Eastern transit trade, chronic unfavorable balance of payments, no agricultural or industrial revolution, and tax-farming that replaced productive land management with short-term extraction. The population numbers are stark: Egypt fell from roughly eight million in Roman times to three and a half million by 1800. Turkey, Syria, and Egypt all had lower populations in 1800 than in 1600. The Turkish share of European trade collapsed from one-half of French commerce in the late sixteenth century to one-twentieth by the late eighteenth; from one-tenth of British commerce to one-hundredth.

Christian minorities -- Greek, Armenian, Jewish -- seized the new commercial opportunities. A 1912 list of forty private bankers in Istanbul showed twelve Greeks, twelve Armenians, eight Jews, five Levantine Europeans, and no Turks. Of thirty-four Istanbul stockbrokers, eighteen were Greek, six Jewish, five Armenian -- again, no Turks. The Middle East shifted from exporting manufactured textiles to importing them, now exporting only raw materials. Even coffee and sugar, originally Middle Eastern products, returned from European colonies.

The social consequences of economic decline reshaped the region's internal balance of power. Christian minorities -- Greek, Armenian, Jewish -- seized the new commercial opportunities as merchants, shippers, and bankers, gaining wealth, education, and influence disproportionate to their numbers. A 1912 list of forty private bankers in Istanbul included twelve Greeks, twelve Armenians, eight Jews, five Levantine Europeans, and no Turks. Of thirty-four stockbrokers, eighteen were Greek, six Jewish, five Armenian -- again, not a single Turk. The Middle East shifted from exporting manufactured textiles to importing them, now exporting only raw materials like cotton, mohair, and silk.

Two critical areas of technological backwardness stood out: shipbuilding and weapons. The Ottoman government hired European naval engineers and bought vessels from Sweden and the United States. Road and canal networks were neglected; wheeled transport was almost unknown at the start of the nineteenth century. Even basic innovations like the water mill and windmill had "little or no impact." The Ottoman road network was so poor that basic goods could not move economically between provinces.

The capitulations system -- privileges originally granted by confident Ottoman sultans to European traders -- became instruments of penetration as Ottoman power declined. European missions distributed berats (protection certificates) to increasing numbers of local merchants, creating near-unlimited rights of interference in Ottoman internal affairs. The Ottoman government began floating loans on European money markets during the Crimean War -- first loan 3 million pounds at 6% in 1854. Between 1854 and 1874, loans were raised almost every year, totaling roughly 200 million pounds nominal. On 6 October 1875, the government defaulted. The resulting Council of the Administration of the Public Debt, controlled by foreign creditors, was granted certain revenues "absolutely and irrevocably until complete liquidation of the debt." By 1911, the Debt Council employed 8,931 people -- more than the Ottoman Ministry of Finance itself.

The traditional Ottoman land system had nearly collapsed. The sipahi cavalryman with his timar was almost extinct by the nineteenth century; vacated timars became tax-farms and then malikane (life tenures that became heritable) -- the economic basis for the a'yan (provincial notables). From the early eighteenth century, derebeys ("valley lords") arose in Anatolia as local hereditary rulers. By the early nineteenth century, almost all of Anatolia was in derebey hands. In the Balkans, Tepedelenli Ali Pasha at Yannina maintained diplomatic relations with foreign powers. Egypt was virtually autonomous. In Arabia, the House of Saud openly flouted Ottoman authority.

The scientific wave broke against the dikes of literature and jurisprudence. -- A distinguished Turkish historian on why the Middle East fell behind

The core diagnosis was not internal collapse but failure to keep pace with Western advances in science, technology, warfare, and governance. Ottoman decline was a relative phenomenon: Europe was changing faster than anyone could follow. Lady Mary Wortley Montagu, writing in 1717, described the ulema as "the only men really considerable in the Empire" -- their lands and revenues untouchable even by the sultan. The religious establishment that preserved social stability also preserved intellectual stagnation. The population numbers tell the story starkly: Egypt fell from roughly eight million in Roman times to three and a half million by 1800. Turkey, Syria, and Egypt all had lower populations in 1800 than in 1600. The Turkish share of European trade collapsed from one-half of French commerce in the late sixteenth century to one-twentieth by the late eighteenth; from one-tenth of British commerce to one-hundredth.

Lewis provides a vivid illustration of the coffee- and-sugar inversion. Both coffee and sugar were originally Middle Eastern products introduced to Europe. By the early eighteenth century, Dutch colonists were growing coffee in Java; French merchants were exporting West Indian coffee to Turkey; colonial sugar dominated the Middle Eastern market. Colbert in 1671 ordered a refinery in Marseilles specifically to export colonial sugar to Turkey. By the end of the eighteenth century, when a Turk or Arab drank coffee with sugar, both had been grown in European colonies. "Only the hot water was of local provenance." The inversion captures in a single domestic scene the entire reversal of the global economic order.

Chapter 16: Response and Reaction

For centuries, Muslims had viewed history as a story of God's truth being extended to all mankind; military victory manifested divine favor. Defeat was therefore theologically devastating, not merely practically inconvenient. Architecture tells the story: the great imperial mosques of Istanbul expressed strength, while the modest Topkapi Palace reflected Islamic restraint -- crowds greeted new sultans with "Sultan, do not be proud, God is greater than you." By 1755, the Nuruosmaniye Mosque showed Italian baroque ornamentation -- "the first sign of faltering self-confidence." By 1853, the lavish Dolmabahce Palace illustrated "the ambitious aims and confused directions of the nineteenth-century reforms."

Military reform was always the entry point, but it pulled everything else behind it. Reformers "intended no more than to open a sluice" but "started a flood they could not control." Educated officers needed training, which required schools; logistics required administrative reform; arms factories required economic reform. Turkish cadets learning French to read military manuals found access to broader European ideas -- "some of the ideas they found proved more explosive than anything their artillery instructors could offer them."

Two major eighteenth-century Islamic responses emerged. The Naqshbandi dervishes, introduced from India, sparked a revival of Arabic learning. The Wahhabis, puritanical and militant, conquered much of the Arabian Peninsula before their power was destroyed in 1818 -- but their faith survived and revived repeatedly. Resistance to Western invaders was led not by sultans or professional soldiers but by popular religious leaders from these revivalist movements.

Resistance to Western invaders was led not by sultans or professional soldiers but by popular religious leaders from these revivalist movements. Lewis notes a telling pattern: every significant anti-colonial resistance movement in the Islamic world drew its energy from religious revival rather than secular nationalism. The dervish orders that had provided spiritual sustenance during times of peace became military organizations during times of invasion, precisely because the official ulema had developed a doctrine of submission to effective authority -- whoever held power deserved obedience. Popular religious leaders felt no such constraint.

From the 1820s onward, periodicals and daily newspapers accelerated the diffusion of European ideas far beyond what military reformers intended. Turkish cadets learning French to read military manuals discovered Voltaire, Rousseau, and Montesquieu. "Some of the ideas they found proved more explosive than anything their artillery instructors could offer them." A new journalistic style emerged: language shifted from court-chronicle prose to an athletic journalistic mode explaining the Polish insurrection, the American Civil War, and Queen Victoria's speeches to Middle Eastern readers.

Reform thinking split into two competing models: authoritarian, which aimed to implement modernization from above without the distraction of popular government, and liberal, which saw freedom as the true basis of Western power and greatness. "Freedom" in early nineteenth-century Middle Eastern usage meant individual liberty from arbitrary rule -- not yet independence from foreign domination. The constitutional experiments were fitful: Tunisia proclaimed the first Islamic constitution in 1861 (suspended 1864); the Ottoman constitution of 1876 lasted roughly five months before Sultan Abdulhamid dismissed the parliament. It did not meet again for thirty years.

Japan's 1905 victory over Russia electrified the region: the first time an Asian power had defeated a European one. Japan had promulgated a constitution in 1889, and the message was unmistakable -- constitutionalism was the "elixir of life." Constitutional revolutions erupted in Iran in 1906 and in the Ottoman Empire in 1908. Pan-Islamism also emerged in the 1860s and 1870s as a movement for a common Muslim front against Christian empires, partly inspired by German and Italian national unifications. The Ottoman Empire was seen as a potential Prussia for Muslims. But pan-Islamism defined solidarity by religion and community, and was ultimately overshadowed by the idea of the nation.

The Persian Constitutional Revolution that began in December 1905 was a direct response to Japan's demonstration. The first Majlis (national assembly) met in October 1906. But the Anglo-Russian Entente of August 1907 divided Iran into a Russian sphere in the north, a British sphere in the south, and a central belt open to both -- effectively strangling the constitutional experiment from outside. The Ottoman Young Turk revolution of 1908, which briefly united all communities in hope, was similarly exploited: Austria-Hungary immediately annexed Bosnia-Herzegovina, and Bulgaria declared independence. The pattern was consistent: every internal reform effort was undercut by external powers who saw instability as opportunity.

Chapter 17: New Ideas

The Renaissance, Reformation, Enlightenment, and Scientific Revolution all passed unnoticed in the Islamic world because they were expressed in Christian terms and therefore deemed irrelevant. The French Revolution was different: the first European upheaval that did not express itself in Christian language. Even presented by some as anti-Christian, it could be examined on its merits by Muslims. The French Embassy in Istanbul became a center of propaganda; revolutionary literature was translated into Turkish, Arabic, Greek, and Armenian. When Austrians, Prussians, and Russians demanded a ban on French revolutionary symbols, the Ottoman Sublime Porte replied with magnificent indifference that it didn't concern itself with "the headgear or footwear of its foreign guests."

Italy, which is inhabited only by a single race speaking the same language and professing the same religion, experiences so many difficulties in its unification. Judge what would happen in Turkey if free scope were given to all the different national aspirations. It would need a century and torrents of blood to establish even a fairly stable state of affairs. -- Ali Pasha, Ottoman Foreign Minister, 1862 -- his estimate actually fell short

The concept of political freedom was genuinely new in the Islamic world. "Free" in Islamic usage meant not-a-slave -- not a political concept. The opposite of tyranny was not freedom but justice, seen as a ruler's duty, not a subject's right. Western-style citizenship -- participation and representation -- entered via revolutionary France. The Ottoman millet system organized non-Muslim communities as self-governing religious groups under their own leaders and laws. When the 1856 reform edict put all communities on an equal footing, even some Greek Christians objected: "The government has put us together with the Jews. We were content with the supremacy of Islam."

European nationalism hit the Christian minorities first and hardest: Greeks, Serbs, Bulgarians, Armenians, eventually Jews. The Phanariots -- Greek patrician families in Istanbul -- had virtually monopolized key Ottoman positions including the Grand Dragoman, who was effectively in charge of foreign relations. When the Greek War of Independence erupted, the Grand Dragoman was summarily hanged on suspicion of rebel sympathy. The Armenians, called the "faithful millet," took over positions vacated by Greeks and cooperated with the Young Turks in the 1908 revolution. But the same forces of nationalism that destroyed Greek-Ottoman symbiosis eventually destroyed Armenian-Ottoman symbiosis too.

Lewis draws a crucial distinction between patriotism -- political duty binding diverse peoples under a common state -- and nationalism, which defined the nation by language, culture, and descent, and could subvert existing order. The state itself might be alien tyranny; freedom meant national independence. Christian minorities pursued three irreconcilable objectives: equal Ottoman citizenship, national independence, and retention of millet privileges. Egypt was uniquely suited for territorial patriotism -- defined sharply by Nile geography with a continuous identity across millennia -- yet acceptance of Egyptian national identity was "slow, gradual, and contested."

The Armenian catastrophe resulted from the collision between Armenian national aspirations and an Ottoman state fighting for survival. Armenians were worse positioned than Balkan Christians: their majority zones were scattered among increasingly hostile Muslim populations. Massacres in 1895-96, carried out partly through the Hamidiye irregular forces authorized by Sultan Abdulhamid, foreshadowed the far larger deportations of 1915. In 1843, Rabbi Yehuda Alkalai of Sarajevo had written an early proto-Zionist text proposing Jewish return to the Holy Land -- evidence that the nationalist idea was spreading to every community within the empire.

Jews had been useful to the Ottomans in the sixteenth century but declined with Ottoman power, replaced in commerce and government by Christians. The broader pattern was clear: every non-Muslim community experienced the nationalist awakening at different speeds, but the trajectory was always the same -- from acceptance of millet subordination to demand for equality to pursuit of independence. The Ottoman Empire could not satisfy all three demands simultaneously, and its failure to do so was not a failure of will but of logic.

Lewis observes that two main interpretive responses to Israel's later survival would emerge from this period's intellectual ferment: the "imperialist tool" explanation, which cast Zionism as an instrument of Western domination and adopted European antisemitic imagery "with the roles reversed"; and self-criticism, which focused on the deficiencies of Arab society itself and saw Israel as part of Western civilization, not merely Western power. Both responses had roots in the nationalist ideas of this chapter.

The cascade of new ideas -- freedom, equality, nationalism, constitutionalism -- arrived in the Middle East within a single century, compressed in time in a way that Europe had not experienced. Europe had centuries to absorb the Renaissance before confronting the Reformation, and centuries more before the Enlightenment demanded political revolution. The Islamic world received all of these simultaneously, each interacting with the others in unpredictable ways. Nationalism undermined pan-Islamism; constitutionalism clashed with traditional authority; freedom as a political concept collided with justice as a religious duty. The result was not orderly modernization but a perpetual state of ideological crisis that continues to define the region.

Chapter 18: From War to War

For more than a century before its dissolution, the Ottoman Empire fought near-continuous warfare. Russia went to war with the Ottomans four times between 1806 and 1878, each ending in significant territorial loss. Muhammad Ali of Egypt, the most capable regional leader, waged diplomatic and military campaigns against the sultan and was prevented from defeating the Ottomans only by European intervention. He made Egypt an autonomous hereditary principality and launched modernization. In Arabia, the alliance between Muhammad ibn Abd al-Wahhab and the House of Saud presented itself as a renewal of original Islamic expansion, but the Ottomans had gunpowder and succeeded where great empires had failed in the seventh century.

The Ottoman constitutional revolution of 1908, which overthrew Sultan Abdulhamid's despotism and briefly united Turks, Armenians, Muslims, Christians, and Jews in the streets, produced one of history's bitterest disappointments. European powers immediately exploited the upheaval: Austria-Hungary annexed Bosnia-Herzegovina, Bulgaria declared independence, Crete announced union with Greece. A cascade of wars followed -- Italy attacked Tripoli in 1911, the Balkan Wars of 1912-13 stripped away European territories, and a coup installed effective military dictatorship.

World War I was catastrophic. The Sultan-Caliph's proclamation of jihad -- hoping British, French, and Russian Muslim subjects would revolt -- failed completely. The Armenian deportations of 1915, carried out as British forces threatened at the Dardanelles, killed "at least hundreds of thousands, perhaps more than a million" through starvation, disease, exposure, and murder. The Arab Revolt led by Sharif Husayn, though militarily minor, was morally devastating: an Arab ruler of the holy places denouncing the Ottoman sultan's jihad. The war ended with the Fertile Crescent divided into League of Nations mandates -- Iraq under British mandate with King Faysal, Syria and Lebanon under France, Palestine under Britain.

Two extraordinary leaders emerged from the wreckage. Mustafa Kemal (Ataturk) organized resistance in Anatolia, expelled foreign forces, abolished the sultanate, and proclaimed a republic, carrying through a comprehensive program of modernization and -- uniquely in the Muslim world -- secularization. Abd al-Aziz ibn Saud captured the Hijaz, proclaimed himself king, and created Saudi Arabia. In the 1930s, the appeal of fascism and Nazism proved strong: recently unified nations that had forcibly united fragmented peoples, enemies of Britain and France, opponents of Jewish immigration to Palestine. As early as 1933, the British-appointed Mufti of Jerusalem contacted the German consul to offer support.

The Zionist movement, emerging in the late nineteenth century from Eastern European Jewish persecution, added another layer of complexity. The Balfour Declaration of November 1917 committed Britain to support a "National Home for the Jews" -- a commitment incorporated into the League of Nations Mandate and which sharpened Arab resistance with each passing year. Direct rule in the mandated territories proved unworkable; mandatory powers shifted to indirect rule through Arab governments, conceding nominal independence while retaining military bases. This too failed: concessions were always too small and too late.

In the 1930s, liberal and constitutional institutions lost their appeal -- alien, ineffective, and associated with hated imperial powers. Fascist Italy and Nazi Germany offered a seductive alternative: recently unified nations that had forcibly united fragmented peoples, enemies of Britain and France, opponents of Jewish immigration. As early as 1933, Hajj Amin al-Husayni (British-appointed Mufti of Jerusalem) contacted the German consul to offer support. Rashid Ali al-Gaylani seized power in Iraq in April 1941 before being overthrown by British-led forces; his support committee became the nucleus of the Ba'th party. Nasser recorded sympathy for Germany; Sadat was by his own account a willing German intelligence cooperator. A Turkish statesman explained his country's last-minute declaration of war: "We wanted to be on the guest list, not on the menu."

The oil revolution transformed everything. Drilling began in Russian-ruled Azerbaijan as early as 1842; the first refinery was built in Baku in 1863; by the eve of the Russian Revolution, Baku provided 95% of Russia's oil. The British concession in Iran, granted to William Knox D'Arcy, became the Anglo-Persian Oil Company. The internal combustion engine -- car, bus, truck -- replaced horse, donkey, and camel, and combined with printing, radio, cinema, and television to produce far-reaching social transformation.

Lewis assesses the Anglo-French balance sheet with characteristic nuance: the primary motive was strategic, not economic -- denial of the region to other powers and protection of India (British) and North Africa (French). The most positive results came in areas given least importance: economic and practical life was materially better in 1939 than in 1914. Middle Easterners got less benefit than those under direct colonial rule, suffering the drawbacks of imperialism without receiving its full advantages. The key legacy was access to English and French, which opened modern science and culture, and an interlude of liberal economy and political freedom more extensive than anything before or after -- institutions that have since been largely abandoned. The greatest mutual service: the Middle East provided Allied base and support facilities against the Axis; the West saved the Middle East from direct experience of Axis rule.

Chapter 19: From Freedom to Freedom

The post-war independence wave produced states that were largely artificial constructs of colonial borders. Few had genuine historical identities -- Egypt and Morocco being the notable exceptions. Lebanon endured civil wars in 1958, 1975, and 1983. In Jordan, the PLO openly challenged Hashimite authority in September 1970 and was defeated in a bloody confrontation called Black September. Yet Lewis observes that these artificial states proved remarkably persistent: no Arab state has been pulled apart, and no two except arguably Yemen have successfully merged.

The Arab-Israel conflict dominates this chapter. On 14 May 1948, Jewish leaders declared the state of Israel, recognized immediately by the United States and the Soviet Union. An estimated 726,000 Palestinian Arabs fled or were driven from their homes and, uniquely, were neither repatriated nor resettled but kept in camps for generations -- only Jordan offered citizenship. Lewis assigns blame with unusual evenhandedness: the wars of 1948 and 1973 were "unmistakably launched by Arab governments"; 1956 and 1982 by Israel; 1967 is hardest to assign -- participants "like characters in a Greek tragedy." The Six-Day War gained Israel all of mandatary Palestine west of the Jordan, the Golan Heights, and Sinai. The 1979 Egypt-Israel peace treaty was the first with any Arab country.

Cold War superpower involvement contained crises but prevented resolution -- Lewis calls the role of international agencies "the conservation of conflict." Stalin initially saw Israel as a tool to undermine the British position, allowing Czechoslovakia to supply weapons; both the US and USSR gave diplomatic support to Israel in 1948-49. The Baghdad Pact of 1955, inducing Iraq to join with Turkey, Iran, and Britain, backfired badly: Arab states saw it as Western reimposition of dominance, and the Soviets leap-frogged it by establishing close relations with Egypt and other Arab states. The ending of the Cold War was "an essential prerequisite" for any Middle Eastern peace process.

The Iran-Iraq War of 1980-88 lasted longer than World War II, with a death toll exceeding all Arab-Israel wars combined. Its multiple dimensions -- Khomeini versus Saddam, Persian versus Arab, Islamic revivalism versus secular modernism, Sunni versus Shia, oil, territory -- made it the region's most complex conflict. National loyalty trumped sectarianism: Arab minorities in southwestern Iran did not rally to Iraq, and Iraqi Shi'a showed little sympathy for the Iranian revolution. Saddam's quasi-victory emboldened him to invade Kuwait in August 1990. The US-led coalition defeated him quickly but left him in power -- installing a replacement would have required mandate-style occupation, and the US had no desire for a "proconsul in Baghdad." Saddam's strategic miscalculation was fundamental: he failed to account for the collapse of the Soviet Union, which meant he could no longer rely on a superpower patron.

Both British-style constitutional monarchies and French-style parliamentary republics collapsed after independence. Axis ideological influence persisted after Axis military defeat -- ethnic nationalism and authoritarian collectivism had greater appeal than liberal individualism. The 1948 defeat triggered violent removal of rulers: military coups in Syria, Egypt, and Iraq produced dictatorships that adopted socialist rhetoric and Soviet organizational models. Pan-Arabism offered the diagnosis that fragmentation caused weakness; the United Arab Republic of 1958, merging Egypt and Syria, was the most ambitious attempt -- Syria seceded in 1961. By the early 1990s, both Arab and Marxist socialism had failed at everything except "underpinning a series of ruthless and pervasive dictatorships."

Lewis calls the change in women's position "the profoundest and most far-reaching" of all Western- influenced transformations. Polygamous marriage was outlawed in Turkey, Tunisia, and pre-revolutionary Iran. But the pattern was counterintuitive: legal emancipation went furthest in Iraq and South Yemen -- both notoriously repressive regimes -- and lagged in Egypt, the most tolerant Arab society, where conservative male public opinion resisted. Women's rights were worst where fundamentalists held power. Oil revenues freed autocratic governments from financial pressures that elsewhere drove democratization: rulers who did not need to tax their citizens did not need to negotiate with them.

Iran's 1979 revolution was distinct: Islamic rather than European-ideological, rejecting both Western models as "infidel." Yet its revolutionary methods were thoroughly European -- summary trials, mass executions, property confiscation, ideological indoctrination owed far more to Robespierre and Stalin than to Muhammad and Ali. It was the first revolution of the electronic age: Khomeini distributed oratory on cassette tapes and directed followers by the direct-dial telephone service installed by the very Shah he was overthrowing. Its international appeal was strongest among Shi'a populations but reached across the Sunni world too -- Khomeini was seen as an Islamic revolutionary leader, not merely a sectarian or Iranian figure.

They alone -- the peoples and governments of the Middle East -- can decide whether and how to use this window of opportunity while, in an interval of their troubled modern history, it remains open. -- Bernard Lewis, closing sentence

Lewis closes with four possible trajectories: new regional powers competing for hegemony; fragmentation and internecine chaos on the Yugoslavia model; a new jihad provoking a new Crusade; or peace -- with themselves, neighbors, and the outside world, sharing spiritual and material resources for a fuller, freer life. Oil revenues had freed autocratic governments from the financial pressures that elsewhere drove democratization: rulers no longer needed to negotiate with taxpayers. An Algerian summed up the oil curse: "Algeria was once the granary of Rome, and now it has to import cereals to make bread. It is rich in oil and gas, and it has a foreign debt of twenty-five billion dollars and two million unemployed." The Middle Eastern peoples are on their own for the first time in two centuries. What they do with that freedom remains the open question.

Trading & Investing Lessons

Institutional Decay as Value Trap

The Janissary corps and Mamluk system both started as elite, meritocratic institutions and degenerated into hereditary guilds protecting privileges over performance. This maps directly to once-great companies that become bureaucratic value traps -- still profitable from legacy assets but incapable of innovation. The Grand Vizier's boast about silver anchors after Lepanto is the corporate equivalent of a declining company touting its balance sheet while market share erodes.

The Coffee-and-Sugar Inversion: Commodity Disruption

Middle Eastern producers invented the coffee and sugar trade, then watched European colonial powers replicate production elsewhere and export it back. This is the archetypal commodity disruption story: the original producer loses pricing power once the product can be replicated. Modern parallels include OPEC's declining pricing power as shale oil, renewables, and efficiency gains eat into demand. First-mover advantage in commodities is temporary.

The Debt Spiral: Ottoman Sovereign Default

The Ottoman Empire's path from first loan in 1854 to default in 1875 to foreign creditor control by 1881 is a textbook sovereign debt crisis. The Debt Council employing more people than the Finance Ministry is the ultimate loss of sovereignty. For investors: watch the ratio of debt service to revenue, not just the debt level. When a borrower needs new loans to service old ones, the endgame is inevitable.

Regime Change as Black Swan

Lewis identifies "critical junctures" -- moments when historical trajectories diverge irreversibly. The Mongol destruction of Baghdad in 1258, the Ottoman failure at Vienna in 1683, the Young Turk revolution of 1908 -- each reshuffled all existing assumptions. In markets, regime changes (new regulations, technological disruption, geopolitical shifts) are the events that make nonsense of backward-looking models. The lesson: position for resilience across scenarios, not optimization within one regime.

The Oil Curse as Dutch Disease

Oil revenues freed Middle Eastern autocrats from taxpayer accountability -- the political equivalent of Dutch Disease, where a single dominant export destroys diversification. Algeria's trajectory from "granary of Rome" to grain importer with $25 billion in foreign debt is the national version of a company that lets a cash-cow product atrophy all other capabilities.

Social Mobility as Alpha Generation

Lewis's observation that a poor man had better chances in the Ottoman Empire than in post- Revolutionary France suggests that open systems generate more talent per capita. In investing, markets and companies that reward merit over connections tend to outperform over time. The devshirme system, for all its cruelty, harnessed talent from the entire population. Closed systems -- hereditary Janissaries, aristocratic French officer corps -- underperform open ones.

Key Takeaways

Muhammad Was His Own Constantine

The fundamental difference between Islam and Christianity is that Islam never developed separate religious and political institutions. Muhammad governed his community; the caliphate was both church and state. This explains why secularism has been so difficult in the Islamic world -- it requires inventing a separation that never existed.

Bureaucracies Outlast Empires

The same Egyptian bureaucrats who served pharaohs served Romans, then Byzantines, then Arab Muslims -- collecting the same taxes, writing the same documents. The administrative machinery was so opaque that new rulers had no choice but to retain the old clerks. Institutions survive by making themselves indispensable.

The Slave Army Paradox

Military slaves were the Middle East's greatest military innovation, defeating Crusaders and Mongols. But the system contained its own failure mode: slave commanders became monarchs, and their corps became hereditary guilds. Every institution designed to prevent entrenchment eventually entrenches itself.

Economic Decline Was Structural, Not Event-Driven

The Portuguese voyages get blamed for Middle Eastern commercial decline, but trade continued for more than a century after Vasco da Gama. The real causes were deeper: exhaustion of mines, military aristocracies indifferent to commerce, absence of agricultural innovation, and the quiet capture of Mediterranean markets by Italian merchants with more active commercial methods.

The Opposite of Tyranny Was Justice, Not Freedom

In Islamic political thought, the remedy for bad government was a just ruler, not institutional checks on power. Political freedom as a right held by citizens was a genuinely foreign concept, imported via revolutionary France. This explains why constitutional experiments failed so consistently: the intellectual infrastructure for limited government did not exist.

Printing Resistance Had Civilizational Consequences

Gutenberg printed in the 1450s; the first authorized Turkish printing press appeared in 1727 -- nearly three centuries later. The resistance was not ignorance but active choice: calligraphers opposed the technology, religious authorities were suspicious, and no market demand existed. The consequences for science, education, and public discourse were incalculable.

Diversity Without Unity

At its peak, Islamic civilization was the most diverse, multicultural society on earth -- truly multiracial, polyethnic, intercontinental. But it lacked the institutional mechanisms to harness that diversity into sustained innovation. The madrasa never included history or natural science in its curriculum; the bureaucracy recruited by patronage, not examination. Diversity is necessary for civilizational greatness but not sufficient.

Contrarian Ideas

Ottoman Rule in Europe Was Better Than Its Reputation

In the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries, refugees fled west to east, not east to west. Jews expelled from Spain in 1492 found safety in Ottoman lands. When Ottoman rule ended, subject Christian nations survived with languages, cultures, and religions intact -- the same cannot be said for Muslims expelled from the Balkans or Spain. Luther warned that the oppressed poor might prefer Turkish rule.

The Mongol Destruction Was Overrated

The caliphate was already a hollow shell when the Mongols arrived. Iran recovered rapidly and produced a cultural flowering under converted Mongol khans. The positive contribution -- opening direct routes between the Middle East and China -- is routinely overlooked. The real permanent damage was not the Mongol invasion itself but the Bedouin who moved into the broken irrigation systems and stayed.

The Portuguese Did Not Cause Commercial Decline

Middle Eastern trade continued for more than a century after Vasco da Gama reached India. The Discoveries accelerated decline but did not cause it. The root causes were internal: exhaustion of mineral resources, military aristocracies indifferent to commerce, and the quiet capture of Mediterranean markets by Italian city-states using more sophisticated commercial methods.

Islamic Civilization, Not European, Was the Better Candidate for Modernity

In the high Middle Ages, Islamic civilization was multiracial, polyethnic, commercially sophisticated, and scientifically advanced. Europe was poor, parochial, and monochrome. An observer in the twelfth century would have bet on Islam to produce modernity. The reversal requires explanation, and Lewis provides it through structural analysis rather than cultural essentialism.

Frameworks

The Five-Part Structural Analysis

Lewis organizes 2,000 years into five frames: antecedents (what came before Islam), the rise of Islam, cross-sectional analysis of institutions, the challenge of modernity, and responses to that challenge. The cross-sectional chapters (8-13) are the analytical core -- they examine the state, economy, elites, common people, religion, and culture as structures rather than events, showing how institutions persisted across regime changes.

Internal vs. External Change

The fundamental distinction between change that arises from within a civilization and change imposed from outside. European cafe culture evolved internally; Middle Eastern coffee-house culture was imported. This framework applies to every domain: military reform, constitutional government, economic development. Imposed change creates resistance; organic change creates ownership.

The Westernization Sequence

Military first, then palace, then officialdom, then urban population, then (partially) rural population. This sequence held across every Middle Eastern country. Music is the last frontier -- the deepest expression of culture and therefore the last to change. The sequence predicts where modernization will succeed (military, technology) and where it will meet strongest resistance (culture, religion, family structure).

Four Post-Cold War Trajectories

Lewis's closing framework: regional hegemonic competition, fragmentation and chaos (the Yugoslavia path), holy war provoking counter-crusade, or peace through shared resources. Written in 1995, the framework proved remarkably prescient -- elements of all four trajectories have played out in the subsequent three decades.

What Didn't Fit

The Silkworm Heist of 552 CE

Two Nestorian monks smuggled silkworm eggs from China to Byzantium in 552 CE, ending the Chinese monopoly on silk production. This single act of industrial espionage reduced the strategic importance of the entire trans-Asian silk trade that had driven centuries of Persian-Byzantine conflict, funded Arabian middlemen, and shaped the geography of power. The reduction in silk-route revenues may have contributed to the weakening of both empires on the eve of the Arab conquests. It is one of history's most consequential thefts, and Lewis mentions it almost in passing.

The Armenian Spy's Coffee-House

The first coffee-house in Vienna was granted to an Armenian who had served as a spy for Austrian intelligence during the second Turkish siege. He received captured coffee beans as his reward and opened the establishment that launched European coffee culture. The detail captures something Lewis loves: a minor figure at the intersection of espionage, commerce, and cultural exchange, whose individual initiative had outsized civilizational consequences. Vienna's coffee-house culture -- which shaped European intellectual life from Mozart to Freud -- began as payment for intelligence work during an Ottoman military disaster.

The Pig Taboo as Geographic Limit

Lewis relays the provocative argument that the Islamic prohibition on pork set the geographical limits of Islamic expansion. Islam did not take deep root among pig-rearing peoples in Spain, the Balkans, and western China -- all frontier zones where Islamic rule was eventually reversed. The pig requires settled, forested conditions incompatible with the pastoral nomadism that accompanied Islamic armies. Whether the taboo was cause or symptom, the correlation between pig-rearing cultures and resistance to permanent Islamization is striking enough to deserve mention.

Ibrahim Muteferrika and the Lost Printing Revolution

When Arabic printing was finally authorized in 1727, the Hungarian convert Ibrahim Muteferrika -- a former Unitarian minister who had converted to Islam and become an Ottoman diplomat -- printed just seventeen books before his death in 1745. The entire Islamic printing output in its first two decades was less than a single European city produced in a month by the 1450s. Muteferrika wrote a treatise arguing that printing would benefit the Ottoman state, and the chief mufti issued a fatwa authorizing it -- but only for non-religious texts. The Qur'an was not printed in the Islamic world until well into the nineteenth century. The gap between Gutenberg (1450s) and authorized Ottoman printing (1727) represents one of the most consequential technological delays in history.

Revenue in Elephants and Pomegranates

Lewis reproduces revenue tabulations from Harun al-Rashid's reign that read like an imperial shopping list: eastern provinces yielded 400 million dirhams; in-kind deliveries from Sind included elephants and aloes wood; from Qumis, silver ingots and pomegranates; from Armenia, embroidered carpets and salted fish. Live deliveries included horses, mules, falcons, and slaves. The sheer variety of tax payments reveals an empire of extraordinary geographic and economic diversity -- and one that had not yet developed the monetary abstraction to convert all revenue into a single currency. By contrast, the Ottoman budget of 1669-70, denominated entirely in aspers, shows both modernization and the flattening of diversity into fungible units.

About the Author

Bernard Lewis (1916-2018) was a British-American historian of Islam and the Middle East, widely regarded as the Western world's foremost scholar of the region for over half a century. Born in London, he studied at the School of Oriental and African Studies and the University of Paris, earning his PhD at age 23. He served in British military intelligence during World War II, then taught at SOAS before moving to Princeton University in 1974, where he held the Cleveland E. Dodge Professorship of Near Eastern Studies.

Lewis was unusual among Western scholars for his command of Arabic, Persian, and Turkish -- all three of the major Islamic languages -- and his extensive use of Ottoman archival sources that other scholars could not read. He was the first Western scholar granted access to the Ottoman archives in Istanbul. His dozens of books span the full range of Middle Eastern history from the Assassins to modern Turkey, and his essays reached a wide general readership through publications like The Atlantic and Foreign Affairs.

Lewis became controversial in his later years for his views on the Iraq War and the "clash of civilizations" thesis. His critics, particularly Edward Said, accused him of Orientalism and essentializing Islamic culture. His defenders pointed to his unmatched linguistic range, archival depth, and the fact that his historical work -- including this book -- consistently shows more nuance and sympathy for Islamic civilization than his political commentary suggested. He died at 101, having shaped the field of Middle Eastern studies more profoundly than any other single scholar.

Why This Book Matters

The Middle East matters because it is the only one-volume history that bridges the pre-Islamic and Islamic eras while maintaining analytical depth across two millennia. Most histories either end with Christianity or begin with Islam, leaving a conceptual gap between antiquity and the medieval Islamic world. Lewis fills it by showing how Roman, Persian, Jewish, and Christian institutional patterns were absorbed, transformed, and sometimes preserved intact under Islamic rule. The bureaucratic continuity from pharaohs through caliphs, the transmission of Greek science through Arabic translation, the survival of Persian administrative culture under new masters -- these threads are invisible without the long view Lewis provides.

The cross-sectional chapters on state, economy, elites, and culture (Chapters 8-13) are particularly valuable because they analyze institutions rather than events. Most narrative histories jump from conquest to conquest; Lewis pauses to ask how governments actually collected taxes, how courts dispensed justice, how merchants moved goods across continents, how poets served as media outlets. This structural approach makes the book useful long after the political details fade. Written in 1995, before 9/11 and the Iraq War reshaped Western perceptions of the region, it has the advantage of scholarly distance that later works often lack.

For anyone trying to understand why the modern Middle East looks the way it does -- why secularism has struggled, why democratization has failed, why oil wealth has not produced broad development, why the Israeli-Palestinian conflict resists resolution -- Lewis provides the deep structural context that no amount of current-affairs reporting can supply. The answer is never one thing; it is two thousand years of institutional evolution, and this book is the best single-volume guide to that evolution in print.
