Glossary

Political & Administrative Terms

Caliph (Khalifa): Successor to the Prophet Muhammad as head of the Muslim community. The title carried both political and religious authority but was never equivalent to a pope -- there was no separate Church to head.

Sultan: Title meaning "power" or "authority," adopted by the Seljuk Turks and later the Ottomans to denote actual governing power, as distinct from the caliph's religious legitimacy.

Wazir (Vizier): Chief minister heading the entire governmental apparatus. An Abbasid innovation adapted from Sasanid Persian practice. The Ottoman grand vizier wielded enormous power.

Divan: Originally a list or register, it came to mean a government department and eventually the imperial council itself. The Ottoman Divan-i Humayun was a formal institution with prescribed membership.

Dhimmi: Protected non-Muslim subject living under Islamic rule. Christians, Jews, and Zoroastrians accepted subordinate status in exchange for security, religious freedom, and communal autonomy.

Millet: Ottoman system of organizing non-Muslim communities as self-governing religious groups under their own leaders and laws. There were four: Muslim, Greek Orthodox, Armenian, and Jewish.

Capitulations: Privileges granted by Ottoman sultans to European states, allowing their citizens to trade without the disabilities imposed on non-Muslim Ottoman subjects. Originally generous; later exploited as Ottoman power declined.

Religious & Legal Terms

Shari'a: The Holy Law of Islam, derived from the Qur'an and the hadith. Lewis calls it "one of the major intellectual achievements of Islam." It governed personal, commercial, and criminal matters.

Ulema: The learned class of Islamic scholars and jurists. Not priests -- Islam has no ordination or sacraments -- but specialists in religious law who wielded enormous moral authority.

Fatwa: A legal opinion issued by a mufti (jurisconsult) on a point of Islamic law. Not binding like a court judgment but highly influential.

Jihad: Literally "striving in the path of God." The overwhelming majority of early authorities discuss it in military terms, though some modern theologians emphasize spiritual interpretation.

Ijtihad: Independent legal reasoning within the Shari'a framework. Sunni jurists declared "the gate of ijtihad closed" around 900 CE; Shi'a jurists never accepted this closure.

Waqf: Pious endowment in perpetuity, typically funding mosques, madrasas, and charitable works. A major source of income for the ulema, independent of state control.

Military Terms

Mamluk: Arabic for "owned" -- a military slave, usually Turkish or Circassian, bought young and trained for war. The system produced both the finest armies and the greatest internal threat to Islamic rulers.

Janissary: Elite Ottoman infantry recruited through the devshirme (levy of Christian boys). Originally celibate and fanatically loyal, they eventually became a hereditary corporation that held sultans hostage.

Devshirme: The Ottoman levy of boys from Christian populations for state and military service. Condemned by Christians but also a vehicle for extraordinary social mobility.

Sipahi: Ottoman feudal cavalryman granted a timar (land fief) in exchange for military service. The backbone of early Ottoman armies, gradually replaced by professional forces.

Cultural & Social Terms

Madrasa: Islamic theological college, the cornerstone of higher education from the 11th century onward. Modeled partly on Ismaili mission schools and systematized by the Seljuk minister Nizam al-Mulk.

Sufi: Islamic mystic. Sufism began as individual spiritual practice and became a mass movement organized in brotherhoods (tariqas). Sufi sheikhs often had more popular influence than the official ulema.

Adab: Secular literary culture -- poetry, history, belles-lettres -- as distinct from religious learning (ilm). The domain of the scribal bureaucratic class.

Introduction: The Coffee-House

Lewis opens with a deceptively simple image: a man sitting in a Middle Eastern coffee-house. He has a chair, a table, coffee, a cigarette, a newspaper, and a television. Every single object surrounding him is a Western import. The chair and table vanished from the region after the Arab conquest and returned only under European influence. Coffee was an Ethiopian bean popularized through Yemen. The cigarette is American tobacco introduced by English merchants. The newspaper is a European invention. Even the coffee-house itself fills a social void created by Islam's prohibition of alcohol -- there were no taverns to gather in.

The key distinction Lewis draws is between internal and external change. In Europe, the objects in a cafe evolved from within the culture. In the Middle East, they arrived from outside, from an alien civilization. This asymmetry defines the entire modern encounter between Islam and the West. Dress tells the story most vividly: clothing in the Middle East functions as an identity marker signaling religion, tribe, and status. Islamic tradition warned that "he who tries to resemble people becomes one of them," making Western dress a matter not of fashion but of allegiance.

We want to wear civilized clothes. -- Kemal Ataturk, for whom civilization meant modern -- that is, Western -- civilization

The sequence of Westernization always followed the same pattern: military first, then palace, then officialdom, then the urban population. Sultan Mahmud II introduced the fez in the 1820s as a reform headgear, and it was reviled as an infidel innovation. A century later, Ataturk abolished the fez and mandated European hats, which were opposed just as fiercely for identical reasons. Headgear was always the last element to change because peaked brims prevented prostration in prayer.

Even states that violently rejected the West kept its political furniture. After Iran's 1979 Islamic Revolution, officials still wore Western coats and trousers -- only the missing necktie marked their refusal. The Islamic Republic maintained a written constitution, a legislative assembly, and elections, all Western forms. Khomeini framed America not as an imperialist but as "the Great Satan" -- a tempter. Satan does not conquer; he seduces. The battle Lewis identifies is between those who hate and fear the seductive power of Western life and those who see it as a new opportunity in a continuing interchange of civilizations.

Music, Lewis notes, is the last frontier. Western art music has made almost no inroads in the Middle East, in striking contrast with Japan and China. Art was the first domain Westernized; music is the last, because music is the final cultural product a newcomer from outside can understand, accept, and perform. The observation is characteristically Lewis: a small cultural fact that illuminates a deep structural truth.

Chapter 1: Before Christianity

At the start of the Christian era, the Middle East was divided between two great imperial powers. The western half belonged to Rome, which controlled the eastern Mediterranean from the Bosphorus to the Nile. The eastern half belonged to Persia, which its own people called Iran. Between them lay the Fertile Crescent, a contested borderland that would remain a battlefield for six centuries. Egypt, the oldest surviving civilization, had been conquered successively by Persians, Macedonians, and Romans, yet retained a distinct identity defined by Nile geography. Most modern country names in the region are conscious evocations of rediscovered antiquity, not evidence of uninterrupted survival.

The linguistic history is a catalogue of erasure and replacement. Egyptian hieroglyphs gave way to demotic, then Coptic, then Arabic after the Islamic conquest. Even Christian Egyptians eventually adopted Arabic; Coptic survives only in liturgy. The name "Syria" appeared first in Greek -- Herodotus thought it a shortened form of Assyria -- then vanished after the Arab conquest, reappearing under European influence in the nineteenth century. Most dramatically, after the Bar-Kokhba revolt of 135 CE, the Romans renamed Judaea as Palestine after the long-forgotten Philistines and rebuilt Jerusalem as Aelia Capitolina with a Jupiter temple on the Temple site. The erasure of Jewish nomenclature was deliberate and nearly total.

Aramaic became the international language of commerce and diplomacy across the entire Fertile Crescent, Persia, and Egypt, surviving today only in Eastern Church rituals and a few remote villages. Arabic, the last Semitic language to enter the region, was confined to Arabia itself. The linguistic groundwork for Islamic civilization was centuries in the making.

You only have I known of all the families of the earth: therefore I will punish you for all your iniquities. -- Amos 3:2

The theological foundations run equally deep. Zoroaster preached one supreme deity locked in cosmic struggle with evil. Cyrus the Mede, after overthrowing Babylon, authorized the Jews' return from captivity and ordered the Temple rebuilt at government expense -- Isaiah treated him as anointed by God. Between the pre- and post-captivity Hebrew Bible, notable theological shifts appeared that plausibly came from Iranian influence: the cosmic struggle between good and evil, judgment after death, and the idea of a messiah at the end of time. Karl Jaspers called the period from 600 to 300 BCE the axial age -- simultaneous spiritual breakthroughs in China, India, Iran, Israel, and Greece that permanently shaped human consciousness.

Only two peoples effectively resisted Hellenistic and Roman cultural domination: the Persians and the Jews, "with very different results." Greeks and Jews shared a remarkable characteristic: they defined the Other by culture and religion rather than by blood, making their barriers crossable. You could become Greek by adopting Greek culture; you could become Jewish by adopting Jewish religion. Rome extended this principle further still, inventing citizenship as a portable, expandable political identity. The accessibility of Greek culture, Jewish religion, and Roman polity all helped prepare the way for Christianity's rise and spread.

Chapter 2: Before Islam

Three developments transformed the Middle East between the first and seventh centuries. Christianity rose from a protest against the Roman order to become its official religion -- Constantine captured the empire for Christianity, and Christianity was in turn captured by the empire. Justinian used the full apparatus of Roman power to impose a single state-approved doctrine. The center of gravity shifted east when Constantine founded Constantinople; after 395 CE, the western empire fell while the eastern Byzantine Empire survived a thousand more years. Throughout, Hellenistic culture penetrated the region so deeply that the New Testament was written in Greek and the Old Testament translated into the Septuagint in Alexandria.

The Perso-Byzantine rivalry was the defining political fact of the pre-Islamic Middle East. The two empires fought continuously, broken only by a single Long Peace from 383 to 502 CE. Shapur I captured the Roman emperor Valerian in battle and had it carved in stone on Iranian mountains, showing the Persian shah with his foot on the emperor's neck -- the relief is still visible today. The wars had deep economic roots: control of the silk trade from China and the spice trade from India. Romans paid for both mainly in gold coin because the Mediterranean world had little else to offer in exchange. Persians profited as middlemen on the overland routes, giving both empires powerful reasons to seek alternative paths that bypassed the other's territory.

Are you not those Romans, who have ten tongues and one deceit? It is strange and unnatural for a Turk to lie. -- A Turkish khan to Byzantine envoys, 576 CE

Along the border, both empires maintained client principalities as buffers. The Nabataeans at Petra served Rome; Palmyra operated the trans-desert trade route until Queen Zenobia made a bid for independence and was defeated by Emperor Aurelian. The collapse of these buffer states left Arabia completely free of external authority by 100 CE, surrounded by small principalities living off caravan trade routes. The stage was being set.

The sixth century brought a dramatic reversal. Renewed Perso-Byzantine conflict transformed Arabia as both empires wooed Arabian peoples, subsidized chiefs, and fought wars by proxy through client kingdoms: Ghassan for Byzantium and Hira for Persia. In Yemen, a king of the Himyarites converted to Judaism around 525 CE, creating the first Jewish monarchy in centuries. Byzantium sent Christian Ethiopia against Jewish Yemen; the Ethiopians crushed southern Arabia but couldn't hold it; Persians moved in. By the end of the sixth century, both great powers had weakened. Arabians had absorbed military tactics, new commodities, and -- crucially -- writing. They had grown dissatisfied with primitive paganism but found neither Christianity nor Zoroastrianism fully satisfying. The Hanif, seekers who abandoned paganism but rejected all existing faiths, became among the earliest converts when a new revelation arrived.

Chapter 3: Origins

Islamic origins are known almost entirely from Muslim scriptures, traditions, and historical memory. There is virtually no contemporary external testimony for decades after the events. Lewis is characteristically honest: "much of the traditional narrative must remain problematic, while the critical history is at best tentative." What is certain is that Muhammad ibn Abdallah, born around 571 CE in the Quraysh tribe of Mecca, received his first revelation on a night in Ramadan when the angel Gabriel appeared to him on Mount Hira.

Recite in the name of thy Lord who created all things, who created man from clots of blood. Recite, for thy Lord is the most generous, who taught by the pen, who taught man what he did not know. -- First verses of the Qur'an, chapter 96

The Hijra of 622 CE -- Muhammad's migration from Mecca to Yathrib, renamed al-Madina -- was the pivotal transformation. In Mecca, Muhammad was a private individual opposing hostile rulers. In Medina, he became a ruler wielding political, military, and religious authority simultaneously. Moses led his people through the wilderness but never entered the promised land; Jesus was crucified. Muhammad conquered his enemies and governed his community. The Umma, which began as a religious community, became a state. It would soon become an empire.

Muhammad died on 8 June 632 CE, the "Seal of the Prophets." His inner circle chose Abu Bakr as the first caliph -- "successor" and "deputy." Within little more than a century, Arab armies had built an empire stretching from the borders of India and China in the east to the Atlantic in the west. The conquest succeeded for several reasons: Arab taxes were lower than what the conquered peoples had been paying; all monotheist religions received legal tolerance without the Byzantine obsession with enforcing doctrinal orthodoxy; and the Arab army possessed unmatched mastery of the desert, which served them as sea power would later serve maritime empires.

The Qur'an was explicit: "There is no compulsion in religion." The war aim was not to impose Islam but to establish Arab political dominion. Lewis insists that "it is the Arabization and Islamization of the peoples of the conquered provinces, rather than the actual military conquest itself, that is the true wonder of the Arab empire." The process unfolded through garrison cities -- Kufa and Basra in Iraq, Fustat in Egypt, Qayrawan in Tunisia -- purpose-built at the edge of desert and farmland, which became engines of cultural transformation.

The conquests unfroze vast accumulated wealth. Tenth-century historian al-Masudi recorded staggering fortunes among the early elite: Caliph Uthman died with 100,000 dinars in cash; al-Zubayr held 50,000 dinars plus 1,000 horses and 1,000 slaves; Talha's Iraqi estates yielded 1,000 dinars per day. Persian governing classes contributed far more to Islamic development than their Byzantine counterparts because Byzantine magnates could withdraw to Constantinople while Persians had nowhere to go. Most consequentially, the Arab conquest ended the Rome-Persia trade route conflict for the first time since Alexander the Great, unifying the entire Middle East in a single commercial system.

But the most dangerous threats came from within. Three of the first four caliphs were assassinated. When Uthman was murdered in 656 by Egyptian mutineers who installed Ali -- the Prophet's cousin and son-in-law -- the resulting schism produced the Shi'a. After Ali's assassination by a Kharijite in 661, Mu'awiya founded the Umayyad dynasty. Succession became hereditary in fact though never in principle. One supporter announced Mu'awiya's heir by pointing first to the candidate, then to his sword: "The Commander of the Faithful is that one! And if he dies, then that one! And if anyone objects, then this one!"

The Battle of Karbala in 680 CE -- where Husayn, grandson of the Prophet, and roughly seventy followers were killed by Umayyad forces -- became the central trauma of Shi'ite consciousness. Lewis observes that the doctrinal differences between Sunni and Shi'a are of minor importance, far less than those dividing rival Christian churches. But the Shi'ite sense of martyrdom and persecution raised a psychological barrier that has never been fully dismantled. Abd al-Malik consolidated Umayyad power by making Arabic the official language of administration and minting Islamic gold coinage inscribed with Qur'anic verses that directly challenged Christian theology: "He is God, one, eternal; he does not beget nor is he begotten."

Chapter 4: The Abbasid Caliphate

On 9 June 747, Abu Muslim, a manumitted Persian slave leading a militant sect, unfurled black flags of revolt in the eastern province of Khurasan. The movement championed the Abbasids, descendants of the Prophet's uncle, and by 750 had destroyed Umayyad power entirely. The common interpretation frames this as a Persian victory over Arabs, but Lewis disagrees. Shi'ism was of Arab origin; the movement served an Arab pretender; Arabic remained the sole language of government. What Arabs lost was not power itself but their exclusive right to its fruits. Under the Umayyads, only full Arabs reached the top. Under the Abbasids, the passport to power was the favor of the ruler, not noble descent.

The new capital, Baghdad -- built by al-Mansur on the Tigris near the ruins of the Persian capital Ctesiphon, reportedly using bricks from Chosroes' palace -- became the center of a cosmopolitan civilization. Iranian cultural influence surged: the Barmecide family, Central Asian Iranians descended from the Buddhist priesthood of Balkh, served as viziers until their dramatic fall under Harun al-Rashid in 803. Sasanid administrative models, court ceremonial, and political texts were translated wholesale into Arabic. A standing army on the Persian model replaced Arab tribal levies.

The Abbasid caliph became an autocrat in the old Middle Eastern style, claiming divine origin for his authority and exercising it through a vast bureaucracy. When al-Ma'mun tried to impose rationalist Mu'tazila theology as state doctrine and persecuted dissenters, the Sunni ulema proved strong enough to resist. His successor al-Mutawakkil was forced to suppress the Mu'tazila to gain popular Sunni support. The lesson was permanent: no subsequent dynasty attempted to dictate doctrine to the religious institution. The attempt at an Erastian Islam failed once and for all.

By God, our booty, which was shared, has become a perquisite of the rich. Our leadership, which was consultative, has become arbitrary. Our succession, which was by the choice of the community, is now by inheritance. -- Sudayf, an early Abbasid supporter, already disillusioned

Structural decline set in quickly. Spain and North Africa became virtually independent. Egypt fell to Turkish praetorian Ahmad ibn Tulun in 868 and was thereafter almost never ruled from Baghdad. A fiscal crisis -- spendthrift court combined with loss of provincial revenues -- forced caliphs to farm out taxes to local governors who became de facto autonomous rulers. Lewis identifies the tragic structural paradox at the heart of Islamic governance: only a stronger state could preserve community cohesion, yet a stronger state necessarily compromised Islamic social ideals. Every rebellion produced more centralized autocracy, pushing the system further from the Islamic ideal and closer to the ancient Middle Eastern model of despotic rule.

By 946, the Shi'ite Buyids of Persia occupied Baghdad itself, keeping the caliph as a useful figurehead. The caliphate became purely titular. Meanwhile, an Iranian national revival produced the Samanid dynasty at Bukhara, whose court fostered the birth of new Persian literature written in Arabic script -- profoundly Islamic but distinctively Persian. The Shi'a split into Twelvers, who followed a moderate path and awaited the return of a hidden imam, and Ismailis, who inherited extremist doctrines and revolutionary methods. The Ismaili Fatimid caliphate, established in North Africa in 908 and transferred to newly built Cairo in 969, became the first dynasty to openly reject Abbasid authority. At its peak, the Fatimid empire controlled all of North Africa, Sicily, Egypt, Syria, and western Arabia, operating a religious propaganda apparatus that functioned like a party machine in a one-party state.

Chapter 5: The Coming of the Steppe Peoples

By the eleventh century, the Islamic world showed signs of deep weakness: fragmentation into autonomous sovereignties, powerless caliphs, most of the empire under Shi'ite rulers, economic deterioration, and a shortage of precious metals pushing the economy toward quasi-feudal arrangements. Attacks came from three directions simultaneously. From Europe, Christians reconquered Sicily and Spain while Crusaders arrived in the Near East. From Africa, the Banu Hilal and Banu Sulaym tribes devastated North Africa. But the most permanent transformation came from the east: the steppe peoples, first Turks, then Mongols.

Muslims first imported Turks as military slaves -- Mamluks -- trained from childhood for war. Turkish commanders gradually displaced Arabs and Persians from military command, beginning a Turkish domination that would last a thousand years. The decisive moment came in 960 when the Karakhanids, an entire free Turkish people beyond the Islamic frontier, converted to Islam en masse. Lewis makes a striking observation: Turks surrendered to Islam more completely than Arabs or Persians. There is no Turkish equivalent to Arab memories of pre-Islamic heroism or Persian pride in pre-Islamic glory. Pre-Islamic Turkish civilizations, religions, and literatures were blotted out and forgotten; even the name "Turk" became synonymous with "Muslim."

The Seljuk grandsons of their namesake crushed existing powers and entered Baghdad in 1055. They adopted the title sultan -- the first rulers to inscribe it on coins. By 1079, Seljuks had seized Syria and Palestine from the Fatimids and conquered most of Anatolia from Byzantium, making it a Muslim Turkish land permanently. Seljuk administration relied on Persian bureaucracy, led by the great minister Nizam al-Mulk, who systematized earlier tax-farming abuses into a formal land-based system that amounted to feudalism. He also established the madrasa system of theological colleges across the empire, modeled partly on Ismaili mission schools -- using the enemy's institutional innovation against them.

The Crusaders arrived during Seljuk internal weakness and established Latin principalities. It took the Kurdish officer Saladin to mount an effective counter-campaign. After abolishing the Fatimid caliphate in Egypt, Saladin launched his jihad in 1187, recaptured Jerusalem, and expelled the Crusaders from all but a narrow coastal strip. Lewis notes that Sunni Muslim contemporaries actually considered Saladin's liquidation of the Fatimid Shi'ite caliphate a greater achievement than defeating the Franks -- the Ismaili threat was seen as far more dangerous than any Christian one.

The most radical opposition came from the Assassins, followers of Hasan-i Sabbah, who seized the mountain fortress of Alamut in 1090 and waged a campaign of political murder against rulers -- including Nizam al-Mulk himself. Their terror was not ended until the Mongol invasions of the thirteenth century. The Assassins were, in Lewis's assessment, the last serious attempt by the Shi'a to overthrow the Sunni establishment by force.

Chapter 6: The Mongol Aftermath

In the spring of 1206, a Mongol prince named Temujin unfurled the white banner with nine horsetails at the sources of the Onon river and took the title Jenghiz Khan. When a caravan of 450 Muslims was pillaged at Utrar and the merchants put to the sword, Jenghiz led his armies across the Jaxartes. By 1220, Bukhara, Samarqand, and all of Transoxania had fallen. His grandson Hulagu crushed even the Assassins in their mountain fortresses. On 20 February 1258, the last Abbasid caliph, al-Musta'sim, was put to death. The House of Abbas, titular head of Sunni Islam for almost five centuries, ceased to reign.

Lewis offers a characteristically contrarian reassessment. The immediate effects were "probably not as great as has sometimes been suggested." The caliphate had already ceased to be effective; the Mongols "did little more than lay the ghost of something that was already dead." Egypt was never conquered. At 'Ayn Jalut in 1260, the Mamluk army under Baybars decisively defeated the Mongols, and they never broke through again. Iran was hard hit but recovered rapidly; after the Mongol khans converted to Islam in 1295, they fostered a cultural flowering that produced poets Sa'di and Hafiz. The Mongol empire's positive contribution was unprecedented: for the first time, a single dynasty united the civilizations of the Middle East and the Far East, opening overland routes to China.

The Jewish convert Rashid al-Din, serving as vizier, compiled a universal history using a research team that included two Chinese scholars, a Buddhist hermit from Kashmir, a Mongol tribal specialist, and a Frankish monk -- anticipating European universal historiography by half a millennium. But the permanent damage was real. Baghdad and Iraq never recovered their central position. Irrigation works collapsed, civil government broke down, and Bedouin moved into the breaches the Mongols had opened. Unlike the Mongols, the Bedouin stayed. Trade routes shifted north and east to Anatolia and Iran, and south to Egypt and the Red Sea, leaving Iraq to centuries of stagnation.

Tamerlane, the last great steppe conqueror, crushed the Ottoman sultan Bayezid at Ankara in 1402 and captured him, but built nothing lasting. His death in 1405 ended the age of great steppe migrations that had begun in the tenth century. The post-Mongol world divided into two cultural zones: a northern Persian-speaking civilization stretching from Iran to Mughal India, and a southern Arabic-speaking civilization centered on Egypt. Sufism, propelled by al-Ghazali's fusion of mysticism and orthodoxy, became the new unifying force. Both the Ottoman and Safavid dynasties that would dominate the next era had Sufi origins.

Chapter 7: The Gunpowder Empires

Among the principalities that emerged from the post-Mongol chaos, one grew into an empire. The Ottomans, named for their founder Osman, held a strategic position on the border of Byzantine Bithynia, near Constantinople. They took Bursa in 1326, crossed the Dardanelles into Europe in 1354, and won the battles of Maritza and Kosovo that brought the Balkans under their control. On 29 May 1453, janissaries stormed Constantinople, the last emperor died fighting, and the crescent was raised above Hagia Sophia. Mehmed the Conqueror had achieved what Arab armies had attempted and failed for centuries.

The new Safavid dynasty in Iran, brought to power by a radical Shi'ite movement, posed an existential religious threat. Millions of potential Shi'ite sympathizers lived inside Ottoman Anatolia. The linguistic irony was delicious: in the angry pre-war correspondence, the Ottoman sultan wrote in Persian (the language of cultivated gentlemen) while the Safavid shah wrote in Turkish (the language of his tribal origins). At the Battle of Chaldiran in 1514, Ottoman janissaries and artillery crushed Iranian forces, but Selim the Grim did not pursue eastward. The long Ottoman-Safavid struggle continued with sectarian massacres on both sides.

Selim then turned south in a lightning campaign of 1516-17 that overthrew the Mamluk sultanate. Egypt, Syria, and western Arabia fell under Ottoman rule. The sultans now controlled Mecca and Medina. Under Suleyman the Magnificent, the empire reached its zenith: the Battle of Mohacs in 1526 shattered Hungary, and Ottoman fleets challenged the Portuguese in the Indian Ocean. An Elizabethan historian called the Turkish empire "the present Terror of the World."

The might of our Empire is such that if it were desired to equip the entire fleet with silver anchors, silken rigging, and satin sail, we could do it. -- Grand Vizier Sokollu after the defeat at Lepanto, 1571 -- real decline already underway but hidden behind bravado

The limits of Ottoman power were already visible. The first siege of Vienna failed in 1529. The second, in 1683, ended in total defeat. The Treaty of Sitvatorok in 1606 was the first negotiated between equals, using the sultan's own title for the Habsburg monarch -- previously just "the king of Vienna." Lewis delivers a memorable verdict: the seventeenth century began with a grudging concession of equality and ended with an unequivocal admission of defeat.

Internal decay tracked military decline. Janissaries, originally celibate slaves recruited from Christian captives, were allowed to enroll their sons from 1568 and opened to anyone who could buy in by the 1590s. They degenerated from an elite force into "a kind of armed rabble, of little use against a disciplined enemy." The feudal cavalry system collapsed; a new class of provincial notables -- the a'yan -- interposed between government and peasantry. Yet Lewis offers a contrarian assessment of Ottoman rule in Europe: in the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries, refugees fled west to east, not east to west. Jews expelled from Spain in 1492 found refuge in Ottoman lands. When Ottoman rule ended, subject Christian nations survived with their languages, cultures, and religions intact. Martin Luther warned in 1541 that the poor, oppressed by greedy German princes, might prefer to live under the Turks.

Chapter 8: The State

The Middle East inherited two models of governance. The Byzantine model maintained a distinction between state and church -- imperfect but real. The emperor bore responsibility for both but could not make himself pope. The Persian model was a military despotism sanctified by a Zoroastrian state church, with an aristocratic closed society governed through a professional bureaucracy. The key strategic difference: Persia's capital Ctesiphon sat exposed on the near side of the Iranian plateau and fell in 637; Constantinople, protected behind the Anatolian plateau, survived all Arab assaults and stood for eight more centuries.

Islam created something fundamentally different. Unlike the Germanic tribes who invaded Rome and adopted its institutions, the Arab conquerors abolished the old order and built their own. Christians had been persecuted for three centuries before Constantine, developing the Church as a separate institution to survive. Muhammad had no such need. During his lifetime, Islam was simultaneously a political and religious allegiance; the Prophet's community in Medina was a state. There was no Caesar. God was the head of the state, and Muhammad his Prophet taught and ruled on his behalf.

In Muslim teaching and experience, there was no Caesar. God was the head of the state, and Muhammad his Prophet taught and ruled on his behalf. -- Bernard Lewis

The caliph was not "pope and emperor in one" because the distinction between spiritual and temporal authority did not exist. His task was to uphold the faith, create conditions for subjects to live as good Muslims, and expand the Islamic state until the whole world was opened to "the light of Islam." Islamic sovereignty was never territorial or ethnic -- there were no equivalents to "King of England." Pre-Islamic Arabian tribal culture provided a powerful check on autocracy: the tribal chief held power only by consent of senior tribesmen who could appoint and dismiss him. Arabic malik (king) was one of God's names; applied to a human ruler, it was pejorative. The Qur'an uses it for Pharaoh, the prototype of unjust tyrannical rule.

In practice, caliphal power decayed into the pattern Walter Bagehot described for the British monarchy: the caliph became the "dignified" element (authority, legitimacy) while the sultan became the "efficient" element (actual governance). The Ottomans made the most ambitious attempt in Islamic history to establish the Holy Law as the effective law of the land, developing the divan as a formal council with prescribed membership and regular agenda. But even they could not escape the structural paradox: early revenue data from Harun al-Rashid's reign shows eastern provinces yielding 400 million dirhams, with in-kind taxes including elephants from Sind, carpets from Armenia, and slaves -- the apparatus of a vast empire that could not sustain its own coherence.

Chapter 9: The Economy

Pre-modern Middle Eastern economic history remains severely underexplored, primarily because most medieval states were overthrown by internal upheavals or external invasions that dispersed their archives. Only the Ottoman archives survive, and those are of "daunting difficulty and immensity." Lewis detects a deep contradiction in Muslim attitudes toward government: the state was both divinely ordained and commonly viewed as evil, contaminating, dangerous. A saying attributed to the Prophet warned that "government and paradise cannot be combined."

Agriculture was the foundation, dependent on two types: river-valley irrigation along the Nile, Tigris-Euphrates, and Central Asian rivers, and inferior rainfall agriculture in Syria and Iran. The Islamic conquests created a remarkable agricultural revolution by unifying territory from Europe to China's borders, enabling the westward movement of crops: rice, sorghum, sugar cane, cotton, watermelons, aubergines, artichokes, oranges, and bananas all traveled under Islamic rule. But the simple wooden wheelless plough of antiquity remained in use because rich river-valley soils gave bountiful harvests without innovation. Lewis identifies a structural explanation: the absence of the European monastery, where educated men applied disciplined minds to farming, and the educated farmer. Educated Middle Easterners did not farm; farmers were not educated.

The vocabulary of Western commerce preserves the memory of Middle Eastern trade dominance: muslin from Mosul, damask from Damascus, gauze from qazz, mohair from mukhayyar, taffeta from Persian taftah. Islamic conquests created the first single political-cultural unit from Morocco to the borders of India, with Arabic as a universal commercial language and the annual hajj pilgrimage stimulating long-distance trade. A sophisticated banking system emerged from the intersection of gold coinage in former Byzantine territories and silver coinage in former Sasanid ones.

The slave trade was vast and long-lasting. Islamic law restricted enslavement to infidels captured in holy war, cutting off local replenishment and creating insatiable demand from beyond the frontiers. Three source regions supplied the trade: Europe (where "slave" derives from "Slav"), the Eurasian steppe (mainly Turks, later Caucasians), and sub-Saharan Africa. Barbary corsairs raided as far as Iceland in 1627 and Baltimore, Ireland in 1631. Yet the medieval Islamic economy was not primarily slave-based; most slaves were domestic rather than productive laborers.

Lewis's most vivid illustration of economic decline is the coffee-and-sugar inversion. Both were originally Middle Eastern products exported to Europe. By the early eighteenth century, the Dutch were growing coffee in Java, the French were exporting West Indian coffee to Turkey, and colonial sugar had captured the Middle Eastern market. By the end of the century, when a Turk or Arab drank coffee with sugar, both had been grown in European colonies. "Only the hot water was of local provenance." The deeper cause was not the Portuguese voyages but structural: exhaustion of mines, the Black Death, domination by military aristocracies indifferent to commerce, and the quiet capture of Mediterranean trade by Italian cities operating with more active commercial methods.

Chapter 10: The Elites

Islamic society recognized a fundamental division between the khassa (the privileged elite) and the amma (the undifferentiated mass). In principle, Islam was radically egalitarian -- no superiority by birth, race, or social status; only piety and good deeds confer honor. In practice, wealth and power holders naturally sought to transmit advantages. Until Ottoman times, most regimes were too short-lived to establish entrenched aristocracies, making the Islamic world, paradoxically, more socially mobile than medieval Europe. What defined the khassa was not primarily birth or wealth but occupation and education -- mastery of Arabic, Persian, or Turkish and command of classical literary traditions.

The bureaucracy may have originated in the Middle East itself, arising to organize irrigation in river valley societies. By the fourth millennium BCE, Egyptian pharaohs were draining swamps and conducting external trade, generating a specialized class of clerks and scribes. This bureaucratic continuity survived every regime change: pharaohs, Hellenistic rulers, Romans, Christian Byzantines, Arab Muslims. After the seventh-century Arab conquests, nothing changed in day-to-day government. Egyptian Christian officials continued collecting the same taxes, writing the same documents, dating by the old Christian era -- only the ultimate destination of revenues changed. "No one could read the accounts except the accountants," and old bureaucratic families kept their craft secrets.

God made you secretaries in the most distinguished positions, men of culture and virtue, of knowledge and discernment. By your means the excellences of the Caliphate are well-ordered. -- 8th-century bureaucrat to his colleagues

The ulema formed a parallel elite. Islam has no priesthood in the theological sense -- no ordination, no sacraments, no priestly mediation. The closest parallel is the Jewish rabbi after the destruction of the Temple: teacher and jurist, not priest. In early centuries, pious ulema viewed state salaries as tainted by extortion, and refusing appointment was a mark of integrity. An unofficial separation of powers evolved: the state conceded exclusive competence of the ulema in matters of Holy Law, while the ulema's aloofness gave them immense moral authority. As ulema moved closer to the state in Ottoman times, popular influence passed to Sufi sheikhs and dervish brotherhoods.

The military elite underwent its own revolution. The Turkish slave army system, inaugurated by caliph al-Mu'tasim in the ninth century, was designed to prevent hereditary military aristocracies: slave soldiers had no kinship networks, each generation replaced by a new intake rather than by sons. The system worked brilliantly -- slave armies defeated both the Crusaders and the Mongols. But its failure mode was predictable: loyalty ran to regiment and officers, not sovereign, and slave commanders eventually became monarchs themselves. The Ottoman Janissaries were the last great slave army, surviving into the nineteenth century. As late as 1863, Egypt sent an expeditionary force to Mexico composed largely of black slaves recruited from the Upper Nile.

Islam's favorable view of commerce distinguished it from medieval Christianity. The Qur'an explicitly approves trade; numerous hadith praise the honest merchant. The ninth-century writer al-Jahiz argued that merchants enjoy security and dignity while government servants endure humiliation. But Islamic land tenure was fundamentally different from European feudalism: the Islamic grantee had no seigneurial rights, could not dispense justice, maintain a private army, or grant sub-fiefs. Autocratic rulers preferred all wealth to derive from the state. Modern technology finally resolved this ancient struggle in favor of the autocrat: "even the pettiest of modern dictators has greater control than even the mightiest of Arab caliphs, Persian shahs and Turkish sultans."

Chapter 11: The Commonalty

All three Abrahamic religions in practice limited full equality to those possessing four characteristics: free, adult, male, co-religionist. The subordinate categories -- slave, child, woman, unbeliever -- each had a path of exit except one. The slave could be freed; the unbeliever could convert; the child grew up. The woman was, in Lewis's blunt phrasing, "irredeemably fixed in her inferiority" under traditional religious law. Islam originally improved women's status over pagan Arabian practice, granting property rights, prohibiting infanticide, and limiting polygamy to four wives. But in inheritance and testimony, a woman counted as half a man.

O, people. We have created you from one male and one female. Indeed the noblest among you in the sight of God is the most God-fearing. -- Qur'an 49:13

The dhimmi system for non-Muslims was, by the standards of its time, remarkably tolerant. Christians, Jews, and Zoroastrians accepted subordination in exchange for security of life and property, freedom of worship, and extensive communal autonomy. They paid the jizya poll tax, could not testify in Muslim courts, and wore distinguishing dress. In practice, restrictions were frequently unenforced, and dhimmis often acquired great wealth and political influence. Violent outbreaks were rare, usually triggered by excessive exercise of power by dhimmi officials. Lewis makes a notable claim: "even at the beginning of the nineteenth century a poor man of humble origin had a better chance of attaining to wealth, power and dignity in the Ottoman Empire than in any of the states of Christian Europe, including post-Revolutionary France."

Radical protest movements throughout Islamic history challenged barriers between rich and poor, Arab and non-Arab, white and black. But Lewis observes that none ever questioned the subordination of slave, woman, or unbeliever -- these were accepted as natural categories. The relationship between nomad and settled population defined much of social life: strong government kept nomads quiet; weak government unleashed raids on oases, villages, and caravans. When inspired by a new religious teacher, nomads could conquer settled country and establish entirely new dynasties. The Jelali brigands who ravaged Ottoman Anatolia in the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries, recruited from discharged soldiers and landless peasants, are commemorated in Anatolian folk poetry as a voice of the dispossessed.

Chapter 12: Religion and Law

Islam became a majority religion gradually across the conquered territories. The fates of minorities varied enormously: Georgia, Armenia, and Ethiopia retained their Christian character; North African Christianity died out entirely; Zoroastrians fared badly and fled to India as Parsees; Jews received a considerable improvement over their treatment under Christian rule. The word "Islam" itself means "surrender" -- giving oneself wholly to God. Muhammad's mission was understood as the final phase of monotheistic revelation: Moses, David, and Jesus each brought a book; Muhammad brought the Qur'an, completing and superseding all prior scripture.

The mosque is not the Muslim equivalent of a church. There is no institutional hierarchy, no sacraments, no ordained priesthood. The mihrab indicates the direction of Mecca; the minbar serves as pulpit for the Friday sermon. There is no altar, no liturgical music; sculpture is rejected as "blasphemy verging on idolatry." Decoration takes the form of abstract geometric designs and calligraphy -- writing the Qur'an is itself an act of worship. The mosque succeeded the Roman forum and Greek agora as center of political life. The weekly khutba sermon named the ruler; omitting a name was a declaration of independence.

The Shari'a -- the Holy Law -- is what Lewis calls "one of the major intellectual achievements of Islam." In principle, only God's law is valid; no human being possesses legislative power. In practice, new law was made constantly but always in disguise. Solutions came from below as custom, from above as regulations, and from jurists as interpretation. One of the first Muslim visitors to leave a written account of England described the House of Commons with astonishment: the English, lacking a divine law, were "reduced to the expedient of making their own laws in accordance with the necessities of time and circumstance." Four Sunni schools of law -- Hanafi, Shafi'i, Maliki, Hanbali -- coexisted in mutual toleration, justified by ijma (consensus): "My community will not agree upon an error."

Heresy is not a category of Muslim law. The nearest equivalent is bid'a (innovation), primarily a social offense: "Every novelty is an innovation, every innovation is an error, every error leads to Hellfire." The real charge was apostasy, which constituted treason against the community and carried death. A sectarian with extreme beliefs remained legally a Muslim. The dying words attributed to al-Ash'ari capture the principle: "I testify that I do not consider any who pray towards Mecca as infidels. All turn their minds in prayer towards the same object. They differ only in expression."

Jihad -- literally "striving in the path of God" -- was discussed by the overwhelming majority of early authorities in military terms. Every manual of Shari'a law has a chapter on it regulating hostilities, conduct of war, and division of booty. The Muslim jihad was perceived as unlimited, to continue until the whole world adopted Islam or submitted. The Christian Crusade was a delayed, limited response -- concerned with defense and reconquest of lost territory. By the ninth century, repeated failures against Constantinople led Muslim rulers to accept a permanent non-Muslim frontier. When Crusaders arrived in the late eleventh century, Muslim reaction was almost nonexistent -- jihad had faded so completely that some Muslim rulers even allied with Crusader princes against other Muslims. Jihad was revived by Saladin and foundered definitively under the walls of Vienna in 1683.

Paradise is in the shadow of swords. -- Islamic tradition

Sufism began as individual mystical experience and became a mass social movement organized in brotherhoods. Al-Ghazali, the greatest medieval Islamic theologian, resigned his Baghdad professorship in 1095, spent ten years in solitary study, and found truth in Sufism -- bringing mystical teaching into the Sunni mainstream. Rumi, the greatest Sufi poet, wrote in Persian, Turkish, and Greek from his home in Konya. In the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries, when the ulema had developed a doctrine of submission to effective authority, it was the dervish orders who led popular resistance against European imperialism. Religion was not merely belief or worship in the Islamic world -- it was "the ultimate basis of identity, the primary focus of loyalty, the sole legitimate source of authority."

Chapter 13: Culture

The Middle East stands apart from India and China in two features: diversity and discontinuity. China maintained unbroken continuity from antiquity -- same language, script, and religious tradition. India had the cohesive force of Hinduism, Nagari script, and Sanskrit classics. The Middle East experienced successive waves of Hellenization, Romanization, Christianization, and Islamization that wiped out the greater part of ancient written culture. Ancient languages -- Egyptian, Assyrian, Babylonian, Hittite -- were abandoned and recovered only by Western scholars. The region lacks even a collective name comparable to "India" or "China."

Arabic assumed the role of Greek in the Hellenistic world, Latin in Europe, Sanskrit in South Asia. When Persian and Turkish reappeared as literary languages, both were written in Arabic script with extensive Arabic vocabulary. Lexical fossils reveal the layers of cultural exchange: kimiya (chemistry), falsafa (philosophy), shurta (police, from Roman auxiliary cohort), askar (army, from Latin exercitus). Most remarkably, al-sirat al-mustaqim -- the "straight path" of the Qur'an -- derives from Latin strata, meaning the Roman road.

Classical Islamic perception ranked only literature among civilized arts. Musicians were socially inferior -- slaves or low-status persons -- and the absence of standard notation means Islamic classical music survives only through fallible oral memory. Painters acquired respectable court status only in Ottoman Turkey and Safavid Iran. Sculpture was effectively forbidden throughout. When Mehmed the Conqueror had his portrait painted by the Italian Bellini, his more pious son disposed of the entire collection. Poetry served as mass media: the poet al-Darimi composed a verse about a woman in a black veil to help a merchant sell unsold black veils in Medina -- Lewis calls it "perhaps the earliest known occurrence of the singing commercial."

Islamic science during the high Middle Ages far surpassed Europe. Ninth-century patrons commissioned Arabic translations of major Greek works in mathematics, astronomy, physics, chemistry, and medicine -- though they did not translate Greek historians ("pagan gibberish") or poets. "Arabic numerals" came from India; trigonometry was largely a Middle Eastern innovation; algebra was entirely so. Omar Khayyam was famous in the East for algebra, known in the West for his quatrains. Ibn Sina (Avicenna) compiled the Canon, which dominated European medical studies for centuries. Paper reached the Islamic world in 751 CE when Arab forces captured Chinese paper makers. But printing met fierce resistance: Bayezid II banned it in 1485, and Arabic printing was officially authorized only in the early eighteenth century.

Yet it was the poor, parochial, monochrome culture of Christian Europe that advanced from strength to strength, while the Islamic civilization of the Middle East suffered a loss of creativity, of energy, and of power. -- Bernard Lewis

At its peak, Islamic civilization was "in many ways the apex of human achievement" -- truly multiracial, polyethnic, intercontinental, the first religion to make significant progress toward universal civilization. S.D. Goitein called it the "intermediate civilization," intermediate in time between antiquity and modernity, and in space between southern Europe and Southeast Asia. Lewis poses the implicit question that haunts the rest of the book: in the high Middle Ages, it might have seemed that Islamic rather than Latin Christian civilization offered the greater promise of advancing toward modernity. Why did the opposite happen?

Chapter 14: Challenge

The Islamic worldview divided humanity into the Dar al-Islam (House of Islam) and the Dar al-Harb (House of War). Christian Europe was the one recognized rival -- a world faith with its own universal mission, political support, and military power. For roughly a thousand years, Muslims held the upper hand: Crusader losses in Spain and Sicily were more than offset by the Ottoman advance deep into southeastern Europe. Northern European "barbarians" were beneath notice -- "uncouth primitives offering neither threat nor attraction." The immense Islamic contribution to European civilization -- Greek science preserved and transmitted, Indian numerals, Chinese paper, new crops and cultivation techniques -- flowed largely one way.

The turning point came on 12 September 1683, when Turkish armies withdrew from their second failed siege of Vienna. The Treaty of Carlowitz in 1699 was the first time Ottomans signed peace on terms set by victors; they sought mediation from England and Holland to soften the blow. A contemporary Turkish chronicler called it "a calamitous defeat, of such magnitude that there has never been its like since the first appearance of the Ottoman state." The Treaty of Kucuk Kaynarca in 1774 gave Russia territory, free navigation of the Black Sea, and -- through careful misinterpretation of a clause about a Russian church in Istanbul -- a claim to intervene on behalf of all Orthodox Christian subjects of the sultan. The Russian annexation of Crimea in 1783 was the first loss of an old Muslim land to Christian conquerors.

Bonaparte's Egypt expedition in 1798 taught a double lesson: a small Western force could conquer a Middle Eastern heartland with ease, and only another Western power could expel it. Local Egyptians played a "comparatively minor role." Through the nineteenth century, European powers nibbled at the edges: France took Algeria in 1830, Britain occupied Aden in 1839 and Egypt in 1882, Russia invaded northern Iran. By 1912, virtually the entire Islamic Middle East was either directly controlled or heavily influenced by European powers. Lewis frames it as a pincer movement formed by the expansion of Europe at both ends since the sixteenth century.

Chapter 15: Change

The economic decline was both relative and absolute. Contributing factors cascaded: rising military costs, price inflation from New World silver, transoceanic routes bypassing Middle Eastern transit trade, chronic unfavorable balance of payments, no agricultural or industrial revolution, and tax-farming that replaced productive land management with short-term extraction. The population numbers are stark: Egypt fell from roughly eight million in Roman times to three and a half million by 1800. Turkey, Syria, and Egypt all had lower populations in 1800 than in 1600. The Turkish share of European trade collapsed from one-half of French commerce in the late sixteenth century to one-twentieth by the late eighteenth; from one-tenth of British commerce to one-hundredth.

Christian minorities -- Greek, Armenian, Jewish -- seized the new commercial opportunities. A 1912 list of forty private bankers in Istanbul showed twelve Greeks, twelve Armenians, eight Jews, five Levantine Europeans, and no Turks. Of thirty-four Istanbul stockbrokers, eighteen were Greek, six Jewish, five Armenian -- again, no Turks. The Middle East shifted from exporting manufactured textiles to importing them, now exporting only raw materials. Even coffee and sugar, originally Middle Eastern products, returned from European colonies.

The capitulations system -- privileges originally granted by confident Ottoman sultans to European traders -- became instruments of penetration as Ottoman power declined. European missions distributed protection certificates to increasing numbers of local merchants, creating near-unlimited rights of interference in Ottoman internal affairs. The Ottoman government began floating loans on European money markets during the Crimean War -- first loan 3 million pounds at 6% in 1854. Between 1854 and 1874, loans were raised almost every year, totaling roughly 200 million pounds. On 6 October 1875, the government defaulted. The resulting Debt Council eventually employed 8,931 people -- more than the Ottoman Ministry of Finance itself.

The scientific wave broke against the dikes of literature and jurisprudence. -- A distinguished Turkish historian on why the Middle East fell behind

The core diagnosis was not internal collapse but failure to keep pace with Western advances in science, technology, warfare, and governance. Ottoman decline was a relative phenomenon: Europe was changing faster than anyone could follow. Lady Mary Wortley Montagu, writing in 1717, described the ulema as "the only men really considerable in the Empire" -- their lands and revenues untouchable even by the sultan. The religious establishment that preserved social stability also preserved intellectual stagnation.

Chapter 16: Response and Reaction

For centuries, Muslims had viewed history as a story of God's truth being extended to all mankind; military victory manifested divine favor. Defeat was therefore theologically devastating, not merely practically inconvenient. Architecture tells the story: the great imperial mosques of Istanbul expressed strength, while the modest Topkapi Palace reflected Islamic restraint -- crowds greeted new sultans with "Sultan, do not be proud, God is greater than you." By 1755, the Nuruosmaniye Mosque showed Italian baroque ornamentation -- "the first sign of faltering self-confidence." By 1853, the lavish Dolmabahce Palace illustrated "the ambitious aims and confused directions of the nineteenth-century reforms."

Military reform was always the entry point, but it pulled everything else behind it. Reformers "intended no more than to open a sluice" but "started a flood they could not control." Educated officers needed training, which required schools; logistics required administrative reform; arms factories required economic reform. Turkish cadets learning French to read military manuals found access to broader European ideas -- "some of the ideas they found proved more explosive than anything their artillery instructors could offer them."

Two major eighteenth-century Islamic responses emerged. The Naqshbandi dervishes, introduced from India, sparked a revival of Arabic learning. The Wahhabis, puritanical and militant, conquered much of the Arabian Peninsula before their power was destroyed in 1818 -- but their faith survived and revived repeatedly. Resistance to Western invaders was led not by sultans or professional soldiers but by popular religious leaders from these revivalist movements.

Reform thinking split into two competing models: authoritarian, which aimed to implement modernization from above without the distraction of popular government, and liberal, which saw freedom as the true basis of Western power and greatness. The constitutional experiments were fitful: Tunisia proclaimed the first Islamic constitution in 1861 (suspended 1864); the Ottoman constitution of 1876 lasted roughly five months before Sultan Abdulhamid dismissed the parliament. It did not meet again for thirty years. Japan's 1905 victory over Russia electrified the region: constitutionalism was suddenly seen as the "elixir of life." Constitutional revolutions erupted in Iran in 1906 and in the Ottoman Empire in 1908.

Chapter 17: New Ideas

The Renaissance, Reformation, Enlightenment, and Scientific Revolution all passed unnoticed in the Islamic world because they were expressed in Christian terms and therefore deemed irrelevant. The French Revolution was different: the first European upheaval that did not express itself in Christian language. Even presented by some as anti-Christian, it could be examined on its merits by Muslims. The French Embassy in Istanbul became a center of propaganda; revolutionary literature was translated into Turkish, Arabic, Greek, and Armenian. When Austrians, Prussians, and Russians demanded a ban on French revolutionary symbols, the Ottoman Sublime Porte replied with magnificent indifference that it didn't concern itself with "the headgear or footwear of its foreign guests."

Italy, which is inhabited only by a single race speaking the same language and professing the same religion, experiences so many difficulties in its unification. Judge what would happen in Turkey if free scope were given to all the different national aspirations. It would need a century and torrents of blood to establish even a fairly stable state of affairs. -- Ali Pasha, Ottoman Foreign Minister, 1862 -- his estimate actually fell short

The concept of political freedom was genuinely new in the Islamic world. "Free" in Islamic usage meant not-a-slave -- not a political concept. The opposite of tyranny was not freedom but justice, seen as a ruler's duty, not a subject's right. Western-style citizenship -- participation and representation -- entered via revolutionary France. The Ottoman millet system organized non-Muslim communities as self-governing religious groups under their own leaders and laws. When the 1856 reform edict put all communities on an equal footing, even some Greek Christians objected: "The government has put us together with the Jews. We were content with the supremacy of Islam."

European nationalism hit the Christian minorities first and hardest: Greeks, Serbs, Bulgarians, Armenians, eventually Jews. Lewis draws a crucial distinction between patriotism -- political duty binding diverse peoples under a common state -- and nationalism, which defined the nation by language, culture, and descent, and could subvert existing order. Christian minorities pursued three irreconcilable objectives: equal Ottoman citizenship, national independence, and retention of millet privileges. The Armenian catastrophe of 1895-96 and the mass deportations during World War I resulted from the collision between Armenian national aspirations and an Ottoman state fighting for survival.

Chapter 18: From War to War

For more than a century before its dissolution, the Ottoman Empire fought near-continuous warfare. Russia went to war with the Ottomans four times between 1806 and 1878, each ending in significant territorial loss. Muhammad Ali of Egypt, the most capable regional leader, waged diplomatic and military campaigns against the sultan and was prevented from defeating the Ottomans only by European intervention. He made Egypt an autonomous hereditary principality and launched modernization. In Arabia, the alliance between Muhammad ibn Abd al-Wahhab and the House of Saud presented itself as a renewal of original Islamic expansion, but the Ottomans had gunpowder and succeeded where great empires had failed in the seventh century.

The Ottoman constitutional revolution of 1908, which overthrew Sultan Abdulhamid's despotism and briefly united Turks, Armenians, Muslims, Christians, and Jews in the streets, produced one of history's bitterest disappointments. European powers immediately exploited the upheaval: Austria-Hungary annexed Bosnia-Herzegovina, Bulgaria declared independence, Crete announced union with Greece. A cascade of wars followed -- Italy attacked Tripoli in 1911, the Balkan Wars of 1912-13 stripped away European territories, and a coup installed effective military dictatorship.

World War I was catastrophic. The Sultan-Caliph's proclamation of jihad -- hoping British, French, and Russian Muslim subjects would revolt -- failed completely. The Armenian deportations of 1915, carried out as British forces threatened at the Dardanelles, killed "at least hundreds of thousands, perhaps more than a million" through starvation, disease, exposure, and murder. The Arab Revolt led by Sharif Husayn, though militarily minor, was morally devastating: an Arab ruler of the holy places denouncing the Ottoman sultan's jihad. The war ended with the Fertile Crescent divided into League of Nations mandates -- Iraq under British mandate with King Faysal, Syria and Lebanon under France, Palestine under Britain.

Two extraordinary leaders emerged from the wreckage. Mustafa Kemal (Ataturk) organized resistance in Anatolia, expelled foreign forces, abolished the sultanate, and proclaimed a republic, carrying through a comprehensive program of modernization and -- uniquely in the Muslim world -- secularization. Abd al-Aziz ibn Saud captured the Hijaz, proclaimed himself king, and created Saudi Arabia. In the 1930s, the appeal of fascism and Nazism proved strong: recently unified nations that had forcibly united fragmented peoples, enemies of Britain and France, opponents of Jewish immigration to Palestine. As early as 1933, the British-appointed Mufti of Jerusalem contacted the German consul to offer support.

The oil revolution transformed everything. Drilling began in Russian-ruled Azerbaijan as early as 1842; by the eve of the Russian Revolution, Baku provided 95% of Russia's oil. The British concession in Iran, which became the Anglo-Persian Oil Company, set the pattern. Lewis assesses the Anglo-French balance sheet with characteristic nuance: the primary motive was strategic, not economic; the most positive results came in areas given least importance; Middle Easterners got less benefit than those under direct colonial rule. The key legacy was access to English and French, which opened modern science and culture, and an interlude of liberal economy and political freedom more extensive than anything before or after -- institutions that have since been largely abandoned.

Chapter 19: From Freedom to Freedom

The post-war independence wave produced states that were largely artificial constructs of colonial borders. Few had genuine historical identities -- Egypt and Morocco being the notable exceptions. Lebanon endured civil wars in 1958, 1975, and 1983. In Jordan, the PLO openly challenged Hashimite authority in September 1970 and was defeated in a bloody confrontation called Black September. Yet Lewis observes that these artificial states proved remarkably persistent: no Arab state has been pulled apart, and no two except arguably Yemen have successfully merged.

The Arab-Israel conflict dominates this chapter. On 14 May 1948, Jewish leaders declared the state of Israel, recognized immediately by the United States and the Soviet Union. An estimated 726,000 Palestinian Arabs fled or were driven from their homes and, uniquely, were neither repatriated nor resettled but kept in camps for generations -- only Jordan offered citizenship. Lewis assigns blame with unusual evenhandedness: the wars of 1948 and 1973 were "unmistakably launched by Arab governments"; 1956 and 1982 by Israel; 1967 is hardest to assign -- participants "like characters in a Greek tragedy." The Six-Day War gained Israel all of mandatary Palestine west of the Jordan, the Golan Heights, and Sinai. The 1979 Egypt-Israel peace treaty was the first with any Arab country.

Cold War superpower involvement contained crises but prevented resolution -- Lewis calls the role of international agencies "the conservation of conflict." The Iran-Iraq War of 1980-88 lasted longer than World War II, with a death toll exceeding all Arab-Israel wars combined. National loyalty trumped sectarianism: Arab minorities in Iran did not rally to Iraq, and Iraqi Shi'a showed little sympathy for the Iranian revolution. Saddam Hussein's quasi-victory emboldened him to invade Kuwait in 1990, miscalculating that the collapse of the Soviet Union had removed his superpower safety net.

Both British-style constitutional monarchies and French-style parliamentary republics collapsed after independence. Axis ideological influence persisted after Axis military defeat -- ethnic nationalism and authoritarian collectivism had greater appeal than liberal individualism. The 1948 defeat triggered violent removal of rulers: military coups in Syria, Egypt, and Iraq produced dictatorships that adopted socialist rhetoric and Soviet organizational models. Pan-Arabism offered the diagnosis that fragmentation caused weakness; the United Arab Republic of 1958, merging Egypt and Syria, was the most ambitious attempt -- Syria seceded in 1961. By the early 1990s, both Arab and Marxist socialism had failed at everything except "underpinning a series of ruthless and pervasive dictatorships."

Iran's 1979 revolution was distinct: Islamic rather than European-ideological, rejecting both Western models as "infidel." Yet its revolutionary methods were thoroughly European -- summary trials, mass executions, property confiscation, ideological indoctrination owed far more to Robespierre and Stalin than to Muhammad and Ali. It was the first revolution of the electronic age: Khomeini distributed oratory on cassette tapes and directed followers by the direct-dial telephone service installed by the very Shah he was overthrowing.

They alone -- the peoples and governments of the Middle East -- can decide whether and how to use this window of opportunity while, in an interval of their troubled modern history, it remains open. -- Bernard Lewis, closing sentence

Lewis closes with four possible trajectories: new regional powers competing for hegemony; fragmentation and internecine chaos on the Yugoslavia model; a new jihad provoking a new Crusade; or peace -- with themselves, neighbors, and the outside world, sharing spiritual and material resources for a fuller, freer life. Oil revenues had freed autocratic governments from the financial pressures that elsewhere drove democratization: rulers no longer needed to negotiate with taxpayers. An Algerian summed up the oil curse: "Algeria was once the granary of Rome, and now it has to import cereals to make bread. It is rich in oil and gas, and it has a foreign debt of twenty-five billion dollars and two million unemployed." The Middle Eastern peoples are on their own for the first time in two centuries. What they do with that freedom remains the open question.

Trading & Investing Lessons

Institutional Decay as Value Trap

The Janissary corps and Mamluk system both started as elite, meritocratic institutions and degenerated into hereditary guilds protecting privileges over performance. This maps directly to once-great companies that become bureaucratic value traps -- still profitable from legacy assets but incapable of innovation. The Grand Vizier's boast about silver anchors after Lepanto is the corporate equivalent of a declining company touting its balance sheet while market share erodes.

The Coffee-and-Sugar Inversion: Commodity Disruption

Middle Eastern producers invented the coffee and sugar trade, then watched European colonial powers replicate production elsewhere and export it back. This is the archetypal commodity disruption story: the original producer loses pricing power once the product can be replicated. Modern parallels include OPEC's declining pricing power as shale oil, renewables, and efficiency gains eat into demand. First-mover advantage in commodities is temporary.

The Debt Spiral: Ottoman Sovereign Default

The Ottoman Empire's path from first loan in 1854 to default in 1875 to foreign creditor control by 1881 is a textbook sovereign debt crisis. The Debt Council employing more people than the Finance Ministry is the ultimate loss of sovereignty. For investors: watch the ratio of debt service to revenue, not just the debt level. When a borrower needs new loans to service old ones, the endgame is inevitable.

Regime Change as Black Swan

Lewis identifies "critical junctures" -- moments when historical trajectories diverge irreversibly. The Mongol destruction of Baghdad in 1258, the Ottoman failure at Vienna in 1683, the Young Turk revolution of 1908 -- each reshuffled all existing assumptions. In markets, regime changes (new regulations, technological disruption, geopolitical shifts) are the events that make nonsense of backward-looking models. The lesson: position for resilience across scenarios, not optimization within one regime.

The Oil Curse as Dutch Disease

Oil revenues freed Middle Eastern autocrats from taxpayer accountability -- the political equivalent of Dutch Disease, where a single dominant export destroys diversification. Algeria's trajectory from "granary of Rome" to grain importer with $25 billion in foreign debt is the national version of a company that lets a cash-cow product atrophy all other capabilities.

Social Mobility as Alpha Generation

Lewis's observation that a poor man had better chances in the Ottoman Empire than in post- Revolutionary France suggests that open systems generate more talent per capita. In investing, markets and companies that reward merit over connections tend to outperform over time. The devshirme system, for all its cruelty, harnessed talent from the entire population. Closed systems -- hereditary Janissaries, aristocratic French officer corps -- underperform open ones.

Key Takeaways

Muhammad Was His Own Constantine

The fundamental difference between Islam and Christianity is that Islam never developed separate religious and political institutions. Muhammad governed his community; the caliphate was both church and state. This explains why secularism has been so difficult in the Islamic world -- it requires inventing a separation that never existed.

Bureaucracies Outlast Empires

The same Egyptian bureaucrats who served pharaohs served Romans, then Byzantines, then Arab Muslims -- collecting the same taxes, writing the same documents. The administrative machinery was so opaque that new rulers had no choice but to retain the old clerks. Institutions survive by making themselves indispensable.

The Slave Army Paradox

Military slaves were the Middle East's greatest military innovation, defeating Crusaders and Mongols. But the system contained its own failure mode: slave commanders became monarchs, and their corps became hereditary guilds. Every institution designed to prevent entrenchment eventually entrenches itself.

Economic Decline Was Structural, Not Event-Driven

The Portuguese voyages get blamed for Middle Eastern commercial decline, but trade continued for more than a century after Vasco da Gama. The real causes were deeper: exhaustion of mines, military aristocracies indifferent to commerce, absence of agricultural innovation, and the quiet capture of Mediterranean markets by Italian merchants with more active commercial methods.

The Opposite of Tyranny Was Justice, Not Freedom

In Islamic political thought, the remedy for bad government was a just ruler, not institutional checks on power. Political freedom as a right held by citizens was a genuinely foreign concept, imported via revolutionary France. This explains why constitutional experiments failed so consistently: the intellectual infrastructure for limited government did not exist.

Printing Resistance Had Civilizational Consequences

Gutenberg printed in the 1450s; the first authorized Turkish printing press appeared in 1727 -- nearly three centuries later. The resistance was not ignorance but active choice: calligraphers opposed the technology, religious authorities were suspicious, and no market demand existed. The consequences for science, education, and public discourse were incalculable.

Diversity Without Unity

At its peak, Islamic civilization was the most diverse, multicultural society on earth -- truly multiracial, polyethnic, intercontinental. But it lacked the institutional mechanisms to harness that diversity into sustained innovation. The madrasa never included history or natural science in its curriculum; the bureaucracy recruited by patronage, not examination. Diversity is necessary for civilizational greatness but not sufficient.

Contrarian Ideas

Ottoman Rule in Europe Was Better Than Its Reputation

In the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries, refugees fled west to east, not east to west. Jews expelled from Spain in 1492 found safety in Ottoman lands. When Ottoman rule ended, subject Christian nations survived with languages, cultures, and religions intact -- the same cannot be said for Muslims expelled from the Balkans or Spain. Luther warned that the oppressed poor might prefer Turkish rule.

The Mongol Destruction Was Overrated

The caliphate was already a hollow shell when the Mongols arrived. Iran recovered rapidly and produced a cultural flowering under converted Mongol khans. The positive contribution -- opening direct routes between the Middle East and China -- is routinely overlooked. The real permanent damage was not the Mongol invasion itself but the Bedouin who moved into the broken irrigation systems and stayed.

The Portuguese Did Not Cause Commercial Decline

Middle Eastern trade continued for more than a century after Vasco da Gama reached India. The Discoveries accelerated decline but did not cause it. The root causes were internal: exhaustion of mineral resources, military aristocracies indifferent to commerce, and the quiet capture of Mediterranean markets by Italian city-states using more sophisticated commercial methods.

Islamic Civilization, Not European, Was the Better Candidate for Modernity

In the high Middle Ages, Islamic civilization was multiracial, polyethnic, commercially sophisticated, and scientifically advanced. Europe was poor, parochial, and monochrome. An observer in the twelfth century would have bet on Islam to produce modernity. The reversal requires explanation, and Lewis provides it through structural analysis rather than cultural essentialism.

Frameworks

The Five-Part Structural Analysis

Lewis organizes 2,000 years into five frames: antecedents (what came before Islam), the rise of Islam, cross-sectional analysis of institutions, the challenge of modernity, and responses to that challenge. The cross-sectional chapters (8-13) are the analytical core -- they examine the state, economy, elites, common people, religion, and culture as structures rather than events, showing how institutions persisted across regime changes.

Internal vs. External Change

The fundamental distinction between change that arises from within a civilization and change imposed from outside. European cafe culture evolved internally; Middle Eastern coffee-house culture was imported. This framework applies to every domain: military reform, constitutional government, economic development. Imposed change creates resistance; organic change creates ownership.

The Westernization Sequence

Military first, then palace, then officialdom, then urban population, then (partially) rural population. This sequence held across every Middle Eastern country. Music is the last frontier -- the deepest expression of culture and therefore the last to change. The sequence predicts where modernization will succeed (military, technology) and where it will meet strongest resistance (culture, religion, family structure).

Four Post-Cold War Trajectories

Lewis's closing framework: regional hegemonic competition, fragmentation and chaos (the Yugoslavia path), holy war provoking counter-crusade, or peace through shared resources. Written in 1995, the framework proved remarkably prescient -- elements of all four trajectories have played out in the subsequent three decades.

What Didn't Fit

The Silkworm Heist of 552 CE

Two Nestorian monks smuggled silkworm eggs from China to Byzantium in 552 CE, ending the Chinese monopoly on silk production. This single act of industrial espionage reduced the strategic importance of the entire trans-Asian silk trade that had driven centuries of Persian-Byzantine conflict, funded Arabian middlemen, and shaped the geography of power. The reduction in silk-route revenues may have contributed to the weakening of both empires on the eve of the Arab conquests. It is one of history's most consequential thefts, and Lewis mentions it almost in passing.

The Armenian Spy's Coffee-House

The first coffee-house in Vienna was granted to an Armenian who had served as a spy for Austrian intelligence during the second Turkish siege. He received captured coffee beans as his reward and opened the establishment that launched European coffee culture. The detail captures something Lewis loves: a minor figure at the intersection of espionage, commerce, and cultural exchange, whose individual initiative had outsized civilizational consequences. Vienna's coffee-house culture -- which shaped European intellectual life from Mozart to Freud -- began as payment for intelligence work during an Ottoman military disaster.

The Pig Taboo as Geographic Limit

Lewis relays the provocative argument that the Islamic prohibition on pork set the geographical limits of Islamic expansion. Islam did not take deep root among pig-rearing peoples in Spain, the Balkans, and western China -- all frontier zones where Islamic rule was eventually reversed. The pig requires settled, forested conditions incompatible with the pastoral nomadism that accompanied Islamic armies. Whether the taboo was cause or symptom, the correlation between pig-rearing cultures and resistance to permanent Islamization is striking enough to deserve mention.

Ibrahim Muteferrika and the Lost Printing Revolution

When Arabic printing was finally authorized in 1727, the Hungarian convert Ibrahim Muteferrika -- a former Unitarian minister who had converted to Islam and become an Ottoman diplomat -- printed just seventeen books before his death in 1745. The entire Islamic printing output in its first two decades was less than a single European city produced in a month by the 1450s. Muteferrika wrote a treatise arguing that printing would benefit the Ottoman state, and the chief mufti issued a fatwa authorizing it -- but only for non-religious texts. The Qur'an was not printed in the Islamic world until well into the nineteenth century. The gap between Gutenberg (1450s) and authorized Ottoman printing (1727) represents one of the most consequential technological delays in history.

Revenue in Elephants and Pomegranates

Lewis reproduces revenue tabulations from Harun al-Rashid's reign that read like an imperial shopping list: eastern provinces yielded 400 million dirhams; in-kind deliveries from Sind included elephants and aloes wood; from Qumis, silver ingots and pomegranates; from Armenia, embroidered carpets and salted fish. Live deliveries included horses, mules, falcons, and slaves. The sheer variety of tax payments reveals an empire of extraordinary geographic and economic diversity -- and one that had not yet developed the monetary abstraction to convert all revenue into a single currency. By contrast, the Ottoman budget of 1669-70, denominated entirely in aspers, shows both modernization and the flattening of diversity into fungible units.

About the Author

Bernard Lewis (1916-2018) was a British-American historian of Islam and the Middle East, widely regarded as the Western world's foremost scholar of the region for over half a century. Born in London, he studied at the School of Oriental and African Studies and the University of Paris, earning his PhD at age 23. He served in British military intelligence during World War II, then taught at SOAS before moving to Princeton University in 1974, where he held the Cleveland E. Dodge Professorship of Near Eastern Studies.

Lewis was unusual among Western scholars for his command of Arabic, Persian, and Turkish -- all three of the major Islamic languages -- and his extensive use of Ottoman archival sources that other scholars could not read. He was the first Western scholar granted access to the Ottoman archives in Istanbul. His dozens of books span the full range of Middle Eastern history from the Assassins to modern Turkey, and his essays reached a wide general readership through publications like The Atlantic and Foreign Affairs.

Lewis became controversial in his later years for his views on the Iraq War and the "clash of civilizations" thesis. His critics, particularly Edward Said, accused him of Orientalism and essentializing Islamic culture. His defenders pointed to his unmatched linguistic range, archival depth, and the fact that his historical work -- including this book -- consistently shows more nuance and sympathy for Islamic civilization than his political commentary suggested. He died at 101, having shaped the field of Middle Eastern studies more profoundly than any other single scholar.

Why This Book Matters

The Middle East matters because it is the only one-volume history that bridges the pre-Islamic and Islamic eras while maintaining analytical depth across two millennia. Most histories either end with Christianity or begin with Islam, leaving a conceptual gap between antiquity and the medieval Islamic world. Lewis fills it by showing how Roman, Persian, Jewish, and Christian institutional patterns were absorbed, transformed, and sometimes preserved intact under Islamic rule. The bureaucratic continuity from pharaohs through caliphs, the transmission of Greek science through Arabic translation, the survival of Persian administrative culture under new masters -- these threads are invisible without the long view Lewis provides.

The cross-sectional chapters on state, economy, elites, and culture (Chapters 8-13) are particularly valuable because they analyze institutions rather than events. Most narrative histories jump from conquest to conquest; Lewis pauses to ask how governments actually collected taxes, how courts dispensed justice, how merchants moved goods across continents, how poets served as media outlets. This structural approach makes the book useful long after the political details fade. Written in 1995, before 9/11 and the Iraq War reshaped Western perceptions of the region, it has the advantage of scholarly distance that later works often lack.

For anyone trying to understand why the modern Middle East looks the way it does -- why secularism has struggled, why democratization has failed, why oil wealth has not produced broad development, why the Israeli-Palestinian conflict resists resolution -- Lewis provides the deep structural context that no amount of current-affairs reporting can supply. The answer is never one thing; it is two thousand years of institutional evolution, and this book is the best single-volume guide to that evolution in print.
