## Preface

- Lewis fills the gap between histories that **end with Christianity** or **start with Islam** — Persia and Byzantium were co-rulers of the region for centuries and deserve serious treatment, not just backdrop status
- Methodology: reduces political narrative to minimum; focuses on **social, economic, and cultural change**; uses chronicles, travellers, inscriptions, poetry, anecdote
- Goal: establish continuity between ancient and modern Middle East; Hellenization/Romanization/Christianization obliterated the *memory* (not all traces) of ancient civilizations

## Part I: Introduction

### The Coffee-House as Lens
- Lewis uses a **Middle Eastern man in a coffee-house** — chair, table, coffee, cigarette, newspaper, TV — as a device to map Western impact on the region
- Key distinction: changes in European café culture arose **from within**; changes in the Middle Eastern café came **from outside**, from alien cultures
- Every object in that coffee-house — chair, table, cigarette, newspaper, radio, clock — is a Western import

### Dress and Identity
- Clothing in the Middle East functions as **identity marker**: signals ethnic group, religious denomination, tribe, region, occupation
- Islamic traditions: *'Do not dress like the infidels'*; *'The turban is the barrier between unbelief and the Faith'*; *'He who tries to resemble people becomes one of them'*
- In Turkish, *şapka giymek* ("to put on a hat") formerly meant to become a renegade — as loaded as "to turn one's coat" in English
- **Westernization sequence**: military first → palace → officialdom → urban population

### Headgear: The Deepest Symbol
- Ottoman gravestones carved with the deceased's specific headgear (kadi's cap, janissary's sleeve cover, etc.)
- **Sultan Mahmud II** (r. 1808–1839) introduced the **fez** (*tarbush*) as a reform headgear — initially reviled as infidel innovation, eventually became a Muslim symbol
- **Kemal Atatürk** abolished the fez in 1925, mandating European hats — opposed as fiercely as its introduction, for identical reasons
- Practical obstacle: peaked/brimmed European hats prevent prostration in prayer — even in Westernized armies, headgear was the **last** element to change

### Mongol Dress Adoption — A Historical Parallel
- 13th c.: Muslim rulers (even in Egypt, never conquered) adopted Mongol dress after Mongol military victories — Mongol dress was **the dress of military victory**
- Continued until 1315: Sultan of Egypt ordered officers to shear their Mongol-style locks — after Mongol rulers converted to Islam
- Lewis's point: no similar restoration occurred with Western military dress. The Western uniform remains

> 'We want to wear civilized clothes.' — Atatürk. For him, civilization meant modern, that is, Western, civilization.

- After Iran's 1979 Islamic Revolution: officials still wore Western coats and trousers — only the **missing necktie** marked their refusal
- Women's dress: far greater resistance; Atatürk never legally prohibited the veil. Veil abolition in Turkey came via **social osmosis**, not legal force
- Even anti-Western states wear the Western "coat" in political form: **written constitution**, **legislative assembly**, **elections** — all maintained in the Islamic Republic of Iran

### Furniture, Coffee, and Printing
- Tables and chairs existed in antiquity, then **disappeared after the Arab conquest** — Arabs came from a land of scarce wood; furnishing was wool/leather
- Ottoman miniatures (early 18th c.) show European visitors identifiable precisely because **they alone sit on chairs**
- **Coffee**: Ethiopia → southern Arabia → Istanbul; two Syrians opened the first Istanbul coffee-houses under **Sultan Süleyman** (1520–1566); the Aleppo owner reportedly returned home after three years with **5,000 gold pieces profit**
- **Sultan Murad IV** (1633) prohibited both coffee and tobacco and ordered executions; tobacco eventually declared lawful in a *fatwa* by chief mufti **Mehmed Bahai Efendi** — who had himself been exiled for smoking — on the legal principle that **all that is not forbidden is permitted**
- **Tobacco**: introduced by English merchants, early 17th c., of American origin

### The Press
- The **alphabet** is a Middle Eastern invention; **paper** arrived from China in the 8th c.; **printing** bypassed the Middle East en route from China to the West
- Spanish Jewish refugees expelled in 1492 brought printed books and the knowledge to produce them
- First Muslim printing press in Istanbul in the **early 18th c.**, staffed by Jewish and Christian typesetters
- **Moroccan ambassador al-Ghassani** (1690) described European newspapers as 'the writing mills which publish reports, purporting to contain the news, but full of sensational lies'
- **1795**: French establish *Gazette Française de Constantinople* — first newspaper in the region
- **Napoleon in Egypt** (1798): French newspapers published in Cairo
- **Muhammad 'Ali** and **Sultan Mahmud II** — rivals who both founded local vernacular presses (Muhammad Ali first)
- **Crimean War** (1850s): telegraph arrived — **decisive turning point**; the region became "hooked on a daily fix of news"
- New journalistic style: language shifted from court-chronicle prose to athletic journalistic style — explaining the **Polish insurrection, American Civil War, Queen Victoria's speeches** to Middle Eastern readers

> 'When Muslims complain about the two great dangers of imperialists and missionaries, they are right at least in this respect; it was the imperialists and the missionaries who gave them the daily newspaper.' — Lewis

### Broadcasting and Media
- Radio inaugurated in Turkey in 1925 — only three years after London; Egypt 1934
- **Italian fascist government** inaugurated regular Arabic radio service from Bari in 1935 — first foreign propaganda broadcasting in Arabic, triggering a propaganda war
- **Iranian Revolution of 1979**: Khomeini's orations distributed on cassettes — "the first electronically operated revolution in world history"

### Portraiture and Cinema
- Ruler's portrait on the coffee-house wall is a **radical innovation** — Islamic tradition prohibits representational images
- **Sultan Mehmed II** had his portrait painted by Italian **Bellini**; his more pious son disposed of the collection
- Islam's rejection of representational art → **abstract/geometrical design** and **Qur'anic inscriptions** as primary decorative mode
- Silent films in Egypt from **1897**; local films by **1917**; Egyptian film industry now third largest in the world after USA and India

### Music: The Last Frontier
- Western art music has made almost no inroads — striking contrast with Japan and China
- **Art** was the first domain Westernized; **music** is the last — because music is the final cultural product a newcomer from outside can understand, accept, and perform

### The Closing Argument
- Khomeini's framing: USA as 'the Great Satan' — not an imperialist but a **tempter**. "Satan does not conquer; he seduces."

> 'The battle is still going between those who hate and fear the seductive power of the Western way of life, and those who see it as a new advance and a new opportunity in a continuing and fruitful interchange of cultures and civilizations.'

## Part II: Antecedents

## Chapter 1: Before Christianity

### The Geopolitical Frame
- At the start of the Christian era, the Middle East was divided between **two imperial powers**:
  - Western half: **Roman Empire** (eastern Mediterranean, Bosphorus to Nile delta)
  - Eastern half: **Persian Empire** (called 'Iran' by its own people)
- Most ancient surviving culture: **Egypt** — civilization thousands of years old despite successive conquests by Persians, Macedonians, and Romans; defined sharply by Nile geography
- Modern country names that appear continuous are often **conscious evocations of rediscovered antiquity**, not uninterrupted survival

### Languages and Names
- **Egypt**: hieroglyphic → demotic → **Coptic** (Egyptian in Greek alphabet); after Islamic conquest, even Christian Egyptians adopted Arabic; Coptic survives only in liturgy
- **Iraq**: Persian imperial capital at **Ctesiphon** (near Baghdad); "Baghdad" is Persian for "God gave"; medieval Arab usage of "Iraq" denoted only the southern half
- **Syria**: "Syria" first appears in Greek; **Herodotus** says it's shortened Assyria; name disappeared after Arab conquest (7th century); region called **Sham**; reappeared under European influence in the 19th c.; officially became a country name with the **French Mandate** after WWI
- **Palestine**: "Canaan" in the Hebrew Bible → "Judah" + "Israel" after David/Solomon → **Judaea** under Rome → after **Bar-Kokhba revolt (135 CE)** Romans renamed the country **Palestine** (after the long-forgotten Philistines), obliterating Jewish nomenclature; Jerusalem → **Aelia Capitolina** with a Jupiter temple on the Temple site
- **Persia**: strictly refers only to the province of **Pars/Fars**; name imposed on the world in **1935**: **Iran** — from ancient *aryānam* meaning "land of the Aryans"
- **Aramaic** became the international language of commerce and diplomacy across the Fertile Crescent, Persia, and Egypt; survives today only in Eastern Church rituals and a few remote villages
- **Arabic** was the last Semitic language to enter the region; confined to Arabia at the start of the Christian era

### Origins of Monotheism
- Pre-conquest Middle East: syncretistic, polytheistic; eastern cults (**Isis, Adonis, Cybele**) spread to Rome itself
- **Akhenaton** (pharaoh, 14th c. BCE) wrote hymns to one god — sporadic and local; Jews were the first to make **ethical monotheism** permanent

> 'You only have I known of all the families of the earth: therefore I will punish you for all your iniquities' — Amos 3:2

### Zoroastrianism and the Jewish-Persian Connection
- **Zoroaster** (6th–5th c. BCE): one supreme deity (good) in constant struggle with evil
- **586 BCE**: **Nebuchadnezzar** destroyed Jerusalem, sent Jews into Babylonian captivity
- **Cyrus the Mede** overthrew Babylonians, authorized the Jews' return, ordered the Temple rebuilt at government expense; Isaiah 44:28 treats him as anointed by God
- Between pre- and post-captivity Hebrew Bible: notable theological shifts plausibly from Iranian influence: **cosmic struggle between good and evil**, **judgment after death**, **messiah** at the end of time
- **Karl Jaspers'** "**axial age**" (600–300 BCE): simultaneous spiritual breakthroughs — Confucius/Lao-Tse in China, Buddha in India, Zoroaster in Iran, the prophets in Israel, philosophers in Greece
- **Ardashir** (226–240 CE) restored **Zoroastrianism** as state religion — possibly the **first example in history** of a state religion with state-imposed orthodoxy and a hierarchic priesthood concerned with detecting and repressing heresy
- Resistance to Islam in Iran came **not** from orthodox Zoroastrian priesthood (too tied to state power) but from **Zoroastrian heretics** — those accustomed to opposition

### Major Zoroastrian Heresies
- **Mithraism**: won many followers in the Roman Empire, especially the military; temples found as far as England
- **Manicheism**: **Mani** (216–277 CE) — blend of Christian and Zoroastrian ideas; Mani martyred 277 CE
- **Mazdak** (early 6th c. Iran): religious communism; inspired later dissident **Shi'ite** movements in Islam

### Alexander, Greeks, and Romans
- **Alexander the Great** extended Greek cultural influence from Iran to India to Egypt; after his death, conquests divided into three kingdoms based on **Iran**, **Syria**, and **Egypt**
- Only two peoples resisted Hellenistic/Roman dominance: the **Persians** and the **Jews** — "with very different results"
- Greeks and Jews uniquely defined the Other by **culture/religion rather than blood** — barriers crossable (become Greek by adopting Greek culture; become Jewish by adopting Jewish religion)
- Romans extended this further — invented **citizenship** as a portable, expandable political identity; eventually extended to all provinces

> This accessibility of Hellenistic culture, Jewish religion, and Roman polity all helped to prepare the way for the rise and spread of Christianity.

## Chapter 2: Before Islam

### Three Major Developments, 1st–7th Century CE
1. **Rise of Christianity**: protest against Roman order; **Constantine** (311–337 CE) — Christianity captured the Roman Empire and "was in a sense captured by it"; **Justinian** (527–569 CE) used full Roman power to impose a single state-approved doctrine
2. **Shift of Roman center of gravity east**: Constantine founded **Constantinople**; **395 CE** after Theodosius, Empire split — western Rome fell; eastern **Byzantine** Empire survived 1,000 more years; "Byzantine" is a modern scholarly coinage — Byzantines called themselves **Romans**
3. **Hellenization of the Middle East**: New Testament written in Greek; Old Testament translated into Greek (**Septuagint**) in Alexandria; non-Greek languages profoundly influenced by Hellenistic philosophy and science

### Byzantine vs. Persian Rivalry — The Defining Political Fact
- Endless Perso-Byzantine wars; broke only once — the **Long Peace** (383–502 CE)
- **Shapur I** (240–271 CE) captured Roman emperor **Valerian** in battle; had it carved in stone on Iranian mountains — still visible — showing the Persian shah with his foot on the emperor's neck
- **Territorial roots**: Rome claimed Armenia and Mesopotamia (Trajan had conquered them); Persia claimed Syria, Palestine, even Egypt (Cambyses conquered it in 525 BCE)
- **Economic roots**: control of **silk trade from China** and **spices from India**; Romans paid mainly in **gold coin** (little the Mediterranean world could offer in exchange); Persians profited as middlemen
- Byzantine strategy: develop alternative routes **bypassing** Persian territory — northern steppe (Chinese → Turkish khans → Black Sea → Byzantium) or southern sea routes (Indian Ocean → Red Sea → Egypt)

### Border Principalities
- Classical imperial policy: **woo the tribal chiefs** with money, titles, military aid — preferred indirect rule
- **Nabateans**: capital at **Petra** — a flourishing caravan city; served as Roman buffer state
- **Augustus** sent an expedition to conquer Yemen (25 BCE) — "a dismal failure"; Romans never again tried military penetration into Arabia proper
- **Palmyra** (Tadmur): operated the trans-desert route from Mediterranean to Mesopotamia; **Zenobia** (Arab name Zaynab) made a final bid for independence in the 3rd c. — defeated by Emperor **Aurelian**

> 'Whom we never found desirable either as friends or as enemies' — Ammianus Marcellinus on the desert Saracens (XIV, 4.1)

### Pre-Islamic Arabia — The Stage Is Set
- **Trajan** (114–117 CE): occupied Armenia, crossed Tigris, captured **Ctesiphon**, reached Persian Gulf; a major Jewish revolt in Judaea coincided — likely not coincidental
- Arabia c. 100 CE: completely free of external authority; surrounded by small principalities living off the **caravan trade routes** through Arabia to Yemen
- **Zenobia's** defeat and Roman annexation of **Palmyra** collapsed the balance of power; trade routes declined; South Arabia sank into a "dark age" — camel nomadism spread; Muslims call this period **Jāhiliyya** (Age of Ignorance)

### 6th Century Reversal
- Renewed **Perso-Byzantine conflict** transformed Arabia: both empires wooed Arabian peoples, subsidized them, honored their chiefs
- Byzantine envoys negotiated with **Turkish khans** of Central Asia for the northern silk route

> "Are you not those Romans, who have ten tongues and one deceit? … It is strange and unnatural for a Turk to lie." — A Turkish khan to Byzantine envoys, 576 CE (recorded by Menander)

- Two **client principalities** re-emerged:
  - **Ghassān** (Byzantine side) — Arab, Christian, in modern Jordan; chief made a **Patrician of the Roman Empire**
  - **Hīra** (Persian side) — Arab, Christian; a **war by proxy**

### The Jewish-Yemen Episode
- King of the **Himyarites** (Yemen) converted to Judaism c. 525 CE — first Jewish monarchy in centuries; simultaneously Byzantine's rival Jews on Tiran island subjugated; Lewis finds the coincidence suspicious — Jewish element at both ends of the Red Sea, both reportedly **pro-Persian and anti-Byzantine**
- Byzantines brought **Ethiopia** (Christian) against Jewish Yemen; Ethiopians crushed southern Arabia but couldn't hold it; **Persians** moved in — Yemen became a Persian satrapy during Muhammad's lifetime, a major Byzantine defeat
- **552 CE**: two **Nestorian monks** smuggled silkworm eggs from China to Byzantium — ending the Chinese silk monopoly and reducing the strategic importance of the entire trade dispute
- End of 6th century: both Ethiopians and Persians weakened; Arabians absorbed military tactics, new commodities, **writing** (they learned to create a script), and religious dissatisfaction — grew **dissatisfied with primitive paganism**
- Christianity made inroads (borderland Arabs largely Christian by 7th century); **Jews** prominent in Yemen and Hijaz, thoroughly Arabized; Persian religion won **almost no converts** — too ethnically specific
- The **Hanīf**: pre-Islamic seekers who abandoned paganism but rejected all existing faiths — became among the earliest converts to Islam

## Part III: The Dawn and Noon of Islam

## Chapter 3: Origins

### Source Problem
- Islamic origins known almost entirely from **Muslim scriptures, traditions, and historical memory** — no contemporary external testimony for decades
- Lewis: "much of the traditional narrative of early Islamic history must remain problematic, while the critical history is at best tentative"

### Muhammad's First Revelation
- **Muhammad ibn 'Abdallah**, born c. **571 CE**, tribe of **Quraysh**, oasis town of **Mecca**
- On a night in **Ramadan**, Angel **Gabriel** came to Muhammad on **Mount Hira**:

> "Recite in the name of thy Lord who created all things, who created man from clots of blood. Recite, for thy Lord is the most generous, who taught by the pen, who taught man what he did not know." — First four verses of **Qur'an**, chapter 96

- **622 CE**: the **Hijra** (migration) — Muhammad's move to **Yathrib** (renamed **Al-Madīna**, "the City"); the community became the ***Umma***; Muslim calendar reckoned from this year
- In Mecca: private individual against hostile rulers. In Medina: **ruler wielding political, military, and religious authority simultaneously** — key contrast with Moses and Jesus

> "The Umma, which began as a community, had become a state. It would soon become an empire." — Lewis

### Death of the Prophet and the Caliphate
- Muhammad died **8 June 632 CE** — the "Seal of the Prophets"
- Inner circle chose **Abū Bakr** (earliest, most respected convert)
- Title: ***Khalīfa*** — "successor" and "deputy"; *Khalīfat Allāh* = Deputy of God (far-reaching theological implications)

### Speed and Scope of the Arab Conquests
- Within **little more than a century**: empire stretching east to borders of India and China, west along North Africa to the Atlantic, north into Europe
- Lewis: "surely one of the swiftest and most dramatic changes in the whole of human history"
- **Berbers**: fierce initial resistance, then joined Arab conquerors; **Persians**: state destroyed, found structure in Islam; **Fertile Crescent Christians** and **Coptic Egyptians**: found Arab masters **less demanding, more tolerant** than Persian or Byzantine rulers; **Jews**: position improved under Arab Muslim state

### Why Conquest Succeeded
- Key advantage: **lower Arab taxes** (especially for Muslims); **legal tolerance** for all monotheist religions without enforcing Byzantine orthodoxy disputes
- Arab army's secret: **mastery of desert** — as sea power was to later maritime empires

> "The Arabs were at home in the desert; their enemies were not. To them, the desert was friendly, familiar, and accessible; to their enemies, it was a remote and terrible wilderness." — Lewis

- **Amsar** (garrison towns): purpose-built at edge of desert and farmland — **Kūfa** and **Basra** (Iraq), **Fustat** (Egypt), **Qayrawan** (Tunisia) — the engines of **Arabization** of the provinces
- Qur'an explicit: *"There is no compulsion in religion"* (2:256) — war aim was NOT to impose Islam, but to establish Arab political dominion

> "It is the Arabization and Islamization of the peoples of the conquered provinces, rather than the actual military conquest itself, that is the true wonder of the Arab empire." — Lewis

### Economic Windfall
- Arab conquests **unfroze vast accumulated wealth** held in private, public, and Church possession
- 10th-century historian **al-Mas'ūdī** records staggering fortunes: Caliph **'Uthmān** at death: 100,000 dinars cash; **Al-Zubayr ibn al-'Awwām**: 50,000 dinars cash + 1,000 horses + 1,000 slaves; **Ṭalḥa**: Iraqi estates yielding 1,000 dinars/day
- Persian governing classes contributed far more to Islamic development than ex-Byzantine population — Byzantine magnates could withdraw to Constantinople; Persians had **nowhere to go**
- Arab conquest **ended the Rome-Persia trade route conflict** for the first time since Alexander the Great — unified the entire Middle East in a single commercial system

### Internal Arab Conflicts — The Rashidun and the First Civil War
- Most dangerous early threats: **Arab vs. Arab** rivalries — north vs. south Arabia, early vs. late converts, sons of free Arabs vs. sons of Arab fathers and foreign concubines
- First four caliphs: ***Rashidan*** ("rightly guided ones") — chosen by peers; none succeeded by hereditary right; 3 of 4 were killed by assassins
- **'Umar** (r. 634–644): killed by a disgruntled Christian slave; adopted title ***Amir al-Mu'minin*** ("Commander of the Faithful")
- 'Umar's deathbed *shura* (committee of six) chose **'Uthman**, from the Meccan clan of **Umayya** — only representative of the Meccan aristocracy among early converts; lacked his predecessors' personal prestige
- **656 CE**: Egyptian Arab mutineers killed 'Uthman; installed **'Ali ibn Abi Talib** (Prophet's cousin and son-in-law); his supporters = *Shi'at 'Ali* — the **Shi'a**
- **661 CE**: 'Ali murdered by a **Kharijite** assassin after five years of near-continuous struggle

### Mu'awiya and the Umayyad Caliphate (661–750 CE)
- **Mu'awiya ibn Abi Sufyan**, governor of Syria and cousin of 'Uthman, triumphed; commanded a seasoned army hardened on the Byzantine frontier
- Succession became **dynastic in fact, never in principle**; announced by sword, not consensus:

> "The Commander of the Faithful is that one!" [pointing to Mu'awiya] "And if he dies, then that one!" [pointing to Yazid] "And if anyone objects, then this one!" [pointing to his sword]. Mu'awiya said to him: "You are the prince of orators." — 9th-century Arabic source

- Umayyads replaced weakened religious bond with **ethnic Arab aristocracy** — only those of pure Arabian descent on both sides admitted to highest power
- Modern scholarship more balanced than Arab Islamic tradition: Umayyads credit for maintaining stability during dangerous internal struggles

### Kharijites vs. Shi'a: Two Poles of Opposition
- **Kharijites**: extreme tribal egalitarianism — any believer could be caliph; authority always revocable
- **Shi'a**: exact opposite — caliphate belongs by divine right to the Prophet's family alone

### Battle of Karbala (680 CE)
- **Husayn**, son of 'Ali and grandson of the Prophet, led an insurrection in Iraq; on the **10th of Muharram** at **Karbala'**, Husayn, his family, and ~70 followers defeated and killed by Umayyad force; sole survivor: a sick child, 'Ali son of Husayn
- **Karbala' became the central trauma of Shi'ite consciousness** — annual commemoration of martyrdom, guilt, and expiation

> "The doctrinal differences between Sunni and Shi'a Muslims are of minor importance, far less than those that divide the rival churches of Christendom. But the Shi'ite sense of martyrdom and persecution… raised a psychological barrier between them and the Sunni state and majority." — Lewis

### 'Abd al-Malik and Umayyad Consolidation (685–705 CE)
- Arabic replaced Greek and Persian as the official language of administration
- **694 CE**: new **Islamic gold coinage** (*dinars*) with Qur'anic inscriptions challenging Christian doctrine: *"He is God, one, eternal; he does not beget nor is he begotten."* — Byzantine emperor went to war in protest; coins correctly read as a civilizational declaration
- **Dome of the Rock** (691–692 CE) built on the **Temple Mount** in Jerusalem — first great Islamic religious structure; inscriptions repeatedly assert divine unity and reject the Trinity; Lewis reads it as declaring Islam the **final dispensation**, not merely the latest in a succession
- **Qusayr 'Amra** (Jordanian desert lodge, early 8th c.) wall painting: caliph enthroned while six rulers pay homage — **Caesar** (Byzantine), **Roderic** (last Visigoth king of Spain), **Chosroes** (Persian), **Negus of Ethiopia**, and two others — not humiliated captives, but subordinate: universal Islamic supremacy

### Fall of the Umayyads
- After **Hisham** (724–743), four short reigns brought rapid collapse; tribal feuding, Kharijite and Shi'ite sectarianism, and powerful opposition from **Khurasan** (eastern Iranian province)

## Chapter 4: The 'Abbasid Caliphate

### The Revolution of 747–750 CE
- **9 June 747**: **Abu Muslim**, a manumitted Persian slave leading a militant sect, unfurled black flags of revolt in **Khurasan**
- Movement championed the **'Abbasids**, descendants of the Prophet's uncle **al-'Abbas**
- **749**: 'Abbasid armies crossed the Euphrates; **Abu'l-'Abbas** hailed as caliph with title ***al-Saffah***; 750: Umayyad power destroyed in Iraq and Syria

### Was It Persian vs. Arab?
- Common interpretation: 'Abbasid revolution = Persian victory over Arabs
- **Lewis's counter-argument**: Shi'ism was of Arab origin (strongest in southern Iraq); the movement served an Arab pretender; Arabic remained the sole language of government
- What Arabs lost: not power itself, but the **exclusive** right to its fruits — now shared with half-Arabs, Persians, and others. Under Umayyads: only full Arabs at top; under 'Abbasids: **favor of the ruler**, not noble descent, was the passport to power

### Baghdad
- Capital moved from Syria to Iraq — old center of Mesopotamian cosmopolitan civilization
- **Al-Mansur** (754–775) built a permanent capital on the Tigris near the ruins of **Ctesiphon** — bricks from Chosroes' palace reportedly used in caliphal residences
- Official name: ***Madinat al-Salam*** ("City of Peace") — known by the small town's name: **Baghdad**

### The Barmecides and Iranian Influence
- **Khalid al-Barmaki** became chief minister; the **Barmecides** (Central Asian Iranians descended from Buddhist priesthood of Balkh) served as ***wazirs*** for the first half-century — until fall under **Harun al-Rashid** in 803
- Iranian cultural influence surged: Sasanid administrative models, court ceremonial, political texts translated into Arabic; a **standing army on the Persian model** replaced Arab tribal levies

### 'Abbasid Ideology and Failure of Theocracy
- 'Abbasid caliph became an **autocrat in the old Middle Eastern style**: claimed divine origin for authority, rested it on armed forces, exercised it through a vast bureaucracy
- **Al-Ma'mun** (813–833): tried to impose **Mu'tazila** rationalist theology as state doctrine; persecuted dissenters — **failed**; **al-Mutawakkil** (847–861) needed Sunni popular support against Turkish soldiers and was forced to suppress the Mu'tazila
- **Key lesson**: the **Sunni ulema** were already strong enough to resist state imposition of doctrine — attempt at an Erastian Islam failed permanently; no subsequent dynasty tried to dictate doctrine to the religious institution

### Structural Decline
- **West**: Spain and North Africa became virtually independent under their own amirs
- **868**: Egypt fell to Turkish praetorian **Ahmad ibn Tulun** — Egypt thereafter almost never ruled from Baghdad
- **Fiscal crisis**: spendthrift court + loss of provincial revenues → caliphs farmed out revenues to local governors as **tax-farmers**, who became de facto autonomous rulers
- The tragic structural paradox: **only a stronger state could preserve community cohesion** — yet a stronger state necessarily compromised Islamic social ideals; every rebellion resulted in **more centralized autocracy** — further from the Islamic ideal, closer to the ancient Middle Eastern model

### Final Degradation and the Buyid Takeover
- From **al-Mu'tasim** (833–842) onward, caliphs became puppets of their own generals
- Grant of title *amir al-umara'* (Commander of Commanders) to governor of Iraq = formal recognition that a governing authority existed beside the caliph
- **17 January 946**: the **Shi'ite Persian house of Buyeh** occupied Baghdad; caliph now controlled by a Shi'ite who kept him as a useful figurehead; caliphate purely titular until Mongol conquest (1258)

### Iranian Intermezzo (9th–11th centuries)
- Between Arab decline and Turkish rise: **Iranian national revival** in unmistakably Islamic form
- Key dynasties: **Tahirids → Saffarids → Samanids → Buyids**
- Most culturally active: **Samanids** (capital: Bukhara) — official language Persian; birth of **new Persian literature** in Arabic script, profoundly Islamic but distinctively Persian — the 10th–11th century renaissance

### Shi'ite Sectarian Split: Twelvers vs. Ismailis
- After sixth imam **Ja'far al-Sadiq**'s death (765), split into two groups:
  - **Twelvers** (*Ithna Ashariyya*): followed Musa through the twelfth imam (who disappeared — messianic return awaited); relatively moderate, minor differences from Sunni
  - **Ismailis**: followed Isma'il; inherited extremist doctrines and insurrectionary methods

### The Fatimid Caliphate
- **908**: Ismaili pretender **'Ubaydallah** enthroned in North Africa as first **Fatimid caliph**
- **969**: fourth Fatimid caliph **al-Mu'izz** conquered Egypt, built **Cairo** as his capital
- First dynasty to openly reject Abbasid authority and found a rival caliphate
- Fatimid economic strategy: divert eastern trade from **Persian Gulf to Red Sea**, strengthening Egypt and weakening Iraq
- At peak under **al-Mustansir** (1036–1094): controlled all North Africa, Sicily, Egypt, Syria, western Arabia
- **1056–7**: pro-Fatimid general briefly seized Baghdad and proclaimed Fatimid sovereignty from Abbasid pulpits — driven out the following year
- Religious branch functioned like a **party apparatus in a one-party state** — Ismaili propaganda reached from Iraq to the borders of India
- Wazir **Ya'qub ibn Killis** launched commercial expansion; Fatimid fleets controlled the eastern Mediterranean; Indian trade flowed through port of **'Aydhab** on the Sudanese coast
- Fatimids never achieved final victory over Abbasids; power dwindled after al-Mustansir's death (1094)

## Chapter 5: The Coming of the Steppe Peoples

### Signs of Islamic Weakness by the 11th Century
- Fragmentation into autonomous regional sovereignties; caliphs powerless even in their own capital
- Most of the empire from Iran to Egypt under Shi'ite rulers
- Economic deterioration: China trade dwindled; shortage of precious metals → quasi-feudal economy
- Cultural: 8th–10th century had seen "**The Renaissance of Islam**" (translation of Greek philosophy/science into Arabic); by 11th century this culture was insecure — limited to urban leisure classes

### Multi-Front Attacks
- **Europe**: Christian reconquest of Sicily and Spain; Crusaders arrive in the Near East
- **Africa**: **Banu Hilal** and **Banu Sulaym** tribes burst from Upper Egypt, swept through Libya and Tunisia — devastation from which Arab North Africa never fully recovered
- **East**: the steppe peoples — Turks, then Mongols — the most permanent in their effects

### The Turks: From Slaves to Rulers
- Muslims first imported Turks as military slaves — **Mamluks** (Arabic: "owned") — trained from childhood for war
- First to use them extensively: caliph **al-Mu'tasim** (833–842)
- Turkish commanders gradually ousted Arabs and Persians from military and thus political hegemony — Turkish domination lasted **1,000 years**

### The Karakhanid Conversion — A Turning Point
- **960**: **Karakhanids** (a Turkish dynasty beyond the Islamic frontier) converted to Islam — not just individuals, but an entire free Turkish people (reportedly 200,000 tents)
- Turks surrendered to Islam more completely than Arabs or Persians — **no Turkish equivalent** to Arab memories of pagan heroism or Persian pride in pre-Islamic glories; pre-Islamic Turkish civilizations, religions, and literatures were "blotted out and forgotten"; even the name "Turk" became synonymous with "Muslim"

### The Seljuk Conquest
- Grandsons of Seljuk — **Tughrul and Chagri** — crushed the Ghaznavids; by 1037: prayers recited in Seljuk names in mosques of **Marv and Nishapur**
- **1055**: Tughrul entered Baghdad, expelled the last Buyid amir
- By 1079: Seljuks seized Syria and Palestine from Fatimids; conquered most of Anatolia from Byzantium — became a **Muslim Turkish land permanently**
- Title **"sultan"** adopted officially by Tughrul — first rulers to inscribe it on coins; has remained the title for supreme power ever since
- Seljuks reunited most of the Middle East under a single authority for the first time since early Abbasids; retained caliphs as nominal rulers, strengthened their position by eliminating sectarian regimes

### The Crusaders (from 1096)
- Arrived during Seljuk internal weakness; established Latin principalities at **Antioch, Edessa, Tripoli, Jerusalem**
- Kurdish officer **Salah al-Din** (Saladin) — sent to Egypt as wazir to the dying Fatimids and representative of **Nur al-Din**; 1172: abolished the Fatimid caliphate, restored Abbasid nominal supremacy in Egypt
- After Nur al-Din's death (1174), Saladin seized Muslim Syria → *jihad* 1187; by death (1193): recaptured Jerusalem, expelled Crusaders from all but a narrow coastal strip
- Crusading states survived another century only because Saladin's empire fragmented; finally extinguished by the Mamluks

### Seljuk Administration: Feudalization of Islam
- Seljuks relied on Persian bureaucracy; key figure: great minister **Nizam al-Mulk**
- Nizam al-Mulk systematized tax-farming abuses into a formal land-based system — essentially **feudalism**: land granted to officers in exchange for providing armed men; grants carried rights to actual tax revenues
- Social fallout: Iranian aristocracy displaced by new Turkish military ruling class

### The Assassins
- Chief opposition to the Seljuks: Ismaili Shi'a in a radical new form
- **Hasan-i Sabbah** reformed Ismaili movement = "**New Preaching**" — called **Assassins** in Europe (from Arabic *hashish*)
- **1090**: Hasan-i Sabbah seized the mountain fortress of **Alamut** in northern Persia
- Grand Masters commanded fanatical followers in a campaign of political murder against rulers — including **Nizam al-Mulk himself** (1092)
- Assassin terror not finally ended until the **Mongol invasions** of the 13th century
- Lewis's assessment: the Assassins were the **last serious attempt** by the Shi'a to overthrow the Sunni establishment

### The Sunni Revival
- **Sunni revival** began in early 11th century in Khurasan under Ghaznavid Turks (only major area not under Shi'ite rule)
- Three goals: (1) overthrow Shi'ite regimes and restore caliphate — **almost completely achieved**; (2) reformulate Sunni doctrine; (3) integrate the religious institution into political life — the most difficult
- Key instrument: **madrasa** (orthodox theological college), modeled on Ismaili mission schools in Cairo; **Nizam al-Mulk** established a madrasa in Baghdad after the Seljuk conquest; spread empire-wide; extended to Egypt by **Saladin**
- **Sufism** absorbed much of Shi'ism's emotional content and stayed within the Sunni fold
- "The ancient dichotomy of religious and political authority... was retained and indeed institutionalized, in the parallel sovereignties of caliph and sultan"

### The Mongol Invasion

### Jenghiz Khan's Rise
- Mongol prince **Temujin** united warring nomadic tribes; took title **Jenghiz Khan**; spring **1206**: unfurled the white banner with nine horsetails at the sources of the Onon river
- Trigger for Islamic invasion: caravan of ~450 Muslims pillaged at **Utrar** on the Jaxartes; merchants put to the sword
- **1219**: Jenghiz led armies across Jaxartes; by 1220: Bukhara, Samarqand, all Transoxiania taken; eastern Iran (Marv, Nishapur) followed
- Mongol prince **Hülägü** crossed Oxus with orders to conquer all of Islam as far as Egypt; crushed even the Assassins in their mountain castles
- **20 February 1258**: last caliph **al-Musta'sim** and as many family members as could be found were put to death — the **House of Abbas**, titular head of Sunni Islam for almost five centuries, ceased to reign

### Reassessing the Mongol Impact
- Lewis's corrective: the immediate moral effects were "probably not as great as has sometimes been suggested" — the caliphate had already ceased to be an effective institution; "the Mongols did little more than lay the ghost of something that was already dead"
- **Egypt**: never conquered by Mongols (remained center of Arabic culture)
- **Syria**: only raided; after **Battle of 'Ayn Jalut** (1260), Mamluk army of Egypt decisively defeated the Mongols → Syria incorporated into the Egyptian sultanate
- **Iran**: hard hit but recovery was rapid; after conversion to Islam (1295), Mongol khans fostered reconstruction and cultural flowering — poets **Sa'di** (1184–1291) and **Hafiz** (c. 1320–1389), astronomer **Qutb al-Din**, architect **Qawam al-Din** (builder of the Gawhar Shad Mosque in Mashhad)
- Positive Mongol contribution: for the first time united under one dynasty the civilizations of the Middle East and the Far East — opened overland routes to China
- **Rashid al-Din** (1247–1318) — a Jewish convert to Islam, physician, wazir — compiled a universal history using a team including two Chinese scholars, a Buddhist hermit from Kashmir, a Mongol tribal specialist, and a Frankish monk — "anticipated Europe by half a millennium" in universal history

### Permanent Damage: The Fall of Iraq
- **Baghdad and Iraq never recovered their central position** — irrigation works collapsed; civil government broke down
- Bedouin moved into the breaches left by Mongols — and unlike Mongols, stayed
- Trade routes shifted north and east (Anatolia, Iran) and south (Egypt, Red Sea) — leaving Iraq to "centuries of stagnation and neglect"

### Post-Mongol Cultural Division
- **Northern zone (Persian civilization)**: Iranian plateau → Anatolia → Central Asia → Mughal India; Persian the literary/artistic language
- **Southern zone (Arabic civilization)**: Egypt as the new center, extending into the Fertile Crescent and Arabia

## Chapter 5 (cont.) / Chapter 6: The Mongol Aftermath

### Sufism as the New Unifying Force
- **Al-Ghazali's** compromise between mysticism and orthodoxy was the pivot — merged Sufi practice with Sunni theology
- Turkish tribes first converted by wandering Sufi monks, not orthodox theologians; the shock of Mongol conquest drove theologians and common people into each other's arms
- Both **Ottoman** and **Safavid** dynasties had Sufi origins

### Il-Khan Iran and the Battle of 'Ayn Jalut (1260)
- **Hülägü** advanced through Armenia, took Aleppo and Damascus; **'Ayn Jalut** ("Spring of Goliath"), September 1260: Mamluk army under **Baybars** (Kipchak Turk) utterly defeated the Mongols — Egypt immediately reoccupied all Syria; Mongols never broke through again
- Diplomatic irony: Mongols and Christian Europe negotiated a joint front against Islam — produced nothing; Baybars countered by allying with Muslim Mongol **Berke Khan** (Golden Horde) against the Il-Khans

### Tamerlane — Last Convulsion of the Steppe
- **Timur Lang** (Tamerlane): born Turkicized Mongol, married into house of Jenghiz Khan; conquered Central Asia, Iran, defeated Golden Horde, raided India, overran Syria, humbled Mamluks
- **1402**: crushed **Bayezid** (Ottoman sultan) at **Ankara** — captured him; Bayezid later committed suicide; Timur died 1405 preparing invasion of China
- Lewis's verdict: "a great conqueror, but no empire-builder" — empire dissolved immediately after his death; Timur's death = end of the great steppe migration that began in the 10th century

### Post-Timur Fragmentation
- Western Iran/Mesopotamia: **Black Sheep** and **White Sheep** Turkoman confederacies
- Eastern Iran/Transoxania: **Timurid** successors — brilliant cultural flowering at Bukhara, Samarqand, Herat — the classical age of Turkish literary culture

### The Mamluk Sultanate of Egypt

#### The Mamluk Takeover (1250)
- **Ayyubid** dynasty (founded by Saladin) lost real power to Turkish Mamluks by mid-13th century
- **Shajar al-Durr** ("Tree of Pearls"), the dead sultan's concubine, kept his death secret and issued orders in his name until a new sultan arrived — held the state together; **Turan Shah** arrived, defeated **King Louis IX** of France, captured him; Louis ransomed himself; Mamluks then murdered Turan Shah and proclaimed Shajar al-Durr as sultan
- Caliph in Baghdad protested enthroning a woman:

> 'If there is not a man left among you whom you can appoint, tell us and we shall send you one.' — Abbasid Caliph to the Mamluks of Egypt

- **Baybars** made himself sultan in 1260 after victory at 'Ayn Jalut; installed an Abbasid refugee as shadow caliph in Cairo — purely ceremonial, "court pensioners" with no power; shadow caliphate ended 1517 when Ottomans conquered Egypt

#### Mamluk System
- **Quasi-feudal**: officers received land grants for life but lived in Cairo, not on estates — interested in revenue, not possession; no chateaux, no strong local authority — fundamentally different from Western feudalism
- Ruling class often could not speak Arabic; Mamluks = bought slaves, mainly Kipchak Turks, then Circassians, Greeks, Kurds, some Europeans

#### Mamluk Decline
- Trade with Europe was vital — Mamluk fiscal strength depended on transit customs; Timur's 1400–1401 sack of Damascus + plague, locusts, Bedouin raids = a blow the sultanate never recovered from
- **Portuguese** opened direct sea lanes to India → outflanked Egyptian commerce; **Qansawh al-Ghawri** (1500–1516) sent Egyptian fleet to India — Portuguese defeated it, then systematically destroyed Muslim merchant shipping in Indian Ocean and Red Sea
- Mamluks socially/psychologically resistant to firearms — cavalry warriors viewed guns as "unchivalrous"; mounted lancers "hopelessly outclassed by the musket-armed infantry and artillery of the Ottomans"

### Anatolia and the Rise of the Ottomans

#### Ottoman Origins
- Among post-Mongol principalities, one grew into empire: the **Ottomans**, named for founder **Osman** (first quarter of the 14th century)
- Strategic advantage: position on border of Byzantine Bithynia, near Constantinople — maximum opportunity
- 1326: took **Brusa** (Bursa) — first Ottoman capital; 1354: crossed Dardanelles into Europe; battles of **Maritza** (1371) and **Kosovo** (1389) — Balkans brought under Ottoman rule
- **Genoese** immediately approached Ottomans for military alliance in 1352 — first Ottoman-Genoese commercial treaty

#### Bayezid I and Timur's Blow
- Crushed Western European chivalry at **Nicopolis** (1396); defeated and captured by Timur at **Ankara** (1402); committed suicide in captivity
- **Mehmed I** reunited empire by 1413; **Murad II** (1421–1451) resumed European expansion; emergence of Turkish national consciousness in literature

#### The Devshirme System
- **Devshirme**: levy of boys from Christian populations for Ottoman army and state service — inaugurated late 14th c., regularized from ~1430

> "There should be choice made, of valiant and industrious youths, out of the children of unbelievers, fit for the service, whom they should likewise innoblize, by the faith of Islam … they were commonly known by the name of Janissarie [that is, the new army]." — Ottoman historian Sadeddin (16th c.)

- Effect: **Christian energies harnessed to Ottoman state**; solved the tension between border-warrior army culture and orthodox Islamic state structure; **Janissaries** became the empire's elite infantry force

#### Ottoman Religious Institutionalization
- Lewis's strong claim: "The Ottomans made what was perhaps the only really serious attempt, in a Muslim state of high material civilization, to establish the Holy Law of Islam as the effective law of the land"

#### Fall of Constantinople (1453)
- **29 May 1453**: janissaries stormed Constantinople; last emperor **Constantine** died fighting; crescent raised above **Hagia Sophia**; Mehmed II henceforth known as ***Fatih*** (the Conqueror)

## The Gunpowder Empires

### Ottoman Consolidation
- Mehmed's subsequent campaigns: subjugated Greek despotates in Morea, Serbia, Bosnia; took Trebizond
- **Deliberately restrained eastward**: "to seek the destruction of ancient dynasties of the great sultans of the people of Islam is not good practice"
- Mamluk sultanate in final decline — Lewis calls it "a kind of Arab Byzantium"; in Anatolia and Iran, Turks and Persians had taken cultural/political leadership; Egypt/Syria in "long silver age" — preserved classical heritage but no longer innovative

### The Safavid Challenge
- New **Safavid** dynasty of Iran: brought to power by radical **Shi'ite** movement; posed existential religious threat to Ottoman Sunni order — millions of potential Shi'ite sympathizers inside Ottoman Anatolia; Safavid family had Turkish origin and support in Turkish Anatolia
- **1511**: dangerous Shi'ite revolt in central Anatolia; **1512**: Bayezid abdicated in favor of **Selim I** ("Selim the Grim")
- Linguistic irony: in the angry pre-war correspondence, the Ottoman sultan wrote in Persian (language of cultivated gentlemen) while the Safavid shah wrote in Turkish (language of his tribal origins)
- **Battle of Chaldiran (23 August 1514)**: Ottoman janissaries and artillery crushed Iranian forces; Selim occupied Tabriz but withdrew — did not pursue eastward; long Ottoman-Safavid struggle followed with sectarian massacres on both sides

### Ottoman Absorption of Arab World (1516–17)
- **Short war 1516–17**: overthrew Mamluk sultanate; Egypt, Syria, western Arabia under Ottoman rule; sovereignty extended westward across North Africa, southward down Red Sea
- Ottoman sultans now ruled **Mecca** and **Medina** — great prestige, great responsibility

### Süleyman the Magnificent (1520–1566) — Peak of Ottoman Power
- **Battle of Mohacs (1526)**: shattered the army of Hungary

> "With flashing swords like burning flames they charged the doomed but stout-hearted infidels … At the Sultan's command, the janissary musketeers fired volleys … and in an instant sent hundreds, or rather thousands, of them straight to Hell." — Kemalpashazade

- 1529: first siege of **Vienna** — failed; Ottoman fleets challenged Portuguese in Indian Ocean
- **Richard Knolles** (Elizabethan historian): called the Turkish empire "the present Terror of the World"

### Limits of Ottoman Power
- Failed Vienna siege (1529) inaugurated 150 years of inconclusive struggle; **1683**: second Vienna siege = total Ottoman defeat
- **Battle of Lepanto (1571)**: first major Ottoman naval defeat in Mediterranean; Ottomans rebuilt the fleet
- **Grand Vizier Sokollu Mehmed Pasha** to Sultan Selim II after Lepanto: "The might of our Empire is such that if it were desired to equip the entire fleet with silver anchors, silken rigging, and satin sail, we could do it." — real decline already underway but hidden

### Safavid Iran — Firearms
- Safavids survived Chaldiran and adapted; **Shah 'Abbas** (1587–1629) built new infantry and artillery after Ottoman model
- English brothers **Anthony and Robert Shirley** arrived 1598 and served in Persian military for years
- **Isfahan**: Abbas moved capital there in 1597 — *"Isfahan nisf-i jehan"* ("Isfahan is half the world")
- **1616**: English East India Company arrives at Surat; English merchants helped Persians retake **Hormuz** (held by Portuguese since 1514) in 1622

### Russian Expansion
- **1480**: Tsar Ivan the Great threw off "the Tatar yoke"; 1552: seized Volga Tatar capital **Kazan**; 1556: seized Caspian port **Astrakhan**
- Crimean Tatars maintained allegiance to Ottoman sultans; Black Sea remained under Turkish-Tatar control with active trade in foodstuffs and **East European slaves**
- **1637**: Don Cossacks seized Turkish fortress of **Azov** independently — then offered it to the Russian tsar who refused, unwilling to risk full war with Ottomans: "The Russian road to the Black Sea was not yet open, but it was clearly marked."

### Treaty of Sitvatorok (1606) — Turning Point
- First treaty negotiated on the frontier **between equals**, not dictated by victors in Istanbul; used the sultan's own title ***Padishah*** for the Habsburg monarch — previously just "the king of Vienna"

> "The seventeenth century began with a grudging concession of equality; it ended with an unequivocal admission of defeat." — Lewis

### Ottoman Internal Decay
- **Sipahis** (feudal cavalry) were the backbone of the early Ottoman state; their decay had several causes: sultan's preference for professional slave troops; technological change requiring specialized long-service infantry; timar (military fief) revocable and conditional — fiefs increasingly seized and absorbed into the sultan's domain
- New propertied class in the provinces — the ***a'yan*** ("notables") — interposed between government and peasantry; by the 18th century, the government formally delegated provincial administration to them

### Collapse of the Janissary Corps
- Originally recruited exclusively from Christian captives via the ***devshirme***; celibate, dedicated

> "From time immemorial it has been unlawful for janissaries to marry … The state of a janissary is a state of celibacy." — *Kavanim-i-Yenicheriyan*

- **1568**: Selim II allowed janissaries to enroll their sons; by 1592, "sons of slaves" constituted the majority; then thrown open to anyone who could buy their way in

> "… a vile rabble of contemptible interlopers entered the respected household … tradition and respect disappeared entirely…" — Historian Selaniki Mustafa

- End result: the corps became a **hereditary corporation** with jealously guarded privileges; nominally slaves of the sultan, the janissaries were often his masters; "still nominally soldiers, they degenerated into a kind of armed rabble … of little use against a disciplined enemy in the field"

### A Contrarian Assessment of Ottoman Rule in Europe
- In the 15th–16th centuries, **refugees fled West to East**, not East to West — Jews expelled from Spain in 1492 found refuge in Ottoman lands
- When Ottoman rule ended in Europe, subject Christian nations survived "with their languages, their cultures, their religions, even, to some extent, their institutions, intact" — the same cannot be said of Muslims expelled from the Balkans or Moorish Spain
- Even the *devshirme*, much condemned, had a positive aspect: "the humblest villager could rise to the highest and most powerful offices in the state" — social mobility impossible in contemporary aristocratic Christendom
- **Martin Luther** (1541) warned that the poor, oppressed by greedy princes, might prefer to live under the Turks

### Süleyman's Death and the Eastern Question
- **5–6 September 1566**: Süleyman died in his tent during the siege of Szigetvár in Hungary; grand vizier kept the death secret for three weeks, carrying the embalmed body in a litter, until Selim II was safely installed
- Brief revivals: **Murad IV** (1623–1640); grand viziers **Mehmed Köprûlû** and son **Ahmed** (in office 1656–1678) stopped internal rot
- **1683**: Grand vizier **Karamustafa Pasha** launched second attempt on Vienna — defeated decisively and finally
- "Instead of the strength, it was now the weakness of the Ottoman state that posed a problem for Europe. That problem came to be known as 'the Eastern question'."

## Part IV: Cross-Sections

## Chapter 8: The State

### Two Models: Byzantine and Persian

**Byzantine Model:**
- After Constantine, emperor was Christian ruler; distinction between *imperium* (state) and *sacerdotium* (church) established — but not full separation
- **Agapetus** to Emperor **Justinian** (~530 CE): "Having a dignity which is set above all other honours, sire, you render honour above all to God, who gave you that dignity…"
- Byzantine emperor bore ultimate responsibility for both Church and State; duty to enforce **orthodoxy**; titles included ***kosmokratōr*** (ruler of the world) and ***khronokratōr*** (ruler of time)

**Persian (Sasanid) Model:**
- **Shapur I** (260 CE): "I, lord Shapur, worshipper of Mazda, King of Kings of Iran and of non-Iran, of the race of the gods…"
- Feudal structure replaced by military despotism; aristocratic families retained power through the **bureaucracy** — the Persian scribal patrician class would reappear in Islamic times
- Sasanid Persia: aristocratic, closed upper-class society with traditions of **chivalry and courtesy** generally lacking in the Greco-Roman world; a Zoroastrian state Church sanctified royal power
- Key vulnerability: Sasanid capital **Ctesiphon** was in Iraq, on the *near* side of the Iranian plateau — lost in 637; no center around which to rally. Byzantine **Constantinople** was protected behind the Anatolian plateau — survived all Arab assaults

### Islam and the State: A Fundamentally Different Tradition
- The Arab Muslim invaders differed from Germanic invaders of Rome: Germans accepted Roman imperial institutions; Arabs **abolished the old order** and created their own sovereign institution
- Christians were a minority subject to persecution for three centuries before Constantine — they developed the **Church** as a separate institution to survive
- Muhammad was "his own Constantine" — during his lifetime, Islam was simultaneously a political and religious allegiance; the Prophet's community in Medina was a **state**

> "In Muslim teaching and experience, there was no Caesar. God was the head of the state, and Muhammad his Prophet taught and ruled on his behalf." — Lewis

### The Caliphate
- The caliph was NOT "pope and emperor in one" — no distinction between *imperium* and *sacerdotium*; no separate Church with its own head
- Caliph's task: uphold and protect the faith; create conditions for subjects to live as good Muslims; expand the Islamic state until "the whole world was opened to the light of Islam"; Muslim historiography calls the early conquests ***futuh*** ("openings")
- Islamic sovereignty titulature is **NOT territorial or ethnic** — no equivalents to "King of England"; during Ottoman-Safavid wars, sultan and shah each used the other's universal Islamic titles as insults

### Foundational Questions of Succession
- **Shi'a**: caliphate hereditary in the line of the Prophet — all caliphs except Ali were usurpers
- **Sunni**: caliphate elective; any member of the Prophet's tribe **Quraysh** eligible; electorate never authoritatively defined; logical endpoint: reigning caliph nominating his heir — the elective fiction preserved through nomination

### Pre-Islamic Arab Anti-Monarchism
- Early caliph power was NOT despotic — limited by Islamic political ethics AND by pre-Islamic Arabian anti-authoritarian tradition
- **'Abid ibn al-Abras** (pre-Islamic poet) described his tribe as *laqah* — "one that never submitted to a king"
- Arabic *malik* (king) is one of the divine epithets — sacred when applied to God, negative when applied to humans; Qur'an uses *malik* for **Pharaoh** — prototype of unjust tyrannical rule

### Tribal Roots of Islamic Political Concepts
- Pre-Islamic tribal chief (*shaykh*) held power only by freely given consent of "those who bind and loose" — senior tribesmen who could appoint and dismiss; first among equals, arbitrator in disputes
- The **caliphate** was conceived as a "super sheikhdom" — the same authority scaled to all united Muslim tribes
- **Majlis** (place where one sits): tribal council where the chief dispensed justice, made political decisions — survived into early caliphate, became more formalized

> "By God, our booty, which was shared, has become a perquisite of the rich. Our leadership, which was consultative, has become arbitrary. Our succession, which was by the choice of the community, is now by inheritance." — Sudayf, 'Abbasid supporter

### Ottoman Institutional Development
- **Divan-i humayun** (imperial council): formal Ottoman institution; prescribed membership, fixed meeting times, regular agenda
- **Mehmed II**: first sultan to stop presiding in person — withdrew behind a screen; **Süleyman**: ceased attending even from behind the screen
- **Meshveret** (consultation): Ottoman *ad hoc* councils for specific crises; very frequent during late 18th c. crises
- The formal consultative council traces to Mongol tribal council (*kurultay*) via Persian *davan-i buzurg*

### Decline of Caliphal Power
- From the 10th century onward, 'Abbasid caliphs lost control of provinces, capital, then palace; **separation of powers** strategy failed when governor absorbed the other functions
- By the 10th c., almost all provinces were hereditary principalities giving only token recognition — Friday prayer mention, coinage — to the caliph
- **Walter Bagehot's** "dignified vs. efficient" distinction applies: caliph = dignified (authority, legitimacy); sultan = efficient (power, governance)
- **Ottoman conquest of Egypt (1517)**: sultans absorbed the caliphal title into their titulature; it retained little significance until revived in the late 18th c.

### Bureaucracy, Administration, and Finance
- Early caliphate inherited existing bureaucracies — **Persian** in Iraq/Iran, **Christian** in Syria/Egypt — kept running, remitting proceeds to the new Arab government
- 'Abbasid administration divided into departments (*diwans*): Chancery, Finance, Army, Public Works, Internal Security, Domain Lands, Royal Slaves, Courier/Intelligence Service, Pious Foundations
- The ***wazir***: head of the entire governmental apparatus — "one entrusted with a burden or duty"; an 'Abbasid innovation adapted from **Sasanid** practice; **Ottoman grand vizier**: annual income ~2.5 million aspers under Süleyman

### Taxation and Revenue Data
- **Three Islamic taxes**: *kharaj* (land tax), *jizya* (poll tax on non-Muslims), *zakat/ushr* (tithe paid by Muslims); supplemented by *mukas* — extra duties deplored by jurists but collected by all Muslim rulers
- Revenue tabulations from **Harun al-Rashid**: eastern provinces ~400 million dirhams; western provinces ~5 million dinars; in-kind taxes from Sind: elephants, aloes wood; from Qumis: silver ingots and pomegranates; from Armenia: embroidered carpets, salted fish; live deliveries included horses, mules, falcons, slaves
- **Al-Muqtadir** (918–19 CE): net income from all provinces = 14,501,904 dinars
- **Ottoman budget 1669–70**: revenue 612 million aspers; expenditure 637 million aspers — of which 398 million for armed forces

## Chapter 9: The Economy

### Documentation Problem
- Pre-modern Middle Eastern economic/social history severely underexplored — primarily a documentation problem
- **Medieval Middle Eastern states** (except the Ottoman Empire) were overthrown by internal upheavals and external invasions; archives dispersed and lost
- Ottoman archives are "of daunting difficulty and immensity" — much work remains

### Attitudes Toward Government
- Deep contradiction in Muslim attitudes: the state = divinely ordained AND commonly seen as evil, contaminating, dangerous
- Saying attributed to the Prophet: "government and paradise cannot be combined"
- **'Amr ibn al-'As** (Arab conqueror of Egypt): "That would be as if I were to hold the horns of the cow while he milked it."

### Agriculture: The Foundation
- Two types: **river-valley agriculture** (Nile, Tigris-Euphrates, Oxus, Jaxartes) and **rainfall agriculture** (Syrian valleys, Syro-Palestinian coast, Iran, Turkey) — rainfall agriculture inferior
- Notable lack of forests/timber; by Islamic Middle Ages, timber imported from Africa, India, and Southeast Asia

### Crops: Introduction and Diffusion
- **Cereals**: barley, millet, and primitive wheat earliest; wheat dominant by medieval times; **rice** introduced from India — Arab conquerors at Basra (~7th c.) encountered rice and thought it was poison; learned to eat it only after a horse survived eating it overnight
- **Sugar cane** — introduced from further east under Arab-Muslim rule; Persian names *sheker* and *qand* survive in modern English ("sugar," "candy"); cultivation spread Egypt → North Africa → Muslim Spain → Atlantic islands → New World (the plantation system traveled with it)
- **Spices** — grown locally and imported from South/Southeast Asia; major export to the West until European maritime powers opened direct sea routes to Asia
- Industrial fiber crops: **flax** (ancient, especially Egypt), **cotton** (from East Asia, moved westward), **mulberry** (fed silkworms, cultivated from 6th century)

### Fruits, Vegetables, and Curiosities
- Most fruits of eastern (Persian) origin: **peach**, **apricot**
- Vegetables like **spinach**, **aubergine** (eggplant), **artichoke** still known in the West by their Persian or Arabic names
- **Orange** in most Middle Eastern languages is *bortaqal* (Arabic) / *portakal* (Turkish) — meaning "Portugal"; sweet orange introduced to the Middle East by **Portuguese merchants** in early 16th century; bitter orange (*narang*) was known earlier — mentioned by 9th-century poet **Ibn al-Mu'tazz**
- **Ibrahim Pechevi** (Turkish historian, ~1635) on tobacco's arrival:

> "The fetid and nauseating smoke of tobacco was brought in the year 1009 [1600–01 CE] by English infidels, who sold it as a remedy for certain diseases of humidity … Many even of the great ulema and the mighty fell into this addiction."

### Coffee Origins
- Originated in **Ethiopia**, possibly named from the province of **Kaffa**; introduced to **Yemen** in the 14th or 15th century; a Sufi traveller brought it to Aden — Sufis used it to stay awake during devotional exercises
- By 1511 attested in Mecca; spread via pilgrims and merchants across the Islamic world
- First coffee-house in Europe opened in Vienna after the second Turkish siege — granted to an Armenian spy as reward for services to Austrian intelligence
- **Why coffee-houses thrived**: Islam bans alcohol; drinking was clandestine; there was no tavern equivalent in Muslim cities; the coffee-house filled this social void

### Agricultural Technology: Why It Stayed Rudimentary
- Simple wooden, **wheelless plough** of antiquity remained in use; rich river-valley soils gave bountiful harvests (sometimes 2–3 crops/year) — no incentive to innovate
- Key structural explanation: absence of two European phenomena — the *monastery* (where educated men applied disciplined minds to farming) and the *educated farmer* (e.g., the English country gentleman). **Educated Middle Easterners did not farm; farmers were not educated**

### The Islamic Agricultural Revolution
- Islamic conquests created a single political-economic unit from Europe to China's borders; westward crop movement under Islam: **rice, sorghum, sugar cane, cotton, watermelons, aubergines, artichokes, oranges, bananas**, plus fodder, fiber, spice, medicinal, and cosmetic plants
- Medieval Muslim travellers recorded astonishing variety — a 1400 CE account of North Africa lists 65 kinds of grapes, 36 kinds of pears, 28 kinds of figs, 16 kinds of apricots

### Irrigation and Soil Degradation
- Middle Easterners excelled at **irrigation** — elaborate systems of dykes, reservoirs, and canals; required not just farmers but **technocrats and bureaucrats**; some historians see centralized irrigation works as the nucleus of the **bureaucratic state and command economy**
- When civil order broke down, nomads entered cultivated lands; goats (unlike sheep) tore out grass by roots, stripped bark from trees — severe topsoil loss
- **Ibn Khaldun** (14th c.): 'ruin and devastation prevail' in North Africa, where once a flourishing civilization stood
- Overall agricultural yield declined from late Roman times onward; Islam born in a caravan city — the **Shari'a** paid little attention to peasantry beyond tax collection; military officers controlled agricultural land with no interest in long-term productivity

### Pastoralism and Nomad-Peasant Divide
- **The pig taboo**: Islam (shared with Judaism) excluded pigs — some historians argue the pig set the *geographical limits* of Islamic expansion; Muslim faith didn't take root among pig-rearing peoples in Spain, the Balkans, and western China
- **Fundamental division**: in Western Europe, agriculture and stock raising were integrated; in the Middle East, an *immemorial separation and conflict* existed between peasant and nomad — symbolized by **Cain and Abel** (cultivator vs. nomad herder; God favored the nomad's offering, but historically it was usually peasants who suffered nomadic depredations)

### Industry and Commerce
- **Textiles**: dominant medieval industry; Middle Eastern origin evidenced in English vocabulary — *muslin* (Mosul), *damask* (Damascus), *gauze* (*qazz*), *mohair* (*mukhayyar*), *taffeta* (Persian *taftah*)
- **Gold mines near 'Allaqi** (between Egypt and Sudan): quest for gold and slaves was a primary motive for Islamic expansion south of the Sahara
- Nearly all energy from human and animal muscle; no wheeled vehicles — shortage of wood and metals; **Ibn Battuta** (14th c.) thought wheeled carts among Turkish steppe peoples remarkable enough to record; **Volney** (18th c.) noted no wagon or cart in all of Syria — peasants feared government confiscation
- Transport by **pack animals** or water: camel carries 1,200 lbs, 200 miles/day, 17 days without water — far cheaper than roads and carts in arid climate; sea/river transport far cheaper than overland

### The Ban on Usury
- Qur'an explicitly permits buying and selling; explicitly bans **usury** (*riba*) — one tradition rates a single act of usury worse than thirty-three acts of fornication
- Solution: *hiyal shar'iyya* ("legal devices") — formal compliance with the letter of the law while organizing credit, investment, partnerships, and banking in practice

### Islamic Commerce
- Islamic conquests created the first single political-cultural unit from Morocco to the borders of India and China; **Arabic** served as universal commercial language (unlike Latin in Europe, only a clerical tongue)
- **Hajj** (pilgrimage to Mecca) — annual obligation greatly stimulating long-distance trade and a sense of common identity
- **Bimetallism**: gold coinage in former Byzantine territories + silver in former Sasanid territories → sophisticated monetary exchange and ultimately a **banking system**

### The Slave Trade
- Islamic law: the natural condition of humankind is **freedom**; only permissible enslaved: **infidels captured in holy war** — this cut off local slave replenishment → created insatiable demand → vast long-distance slave trade from beyond imperial frontiers
- Eunuchs in high demand for palaces; castration forbidden by Islamic law, so performed on the frontiers before slaves entered Islamic territory
- Three slave source regions:
  1. **Europe** (Slavic peoples — English "slave" derives from "Slav"): **Barbary corsairs** by 17th c. raided Atlantic coasts — Iceland (1627: 242 captives), Baltimore, Ireland (June 20, 1631: 107 persons)
  2. **Eurasian steppe** (Turks): when Turkish steppe Islamized, supply shifted to **Caucasians** (Georgian and Circassian) — blocked by Russian conquest of the Caucasus (early 19th c.)
  3. **Sub-Saharan Africa** (most lasting supply): three routes (East Africa by sea; Sudan by Nile; West Africa across Sahara)
- **Medieval Islamic economy was NOT primarily based on slave labor** — most slaves were domestic rather than productive laborers

### Major Trade Commodities
- **Silk** — most important luxury good; often a royal monopoly; importation from the East was a major theme in political and military history of transit regions
- **Incense** — "the oil trade of the ancient world"; essential in Greco-Roman temples and Christian churches; Islam has no use for incense in ritual — after Islamic spread, its importance collapsed within the Islamic world
- **Spices** — especially **pepper** from the **Malabar coast**; replaced incense as the dominant commodity; spice merchants were wealthy and highly respected

### The Decline of Middle Eastern Trade
- In the high Middle Ages, Islamic Middle East commerce was **ahead of Europe** in every way; by end of Middle Ages the roles were **reversed**
- **Contrarian claim**: decline was NOT caused by the Portuguese voyages — trade continued for more than a century after **Vasco da Gama** arrived in India; the Discoveries accelerated decline but did not cause it
- Root causes:
  - Exhaustion/loss of mines and precious metals; Islamic states short of money precisely when Europeans found New World gold and silver
  - **Black Death** and natural disasters; Mongols in the East; **Hilali Bedouin** devastating North Africa
  - Domination by **military aristocracies** with little concern for commerce or production
  - Mediterranean sea trade captured by **Italian cities** — not by conquest but by more active commercial methods
  - Middle Eastern agriculture and industry no longer produced exportable surplus — traders became dependent on **transit trade** between Europe and the East; when that route was diverted, the blow was devastating
  - **Ottoman stability** paradoxically facilitated European penetration: Ottoman armies ruled the land, Ottoman fleets dominated the seas — but European merchants "quietly and peacefully captured the markets"

## Chapter 10: The Elites

### The Khassa/ʿAmma Distinction

- Islamic society divided between **khassa** ("special," the privileged) and
  **ʿamma** ("general," the undifferentiated mass)
- Islam was in principle egalitarian — no superiority by birth, race, or
  social status; only piety and good deeds confer honor
- But Islam recognized basic inequalities: man/woman, freeman/slave,
  believer/infidel — and wealth/power holders sought to transmit advantages
- Until Ottoman times, most regimes were too short-lived to establish
  entrenched aristocracies
- Two ethnic exceptions: **Arabs** (creators of the Islamic polity) and
  **Turks** (near monopoly of military command) — both eventually merged
  into broader Muslim identities

### Kafāʾa — Social Equality in Marriage

- The one context where Islamic law explicitly discusses social ranking:
  *kafāʾa* (equality in marriage) — a father/guardian can annul a marriage
  that demeans the family
- **Mālik ibn Anas**: "All the people of Islam are equal to one another"
- Another school (influenced by Persian hierarchic notions): *kafāʾa*
  extends to pedigree, profession, and financial status

### What Defined the Khassa

- Not purely economic or birth — sustained poverty over generations
  was incompatible with khassa membership; **occupation** and
  **education** were key determinants
- Mastery of Arabic, Persian, and Turkish defined educated elites
  across three major regions
- United by **common literary language**, classical tradition, and shared
  codes of behavior — a transnational educated class
- In early periods (ʿAbbasid Baghdad, Fatimid Cairo), Christian and Jewish
  poets, scientists, scholars moved in the same circles as Muslim colleagues;
  later, non-Muslim communities were gradually isolated

### The Bureaucracy

- Bureaucracy may have **originated in the Middle East** — arose to organize
  irrigation in river valley societies; by the 4th millennium BCE,
  Egyptian pharaohs were draining swamps and conducting external trade →
  writing as a specialized skill → a new class of clerks and scribes
- Bureaucracy persisted through every regime change in Egypt:
  pharaohs → Hellenistic → Romans → Christian Byzantines → Arab Muslims
- Same continuity in Iraq and Iran: from **Babylonian** and ancient Persian
  monarchies into Sasanid times and then under Muslim caliphs

### Characteristics of Middle Eastern Bureaucracies

- **Written continuity** — government conducted in writing; letters and
  accounts essential; functionally demarcated, hierarchically ordered
- **Salaried employment** — scribe paid a wage, not by land grant or inheritance
  - When rulers paid in grants instead of cash → "a sure prescription for
    administrative breakdown"
- Administrative language: Greek in the western half, Persian in the eastern
  half before Islam; Egyptian papyri (surviving due to dry climate) allow
  uniquely detailed tracing across Roman, Byzantine, and Islamic periods

### Bureaucratic Continuity After Islamic Conquest

- Most astonishing example: after the 7th-century Arab conquests,
  **nothing changed in day-to-day government**
- Egyptian Christian officials continued collecting the same taxes,
  writing the same documents, dating by the old Christian era — only the
  ultimate destination of revenues changed
- Arab conquerors could not take over: "no one could read the accounts
  except the accountants" — old bureaucratic families kept their craft secrets
- By the 2nd century of the Muslim era, Arab rulers imposed Arabic on staffs;
  old families mostly just learned Arabic; as late as the 13th–14th centuries,
  pious Muslims complained that **Copts** were still running Egyptian
  administration

### The Katib and Scribal Families

> "God … made you secretaries in the most distinguished positions, men of
> culture and virtue, of knowledge and discernment. By your means the
> excellences of the Caliphate are well-ordered." — 8th-c. bureaucrat to
> his colleagues

- Bureaucrats known as ***katib*** (scribe); wore distinctive cloak
  (*darraʿa*); led by the **vizier** — before militarization, the vizier
  took precedence over all dignitaries; his ceremonial emblem: an **inkpot**
- Recruitment by **apprenticeship**, not public examination (public exams
  were a Chinese invention that reached the Islamic world only from the West)
  — patronage as a political weapon that persists into modern times
- Two types of Islamic learned literature:
  - ***Adab*** — poetry, history, belles-lettres; domain of the scribal class
  - ***ʿIlm*** ("knowledge") — religious sciences; domain of the **ulema**

### The Ulema — Men of Religion

- **Islam has no priesthood** in the theological sense — no ordination, no
  sacraments, no priestly mediation; in principle anyone with knowledge can
  lead prayer or preach; closest parallel: **Jewish rabbis** after destruction
  of the Temple — teachers and jurists, not priests
- In practice, law became so extensive that full-time specialists were needed
- Ulema ranged from village mosque official to legal dignitaries:
  - ***Qadi*** — judge appointed by the ruler to administer the Holy Law
  - ***Mufti*** — independent jurisconsult; more highly esteemed than the
    qadi (whose state appointment compromised integrity in popular perception)
  - ***Muhtasib*** — government inspector of markets and morals
  - **No advocates** until the 19th century — the function was previously
    unknown to Muslim jurisprudence
- Ulema funded largely by ***waqf*** (pious endowment in mortmain)

### State vs. Ulema — The Uneasy Relationship

- In early centuries: **mutual suspicion and distance** — pious ulema viewed
  state salaries as tainted by extortion; refusing a state appointment was
  a mark of integrity
- An unofficial **separation of powers** evolved: the state conceded exclusive
  competence of the ulema in matters of Holy Law; the ulema's aloofness gave
  them immense moral authority over ordinary people
- Ulema split into two groups: the very devout (incorruptible) and the
  compliant (accepted public office, forfeited moral authority)

### Convergence in the 12th–13th Centuries

- Threats from Crusades, Mongols, and Sunni-Shia conflicts drove
  **closing of ranks**
- Key institution: the ***madrasa*** — seminary that became the major center
  of Muslim higher education; classical form dates from the 11th century
- The **House of Wisdom** in Baghdad, founded by al-Maʾmun (early 9th c.),
  modeled on the academy of **Gondeshapur** in Persia (Nestorian scholars
  fleeing Byzantine persecution), itself modeled on **Alexandria and Antioch**

### Ulema Integrated into the Ottoman State

- In the **Ottoman Empire**, the ulema became a third branch of imperial
  government alongside bureaucracy and military; headed by the
  ***sheykh al-Islam*** — chief mufti of the capital
- As ulema moved closer to the state, popular influence passed to
  **Sufi sheikhs** — **dervishes** in brotherhoods who supplied spiritual
  sustenance and communal solidarity lacking in official Islam

### Other Intellectual Elites

- **Physicians**: Islamic medical knowledge far surpassed Europe in the
  high Middle Ages; by early modern times they had fallen badly behind;
  not until the 19th century did reforming rulers send students to European
  medical schools
- **Poets**: even minor potentates maintained a court poet; served as
  **mass media** in the absence of print — publication of news and
  projection of favorable image
- **Historians**: medieval historians mostly from the bureaucratic or
  religious classes — in later Ottoman Empire, the **Imperial Historiographer**
  office was formalized; the last imperial historiographer became first
  president of the Ottoman Historical Society

### The Military Elite

- Even in Syria (the most heavily policed eastern Roman province), never
  more than **four legions** in peacetime
- Auxiliary forces included specialized units: ***Alae Dromedariorum***
  (camel-riders); **Arab tribesmen** from desert borderlands gained warfare
  skills that proved decisive during the later Islamic conquests
- Police duties gave the name ***shurta*** (police forces of later Islamic
  regimes) — surviving in Arabic from the Roman auxiliary cohort
- **Persian cataphracts** — mail-clad horsemen with lances and bows;
  **the stirrup** was a major Persian military innovation, making armored
  lancers "the battle-tank of early medieval warfare"
- **Chosroes I (531–579 CE)**: major military reforms — less feudal,
  more professional; soldiers received pay, equipment, strict discipline;
  replaced single supreme commander with a hierarchy of generals
  - Drove Ethiopians from Yemen, ended the Hephthalite threat,
    invaded Syria and **sacked Antioch** — but could not withstand the Arabs

### Arab Tribal Armies to Standing Forces

- Early Muslim armies: not full-time professionals — Arab armies billeted
  in **garrison cities** (*amsar*): **Basra** and **Kufa** (Iraq), **Fustat**
  (Egypt), **Qayrawan** (Tunisia)
- Syria was the exception: forces organized in territorial corps along old
  Byzantine divisions; became the de facto **standing army** of the
  **Umayyad caliphs**

### The Turkish Slave Army — A Structural Revolution

- **Abbasid caliph al-Muʿtasim** (r. 833–842 CE): recruited **Turkish slaves**
  captured young in steppe lands — within a short time, fighting forces of
  most Muslim rulers consisted predominantly of slaves, principally Turkish
- In North Africa and Spain: **Slavic slaves** from Europe; in Morocco and
  Egypt: black African slaves occasionally used
- Core logic: slave soldiers were alien, had no kinship networks or local
  loyalties, each generation replaced not by sons but by a new intake —
  preventing the formation of a hereditary military aristocracy

> "The Youths then that are designed for the great Offices … must be such
> as are … taken in War, or presented from remote parts … they will find no
> Relations … than that of their Great Master, to whom they are taught, and
> necessity compels them to be faithful." — **Paul Rycaut**, English observer,
> mid-17th century

- System worked well: slave armies defeated the **Crusaders** and halted the
  **Mongols** — the two greatest external threats to Islamic civilization
- Failure mode: loyalty was to regiment and officers, not sovereign; slave
  commanders eventually became monarchs themselves — founding short-lived
  dynasties; in late medieval Egypt, the **Mamluk** sultanate carried slave
  recruitment and succession to the throne itself
- **Ottoman Janissaries** were the last great slave army — survived until
  the early 19th century; in 1863, Egypt sent an expeditionary force to Mexico
  composed largely of **black slaves** recruited from the Upper Nile —
  slavery in military use persisted that late

### Land, Commerce, and the Economic Elite

- **Islam's favorable view of commerce**: Qur'an explicitly approves trade;
  numerous hadith praise the honest merchant; earning a livelihood seen as
  obligatory — one must be fed to serve God properly
- **Al-Jahiz** (d. 869), "In Praise of Merchants": merchants enjoy security
  and dignity; government servants endure humiliation and sycophancy;
  God chose a merchant community for His final revelation
- **Land tenure — NOT feudalism**: independent smallholding rare due to
  dependence on artificial irrigation, which required and enabled central control
- Three types of land grant:
  1. **Early caliphal grants** (*iqta'*): permanent, heritable, alienable
  2. **Fiscal delegation**: state grants right to *collect taxes* from an area —
     not ownership; in principle temporary, in practice often became hereditary
  3. **Tax-farming**: state assigns taxes for a lump sum; tax-farmer extracts
     maximum in short term — structurally damaging to long-term productivity
- Islamic grantee had **no seigneurial rights**: could not dispense justice,
  maintain a private army, or grant sub-fiefs — the differences from European
  feudalism remain greater than the resemblances
- **Key dynamic**: autocratic rulers tried to prevent hereditary landowning
  classes; preferred all wealth and authority to derive from the state
- Modern technology finally resolved this struggle in favor of the autocratic
  state: "even the pettiest of modern dictators has greater control than even
  the mightiest of Arab caliphs, Persian shahs and Turkish sultans. The
  traditional restraints on tyranny have gone."

## Chapter 11: The Commonalty

### Islam's Egalitarian Claim — and Its Limits

> "O, people. We have created you from one male and one female… Indeed
> the noblest among you in the sight of God is the most God-fearing."
> — Qur'an 49:13

- All three Abrahamic religions in practice limited full equality to those
  possessing four characteristics: **free, adult, male, co-religionist**
- The four subordinate categories: the **slave**, the **child**, the
  **woman**, the **unbeliever** — each had a path of exit except the woman,
  who was "irredeemably fixed in her inferiority" in traditional religious law
  - Slave: freed by master's will | Unbeliever: could convert | Child: grew up
  - Woman: no exit

### Intermediate Categories

- Between free and slave: the **freedman**; between male and female:
  the **eunuch** — uniquely able to move between male and female space;
  between true believer and unbeliever: Christians and Jews ("people of the book")

### Slavery in Islamic Society

- **Islamic law** severely restricted enslavement — limited to non-Muslims
  captured in war; no enslaving of free Muslims
- Islamic world was **not primarily a slave-based economy** like Greco-Roman
  world; agriculture relied on free or semi-free peasants
- Main uses: **domestic** (palaces, homes — mainly African) and **military**
  (Turkish and Caucasian)
- **Baqt Treaty with Nubia (651–62 CE)**: Nubians agreed to provide
  **360 slaves per year** to Muslims in exchange for meat and lentils
- Slave revolts: East African slaves in Iraq revolted; the most important
  rebellion lasted **868–883 CE**, defeated several imperial armies,
  threatened the caliphate itself
- Slavery abolished: in colonial empires in the 19th century; in independent
  Middle Eastern states in the 20th century

### Women in Islamic Society

- Islam originally an **improvement** over pagan Arabian practice: gave women
  property rights, protected them from ill treatment, outlawed female infanticide
- **Polygamy**: legal, limited to four wives; in practice mostly confined to
  the wealthy; supplemented by **concubinage** (unmarried slave women)
- In inheritance and testimony: woman valued as **half a man**

### The Dhimmi — Tolerated Non-Muslims

- ***Dhimmi*** (people of the pact): protected non-Muslim subjects — Christians,
  Jews, and (in the East) **Zoroastrians**
- The **dhimma** was a contract: dhimmis acknowledged Islamic supremacy
  and accepted subordination; in return received security of life and
  property, freedom of worship, extensive communal autonomy
- Required to pay the **jizya** (poll tax) — Muslims exempt
- Restrictions: could not testify before Muslim courts; could not build new
  places of worship; required to wear **distinguishing dress**; could ride
  only donkeys or mules
- In practice: restrictions frequently unenforced; dhimmis often acquired
  great wealth and exercised economic and occasional political power
- Violent outbreaks against dhimmis were rare, usually triggered by excessive
  exercise of power by dhimmi officials
- **Dhimmis fared better under Sunni than sectarian rulers**

### Social Mobility and the Common People

- Notable claim: "even at the beginning of the nineteenth century a poor man
  of humble origin had a better chance of attaining to wealth, power and
  dignity in the **Ottoman Empire** than in any of the states of Christian
  Europe, including post-Revolutionary France"
- Radical protest movements throughout Islamic history challenged barriers
  between rich and poor, Arab and non-Arab, white and black —
  **notably: none ever questioned** the subordination of slave, woman,
  or unbeliever
- **Nomads**: strong government = nomads quiet; weak government = nomads
  raid oases, villages, caravans; when inspired by a new religious teacher,
  nomads could conquer settled country and establish new dynasties
- **Jelâli**: brigand bands ravaging Ottoman Anatolia in the 16th–17th
  centuries, recruited from discharged soldiers, landless peasants,
  unemployed madrasa graduates — commemorated in Anatolian folk-poetry

## Chapter 12: Religion and Law

### Islam's Rise and Non-Muslim Communities

- Islam became a majority religion gradually — precise timing still unclear
- Varied fates: Georgia, Armenia, Ethiopia retained Christian character;
  North African Christianity died out entirely; **Zoroastrians** fared badly —
  fled to India as **Parsees**; Jews received a considerable improvement over
  treatment under Christian rule

### The Meaning of Islam

- **'Islam'**: Arabic for "surrender" — giving oneself wholly to God;
  also likely conveyed "entirety" (monotheism vs. polytheism)
- Muhammad's mission seen as the **final phase** of monotheistic prophets:
  Moses, David, Jesus — each brought a book; Muhammad brought the **Qur'an**,
  completing and superseding all prior revelation
- Three layers: (1) religion as taught by the Prophet; (2) full system of
  theology + law elaborated by jurists; (3) civilizational usage — "Islam"
  as equivalent of "Christendom"

### The Mosque (*Masjid*)

- **NOT the Muslim equivalent of a church**: no institutional hierarchy, no
  sacraments, no ordained priesthood
- **Mihrab**: niche indicating direction of Mecca; **Minbar**: raised pulpit
  for Friday sermon; no altar, no liturgical music, sculpture specifically
  rejected as "blasphemy verging on idolatry"
- Decoration: abstract/geometric designs + **calligraphy** — writing the
  Qur'an is itself an act of worship
- Arabs first shared, then took over the **Church of St John in Damascus**;
  later converted **Santa Sophia** in Constantinople
- **Minaret** styles varied by region — step-towers of Babylon, Syrian
  church steeples, Egyptian lighthouses all influenced local forms

### The Mosque as Political Space

- Succeeded the Roman **forum** and Greek **agora** as center of polity
- The weekly sermon (**khutba**) included bidding prayer naming the ruler —
  **omitting a name from the khutba = declaration of independence**

### The Sources of Islamic Law: Shari'a

- **Hadith**: oral traditions of Muhammad's actions and utterances; scholars
  questioned authenticity even in medieval times; still revered as second only
  to Qur'an
- **Shari'a**: Lewis calls it "one of the major intellectual achievements of Islam"

> Mirza Abu Talib — one of the first Muslim visitors to leave a written
> account of England — described the House of Commons with astonishment:
> the English, he explained, lacking a divine law, were "reduced to the
> expedient of making their own laws in accordance with the necessities of
> time and circumstance."

### How Shari'a Actually Worked

- In principle: only God's law valid; no human legislative power
- In practice: new law was made constantly but always disguised — no room for
  legislative assemblies as in European democracy
- Solutions from below = **custom**; from above = **regulations**;
  from jurists = **interpretation**
- Key mechanisms:
  1. **Siyasa** (discretionary powers of the ruler): extra-shari'a powers
     including capital punishment
  2. **Kanun**: Ottoman-era structured rule sets that update shari'a without
     superseding it — drew on local custom and prior edicts
- Jurists' principle: **"the rules change as the times change"**

### Orthodoxy, Consensus, and Heresy

- No single imposed orthodoxy in Islam — a **deliberate rejection**, not omission
- **Ijma'** (consensus): universally accepted test of right belief — based on
  hadith: "My community will not agree upon an error"
- Four surviving Sunni schools of shari'a law: **Hanafi, Shafi'i, Maliki,
  Hanbali** — coexisted in mutual toleration, justified by ijma'
- **Ijtihad**: independent reasoning within shari'a; from ~900 CE, Sunni
  jurists declared "the gate of ijtihad was closed"; Shi'a never accepted
  this — their ulema are called **mujtahid**
- **Bid'a** (innovation): nearest Muslim equivalent to heresy — primarily a
  **social** offense (breach of tradition): "Every novelty is an innovation,
  every innovation is an error, every error leads to Hellfire"
- **Heresy is not a category of Muslim law** — the real charge is **apostasy**
  (unbelief, *kufr*), which is treason against the community and carries
  death; a sectarian, even with extruded beliefs, remained legally a Muslim

> **Al-Ash'ari** (d. 935–6), attributed last words: "I testify that I do
> not consider any who pray towards Mecca as infidels. All turn their minds
> in prayer towards the same object. They differ only in expression."

### The Five Pillars of Islam

- **Shahada** (declaration): "God is one and Muhammad is his Prophet"
- **Salat** (ritual prayer): five times daily; requires ritual purity and
  facing Mecca; distinguished from **du'a'** (personal, spontaneous prayer)
- **Friday**: day of public communal prayer — unlike Jewish Saturday or
  Christian Sunday, NOT originally a day of rest; weekly holiday from work
  became common only in Ottoman times
- **Hajj** (pilgrimage): obligatory once in a lifetime; to Mecca and Medina;
  culminates in circumambulation of the **Ka'ba**, which contains the **Black Stone**
- The pilgrimage produced immense social effects: wealthy pilgrims brought slaves;
  merchant pilgrims combined hajj with trade; scholar pilgrims acquired books —
  created a **network of communications** across the far-flung Muslim world

### The Five Pillars — Fasting and Zakat

- **Ramadan**: fast sunrise to sunset during the ninth month
- **Zakat**: financial levy; originally charitable almsgiving, evolved into
  a quasi-tax signifying formal acceptance of Islam
- Basic prohibitions: **pork** (shared with Judaism), **alcohol** (uniquely
  Muslim), **fornication**, **taking of interest** — effects on social and
  economic life "were—and still are—profound and far-reaching"

### Jihad

- **Jihad**: literal meaning = *striving in the path of God*; some modern
  theologians interpret it spiritually, but **overwhelming majority of early
  authorities discuss jihad in military terms**
- Every manual of shari'a law has a chapter on jihad regulating: opening and
  cessation of hostilities, conduct of war, division of booty
- Rules of conduct: don't kill women/children unless attacked first; no torture;
  honor agreements — but victors accorded extensive rights over vanquished,
  including enslavement

> *Paradise is in the shadow of swords.*
>
> *God marvels at people who are dragged to paradise in chains.*

- **Christian Crusade**: a delayed, limited *response* to jihad — concerned
  primarily with defense/reconquest of lost Christian territory
- **Muslim jihad** perceived as **unlimited** — to continue until the whole
  world adopted Islam or submitted to Muslim rule; peoples of "revealed
  religions" (Jews, Christians) could continue practicing under fiscal
  disabilities; polytheists faced conversion, death, or slavery
- **846 CE**: Arab fleet sacked Ostia and Rome; Pope Leo IV offered heavenly
  reward to those dying fighting Muslims — direct precursor of the Crusade

### Evolution and Decline of Jihad

- By the 9th century: repeated failures to take Constantinople led Muslim
  rulers to accept a permanent non-Muslim frontier
- **Crusader arrival (late 11th c.)**: Muslim reaction was almost none — jihad
  had faded so completely that some Muslim rulers even allied with Crusader
  princes against other Muslims
- **Saladin's counter-crusade**: triggered by **Reynald of Châtillon**, who in
  1182 raided Muslim caravans, attacked Mecca-bound pilgrims, and launched a
  Red Sea naval expedition — an unignorable provocation
- **Sunni Muslim contemporaries** saw Saladin's chief achievement not as
  defeating Crusaders but as **liquidating the Fatimid caliphate** and
  restoring Sunni unity — the Isma'ili Shi'a threat was seen as far more
  dangerous than the Franks
- **Al-Malik Al-Kamil** (ruler of Egypt) ceded Jerusalem to Emperor
  **Frederick II** in 1229 — within a generation of Saladin's great victory
- Jihad foundered definitively **under the walls of Vienna in 1683**
- In late 18th/early 19th c.: jihad increasingly framed as **defensive**
  against European imperial conquest

> Bektashi dervish anecdote during 1690 war with Austria: *'Hey, you fools,
> why do you squander your lives for nothing? While the Ottoman emperor enjoys
> himself in his palace, and the Frankish king disports himself in his country,
> I can't think why you should give your lives fighting on the mountaintops!'*

### Dervish Brotherhoods and Sufism

- **Sufism**: began as individual mystical experience, became a mass social
  movement; organized in brotherhoods (**tariqa**); leaders called **dervishes**
- Sufis were politically quietist — served as pastors and guides; worship
  mystical, passionate, ecstatic; used **music, song, and dance** to seek
  union with God; "remained part of the people"
- **Al-Ghazali** (1059–1111): greatest medieval Islamic theologian; resigned his
  Baghdad professorship in 1095, spent **10 years** in solitary study, found
  truth in Sufism; defended mainstream Sunni positions against Shi'a esoterism
  AND philosophical rationalism while bringing Sufi teaching into the mainstream
- **Jalal al-Din Rumi** (1207–1273): born in Balkh, settled in **Konya**;
  greatest Sufi poet; wrote in Persian, Turkish, and Greek

> *I am neither Christian nor Jew, neither Magian nor Muslim / I am not from
> east or west, not from land or sea … / My place is placeless, my trace is
> traceless.*

- **Mevlevi brotherhood** (founded by Rumi, the "dancing dervishes"): co-opted
  by Ottoman state; by 1648 their head officiated at the girding of the sword
  of Osman ceremony
- In late 18th/early 19th c. (during colonial expansion): **dervish orders
  led popular anti-imperial resistance** — the ulema had developed a doctrine
  of submission to effective authority

### Religion as the Basis of Identity

- For Muslims (and Jews/Christians under Muslim rule), religion was not merely
  belief or worship — it was **"the ultimate basis of identity, the primary
  focus of loyalty, the sole legitimate source of authority"**
- Ethnic nations and territorial states existed but **never acquired the
  political importance they held in Europe**

## Chapter 13: Culture

### Middle Eastern Cultural Distinctiveness

- Middle East contrasts sharply with India and China on two features:
  **diversity** and **discontinuity**
- China: unbroken continuity from antiquity — same language, script, religion
- India: cohesive — Hindu religion, Nagari script, Sanskrit classics as
  constant unifying force
- **Middle East**: successive waves — **Hellenization → Romanization →
  Christianization → Islamization** — together wiped out the greater part of
  ancient Middle Eastern written culture
- Ancient languages (Egyptian, Assyrian, Babylonian, Hittite) were abandoned;
  recovered only by Western scholars — the region lacks even a collective name
  comparable to "India" or "China"
- Key contrast with Europe: barbarian peoples who overran Rome adopted Roman
  religion (Christianity) and used Latin — conferring legitimacy through
  continuity; **Muslim Arabs brought their own religion, language, and scripture
  — a complete break, not adaptation**

### Arabic and the Islamic World

- Arabic assumed the role of **Greek in the Hellenistic world, Latin in Europe,
  Sanskrit in South Asia**
- When Persian and Turkish later reappeared as literary languages, both were
  written in **Arabic script** with extensive Arabic vocabulary
- Lexical survivals: *kimiya* (chemistry), *falsafa* (philosophy), *shurta*
  (police, from Roman auxiliary cohort), *ʿaskar* (army, from Latin *exercitus*)
- Notable: *al-sirat al-Mustaqim* (the "straight path" of the Qur'an) derives
  from Latin *strata*, i.e., the Roman road — "the street"

### Three Major Languages

- **Arabic**: dominant in Arabian Peninsula, Fertile Crescent, North Africa
- **Persian** (*Farsi*): Iran, Afghanistan, Tajikistan
- **Turkish**: vast region from Black Sea to the Pacific
- Surviving minorities: **Aramaic** and **Coptic** (diminishing Christian
  minorities), **Berber** and **Kurdish** (widely spoken but no standard
  written form), **Hebrew** (survived as religious language, revived in modern
  times)

### The Arts

- Classical Islamic perception: **only literature** ranked among civilized arts
- **Musicians**: socially inferior — slaves or low-status persons; no standard
  notation system; Islamic classical music survives only through fallible oral
  memory
- **Visual arts**: largely anonymous through much of the Middle Ages
- Painters acquired respected court status only in **Ottoman Turkey** and
  **Safavid Iran**
- **Sculpture**: effectively forbidden throughout
- **Portrait of Mehmed the Conqueror** by **Bellini** — sold by his pious son
  **Sultan Bayezid**; Muslim rulers did not put faces on coinage
- Medieval Islamic interiors: **no tables or chairs**; instead carpets, mattresses,
  cushions — materials from nomadic economy; **textile arts** dominated

### Arabic Poetry — The Qasida

- Classical Arabic poetry begins (traditionally) in the **6th century CE** —
  the **qasida** (desert encampment ode) was the dominant form
- Much of the "pre-Islamic" corpus is suspect — unquestioned contemporary poetry
  begins only with **Umayyad caliphs in Syria**
- Original qasida: a tribal boast; in Umayyad court poetry the vaunt became
  a **panegyric** for the sovereign/patron
- **Udhri school** (named for the tribe of 'Udhra, "whose sons die of love"):
  a cult of unrequited love — a compromise between sensual love and new
  religious morality

### Wine Poetry

- Wine figures prominently in Arabic, Persian, and Turkish poetry despite the
  prohibition — Muslims had to go to **non-Muslim subjects** to drink
- Christian convent in Arabic poetry, Zoroastrian lodge in Persian poetry,
  both acquired the connotation of tavern
- **Sufi poetry** uses Bacchic and erotic imagery to symbolize mystical union
  with God — parallels the Song of Songs

### Poetry as Political Propaganda

- Panegyric and **satire** (*hija'*) were the chief public functions of the
  medieval poet — equivalent to journalism and propaganda
- Arabic *hija'* is cognate with Hebrew *hegeh* (witchcraft) — satire was
  understood as a real curse, not mere insult
- The Prophet himself employed a panegyrist and had satirists executed
- Famous example: the poet **al-Darimi** composed a verse about a woman in a
  black veil to help a merchant sell unsold black veils in Medina — the poem
  went viral; Lewis calls this "perhaps the earliest known occurrence of the
  singing commercial"

### Other Literary Forms

- **Firdawsi** (10th c.): the ***Shahnama*** — long narrative poem of the
  gods and heroes of ancient Iran; status comparable to the *Iliad* and *Aeneid*
- **Maqama**: short piece in **saj'** (rhymed, rhythmic prose) — combines
  travelogue, dialogue, sermon, and social comment; considered a masterpiece of
  Arabic literature
- **Theatre** disappeared from the Middle East in the Islamic period, possibly
  due to its association with pagan rites; shadow-play and puppet theatre
  emerged (first attested 14th c., Egypt and Turkey)
- The **ta'ziya**: Shi'ite passion play commemorating Husayn's martyrdom at
  Karbala — despite its centrality in modern Shi'a ceremonies, it dates only
  from the **late 18th century**

### Islamic Historiography

- Islam was imbued from the beginning with a **strong sense of history**;
  the Qur'an itself is full of historical lessons

> "We tell you stories of the apostles, which will strengthen your heart."
> — Qur'an 11:120

- Three types:
  1. **Heroic narrative** — campaigns of the Prophet, early conquests
  2. **Legal-theological** — preserving acts and utterances as precedents
  3. **Bureaucratic** — by ʿAbbasid times, aimed at civil servants
- **Non-Muslim history** was considered valueless — "the meaningless gyrations
  of ancient pagans"
- After the 10th–13th c. struggles, history was increasingly written by
  *madrasa*-trained scholars whose primary expertise was religious science;
  the *madrasa* never formally included history in its curriculum

### Islamic Science and Scholarship

- Islam, unlike Christianity, **did not translate the Qur'an** — all Muslims
  were required to study and recite it in Arabic only → drove massive
  development of **Arabic grammar and lexicography**
- 9th century: Arab patrons commissioned Arabic translations of major Greek
  works in mathematics, astronomy, physics, chemistry, medicine, geography
- Translators were mostly **non-Muslims**: Christians, Jews, and **Sabians**
  (the only group with sufficient Greek knowledge)
- They did NOT translate Greek historians (deemed "pagan gibberish") or poets
- Only **one Latin work** was translated: the late chronicle of **Orosius**
- **"Arabic numerals"** (positional numbering with zero) came from India;
  Middle Easterners in the 9th c. made them the basis of a new arithmetic
- **Trigonometry** largely a Middle Eastern innovation; **algebra** entirely so
- **ʿUmar (Omar) Khayyam** (d. 1131): famous in the East for algebra, known
  in the West for his quatrains
- **Muhammad ibn Zakariya al-Razi** (d. 920, known as **Rhazes**): perhaps
  the greatest medieval physician; wrote the seminal work on smallpox
- **Ibn Sina** (980–1037, **Avicenna**): compiled the *Canon*, vast medical
  encyclopaedia translated into Latin by **Gerard of Cremona** (13th c.),
  which dominated European medical studies for centuries
- Many leading physicians were Christians and Jews, but they formed part of
  **a single scholarly community** with their Muslim colleagues

### Paper and Printing

- **Paper** (Chinese invention) reached the Islamic world in **751 CE** when
  Arab forces in Central Asia captured Chinese paper makers after a skirmish;
  spread to Spain by the 10th century
- Caliph **Harun al-Rashid** ordered paper used in government offices because
  writing on paper could not be erased without leaving traces
- Printing met fierce resistance: **Bayezid II** banned it by decree in 1485
- **Jewish refugees from Spain** introduced printing to the Ottoman Empire;
  established presses in Istanbul and Salonika by the early 16th century —
  permitted to print only in Hebrew or European languages, not Turkish or Arabic
- First Arabic printed text: a *horologium breve* at Fano in **1514**
- Arabic printing officially authorized only in the **early 18th century**:
  **Said Efendi** (son of the Ottoman ambassador to Paris in 1721) lobbied
  for it; the **chief mufti** issued a *fatwa* authorizing printing of Turkish
  books; imperial *ferman* issued **5 July 1727**
  - First book: a two-volume dictionary, printed **1729**
  - By collaborator **Ibrahim Müteferrika's** death (1745): **17 books** printed

### Islamic Civilization: A Summary Assessment

- At its peak, Islamic civilization was **"in many ways the apex of human
  civilization achievement"** to that date — truly multi-racial, polyethnic,
  intercontinental; the **first religion** to make significant progress toward
  a universal civilization
- **S.D. Goitein**: the Islamic world was the **"intermediate civilization"** —
  intermediate in time (between antiquity and modernity) and in space
  (southern Europe to Southeast Asia)
- Lewis's implicit argument: in the high Middle Ages, it might have seemed
  that **Islamic rather than Latin Christian civilization** offered the greater
  promise of advancing toward a modern universal civilization

> "Yet it was the poor, parochial, monochrome culture of Christian Europe that
> advanced from strength to strength, while the Islamic civilization of the
> Middle East suffered a loss of creativity, of energy, and of power."

## PART V: The Challenge of Modernity

## Chapter 14: Challenge

### The Islamic World-View: Dar al-Islam vs. Dar al-Harb

- Muslim civilization self-defined by religion: the **Dar al-Islam** (House
  of Islam) vs. **Dar al-Harb** (House of War)
- The Mongol invasion — the one great pagan threat from the East — was absorbed:
  conquerors converted and assimilated into Islam
- **Christian Europe** was the one recognized rival: a world faith with its
  own universal mission, political support, and military power
- Northern/western European "barbarians" were initially beneath notice:
  "uncouth primitives offering neither threat nor attraction"
- For ~1,000 years, Muslims held the upper hand — Crusader losses in Spain,
  Sicily offset by Ottoman advance deep into southeastern Europe

### Islamic Contribution to Europe

- Immense: Greek science and philosophy (preserved by Muslims, forgotten in
  Europe), Indian numbers, Chinese paper, oranges, lemons, cotton, sugar,
  crop cultivation techniques
- European contributions to Islam were mainly **material and technical**:
  clocks, watches, eyeglasses, telescopes; food plants from the Americas
  (maize, potato, tomato, tobacco)
- Most important European contribution: **weaponry** — Ottomans adopted
  firearms on a vast scale, gaining decisive advantage over rival Muslim powers

> **Saladin** justified allowing European merchants in reconquered seaports:
> *"there is not one of them that does not bring and sell us weapons of war,
> to their detriment and to our advantage"*

### The Turning Point: 1683 and After

- **September 12, 1683**: Turkish armies besieging Vienna withdrew — their
  second failed attempt; followed by crushing field defeats and loss of cities
- **Treaty of Carlowitz (1699)**: first time Ottomans were compelled to sign
  peace on terms determined by victors; sought mediation from England and
  Holland to mitigate losses

> "This was a calamitous defeat, of such magnitude that there has never been
> its like since the first appearance of the Ottoman state." — contemporary
> Turkish chronicler

### Treaty of Küçük Kaynarca (1774)

- **Empress Catherine II**: described it as a success *"the like of which
  Russia has never had before"*
- Three categories of gains: **territory** (Crimean ports, path to annexation),
  **trade** (free navigation in Black Sea and into Mediterranean), **influence**
  (right to open consulates; right to build a Russian church in Istanbul)
- The church clause — limited on paper — was expanded by "careful
  misinterpretation" into a right of intervention on behalf of **all Orthodox
  Christian subjects** of the Ottoman sultan
- **Loss of Crimea (1783)**: first loss of an old Muslim land to Christian
  conquerors — "a bitter blow to Muslim pride"

### Western Expansion from the Sea

- Portuguese voyages framed as continuation of Crusades and Reconquista
- **British Embassy in Istanbul** was maintained and paid by the **Levant
  Company** (chartered trading company) until the early 19th century
- Dutch and British power in Asia deflected the spice trade from the Red Sea
  and Persian Gulf, hitting Middle Eastern transit trade hard

### Bonaparte's Egypt Expedition (1798)

- **Double lesson**: a small Western force could conquer a Middle Eastern
  heartland with ease; only another Western power could expel them
- Local Egyptians played "comparatively minor role" — the struggle was mainly
  between French and British forces

### 19th Century Nibbling at the Edges

- 1830: France annexed Algeria; 1839: Britain occupied Aden;
  1853: British naval paramountcy in Persian Gulf established
- **Crimean War** (1853–56): first war with Western allies on Turkish soil —
  "direct contacts with the West brought immense changes"
- 1881: France occupied Tunisia; 1882: Britain occupied Egypt;
  1911: Russia invaded northern Iran; 1912: France established Morocco
  protectorate; Italy annexed Libya

> "The Islamic Middle East was now caught in a pincer movement formed by
> the expansion of Europe at both ends since the sixteenth century."

## Chapter 15: Change

### Economic Decline: Relative and Absolute

- Contributing factors:
  - Rising cost of armament and war; price inflation from New World silver
  - Transoceanic trade routes bypassed Middle Eastern transit trade
  - Chronic unfavorable balance of trade; steady outflow of gold/silver
  - No agricultural or industrial revolution; technological stagnation
  - **Tax-farming** replaced military grant system — adverse effects on
    countryside and central authority
  - Population decline: Turkey, Syria, Egypt all had lower populations in
    1800 than in 1600; Egypt fell from ~8M (Roman times) to ~3.5M by 1800
- Turkish share in European trade collapsed:
  - With France: from 1/2 (late 16th c.) to 1/20 (late 18th c.)
  - With Britain: from 1/10 (mid-17th c.) to 1/100 (end of 18th c.)
- Middle East shifted from exporting manufactured textiles to importing them;
  now exporting raw materials (cotton, mohair, silk)

### Social Consequences of Economic Change

- **Christian minorities** (Greek, Armenian, Jewish) seized new commercial
  opportunities as merchants and shippers — gained wealth, education, influence
- 1912 list of 40 private bankers in Istanbul: 12 Greeks, 12 Armenians, 8 Jews,
  5 Levantines/Europeans — and **no Turks**
- Istanbul stockbrokers (34 listed): 18 Greeks, 6 Jews, 5 Armenians, no Turks

### Technological Backwardness and the Core Diagnosis

- Two critical areas: **shipbuilding** and **weapons**; Ottoman government
  hired European naval engineers and bought vessels from Sweden and the US
- Road/canal network neglected; wheeled transport almost unknown in the Middle
  East at the start of the 19th century — transport by pack animal or river boat
- Even basic innovations (water mill, windmill) had "little or no impact"
- Ottoman decline was not primarily from internal changes but from **failure
  to keep pace with Western advances** in science, technology, war, government

> "The scientific wave broke against the dikes of literature and jurisprudence."
> — distinguished Turkish historian

### The Coffee and Sugar Inversion

- Vivid illustration: both coffee and sugar were originally Middle Eastern
  products introduced to Europe
- By early 18th century: Dutch growing coffee in Java; French exporting West
  Indian coffee *to Turkey*; by 1739, colonial coffee reached as far as Erzurum
- In 1671, **Colbert** ordered a refinery in Marseilles to export colonial sugar
  to Turkey — West Indian sugar soon dominated the market
- By end of 18th century: when a Turk or Arab drank coffee with sugar,
  both the coffee and sugar had been grown in European colonies.
  "Only the hot water was of local provenance."

### The Capitulations System

- **Capitulations** (Latin *capitula*): privileges granted by Ottoman rulers
  to Christian states, allowing their citizens to reside and trade without the
  fiscal and legal disabilities imposed on the sultan's non-Muslim subjects
- Originally accorded to Italian maritime states (14th–15th c.); extended to
  France (1569), England (1580)
- As Ottoman power declined, European missions began distributing **berats**
  (protection certificates) to increasing numbers of local merchants —
  near-unlimited right of interference in Ottoman internal affairs

### Debt and Financial Penetration

- Ottoman government began floating loans on European money markets during
  the **Crimean War**: first loan £3 million at 6% (London, 1854)
- Between 1854 and 1874, foreign loans raised almost every year; nominal total
  ~**£200 million**
- **6 October 1875**: Ottoman government defaulted on debt
- **20 December 1881**: 'Council of the Administration of the Public Debt'
  set up, controlled by foreign creditors; certain revenues ceded "absolutely
  and irrevocably … until complete liquidation of the debt"
- By 1911: the Debt Council had **8,931 employees** — more than the Ottoman
  Ministry of Finance itself

### Ottoman Internal Weakness — The A'yan and Derebeys

- Traditional Ottoman kingpin: the **sipahi** cavalryman with a land grant
  (**timar**); system nearly extinct by the 19th century; vacated timars
  became **tax-farms** and then **malikane** (life tenures that became
  heritable) — economic basis for the **a'yan** (provincial notables)
- From early 18th century, **derebeys** ("valley lords") arose in Anatolia —
  local hereditary rulers; by early 19th century, almost all of Anatolia was
  in the hands of derebey families
- In the Balkans: **Tepedelenli Ali Pasha** (Yannina) maintained diplomatic
  relations with foreign powers; **Egypt** virtually autonomous; in Arabia,
  the **House of Sa'ud** openly flouted Ottoman authority

> **Lady Mary Wortley Montagu**, 1717, on the ulema: "They are the only men
> realy considerable in the Empire; all the profitable Employments and church
> revenues are in their hands. The Grand Signor, tho the general Heir to his
> people, never presumes to touch their lands or money."

## Chapter 16: Response and Reaction

### The Islamic Worldview Before Defeat

- For centuries, Muslims viewed history as a story of God's truth being
  extended to all mankind; military victory manifested God's favor —
  defeat was therefore theologically as well as practically devastating
- A 16th-century Turkish author describing the European discovery of America
  piously hoped the New World would "in due course, be illuminated by Islam
  and added to the Ottoman realm"

### Architecture as Evidence of Faltering Confidence

- The great imperial mosques of Istanbul expressed strength and self-confidence;
  by contrast, the Topkapi Palace was modest — crowds greeted new sultans with:
  *"Sultan, do not be proud, God is greater than you"*
- **Nuruosmaniye Mosque** (1755): structurally Ottoman grand style, but
  ornamentation suggests Italian baroque — "the first sign of faltering
  self-confidence"
- **Dolmabahce Palace** (1853): now the palace, not the mosque, receives lavish
  resources — a mixture of imported European styles, "graphically illustrates
  the ambitious aims and confused directions of the nineteenth-century reforms"

### The Reform Cascade

- Military reform was the entry point, but required educated officers →
  education reform; logistics → administrative reform; arms factories →
  economic reform
- Military reformers "intended no more than to open a sluice" but "started a
  flood they could not control"
- From the 1820s onward: periodicals and daily newspapers accelerated
  diffusion of European ideas
- Turkish cadets learning French to read military manuals found access to
  broader European ideas — "some of the ideas they found proved more explosive
  than anything their artillery instructors could offer them"

### Islamic Reactions: Revivalism and Wahhabism

- Two major 18th-century responses to internal decay and Western encroachment:
  1. **Naqshbandi Dervishes** (Sufi origin, introduced from India): sparked
     revival of Arabic learning and an Egyptian renaissance (aborted by the
     French invasion)
  2. **Wahhabis**: puritanical, militant; conquered much of the Arabian
     Peninsula; power destroyed in **1818** but **faith survived** and revived
     repeatedly; full doctrine found few Middle East disciples but religious
     militancy spread widely
- Resistance to Western invaders was led not by sultans or soldiers but by
  **popular religious leaders** from these revivalist movements

### Two Trends in Reform: Authoritarian vs. Liberal

- **Authoritarian/Central European model**: knew what was best; did not want
  popular government distracting from implementing reforms
- **Liberal/Western European model**: people have rights to be secured through
  representative and constitutional government; **freedom** seen as the true
  basis of Western power, wealth, and greatness

### Constitutionalism: A Fitful History

- "Freedom" in early 19th-century Middle Eastern usage meant individual liberty
  from arbitrary rule — not yet "independence from foreign domination"
- **1861**: Ruler of Tunis proclaimed a constitution — **first in any Islamic
  country**; suspended 1864
- **1876**: Ottoman constitution promulgated by **Sultan Abdülhamid II**;
  parliament sat for ~5 months total across two sessions; dismissed when it
  showed signs of vigor; did not meet again for **thirty years**
- **Japan's 1905 victory** over Russia: first Asian victory over a European
  power — constitutionalism was seen as the "elixir of life"
- **Summer 1906**: Constitutional revolution in Iran; **1908**: **Young Turks**
  compelled the sultan to restore the 1876 constitution

### Pan-Islamism

- Emerged in the 1860s–1870s: common Muslim front against Christian empires;
  partly inspired by German and Italian national unifications — the Ottoman
  Empire seen as a potential Prussia/Piedmont for Muslims
- Defined solidarity by **religion/community**, not ethnicity, language, or
  territory — ultimately overshadowed by liberal ideologies and the idea of
  **the nation**

## Chapter 17: New Ideas

> "Italy, which is inhabited only by a single race speaking the same language
> and professing the same religion, experiences so many difficulties in its
> unification… Judge what would happen in Turkey if free scope were given to
> all the different national aspirations…. It would need a century and
> torrents of blood to establish even a fairly stable state of affairs."
> — **Ali Pasha**, Ottoman Foreign Minister, September 1862 (his estimate
> actually fell short)

### Why the French Revolution Was Different

- Pre-French Revolution European intellectual movements (Renaissance,
  Reformation, Enlightenment, Scientific Revolution) passed **unnoticed** in
  the Islamic world — seen as Christian, therefore irrelevant
- **French Revolution** was the first European upheaval that did NOT express
  itself in Christian terms — even presented by some as anti-Christian
- Secularism had no appeal to Muslims; but a movement free from Christian taint,
  opposed by traditional Ottoman enemies, could be examined on its merits
- French Embassy in Istanbul became a center of propaganda; revolutionary
  literature translated into Turkish, Arabic, Greek, Armenian
- 1793: French ships in Istanbul harbor flew the tricolour; Austrians, Prussians,
  and Russians demanded a ban; the Ottoman Sublime Porte replied it didn't concern
  itself with "the headgear or footwear of its foreign guests"
- French rule over the Ionian Islands (1797–1799) introduced: abolition of
  noble privileges, elections, talk of liberty and equality

### The Meaning of "Freedom" in Islamic Context

- In Islamic usage, "free" meant not-a-slave — **NOT a political concept**
- **In Muslim tradition, the opposite of tyranny was not freedom but justice**
  — seen as a ruler's duty, not a subject's right
- Western concept of **citizenship** (participation, representation) first
  entered via revolutionary France
- **Liberty** (political): genuinely new concept in the Islamic world
- **Equality** between believers and non-believers was a different matter —
  removable only by conversion

### The Ottoman Millet System

- **Millet**: religio-political community administered by its own chiefs under
  its own religious law, in exchange for allegiance and acceptance of dhimmi
  disabilities
- The **four Ottoman millets**:
  - **Muslim**: Turks, Arabs, Kurds, Albanians, Muslim Greeks, Caucasians
  - **Greek**: Ethnic Greeks + Orthodox Serbs, Bulgars, Romanians, Christian Arabs
  - **Armenian**: Armenian Church + Copts + Jacobites
  - **Jewish**: Sephardic Spanish refugees (post-1492) + Arabic and Greek-speaking Jews

> "The patriarchs … were displeased.… Whereas in former times in the Ottoman
> state, the communities were ranked, with the Muslims first, then the Greeks,
> then the Armenians, then the Jews, now all of them were put on the same level.
> Some Greeks objected to this, saying, 'The government has put us together
> with the Jews. We were content with the supremacy of Islam.'"
> — **Cevdet Pasha**, Ottoman observer, on the reform edict of February 1856

### Nationalism Hits the Christian Minorities

- European nationalism's impact was "stronger and more immediate" among
  Christian peoples of the Empire: Greeks, Serbs → Balkan peoples →
  Armenians → eventually Jews
- **1843**: Rabbi **Yehuda Alkalai** of Sarajevo wrote book proposing Jews
  return to Holy Land — an early proto-Zionist text
- Christian minorities pursued three irreconcilable objectives: equal
  citizenship in the Ottoman state; independence or autonomy in a national
  territory; retention of millet privileges (communal laws, educational autonomy)
- **Phanariots**: Greek patrician families who virtually monopolized key Ottoman
  positions including **Grand Dragoman** (effectively in charge of foreign
  relations); Greek War of Independence triggered summary hanging of the
  Grand Dragoman on suspicion of rebel sympathy

### Jews and Armenians: Shifting Fortunes

- **Jews**: useful to Ottomans in 16th century; declined with Ottoman power;
  replaced in business and government by Christians
- **Armenians** (*millet-i Sadika* — "faithful millet"): took over positions
  vacated by Greeks; co-operated with **Young Turks** in 1908 revolution;
  briefly had an Armenian foreign minister post-revolution
- But the same forces of nationalism that had destroyed Greek-Ottoman symbiosis
  eventually destroyed Armenian-Ottoman symbiosis too

### The Armenian Catastrophe

- Armenians worse positioned than Balkan Christians: their majority zones were
  scattered among increasingly hostile Muslim majorities
- 1890 and especially **1895–96**: massacre and terror in eastern Turkey
- **Hamidiye**: irregular force authorized by **Sultan Abdülhamid II** to deal
  with Armenian insurgents — became instrument of mass killing

### Patriotism vs. Nationalism — Key Distinction

- **Patriotism**: political/military duty owed to one's country; aimed to
  *bind together* diverse peoples under a common state loyalty; Ottoman
  reformers hoped it could unify the Empire's diverse communities
- **Nationalism**: defined nation by language, culture, common descent —
  NOT by country or state; could *subvert* existing political order:
  the state itself might be alien tyranny; freedom means national independence
- Egypt uniquely suited for territorial patriotism: defined sharply by geography
  (Nile valley/delta), continuous identity across millennia — yet acceptance of
  Egyptian national identity was "slow, gradual, and contested"

## Chapter 18: From War to War

### Ottoman Wars — Overview

- For more than a century before dissolution, the Ottoman Empire fought
  near-continuous warfare against internal and external enemies
- Between 1806–1878, Russia went to war with the Ottomans **four times**,
  each ending in significant Ottoman territorial loss
- Ottoman defeats would have been far worse without Western intervention

### Muhammad Ali and Wahhabism

- **Muhammad Ali Pasha** (governor of Egypt 1805–1848): waged diplomatic and
  military struggle against the sultan; prevented from defeating the Ottomans
  only by European intervention; made Egypt an **autonomous hereditary
  principality** and launched modernization
- **Wahhabism**: Founder **Muhammad ibn Abd al-Wahhab** (1703–1787), allied
  with **Muhammad ibn Saud** (emir of Dar'iyya, Najd); conquered much of Arabia,
  threatened Syria and Iraq borderlands
- Lewis's sharp observation: Wahhabis presented themselves as a renewal of
  original Islamic expansion, but the Ottomans had **gunpowder** — and
  succeeded where great empires had failed in the 7th century

### Iran — Anglo-Russian Rivalry

- Iran's 19th-century history dominated by Anglo-Russian rivalry:
  - Russia: military conquest, annexed lands on both sides of the Caspian
  - Britain: consolidated India, responded by extending power to Iran's
    southeastern border
- Key milestones: **1864**: British opened first telegraph in Iran; **1872**:
  **Reuter Concession** — British firm granted exclusive right to develop
  mineral resources, establish bank, build railways — cancelled under Russian
  pressure; **1879**: **Cossack Brigade** established — trained, equipped and
  partly officered by Russia; **1901**: British oil concession

### 1905 — A Regional Turning Point

- Russia's defeat by Japan: first time a European imperial power was defeated
  by an Asian nation; Japan had promulgated a constitution (1889); message
  was unmistakable — constitutionalism was the "elixir of life"
- **Persian Constitutional Revolution** began December 1905; first **Majlis**
  (national assembly) met October 1906
- **Anglo-Russian Entente of August 1907** divided Iran into Russian sphere
  (north), British sphere (south), central belt open to both

### Ottoman Constitutional Revolution of 1908

- Overthrow of **Sultan Abdülhamid**'s despotism; Turks, Armenians, Muslims,
  Christians, Jews embraced in streets; then swiftly disappointed

> 'There are few movements in the world that have given rise to such great
> hopes … there are likewise very few movements whose hopes have been so
> swiftly and finally disappointed.' — Turkish historian, 1940

- European powers saw it as opportunity: Austria-Hungary annexed
  Bosnia-Herzegovina; Bulgaria declared independence; Crete announced union
  with Greece

### The Cascade of Wars, 1911–1913

- **September 1911**: Italy attacked Tripoli — last Ottoman foothold in North
  Africa; **First Balkan War** (Oct 1912–May 1913): Bulgaria, Serbia, Greece
  made substantial gains; **Second Balkan War** (June–July 1913): between
  victorious allies; amid all this, Young Turk democracy foundered; **coup
  d'état** January 1913 installed effective military dictatorship

### World War I — Ottoman Entry and the Armenian Deportations

- End of October 1914: Turkish warships (with two German cruisers) bombarded
  Russian Black Sea ports; Sultan-caliph proclaimed **jihad** — hoping British,
  French, Russian Muslim subjects would revolt; they did not
- Spring 1915: Armenian rebels held Van while British were at Dardanelles;
  Ottoman government ordered **deportation and relocation** of Anatolian Armenians
- Deaths: "at least hundreds of thousands … perhaps more than a million" —
  starvation, disease, exposure, and brutal murders by local tribesmen
- Ottoman central government made some effort to curb excesses: ~1,400 courts
  martial held — but limited effect

### The Arab Revolt

- **Sharif Husayn** proclaimed independence of the Hijaz and then "King of
  the Arabs" in 1917; British had made "certain promises concerning an
  ill-defined Arab independence"
- Military significance of Bedouin irregulars minor; but moral significance —
  Arab ruler of the holy places denouncing the Ottoman sultan's jihad — was
  immense for Allied authority over their own Muslim subjects

### WWI: End and Settlement

- December 1917: British capture Jerusalem; October 1918: British capture
  Damascus; **29 October 1918**: Ottoman delegation boards HMS *Agamemnon*
  off Mudros; armistice signed next day
- "The First World War marked the culmination of the retreat of Islam before
  the advancing West"
- Fertile Crescent divided into **League of Nations mandates**:
  - **Iraq**: monarchy under British Mandate, **King Faysal**
  - **Syria/Lebanon**: French Mandate
  - **Palestine**: British Mandate; eastern part became **Transjordan**
    under **Abdallah** (another son of the sharif)

### Rise of Ibn Saud and Atatürk

- **Abd al-Aziz ibn Saud**: captured Hijaz from Husayn (1924–1925); Mecca,
  Medina, Jedda in his possession by end of 1925; **8 January 1926**:
  proclaimed King of the Hijaz and Sultan of Najd; **September 1932**:
  kingdom renamed **Saudi Arabia**
- **Mustafa Kemal** (later **Atatürk**): organized resistance in Anatolia
  1919; expelled foreign forces, annulled draconian peace treaty, abolished
  the sultanate, proclaimed a republic — carried through "extensive and
  comprehensive programme of modernization and — uniquely in the Muslim world —
  of secularization"
- **Reza Khan** (later **Reza Shah**): February 1921 seized power; 1925
  deposed the shah and proclaimed himself shah — founding **Pahlavi dynasty**
  (lasted until 1979); like Atatürk in centralization and modernization;
  unlike Atatürk: made no attempt to disestablish Islam

## Chapter 18 (continued): The Anglo-French Interlude

### Failure of Indirect Rule

- Direct rule proved unworkable; mandatary powers shifted to **indirect rule**
  through Arab governments, conceding nominal independence while retaining
  military bases — this too failed: concessions were always too small and
  too late
- **Anglo-Egyptian Treaty of 1936** — signed only because the Italian invasion
  of Ethiopia created a common threat

### Zionism and the Palestine Problem

- **Zionism** emerged in the late 19th century: Eastern European Jews arrived
  in Palestine inspired by a movement drawing on Jewish religious tradition,
  Jewish nationalism, and the need to escape persecution in Europe
- **Balfour Declaration (November 1917)**: Britain declared support for
  a 'National Home for the Jews' — incorporated into the League of Nations
  Mandate, sharpening Arab resistance

### Axis Attraction in the 1930s

- Liberal and constitutional institutions lost appeal — alien, ineffective,
  associated with hated imperial powers
- **Fascist Italy** and **Nazi Germany** offered a seductive alternative:
  recently unified nations that had forcibly united fragmented peoples,
  enemies of Britain and France, opponents of Jewish immigration to Palestine
- As early as 1933, **Hajj Amin al-Husayni** (British-appointed Mufti of
  Jerusalem) contacted the German consul to offer support
- **Rashid 'Ali al-Gaylani** seized power in Iraq in April 1941 — overthrown
  by British-led forces; his support committee became the nucleus of the
  **Ba'th party**, which later governed both Syria and Iraq
- **Nasser** recorded sympathy for Germany; **Sadat** (by his own account)
  was a willing German intelligence co-operator

> 'We wanted to be on the guest list, not on the menu.' — Turkish statesman
> on why Turkey finally declared war (at the last moment)

### The Oil Revolution

- Oil drilling began in Russian-ruled Azerbaijan as early as 1842; first
  refinery built in **Baku in 1863**; by the eve of the Russian Revolution,
  Baku provided 95% of Russia's oil
- First major concession in Iran granted to **William Knox D'Arcy** →
  acquired by the **Anglo-Persian (later Anglo-Iranian) Oil Company**
- Internal combustion engine: car, bus, truck replaced horse/donkey/camel;
  combined with printing, radio, cinema, TV — far-reaching social transformation

### The Balance Sheet of Anglo-French Domination

- Primary motive was **strategic**, not economic: denial of the region to
  other powers, protection of India (British) and North Africa (French)
- Most positive results came in areas given least importance: economic and
  practical — life was materially better in 1939 than in 1914 or 1918
- Middle Easterners got less benefit than those under direct colonial rule —
  suffered the drawbacks of imperialism without receiving its full advantages
- Key benefit: **English and French languages** opened access to modern world
  science and culture; provided an **interlude of liberal economy and political
  freedom** — more extensive than anything before or after; those institutions
  have since been largely abandoned
- Greatest mutual service: the Middle East provided Allied base/support
  facilities against the Axis; the West saved the Middle East from direct
  experience of Axis rule

## Chapter 19: From Freedom to Freedom

### Post-War Independence Wave

- Three states had full sovereignty after WWI: **Turkey, Iran, Afghanistan**
- Inter-war additions: **Saudi Arabia, Yemen, Iraq, Egypt** (Iraq and Egypt
  still bound by unequal treaties)
- **League of Arab States** formed March 1945: Egypt, Iraq, Syria, Lebanon,
  Saudi Arabia, Yemen, Transjordan
- Independence wave: Libya (1951), Sudan/Tunisia/Morocco (1956), Mauritania
  (1960), Kuwait (1961), Algeria (1962), South Yemen (1967), Gulf States (1971)
- **Soviet Union breakup (1991)**: Transcaucasian and Central Asian republics
  suddenly independent — Azerbaijan, Kazakhstan, Uzbekistan, Kyrgyzstan,
  Turkmenistan (Turkish-related); Tajikistan (Persian)

### Internal Conflicts of New States

- New Arab states largely artificial constructs of colonial borders; few had
  genuine historical identities (exceptions: **Egypt, Morocco**)
- **Lebanon**: civil wars in 1958, 1975–6, and 1983–1991
- **Jordan (September 1970)**: the **PLO** openly challenged Hashimite state
  authority — defeated in a bloody confrontation ('Black September')
- Lewis's assessment: Arab states, despite artificial origins, proved
  **remarkably persistent** in preserving statehood and territorial integrity —
  no Arab state has been pulled apart; no two (except arguably Yemen) have
  successfully merged

### The Arab-Israel Wars

- **April 2, 1947**: British government announced return of the Palestine
  Mandate to the UN; withdrawal set for **15 May 1948**
- **UN Partition Resolution (29 November 1947)**: divided Palestine into a
  Jewish state, an Arab state, and a corpus separatum — passed by two-thirds
  majority but with no enforcement mechanism
- **14 May 1948**: Jewish leadership declared the state of **Israel** — recognized
  *de facto* immediately by the USA, *de jure* by the Soviet Union; Arab
  coalition attacked; Israelis showed unexpected military strength; Arab coalition
  weakened by dynastic rivalries and overconfidence
- Post-ceasefire: Egypt held **Gaza Strip**; Jordan held **West Bank and East
  Jerusalem**; Syria held a small enclave on the Sea of Galilee
- Estimated **726,000** Palestinian Arabs fled or were driven from their homes;
  uniquely, Palestinians were **neither repatriated nor resettled** — kept in
  camps for generations; only **Jordan** offered citizenship
- Simultaneously, Israel absorbed hundreds of thousands of Jews expelled or
  fleeing from Arab countries
- Lewis's balanced assessment: wars of **1948 and 1973** were unmistakably
  launched by Arab governments; **1956 and 1982** by Israel; **1967** is
  hardest to assign — participants like characters in a Greek tragedy
- **Six-Day War (1967)**: Israel defeated Egypt, Jordan, Syria, and an Iraqi
  expeditionary force in six days; gained all of mandatary Palestine west of
  the Jordan, the **Golan Heights**, and the **Sinai Peninsula**
- **1979 Egypt-Israel peace treaty**: first with any Arab country; established
  normal diplomatic relations; Israel withdrew from Sinai
- 1967's defeat ended Arab states' claim to speak for Palestinians; gave new
  importance to the **PLO**, founded three years earlier but previously marginal

### PLO: From Inter-Arab Tool to International Actor

- PLO expelled from Jordan 1970; set up virtual state-within-a-state in Lebanon
- 1982: Israeli forces entered Lebanon, expelled PLO; leadership relocated
  to **Tunis** until 1994
- Late 1980s: the **Intifada** — aimed to weaken and discourage occupation directly
- 1993: PLO and Israel recognized each other; interim agreements transferred
  authority in **Gaza Strip** and **West Bank** to Palestinians

### Cold War as Conflict Preserver

- **Stalin** saw Israel as a tool to undermine British position — allowed
  Czechoslovakia to supply weapons; both US and USSR gave diplomatic support
  to Israel in 1948–9
- **Baghdad Pact** (1955): Iraq induced to join with Turkey, Iran, Britain —
  backfired badly; Arab states saw it as Western reimposition of dominance;
  Soviets leap-frogged it by establishing close relations with Egypt and
  other Arab states
- Superpower involvement **contained** crises but prevented real resolution —
  role of international agencies was "the conservation of conflict"
- Ending of the Cold War was "an essential prerequisite" for any Middle Eastern
  peace process

### Iraq-Iran War (1980–1988)

- Lasted longer than WWII; death toll exceeded all Arab-Israel wars combined
- Multiple dimensions: Khomeini vs. **Saddam Hussein**, Persian vs. Arab,
  Islamic revivalism vs. secular modernism, Sunni vs. Shia, oil, territory
- Notable: Arab minority in SW Iran did NOT rally to Iraq; Iraqi Shia showed
  little sympathy for Iranian revolution — national loyalty trumped sectarianism
- Saddam's quasi-victory emboldened him to invade **Kuwait**, August 1990
- **Kuwait War 1990–1**: US/coalition defeated Saddam quickly but left him in
  power — installing a replacement would have required mandate-style occupation;
  US had no desire for a "proconsul in Baghdad"
- Saddam's strategic miscalculation: he could no longer rely on a superpower
  patron to protect him — he failed to account for the **collapse of the
  Soviet Union**

> "The Middle East was a freer, and also a more dangerous place." — Lewis on
> post-Cold War order

### Post-Independence Political Orders

- Both British (constitutional monarchies) and French (parliamentary republics)
  political models collapsed after independence
- **Axis ideological influence** persisted after Axis military defeat — ethnic
  nationalism, authoritarian/collectivist ideologies had greater regional appeal
  than liberal individualism
- Arab defeat in 1948 triggered violent removal of rulers blamed for the catastrophe:
  - **Syria**: first of series of military coups d'état (March 1949); then Ba'th
    party dictatorship
  - **Egypt**: 1952–4, King Faruq deposed; real power held by **Colonel Nasser**
    and the "Free Officers"
  - **Iraq**: 1958 monarchy overthrown; eventually Ba'th party dictatorship
- **Lebanon**: only Arab state bordering Israel that retained parliamentary
  system after 1948 — eventually overthrown in civil war "due in large measure
  to external intervention"

### Pan-Arabism, Socialism, and Their Failures

- **Pan-Arabism**: diagnosis that Arab fragmentation was root cause of weakness;
  solution: united Arab nation; declined once states achieved independence —
  leaders reluctant to surrender sovereignty; **United Arab Republic (1958)**:
  Egypt and Syria merged; Syria seceded 1961 — most ambitious pan-Arab union
  ended in failure
- As pan-Arabism faded, focus shifted to socialist solutions — imposed from
  above, not from below; encouraged by Soviet example
- By early 1990s: both Arab and Marxist socialism had "failed" — reforms impeded
  rather than advanced economic development; one success only: "underpinning a
  series of ruthless and pervasive dictatorships" copied from worst Central and
  East European models
- Two main interpretive responses to Israel's survival:
  1. **Imperialist tool** explanation — Zionism as instrument of Western
     imperialism; some adopted European antisemitic imagery "with the roles
     reversed"
  2. **Self-criticism** — focus on own society's deficiencies; Israel seen as
     part of Western civilization, not just Western power

### Iran's 1979 Revolution

- Distinct: Islamic rather than European-ideological; leaders rejected both
  left and right Western models as "infidel"
- **Paradox**: revolutionary methods were thoroughly European, not Islamic —
  summary trials, mass executions, mass exile, large-scale property confiscation,
  indoctrination "owes far more to the examples of **Robespierre** and **Stalin**
  than to those of **Muhammad** and **'Ali**"
- First truly modern revolution of the electronic age: **Khomeini** used
  cassette tapes to distribute oratory from exile; directed followers by
  telephone (direct dialing introduced by the Shah he was overthrowing)
- International appeal: strongest among Shia populations; but also powerful
  across Sunni Muslim world — Khomeini seen as Islamic revolutionary leader,
  not just a Shia or Iranian figure

### The Oil Curse

- Oil revenues **freed autocratic governments from financial pressures** that,
  elsewhere, drove democratization — rulers no longer needed to negotiate with
  taxpayers or parliaments
- Oil wealth produced **lopsided development** — dangerously exposed to
  fluctuations in world oil prices and, long-term, to the declining position
  of oil itself

> 'Algeria was once the granary of Rome, and now it has to import cereals to
> make bread. It is a land of flocks and gardens, and it imports meat and
> fruit. It is rich in oil and gas, and it has a foreign debt of twenty-five
> billion dollars and two million unemployed.' — Algerian in a French
> news magazine, attributing this to thirty years of mismanagement

### Cultural Westernization

- **Art**: By late 18th century, miniature painting dying; replaced in 19th
  century by European-style oil painting; one Islamic norm retained: resistance
  to sculpture (statues of Atatürk and the Shah were condemned as "pagan idolatry")
- **Literature**: From mid-19th century, novel and short story replaced
  traditional tale; even literary language irreversibly changed
- **Music**: Least penetrated — "Music is perhaps the profoundest and most
  intimate expression of a culture"; European art music still has limited
  following outside Turkey and Israel

### The Woman Question

- Lewis calls the change in women's position "the profoundest and most
  far-reaching" of all Western-influenced changes
- Polygamous marriage outlawed in Turkey, Tunisia, pre-revolutionary Iran
- **Counterintuitive finding**: legal emancipation of women went furthest in
  Iraq and South Yemen — both notoriously repressive regimes; lagged behind
  in Egypt, the most tolerant Arab society — public opinion (male, conservative)
  resisted; women's rights worst where fundamentalists hold power

### The Crisis of Modernity — Lewis's Conclusion

- Outside powers by the last decade of the 20th century were substantially
  **no longer interested in directing** Middle Eastern affairs — extreme
  reluctance to become involved; concerned with only three things:
  1. A rich and growing **market** for goods and services
  2. A major source of **energy**
  3. **Maintenance of international law and order** to safeguard the first two
- Middle Eastern peoples and governments are **on their own for the first time
  in two centuries**
- Lewis lays out four possible trajectories:
  1. **New regional powers** acting in concert or competing for hegemony
  2. **Fragmentation and internecine chaos** — the Yugoslavia/Somalia path;
     Lebanon's civil war as potential paradigm for the entire region
  3. **Holy war** — a new *jihad* which might provoke a new Crusade in response
  4. **Peace** — with themselves, neighbors, and the outside world; sharing
     spiritual and material resources for a fuller, freer life

> 'They alone—the peoples and governments of the Middle East—can decide whether
> and how to use this window of opportunity while, in an interval of their
> troubled modern history, it remains open.' — Bernard Lewis, closing sentence
